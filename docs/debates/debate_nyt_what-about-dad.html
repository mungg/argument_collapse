What About Dad?

Last month, the mommy wars took front and center with Time magazine’s provocative cover story that asked “Are You Mom Enough?” The month before, Room for Debate discussed whether mothering was bad for feminism, the assertion of Elisabeth Badinter’s “The Conflict.”

Throughout these and earlier spats about raising children, men were largely absent: unheard and unmentioned. But fathers have stepped up. Today’s dads can change diapers and shake up the P.T.A. So why hasn’t the national conversation evolved along with them? Why do we still think of “parenting” as “mothering”?

The reason "parenting" still reads as "mothering" is not that fathers haven't changed — they have, dramatically — but that our cultural infrastructure hasn't caught up. Look at the marketing aisle: changing tables in men's restrooms remain rare, and "family" detergent commercials still feature a harried mom. Look at policy: the FMLA offers unpaid leave, and the U.S. is nearly alone among wealthy nations in providing no paid paternity leave. When California rolled out paid family leave in 2004, take-up among fathers lagged badly, partly because workplaces still signal that a man who prioritizes a child is not serious about his career.

The mommy wars persist because mothers are still presumed to be the default parent, judged on every feeding and sleep-training choice. Fathers, by contrast, are praised for showing up at all — the "babysitting dad" trope. Both framings shortchange men who are genuinely co-parenting.

If we want the national conversation to evolve, we should stop covering fatherhood as a novelty trend piece and start treating caregiving as a gender-neutral adult responsibility — in our journalism, our employers' policies, and our tax code.

In the latest rounds of the mommy wars—from Time’s “Are You Mom Enough?” cover to the debate over Elisabeth Badinter’s “The Conflict”—fathers have been all but invisible. The public agonizes over whether mothers breastfeed long enough, work too much, or sacrifice too little, while the men in the nursery remain spectral. This isn’t because fathers haven’t stepped up. By 2012, involved fatherhood is a lived reality in millions of homes: dads change diapers, coach soccer, and even shake up the P.T.A. The national conversation hasn’t evolved because our cultural infrastructure—language, media, workplace norms, and policy—still stubbornly defines parenting as mothering.

The trouble starts with the script of the “ideal worker,” a full-time, uninterrupted employee who has a spouse at home managing the domestic sphere. Although most mothers are now in the workforce, that script endures. Paternity leave is still rare and often stigmatized; men who request flexible hours are seen as less committed to their careers. When fathers do hands-on care, we describe them as “helping” or “babysitting,” as if they are pinch-hitting for the real parent. The language itself frames fatherhood as auxiliary.

Media and marketing amplify the divide. Parenting magazines and websites overtly target women, and books on fatherhood are treated as a niche. When a father appears in a feature about child-rearing, he is often a novelty—the bumbling but lovable guy who can’t quite master a ponytail. The mommy wars thrive because they pit women against each other over choices the culture still frames as exclusively female, while letting inadequate work-family policies off the hook. It’s easier to argue about attachment parenting than to ask why the United States remains one of the few wealthy nations without paid family leave for mothers or fathers.

A truly evolved conversation would treat co-parents as equal partners with distinct yet overlapping roles. It would ask not just “Are you mom enough?” but “Are we supporting all parents enough?” Until then, the quiet revolution at home will remain out of earshot in the national shouting match.

The national conversation about parenting remains fixated on mothers because our institutional structures and cultural scripts have not caught up to the reality of modern fatherhood. While it is true that fathers today spend significantly more time on child care and household chores than previous generations, the media and the workplace still operate on outdated, gendered assumptions.

Consider the American workplace. Paid paternity leave is vanishingly rare, and taking time off under the unpaid Family and Medical Leave Act remains a financial impossibility for many men. When fathers do attempt to utilize flexible work arrangements to participate in school events or doctor visits, they frequently encounter a "flexibility stigma." Employers implicitly expect men to be ideal workers, unencumbered by domestic duties, while simultaneously expecting women to eventually step back to prioritize family. This structural bias often forces couples into traditional gender roles by default, reinforcing the societal perception that parenting is primarily women’s work.

The media amplifies this structural reality by manufacturing controversies that center exclusively on women. High-profile debates, like Time magazine's recent focus on attachment parenting, frame child-rearing choices as intense maternal referendums. When fathers do appear in these cultural narratives, they are too often relegated to the tropes of incompetent helpers or secondary babysitters, rather than equal, capable co-parents.

To evolve our national conversation, we must move beyond the tired theater of the "mommy wars" and recognize that modern families require structural support for both parents. Until we champion policies like paid family leave for men and demand media portrayals that reflect the engaged reality of today's fathers, the concept of "parenting" will unfairly, and inaccurately, remain synonymous with "mothering."

The question assumes that dads have, in fact, stepped up, and that the lagging conversation is the problem. I'd gently challenge the premise. The "new involved father" is partly real and partly a flattering story we tell ourselves.

Look at the numbers. American fathers in two-earner households do more child care than their own fathers did, yes, but they still do substantially less than mothers in the same households. They are also more likely to do the "fun" caregiving — playing, weekend outings — and less likely to do the relentless, invisible logistics: scheduling doctor's appointments, tracking immunizations, knowing which child has outgrown which shoes, planning birthday parties, remembering that Tuesday is library day. Sociologists call this the "mental load," and surveys consistently find it falls overwhelmingly on mothers, even in couples that pride themselves on equality.

So when commentators ask why "parenting" still means "mothering," one honest answer is: because in most households, it still does. The diaper change is the visible part of an iceberg of cognitive labor, and the iceberg has not shifted nearly as much as the diaper change.

This is not to disparage fathers who are genuinely sharing the load — they exist and they are growing in number. It is to caution against declaring victory prematurely. The danger of the "dads have arrived" narrative is that it lets us stop pushing. It lets policymakers off the hook for paternity leave. It lets husbands feel they have done their share when they have done a third. And it lets the broader culture imagine that the remaining inequalities in parenting are a matter of media catching up, rather than a matter of work yet undone.

The mommy wars are exhausting and often silly, but they are at least an argument about something real. Including dads in the conversation is welcome — provided we include them honestly, asking not "why are they ignored?" but "are they actually doing the work we like to say they are?"

There are two different complaints buried inside the "what about dad?" question, and they pull in opposite directions.

The first is a complaint about cultural representation: that fathers are caricatured as incompetent in commercials, ignored by parenting magazines, and treated as auxiliary at pediatrician visits and school events. This is a real grievance, and the remedy is straightforward — better writing, better marketing, better institutional habits. Address the parent in the room, not the gender you expected.

The second is a complaint about the structure of the parenting discourse itself: the suggestion that "mommy wars" should become "parent wars," that the angst over breastfeeding, working, attachment, and so on should be redistributed across sexes. This is a more complicated wish, and I am not sure it is one we should grant.

The mommy wars are not, fundamentally, a conversation parents are having with each other. They are a conversation that magazines and television manufacture and that women, for historical reasons, are made to feel implicated in whether they want to be or not. Importing dads into this carnival doesn't liberate anyone; it just doubles the market for guilt. The healthier move is to deflate the mommy wars, not to expand them.

What fathers in 2012 actually need is more boring than a Time magazine cover. They need paid leave they can actually use without career penalty. They need custody courts that don't reflexively favor mothers. They need workplaces that grant flex time to men without snickering. They need schools and doctors and coaches who treat them as full parents. They need, in short, the unglamorous infrastructure of equal parenting, not a seat at the table of performative anxiety.

So by all means, ask "what about dad?" — but ask it in the right key. Not "why doesn't the culture worry about dads the way it worries about moms?" but "what would it take for dads to do, and be recognized as doing, an equal share?" The first question leads to think pieces. The second leads to policy. We have enough of the former.

It is easy to look at the cultural sphere—Time magazine covers or mommy blogs—and conclude that society simply forgot about fathers.
