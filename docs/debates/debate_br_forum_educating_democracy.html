Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Deborah Meier has written something rare: an argument against the standards movement that is neither nostalgic nor reactionary, and that takes seriously what schools are actually for. Her diagnosis of a "crisis of human relationships" — of children warehoused in vast institutions, taught by adults stripped of authority, judged by strangers — rings true to anyone who has walked into a 3,000-student high school. And her insistence that democracy requires young people to witness adults making real decisions in the face of disagreement is, to my mind, the strongest argument in the essay. Children cannot learn citizenship from adults who have been reduced to delivery mechanisms for someone else's curriculum.

And yet I find myself unable to follow her all the way. Meier's case rests on a leap from the success of Central Park East and Mission Hill to a general claim about local authority, and that leap deserves more scrutiny than she gives it.

Consider first the history of "local control" in American education. The phrase has a noble lineage in Jefferson and Dewey, but it has an uglier lineage too. It was local control that maintained Jim Crow schooling for generations. It was local control that Prince Edward County, Virginia, invoked to close its public schools rather than integrate. It was local control — embedded in property-tax finance — that produces the spending disparities Jonathan Kozol documented in *Savage Inequalities*. The federal and state interventions Meier mistrusts (Brown, Title I, the Lau decision, IDEA, the Office for Civil Rights) were responses to what local communities, left alone, were doing to children with no political constituency. When Meier writes that "the only local powers we seem to be interested in expanding are those that allow us to re-segregate our schools by race or gender," she names the problem but then asks us to trust the same localism anyway.

Second, the schools Meier holds up as proof of concept are not ordinary schools — they are schools founded by extraordinary educators with self-selected staffs and families who chose them. Central Park East and Mission Hill admit by lottery, but the lottery pool is composed of families who sought them out. This is not a trivial selection effect. The question for policy is not whether a school built by Deborah Meier can outperform a comprehensive high school in the Bronx; it is whether the median principal, in the median district, given the median faculty, can replicate that result. The history of "small schools" initiatives suggests that smallness alone, without the cultural ingredients Meier supplies, accomplishes much less than its champions hope. A policy of "let a thousand Mission Hills bloom" is a wager that those ingredients can be reliably manufactured. I am not sure they can.

Third, Meier underestimates what reasonable parents in low-income communities have actually been asking for. When Chicago and Texas adopted high-stakes accountability, they did so in part because Black and Latino parents were tired of being told their schools were "fine" while their children graduated unable to read. The data Meier cites — that we lead the world in elementary literacy — masks gaps that are themselves catastrophic. The 1996 NAEP showed roughly 40 percent of Black fourth-graders below "basic" in reading. External tests, for all their crudeness, made those gaps visible in a way local judgment had not. Meier is right that a test score is only "a few more right answers on a standardized test." But for a parent who has been told for a decade that her child is doing fine, that information matters.

This is the real tension Meier elides. She wants accountability to remain "in the hands of the local community," but the local community has historically been the entity from which vulnerable children most needed protection. Her answer — that wider publics should "watch" without imposing — is closer to exhortation than to a mechanism. If a school in a poor district is failing its children, and the local community shrugs, what then?

None of this means Meier is wrong about what she is most right about. The Massachusetts test she describes — fifteen to twenty hours long, an inch deep and a mile wide, calibrated to fail 80 percent of fourth-graders — is indefensible on its own terms. The Chicago practice of expelling children into nonexistent "safe schools" is a moral scandal. The pretense that test-score movements of three points measure anything real about learning is a fraud on the public. A serious defender of standards-based reform should concede all of this and ask how to do it better, not whether to do it.

So the argument I would press is this: Meier has correctly identified what standardization cannot do — it cannot create the relationships, the adult presence, the negotiated authority that schools in a democracy require. But she has not shown that abandoning external standards will produce those goods either, except in schools fortunate enough to be founded by people like her. The honest position, I think, is that we need both: tight external floors to protect children whose communities fail them, and wide local latitude above those floors so that schools can become the places Meier describes. The current movement has the first without the second. Her vision has the second without the first. Neither, alone, will educate a democracy.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Deborah Meier’s eloquent warning against the standardization movement deserves a serious hearing. She is right to remind us that schools are not factories, that children need relationships with empowered adults, and that a vibrant democracy thrives on pluralism rather than uniformity. Yet her argument, for all its force, ultimately rests on a misreading of why so many parents, civil rights advocates, and educators have turned to standards in the first place, and on an overly romantic view of local control. The question is not whether we want schools that are humane, intellectually exciting, and accountable to the communities they serve—everyone claims those goals. The question is whether a largely decentralized system, built on the kind of autonomous small schools Meier champions, can deliver equitable and rigorous education to all children, especially those who have historically been shortchanged. I believe the evidence through the 1990s suggests that without some form of common, externally validated standards, we will continue to consign millions of young people to second-class schooling, even as a few model schools flourish.

Meier dismisses the “crisis” that launched the standards movement as a false alarm, noting the roaring economy and America’s high productivity. But the real anxiety in the 1980s and 1990s was not solely economic: it was that too many children, particularly poor and minority children, were leaving school without the basic skills to participate fully in society. National Assessment of Educational Progress data throughout the 1990s revealed stubborn gaps. In 1998, for instance, 17-year-old black and Hispanic students read, on average, at levels typical of 13-year-old white students. In mathematics, the disparities were equally grim. One need not invoke a direct link to economic competitiveness to recognize a moral and civic emergency. A democracy in which large segments of the population cannot read a ballot, parse a newspaper editorial, or understand a mortgage contract is a democracy in name only. Meier is right that the social fabric has frayed, but the academic dimension of that crisis is real and cannot be wished away by redefining the problem purely in terms of relationships.

It is this equity concern that drove many civil rights organizations to embrace standards-based reform. Groups like the Education Trust, founded in 1990, argued that in the absence of clear, common expectations, poor children and children of color were systematically offered watered-down curricula and lower expectations. Local control, they pointed out, had historically been a friend to segregation and neglect. When every school or district defines “well-educated” in its own way, it becomes impossible to know whether a diploma from a wealthy suburb means the same thing as one from an impoverished inner-city or rural school. Meier’s alternative—let a thousand standards bloom, each community setting its own—sounds attractively democratic. But it ignores the brutal asymmetry of power and resources. Affluent communities can afford to set high standards and support them with rich programs; poor communities, often without political clout and with transient leadership, will struggle to do the same. An external floor, defined through a democratic process that includes experts and the public, is not a threat to local creativity but a safeguard against the soft bigotry of low expectations.

Meier’s own schools demonstrate that small, self-governing communities can set demanding standards and hold themselves accountable through performance assessments, portfolios, and external reviewers. She is right that such approaches are richer and more educationally valuable than a single standardized test. But she conflates the instrument with the principle. Yes, some state tests, like the early Massachusetts exams, were poorly designed—mile-wide, inch-deep, and laden with trivia. That is a reason to improve the tests and couple them with local assessments, not to abandon the idea that the larger public has a stake in whether all students meet common benchmarks. Many states in the 1990s, including Kentucky, Vermont, and Maryland, were experimenting with performance-based assessments that went beyond multiple-choice items, and the federal Goals 2000 legislation encouraged such innovation. The standards movement is not monolithic, and it is premature to declare the entire enterprise inherently authoritarian. Used wisely, external standards can empower parents with comparative information, give teachers a shared vocabulary for what students should know and be able to do, and galvanize communities to demand better resources. The problem is not the existence of standards but the high-stakes misuse that Meier rightly decries. The remedy is to design accountability systems that emphasize support and intervention before penalties, and that use multiple measures—not to retreat into a patchwork of idiosyncratic local definitions.

Moreover, Meier’s portrait of the “crisis of human relationships” as the core educational challenge leads her to underestimate the difficulty of scaling up her own successes. Mission Hill and Central Park East are remarkable places, built on decades of exceptional leadership, carefully selected staffs, and families who chose them. Even within the New York and Boston pilot programs, these schools remain small islands in vast systems. The vast majority of the nation’s more than 50 million public school students attend large, bureaucratic institutions serving diverse and often transient populations. It is one thing to assert that all schools should be small and autonomous; it is quite another to show how, in a society as mobile and unequal as ours, such a system would avoid replicating the very inequities it seeks to cure. Without some shared architecture of expectations, children who move from school to school—as poor families frequently do—face bewildering shifts in curriculum and standards. Without some common reference points, states have no way to identify schools that are chronically failing their students and to target resources accordingly. Meier is undoubtedly right that relationships are the medium of powerful learning, but relationships alone do not guarantee that a student masters algebra, learns to write a persuasive essay, or understands the principles of science. The content of education matters, and a democratic society has a legitimate interest in ensuring that all its young citizens are introduced to a broad and ambitious curriculum, not merely to what a particular local community happens to value.

This is not to reject the central democratic insight that Meier offers. She is profoundly right that adults in schools must have the authority to make real decisions, and that young people need to see adults navigating genuine disagreement. Any system that reduces teachers to script-following technicians and principals to compliance officers will sour the very relationships on which learning depends. The challenge is to marry the energy and personal accountability of small-scale communities with the public’s need for some guarantee of quality and equity. There is a middle ground between the “ant colony” and complete local autonomy. It involves states setting broad, clear learning goals—developed with substantial teacher and community input—while leaving schools and districts considerable flexibility in how to reach them. It means using multiple forms of assessment, including locally designed performance tasks, and making the results transparent without attaching a single high-stakes cutoff to each test. It requires, as Meier insists, a much fairer distribution of resources, so that schools serving the most vulnerable students have the materials, well-prepared teachers, and social supports they need. In that sense, she and standards advocates are not as far apart as her essay suggests. The real battle is over balance: how much commonality is necessary to prevent negligence, and how much local freedom is necessary to nurture commitment.

By December 1999, the nation has had nearly two decades of experience with various forms of standards-based reform. The results are uneven: some states have seen encouraging gains, especially for minority students, on NAEP and state tests, while others have stumbled with poorly implemented tests and backlash from parents. But the lesson is not that the fundamental idea is bankrupt. The lesson is that we must do it better—more democratically, more thoughtfully, and with less punitive zeal. Meier’s call for “patient confidence in the good sense of ‘the people’” is inspiring, but it must be paired with a recognition that the good sense of the people has often been absent when the people in question were poor, black, or brown. The history of American education shows that local control can be an instrument of exclusion as readily as of empowerment. A healthy democracy depends both on vibrant local communities and on shared civic commitments that transcend them. Educating a democracy requires standards that are publicly owned, not externally imposed behind a veil of expertise, but it also requires the courage to say that some things—literacy, numeracy, scientific and historical understanding, the capacity to reason—are not optional for any child, anywhere.

Deborah Meier’s eloquent defense of progressive, locally controlled education is both a testament to her extraordinary career and a compelling vision of what schools at their very best can be. Her successes at Central Park East and Mission Hill are legendary precisely because they embody a democratic ideal: small, deeply relational communities where adults and students engage in meaningful, intellectually rich work. However, in using her boutique successes to launch a sweeping attack on the standards-based reform movement, Meier fundamentally misdiagnoses the systemic illnesses that standardized frameworks are attempting to cure. While she fears that standards and assessments will destroy the democratic character of American education, her defense of sweeping local control overlooks a harsh historical reality: decentralization and unchecked local autonomy have frequently served as the very mechanisms of deep educational inequity.

Meier’s argument rests on the assumption that local communities, when left to their own devices and freed from the "dystopia" of central authority, will naturally forge rigorous, democratic, and equitable educational environments. But the history of American public education tells a different story. For generations, local control has been the banner under which systemic inequities have flourished. Before the standards movement began gaining serious political traction in the late 1980s and 1990s, local autonomy effectively meant that schools in affluent, suburban districts offered rigorous, college-preparatory curricula, while inner-city and poor rural schools often relegated their students to watered-down "general" tracks.

When schools are completely insulated from external accountability, the most vulnerable students frequently suffer from the soft bigotry of low expectations. Teachers and local administrators, overwhelmed by the social challenges their students face, can too easily slide into a posture of sympathetic social promotion. A student might graduate high school having felt deeply cared for, yet remain functionally illiterate and entirely unprepared for the rigors of higher education or the workforce. The push for state frameworks—such as the one currently unfolding in Massachusetts—arises not from a desire to turn children into factory drones, but from a moral imperative to guarantee that every child, regardless of zip code, is granted access to a baseline of essential academic skills. Standards are, at their core, a civil rights issue.

Meier dismisses the use of standardized tests as "simple-minded" and advocates instead for portfolio-like assessments, resembling doctoral defenses, to be judged by local communities and external reviewers. Intellectually, this is a beautiful model. Practically, it is highly subjective and exceptionally difficult to scale. How does a state government or a civil rights organization ensure that a portfolio judged as "excellent" by a local committee in a heavily underfunded, segregated district represents the same level of academic mastery as an "excellent" portfolio in an affluent suburb? Without standardized, objective metrics, we have no reliable way to uncover and quantify the very achievement gaps that Meier herself decries. The NAACP and other advocates for minority students have increasingly supported standards and assessments precisely because data forces visibility. You cannot fix a systemic problem that remains hidden behind a veil of localized, subjective grading. Tests reveal the stark disparities that local complacency has historically preferred to hide.

Furthermore, Meier is far too quick to dismiss the economic anxieties that initially sparked the standards movement. She points to the booming economy of this late-1990s moment as proof that our schools are actually doing fine, suggesting the crisis identified in *A Nation at Risk* was a false alarm. But this macro-economic success masks a deeply bifurcated reality. The soaring stock market and high workforce productivity of 1999 are disproportionately driven by the highly educated, fueled by the rapid expansion of the technology sector and the knowledge economy.

Beneath the veneer of this economic boom lies a widening wage gap and the steady decimation of the blue-collar middle class. For a student graduating from a failing high school in Chicago or Boston today without foundational literacy and numeracy, the new economy is profoundly unforgiving. In Dewey’s day, a student who left school with a modest academic foundation could still find a unionized manufacturing job that would sustain a family. Today, that economy is gone. Mastering a common, rigorous baseline of knowledge is no longer about sorting students for a factory line; it is about providing the essential currency required to survive and participate meaningfully in modern civic and economic life.

Meier’s alternative model of schooling is inspiring, but it suffers from a profound scaling problem. Schools like Mission Hill and Central Park East are boutique successes driven by exceptional, charismatic leadership and a highly engaged constituency. Families who enter lotteries to place their children in these schools are already demonstrating a level of proactive engagement that is, sadly, not present in every household. We are tasked with maintaining a national public education system for roughly 50 million children. We cannot build public policy on the mere hope that thousands of visionary leaders will spontaneously emerge in every district to generate rigorous, localized curricula from scratch.

Standardized frameworks provide a necessary safety net. They do not intrinsically prevent schools from being creative or relational; they simply demand that such creativity does not come at the expense of basic academic competency. A state can dictate that a fourth-grader must know how to multiply fractions and understand the causes of the American Revolution without dictating *how* the teacher must impart that knowledge. The state sets the destination; the teachers and the local community can still chart the pedagogical path.

This is not to say that the current implementation of standards-based reform is flawless. Meier is entirely correct to warn against the dangers of excessive high-stakes punishments attached to narrow tests. When a single test score becomes the sole determinant of a child’s graduation or a school’s survival, the curriculum can dangerously narrow, and the joy of learning can be suffocated by endless test-prep. Policymakers must heed her warning that assessments should be broad, fair, and used primarily to direct resources and support where they are most needed, rather than to indiscriminately punish struggling schools or privatize public assets. We must also agree with her powerful assertion that achieving true educational equity requires addressing the profound resource disparities between rich and poor districts. Demanding high standards without providing the funding necessary to reach them is a hollow, cynical exercise.

However, the solution to the misuse of standards is to improve the assessments and to couple high expectations with high support, not to abandon the enterprise of shared standards altogether. Democracy is indeed messy, and Meier is profoundly right that education must foster independent thought, tolerance for ambiguity, and deep civic engagement. But true democratic participation also requires a shared vocabulary, a common historical understanding, and the analytical skills necessary to navigate a complex world.

If we abandon the attempt to define what a well-educated eighteen-year-old should know, we do not empower the marginalized; we abandon them to the luck of the draw. We leave them dependent on the hope that they might land in a school with a visionary leader like Deborah Meier, while knowing full well that thousands of others will languish in schools that demand too little and forgive their own failures too easily. Standards-based reform and local innovation do not have to be mutually exclusive enemies. By setting rigorous, centralized goals for what students must ultimately learn, we can ensure a baseline of democratic equity, while leaving the daily magic of teaching to the educators in the classroom.

Deborah Meier’s warning is indispensable. The movement for standards has too often confused seriousness with severity, accountability with punishment, and equality with sameness. A school system that can double expulsions, consign children to privately run “safe schools,” and then celebrate a small rise in test scores has lost sight of what schools are for. Likewise, a community that treats the presence of minority children as an obstacle to “standards” has revealed that the language of excellence can become a polite cover for retreat from justice. Meier is right to insist that democracy cannot be educated by humiliation, exclusion, or remote control.

But I do not think the conclusion follows that common standards are themselves hostile to democracy. The danger lies not in the public articulation of what children should have a fair chance to learn, but in a particular technocratic and punitive version of standards-based reform. We should reject that version without giving up on the democratic promise of standards: the promise that poor children, immigrant children, disabled children, and children in politically weak communities are entitled to more than whatever their local school happens to offer them.

Local judgment is precious. Yet American educational history gives us little reason to romanticize local control. Local communities have often protected children; they have also segregated them, tracked them, ignored them, underfunded them, and told them—in countless subtle and unsubtle ways—that algebra, literature, science, foreign languages, and serious history were for somebody else’s children. The old system was not a paradise of humane pluralism interrupted by state bureaucrats. It was also a system in which expectations varied dramatically by race, class, and neighborhood, and in which the language of “meeting children where they are” could become a justification for leaving them there.

This is why the equity argument for standards has force. A state that says all children should learn to read demanding texts, write clearly, understand mathematics beyond arithmetic, study history, encounter science, and engage the arts is not necessarily imposing a factory model. It may be making a democratic claim: that access to powerful knowledge should not depend on parental income, zip code, or the luck of getting Deborah Meier as one’s principal. Common standards can make visible the children who have been hidden by averages, promotions, diplomas, and comforting local stories. They can give parents and citizens a language for asking why one school offers laboratories, libraries, and experienced teachers while another offers worksheets and substitutes.

Meier is correct that tests cannot bear the weight now being placed upon them. A single examination should not decide promotion, graduation, teacher worth, or the fate of a school. Testing experts themselves have long cautioned against using any one test for high-stakes decisions about individuals. Scores are influenced by family income, language background, school resources, test familiarity, and plain chance. A child’s work over time, teacher judgment, exhibitions, portfolios, course performance, and outside review can all tell us things a standardized test cannot. The richer forms of assessment developed at Central Park East and in Coalition of Essential Schools are not sentimental indulgences; they are often more demanding than multiple-choice testing.

Still, the limitations of tests do not eliminate the need for external evidence. Parents should not have to rely entirely on a school’s self-description. Communities can be loving and self-deceiving at the same time. Teachers may care deeply and still miss patterns of failure. Schools may develop beautiful rhetoric around “developmentally appropriate” practice while some children never learn to read fluently. Public institutions owe the public some comparable information, especially because the children least likely to be well served are often least able to exit or complain effectively.

The central issue, then, is design. A democratic standards system would separate standards from standardization. It would define a limited number of important goals, not prescribe every book, topic, method, and minute. It would ask whether students can read, reason, compute, investigate, write, speak, and understand central ideas in history, science, and the arts. It would not pretend that every worthy outcome can be measured cheaply in April. It would use sampling, audits, inspections, school visits, public exhibitions, and multiple forms of student work. It would report results in ways that illuminate inequalities rather than merely rank schools. And it would attach consequences first to adults and systems—resources, support, staff development, leadership—not to children through retention and denial of diplomas.

Meier’s deepest point is that schools teach democracy not only through civics lessons but through their daily organization. Children learn what authority is by watching adults exercise it. If teachers are reduced to script readers and test proctors, students receive a lesson in passivity. If parents are summoned only to hear official verdicts, they learn that public institutions are not theirs. If schools are so large that no adult knows a child well, then calls for “accountability” ring hollow. Smallness, teacher responsibility, public conversation, and real relationships are not extras; they are conditions for serious learning.

But common standards need not destroy those conditions. Indeed, they may protect them if they are framed properly. A small school should be free to organize a year around Boston, Egypt, or China; to combine ages; to use exhibitions; to involve parents; to define habits of mind. But it should also be able to show, publicly and persuasively, that its students are learning to read complex material, write with evidence, use mathematics, understand scientific inquiry, and situate their local studies in larger historical and geographic contexts. The state should not dictate the path, but it may legitimately ask for evidence that the path leads somewhere substantial.

The phrase “standards held by real people who matter in the lives of our young” is powerful, but it should be expanded. The real people who matter include teachers and parents, certainly. They also include citizens across the state who have obligations to children they will never meet. Public education is local in its daily life but civic in its justification. Taxpayers in one town help support children in another; universities and employers depend on schools they do not govern; democratic deliberation requires some shared intellectual ground. A purely local definition of education can become too narrow for children whose futures will not remain local.

Nor should we accept too sharp a contrast between experts and citizens. Expertise is not sovereignty, but it is not the enemy of democracy either. Reading researchers, historians, mathematicians, child psychologists, classroom teachers, and assessment specialists know things worth knowing. Democratic decision-making should bring their knowledge into public argument, not banish it as elitist or enthrone it as final. The problem with many current reforms is not that experts speak, but that they speak through mandates insulated from the experience of teachers, students, and families.

A better settlement would have three parts. First, states should establish broad, public standards after genuine debate, with room for revision and dissent. These standards should be fewer, clearer, and deeper than many current frameworks. Second, schools should have substantial autonomy over curriculum, pedagogy, schedule, staffing, and assessment, provided they make their work public and submit to serious external review. Third, accountability should be reciprocal: if children are expected to meet high standards, states must be accountable for providing qualified teachers, safe buildings, libraries, reasonable class sizes, health supports, and time for teachers to plan together. It is morally incoherent to demand equal outcomes while tolerating grossly unequal conditions.

Meier fears that standards reform will produce a new factory school for future “mindworkers.” That fear is justified if standards mean uniform pacing charts, test-preparation curricula, mass retention, and public shaming. But abandoning common expectations would leave too many children in the old factory, where low-level work is quietly renamed “appropriate” and diplomas conceal inequality. The democratic task is not to choose between central authority and local community, but to discipline each by the other.

We need schools with faces, histories, arguments, rituals, and adults who know children well. We also need public guarantees that every child is invited into the common inheritance of literacy, numeracy, science, history, art, and civic argument. Standards worthy of a democracy would not tell Mission Hill to stop being Mission Hill. They would ask every school, including Mission Hill, to show what its students can do—and would ask the rest of us what we must do to make such work possible everywhere.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Deborah Meier's essay raises concerns about standards-based reform that deserve serious engagement, and she is surely right that the rhetoric of crisis surrounding American education has often been overblown. The economy was indeed thriving in 1999, and the productivity data she cites suggest that the apocalyptic predictions of A Nation at Risk never materialized. Yet while I share her skepticism about centralized bureaucratic solutions and her commitment to democratic participation in education, I find her alternative vision incomplete and her critique of standards-based reform overstated in ways that ultimately do a disservice to the students she rightly cares about.

Meier's strongest argument concerns the social and political disengagement she diagnoses: low voter turnout, weakened communities, the disconnection between young people and responsible adults. Her observations about the atomization of American life and its effects on civic culture are compelling, and her emphasis on the importance of schools as communities where relationships matter strikes me as fundamentally correct. The factory model of education she critiques was always inadequate to the task of preparing young people for democratic citizenship, and her alternative of smaller, more personal schools where adults and children know one another genuinely reflects what we know about effective learning environments.

Where I must part ways is with her dismissal of expert knowledge and her romanticization of local control. Meier argues that "experts should be subservient to citizens" and that locally determined standards are inherently more legitimate than those developed by specialists. This sounds appealingly democratic, but it ignores the substantial evidence that without some external framework, local schools often perpetuate existing inequalities rather than challenging them. The Lynnfield example she cites is instructive: a predominantly white, middle-class community chose to end a program that brought minority children into their schools, and they did so in the name of local control and their own definition of educational quality. Meier treats this as evidence of standardization's failure to ensure equity, but it might equally be seen as evidence that local communities cannot always be trusted to protect the interests of all children. The history of American education is littered with local "community control" that meant community segregation, tracking that sorted students by race and class, and curricula that reflected local prejudices rather than broader humanistic values.

This is not to say that external standards automatically produce justice—clearly they do not, as the Chicago example demonstrates. But the answer to bad standardization is better standardization, not no standardization. The problem with the Massachusetts tests Meier describes is not that they exist but that they are poorly designed: too comprehensive, too punitive, too disconnected from what good instruction actually looks like. A more thoughtful approach to standards might specify essential knowledge and skills without reducing education to test preparation, might include multiple forms of assessment rather than high-stakes standardized tests, and might use evidence of student learning to improve instruction rather than to punish schools.

Meier's own alternative model, the Mission Hill School, is genuinely impressive, and I do not doubt that students thrive in such environments. But Mission Hill operates within a public system that provides funding, enforces basic regulations, and does demand some accountability for results. The school is not entirely autonomous; it must meet state requirements, accept external assessment, and justify its practices to oversight bodies. This suggests that even in Meier's preferred model, some framework of external standards and accountability is necessary. The question is not whether such a framework should exist but how it should be designed to support rather than undermine good teaching.

I am also struck by a tension in Meier's argument. She insists that her approach is not utopian, yet the schools she points to as evidence—Mission Hill, Central Park East—are small, labor-intensive enterprises that depend on exceptional leadership and committed teachers. These schools work because dedicated educators have created conditions of trust, reflection, and mutual accountability within them. But how do we scale such conditions across a system that employs millions of teachers and educates tens of millions of students? Meier gestures at "charter schools" and "home schooling" as potential allies, but these mechanisms have their own problems, and they do not add up to a systematic alternative to standardization.

Perhaps most troubling is Meier's treatment of the evidence on educational effectiveness. She dismisses concerns about declining achievement as "at best weak," but this understates the genuine ambiguities in the data. International comparisons show American students performing below their counterparts in many other developed countries, particularly in mathematics and science. The gap between affluent and poor students remains vast. These are not problems that can be solved by community relationships alone, however valuable such relationships are. We need both: schools that are warm, relational, and locally rooted, and also schools that demand rigorous intellectual preparation for all students, including those who would otherwise be left behind.

Meier is right that standardization alone will not improve American education, and she is right that the discourse of crisis has often obscured more than it has illuminated. But the answer is not to abandon standards and retreat to local autonomy. It is to design standards more thoughtfully, to measure what matters, and to hold schools accountable for results while trusting teachers and communities to determine how to achieve those results. Democracy requires both public accountability and local initiative, both expert knowledge and community wisdom. The challenge is to get the balance right, and Meier's essay, for all its valuable insights, does not quite meet that challenge.

Yes, the American economy in 1999 is doing better than the doomsayers of fifteen years ago predicted. Yes, some applications of standards-based reform have been crude, punitive, or politically opportunistic. And yes, small schools with strong cultures often outperform big bureaucratic ones. These are real concessions, and I make them gladly.

Meier writes that "the American economy is soaring, the productivity of our workforce is probably tops in the world, and our system of advanced education is the envy of the world." All true. But the workforce currently driving that productivity was educated in the 1960s and 1970s, in a labor market that absorbed millions of high-school graduates into well-paid manufacturing jobs that no longer exist. The kindergartner entering school today will enter a labor market in which routine cognitive work — bookkeeping, clerical processing, basic technical support — is being automated or offshored at an accelerating rate. The premium on genuine mathematical literacy, scientific reasoning, and clear written expression is going up, not down. The "average is over" economy is coming whether we like it or not.

In that economy, the difference between a young adult who can do algebra and one who cannot is the difference between a career and a series of dead-end jobs. The difference between one who can write a coherent two-page memo and one who cannot is similar. International comparisons — TIMSS, for instance — show American students roughly average in math and science, well behind the East Asian and Northern European countries our graduates will be competing with for the rest of their working lives. Meier brushes this off by pointing to our continuing leadership in technology and invention. But our technology sector is staffed in large part by immigrants and by graduates of a small number of elite schools and universities. It does not represent the average outcome of American K-12 education. It represents the survivors of it.

Meier's response to this concern is essentially: the economy is fine, so stop worrying. I find this unpersuasive. The question is not whether American GDP grew last quarter. The question is whether the typical American eighteen-year-old, in 2010 or 2020, will have the cognitive equipment to earn a middle-class living. On that question, the evidence is genuinely troubling, and the burden of proof has to be on those who think we can continue as we are.

Now, what does standards-based reform actually propose? Stripped of caricature, it proposes four things: (1) public agreement on what students should learn at each grade level; (2) curricula and instruction aligned to those expectations; (3) tests that measure whether students learned it; (4) consequences when they didn't. Meier treats this as a frightening centralization of power. I confess I find it the basic operating logic of any serious enterprise. Hospitals do this. Engineering firms do this. Even Meier's beloved small schools do something like this internally — she describes Mission Hill's graduation portfolios, judged against shared criteria, in terms that any standards advocate would recognize.

A Boston employer hiring a graduate of a Roxbury high school needs to know roughly what that graduate can do, just as a college admissions officer in Amherst needs to know what an applicant from Worcester has learned. When every school sets its own standards, the diploma becomes uninterpretable — and the burden falls on employers and colleges to invent their own screens, which they do, usually in ways that favor children from already-privileged backgrounds.

Meier suggests that test scores, transcripts, and other indicators are already abundant. True. But the standards movement is not adding new tests so much as trying to make existing assessment systems coherent — to tie what is taught to what is tested to what counts for graduation. That coherence is a public good. Its absence is what produces the absurd situation in which a student can earn As in high school courses and still fail a basic skills exam.

I also want to challenge Meier's framing of authority. She writes that "in fundamental questions of education, experts should be subservient to citizens." This sounds democratic, and in one sense it is: voters and their representatives should set the broad goals of public education. But citizens, through their legislators, are precisely who is driving the standards movement. State standards in Massachusetts, Texas, North Carolina, and elsewhere were adopted by elected officials responding to public demand for clearer accountability. Meier wants to treat this as the imposition of an "expert" elite, but the elite she is really objecting to is the elected one. The genuine experts — testing psychometricians, curriculum scholars, university researchers — have been, on the whole, more cautious about high-stakes uses of tests than the politicians have. If Meier wants to argue against democratic majorities in favor of professional educator autonomy, she should say so plainly.

I think she is right that an 80 percent fourth-grade failure rate would be politically and pedagogically disastrous, and that cut scores need to be set thoughtfully. I think she is right that high-stakes consequences for individual children, especially young children, need to be approached with great care. I am wary of holding back third-graders on the basis of a single test, and I would prefer that grade-retention and diploma-denial decisions be based on multiple sources of evidence. The reformers who push for crude, one-shot tests are giving the whole movement a bad name.

But these are arguments for better implementation, not for abandoning the project. The deep insight of standards-based reform is that you cannot improve what you do not measure, and you cannot hold a public institution accountable without telling it, in advance, what it is being held accountable for. That insight is correct. The American economy in 2020 will reward children whose schools took it seriously and punish children whose schools did not. Meier's small schools will continue to flourish for the families lucky enough to find them, and I salute that. But for the 95 percent of American children who attend regular public schools, the question is whether we can build a system that asks more of them and helps them meet it. Standards, tests, and accountability — designed humanely, implemented patiently — are the only serious answer on the table.

The Romance of the Small School

Deborah Meier's "Educating a Democracy" is one of the most eloquent statements of the progressive case against standards-based reform that I have read. It is also, I want to suggest, a deeply nostalgic document — one that imagines a future for American schooling by misremembering its past and overgeneralizing from a small set of unusually successful institutions. I want to take both Meier and her standards-movement opponents to task, because I think this debate, as currently staged, is impoverishing our public conversation about education.

Let me start with the standards reformers, since they make up the side currently winning. Meier is right that the rhetoric of "world-class standards" has run far ahead of any actual evidence. The empirical foundation for A Nation at Risk was always wobbly, as Richard Rothstein and others have shown. The link between school test scores and national economic performance is loose at best, as Meier notes by pointing out that our economy is booming while our TIMSS rankings are middling. And the implementation of standards-based reform in places like Texas, Chicago, and now Massachusetts has been crude, punitive, and frequently incoherent — long lists of skills no real teacher could teach in a year, tests of dubious validity, cut scores set by political feel, and "accountability" that falls almost entirely on the powerless. On all of this, Meier is correct, and the standards advocates owe the public a much more humble account of what they know and don't know.

But Meier's alternative also has problems, and I want to name them. Her argument rests, again and again, on the demonstrated success of a particular kind of school: Central Park East, Mission Hill, the Boston Pilot schools, the small alternative schools that flowered in New York between 1975 and 1995. These are real institutions with real outcomes, and they deserve celebration. But they share a set of features that should make us cautious about generalizing.

First, they are schools of choice. Families who attend them have actively opted in, which means the parent population is, on average, more engaged, more aspirational, and more willing to put up with institutional eccentricities than the average urban parent. This selection effect is large. We know from countless studies — including studies of magnet schools, Catholic schools, and early charter schools — that simply moving children from assigned schools to chosen ones produces meaningful gains in outcomes, holding everything else equal.

Second, they are founder-led. Meier herself launched Central Park East; the Mission Hill model bears her imprint; the Pilot schools were created by teams of educators with strong shared visions. Such schools are not reproducible at scale because charismatic founders are not reproducible at scale. Every successful small-school network in American history has hit a ceiling at exactly the point when it ran out of true believers to staff its expansion.

Third, they are populated by self-selected faculty. Teachers come to Mission Hill because they want to teach at Mission Hill. They accept long hours, intense collaboration, and constant scrutiny because they are bought into the mission. Most American teachers are not in self-selected faculties. They are assigned to schools by districts; they work alongside colleagues they did not choose; and many would actively resist the kind of intensive, mission-driven culture Meier describes.

Fourth, they are small. This is the easiest variable to scale, and to her credit, Meier emphasizes it. But even here, building 90,000 small schools out of America's current stock of medium-to-large ones is not a trivial undertaking, and the evidence on what happens when you simply break a big school into smaller administrative units, without changing anything else, is mixed.

I dwell on these features not to disparage Meier's schools, which I admire, but to point out that the policy question is not "should we have more Mission Hills?" — to which the answer is obviously yes — but "what should we do about the 50 million children who will not attend a Mission Hill?" Meier's essay offers surprisingly little guidance on this. Her implicit answer seems to be: make every school more like Mission Hill. But the conditions that make Mission Hill work — choice, founder vision, self-selected faculty, scale — cannot all be reproduced by policy fiat. Some of them are in tension with public schooling itself.

The standards movement, for all its flaws, is at least an attempt to answer the question of what to do about ordinary schools — the ones with assigned populations, average teachers, average principals, and no charismatic founder. Its bet is that clear public expectations, plus measurement, plus consequences, will move the median school forward. It may be a bad bet, but it is a bet about the actual distribution of American schools, not about the best ones.

A second concern about Meier's argument is its treatment of democracy. She insists that "in fundamental questions of education, experts should be subservient to citizens," and that local communities should make the key decisions about what their children learn. This sounds democratic, but it conceals a sleight of hand. Whose local community? American schools have always been governed locally; that is precisely how we ended up with Jim Crow schooling, with creationism in Kansas, with grossly unequal per-pupil spending across district lines. Local democratic decision-making has produced some of the worst outcomes in American educational history, and "deference to the community" has often meant deference to its most powerful or most fearful members.

Meier knows this. At one point she concedes that "the only local powers we seem to be interested in expanding are those that allow us to re-segregate our schools by race or gender." But this concession is fatal to her argument. If local control produces bad outcomes when uncorrected by external pressure, then external pressure — including the kind embodied in standards, tests, and federal civil-rights enforcement — is part of how democracy actually works for vulnerable minorities. Meier wants the protective external pressure (civil-rights regulations, anti-segregation rules) but not the academic external pressure (standards, tests). The distinction is hard to defend on principle.

A third concern is her diagnosis of the deeper crisis. Meier argues that the real American crisis is one of human relationships — adolescents disconnected from adults, communities frayed, voter turnout low. I think she is right that something has gone wrong with American adolescence, and that schools are part of the picture. But the causal chain from "make schools smaller and more loving" to "fix democracy" is a long one, and Meier asks us to take a great deal of it on faith. Voter turnout in the United States has been declining since the 1960s; large high schools are one of many possible explanations and probably not the largest. Civic disengagement has structural causes — money in politics, the decline of unions and mass-membership organizations, residential segregation, media fragmentation — that no school reform, however humane, will touch.

What would I propose instead? A modest synthesis. Yes to common standards, but written by working teachers and disciplinary experts together, kept short and clear, and revised on a regular cycle. Yes to public assessments, but multiple kinds — including performance tasks and school-quality reviews, not just bubble tests. Yes to accountability, but aimed at adults and institutions, not at individual children, and yes especially to accountability for the inputs we know matter (teacher quality, class size, curricular resources). Yes to small schools of choice — fund them, replicate them, free them from regulations that don't make sense — but do not pretend they are a system-wide answer. And yes to local democratic voice, but always in dialogue with the wider public interest, including the interests of children whose own communities may not be willing or able to give them what they need.

Meier's essay is a useful corrective to standards-movement triumphalism, and I am grateful for it. But the answer to a bad theory of school reform is not a romantic theory of school reform. It is a sober one.

Deborah Meier frames her argument as a defense of democracy. Begin with what Meier says. A democracy, she argues, requires citizens who can think for themselves, who have learned to negotiate disagreement, who have seen adults exercise judgment in the face of uncertainty, and who feel themselves to be agents capable of influencing their world. From this she infers that schools must be plural in their definitions of "well-educated," that authority should be local, and that experts should be "subservient to citizens." A uniform national standard, on her view, undermines the democratic personality by training children in deference to distant authority.

There is something to this. A school that runs on test prep is unlikely to graduate independent thinkers. A teacher who has been reduced to a delivery mechanism for a state framework will not model intellectual seriousness. Meier is right that democratic dispositions cannot be taught by lecture; they have to be practiced, and they have to be witnessed in the practice of the adults around the child. So far, so good.

But Meier slides from these observations to a much stronger claim: that pluralism about what counts as a good education is itself democratic, and that any common standard is a threat to democratic life. This is where I get off the train. The democratic tradition has, in fact, always insisted on something like a common education — not a uniform education, but a shared one. Jefferson, whom Meier quotes selectively, was an advocate of common public schooling for exactly the reason that self-government requires a citizenry that can read the same texts, follow the same arguments, and participate in the same public conversation. Horace Mann's common school movement, which built the institutional foundation of American public education, was animated by the same idea. So was W. E. B. Du Bois's argument for academic, not merely vocational, education for black children. The tradition Meier invokes is in fact much more friendly to shared standards than her essay acknowledges.

Why? Because democracy is not just a matter of personal disposition. It is also a matter of shared cognitive resources. Citizens have to be able to talk to one another about public questions, which requires that they share enough knowledge — historical, scientific, mathematical, literary — to make the conversation possible. A democracy in which children in different schools learn different histories, encounter different canonical texts, develop different vocabularies for thinking about the natural world, is not more democratic than one with common content; it is more fragmented. E. D. Hirsch has been making this argument for over a decade, and while I think he overreaches in particulars, the central insight is sound. The democratic case for common content is at least as strong as Meier's case against it.

Meier's response would presumably be that common content can emerge from local dialogue, and that families and teachers, working together, can decide what their children should know. But this answer founders on scale. A democracy of 270 million people cannot operate on the cognitive resources of 90,000 separately negotiated curricula. At some point, we need to be able to assume that a high-school graduate has read the Declaration of Independence, knows the basic outline of the Civil War, can read a graph, can solve a linear equation, can identify the major branches of government. Without these common reference points, public deliberation becomes harder, not easier. The fragmentation of common knowledge is one of the things that is wrong with American democracy in 1999, not one of its hopeful features.

There is a second problem with Meier's democratic argument, which is her treatment of expertise. She writes that "experts should be subservient to citizens." This formulation will appeal to anyone who has been bullied by a credentialed bureaucrat. But it conflates two different things: the question of who sets the goals of public education (citizens, through democratic process — agreed) and the question of who decides whether those goals are being met (which often requires technical expertise that ordinary citizens, including ordinary parents and teachers, do not have). A democracy that wants effective public institutions cannot dispense with expertise; it can only insist that expertise be accountable to democratic purposes. Meier's framing — experts versus citizens — obscures this.

Her stronger formulation is that children need to see "the adults in their lives" exercising judgment. Yes. But the adults in our lives include not only family members and neighborhood teachers but also the wider community of fellow citizens, present and past, whose accumulated knowledge constitutes the curriculum. A child who has read Frederick Douglass has been mentored by Frederick Douglass; a child who has worked through a geometry proof has been mentored by Euclid. The "experts" Meier wants to demote include the dead, who have a vote in this democracy too. Curriculum is not a usurpation of local judgment by distant power; it is the way the past speaks to the present.

A third point. Meier's diagnosis of the real American crisis — the disconnection of young people from responsible adults — is partly persuasive. But I would add to it a diagnosis she resists: the disconnection of young Americans from a shared cultural inheritance. A child who graduates from high school knowing little of American history, less of world history, almost no literature outside her own contemporary moment, and unable to engage with serious public arguments is not a free citizen. She is a vulnerable consumer of whatever passes by on her screen. The standards movement, in its better forms, is partly an attempt to insist that public schools transmit the cultural and intellectual inheritance that democratic citizenship requires. To frame this as anti-democratic is to misunderstand what democracy needs.

None of this is a defense of the punitive, test-driven, narrow version of standards reform Meier rightly criticizes. The Chicago expulsion stories are appalling. The 80-percent fail-rate Massachusetts test is a scandal. The reduction of teachers to script-readers is a betrayal of the profession. On these points Meier and I are allies.

But the answer to bad standards is good standards, not pluralism for its own sake. A democratic curriculum is one that is rich, demanding, shared, and open to argument — not one that varies from school to school depending on local taste. The point of a democratic education is not to confirm children in the views of their parents and neighbors; it is to give them access to the wider human conversation so that they can ultimately judge for themselves. That access is hard to provide without some shared content, some common assessments, and some willingness on the part of adults to say: these are the things we believe an educated person should know.

Meier ends her essay with an appeal for "a little more commitment to democracy." I share the appeal. But the democracy I want my children to inherit is one in which they can argue with their fellow citizens because they have learned the same difficult things — not one in which every school is its own republic. Meier's vision is humane, and her schools are admirable. But her democratic theory is, I think, too thin to bear the weight she puts on it. A democracy needs both: local communities that know and love their children, and a wider commons of shared knowledge that makes citizenship possible. The standards debate, at its best, is about how to honor both. Meier honors only the first.
