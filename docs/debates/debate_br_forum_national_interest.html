Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Stephen Walt has produced the most coherent case yet for what a post-Iraq American grand strategy ought to look like, and much of his diagnosis is hard to dispute. The Bush administration has confused primacy with omnipotence, treated allies as ratifiers rather than partners, and translated a sound concern about catastrophic terrorism into a doctrine of preventive war that, as the missing Iraqi stockpiles demonstrate, cannot survive contact with imperfect intelligence. Walt is also right that the basic structural fact of our position—oceans, friendly neighbors, GDP roughly thirty percent of the planet's—gives Washington a luxury that few great powers in history have enjoyed: we can afford to be patient. But his preferred alternative, offshore balancing, is presented with a confidence that the strategy's own historical record does not quite warrant, and several of his specific recommendations rest on assumptions that deserve interrogation.

Start with the Persian Gulf, which Walt identifies as one of three regions of vital interest while simultaneously urging that we withdraw to a balancing posture there. The trouble is that the local materials for a balance are thin. Iraq is shattered, Saudi Arabia is a brittle monarchy whose conventional forces have never been impressive, and the remaining Gulf states are micro-states. The natural regional hegemon, were the United States to step back, is Iran—a state Walt elsewhere identifies as a proliferation concern. Offshore balancing worked in 1945–1990 partly because Britain stayed east of Suez until 1971 and because Iraq and Iran roughly cancelled each other out. Neither condition obtains today. We learned in 1990 that "over the horizon" can mean six months of frantic deployment while a fait accompli congeals on the ground. Walt should say more about whether his strategy can actually deter a future Saddam, or whether it is really a strategy for accepting outcomes we would once have reversed.

The reassurance problem is similarly underdeveloped. Walt argues that allies should "bend over backward" to keep us, not the reverse. There is something to this: free-riding is endemic to alliance politics, and Europeans in particular have grown comfortable consuming American security as a public good. But the same logic that gets a Saudi base agreement can get a Japanese nuclear weapon. Tokyo, Seoul, Riyadh, and Ankara have all entertained nuclear options in the past and were dissuaded largely by ironclad American commitments. Play hard-to-get with such partners and you may discover that the cost of primacy includes a more proliferated world—the very outcome Walt rightly identifies as our most urgent danger.

That tension points to a deeper one in the essay. Walt is reassuringly sober about nuclear terrorism, and his proposal for a grand bargain—deeper U.S. cuts and security guarantees in exchange for a tougher nonproliferation regime—is worth taking seriously. Yet he also argues, against the conventional wisdom, that "rogue" states would never hand a weapon to terrorists because they could not control the consequences. This is too neat. It assumes regimes are unitary rational actors with tight command over their arsenals. The A.Q. Khan network—a state-sanctioned proliferation enterprise that operated for years under the noses of Pakistan's leadership—suggests that leakage is not a hypothetical. The same Pakistani state that Walt would presumably treat as a partner in counterterrorism contains scientists who knowingly transferred centrifuge designs to Libya, Iran, and North Korea. Deterrence may suffice against deliberate state transfer; it does considerably less against opportunism, theft, or the chaos that follows regime collapse.

On Israel and the Palestinians, Walt's policy prescription—pressure both sides toward a Clinton-parameters settlement, end unconditional support if Israel refuses—is defensible, but his causal claim is overstated. He treats the conflict as the linchpin of Arab and Muslim hostility to the United States. Bin Laden's own writings, including the 1996 and 1998 declarations, foreground the American military presence on the Arabian peninsula, sanctions against Iraq, and apostate regimes from Riyadh to Cairo. Palestine appears, but it is one item in a list, and it became more prominent in his rhetoric after 2000 as a recruiting device. A just settlement would be desirable on its own terms and would deprive jihadists of a useful talking point. It would not dissolve a movement whose grievances are deeper and more theological than Walt's analysis allows.

Where Walt is most persuasive is on tone, posture, and the discipline of strategy itself. The "axis of evil" formulation was diplomatic malpractice; lumping adversaries together is the surest way to drive them together. The contrast he draws between the Libyan negotiation and the Iraq war is devastating and instructive. So is his insistence that public diplomacy needs a defensible product, not just better salesmanship. And his closing observation—that the United States has starved the institutional capacities (languages, area expertise, intelligence, foreign service) that a serious great power needs—is the kind of unfashionable truth that ought to be repeated until someone in Washington listens.

Offshore balancing, in short, is the right corrective to the current administration's overreach, but it is not quite the finished doctrine Walt suggests. It needs harder thinking about who balances in the Gulf, how to reassure allies without indulging them, and how to handle proliferation pathways that deterrence alone cannot close.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Stephen Walt’s call for offshore balancing is elegantly argued and a welcome corrective to the excesses of the past four years. In particular, his demolition of preventive war as both unworkable and damaging to America’s global standing is devastating and timely. Yet for all its surface appeal, the strategy he advocates ultimately demands a leap of faith that the United States, in early 2005, cannot responsibly take. The central question is not whether American power should be used with restraint — that is unobjectionable — but whether a grand strategy of deliberate retrenchment, of “playing hard-to-get,” would in fact preserve American primacy or inadvertently accelerate the very balancing it seeks to avoid. Walt’s analysis underestimates the stabilizing role that forward-deployed forces and deep alliance commitments have played, and it overestimates the ability of regional powers to manage their own security without triggering precisely the kind of arms races and aggression that would eventually drag the United States back in under far worse conditions.

Walt draws a sharp contrast between the global hubris of the Bush administration and the more restrained selective engagement of the first Bush and Clinton years. He rightly praises the defensive posture and multilateral habits of that earlier era, yet he then concludes that selective engagement was “not selective enough.” This criticism, however, conflates execution with design. The mistakes Walt identifies — overextension in the Balkans, dual containment fueling al-Qaida, blind backing of Israel — were not inherent to the strategy itself. They were choices that a wiser administration could have avoided within the same framework. The lesson of the 1990s is not that forward presence is futile; it is that the United States must be more discriminating about when and how it uses its leverage, not that it should withdraw that leverage entirely.

Walt’s primary case for offshore balancing rests on the claim that America’s military footprint generates resentment and drives adversaries together. There is truth here, but the causal chain is more complex than he allows. The U.S. naval and air presence in East Asia, for example, has not provoked balancing coalitions against Washington. On the contrary, it has reassured nervous neighbors — Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore — that they do not need to develop independent power-projection capabilities or, worse, nuclear weapons to counter a rising China. Walt acknowledges this by endorsing a “significant” continued presence in Asia, but his logic for doing so — to lay the foundation for containing China if necessary — is exactly the kind of open-ended commitment offshore balancing is supposed to avoid. If the United States remains in Asia to manage great-power competition, why pull out of Europe, where a resurgent Russia or a fragmenting Balkans might one day require the same reassurance? The inconsistency suggests that regional stability depends less on the region’s intrinsic characteristics and more on the tangible, day-to-day signals that only stationed forces and integrated commands can send.

Moreover, Walt’s faith in regional actors to balance against threats on their own presumes a world of rational, status-quo powers. History tells a different story. In the Persian Gulf, a true offshore balancing posture would mean leaving Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and the smaller Gulf states to check a revisionist Iran or a recovering Iraq largely by themselves. Yet these states have consistently preferred to free-ride on American protection, and their own military capabilities are uneven. The likely outcome is not a stable local balance but a regional arms race, with states pursuing their own weapons of mass destruction precisely because the ultimate guarantor has stepped back. Walt’s own logic on nuclear incentives — that the “axis of evil” label drove North Korea and Iran to seek a deterrent — applies equally here: if the United States suddenly signals it will intervene only after overt aggression, vulnerable states will redouble their efforts to acquire the means to deter attack on their own. The result could be a more nuclearized Middle East, not a tranquil one.

This connects to Walt’s proposed “grand bargain” on nonproliferation. He is right that America’s nuclear posture is hypocritical and that reducing the salience of nuclear weapons would be wise. But he is too sanguine about the willingness of North Korea and Iran to trade away their programs for nonaggression pledges and a somewhat smaller U.S. arsenal. Pyongyang has already violated the 1994 Agreed Framework and now boasts of nuclear weapons; Tehran continues to pursue the full fuel cycle under cover of the NPT. Their motivations are not solely a reaction to American threats but also a quest for regional influence and regime survival. Walt’s bargain assumes that security guarantees, even if credible, would be sufficient. Yet the United States guaranteed the security of Ukraine in 1994 in exchange for nuclear disarmament, and that promise looks fragile today. Rogue regimes are unlikely to bet their survival on the word of a superpower that can change presidents every four years, especially after watching Libya’s quick turnabout and Saddam’s fate. Walt’s strategy would thus leave the United States with fewer forward-deployed forces to detect and prevent smuggling, and with no on-the-ground leverage to enforce compliance. The record of the past decade suggests that nonproliferatio n works best when it combines credible threats, inspections, and a visible military commitment to the security of threatened states — precisely the posture of selective engagement.

The most emotionally charged section of Walt’s essay concerns Israel-Palestine, and here his diagnosis is partly correct: the conflict inflames anti-Americanism and fuels terrorism. But his remedy — conditioning aid on an Israeli withdrawal to the 1967 lines and imposing a settlement if necessary — misreads the realities of both Israeli politics and Palestinian governance. The Clinton parameters were indeed accepted with reservations by both sides, but those reservations proved fatal, and the subsequent intifada demonstrated that some Palestinian factions would not accept a two-state solution if it meant renouncing the right of return. Until the Palestinian Authority can credibly control violence and until a stable Israeli majority can be built for painful territorial concessions, an American ultimatum would likely cause the Israeli government to rally nationalists, fracture the peace camp, and plunge the region into even deeper chaos — all while doing nothing to weaken the appeal of bin Laden’s narrative. Offshore balancing cannot wish away the fact that America’s entanglement with Israel is not merely a matter of lobby influence; it reflects deep-seated cultural ties and a strategic commitment that, for better or worse, successive administrations have deemed in the national interest. A heavy-handed rupture would undercut, not enhance, America’s reputation for reliability and would likely spill over into even greater instability in which the United States would find itself drawn back in, but this time without the trust of either party.

Walt is at his most persuasive when he calls for maturity, for a foreign policy less captive to domestic grandstanding and more attentive to how America’s power is perceived. The nation would indeed benefit from a sustained investment in diplomacy, language training, and the patient cultivation of global legitimacy. But the offshore balancing he champions risks looking to the world less like confident restraint and more like petulant withdrawal. The measured, alliance-based leadership that he himself lauds in the Bush 41 and Clinton years remains the most plausible path: it keeps America present enough to shape events before they become crises, reassures allies so they do not arm recklessly, and provides the leverage to nudge friends and foes alike toward outcomes that serve American interests. Selective engagement, correctly applied, is not a halfway house to offshore balancing; it is the only grand strategy that honestly confronts the fact that the world’s only superpower cannot be an ordinary balancer. It must lead, however cautiously, or eventually learn the costs of leading from behind.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Stephen M. Walt accurately diagnoses the central diplomatic and strategic missteps of the Bush administration. He is entirely correct that the doctrine of preventive war, the rush into the Iraq quagmire, and the dismissive attitude toward traditional allies have severely damaged America’s global standing and squandered the unprecedented international solidarity that followed the September 11 attacks. However, while his critique of “global hegemony” is trenchant, his proposed alternative—“offshore balancing”—relies on an outdated, state-centric paradigm that fundamentally misreads the security environment of the 21st century.

Walt advocates withdrawing from forward deployments, reducing our military footprint, and relying on regional actors to maintain the balance of power. While this strategy successfully contained Soviet expansionism in Europe and Asia during the Cold War, it is ill-suited to the primary threats we face today: transnational terrorism and the proliferation of weapons of mass destruction by rogue regimes.

The core assumption of offshore balancing is that regional powers will check one another if the United States plays “hard-to-get.” But in the Middle East, leaving the region to its own devices does not create a stable equilibrium; it creates a vacuum. We do not have to guess what happens when the United States relies on “local allies” and “offshore” posture in weak or failed states—we saw it in Afghanistan before 2001. Transnational terrorist networks like al Qaeda are not states. They are not constrained by traditional balance-of-power dynamics, and they thrive precisely where regional state authority is absent or contested.

Furthermore, Walt’s historical memory of offshore balancing in the Middle East is selectively rosy. He claims the United States pursued this policy “successfully from 1945 to 1990.” In reality, relying on regional balancers meant propping up the Shah of Iran, whose collapse led to a hostile theocracy, and later implicitly backing Saddam Hussein’s Iraq to balance the Ayatollahs, which directly set the stage for the 1991 Gulf War. The idea that we can retreat over the horizon and expect local actors to peacefully manage the world's most volatile region ignores decades of history.

This misdiagnosis of adversary motivations extends to Walt’s proposed “grand bargain” with Iran and North Korea. He suggests that these regimes seek nuclear weapons primarily as a defensive deterrent against Bush’s “axis of evil” rhetoric and the threat of American preventive war. Consequently, he argues that if the United States were to abandon its own nuclear modernization and offer nonaggression pacts, these states would verifiably disarm.

This is a dangerous misreading of history. Both Pyongyang and Tehran were actively pursuing covert nuclear weapons programs long before George W. Bush took office, during the very years of “selective engagement” under the Clinton administration. These regimes seek nuclear weapons not merely to deter American primacy, but to ensure domestic regime survival, project regional coercion, and achieve international prestige. Offering to unilaterally degrade America’s nuclear arsenal will not alter the fundamental strategic calculus of Kim Jong Il or the Iranian mullahs. Rewarding their covert proliferation with a “grand bargain” would only signal to other prospective proliferators that nuclear blackmail is a viable geopolitical strategy.

Similarly, Walt oversimplifies the roots of Islamic terrorism by placing excessive blame on the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and America’s relationship with Israel. While achieving a just and lasting peace between Israelis and Palestinians is a vital U.S. interest, Walt overstates its centrality to transnational jihadism. Osama bin Laden’s 1998 fatwa declaring war on Americans was primarily driven by the presence of U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia and the sanctions regime against Iraq; the Palestinian cause was a secondary, rhetorical tool used to broaden his appeal.

Moreover, Walt’s suggestion that the United States should threaten to sever its alliance with Israel if it does not accede to a U.S.-imposed withdrawal ignores the bloody realities on the ground. Following the collapse of the 2000 Camp David summit and the brutal violence of the Second Intifada, Israeli security concerns cannot simply be waved away by American dictates. Pressuring a democratic ally with the threat of abandonment, while simultaneously offering diplomatic carrots to rogue dictatorships like Iran and North Korea, sends a disastrous message about the reliability of American security guarantees.

America’s unipolar moment does require a profound recalibration. We must abandon the hubris of armed democratization, use military force with far greater forbearance, and aggressively rebuild our public diplomacy and alliances. But retreating to an offshore posture and outsourcing global security to regional actors is a recipe for instability in an era where threats cross oceans with ease. The United States must remain deeply, physically, and diplomatically engaged in the world, not as an empire, but as the indispensable anchor of the international system. Restraint is a virtue, but withdrawal is not a strategy.

Stephen Walt is surely right about the first principle: American power is a precious national asset, not a toy to be displayed or a solvent that can dissolve every political problem. The United States is strong enough to make grave mistakes and survive them, but not strong enough to make them cost-free. Iraq has already taught that a brilliant campaign can be followed by a grinding occupation, and that legitimacy, intelligence, language, and local politics are not luxuries appended to military power; they are part of power itself. Walt is also right that preventive war is a dangerous doctrine when elevated from an exceptional possibility into a general rule. A superpower that announces a right to strike when and where it chooses should not be surprised when others seek deterrents or when allies become anxious.

But Walt’s proposed cure—offshore balancing—risks learning too much from Iraq and too little from the broader history of American success. The problem with the Bush administration’s first term was not that America remained too engaged in the world. It was that engagement was too often impatient, unilateral, and inattentive to the political foundations of consent. The answer is not to make America “hard to get,” but to make American leadership more disciplined, more legitimate, and more reliable.

Offshore balancing sounds prudent because it promises selectivity. In practice, however, the line between prudent restraint and dangerous absence is hard to draw before the crisis arrives. Walt says the United States should rely on local actors to preserve regional balances, intervening only when they fail. But local actors often fail precisely because they doubt American staying power or because they fear abandonment more than domination. In Asia, for example, the American presence does not merely prepare for a possible future balance against China; it also dampens the security competition among Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China. A reduced or ambiguous American commitment could encourage Japan to remilitarize faster, South Korea to hedge, Taiwan to take risks, or China to test limits. The American role is not simply a tripwire against hegemony; it is part of the political architecture that prevents miscalculation.

The same is true, though differently, in Europe. Walt is correct that Europe no longer faces the Red Army, and the United States should expect wealthy democratic allies to bear more of the burden. But NATO has not been merely a military garrison. It has been the principal mechanism by which Germany’s power was embedded, Europe’s security dilemmas were softened, and new democracies in Central and Eastern Europe were anchored to the West. A drastic American drawdown might not produce immediate catastrophe, but it would weaken an institution that has magnified American influence at modest cost. The United States should not keep troops abroad out of habit, but neither should it treat forward presence as a mere imperial footprint. Sometimes presence is reassurance, and reassurance is cheaper than rescue.

Walt’s strongest case concerns the Middle East. The United States should not aspire to run the region, and the occupation of Iraq shows how quickly liberation can become entanglement. Yet even here, offshore balancing is too tidy. For decades Washington did rely on regional pillars—Iran under the Shah, then Iraq against revolutionary Iran, then conservative Arab monarchies—and the results were mixed at best. Balancing from offshore did not spare us from association with autocracy, oil politics, radical resentment, or war. Nor is it clear that a lighter American footprint by itself would drain anti-Americanism. Bin Laden’s indictment of the United States includes troops in Saudi Arabia, but also sanctions on Iraq, support for Israel, support for Arab regimes, and the cultural and political influence of the West. Some grievances can be reduced by wiser policy; others will persist because they are rooted in ideological hostility to pluralism, secular law, and American society itself.

On Israel and Palestine, Walt is right that unconditional American support for settlement expansion is bad for America and bad for Israel. No serious friend of Israel should pretend that permanent occupation can be reconciled with democracy, Jewish self-determination, and Palestinian political rights. The Clinton parameters remain the best available outline: two states, security guarantees, territorial compromise, a divided or shared Jerusalem, and a realistic solution to refugees. Washington should be prepared to press both sides much harder. But Walt understates Palestinian agency and the political difficulty created by terrorism. The second intifada, suicide bombings, and the collapse of trust after Camp David and Taba have profoundly shaped Israeli opinion. Pressure on Israel must be matched by unmistakable demands that Palestinian leaders dismantle terrorist organizations, end incitement, and build accountable institutions. With Arafat gone and a new Palestinian leadership emerging, there may be an opening. It will be lost if America appears simply to switch clients rather than insist on reciprocal obligations.

Walt’s nuclear argument is also persuasive in part. The United States cannot credibly preach nonproliferation while implying that nuclear weapons are the ultimate badge of security and status. Reducing excessive arsenals, securing Russian materials, strengthening inspections, and shutting down illicit networks should be central tasks. But nonaggression pledges to regimes such as North Korea and Iran are not magic keys. These governments seek nuclear capabilities not only because they fear America, but because nuclear weapons enhance regime prestige, regional leverage, and domestic legitimacy. A grand bargain is worth exploring, especially through multilateral talks, but it must be backed by verification, sanctions for cheating, and a willingness to contain if diplomacy fails.

The deepest issue is democracy promotion. Walt rightly rejects democracy by invasion. Still, the United States should not retreat to example alone. The democratic revolutions in Eastern Europe, the enlargement of NATO and the European Union, support for civil society, election monitoring, broadcasting, and pressure on dictators have all mattered. Even in the Middle East, the desire for accountable government is not an American invention. The Iraqi election just held, despite violence and Sunni alienation, reminds us that many people will risk death to vote. We should be humble about what that means, but not cynical.

The better grand strategy is reformed selective engagement: preserve alliances, reduce unnecessary military exposure, renounce preventive war as doctrine, repair legitimacy, invest in diplomacy and intelligence, and use American power with patience. Primacy will endure not because America is offshore, but because others believe its presence is preferable to the alternatives. That requires restraint, yes—but also steadiness.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Walt's essay represents a compelling synthesis of realist principles with policy prescriptions that are both intellectually coherent and practically challenging. His central argument—that offshore balancing best serves American interests by husbanding power while minimizing resistance—rests on a sophisticated understanding of how primacy generates its own constraints. Yet several elements of his analysis warrant further scrutiny, particularly his treatment of the diffuse threats that characterize the post-Cold War security environment.

Walt correctly identifies the fundamental tension between American hegemony and the cooperation it requires. The Bush administration's unilateralism, as he demonstrates, has undermined precisely the alliances and institutions that amplify American power. The invasion of Iraq stands as testament to the dangers of preventive war: a campaign launched on contested intelligence, pursued without meaningful Security Council authorization, and now mired in nation-building precisely where such efforts were most likely to fail. Walt's critique of the 2002 National Security Strategy's conflation of preemption with preventive war deserves particular attention, as this semantic and strategic confusion has done genuine damage to America's standing.

His analysis of the "axis of evil" rhetoric is similarly astute. Categorizing Iraq, Iran, and North Korea together obscured critical differences among regimes with distinct motivations and vulnerabilities, while giving these states additional incentive to cooperate rather than compete. The Libyan case demonstrates that differentiated approaches can yield results; Moammar Gaddafi's abandonment of his nuclear program resulted from sustained engagement tailored to Libyan specificities, not from the blunt instruments of regime change or economic isolation alone.

Where Walt's argument becomes more problematic is in his treatment of what might be called the commitment problem inherent in offshore balancing. By design, this strategy reduces American presence in regions where vital interests are not directly threatened. Yet the history of the 1990s demonstrates the risks of such restraint. American inaction during the Rwandan genocide and the Bosnian massacres did not merely represent moral failures; they weakened the credibility of American commitments more broadly and created vacuums that festered into later crises. Walt acknowledges that humanitarian intervention remains permissible under his framework, but the standard he proposes—confidence that horrors can be prevented at acceptable cost—risks becoming a perpetual excuse for inaction.

The Israeli-Palestinian question presents perhaps the greatest tension in Walt's analysis. His recommendations regarding unconditional pressure on Israel to withdraw to 1967 borders and his threat to end economic and military support if Israel refuses are strategically sensible but politically unfeasible within the American domestic context he himself describes. The coalition that has historically supported unconditional Israeli backing extends far beyond any single interest group; challenging it would require a transformation of American political culture that offshore balancing alone cannot accomplish. Walt recognizes that effective public diplomacy requires a credible product, but he underestimates how deeply domestic political constraints limit the product America can offer.

His emphasis on nuclear terrorism as the paramount threat is well-founded, and his proposed grand bargain regarding nuclear arsenals merits serious consideration. Yet the assumption that American nuclear retrenchment would persuade states like Iran or North Korea to abandon programs they view as essential to their survival seems optimistic. These states have pursued nuclear capabilities not merely because of American nuclear policies but because they perceive existential threats that nuclear weapons alone can offset. The 2003 invasion of Iraq demonstrated that non-nuclear states face potential American military intervention; this lesson reinforces rather than undermines the logic of nuclear proliferation from the perspective of regimes like Tehran's.

Walt is right that preventive war has proven unworkable and that American power operates most effectively when perceived as legitimate. His recommendations for military restraint, institutional cooperation, and balanced Middle East diplomacy reflect sound strategic instincts. Yet offshore balancing requires a level of strategic patience and domestic political discipline that American democracy has historically struggled to sustain. The challenge is not merely identifying the correct grand strategy but maintaining the political consensus necessary to implement it.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Stephen Walt's essay is elegant, learned, and wrong in most of the ways that matter. He has produced what amounts to a counsel of retreat dressed in the language of prudence, and we should be candid about what his program would mean: a United States that watches from the horizon while tyrannies consolidate, proliferators advance, and our friends are picked off one by one. He calls this "offshore balancing." History will likely call it something less flattering.

Begin with the central premise. Walt argues that American primacy provokes resistance, and that the way to soften resistance is to husband our power, defer to local balancers, and abandon the project of democratic transformation. There is a kernel of truth here: nobody enjoys being lectured by a hegemon, and clumsy diplomacy costs us. But Walt mistakes a problem of execution for a problem of strategy. The Bush Doctrine has not failed because it sought to enlarge the democratic peace; it has had a hard time because liberating and reconstructing Iraq is genuinely difficult, because we underestimated the post-war task, and because the enemies of decent governance fight back. The remedy is to fight more skillfully, not to retreat to the offshore.

Consider the actual record Walt elides. Afghan women voted last fall — voted — in numbers nobody predicted. Iraqis went to the polls a week ago under threat of murder, holding up purple fingers in defiance of the Baathist and jihadist holdouts who think they can reclaim that country. Walt presents offshore balancing as "America's traditional grand strategy." This claim deserves scrutiny. The history is more complicated than the term implies.

For the first century and a half of the republic, American grand strategy was something we might more accurately call continental expansionism plus hemispheric primacy. We were not balancing Eurasian powers from offshore; we were ignoring them while we conquered a continent, then enforcing the Monroe Doctrine to keep them out of our neighborhood. The classical offshore-balancer role — the one Walt has in mind — was Britain's, not ours. We did not act in the European balance of power in any sustained way until 1917, and even then reluctantly and incompletely.

When we did intervene — in 1917, in 1941 — the pattern was not the deft, calibrated balancing Walt describes. It was waiting too long, intervening massively, defeating the hegemonic challenger, and then withdrawing. The 1919 withdrawal was rapid and largely indifferent to the consequences in Europe. The result of that withdrawal — combined with our retreat from the League of Nations, our protectionism, our isolationism in the 1930s — is part of what produced the Second World War. "Offshore balancing" in its inter-war American form did not preserve a workable balance of power in Eurasia. It produced two existential threats that we then had to defeat at enormous cost.

After 1945, American leaders consciously chose not to repeat this pattern. They built forward-deployed alliances, garrisoned Germany and Japan and Korea, kept fleets in the Mediterranean and the Western Pacific, and committed themselves to defending allies in advance rather than waiting until aggression had succeeded. This was not offshore balancing. This was, in the lexicon Walt himself uses, selective engagement — and it produced, over forty years, the most successful peacetime outcome in great-power politics in modern history. The Cold War ended without a great-power war. The Soviet bloc collapsed without a shot fired in anger between the major powers. Western Europe, East Asia, and eventually Eastern Europe converted to liberal democracy and market economies. This is the world we now inherit. It was not built by offshore balancing.

So when Walt urges us to return to offshore balancing, he is not really urging a return to tradition. He is urging us to abandon the strategic posture that worked spectacularly well from 1947 to 1991 and to adopt instead something closer to the strategic posture that failed spectacularly between the World Wars. The case for this would have to be that the conditions which made forward engagement necessary in 1947 have changed sufficiently that we can now return to the inter-war model without inviting inter-war consequences. Walt asserts this; he does not demonstrate it.

Consider the conditions. We no longer face a Soviet adversary, true. But we face several emerging challenges that the inter-war American republic did not face. A nuclear North Korea. A nuclearizing Iran. A rising China. A nuclear-armed Pakistan whose government may not survive its own internal contradictions. Transnational terrorist networks willing and possibly able to acquire weapons of mass destruction. The vulnerability of our economy to disruptions of global trade, oil supply, and the financial system. The inter-war republic could afford to retreat across the oceans because the oceans were genuine moats and the threats were genuinely distant. Our oceans are no longer moats. The threats are no longer distant. Walt's framework, applied to this environment, would not preserve our security. It would expose us to risks we used to be able to outrun by virtue of geography, but no longer can.

Consider also the counterfactual Walt himself proposes for Iraq. He says that had we continued the inspections process, the United States "would have scored a resounding diplomatic victory" and Saddam would have been "contained." Perhaps. But this assumes that the inspections regime was sustainable indefinitely. By late 2002 the sanctions architecture was crumbling — Russia, France, and China were lobbying for relaxation, smuggling was rampant, the oil-for-food program was riddled with corruption that we now know reached into the upper echelons of the UN itself. Walt's "containment" was not stable. It was a strategic position that was eroding by the month. Whether the war was the right answer is a legitimate question. Whether the status quo ante was sustainable is a different question, and Walt finesses it.

I want to make a more general point. Strategic concepts like "offshore balancing," "selective engagement," and "primacy" are heuristics. They illuminate trade-offs, but they do not by themselves prescribe policy. The hard work is in the particulars: what to do about Iran, what to do about Taiwan, what to do about Pakistan, what to do about the Saudi succession, what to do about the Mexican northern border, what to do about North Korean missile tests, what to do about the Russian energy lever in Europe. Walt's essay is largely silent on these specific cases or treats them in passing. Real strategy is built case by case, with constant attention to how the cases interact. The offshore-balancing framework can illuminate some of them. It cannot answer most.

There is one point on which I am in full agreement with Walt: American foreign policy under the current administration has been less prudent, less consultative, and less strategically disciplined than the situation requires. Many of the specific reforms he proposes — abandoning the preemption doctrine as declaratory policy, investing seriously in public diplomacy, engaging the Israeli-Palestinian conflict more energetically, taking proliferation seriously as the gravest threat — are reforms I would endorse. We can pursue these reforms within a strategic posture closer to selective engagement than to offshore balancing. The errors of the past four years do not require us to abandon the framework that brought us through the Cold War. They require us to recover its discipline.

Walt's essay is, in the end, less a defense of a tradition than a proposal for a new tradition. It deserves serious debate on those grounds, and not on the false ground of historical recovery.

El Baradei's cigarette analogy lands because it is fair. On this point Walt is right and the Bush administration has been wrong.

First, the bargain treats the demand for nuclear weapons as fundamentally a response to American behavior. If we are less threatening, the argument goes, others will have less reason to seek deterrents. But this misreads the history of proliferation. The countries that have most actively pursued nuclear weapons in recent decades — Pakistan, India, North Korea, Iran, Iraq, Libya before its reversal — have done so for reasons that have little to do with American behavior toward them. Pakistan went nuclear because India did. India went nuclear because of China and because of postcolonial prestige imperatives. North Korea pursued the bomb under Kim Il-Sung in the 1980s when American policy toward Pyongyang was, if anything, neglectful rather than threatening. The implication: even if the United States offered nonaggression pacts and dramatically reduced its arsenal, the demand for nuclear weapons in these states would not disappear. Iran's clerical regime is not seeking the bomb because it fears American preventive war; it is seeking the bomb because it seeks regional hegemony, deterrence against Israel, and immunity from outside interference in its domestic affairs. North Korea wants the bomb because the regime believes (correctly, in light of the Iraqi precedent) that nuclear weapons are the most reliable insurance policy for its survival. A nonaggression pact from Washington might be useful at the margin, but it will not address the deeper drivers.

Second, the bargain understates the verification problem. Walt asks the United States to make concrete, irreversible concessions — dismantling warheads, abandoning weapons programs, signing binding pacts — in exchange for "verifiable abandonment" by states whose record on transparency is, to put it mildly, problematic. The Iranian dossier of the past three years has been one of repeated revelations of undeclared facilities — Natanz, Arak, Lavizan — that the IAEA discovered through intelligence rather than through declared inspections. The lesson of both cases is that "verifiable abandonment" is harder than the phrase suggests, and that any bargain structured as American concessions for adversary verifiable behavior is structurally biased toward our disappointment.

The grand bargain Walt describes does not really address this pathway, because Khan's customers were not states bargaining for nonaggression pacts; they were regimes pursuing nuclear capability through clandestine channels.

North Korea probably has a small number of weapons; Iran will likely cross the threshold within a decade unless something dramatic intervenes. The question is not how to roll these programs back, which may be impossible, but how to deter their use, prevent further proliferation outward, and limit the regional consequences.

Third, do exactly what Walt recommends on Article IV — close the loophole that allows non-weapons states to develop the full fuel cycle under the guise of peaceful nuclear energy. This is a critical structural reform, and it requires American leadership.

Fourth, accelerate cooperative threat reduction. The Nunn-Lugar program has been one of the most cost-effective security investments in American history. It should be expanded, extended beyond the former Soviet Union, and protected from the budget axe.

Fifth, on the American arsenal: yes, reduce, but do not naively assume that reductions buy us reciprocal behavior. Reduce because deep reductions serve our own strategic interests, not because they purchase Iranian or North Korean restraint.

The threat of nuclear terrorism is real, and Walt is right to elevate it. But the response cannot be a grand bargain that overpromises what unilateral American concessions can achieve. It must be a relentless, technical, sustained effort to secure materials, interdict transfers, harden borders, and deter use. That effort requires more American engagement, not less.
