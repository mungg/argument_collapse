Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Katznelson has written a bracing diagnosis, and his central claim—that representative legislatures remain indispensable, and that the contemporary turn toward deliberative, populist, and plebiscitary substitutes risks a slow drift toward Peronist-style "follower democracy"—deserves to be taken seriously. But the essay's elegance partly conceals a question it never quite confronts: why are legislatures actually failing? Until we answer that, the call to defend representative institutions risks defending the wrong thing.

Consider the U.S. Congress, Katznelson's implicit central case. Its dysfunction is not primarily the product of citizens turning away from representation in favor of Occupy encampments or California-style referenda. It is the product of choices made *within* representative institutions: the routinization of the Senate filibuster into a sixty-vote supermajority requirement, the Hastert Rule, the gerrymandering of House districts after 2010, the collapse of regular order in appropriations, and a campaign-finance regime—post-*Citizens United*, post-*McCutcheon*—that Katznelson rightly flags but treats as one symptom among many. These are pathologies of representation itself, not invasions from some anti-representative outside. If the legislature has become, as Katznelson worries, unable to combine "opinion" with "will," that is because its internal rules increasingly privilege veto over decision.

This matters because Katznelson's framing tends to cast deliberative and participatory innovations as competitors to legislatures, when in practice they are often the only forces pushing legislatures to function. He concedes this in his nod to Latin American participatory budgeting, but the concession deserves more weight. The Tea Party and Occupy are not, despite his pairing, symmetrical phenomena: the Tea Party reshaped a major party from within and produced legislators, committee chairs, and a speakership crisis; Occupy did almost none of those things. Populism, in other words, has worked through representation, not around it. The danger Katznelson identifies—Orbán's "illiberal state"—arises not when citizens demand more voice but when winning parties dismantle the countermajoritarian scaffolding (independent courts, free press, civil-service insulation) that protects pluralism between elections. That is a problem of liberalism more than of representation, and Katznelson's late aside about "de-liberalization" being the truer term seems exactly right and underdeveloped.

A second underweighted issue is scale. Locke and Mill wrote about polities whose legislative agendas were a tiny fraction of what modern administrative states confront. The "delegation to courts and executive agencies" Katznelson laments is not simply legislative laziness; it is, in part, a response to the impossibility of statutorily resolving every regulatory question about pharmaceuticals, derivatives, or carbon. Defending the "dignity of legislation" requires saying what legislatures should actually decide and what they should structure others to decide. Without that, "more Congress" is a slogan, not a program.

None of this refutes Katznelson's core warning. He is right that a polity which loses the habit of impermanent, openly contested, procedurally bounded lawmaking loses something democracy cannot replace by acclamation or referendum. But the path back to robust representation runs through reforming representation's own machinery—suffrage protections, campaign finance, legislative procedure, party organization—rather than through scolding citizens for seeking voice elsewhere. Two cheers for legislatures, by all means; but only if we are willing to rebuild them.

Katznelson’s analogy of the Capitol dome enclosed in scaffolding suggests a temporary restoration of a still-sound structure. But what if the visible crisis of legislative paralysis is only the outward sign of a deeper, structural hollowing out? The anxieties of 2015 are not the same as those of the 1930s. Then, the threat was a rival authoritarian model claiming superiority; now, the threat is internal: the comprehensive capture of representative government by economic elites, which empties formal democratic procedures of their meaning before any illiberal alternative even needs to take power.

The core problem is the wholesale violation of what Katznelson, following Claus Offe, calls the “principle of non-convertibility”—the norm that unequal wealth should not translate into unequal political influence. That norm has collapsed. A year ago, the political scientists Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page published their study “Testing Theories of American Politics,” which found near-zero independent influence for the preferences of average citizens on policy outcomes, while economic elites and organized business interests wield decisive impact. This is not merely a distortion; it is a different kind of regime operating under the shell of representative institutions. When Congress reliably rejects measures that commanding majorities of Americans support—from increasing the minimum wage to enacting universal background checks for firearm purchases—the legislature’s “dignity” becomes a mocking abstraction for most citizens.

Katznelson is right to sound the alarm about the turn away from representative institutions toward plebiscitary, populist, or deliberative shortcuts, and his reminder that Peronism and Orbán-style illiberalism are not dead letters is well taken. But he underestimates the rationality of that turn. The Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street, for all their differences, both registered a fundamental breakdown in the circuit of representation. They were not simply impatience with messy legislative bargaining; they were responses to a system in which that bargaining had become a closed loop among donors, lobbyists, and career politicians, impervious to broad public sentiment. When the door is locked, trying the side windows is not a romantic Rousseauian fantasy but a natural, if risky, reaction.

The “background consensus” that political scientists from Robert Dahl onward identify as a prerequisite for democratic compromise cannot be restored by better institutional design or more civic-minded exhortation. That consensus in the mid-twentieth century rested on material conditions—a historic compression of inequality, strong labor unions, and robust income growth widely shared across classes—that have been systematically dismantled. Since the 1970s, deregulation, financialization, and the crushing of organized labor have produced Gilded Age levels of inequality, splitting the country into a minority that experiences representative government as responsive and a majority that experiences it as a rigged game. The resulting crisis is not one of process but one of power.

The greatest danger today is not that democracy will be suddenly overthrown but that it will continue to decay in plain sight—elections held, laws passed, the dome glowing at night—while the people lose the effective capacity to direct their own collective fate. Restoring faith in representative institutions therefore requires far more than procedural fixes. It demands a serious confrontation with the vast inequalities of economic power that have already made a mockery of democratic citizenship. Otherwise, the scaffolding might indeed be the prelude not to repair but to a long, slow, and quietly desperate demolition.

Ira Katznelson’s metaphor of the scaffolded Capitol perfectly captures the paradox of our current political moment: the architectural shell of our democracy is meticulously preserved, while the institutional machinery inside has ground to a halt. Katznelson rightly identifies that representative legislatures—the vital engines that translate public opinion into collective will—are paralyzed by moneyed interests and hyper-partisanship. Yet, in defending the traditional legislative process against the rising tide of populist and plebiscitary alternatives, he risks treating the symptoms of our democratic anxiety as the disease itself.

Katznelson warns that bypassing the messy, adversarial lawmaking process in favor of direct action or unmediated populist movements invites the "illiberal authoritarianism" seen in Viktor Orbán’s Hungary or Juan Perón’s Argentina. He asks us to embrace the "moral ambiguity" of representative democracy, trusting in procedural correctness over guaranteed substantive outcomes. This is a noble, Lockean ideal. However, it demands a baseline condition that, by Katznelson’s own admission, no longer exists in the United States: the strict separation of wealth from political influence.

Citing Claus Offe’s "principle of non-convertibility," Katznelson acknowledges that unequal economic assets are now seamlessly converting into unequal political power, exacerbated by the post-*Citizens United* landscape. But we must follow this premise to its logical conclusion. When the legislative arena is structurally captured by elite interests, asking citizens to respect the "dignity of legislation" is tantamount to asking them to legitimize a rigged game. The recent populist eruptions—from the Tea Party on the right to Occupy Wall Street on the left—are not borne of an inherent disdain for representative democracy. They are deeply rational reactions to a legislative branch that has insulated itself from the actual preferences of the electorate.

When ordinary citizens realize that their collective voices cannot compete with the lobbying power of transnational corporations or the bottomless war chests of mega-donors, their turn toward direct democracy, executive action, or outsider populism is not a rejection of representation. It is a desperate search for it.

Katznelson ends by invoking Robert Dahl, wondering how democracies can function when the "underlying consensus" on policy evaporates. But we must ask why that consensus fractured. It did not merely dissolve in a vacuum. It was shattered by decades of widening economic inequality, the hollowing out of the middle class, and the devastating fallout of the 2008 financial crisis—crises that our representative institutions failed to prevent and struggled to mitigate. A polity cannot maintain a serene, procedural consensus when a significant portion of its citizens feels that their fundamental economic and social security is consistently ignored by the very people elected to protect it.

If we are to avoid the slide into Orbán-style illiberalism, we cannot simply scold the public for losing faith in parliaments and congresses. We must fundamentally alter the structural incentives of the legislature itself. The scaffolding around the Capitol must extend inward to campaign finance, voting rights, and the elimination of arcane, counter-majoritarian procedural rules that stifle debate. Only by aggressively fortifying the barriers between unequal wealth and equal citizenship can we restore the legitimacy of normal politics. Until the legislature actually represents the public, we should not be surprised when the public looks elsewhere for its voice.

Ira Katznelson is right to warn against the fantasy that democracy can be rescued by bypassing representation. The impatience is understandable. In the United States, Congress has become a byword for futility: shutdowns, debt-ceiling brinkmanship, routine filibusters, gerrymandered House districts, and a campaign finance system in which billionaires can plausibly imagine themselves as parties of one. But the conclusion that legislatures are obsolete is exactly wrong. If legislatures fail, the answer is not to enthrone courts, executives, experts, or “the people” in some unmediated form. It is to rebuild the institutions that make popular conflict politically usable.

Still, Katznelson’s defense of representation risks sounding too much like a defense of legislatures as they are. The central problem is not simply that citizens have lost patience with the dignity of lawmaking. It is that many citizens have good reason to doubt that lawmaking is actually representative. When the Supreme Court disables key campaign finance restraints in Citizens United and McCutcheon, when Shelby County weakens the Voting Rights Act, when state governments experiment with voter ID laws and restrictions on early voting, and when legislative districts are drawn to protect incumbents from meaningful competition, skepticism is not anti-institutional romanticism. It is an empirical judgment.

The principle Katznelson borrows from Claus Offe—the non-convertibility of economic power into political power—is the crux. But it cannot be secured by reverence for legislatures alone. It requires countervailing organizations: unions, parties, civic associations, local newspapers, churches, neighborhood groups, and social movements capable of translating diffuse interests into public claims. Mid-century American democracy did not work merely because Congress was more decorous. It worked because organized labor, civil rights groups, farm organizations, party machines, and voluntary associations connected citizens to governing institutions. Those organizations were often exclusionary and corrupt; they also gave ordinary people leverage.

That mediating world has thinned. Union density has fallen dramatically. Local journalism has weakened. Parties have become at once more ideologically sorted and less organizationally rooted. Money has filled the vacuum. In this setting, populist movements such as the Tea Party and Occupy should not be dismissed chiefly as temptations to illiberalism. They are also symptoms of representational failure. Their weakness is not that they mobilize citizens outside legislatures, but that they have difficulty becoming durable democratic intermediaries. Mobilization without organization dissipates; organization without accountability ossifies. Democracy needs both.

Nor should deliberative or participatory reforms be treated mainly as rivals to representation. Participatory budgeting, citizens’ assemblies, public consultations, and local planning experiments can be decorative, but they can also educate citizens, reveal neglected needs, and pressure representatives to act. The test is institutional connection. Do these practices feed into budgets, statutes, oversight, and party platforms? Or do they become therapeutic exercises that leave real power untouched? The danger lies less in “more democracy” than in democracy without channels of consequence.

The same is true of executive action. President Obama’s resort to administrative authority on immigration, climate, and labor regulation has provoked charges of unilateralism. Yet it has also reflected congressional paralysis. The constitutional danger is real: liberals should not celebrate executive power only when they like the executive. But the deeper danger is a legislature structured to make inaction the default even when public problems demand response. A democracy in which every major policy question becomes hostage to veto points invites precisely the impatience Katznelson fears.

The repair of representation therefore requires institutional reforms as well as civic virtues. Voting should be easy, equal, and protected. Districting should not be a tool of partisan self-entrenchment. Campaign finance rules should amplify small donors and disclose large ones. Congress needs more staff capacity and less dependence on lobbyists for expertise. The filibuster should not operate as a routine supermajority requirement. Parties should be porous enough to admit insurgency but strong enough to govern.

Katznelson’s “two cheers” are well placed. Liberal democracy does not promise justice once and for all; it promises a way to argue, revise, and lose without being silenced. But citizens will accept the moral ambiguity of democratic procedure only if they believe the procedure is not rigged in advance. Representation is indispensable. That is precisely why it must be made more representative.

Katznelson offers a compelling diagnosis of what ails contemporary liberal democracies, but his analysis leaves important questions unasked. While he correctly identifies the structural weaknesses of representative institutions and the seductive appeal of alternatives that bypass legislatures, his prescription—what he calls "a robust debate about these questions"—seems inadequate to the severity of the problem he describes. If democracies face not merely episodic dysfunction but a genuine crisis of legitimacy, procedural reforms and renewed deliberative commitment may prove insufficient.

The historical parallel Katznelson draws with the interwar period is illuminating but potentially misleading. He argues that the New Deal rescued democracy by demonstrating that representative institutions could address systemic crises. Yet this success was not purely a product of institutional competence; it depended on extraordinary circumstances—economic catastrophe, existential external threat, and a genuine transformative leadership—that cannot be manufactured by deliberative will or procedural refinement. Today's challenges, while serious, lack the same clarity of existential stakes that forced the political system to transcend its divisions.

More fundamentally, Katznelson's account underestimates the degree to which the crisis he describes reflects not a failure of will but a structural transformation in the relationship between democratic institutions and the economic system they are meant to govern. The "principle of non-convertibility" that Offe identifies—ensuring that economic inequality does not automatically translate into political inequality—has never been fully realized, but its erosion has accelerated dramatically in recent decades. This is not merely a question of campaign finance reform or voter access, though these matter. It reflects a deeper problem: that representative institutions designed for an earlier phase of capitalism are increasingly unable to regulate an economic system that operates on a global scale with minimal democratic accountability.

Katznelson is right to warn against alternatives that replace rather than complement representation. But he gives insufficient attention to why these alternatives gain traction. Populism, plebiscitary politics, and deliberative experiments do not arise simply because citizens are impatient with procedural complexity; they flourish when representative institutions demonstrably fail to address their concerns. The question is not whether to strengthen representation but how to make representation capable of delivering meaningful outcomes.

Here, Katznelson's invocation of Locke's confidence in the legislature points in the right direction, but his Lockean framework may itself be part of the problem. The legislative-centric democracy that Locke envisioned assumed a relatively contained scope of governmental action and a relatively homogeneous polity. Neither assumption holds in the twenty-first century. A revitalized democracy may require not just stronger legislatures but a rethinking of what representation means in a system characterized by deep pluralism, vast economic power beyond state boundaries, and public problems that resist legislative solutions.

This does not mean abandoning the project of strengthening representative institutions. It means recognizing that institutional repair alone, however necessary, cannot address the structural disjuncture between democratic governance and economic power. The anxieties Katznelson describes will not be resolved by appeals to civic virtue or procedural legitimacy. They require confronting the question that liberal democratic theory has consistently avoided: how representative institutions can exercise meaningful control over economic forces that now operate largely beyond their reach. Without addressing this fundamental mismatch, debates about participation, legislative procedure, and deliberative quality remain exercises in managing decline rather than reversing it.

**The Real Crisis Is Economic, Not Institutional**

Ira Katznelson's elegant tour of democratic anxiety treats the legislature as the patient and prescribes more respect for representation as the cure. But this gets the diagnosis backward. The reason American citizens have lost faith in Congress, the reason European publics flirt with Marine Le Pen and Syriza, the reason Tea Partiers and Occupiers alike are convinced that "the system is rigged," is not that we have forgotten the dignity of legislation. It is that for roughly four decades, the political economy of the advanced democracies has produced stagnant wages, ballooning wealth at the top, and a generation entering adulthood poorer than its parents. The institutional symptoms Katznelson catalogues—polarization, money's grip on politics, the retreat to executive action—are downstream of this.

Consider the evidence accumulating since Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* landed in English last year. The top 1 percent's share of national income in the United States has returned to levels not seen since the 1920s. Median household income is lower in real terms than it was in 1999. Labor's share of GDP is falling across the OECD. Meanwhile, recent work by Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page has shown that, on issues where the preferences of affluent Americans diverge from those of the median voter, policy tracks the affluent almost perfectly and the median voter not at all. This is not Claus Offe's "non-convertibility" softening at the edges; it is its outright inversion.

Katznelson nods at all this when he mentions Citizens United and voter ID laws. But he frames it as one challenge among several, and then pivots quickly to the more abstract worry about whether deliberative or plebiscitary alternatives might erode representative institutions. The pivot is revealing. It treats the institutional form as primary and the distributive content as a complication. I think the relationship runs the other way. The New Deal—Katznelson's own example of democratic rescue—did not save American democracy by perfecting parliamentary procedure. It saved democracy by delivering Social Security, the Wagner Act, federal deposit insurance, rural electrification, and a tax code that placed real burdens on the wealthy. The institutions were vindicated because they produced *material results* for ordinary people.

By contrast, the great legislative achievements of the past quarter-century—NAFTA, financial deregulation, the bipartisan reconfiguration of welfare, the Bush tax cuts, even the Affordable Care Act in its insurance-company-friendly final form—have either widened inequality or, at best, been compromises so freighted with concessions to powerful interests that they failed to feel like victories. Why are we surprised that voters tune out, gravitate toward populist outsiders, or embrace plebiscitary shortcuts? They have watched representative institutions function smoothly for thirty years while their economic situation deteriorated.

This reframing has implications for what reform should look like. Katznelson worries about "seductive substitutes" for representation and warns against Peronism. Fair enough. But the surest antidote to Peronism is not a stern lecture about the dignity of legislation. It is a representative politics that *delivers*—that taxes capital, raises wages, regulates finance, and restores some real correspondence between work and reward. The Scandinavian democracies are not immune from populist temptation, but they are notably less convulsed by it, and that is not because their parliaments are procedurally more elegant than ours. It is because their welfare states still buffer ordinary people against market shocks.

There is also a more uncomfortable point lurking here. Katznelson invokes Juan Linz on the need for "policy neutrality" and "background agreement"—the willingness to live without a singular sense of the public interest. This worked tolerably well during the postwar boom, when growth was broad-based and a rising tide really did lift most boats. Procedural neutrality is much harder to sustain when the underlying economic game is producing systematically lopsided results. People then quite reasonably suspect that "neutrality" is a euphemism for accepting the existing distribution.

I do not want to dismiss the institutional concerns Katznelson raises. Filibuster abuse, gerrymandering, the budget reconciliation maze—these matter. But fixing them in the absence of a more egalitarian economic settlement will feel, to most citizens, like rearranging furniture. If we want representative democracy to recover its legitimacy, we should worry less about whether deliberative polling will distract us from Congress and more about whether Congress is capable of writing laws that visibly improve life for the bottom three-quarters of the income distribution. That, not procedural piety, is what saved democracy the last time.

**In Defense of the Populist Surge**

Ira Katznelson's essay reads as a warning against the populist temptation: turn your back on representative institutions, he suggests, and you risk slouching toward Peronism, toward Orbán's "illiberal democracy," toward an ersatz politics in which the people are flattered while their actual power dissolves. The warning is not baseless. But it badly underestimates what populist energy is for, and it treats the symptoms of legislative dysfunction as if they were the disease.

Start with what populism has actually been doing in the United States. The Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street are Katznelson's twin exhibits of the populist disorder. Yet consider what these movements have in common: both arose in direct response to the 2008 financial crisis and the bailout that followed. Both were animated by the perception—correct, in my view—that the formal channels of representative democracy had been captured by financial interests, and that ordinary citizens had no avenue through normal politics to register their objection to socializing losses while privatizing gains. The Troubled Asset Relief Program passed with bipartisan support, against majority public opposition, after Congress was warned that the alternative was catastrophe. If that is a paradigm of "the dignity of legislation," then dignity is not what we need.

Populism in this context is not the abandonment of representation; it is a demand that representation become real again. When Occupy chanted about the 99 percent, it was not proposing to dissolve Congress. It was insisting that the agenda Congress considered—the issues that even made it into legislative debate—be expanded to include the structural inequities that the bipartisan center had ruled off-limits. Bernie Sanders's surprising traction this summer in Iowa and New Hampshire is the legislative-electoral echo of that demand. Donald Trump's surge on the right, however much one deplores his rhetoric, is a parallel revolt: a base of voters telling the Republican establishment that its priorities are not theirs.

Katznelson invokes Nadia Urbinati's *Democracy Disfigured* to caution against bypassing representation. But Urbinati's own work emphasizes that representation is constituted, not just by elections and legislatures, but by an ongoing relationship between citizens and the represented, mediated by parties, movements, opinion-formation, and protest. To treat extra-parliamentary mobilization as a threat to representation is to misunderstand representation itself. The civil rights movement was extra-parliamentary. So were the women's suffrage and labor movements. Each forced legislatures to act when legislatures, left to themselves, would not have. The legitimate fear is not populism but populism's degenerate forms: caudillismo, plebiscitary acclamation, the strongman who claims to embody the people without check.

Katznelson conflates these. He moves quickly from the Tea Party and Occupy to Perón and Orbán, as if the distinction between mass democratic movements demanding policy change and authoritarian leaders dismantling civil liberties were a matter of degree on a single spectrum. It is not. Orbán's offense is not that he listens to popular grievance. It is that he has gutted judicial independence, captured the media, gerrymandered electoral districts, and harassed civil society. These are specific institutional crimes that have nothing intrinsically to do with populist mobilization. Indeed, robust popular mobilization in Hungary—the kind Katznelson seems wary of—is exactly what would be needed to push back against him.

There is also a class question Katznelson dances around. Defenses of legislative dignity tend to be made by people for whom legislatures already work tolerably well: the well-connected, the credentialed, the professionally articulate. For those whose interests are systematically excluded from the legislative agenda—the bottom income deciles, racial minorities, the formerly incarcerated, residents of "sacrifice zones"—the dignity of legislation often looks like the dignity of being ignored politely. Telling them to wait their turn through proper procedural channels is not a neutral counsel. It is a request that they accept the verdict of institutions that have demonstrably failed them.

I share Katznelson's anxiety about authoritarianism. But the path to authoritarianism in our time runs not through too much popular energy but through too little channel for it. When representative institutions calcify, when elites treat legitimate grievance as mere noise, the populist energy that might have renewed democracy instead finds a demagogue to ride it. The way to forestall that is not to defend legislatures as they are. It is to make them responsive enough that populism can do its proper work: forcing the represented to remember whom they represent.

**The Problem Is Constitutional, Not Cultural**

Katznelson's essay drifts toward a cultural diagnosis. The trouble with American democracy, by his account, involves an erosion of "civic virtue," a softening of Offe's "non-convertibility" principle, the temptation of populist shortcuts, and the absence of a "background agreement" that once made legislative compromise possible. I find this account underspecified at exactly the place where specificity matters. The most powerful explanation for legislative paralysis in the United States is not cultural decline or moneyed corruption, important as those are. It is the architectural mismatch between an 18th-century constitution and a 21st-century party system.

Consider what the American constitutional order requires for ordinary lawmaking. A majority of the House. Sixty votes in a Senate where Wyoming and California have equal weight. Presidential signature, or the supermajority to override a veto. In the modern era, with disciplined parties sorted ideologically (something that was not true for most of American history), the realistic threshold for non-trivial legislation is unified party control of all three veto points—a condition that obtained for only about twenty of the past sixty years. Even under unified control, the filibuster, the Byrd rule, and the committee system create endless internal veto points. We have built, in effect, a system whose default output is stalemate.

For most of the twentieth century, this didn't matter as much, for two reasons. First, the parties were ideologically heterogeneous: Southern Democrats and liberal Republicans created cross-cutting coalitions that allowed bipartisan majorities to form on individual issues. Second, the courts and administrative agencies were widely understood to have legitimate roles in elaborating policy where Congress declined to legislate in detail. Both conditions have weakened. The parties have sorted, eliminating the cross-cutting coalitions; and the legitimacy of administrative and judicial policy-making is now itself contested, partly because Congress, unable to legislate, increasingly forces those institutions to make policy that legislatures would in other systems handle directly.

Compare a Westminster system. There, an electoral majority produces a government that can in principle enact its program. Voters can hold that government accountable at the next election: results are visible, responsibility is clear. In the U.S. system, when nothing happens, no one knows whom to blame. The president blames Congress; Congress blames the president; each party blames the other; voters give up and vote on tribal cues, or stay home. That is not a failure of civic virtue. It is a predictable response to an opaque accountability structure.

This matters because Katznelson's prescriptions, to the extent he offers any, are largely cultural and exhortatory: rebuild background agreement, respect the dignity of legislation, resist populist shortcuts. But cultural exhortation does not move outcomes if the underlying institutional incentives push in the opposite direction. The minority party in a system with frequent divided government has every incentive to obstruct, because obstruction is rewarded with the next election. The majority party has every incentive to legislate maximally during its brief windows of unified control, because the policy gains will likely be the only ones it gets. Both behaviors are rational. Calling them uncivil does not change the calculus.

What would constitutional reform look like? Abolishing or reforming the filibuster, which is not in the Constitution and was a procedural accident, would help. Curbing partisan gerrymandering through independent redistricting commissions would help. Moving toward proportional representation in House elections, which is constitutionally permissible, would help. Reform of campaign finance—which, yes, requires either a constitutional amendment or a different Supreme Court—would help. These are concrete, available levers, and they do not require us to invent a new civic culture.

Katznelson is right that representation matters and that bypassing legislatures has dangers. But he writes as if the legislature were a vessel that could be filled or emptied by attitudes of respect. Legislatures are mechanisms. They produce outputs given inputs. If the mechanism systematically converts polarized inputs into stalemate, the answer is to redesign the mechanism, not to scold the inputs.

There is a deep American reluctance to admit that the Constitution itself might be part of the problem. Our founding document carries a quasi-religious aura. But James Madison would have been astonished to learn that 60 votes in the Senate had become the normal threshold for legislation. The Framers built veto points to slow majoritarian passion; they did not build a system designed to require supermajorities for the conduct of routine government. Recovering ordinary representative democracy may require us to admit that the design needs ordinary repair.

**The Missing Variable: Mass Media**

Katznelson's diagnosis is impressively wide-ranging, but a curious absence haunts it. Nowhere does he discuss, except in passing, the transformation of the media environment in which contemporary democratic publics actually form their opinions. This omission is striking because no plausible account of legislative dysfunction, polarized publics, or the rise of populist mobilization can be complete without it.

Consider the changes of the past two decades. The collapse of the mass-circulation regional newspaper. The fragmentation of broadcast television into hundreds of cable channels, with ideologically sorted news outlets on both right and left. The migration of younger audiences from any traditional news source to social media platforms whose algorithms reward outrage and tribal identification. The disappearance of the "shared facts" that, however imperfectly, the era of Cronkite produced. The collapse of local journalism, leaving thousands of municipalities and counties with no professional coverage of their own governments. These are not background conditions. They are constitutive of what contemporary democratic citizenship feels like.

Dahl, whom Katznelson cites, insisted that "enlightened understanding" was a necessary condition of democracy: citizens must have access to information sufficient to form considered judgments about the public interest. The institutions that supplied that information—imperfectly, but really—have been hollowed out, and nothing has yet emerged to replace them. The result is not merely uninformed citizens but, more insidiously, asymmetrically misinformed ones, each side convinced that the other lives in a fabricated reality. Background agreement, the condition Katznelson rightly identifies as essential to representative democracy, becomes nearly impossible when citizens cannot agree on what is happening.

This media transformation interacts powerfully with the legislative dysfunction Katznelson describes. Members of Congress, like everyone else, now operate in a media environment that rewards confrontation over compromise. A floor speech denouncing the other side, posted to YouTube and circulated through ideological networks, generates donations and fundraising lists. A quiet act of legislative collaboration generates nothing—or, worse, a primary challenge from the activist base of one's own party. The incentives facing representatives have been restructured by media incentives, and we should not be surprised that legislative behavior has changed accordingly.

The populist movements Katznelson catalogues are partly products of this environment. The Tea Party's national infrastructure was substantially built through Fox News and conservative talk radio. Occupy spread through Twitter and Tumblr. Trump's current ascendancy is unimaginable without the symbiosis between his media-savvy provocation and a 24-hour news cycle that cannot help amplifying him. To analyze these movements without attending to the platforms that constituted them is to analyze the play without the stage.

Katznelson's framework, drawn from the constitutional and political theory of the 18th through 20th centuries, struggles with this dimension because the theorists he invokes—Locke, Mill, Madison, Schmitt, Dahl, Urbinati—largely assumed a media environment that no longer exists. Locke's commonwealth had pamphlets and coffeehouses. Mill's England had a relatively integrated public sphere shaped by a handful of national newspapers and journals. Dahl's mid-century America had three television networks and a robust local press. The institutions of representative democracy were designed to mediate among publics that, however unequal in influence, shared a basic informational substrate.

What does it mean to demand legislative deliberation, the patient construction of compromise, the suspension of disbelief about substantive justice, in an environment where every political move is instantly framed by partisan media as either a betrayal or a triumph? The "physics of consent" Jeremy Waldron describes presupposes a public that can absorb the slow, often boring work of legislation. Today's public is being trained—by platforms whose business models depend on engagement—to find that work invisible or insufferable.

I do not have a tidy solution, and neither, I suspect, does anyone. The problem is structural, technological, and economic at once: the advertising-based business model that sustained mass journalism has migrated to platforms with no editorial obligation to citizenship. But any serious essay on democratic anxiety should reckon with this transformation as a primary factor, not an afterthought.

When Katznelson asks "which circumstances reduce the likelihood that [the democratic] framework can flourish," the honest answer must include: an information environment that systematically rewards tribalism, that pulverizes shared facts, that monetizes outrage, and that has no constituency for the slow work of representation. Until we address that, all the institutional reforms in the world will be repairs on a building whose foundation continues to subside.

**Two Cheers Is Plenty: A Skeptical Reply**

Reading Katznelson's lament for representative democracy, I am struck by a peculiar nostalgia at its core. He invokes Forster's "two cheers for democracy" approvingly, but his own essay reads as a plea for the third cheer—a defense of legislative representation as the irreplaceable heart of a decent politics, against the various populist, deliberative, and plebiscitary impostors. I want to suggest that Forster's restraint was wisdom, and that we should be wary of theorists who, in defending democracy against its critics, end up sounding like apologists for the institutional arrangements that have produced our current discontents.

Consider the implicit move Katznelson makes throughout. He acknowledges that legislatures are bedeviled by money, polarization, and procedural dysfunction. He concedes that ordinary citizens have good reasons for dissatisfaction. He grants that participatory budgeting and similar innovations can be valuable. But every time the argument reaches a fork, he turns toward defense of the central institutions: don't abandon parliaments, don't bypass representation, beware the slide to Peronism. The cumulative effect is to make any serious challenge to existing legislative practice look like a step toward Orbán.

This is a familiar rhetorical move, and it has costs. It treats the present configuration of representative democracy as more or less normative—the thing to be preserved against deviation—when in fact the present configuration is a contingent, recent, and badly compromised arrangement that has lost legitimacy in the eyes of many of those it claims to represent. The interesting question is not whether to defend "representation" in the abstract. It is whether the particular institutions we have are adequate to the representational task, and if not, what configurations might do better.

Take Katznelson's framing of deliberative democracy. He summarizes it (via Urbinati) as the idea that "difficult problems would be solved and correct outcomes consensually discerned if only citizens were offered chances to reason together on the basis of an assumption of shared interests and information." Then he dismisses it as making "opinion transcend will." But this caricatures the substantial empirical literature on deliberative practices. James Fishkin's deliberative polling work, the experiments in citizens' assemblies in British Columbia and Ireland, the participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre that Katznelson himself mentions favorably—these are not utopian replacements for legislatures. They are concrete, tested ways of feeding more informed and considered public opinion into representative processes. The choice is not deliberation *versus* representation; it is representation *with* or *without* serious mechanisms of public reasoning.

Similarly with plebiscitary instruments. Katznelson cites California as a cautionary tale, and there are real problems with that state's initiative process—particularly the way Proposition 13 has constrained fiscal policy for decades. But citizen initiatives in Switzerland, used regularly and routinely, have not produced authoritarian backsliding; they have produced a remarkably stable and well-governed federation. The lesson is not that direct democratic instruments are intrinsically dangerous, but that they need to be embedded in institutional contexts that channel them constructively.

There is something class-inflected about the way Katznelson sets up his oppositions. The defense of "representation" against "populism" is, in practice, often a defense of professional politicians, lawyers, and credentialed elites against the unruly preferences of ordinary citizens. I do not mean that Katznelson intends this; his own work, particularly on the New Deal, shows deep sympathy for the working-class politics that drove that era's reforms. But the framework he deploys here, with its repeated warnings against bypassing legislatures, lands in a place that is functionally conservative—a defense of the institutional status quo dressed up as democratic theory.

Forster gave democracy two cheers because he knew better than to give it three. Democracy admits variety and permits criticism; that is enough. It is not the highest political value, nor an end in itself, nor the embodiment of any particular wisdom. It is a workable arrangement for managing disagreement among rough equals. When particular democratic institutions cease to perform that function—as American legislative institutions have, in important ways, ceased to perform it—the appropriate response is not to defend them more passionately but to ask what reforms or supplements might restore the function.

The third cheer is precisely the one we should withhold. Reserved enthusiasm for democratic institutions is what allows us to fix them when they break. Excessive piety is what prevents repair.

**The Forgotten International Dimension**

Katznelson briefly notes that "national borders no longer define a zone of security" and that anxiety about transnational institutions and global processes is part of what ails contemporary democracies. He then drops the thread. This is unfortunate, because the international dimension is arguably the most important variable he names—and it changes the diagnosis significantly.

Begin with the obvious. The advanced democracies became democratic in roughly their current form during a particular historical moment, roughly 1945 to 1975, in which several conditions held: capital was relatively immobile, currencies were pegged or managed, trade was substantial but bounded, immigration was modest, and the security framework was provided largely by American military power and bilateral alliances. Within this framework, national legislatures could in fact set economic policy: they could tax capital because capital could not easily flee; they could set labor standards because the alternative production sites were limited; they could regulate finance because financial institutions were largely domestic. The "embedded liberalism" of the postwar order, as John Ruggie called it, was the political-economic foundation on which postwar democratic legitimacy rested.

Almost every term in that description has changed. Capital is now mobile at the speed of light. Floating exchange rates and integrated capital markets mean that any national government attempting unilateral redistribution faces immediate capital flight and currency pressure. Production chains are global; labor standards become a competitiveness liability. Finance is transnational, regulated through international bodies and treaties that are themselves opaque to most citizens. Migration, both authorized and unauthorized, is at historic levels in many democracies. And the climate problem—perhaps the supreme test of contemporary governance—is irreducibly global.

In this context, the diagnosis of "legislative dysfunction" looks different. Many of the things citizens most want their legislatures to do—stabilize wages, control immigration, regulate finance, prevent climate catastrophe—are no longer fully within national legislative power. The legislatures are dysfunctional partly in the literal sense: they have lost function. Their nominal sovereignty has been hollowed by globalization, while no commensurate democratic structure has emerged at the supranational level. The European Union is the most ambitious experiment in democratic transnationalism, and its current legitimacy crisis—the Greek bailout, the refugee influx—suggests how hard the problem is.

This means that some of what Katznelson takes for populist temptation is in fact a quite rational response to a real deficit. When voters elect populist parties promising to "take back control," they are responding to the accurate perception that the legislatures they elect cannot, in current arrangements, deliver on the matters that matter most. Whether the populist promise of withdrawal can actually deliver is another question—the answer is usually no—but the underlying complaint is well-founded.

It also means that Katznelson's prescription of recommitting to representative legislatures is, on its own, insufficient. A more robust national legislature in a context of transnational economic flows can do more about distribution and regulation than a hollowed one, but it cannot do enough. The honest reformist agenda must include the construction of democratic accountability at transnational levels—a project of staggering difficulty, but one that the urgency of climate change alone makes unavoidable.

Here Katznelson's historical analogy with the New Deal becomes complicated. The New Deal succeeded partly because the United States, even in the 1930s, was a continental economy with a powerful federal state that could regulate the national market it largely contained. The analog today would be regulation at a level commensurate with the global market: something the world does not have and cannot easily build. The historical comparison can mislead us by suggesting that another vigorous burst of national lawmaking could restore democratic legitimacy. The structural situation is genuinely different.

I do not mean to counsel despair. There are openings. Climate diplomacy, the Paris meetings later this year, may yet produce something. International tax cooperation around base erosion and profit shifting is advancing in the OECD. Regional democratic structures may evolve. But any honest reckoning with democratic anxiety must place the international dimension at the center, not at the periphery, of the diagnosis.

Katznelson's essay is rich in the political theory of nation-state democracy from Locke to Linz. The anxiety he describes, however, is precisely the anxiety of a nation-state democratic order whose preconditions have eroded. The theoretical resources of the past are necessary but not sufficient. We need new theory, and new institutions, adequate to the scale of the problems our existing institutions can no longer reach.

**Race, Exclusion, and the Limits of Procedural Democracy**

Katznelson, of all scholars, knows that American democratic institutions were built on racial exclusion. His own book *Fear Itself*, and *When Affirmative Action Was White* before it, document with painful clarity how the New Deal he praises so warmly was made possible by an alliance with white supremacists that systematically excluded Black Americans from its benefits. So I find it surprising that his current essay treats the problem of representation in such formal terms, with race appearing mostly under the heading of "racial equity" as one consequential issue among several that legislatures fail to address.

The omission matters because, in the United States especially, the legitimacy crisis of representative democracy is not separable from its racial history. The institutions Katznelson defends as "the bedrock of representative democracy" were designed in part to protect slaveholding states (the three-fifths clause, the structure of the Senate), reformed only partly after the Civil War (Reconstruction's overthrow), and reformed again only in the mid-20th century (the Voting Rights Act of 1965, the parts of which have now been gutted by *Shelby County v. Holder*). To speak of representation as the noble inheritance of Locke and Madison without centering this history is to engage in a kind of analytic whitewashing.

Consider the present moment. Since 2013, when the Supreme Court invalidated the preclearance regime of the Voting Rights Act, state legislatures across the South have moved aggressively to restrict ballot access: stricter voter ID laws, cuts to early voting, polling place closures concentrated in Black neighborhoods, purges of voter rolls. Katznelson mentions voter ID in passing as an example of Offe's "non-convertibility" principle being violated. But the deeper pattern is one of deliberate, race-conscious efforts to shape the electorate so as to entrench particular partisan outcomes. This is not a marginal failure of legislative dignity. It is an active project to determine which citizens count.

The Black Lives Matter movement, which has shaped American political discourse over the past year, is one of the most important current examples of extra-legislative democratic mobilization. By Katznelson's framework, it might be classed alongside Occupy and the Tea Party as populist energy that should ultimately be channeled back into representative institutions. But this framing badly misdescribes what the movement is. It arose precisely because the conventional representative process—elected mayors, elected DAs, elected legislators—has systematically failed to address police violence against Black citizens. The movement's grievances are not procedural but substantive: that the law, as enforced, treats Black lives as expendable, and that the representatives who could change this either do not or cannot.

When Katznelson invokes Linz on the need for "policy neutrality" and the suspension of disbelief about substantive justice, he is voicing a venerable strand of liberal democratic theory. But this strand has always been most plausible to those whose substantive needs are already adequately met by the going arrangements. For those for whom the going arrangements include lethal force from the state, "policy neutrality" is not a posture available at zero cost. The willingness to live without a singular sense of justice presupposes that the rough working sense of justice is tolerably close to one's own. When it is not, demanding such forbearance is asking the systematically wronged to be more equanimous than their wrongers.

I take Katznelson's broader point about the dangers of bypassing representation. I share his worry that mass mobilization without institutional channels can produce demagoguery rather than reform. But the alternative he gestures toward—patience with legislative process, faith that proper procedure will eventually produce justice—is not equally available to all democratic constituencies. For Black Americans in particular, the historical record is that legislative process produced justice only when extra-legislative pressure forced its hand: the abolitionists, the civil rights movement, the Freedom Riders. The dignity of legislation in those cases was the dignity of finally responding to citizens who refused to be ignored.

A serious account of democratic anxiety in America must place these dynamics at the center. The "background agreement" that Katznelson worries we have lost was, for much of American history, an agreement on terms that excluded large portions of the population. Whatever new agreement is built must be built differently, with the excluded as constitutive participants. That work is being done, imperfectly and contentiously, by exactly the kinds of movements Katznelson treats as warning signs. They deserve to be treated, instead, as democratic actors of the first importance.

**A Conservative Defense of Legislative Decay**

I want to make an unfashionable argument: legislative gridlock is not the catastrophe that Katznelson and other commentators make it out to be. In a constitutional republic of nearly 320 million people, with deep and genuine disagreements about the proper scope of government, the proper balance between liberty and security, and the moral foundations of public life, it is *appropriate* that major policy changes be difficult to enact. The Founders designed our system to require broad consensus for sweeping action. The fact that broad consensus is currently unavailable suggests not that our institutions are failing but that they are working as intended.

Katznelson presents legislative paralysis as a sign of democratic crisis. But consider what Congress is failing to do. It is failing to pass a comprehensive immigration reform that significant portions of the country regard as amnesty for lawbreaking. It is failing to pass climate legislation that significant portions of the country regard as economically ruinous on questionable scientific premises. It is failing to expand entitlements that significant portions of the country regard as already fiscally unsustainable. It is failing to enact gun control measures that significant portions of the country regard as infringements of a constitutional right. In each case, the failure reflects real disagreement, not procedural malfunction.

The implicit premise of Katznelson's essay—and of much center-left commentary on dysfunction—is that there exists, somewhere, a body of correct policy answers that responsible legislatures would adopt if only they were not impeded by polarization and money. The frustration is with the impediment. But this premise is itself contested. Many citizens disagree with the policy program that "functional" legislatures would, on this view, deliver. They are not wrong to use the institutional veto points available to them—the filibuster, federalism, separation of powers—to slow or block changes they consider unwise. They are exercising their constitutional prerogatives.

Notice, too, the asymmetry in Katznelson's examples. The crises of democratic legitimacy he cites all run in one direction: the failure to redistribute, the failure to regulate finance, the failure to address climate. He worries about the influence of "moneyed interests" but not about the influence of well-organized public sector unions, environmental NGOs, or progressive foundations. He invokes Citizens United as a blatant violation of equal citizenship but does not mention that media corporations and 501(c)(3) advocacy organizations had already been exercising unequal political influence for decades. The framework of "non-convertibility" between wealth and political influence is plausibly neutral, but its actual application in Katznelson's argument tracks the priorities of one ideological camp.

There is also a deeper conservative point about the alleged "background agreement" that Linz and Dahl thought necessary to democratic functioning. Such background agreement is not a natural condition; it is an achievement of shared culture, shared institutions, shared formative experiences. The decline of that agreement in contemporary America is real, but it has substantial sources that progressives are reluctant to acknowledge: the erosion of religious commonality, the fragmentation of educational curricula, the abandonment of assimilationist expectations for immigration, the elite cultural disdain for the values of much of the country. You cannot dissolve the shared culture and then bemoan the loss of the shared political agreement that depended on it.

This is not to deny that there are real problems. Excessive delegation to administrative agencies—what Katznelson briefly mentions but does not develop—is a genuine constitutional concern, one Justice Thomas and others have written about with seriousness. The administrative state has accumulated rulemaking authority that effectively bypasses Congress, raising legitimate questions about democratic legitimacy. But the answer is not more legislative activity; it is the constraint of administrative discretion and the return of significant policy questions to the representative bodies that ought to decide them.

Katznelson's anxiety is, in the end, an anxiety about the inability of representative democracy to enact a particular policy program. I share his commitment to representative democracy. I do not share his confidence about what such democracy should produce. A polity that contains the disagreements ours contains will inevitably produce slow and conflicted lawmaking. That is not a failure to be lamented but a feature to be cherished. Two cheers for gridlock: it admits variety, and it permits criticism—of progressive ambitions, among others, that legislatures appropriately decline to ratify.

**A View from the Periphery: What Katznelson Misses About the Global South**

Katznelson's essay, despite its gestures toward Latin America and the global situation, is fundamentally an essay about the established Western democracies, with brief side glances at Hungary and Argentina to illustrate dangers. The implicit map places the United States, Britain, France, and Germany at the center of democratic theory, with peripheral cases serving as cautionary tales. I want to argue that this geography distorts the analysis, and that a more honest accounting from the perspective of the global South complicates Katznelson's narrative in instructive ways.

First, consider the historical narrative. Katznelson tells a story in which liberal democracy faced existential threats from fascism and communism in the mid-20th century, survived through the New Deal and its analogues, triumphed by 1989, and now faces softer but still serious anxieties. From the perspective of much of the world, this story is missing crucial chapters. The same Western democracies whose institutions Katznelson celebrates were, throughout the period of their consolidation, presiding over colonial empires that explicitly denied democratic norms to most of their subjects. France was a democracy in Paris and a brutal occupier in Algeria. Britain was a democracy at Westminster and a colonial autocrat in India and Kenya. The United States was a democracy in much of its territory and a Jim Crow regime in others, and exercised dominion in the Philippines and through interventions across Latin America that frequently overthrew democratically elected governments.

This is not a digression. It bears directly on contemporary democratic theory. The mid-20th-century triumph of Western democracy that Katznelson invokes was a triumph that depended materially on resources, markets, and labor extracted from non-democratic peripheries. The "embedded liberalism" of the postwar West was substantially financed by terms of trade favorable to the West and unfavorable to the decolonizing South. To present the Western democratic settlement as a self-contained achievement and the contemporary anxieties as endogenous to Western politics is to obscure the global political economy that always conditioned Western democracy.

Second, the post-1989 picture looks different from the South. Katznelson notes that "an increasingly authoritarian Russia embraces democratic forms," which is true, but the more interesting story is the broader landscape of "competitive authoritarianism" (in Levitsky and Way's term) that has emerged across Eastern Europe, the post-Soviet space, much of Africa, and parts of Asia. These regimes hold elections, allow opposition parties, and observe many formal democratic conventions—while systematically using state power to ensure that incumbents cannot lose. Hungary under Orbán is one example; Turkey under Erdoğan another; Venezuela's evolving Bolivarianism another. The interesting theoretical question is not whether these are deviations from a Western norm but whether they represent an emerging hybrid type with its own logic.

Third, the populist anxieties Katznelson describes look different from the South. Participatory budgeting, which he mentions in passing, originated in Porto Alegre, Brazil, and has been a significant democratic innovation. The Latin American "pink tide" of the 2000s—Lula in Brazil, Bachelet in Chile, the Kirchners in Argentina, Mujica in Uruguay—was not, in most cases, a slide toward Peronism. It was a serious experiment in combining redistribution with electoral democracy, and its mixed results offer important lessons that Katznelson's brief invocation of Perón obscures. The lessons include both successes—poverty reduction in Brazil, the rebuilding of social contracts after dictatorship—and failures—the corruption that has now engulfed the Brazilian Workers' Party, the dysfunction of Venezuela. But the experiments deserve more than to be filed under "Peronism."

Fourth, the energy of democratic innovation today is, in important ways, in the South. The constitutional moment in Tunisia after 2011, the participatory institutions of Kerala and other Indian states, the experiments in Bolivia and Ecuador with plurinational constitutionalism, the post-apartheid South African constitutional jurisprudence—these are sites of democratic experimentation as significant as anything happening in Western legislatures. They cannot be assimilated to Katznelson's framework, which treats the established Western legislatures as the norm to be defended against deviation.

I do not want to reverse the polarity and treat Northern democracy as discredited and Southern innovation as redemptive. The point is that any adequate theory of democracy in 2015 must be genuinely global in its evidence base and its theoretical resources. Katznelson's essay, valuable as it is, remains parochial in ways that limit its diagnosis. The future of democracy is being worked out in São Paulo and Tunis and Hyderabad, not only in Washington and Brussels.

**The Schmittian Moment**

There is something quietly alarming in the way Katznelson invokes Carl Schmitt only to dismiss him. Schmitt, he tells us, was wrong: postwar legislative democracies disproved the claim that representative regimes possess "intrinsic and unresolvable contradictions requiring a radical shift toward executive power buttressed by the legitimacy of unmediated popular sovereignty." This is the right historical judgment about 1945–1989. I am less sure it is the right judgment about 2015. Schmitt's questions, if not his answers, have returned, and we should sit with them rather than wave them away.

Schmitt's core challenge to liberal parliamentarism, articulated most sharply in *The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923), was that the institution's foundational assumptions—rule by discussion, the conversion of disagreement into provisional consensus through open debate, the belief that truth emerges from the competition of arguments—had become hollow. Parliaments, he claimed, no longer practiced genuine discussion. Decisions were made by party caucuses and interest groups before they reached the floor; speeches were ritual performances; the supposedly truth-seeking process was a façade over what was in fact a calculus of force among organized blocs. The complexity of modern policy, the speed of modern decision-making, the technical expertise required for financial regulation or climate policy or cyber security, may be genuinely incompatible with parliamentary deliberation as classically understood. The justifications are often plausible: legislatures cannot act, decisions must be made, leaders elected on democratic mandates have authority to lead. Katznelson treats plebiscitary democracy as a third disfiguring alternative to legislative representation, alongside deliberative and populist temptations. Consider Switzerland, the country with the world's most extensive use of direct democratic instruments. It is among the world's most prosperous, peaceful, and well-governed states. The Swiss model has not produced authoritarian backsliding, not undermined parliamentary functioning, not led to demagoguery or to the substitution of mass passion for considered judgment. It has produced a political culture in which citizens are unusually engaged with public questions and unusually well-informed about policy details, because they know that their judgment may shortly be required.

The result was both a clear policy outcome and a national experience of democratic self-determination.

Even California, Katznelson's cautionary case, looks more mixed than the standard liberal complaint allows. Yes, Proposition 13 has constrained fiscal policy for decades. Yes, the initiative process is sometimes captured by well-funded interests. But the same process has also produced significant progressive achievements: environmental protections, public health measures, criminal justice reforms. The complaint that voters lack the expertise to decide complex questions cuts in odd directions: it has been used to defeat conservative tax revolts and liberal climate measures alike, which suggests it functions more as elite skepticism of mass judgment than as a principled assessment of capacity.

The deeper point is that Katznelson's framework, in defending representative legislatures, takes for granted that legislative deliberation is more considered, better informed, and more responsive to the public interest than direct popular judgment. This is an empirical claim, and the evidence for it is much thinner than the rhetoric suggests. Legislators in many systems are insulated from direct accountability by gerrymandering and incumbency advantages. They are subject to intense lobbying by organized interests in ways the general public is not. They face perverse incentives toward short-termism (the next election) and toward symbolic position-taking rather than substantive lawmaking. None of this means they are useless, but it means the comparison with direct democratic instruments is not as one-sided as Katznelson implies.

There is also a constitutional argument worth making. Plebiscitary instruments can serve as a check on legislative capture by minoritarian interests. When legislatures are unresponsive to clear majoritarian preferences—as the U.S. Congress arguably is on issues like background checks for gun purchases, where polls show overwhelming support but legislation cannot pass—referenda offer a mechanism for the majority to override capture. These design choices distinguish well-functioning systems like Switzerland from more troubled ones.

Katznelson lumps deliberative, populist, and plebiscitary alternatives together as collectively threatening to representation. The more useful approach is to ask which combinations of representative, deliberative, and direct instruments produce the best democratic outcomes in particular contexts. Switzerland, Ireland, even California with appropriate reforms—these point toward hybrid arrangements that strengthen rather than weaken democratic legitimacy. Katznelson's essay roams across two centuries of political theory and across the major institutional categories of contemporary democracy, but there is a curious gap at its center: he says almost nothing about political parties. Consider the American case. Through most of the 20th century, the two major American parties were ideologically heterogeneous coalitions. The Democratic Party included Northern liberals and Southern conservatives; the Republican Party included Western progressives and Northeastern moderates alongside its conservative wing. This heterogeneity made cross-party coalitions on individual issues normal: civil rights legislation in 1964 passed with majorities of both parties; environmental legislation in the early 1970s had broad bipartisan support; conservative reforms in the 1980s drew Democratic votes.

Beginning in roughly the 1970s and accelerating since, this heterogeneity dissolved. The parties sorted ideologically. The conservative wing of the Democratic Party migrated to the Republicans (the Southern realignment); the liberal wing of the Republican Party either retired or shifted to the Democrats. By the 2000s, every Democratic senator was to the left of every Republican senator on most major measures—a situation without precedent in American political history. The parties became, in effect, parliamentary parties operating within a separation-of-powers system designed for cross-cutting coalitions.

This is the fundamental institutional reason for contemporary gridlock. The American constitutional system requires cooperation across multiple veto points. The party system used to facilitate that cooperation by ensuring that no single party held a monopoly on any major issue position. Now the party system actively impedes cooperation: bipartisan deals expose participants to primary challenges from their party's base. The behavior of contemporary congressional leaders is rational given these conditions, even when it is collectively destructive.

Katznelson notices the symptoms—polarization, the obsessive protection of intensely interested minorities, ideological division—but he does not theorize them as products of party-system change. This matters because the diagnosis affects the prescription. If polarization is the result of moneyed influence and declining civic virtue, the fixes are campaign finance reform and civic education. If polarization is the result of ideological sorting of the party system within a constitutional structure built for unsorted parties, the fixes are structural: changes to primary election rules, changes to redistricting, possibly changes to electoral systems that affect party formation.

The European story is different but parallel. Across Western Europe, the traditional center-left and center-right parties that organized politics for most of the postwar period are losing vote share. New parties on both flanks—the populist right (Front National, AfD, UKIP, Northern League), the populist left (Syriza, Podemos, the Left Bloc)—are reorganizing political competition. The traditional party-system function of aggregating diverse interests into stable governing coalitions is breaking down. Grand coalitions in Germany and Austria, technocratic governments in Italy, the unraveling of bipartisan alternation in Spain and Greece: these are signs that the European party systems built after 1945 are no longer doing their work.

Why did parties change? Several factors converge. The decline of mass-membership parties, replaced by professional cadre operations dependent on donors and media. The migration of working-class voters away from social democratic parties as those parties embraced market liberalism in the 1990s. The cultural cleavages around immigration, European integration, and identity that cut across the old class-based party alignments. The rise of digital media that allows political mobilization outside party structures.

If parties are the central institution through which representation operates—as virtually all serious political scientists, from Schattschneider on, have insisted—then a crisis of representation is fundamentally a crisis of parties. The dignity of legislation that Katznelson celebrates depends on parties capable of organizing legislative coalitions, recruiting capable candidates, articulating coherent policy programs, and structuring electoral choice for voters. When parties cannot do these things, legislatures cannot do their work.

The reform agenda that follows is therefore primarily an agenda of party reform: how to construct parties that can effectively aggregate contemporary social interests, that can resist capture by donors and activist minorities, that can recruit competent legislators, that can structure electoral competition around real policy choices rather than identity markers. This is hard work and we know less than we should about how to do it well. But it is the work that the renewal of representative democracy requires. Katznelson's elegant defense of representation needs, finally, an account of the parties that make representation possible.

It's recognizing that democracy requires an informed citizenry, and that institutions can be designed to promote such information. Katznelson offers no alternative for creating informed citizens except hoping legislatures will somehow educate.

The Tea Party and Occupy Wall Street weren't simply "mobilized people" seeking to bypass representation. They were responses to genuine failures that citizens correctly identified. Wall Street did cause the 2008 financial crisis. Congressional Republicans did prioritize party over country. That citizens mobilized outside normal institutions reflected not demagoguery but institutional failure. Katznelson criticizes these movements for privileging will over opinion, but opinion without will is impotent.

California's initiative process deserves more credit than Katznelson gives. It has produced genuine innovations—term limits, budget reform, environmental regulation—that national institutions couldn't achieve. Yes, the process can produce perverse outcomes, but so can legislatures. California's per capita GDP leads the nation. Its environmental standards drive national policy. These aren't accidental.

Katznelson concludes by calling for debate about "the underlying consensus" democracy requires. But he's vague about what this consensus should contain. Perhaps democracy doesn't require consensus at all—only institutions that allow diverse groups to coexist despite disagreement. Madisonian pluralism was always about managing difference, not transcending it. If consensus were required, democracy would have failed long before today.

He immediately dismisses this possibility, but perhaps he should take it more seriously. Democratic institutions have faced existential crises before and survived. They may survive again.

The scaffolding around the Capitol dome is literally true—and metaphorically apt. Buildings require constant maintenance. The dome was restored in the 1960s after similar decay. American democracy has weathered crises before—the abolition of slavery, the Populist revolt, the Great Depression, Watergate, Vietnam. Each produced genuine damage and genuine repair. The pattern was not decline but oscillation between crisis and recovery.

What would distinguish today from previous crises? Katznelson identifies money in politics, ideological polarization, and executive aggrandizement. But these aren't unprecedented. The Gilded Age saw money completely capture politics. The 1850s saw ideological polarization that produced civil war. The Nixon administration created the imperial presidency. Each was addressed, if incompletely.

The key variable may be institutional resilience—the capacity of democratic structures to self-correct. The Supreme Court's Citizens United decision could be reversed by constitutional amendment or a new Court. The Senate's filibuster is a Senate rule, not constitutional. The Electoral College has twice in sixteen years produced presidents who lost the popular vote, but the political cost has been steep for both. Each of these distortions has generated counter-mobilization.

Katznelson worries about "de-democratization" or "de-liberalization." But liberal democracy has proven surprisingly robust. Hungary's Orbán is explicit about building "illiberal democracy," but Hungary is a small country with limited global influence. China's "autocratic capitalism" works, for now, but faces demographic and economic headwinds that may require political liberalization. The global picture is mixed.

Perhaps anxiety is itself the problem. Citizens who believe democracy is broken may cease participating in ways that would otherwise sustain it. Katznelson's essay, for all its scholarly nuance, may contribute to the very cynicism it diagnoses. Democratic theory has a self-fulfilling dimension: if citizens believe institutions can't work, they won't work. This is the real danger.

Katznelson writes primarily about American democracy but claims his anxieties apply "across the range of established democracies." This is dubious. Germany, despite recent PEGIDA protests, has remarkably stable democratic institutions. Scandinavian countries combine high economic regulation with robust representation. Even Britain, buffeted by EU debates and Scottish nationalism, maintains functional government. If democracy is in crisis, why do most established democracies look remarkably healthy?

The United States is the outlier, not the norm. Its dysfunction stems from specific institutional features: first-past-the-post elections that create two-party monopoly, constitutional structure that gives rural minorities veto power, campaign finance rules that invite corruption. These features were designed by the founders or evolved through path dependence, but they aren't inevitable or universal. Most democracies use proportional representation. Most don't have bicameralism with equal Senate representation. Most regulate campaign finance more stringently.

Katznelson correctly identifies American exceptionalism but treats it as evidence of broader democratic malaise. This is a category error. If American democracy is broken—and it may be—the causes are specific rather than universal. We shouldn't diagnose democracy generally based on one anomalous case.

Consider the Scandinavian model. Those countries have strong labor movements, comprehensive social provision, proportional representation, and vibrant public spheres. They haven't experienced the polarization afflicting the United States. Their legislatures are functional. Their representation is genuinely competitive. The lesson isn't that democracy is dying but that democracy requires proper institutional design.

Katznelson worries about alternatives to representation—populism, deliberative democracy, plebiscitary democracy—but these alternatives exist primarily in countries where representation has failed. Switzerland, with its strong referendum tradition, isn't drifting toward authoritarianism. Its direct democracy complements rather than replaces representation. The problem isn't direct participation but the absence of any effective check on executive power.

America's crisis may be fundamentally about institutional design, not democratic values. If we want functional democracy, we need to fix our institutions: adopt ranked-choice voting, end the Senate filibuster, establish public campaign finance, and reform the Electoral College. These changes are achievable. They don't require philosophical renewal, only political will. Katznelson's call for deeper debate about democratic foundations may be misdirected.

The invocation of Robert Dahl near the essay's beginning signals Katznelson's theoretical framework: pluralist democracy theory, with its emphasis on competition, participation, and polyarchy. This framework served well in the mid-twentieth century but has been substantially critiqued since. Katznelson would have written a stronger essay engaging with these critiques rather than treating Dahl as authority.

Post-Marxist theorists have argued for decades that formal political equality is undermined by economic inequality. Katznelson nods to this with his discussion of the "principle of non-convertibility," but he treats it as a contemporary discovery rather than a long-standing critique. C. Wright Mills, Charles Lindblom, and G. William Skinner all documented how business interests captured democratic politics. This wasn't hidden from view—it was the subject of sustained scholarly attention that Katznelson ignores.

The critique extends further. Robert Michels argued in 1911 that all organizations, including democratic ones, tend toward oligarchy. Schumpeter treated democracy as merely a method for selecting leaders, not a form of government. Postcolonial scholars have shown how Western democracy was imposed on societies with different political traditions. Feminist theorists have documented how "the people" in democratic theory systematically excluded women. Each critique finds democracy's formal promises betrayed by structural inequalities.

The evidence suggests otherwise. Economic inequality has increased dramatically since the 1970s. Social mobility has declined. The middle class has shrunk. If democracy requires equal citizenship backed by strong barriers against wealth's influence, as Katznelson argues, then democracy has failed and is failing.

The solution isn't institutional tinkering. It's economic democracy. Workplace democracy, cooperative ownership, strong unions, and comprehensive social provision would address the structural sources of political inequality. Katznelson barely mentions these alternatives. He's fixated on legislative procedure when the real question is who owns productive assets.

Citizens who came of age during the Bush administration's warrantless wiretapping, the financial crisis, and the Obama administration's drone strikes have no baseline assumption that government serves their interests. They expect dysfunction. They expect corruption. They expect the rich to get richer while everyone else suffers. This cynicism is rational given experience.

The institutions that might restore democratic faith—strong parties, independent media, civic associations—have atrophied. Party identification has declined. Newspapers have collapsed. Unions have shrunk. Civic organizations have lost members. The infrastructure of democratic participation that Tocqueville celebrated has been hollowed out. Without these mediating institutions, citizens face the state directly, without buffers or intermediaries.

The most striking feature of Katznelson's essay is its normative ambiguity. He clearly believes democracy is in trouble, but he never quite says what he thinks should be done. His conclusion calls for "a robust debate about these questions" but offers no answers. The essay operates within a liberal framework that treats democracy as instrument rather than end. Democracy is valuable because it produces good policy, respects rights, and enables legitimate collective decision-making. But what if democracy produces bad policy? What if majority preferences are illiberal or foolish? What if citizens prefer redistribution to the wealthy over their own interests? The liberal framework has no answer.

Perhaps democracy is valuable regardless of its consequences. This is the position of classical republicanism, which valued citizenship and participation as ends in themselves. Globalization has created a world where the most important decisions are made by institutions that lack democratic legitimacy. The European Union makes rules that affect 500 million people without meaningful European-wide democracy. The International Monetary Fund and World Bank impose conditions on borrowing countries. The tension isn't between democracy and authoritarianism but between different forms of democratic legitimacy. National democracy allows citizens to hold representatives accountable through elections. Supranational democracy, where it exists, relies on intergovernmental negotiation that insulates decisions from popular control. When citizens choose between these, they often prefer national democracy, even if it's less effective.

Katznelson treats Orbán's "illiberal democracy" as a threat to democracy. But Orbán's supporters might argue they're defending democratic self-determination against EU technocracy. The EU has imposed austerity on countries whose citizens didn't vote for those policies. It has restricted social provision through budget rules that no parliament adopted. It has extended market liberalization without democratic authorization. Perhaps Orbán's supporters are rejecting not democracy but external imposition.

This analysis doesn't excuse Orbán's attacks on press freedom and judicial independence. I want to conclude by pushing back against the essay's general tone of anxious hand-wringing. Katznelson writes as though democracy's problems are unprecedented and solutions unknown. But the challenges he identifies have been faced before, and responses have emerged.

The concern about money in politics has produced robust public finance systems in many democracies. The concern about legislative dysfunction has produced cabinet systems where the executive dominates parliament. The concern about citizen engagement has produced participatory institutions at local and regional levels. These aren't perfect solutions, but they demonstrate that democracy can adapt.

The comparison to the 1930s should inspire not despair but confidence. Demagogues then claimed that democracy couldn't solve problems. They were wrong. Democratic states in the 1950s and 1960s achieved unprecedented prosperity and security. They built welfare states that reduced inequality and provided security against life's contingencies. They regulated capitalism successfully enough to prevent the crises that had produced fascism.

Today's challenges are serious but not hopeless. Climate change requires rapid decarbonization, which is technologically achievable. Economic inequality requires redistribution, which is politically achievable. Political polarization requires deliberative institutions, which are institutionally achievable. The obstacles are political, not technical.

Katznelson is right that we need "a robust debate about these questions." But we don't need to invent the wheel. We can learn from other democracies that have addressed similar challenges. We can study the historical periods when democracy succeeded. We can build on existing institutions rather than treating democracy as something that must be rebuilt from scratch.

The scaffolding around the Capitol dome will come down. The building will resume its iconic form. So too with democracy: the repair is underway, and the conclusion is in sight. We need only maintain the confidence that our predecessors had when they faced similar crises and emerged stronger.
