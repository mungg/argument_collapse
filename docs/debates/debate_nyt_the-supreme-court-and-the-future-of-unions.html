Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Court's decision in *Harris v. Quinn* stopped short of overturning *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education*, but Justice Alito's majority opinion left little doubt about where a future case might lead. The narrower question — whether home-care workers paid through Medicaid are "full-fledged" public employees — was answered no. The broader question — whether anyone should be compelled to subsidize a union they have not joined — was left ominously open.

The case for fair-share fees rests on a simple bargain. Under exclusive representation, a union must bargain on behalf of every worker in the unit, member or not, and must represent non-members in grievance proceedings. Allowing workers to opt out of paying anything invites the free-rider problem the Court itself recognized in *Abood*: rational employees take the wage gains and protections without contributing to the cost of producing them. Over time, membership and revenue erode, and the union's bargaining capacity withers.

The First Amendment objection — that bargaining with government is inherently political — has rhetorical force but proves too much. Every contract a government signs involves public money and policy choices; by that logic, a defense contractor's lobbying dues would also be unconstitutional to compel. *Abood*'s compromise, splitting chargeable collective-bargaining costs from non-chargeable political expenditures, is workable and has been administered for nearly four decades.

If the Court eventually extends *Harris* to traditional public employees, the likely effect is what we have already seen in Wisconsin after Act 10: sharp declines in membership, revenue, and political clout, particularly for teachers' and service-worker unions. Public-sector labor, the last redoubt of American unionism, would be substantially weakened.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The recent Supreme Court decision in *Harris v. Quinn* is crafted narrowly, yet it cracks open a much larger debate about the future of public-sector unions. The ruling concerned a specific category of home-health workers, but the underlying question—whether non-members can be compelled to pay any fees to a union—is now squarely in the crosshairs. For decades, the answer under *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education* has been a careful yes: employees who decline full union membership may still be required to pay an “agency” or “fair share” fee covering the costs of collective bargaining, contract administration, and grievance processing. This arrangement is not just a convenience; it is the linchpin of stable labor relations.

The justification is straightforward. Federal law obligates a union that gains majority support to represent *all* employees in that bargaining unit, regardless of whether every individual joins. Without some form of mandatory fee, the workplace breeds an obvious free-rider problem. Workers can enjoy the wage increases, benefits, and job protections the union negotiates while contributing nothing, draining the union’s resources and sowing resentment among dues-paying members. Agency fees simply require everyone who benefits to contribute to the cost of securing those benefits, no different in principle from a mandatory bar association fee for attorneys or student activity fees at public universities.

If the Court were to extend its reasoning and strike down fair-share fees across the public sector, the effects would be seismic. In states that have already adopted “right-to-work” laws for government employees, union density and revenues have fallen sharply, not because workers reject collective representation but because it becomes economically rational to ride for free. A nationwide shift would starve unions of the stable funding needed to hire skilled negotiators, conduct research, and process grievances. The predictable result is not a harmonious workplace of freely choosing individuals, but a downward spiral in public-sector wages and working conditions, making it harder to attract and retain qualified teachers, police officers, and sanitation workers. The core mission of public services would suffer, as would the middle-class stability that public employment has historically provided.

The requirement for public employees to pay agency fees—even if they choose not to formally join a union—is fundamentally a matter of basic fairness. Under labor law, unions are legally obligated to provide exclusive representation to all workers in a bargaining unit. This means they must negotiate wages, secure benefits, and defend workplace grievances for everyone, members and non-members alike. Allowing workers to opt out of paying for these representation costs creates an unsustainable free-rider problem. If an employee benefits from a union-negotiated contract, they should contribute to the practical costs of securing it.

Critics of organized labor, and increasingly the conservative wing of the Supreme Court, argue that requiring these fees violates the First Amendment by compelling political speech. But this ignores the careful balance already established by decades of legal precedent. Under the 1977 *Abood* decision, non-members are already protected from funding a union’s political and ideological activities. They are only required to pay their "fair share" for the direct costs of collective bargaining and contract administration. Conflating the negotiation of a standard healthcare plan with partisan political lobbying is a deliberate legal strategy designed to defund labor.

If the logic of Monday's ruling in *Harris v. Quinn* is eventually expanded to encompass all public-sector workers, the effects on organized labor will be devastating. Public sector unions remain the last robust stronghold of the American labor movement. Stripping their ability to collect basic representation fees will severely drain their resources and diminish their bargaining power. This will ultimately depress wages and erode benefits for millions of teachers, firefighters, and civil servants who form the backbone of the middle class.

Monday's ruling in *Harris v. Quinn* was narrower than many feared, but the logic Justice Alito deployed should alarm anyone who cares about a functioning labor movement. The whole point of an "agency fee" is to solve a free-rider problem the law itself creates. Under exclusive representation, a union is legally obligated to bargain for every worker in the bargaining unit and to represent each of them in grievances, whether they pay dues or not. If those workers can opt out of paying, the rational move is to take the wage increases, the health benefits, and the grievance defense for free. Multiply that by enough workers and the union withers — not because workers have rejected it, but because they've been handed an incentive to cheat their colleagues.

That is precisely why the Court, for nearly four decades since *Abood*, has allowed public-sector unions to charge non-members for the costs of collective bargaining itself, while exempting them from the political and ideological spending they oppose. That compromise respects both the First Amendment and the basic economics of collective action.

To the question posed: yes, public employees who benefit from a contract should be required to contribute to the cost of negotiating and enforcing it. They are not being asked to join, to vote with the union, or to fund its politics. They are being asked not to free-ride on their coworkers.

The effect of eliminating agency fees, as the home-care workers in Illinois now know, will be exactly what its proponents intend: weaker unions, lower membership density, less bargaining leverage, and ultimately lower wages and thinner benefits. We already have a natural experiment in the "right-to-work" states of the South and Mountain West, where union density and wages both run conspicuously lower than in fair-share states.

Justice Alito went out of his way to call *Abood* "questionable" and to suggest the Court might revisit it in a future case involving "full-fledged" public employees. That invitation will be accepted. If and when the Court takes the next step, it will not be vindicating worker freedom — it will be constitutionalizing the free-rider problem.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

It is worth resisting the urge to treat *Harris v. Quinn* as either Armageddon or liberation. The decision is genuinely narrow, and the most interesting question it raises is not whether unions will survive but whether the Court is preparing to redraw the line between public employment and citizenship itself.

Justice Alito's majority opinion did not overrule *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education*. It carved out a category — "partial public employees" — to describe the Illinois home-care workers, who are paid through Medicaid but employed in private homes by the disabled and elderly clients they serve. Because they are not full state employees, the Court reasoned, the state's interest in "labor peace" is weaker, and the First Amendment burden of compelled fees is therefore unjustified. Full-fledged public employees — teachers, firefighters, transit workers — are not directly affected. Yet.

The "yet" is the interesting part. Alito spent pages questioning *Abood*'s reasoning, accusing it of resting on a misreading of precedent and a naive faith in the public/private distinction. He all but invited a follow-up case asking the Court to overrule *Abood* outright. The four dissenters understood this perfectly well; Justice Kagan's opinion reads like a brief for a future defense of fair-share fees.

So the right answer to the question — should public employees be required to pay fees? — depends on whether you think the public sector is meaningfully different from the political process. *Abood* said yes: when government acts as employer, it has managerial interests in stable bargaining that justify limited compelled support. The *Harris* majority is openly skeptical of that distinction, treating every interaction between a public union and the state as inherently political speech.

If *Abood* falls, the entire public sector becomes right-to-work overnight. Unions in states like New York, California, and Illinois — where density is high precisely because of fair-share rules — would face significant revenue losses and likely membership declines. Whether that is a feature or a bug depends on one's priors. But everyone watching this docket should understand: Monday was not the destination. It was a road sign.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

I have spent fifteen years organizing public-sector workers, and I want to push back gently on both sides of this debate. The doomsayers warning that the end of agency fees means the end of unions are underestimating workers. The libertarians celebrating "worker freedom" are pretending they aren't funding a coordinated, decades-long campaign by employers to dismantle collective bargaining. Both are missing what actually happens in a workplace.

Agency fees were a comfortable arrangement. They guaranteed unions a revenue base regardless of whether stewards talked to members, whether grievances got fought, whether the contract reflected what the rank-and-file actually wanted. In plenty of locals, that comfort produced sclerosis: staff-driven bureaucracies that processed paperwork, endorsed incumbents, and lost touch with the shop floor. If *Harris* — and whatever comes after it — forces unions to actually organize their own members again, that is not unambiguously bad news.

The labor movements that built American unionism in the 1930s did not have agency fees. They had house visits, shop-floor leaders, strike funds, and a culture of solidarity built one conversation at a time. Some of the most vital union locals I know today are in right-to-work states, where leaders cannot take a single member for granted and therefore actually behave like a movement.

That said, let's not pretend this is about freedom. The plaintiffs in *Harris* were funded by the National Right to Work Legal Defense Foundation, which is in turn funded by exactly the corporate and ideological donors who would also like to see the minimum wage abolished and OSHA defanged. The "dissenting worker" is a convenient face for a project whose real beneficiaries are employers who want a weaker counterparty across the table.

So: should workers be compelled to pay? In an ideal world, no — because unions would have organized them so thoroughly that nearly everyone joined voluntarily. In the world we actually inhabit, removing fair-share fees in the short term will hurt density and bargaining power. In the longer term, it may force a reckoning unions have been avoiding. Both things can be true.

The Supreme Court’s ruling in Harris v. Quinn strikes at the heart of an uncomfortable dilemma: how to square the free-rider problem in labor relations with the free-speech rights of dissenting employees. For decades, the court-sanctioned compromise has been the agency fee—non-members pay only for core representational activities, not for union political speech. The Court’s latest decision leaves that compromise battered but standing, while raising profound questions about the future of public sector unions. The right answer, however, is not found by simply celebrating or condemning this ruling; it lies in rethinking the legal regime that forces unions into the role of reluctant tax-collectors.

Pro-union advocates are right that when a union is legally required to represent every worker in a bargaining unit, allowing free-riding would eventually bankrupt it. But critics are equally right that forcing a public employee to fund an organization whose positions she detests—especially in the context of public employment where bargaining touches on core political questions—is deeply illiberal. The solution is to change the rules, not simply to fight over the fee.

Why should exclusive representation be mandatory? We could allow a union to represent only those who choose to join, with the government then negotiating separate contracts for non-members or handling their employment terms through civil service rules. That would eliminate the free-rider problem entirely, as unions would service only their own members. Or we could require periodic reauthorization votes, so unions must regularly earn the support of a majority to maintain their representational status. Such reforms would make unions more responsive and democratic, while respecting the autonomy of individual employees.

To be sure, weakening exclusive representation would dramatically alter the structure of public sector labor relations. Some see that as a feature, not a bug. But the point is that the current system—where unions are conscripted to negotiate for everyone and then forced to chase down fees from reluctant beneficiaries—satisfies no one. Harris v. Quinn’s narrow holding gives policymakers a reason to reexamine the foundational statutes. Rather than waiting for the Supreme Court to issue a blunt ruling that might destroy public sector bargaining overnight, state legislatures should start crafting new models that align representation, financing, and choice.

The decision applies only to a specific subset of workers—home health care aides and personal assistants whose employment relationship with the state is unusually diffuse. Moreover, the union movement has weathered existential threats before. If the Court eventually overturns Abood, unions will be forced to become leaner, more member-driven, and more explicitly focused on workplace needs rather than broader political crusades. That could actually produce a healthier labor movement. The current system, which relies on state-compelled extraction, has insulated some unions from the need to constantly prove their value to the people they represent.

This is not to minimize the blow that an adverse ruling in a case like Friedrichs would deliver.
