The Public Pension Problem

It’s been a bad week for public workers. On Tuesday, in a ruling that has implications for other cities, a federal judge ruled that Detroit’s public employee pensions are not protected in a federal Chapter 9 bankruptcy. On that same day, the Illinois legislature passed a deal trimming retiree benefits and increasing state contributions, providing a template for other states to follow.

Amid all this, is the continuing refrain that Wall Street fat cats are responsible for “looting the pension funds.” Are government retirees being forced to pay for pension fund problems caused by bad investments? If so, what’s the solution?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The "looting" narrative is emotionally satisfying but analytically thin. Yes, public pension funds have paid hefty fees to Wall Street managers, and some of those bets—especially the rush into alternatives like hedge funds and private equity after 2008—have underperformed net of fees. CalPERS, CalSTRS, and many state systems lost roughly a quarter of their value in 2008-09 and have spent years trying to claw back. But blaming financiers obscures the deeper causes of the crisis now engulfing Detroit, Illinois, New Jersey, and a dozen other jurisdictions.

The primary culprit is political, not financial. For decades, elected officials promised retirement benefits without funding them. Pension sweeteners were a cheap way to reward public unions because the bill came due on someone else's watch. Illinois, the most extreme case, simply skipped or shorted its annual required contributions year after year. New Jersey did the same under governors of both parties. When the actuarial assumptions—typically 7.5 to 8 percent annual returns—proved optimistic, the gap between promises and assets became unbridgeable. Detroit layered on top of this the catastrophic decline of its tax base.

A second cause is the discount-rate convention itself. Economists like Joshua Rauh and Robert Novy-Marx have argued persuasively that discounting guaranteed liabilities at expected risky returns dramatically understates the true cost. Marked to a more appropriate rate, unfunded liabilities nationwide are something like $3 to $4 trillion rather than the $1 trillion governments report. That accounting fiction encouraged the underfunding.

So what is the solution? It must be shared, and it has to be honest. Workers and retirees cannot reasonably be held entirely harmless when the alternative is municipal insolvency, but neither should they bear the full brunt of bad choices made by politicians and investment committees. The Illinois deal—trimming COLAs, raising retirement ages for younger workers, capping pensionable salary, and forcing larger state contributions—is a reasonable template, though it will likely be challenged under the state constitution's protection clause. Judge Rhodes's ruling in Detroit is more radical: it signals that federal bankruptcy can override state pension guarantees, which will sharpen the bargaining leverage of distressed cities everywhere.

Going forward, three reforms matter most. First, mandate honest accounting using risk-free discount rates for accrued benefits. Second, legally require full payment of annual contributions, removing the political option to skip. Third, move new hires toward hybrid plans—a smaller defined benefit combined with a defined contribution component—so risk is shared rather than dumped on taxpayers or retirees a generation hence.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The sight of retired firefighters and city clerks facing benefit cuts is painful, and the instinct to blame Wall Street is understandable. After all, the 2008 financial crisis vaporized trillions in value, and public pension funds, heavily invested in equities and opaque alternative assets, took a beating. Tales of exorbitant fees, pay‑to‑play scandals, and “looting” by money managers feed a narrative of victimized public servants. But this story, while emotionally potent, distracts from a harder truth: the pension crisis was decades in the making, and its root cause is not a handful of greedy bankers but a systemic failure of fiscal governance by state and local politicians.

The core problem is chronic underfunding. For years, governments made pension promises without setting aside the necessary money, relying instead on aggressively high assumed rates of return — often 8 percent or more — to make the long-term math appear manageable. When actual market returns inevitably faltered, the gap between promises and assets yawned open. Illinois, for example, skipped or shorted its pension contributions for decades, even as it borrowed to fund current services. Detroit’s pension funds were loaded with risky debt and illiquid real estate investments well before the 2008 crash, in part because the city needed higher yields to compensate for its failure to make adequate contributions. The Wall Street “looting” narrative obscures this political negligence. Yes, some pension officials were steered into high‑fee products they didn’t understand, and yes, there were disgraceful episodes of corruption. But the larger tragedy is that the system was built on a lie: that benefits could grow faster than the economy, underwritten by a magic investment portfolio that would never suffer a prolonged downturn.

Are retirees being forced to pay for these problems? In Detroit, where a federal judge ruled that pensions are not sacrosanct in bankruptcy, the answer is brutally clear. The Illinois deal, which raises the retirement age and caps cost‑of‑living adjustments, also imposes pain on current workers and future retirees. It is tempting to say they should not suffer for the mistakes of others. But the money simply isn’t there. If pension obligations are not adjusted, the burden falls entirely on current taxpayers — many of whom are less affluent than the retirees they would be paying — and on the very public services that those taxpayers rely on, like schools and police.

So what is the solution? First, we need honest accounting. That means discarding fantasy investment return assumptions and using a risk‑free discount rate to calculate liabilities, which would reveal the true scale of the shortfall. Second, there is no escape from shared sacrifice. Trimming cost‑of‑living adjustments for affluent retirees, as Illinois did, is more defensible than cutting core annuities for the most vulnerable. New employees should likely be enrolled in hybrid or cash‑balance plans that share risk more equitably between workers and taxpayers, though that does nothing to close the existing hole. Third, we must bar the political gimmicks that allowed underfunding in the first place — requiring binding amortization schedules and independent oversight of pension boards.

Scapegoating Wall Street may feel satisfying, but it won’t put a single dollar into a retiree’s pocket. The real culprits sit in statehouses and city halls, and the solution demands that we hold them accountable for promises they were never willing to fund.

In the wake of these rulings, it is tempting to blame Wall Street "fat cats" for looting pension funds and forcing retirees to bear the cost. While the 2008 financial crisis undoubtedly battered institutional portfolios, focusing the blame solely on financial managers is a convenient distraction. First, governments must adopt realistic discount rates that reflect actual market risks, rather than relying on fantasy projections. Second, municipalities must be legally bound to make their full annual required contributions, ending the era of borrowing from retirees to fund today's government operations.

Finally, while current retirees should be protected from severe poverty, modest adjustments to cost-of-living increases and higher employee contribution rates are unavoidable. For new hires, states must transition toward hybrid models or defined-contribution plans that distribute risk more equitably between the taxpayer and the employee. The Detroit ruling and Illinois reform represent two different but complementary approaches to a problem that cannot be blamed entirely on Wall Street. The "looting" narrative, while rhetorically convenient, obscures a more complicated reality.

The investment thesis has merit. Municipal pension funds did chase returns into increasingly exotic instruments, often driven by consultants and intermediaries who extracted fees while delivering underperformance. When markets collapsed in 2008, those who had been most aggressive in chasing yield suffered most. The financial industry's role in pushing these strategies deserves scrutiny.

But investment returns alone don't explain pension crises. The deeper problem is structural: governments routinely failed to make their required contributions, betting that investment returns would cover the gap. They promised benefits without funding them. Detroit's emergency manager correctly identified that legacy costs—benefits already earned—dwarf any investment missteps. Illinois, similarly, faces a crisis rooted in decades of contribution holidays and benefit expansions.

The solution requires addressing both. On investments, pensions need simpler, cheaper strategies—index funds rather than hedge funds. The Illinois model of increasing contributions while trimming future benefits makes structural sense if applied fairly. But retirees who were promised specific benefits shouldn't bear the full weight of political failures they didn't create. The Detroit ruling's hard line on pension protections may be legally sound, but it highlights how bankruptcy law was never designed for this reality.

The real villains are the political incentives that made underfunding the path of least resistance. Wall Street took its fees. Elected officials took credit. And now workers face uncertainty.

The cry that "Wall Street looted the pension funds" makes for satisfying rhetoric, but it obscures what actually happened. Public pension funds were not robbed at gunpoint by hedge fund managers. They were systematically underfunded for decades by elected officials who made promises they had no intention of paying for, used overly optimistic assumed rates of return (typically 7.5% to 8%) to mask the true cost of those promises, and—yes—then handed billions in fees to Wall Street to chase those returns through alternative investments that often underperformed plain-vanilla index funds.

Both things are true. Politicians made the mess. Wall Street is happily charging rent on it.

Detroit is the cleanest illustration. The city's pension funds, for all the talk of their being relatively well-funded, engaged in a series of disastrous deals, including pension obligation bonds and interest rate swaps that blew up in 2008. Emergency Manager Kevyn Orr's filings suggest the funds were also distributing "excess earnings" as bonuses to retirees in years when there were no real excess earnings to distribute. That isn't Wall Street's fault. That's governance failure.

Judge Steven Rhodes' ruling that Michigan's constitutional protection of pensions does not survive federal bankruptcy law is, legally, unsurprising—the Supremacy Clause is the Supremacy Clause—but it is a genuine earthquake politically. Every distressed municipality from Stockton to Providence now knows that Chapter 9 is a tool for pension impairment, not merely bondholder haircuts. Expect more filings.

The Illinois deal points to the other path: a negotiated, statutory restructuring that trims COLAs, raises retirement ages slightly, and locks in higher state contributions. Whether it survives the inevitable challenge under Illinois' famously ironclad pension clause is another matter; I suspect parts of it will not.

So what's the solution? Three things, none of them popular. First, mandate realistic discount rates—closer to the 4–5% range that private plans and economists like Joshua Rauh have long argued for. Honest accounting forces honest budgeting. Second, separate the benefits of current retirees, which deserve strong protection, from the accrual rates of future workers, which are legitimately on the table. A 55-year-old retiree cannot go back and renegotiate her career; a 30-year-old hire can plan around a less generous formula. Third, move new hires into hybrid plans with a defined-benefit floor and a defined-contribution component, as several states have already done.

Blaming Wall Street feels good. Fixing the actuarial fictions that politicians of both parties have hidden behind for thirty years would actually help.

The most useful thing to do after this week's news is to stop talking about "the pension problem" as if it were a single thing. It isn't. There are at least three distinct problems being conflated, and each has a different solution.

The first is the catastrophic case: places like Detroit, Stockton, San Bernardino, and possibly Chicago, where decades of population decline, eroding tax bases, and chronic underfunding have produced obligations that genuinely cannot be paid in full. Judge Rhodes' ruling acknowledges this reality. You cannot squeeze blood from a stone, and pretending otherwise just punishes whichever creditor happens to be last in line. Bankruptcy, however painful, is the honest answer here.

The second is the structural case: states like Illinois, New Jersey, Connecticut, and Kentucky, where the funds are deeply underwater but the underlying economies are perfectly capable of supporting the obligations if the political will exists. Illinois' new law is an imperfect first step. It will be challenged, and it will probably lose some pieces in court, but the basic shape—higher contributions plus modest benefit trims for future accruals—is roughly correct. These states have a political problem, not an arithmetic one.

The third is the design case: the rest of the country. Most state and municipal pension plans are reasonably funded, in the 70–85% range, which is below the gold standard but not catastrophic. For these plans, the question is how to prevent the slide that has consumed Illinois. The answers are well-known: lower discount rate assumptions, mandatory annual contributions, smoothing reforms, and probably hybrid plans for new hires.

What about Wall Street? The "looting" narrative captures something real—public funds pay outsized fees for mediocre alternative-investment returns, and a few funds have been victims of outright fraud or self-dealing. But the data is pretty clear that for the average underfunded plan, investment underperformance is a secondary issue. Contributions skipped or shorted by politicians dwarf the impact of bad investment years.

So what's the solution? Stop letting elected officials defer the cost of current compensation onto future taxpayers. That's the disease. Bankruptcy, benefit cuts, and "Wall Street reform" are all just symptoms management. Until states require full annual funding of accruals by statute—the way private plans must under ERISA—we will keep having this conversation every decade or so, with a different city playing Detroit's role.

There is something deeply wrong with a society in which a federal judge can announce, almost casually, that a 68-year-old retiree's pension is "just another unsecured claim" while the same legal system treated AIG's counterparties as untouchable in 2008. The Detroit ruling is not just a legal event; it is a statement about whose promises matter and whose don't.

I want to push back on the increasingly fashionable view that public pensions are simply unaffordable. The aggregate unfunded liability of U.S. state and local pensions is large—somewhere between $1 trillion and $4 trillion depending on whose discount rate you use—but the United States is a $16 trillion economy. We are not Greece. We are a country that chooses, repeatedly, to make these obligations seem unmanageable by refusing to raise the revenue to meet them.

Consider Illinois. The state has among the lowest flat income tax rates in the industrialized world and exempts retirement income entirely. It refuses to tax services. Its corporate tax structure is riddled with loopholes. The "pension crisis" in Illinois is, to a substantial degree, a revenue crisis dressed up in actuarial clothing. The new law trims benefits—including the cherished 3% compounded COLA that retirees were specifically promised—rather than asking the state's wealthy to pay more. That is a political choice, not an economic necessity.

The "Wall Street looting" frame is partly correct and partly misleading. Pension funds did pay enormous fees to private equity, hedge funds, and consultants who steered them into illiquid, opaque investments that often underperformed. Pay-to-play scandals from New York to New Mexico to California revealed a culture of corruption around public pension investment. And the financial crisis itself—a crisis made on Wall Street—knocked roughly a third off many funds' assets just as boomer retirements accelerated.

But the deeper looting was political. Every governor who skipped a contribution effectively borrowed from retirees at zero interest. Every legislator who voted for benefit sweeteners without funding them did the same.

The solution requires honesty in three directions. Honest accounting, with discount rates that reflect the riskless nature of the obligation. Honest funding, with required contributions that cannot be politically deferred. And honest taxation, with revenue structures that can actually pay for what governments have promised. Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The defined-benefit pension, as it has been practiced in American state and local government, is a fundamentally broken institution. It is broken because it asks elected officials with four-year horizons to fund obligations with forty-year horizons, and it is broken because it relies on actuarial assumptions that no honest financial economist endorses. Detroit and Illinois are not aberrations; they are the leading edge.

Consider the basic mechanics. A government promises a worker a pension. The cost of that promise is discounted to present value using an assumed rate of return on plan assets—usually 7.5% or 8%. This makes the obligation look small today. The politician who makes the promise gets the political benefit; the cost is deferred and disguised. Meanwhile, financial economists (Rauh, Novy-Marx, and many others) have argued that because the pension promise itself is essentially riskless—it's guaranteed, constitutionally in some states—it should be discounted at a riskless rate, perhaps 3–4%. Doing so roughly doubles or triples the reported liabilities.

This isn't an accounting curiosity. It is the difference between a system that is "mostly funded" and one that is catastrophically underwater. The honest measure is closer to $3–4 trillion in unfunded liabilities, not the $1 trillion typically reported.

So when commentators ask whether Wall Street caused this problem: no, not primarily. Wall Street collected fees, sold bad swaps, and did its usual rent extraction, but Wall Street did not invent the fiction that politicians could promise a guaranteed return on a portfolio of risky assets. That fiction is the heart of the public DB pension model.

The solution, painful as it is, is to migrate away from this model. Future hires in most state and local governments should be enrolled in either pure defined-contribution plans (like the federal Thrift Savings Plan) or hybrids with a small DB floor and a larger DC component. Utah did this in 2010. Rhode Island, under Gina Raimondo's reforms, moved partially in this direction in 2011. The result is not a loss of retirement security—DC plans, properly designed with low fees and lifecycle funds, produce perfectly adequate retirements—but a loss of the political incentive to underfund.

Existing retirees and accrued benefits are a harder question. Some impairment, as Detroit shows, may be unavoidable. But the larger lesson is forward-looking: stop digging the hole. The defined-benefit public pension was an experiment, and the experiment has failed.

Behind the legal and actuarial arguments lies a question we don't ask often enough: what is a pension actually for? In the postwar bargain, public workers traded wage growth and the upside of private-sector employment for security in old age. That trade was real. Compare a 30-year teacher's lifetime earnings to a comparably credentialed private-sector worker, and the pension is what makes the math come out roughly even. Strip the pension away mid-stream, and you haven't fixed a budget problem—you've torn up a contract retroactively.

This is why I find the Detroit ruling so disquieting, even though I understand its legal logic. The federal bankruptcy code, applied to a municipality, can override state constitutional protections that workers reasonably relied upon for their entire careers. The retired Detroit librarian who is 70 years old today made career and savings decisions in 1975 based on a constitutional promise. She cannot go back and unmake those decisions. The "solution" of cutting her $20,000 pension is, in a meaningful sense, theft by hindsight.

Now, are bad investments part of the story? Yes, and more than partisans on either side admit. The Detroit pension funds did engage in genuinely scandalous behavior—the "13th check" excess-earnings distributions, the swap deals, the interest-rate bets. Internal governance was poor. And across the country, the romance between public pension trustees and alternative-investment salespeople has cost beneficiaries billions in fees for performance that often trailed simple index funds. CalPERS itself has begun to acknowledge this and is pulling back from hedge funds.

But "looting" overstates it. The bigger problem was political: pensions were used as a hidden form of compensation that allowed politicians to give workers something without showing it in the budget. Both sides—union negotiators and elected officials—participated in this fiction. The bill arrived eventually, as bills do.

What's the way out? I'd suggest a grand bargain in three parts. First, hard protection for retirees already collecting benefits and for accrued benefits of current workers—amend federal bankruptcy law if necessary. Second, mandatory full funding of new accruals, with realistic discount rates and automatic contribution triggers. Third, a genuine conversation about whether some portion of future accruals should shift to hybrid structures, in exchange for the security promised on the rest.

That is harder than blaming Wall Street and harder than blaming pensioners. But it is what an honest reckoning would look like.
