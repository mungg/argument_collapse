Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Zittrain's diagnosis is bracing, and the trend lines he traces—from the Apple II to the iPhone, from open PCs to bot-infested zombies—are real. But the binary that organizes his essay, generative versus sterile, conceals more than it reveals, and several of his proposed remedies deserve sharper scrutiny than the diagnosis itself.

Start with the iPhone, which carries enormous symbolic weight in his argument. The device launched only fourteen months ago, and Apple has already announced a software development kit, due any day now. Whatever one thinks of Apple's gatekeeping, the trajectory complicates the claim that the iPhone is "precisely the opposite" of the Apple II. The interesting questions are not whether a platform is open or closed but how open, to whom, on what terms, and with what costs of exit. The Xbox 360 has a homebrew development pathway (XNA); even the locked-down TiVo has spawned a community of modifiers. Generativity is a continuum, and most successful platforms occupy a middle band where curation and openness coexist. Treating Apple's curation as a civilizational fork in the road risks misreading what is actually a fairly old pattern of platform governance.

This matters because Zittrain's solutions are calibrated to the binary. Take Herdict and StopBadware. Both are admirable, but they rest on a premise—that mainstream users will participate in producing security as a public good—that the previous twenty pages of his essay vigorously undermine. He notes that 81 percent of home PCs lack basic protection. If users will not click "update," why will they install a daemon that surveils their machines on behalf of the commons? Wikipedia works partly because writing an encyclopedia is intrinsically interesting; auditing one's own DLL files is not. The community-defense model may scale to a layer of skilled netizens, but skilled netizens already run grsecurity and read Bugtraq. The mass user is precisely the constituency it cannot reach.

His "Red PC / Green PC" virtualization proposal is more promising—indeed, products like VMware and Parallels already demonstrate the concept—but Zittrain glosses over the hardest part: the "checkpoint Charlie" between the two. Most malicious payloads now travel inside documents and browser sessions, exactly the data users will want to move between virtual machines. Sandboxing is a useful palliative, not a cure, and the more seamless we make it, the less it sandboxes.

The proposal that worries me most is the invitation for ISPs to filter at the gateway. Zittrain hedges, calling this a "stopgap," but stopgaps in network architecture have a way of becoming load-bearing walls. We are already watching Comcast throttle BitTorrent traffic and various national ISPs filter content under public-safety rationales. Once carriers are competent to inspect packets for viruses, they will be competent—and pressured—to inspect them for copyrighted material, for political content, for whatever the next moral panic demands. Zittrain spends pages worrying about appliancization at the endpoints while proposing exactly the kind of intelligence in the middle that the end-to-end principle was designed to prevent. The remedy concedes the patient.

What Zittrain gets profoundly right is that the cybersecurity problem is also a political problem, that "lockdown" is a choice rather than a fate, and that the social architecture around our machines matters as much as their code. The data-portability and appliance-neutrality proposals, in particular, deserve serious legislative attention. But preserving generativity will require resisting not only the seductions of the iPhone but also the temptation, evident even in this essay, to address openness's failures by quietly relocating control somewhere upstream.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Jonathan Zittrain has given us an elegant and troubling map of the digital crossroads we face. His history of the generative Internet—from the Morris worm to the botnet economy—shows why the openness that gave us the Apple II and the Web now threatens to undo itself. The diagnosis is bracingly clear. But when he turns to remedies, his prescriptions rely on a hope that I fear is fading even as he writes: that a diffuse community of netizens, armed with clever tools and a civic ethos, can secure the network without sacrificing its experimental soul. I want to share that hope, but the Internet of March 2008 looks far more unruly than the one his social-layer success stories describe. The garden of generativity is not being overrun by mischievous weeds; it is being strip-mined by organized crime, and a neighborhood watch will not be enough.

Zittrain draws inspiration from Wikipedia, where an active community, aided by version histories and bots, keeps vandalism at bay. But Wikipedia is a single, centralized platform with clear norms and a manageable scale of contributors who self-select for a shared mission. The open Internet is nothing like it. A PC connected to the Net faces threats from anonymous actors spread across dozens of jurisdictions, hardened by profit motives rather than adolescent curiosity. The botnets Zittrain rightly fears are not best understood as a tragedy of the commons; they are industrial-scale rackets. The going rate for a distributed denial-of-service attack is, as he notes, tens of thousands of dollars per day. Spam remains wildly profitable. These enterprises do not respond to gentle shaming or the good opinion of one’s peers. They exploit every weakness left open by a system that, by design, asks no one for permission. The kind of community policing that works for a wiki simply does not scale to an environment where the bad actors outnumber the sheriffs by orders of magnitude and have no stake in the community’s health.

The tools Zittrain champions—Herdict, StopBadware, Google warnings—are worthy experiments, but their scope is heartbreakingly small compared to the flood of new malware we read about every week. Even if Herdict provides a dashboard showing pop-ups and crashes per hour, the users most likely to install it are already the most security-conscious. The vast majority of home PCs, which a 2005 study found lacking even basic protection, will remain dark and unmonitored. The StopBadware partnership with Google flagged thousands of hacked sites, but millions more exist elsewhere, and the cleanup burden quickly overwhelmed the researchers. As long as the barrier to entry for writing and spreading bad code remains near zero—and as long as compromised machines can be bought and sold in bulk—these voluntarist measures will only trim the edges of the problem. Zittrain hints at this himself when he observes that antivirus vendors may not be able to withstand the onslaught, yet he still places faith in the emergence of new civic institutions whose membership would necessarily be a fraction of the user base. The asymmetry is too great.

His technical proposals face a similar hurdle: they ask too much of ordinary people. The idea of splitting a PC into Red and Green virtual machines, with a “checkpoint Charlie” for moving clean data, is ingenious. But it assumes a level of engagement and digital literacy that most users do not have and show no sign of acquiring. Indeed, the very frustration that drives people toward locked-down iPhones and Xboxes is precisely the demand that computing just work without constant housekeeping. A PC that requires you to understand that your Red environment is a sandbox you can reset is still, for most consumers, a PC that comes with homework. The flight to appliances is not only about security; it is about simplicity, about guarantees. When Steve Jobs says, “You don’t want your phone to be like a PC,” he is not just being paternalistic; he is reading the market. If the choice remains between a generative PC that demands eternal vigilance and a sterile appliance that prevents the worst disasters, millions will choose the latter—whatever insurance policy the PC theoretically provides.

Zittrain’s legal and policy interventions are more promising, but they tinker around the edges of the central crisis. Data portability requirements would indeed ease lock-in, but they do nothing to make the PC itself more secure. Appliance neutrality principles, borrowed from the Microsoft antitrust case, could prevent platform bait-and-switches, but only after a platform has first become open. That will not save the generative core of the Net if people have already abandoned it for the walled gardens. The deeper problem is that none of these measures directly alters the incentive structure that makes insecure code so lucrative and so easy to distribute. As long as the costs of infection are externalized to the millions whose machines are silently conscripted into botnets, market forces alone will not correct the imbalance. We may need to confront something Zittrain is reluctant to embrace: a modest, carefully targeted regime of intermediary responsibility.

Strict end-to-end neutrality is a fine ideal, but Zittrain concedes that ISPs might screen out viruses at the network level as a stopgap. That concession could be broadened. If ISPs were obligated—perhaps with safe-harbor protections modeled on the Digital Millennium Copyright Act—to offer basic network-level filtering for known malicious traffic, much of the botnet spam and scanning could be dampened without requiring every user to become a security expert. This is not an invitation to turn ISPs into content police, but a recognition that the endpoints have failed and will continue to fail. A limited, transparent, and auditable role for service providers could buy time for the generative tools Zittrain envisions to mature. It may be a calculated retreat from pure openness, but it is far less damaging than the wholesale migration of users to closed appliances, which is already underway.

The generative Internet is worth defending, but it will not be saved by exhorting millions of users to become netizens or by elegant technical Band-Aids. It will be saved—if it can be—by a pragmatic acceptance that the world of 2008 is not the fraternity of 1988. The hacker ethos is dead; the business model for bad code is robust. If we are to preserve any space for the next Apple II, we will likely have to draw firmer lines, make hard choices about limited network filtering, and accept that some of the wildness of the early Net is irretrievable. Zittrain’s essay is the best starting point for that conversation, but his remedies are the beginning of wisdom, not the end.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Jonathan Zittrain’s diagnosis of the current crisis facing the Internet is as timely as it is alarming. He correctly observes that the open, "generative" nature of the personal computer—the very quality that fueled the digital revolution from the Apple II to the widespread adoption of broadband—is now its greatest vulnerability. As organized crime leverages this openness to build massive botnets, consumers are understandably retreating toward the safety of locked-down, "tethered" appliances like the newly introduced iPhone. Yet, while Zittrain's diagnosis is flawless, his proposed cure relies on an overly optimistic faith in the civic capacities of the average Internet user.

Zittrain suggests that we can save the generative PC by migrating the collaborative, community-driven solutions that have worked at the content layer—most notably Wikipedia—down to the technical code layer. He envisions users deploying quick-reversion technologies and crowdsourced security dashboards like Herdict to act as a digital neighborhood watch. However, this analogy fundamentally misjudges the profound asymmetry of risk between text and executable code.

When a vandal defaces a Wikipedia page, the damage is localized, visible, and easily reversed with a few clicks. The cost of error is virtually zero. When a trusting user accidentally executes a malicious payload on their PC, the consequences are immediate and often invisible. Even if a user relies on the "Red/Green" virtual machine division Zittrain proposes, a compromised "Red" machine can still harvest keystrokes, steal financial data, and quietly participate in distributed attacks against critical infrastructure before it is ever rolled back to a safe state. The trial-and-error ethos that makes wikis so successful is simply far too dangerous when applied to the realm of operating systems and network security.

Furthermore, Zittrain’s reliance on "netizens" to collaboratively evaluate the safety of software places an unrealistic cognitive burden on the mainstream public. The computer users of 2008 are no longer the hobbyists of 1979. Today’s broadband subscribers view the PC as a consumer utility—a way to check email, shop, and watch videos. They do not possess the desire, let alone the technical literacy, to monitor data packets or consult crowdsourced dashboards before running an application. The shift toward tethered appliances is not merely a corporate power grab; it is a rational delegation of risk by consumers who desperately want someone else to act as the gatekeeper.

Because users cannot realistically serve as their own system administrators, the transition toward locked-down appliances and Web 2.0 services is likely irreversible. Consequently, the most crucial elements of Zittrain’s essay are not his technical patches, but his legal and regulatory proposals. If we must entrust our digital lives to the walled gardens of hardware manufacturers and web giants, concepts like data portability and "appliance neutrality" become essential safeguards. We must ensure that these platforms allow users to easily extract their personal data and that companies cannot arbitrarily bait-and-switch developers after achieving market dominance.

The era of the purely generative PC may be closing, victim to its own unmanageable security flaws. We cannot reverse this tide by expecting the social dynamics of an online encyclopedia to secure our network infrastructure. Instead, our immediate challenge is to design robust legal frameworks that hold the architects of our new, tethered appliances accountable to the public interest.

Jonathan Zittrain is right to identify the central danger: the security crisis is not merely that computers are vulnerable, but that our likely responses to vulnerability may destroy the very openness that made networked computing valuable. The temptation to solve insecurity by replacing the personal computer with the appliance is powerful. Consumers do not wake up wanting “generativity.” They want e-mail that works, photos that are not lost, bank accounts that are not emptied, and a phone that still makes calls after they try a new program. If the open platform cannot offer ordinary users a tolerable level of safety, they will rationally choose the closed one.

But the preservation of generativity cannot depend too heavily on turning users into netizens. That is an admirable civic aspiration, and there are domains—Wikipedia is the obvious example—where the analogy works. Yet vandalism in an encyclopedia and malware on a home computer differ in one crucial respect: the costs of participation are radically unequal. A Wikipedia editor can revert a bad edit after seeing it. A computer user may not know that a machine has been compromised, may not understand what process is sending outbound mail, and may be unable to distinguish a legitimate software update from a forged one. Worse, the costs of one user’s inattention are often imposed on others: infected machines send spam, host phishing pages, and participate in denial-of-service attacks. Civic tools can help, but they will not be enough if the basic allocation of responsibility remains so poorly matched to capacity.

The right question, then, is not only how to preserve openness, but how to assign security work to the actors best positioned to perform it while preserving room for unapproved innovation. Today that means taking seriously the roles of operating-system vendors, browser makers, application developers, ISPs, universities, employers, and payment networks—not just end users. The end-to-end principle has served the Internet well, but end-to-end should not become an excuse for dumping all risk onto the least sophisticated endpoint. If a broadband provider can identify a customer machine sending hundreds of thousands of e-mails a day, it should not treat that fact as someone else’s problem. If a browser can warn users away from a site distributing hostile code, it should. If an operating system can make routine work possible without administrator privileges, it should default to that safer condition.

The difficult part is distinguishing protective intervention from gatekeeping. Zittrain’s “least harm to generativity” principle is a useful test, but it needs institutional teeth. Network-level filtering, for instance, may be justified for known spam relays or fast-spreading worms, but it should be transparent, contestable, and narrow. Users should be told when traffic is blocked; technically sophisticated users should have some ability to opt out; and blocking rules should be published at least in general form. Otherwise emergency security measures will become convenient infrastructure for copyright enforcement, political censorship, or commercial discrimination. The same is true of Google’s malware warnings. They are valuable precisely because search engines can change incentives faster than legislatures can. But because they can also crush a small site’s traffic overnight, there must be quick review, clear standards, and a path to correction.

I am also less persuaded that “easy reversion” for PCs can carry as much weight as Zittrain hopes. Version histories and virtual machines are important, and the wiki analogy is illuminating. But malware is not merely a bad edit. A compromised machine can leak passwords, silently alter financial transactions, join a botnet, or encrypt and hide itself before the user even knows there is something to revert. Reversion protects integrity better than confidentiality. Once a tax return, trade secret, or address book has been copied, rolling the machine back does not roll the secret back into the box. Virtualization can reduce damage, but only if users understand the boundary between red and green environments—and experience suggests that users will constantly be asked to move data across that boundary. The hard problem will be making the safe path the easy path.

That suggests a more prosaic agenda than either lockdown or digital civic republicanism: usable security by default. Machines should ship with automatic updating turned on. Applications should be sandboxed where possible, but with documented ways for users to grant additional privileges. Operating systems should make it visible, in ordinary language, when software wants to run at startup, contact the network, alter browser settings, or install additional components. Software installation should resemble informed consent more than a scavenger hunt through a license agreement. The goal should not be to prevent users from taking risks, but to prevent deception and to make risky choices intelligible.

Law has a role here, but it should be aimed less at punishing every author of bad code—many will be unreachable—and more at changing incentives for reachable firms. Deceptive installation practices, undisclosed data collection, and intentionally difficult uninstallation are consumer-protection problems, not just technical nuisances. Vendors that bundle adware or bury material terms in unreadable agreements should face consequences. Companies that hold user data should provide export mechanisms. Platforms that invite developers and users to invest in them should not be able to revoke access arbitrarily once dependence has formed. These interventions do not require government to design the Internet; they require it to police fraud, lock-in, and bait-and-switch behavior.

The best future is not a pure one. We will have open PCs, semi-open phones, closed game consoles, hosted applications, and networks that sometimes intervene. The task is to keep these layers in tension so that none becomes the sole point of control. Generativity will survive not because every device is open, but because enough devices remain open, enough data remains portable, enough networks remain neutral, and enough users can choose experimentation without courting disaster. Zittrain is right to warn that security can become the rationale for a sterile information environment. The answer is not to romanticize insecurity, but to make openness safer than it has been.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Jonathan Zittrain has identified what may be the defining technological dilemma of our era: the Internet and personal computer were designed for openness and generativity, yet this very openness makes them vulnerable to exploitation. His diagnosis of the three eroding safeguards—hacker ethos, professional administration, and the absence of economic incentives for malicious code—is persuasive, and his warning about the slide toward locked-down information appliances deserves serious attention. Yet I want to push back on the optimism embedded in his proposed solutions, particularly those relying on community participation and shared norms.

Zittrain's analogy to Wikipedia is seductive. Wikis thrive despite open editing because reversion is easy and the community policing is robust. He proposes applying this model to PC security through tools like Herdict and collective code evaluation. But the analogy breaks down in important ways. Wikipedia's openness creates problems—vandalism, copyright infringement—that are genuinely reversible. A malicious piece of code, once executed, may permanently compromise data, steal credentials, or enroll a machine in a botnet. The cost of failure is asymmetric: a revert on Wikipedia costs a few keystrokes, while a compromised PC may require the complete reinstallation Zittrain himself describes as the new normal. Community solutions work when the community can respond faster than bad actors can act. The malware ecosystem, powered by economic incentives Zittrain documents, moves with alarming speed.

Moreover, the community solutions he champions depend on participation by users who have the knowledge and motivation to contribute. By March 2008, the digital divide was not merely about access but about sophistication. The users most likely to download and run Herdict are precisely those already running antivirus software and keeping their systems patched. The users most vulnerable—those with older systems, no antivirus, and limited technical knowledge—are least likely to participate in collective defense. A generative solution that only the generative already understand excludes those who need protection most.

Zittrain acknowledges this limitation but resolves it by invoking a "civic institution" model: users as netizens contributing what they can, even if not all can fight fires. This is admirable, but it sidesteps the question of enforcement. Wikipedia succeeds not merely because of contributor ethos but because the Wikimedia Foundation has legal ownership of the platform and can establish terms of service. Who owns the ecosystem of PC security? The security software industry is fragmented and competitive; academic initiatives like StopBadware have limited reach; and users have little transparency into what their machines are doing. Without some form of coordination authority—technical, legal, or both—the collective action problem Zittrain identifies may not be solvable through exhortation alone.

This brings me to the appliance question. Zittrain rightly laments the iPhone's locked-down model and praises Android's user-programmable openness. But by March 2008, Android had existed for mere months as an announced platform, with no devices commercially available. The jury was very much out on whether a genuinely open mobile platform could succeed against Apple's curated approach. Zittrain's faith in Android as evidence of ongoing generative possibility seems premature. The market had spoken, and it was speaking in favor of simplicity over openness. Whatever the merits of that preference, pretending it was not real seemed Pollyannaish.

None of this means Zittrain's framework is wrong. The generative principle remains essential, and his proposed legal interventions—data portability, appliance neutrality, software liability reform—deserve serious consideration. But implementing these requires more than inspiring users to act as netizens. It requires legal frameworks, industry coordination, and perhaps government intervention in ways Zittrain seems reluctant to endorse. The thousand cuts he describes may not be stopped by a thousand individual acts of goodwill.

The Quiet Surrender of End-to-End

Jonathan Zittrain has written a careful and largely persuasive diagnosis of the generative Internet's predicament. Anyone who has watched the slow drift from open PCs toward locked-down devices, or who has tried to clean a relative's malware-encrusted laptop, will recognize the problem he describes. And yet, beneath the irenic prose, Zittrain is asking us to give up something we should not give up lightly: the end-to-end principle. His proposal that "strict loyalty to end-to-end neutrality should give way to a new principle asking that any modifications to the Internet's design or the behavior of ISPs be made in such a way that they will do the least harm to generative possibilities" sounds reasonable. It is also, I would argue, the beginning of a road we have already seen other countries travel, and it does not end where Zittrain hopes it will.

Start with the immediate proposal: ISPs as virus filters. The intuition is straightforward. Endpoints can't be trusted to defend themselves; the network sits in a position to inspect traffic; therefore let the network filter what users plainly do not want. The trouble is that "what users do not want" is precisely the kind of judgment that will not stay technical for very long. Once the carriers have built the inspection apparatus and won the regulatory permission to deploy it, that apparatus is available for whatever filtering the political moment demands. We have already watched Comcast quietly throttle BitTorrent in 2007 — not in the name of security but in the name of network management, which is the same justificatory grammar Zittrain is endorsing. Once the principle is conceded, the principle is conceded for all comers.

This is not paranoia about hypothetical futures. The Chinese filtering system is built on exactly the kind of deep packet inspection Zittrain proposes ISPs use against malware. The European data-retention directive of 2006 obliges carriers to log traffic; it does so easily because the inspection infrastructure already exists. The U.S. government's warrantless surveillance program, exposed in 2005, depended on the cooperation of carriers at precisely the kind of gateway points where Zittrain wants screening to occur. When you build a chokepoint, you should expect everyone with a stake in chokepoints to come knocking. The RIAA already has. So does every government that has ever wanted to know who is talking to whom.

Zittrain knows this. He acknowledges, almost in passing, that "such network screening theoretically opens the door to undesirable filtering." But he reassures us that the speculative risk must be balanced against the growing threat to generativity. I think this gets the calculation exactly backward. The threat to generativity from malware is real but partial: my Aunt Sally may indeed buy an iPhone next, but Aunt Sally was never going to write code anyway. The threat to generativity from a normalized regime of network-level inspection is structural and difficult to reverse. It changes who is allowed to send what to whom, and it changes which intermediaries the state can lean on.

There is also a subtler problem with the case for ISP filtering: it concedes the diagnosis to those who are most interested in changing the architecture for their own reasons. The telephone companies have wanted to be more than dumb pipes for at least a decade. The content industries have wanted carriers to inspect traffic for infringing files since Napster. Law enforcement has wanted predictable insertion points for surveillance since the CALEA debates of the 1990s. All three constituencies will read Zittrain's essay as an invitation, however much he hedges, and they will read his caveats as the soft refusal that comes before the eventual yes.

What is the alternative? Zittrain himself has identified part of it, and it sits uneasily next to his ISP proposal. Herdict, StopBadware, virtual machines with "Red" and "Green" partitions, easy reversion — these are endpoint-centric solutions. They preserve the end-to-end architecture by relocating the locus of defense back to where the end-to-end principle always said it should be: at the endpoints, supplemented by voluntary community institutions. If Zittrain believes these tools can work — and his essay is at its most hopeful when he describes them — then the case for cracking the end-to-end principle weakens proportionately. We should pick a lane. Either the endpoint can be defended through generative, community-based means, or it cannot. If it can, then ISP filtering is unnecessary. If it cannot, then Herdict will not save us either, and we are headed for the appliance future regardless.

A second alternative worth more attention is liability reform aimed not at carriers but at software vendors. The reason Microsoft Windows ships in a state where 81 percent of consumer machines lack effective firewalls is partly that the EULA disclaims all responsibility for the consequences. We would not tolerate this from an automaker. We could, without touching the network at all, require minimum-security defaults, mandate prompt disclosure of vulnerabilities, and impose a duty of care on platform vendors whose products serve as vectors for botnet conscription. This addresses the actual source of the problem — insecure endpoints sold as if they were finished products — while leaving the network alone.

Zittrain is right that the choice between a generative PC and a locked-down appliance is one consumers should not have to make. But the answer is not to make the network resemble the appliance. The answer is to make the endpoint worthy of the network it sits on, and to defend the principle that the bits in the middle are nobody's business but the sender's and the recipient's. The generative Internet was built on that principle. It will not survive its abandonment, however gently the abandonment is dressed.

Generativity Is Not Enough: The Case for Serious Security

Reading Zittrain's essay, one is struck by an odd asymmetry. He paints, accurately, a picture of catastrophe: thirty million machines under criminal control, antivirus vendors who admit they may not be able to keep up, patch windows measured in months, an entire economy of badware now monetized through spam and extortion. Then, having walked us up to the edge of this abyss, he offers a response built largely around volunteer community tools, browser-based warning labels, and toolkits that report PC vital signs to a central authority. It is as if he had diagnosed congestive heart failure and prescribed a brisk walk.

The community-tool approach has been the implicit policy of the Internet for fifteen years. The Computer Emergency Response Team, the CVE database, the antivirus industry, the patient work of Bruce Schneier and others to educate the public — these are all expressions of the volunteerist, distributed-responsibility model Zittrain wants to extend. And here is the uncomfortable fact: it has failed. The data Zittrain himself cites is the data of failure. Not failure in the sense of "we are losing some battles"; failure in the sense of "organized crime owns one in ten home machines and the trend is worsening." The community ethos that policed the early Internet did not scale. There is no reason to believe a marginally improved version of it will scale now.

Consider what Zittrain proposes. Herdict will gather telemetry from voluntarily participating users and produce reputation signals for new code. Who installs Herdict? Sophisticated users. Who runs the compromised machines? Unsophisticated users. The population that needs the protection is precisely the population that will not download the tool, and even if they did, they would not know how to interpret its outputs. This is not a quibble. It is the central defect of every voluntary-adoption security scheme of the past decade. SpamAssassin works for technically savvy administrators; spam still arrives in your mother's inbox. PGP exists; almost nobody uses it. Firewalls ship with Windows XP SP2; the 81-percent figure Zittrain quotes tells us how that has gone.

If we take the security problem as seriously as Zittrain says we should, we need to consider tools he is unwilling to consider. Three in particular.

First, mandatory security standards for consumer software. We require seatbelts in cars, child-resistant caps on medication, and electrical safety standards on appliances. The reason consumer operating systems ship in insecure default configurations is that vendors face no liability for the externalities their products impose on the rest of the network. A regulatory floor — minimum default firewall settings, mandatory automatic patching for security updates, a duty to disclose vulnerabilities within a defined window — would impose costs on Microsoft, Apple, and the Linux distributions, but it would also reduce the population of compromised endpoints by orders of magnitude. Zittrain worries about the chilling effect on generativity. I worry about the chilling effect of botnets on the willingness of ordinary users to do anything online at all.

Second, ISP-level responsibility, including the disconnection of compromised machines. Several Australian and Finnish ISPs have begun quarantining infected customers — not filtering content, but notifying users and limiting bandwidth until the infection is cleared. This is not censorship; it is the network analogue of a public health quarantine. The end-to-end principle was never meant to mean that an endpoint pumping out a million spam messages an hour has an inviolable right to do so. Zittrain gestures at this with his call for ISPs to do "virus screening," but he is too tentative. The right model is not screening; it is accountability. If your machine is part of a botnet, your carrier should be empowered, and indeed required, to interrupt your service until it isn't.

Third, identity and authentication at the network layer. Spam, phishing, and most fraud rely on the cheapness of forged identity. Zittrain rightly observes that the hacker ethos has dissolved as the user base has grown, but he draws the wrong inference. The inference is not that we need to rebuild that ethos through Wikipedia-style community institutions; the inference is that anonymity at scale, in a system without reputation, produces the tragedy of the commons we are now living through. This need not mean abolishing pseudonymity — there are cryptographic schemes that allow accountable pseudonymity — but it does mean that the architecture's blithe assumption that every packet is as legitimate as every other has to change.

Zittrain will object that all of this risks generativity. So it does. But generativity is not a terminal value; it is an instrumental value, justified by the goods it produces. If those goods are being eaten alive by a parasitic criminal economy that the generative architecture itself enables, then the calculus has changed. We do not preserve the openness of a city by refusing to have police; we have police precisely so that the openness is bearable. The Internet has been operating as a city without police, on the theory that the residents would behave themselves. The residents have not behaved themselves.

There is also a political point worth making. The longer the legitimate security problem festers, the more drastic the eventual intervention will be. If we will not consider seatbelt-style regulation now, while the problem can still be addressed with measured tools, we are inviting the kind of post-catastrophe legislation that genuinely will wreck the Internet. The Patriot Act is the model here, not the counterexample. A serious, calibrated security policy now is the best protection against an unserious, draconian policy later. Zittrain's hopeful volunteerism, however appealing, will not buy us that protection. It will buy us, at most, the dignity of saying we tried something gentle before the heavy hand arrived.

The False Binary of Sterile and Generative

Jonathan Zittrain has built his argument around a dichotomy: the generative PC, descendant of the Apple II, versus the sterile appliance, exemplified by the iPhone and Xbox. The PC, in his telling, accepts code from anywhere; the appliance accepts only what its manufacturer permits. The future hangs in the balance between these two paradigms. I want to suggest that the dichotomy, while rhetorically powerful, does not survive contact with the actual landscape of digital devices in early 2008, and that the prescriptions that follow from it are correspondingly off-target.

Begin with the iPhone, Zittrain's central exhibit. The iPhone shipped in mid-2007 as a closed device. But within months a community of jailbreakers had produced functional third-party software, and Apple's response was telling: not the legal warfare one might have expected from a determined gatekeeper, but a series of cat-and-mouse software updates, and then — last October — the announcement that an official software development kit would ship in February 2008. We are now living through that release. Whatever else one wants to say about Apple's terms, the iPhone is becoming a platform for third-party developers, on a faster timeline than the Macintosh did in the 1980s. Zittrain's essay acknowledges the SDK in a parenthetical and moves on, but this is the very evidence that complicates his thesis. The "sterile appliance" did not stay sterile. Market pressure, developer pressure, and consumer pressure converted it.

The same story repeats elsewhere. The Xbox 360 has been the target of homebrew communities since launch, and Microsoft announced XNA Game Studio Express in 2006 specifically to allow non-Microsoft developers to write games for it. The TiVo has spawned an entire ecosystem of hacks, mods, and at least one quasi-supported developer program. The Kindle, just released, accepts user-uploaded content via a documented (if clunky) path. Even my mobile phone runs Java applets I can install over the air. To describe these devices as belonging to a single category called "sterile" and another called "generative" is to flatten distinctions that matter.

A more accurate picture would describe a spectrum, with at least three meaningful axes: who can write software for the device, who decides what gets installed, and how reversible the lock is. The Apple II scores high on all three: anyone could write, the user decided, and there was no lock to reverse. A perfectly closed pacemaker would score low on all three. The interesting devices — iPhone, Xbox, Android phones being announced as we speak — sit at various intermediate points, and crucially, they move along these axes over time, often toward greater openness rather than away from it. The historical trajectory Zittrain warns against, in which "appliances tethered to a network of control" displace generative endpoints, is genuinely contested by these movements.

Why is the appliance opening up? Because the same generative pressures Zittrain celebrates in PCs also operate on appliance manufacturers, just with a delay. A closed device that resists third-party innovation forgoes the network effects that come from a developer ecosystem. The iPhone with an SDK is more valuable than the iPhone without one — to users, to Apple, and to AT&T — because the long tail of niche applications turns a phone into a personal computing platform people will pay more for and switch carriers for. Apple is not being forced into openness by regulators; it is being pulled there by the same logic that made the Apple II succeed where the Lisa did not.

This matters for policy because Zittrain's prescriptions assume the appliance threat is exogenous — an external force that must be checked by legal innovation like "appliance neutrality." But if the threat is partially self-correcting through ordinary market dynamics, then heavy interventions like obligating Microsoft to allow third-party code on the Xbox after some bait-and-switch tipping point are solutions to a problem that is partly solving itself. Worse, such interventions may discourage exactly the experimentation with intermediate models — moderated platforms, curated app stores, sandboxed runtimes — that we should want to see. The walled garden model, of all things, may turn out to be a generative compromise: it admits third-party code while screening out the worst of the malware that Zittrain rightly worries about. App store curation is, in a real sense, the StopBadware idea built into the platform itself.

Zittrain also underestimates how much generativity has moved up the stack. He nods to "Web 2.0" but treats it largely as a constraint — software-as-service whose generativity is contingent on platform vendors. This understates what has happened. Facebook's developer platform launched in May 2007 and within months had tens of thousands of applications. The mash-up culture around Google Maps, Flickr, and Twitter's nascent API has produced more end-user-facing innovation in two years than the Windows ecosystem did in some five-year stretches. Yes, these platforms can withdraw permission. So can Microsoft revoke a digital signature, and so could the long-dead BBSes of the 1980s shut down their phone lines. Conditional generativity is still generativity, and the relevant question is not whether the platform vendor could in principle become hostile, but whether the conditions under which it would do so are economically plausible.

The deeper point is that "generativity" is not a property of a single device, viewed in isolation. It is a property of an ecosystem, and ecosystems contain mixes of open and closed components. The Internet itself runs on closed router firmware. The Apple II ran on closed Microsoft BASIC. The Web runs on browsers some of which are open and some of which are not. The question is not whether any given component is generative, but whether the system as a whole leaves room for innovation by unprivileged participants. By that measure, 2008 is, despite the malware plague, a more generative moment than 1998.

None of this is to dismiss Zittrain's worry. The trends toward tethering are real, and the regulatory choices made now will shape how the next decade unfolds. But the framing of generative vs. sterile, hero PC vs. villain appliance, forecloses the more interesting and more accurate question: how do we cultivate the conditions under which platforms, of whatever architectural shape, continue to invite outside contribution? That question has many answers, and very few of them look like the legal mandates Zittrain proposes.

What Zittrain Misses About Software Freedom

There is a word that appears, by my count, only once in Zittrain's essay, and even then in a passing reference: "free software." For someone writing a book-length argument about the openness of computing platforms, this is a striking omission. The free software movement — and more broadly the open source movement that grew out of it — has, for twenty-five years, been the most sustained, most organized, and most consequential effort to preserve exactly the kind of user autonomy Zittrain wants to defend. To frame the question of computing freedom around "generativity" while marginalizing the people and institutions that have actually been fighting this battle is to miss the point.

Consider Zittrain's central anxiety. He worries that PCs will become locked down, that users will lose the ability to install code from arbitrary sources, that platforms will reserve to themselves the power to determine what software runs. But this is precisely the diagnosis Richard Stallman offered when he founded the GNU Project in 1983, and the GNU/Linux operating system is the world's primary working response to it. A user running Debian today owns her machine in a way no Windows user does. She has not only the legal permission but the practical tools to inspect, modify, and replace any component of the system, including the kernel. If the worry is that "the PC will itself have become an information appliance, not easily reconfigured or extended by its users," then the answer is staring us in the face: use a PC that respects your freedom to reconfigure it.

Zittrain dismisses this in a single sentence: "Many technologically savvy people think that bad code is simply a Microsoft Windows issue. They believe that... 'better' counterparts (GNU/Linux and Mac OS, or the Firefox and Opera browsers) can help shield a user. But the added protection does not get to the fundamental problem, which is that the point of a PC—regardless of its OS—is that its users can easily reconfigure it to run new software from anywhere." This is a non-sequitur. The point is not that GNU/Linux is invulnerable to malware (no system is). The point is that the social and technical institutions of free software produce a fundamentally different relationship between the user and the platform. The package manager, the cryptographically signed software repository maintained by a distributed community of volunteers, the public review of every line of code — these are not minor implementation details. They are an answer to exactly the problem Zittrain is worried about, an answer developed over two decades by people who took the problem seriously before it was fashionable.

Why does Zittrain not engage with this? I think it is because his framing of the problem is fundamentally consumerist. He treats the PC user as someone who clicks icons, downloads programs, and either makes good or bad decisions about installation. The free software answer is not to give that user better warning labels or community telemetry; it is to change the user's relationship to the system by giving her access to a curated, transparent, accountable supply chain of software. Most Debian users have never been infected by a Windows-style virus, not because they are technically more skilled (many are not), but because the institutional structure of the system makes infection vastly harder. The repository model is the StopBadware idea, except that it works.

The omission is more than philosophical. It distorts the policy recommendations. Zittrain proposes changes to copyright and patent law "to help free software" — and these proposals, particularly the statute of limitations reform, are welcome. But his treatment is brief and defensive, as if free software were a fragile dissident movement requiring protection. In fact, free software is the institutional infrastructure on which much of the generative Internet runs. Apache serves most of the world's web pages. Linux runs Google. The BIND server resolves nearly every DNS query. To treat this as a side issue while devoting paragraphs to "Red" and "Green" virtual machines is to misallocate analytical attention.

There is also a normative point. Zittrain's "generative" language is curiously agnostic about the structure of the platforms whose generativity he wants to preserve. A proprietary platform can be generative, in his sense, if its vendor permits third-party software. Windows is generative. iPhone, post-SDK, will be generative. The Facebook platform is generative. But these are all generative at the sufferance of a vendor who can revoke permission at any time, and Zittrain's whole essay is, at bottom, an extended worry about exactly that contingency. The free software movement's answer — give users not just permission but rights, embedded in copyright licenses they can enforce — is the right answer to that worry. The GPL is not just a license; it is a legal architecture that makes generativity non-revocable. No vendor can iBrick a properly licensed free program, because the user has source code and modification rights as a matter of law.

If you want to preserve generativity, in other words, the question is not how to nudge proprietary vendors toward better behavior through "appliance neutrality" rules. It is how to ensure that users have alternatives whose generativity does not depend on vendor good will. That means: promoting free software in education, government procurement, and public infrastructure; defending the legal validity of copyleft licenses against the increasing pressure of software patents and DRM circumvention laws; reforming the DMCA so that users can lawfully unlock their own devices; and rejecting the entire trusted-computing apparatus that threatens to remove from users the root authority over their own hardware.

These would be substantively more demanding interventions than the ones Zittrain proposes. They would also be more effective, because they address the structure rather than the symptoms. The reason consumers buy iPhones rather than openly programmable phones is not that they have weighed and rejected generativity; it is that the market has not produced an equally polished free alternative. Public policy can shift that. Zittrain's essay would have been more valuable had it taken that possibility seriously.

Let the Market Decide What "Generative" Is Worth

Jonathan Zittrain's essay is animated by an assumption that deserves to be challenged directly: that consumers who choose locked-down devices over generative PCs are making a choice they would not make if they understood what they were giving up. He acknowledges that "it is entirely reasonable for consumers to factor security and stability into their choice," but then adds, "it is an undesirable choice to have to make." The whole prescriptive program — appliance neutrality mandates, data portability rules, ISP responsibilities, statute-of-limitations tweaks for software copyright — flows from a conviction that the market, left to its own devices, will deliver a worse outcome than enlightened policy could deliver.

I think this is wrong as both economics and politics, and I want to defend a different position: that the rise of appliances alongside generative PCs is a healthy market response to genuine consumer preferences, that the regulatory remedies Zittrain proposes are likely to do more harm than good, and that the generative Internet's best protection lies not in clever legal architecture but in the continuing usefulness of generative platforms to the people who actually value them.

Begin with what the data tell us. PCs are not disappearing. Worldwide PC shipments grew roughly 13 percent in 2007. The Linux community is healthier than at any point in its history, with Ubuntu pushing toward genuine mass-market viability. Web development tools have proliferated; the cost of starting a Web service has collapsed; cloud computing services like Amazon EC2 (launched 2006) have made the resources needed to build generative platforms accessible to teenagers with credit cards. The thesis that the PC and the open Internet are being eclipsed by appliances has to confront an awkward fact: every category of generative activity Zittrain cares about is growing, not shrinking. Blog creation, open source contributions, indie game development, podcasting, Web 2.0 mashups — all up sharply since 2003.

What is also growing is the appliance market. iPods sold roughly 50 million units last year. The DVR has become standard equipment. Smartphones are reshaping mobile communication. But this is not a substitution story; it is a complementarity story. The same household that bought a TiVo also bought a laptop. The teenager with an iPod also runs Firefox. Appliances are filling niches that PCs filled poorly — they are not eating PCs alive. To treat the success of appliances as a threat to the PC ecosystem is to misread the market structure. Appliances and PCs occupy different parts of the consumer surplus curve, and consumers happily buy both.

Why might consumers reasonably prefer appliances for some purposes? Because the marginal generativity Zittrain wants them to value is worth less to most people than the convenience and reliability the appliance delivers. This is not false consciousness. It is the ordinary operation of revealed preference. The retiree who wants to read email and look at grandchildren photos derives almost no value from the ability to write and install a kernel module. The teenager who wants to play music does not need her iPod to also be a development environment. The appliance is a rational specialization, and the existence of specialized devices does not threaten the existence of general-purpose ones any more than the existence of toasters threatens the existence of ovens.

Now consider Zittrain's proposed remedies. "Appliance neutrality" — a legal regime obligating platforms that have accepted third-party code to continue doing so — sounds elegant but is administratively a nightmare. When has a platform "welcomed" third-party contribution sufficiently to trigger the obligation? How does a regulator decide that a security-motivated restriction on third-party code is really a bait-and-switch? The Microsoft antitrust case Zittrain analogizes to consumed a decade of litigation and produced ambiguous results; replicating that machinery across every consumer device category would impose enormous costs while delivering uncertain benefits. The result would be predictable: platforms would refuse to open at all, lest opening trigger ongoing legal obligations they cannot quantify in advance. The mandate that aims to preserve generativity would, by its own incentive structure, reduce it.

Mandatory data portability has similar problems. As a default rule it is sensible, and most major services have moved in this direction voluntarily — Google's Data Liberation Front, Yahoo's data export tools, and the various OpenID initiatives are evidence that competitive pressure is doing the work. A legal mandate, by contrast, would have to define what "data" means in contexts where the very concept is contested (Is your social graph yours? Is metadata derived from your behavior yours?). And it would freeze the answer at whatever point in the evolution of services regulators happen to codify.

The deeper objection is to the underlying premise that "we" — meaning policymakers — need to step in to preserve a particular architectural vision. The generative Internet was not built by policy. It was built by hobbyists, researchers, entrepreneurs, and standards bodies operating largely without government direction. When governments did intervene — antitrust against AT&T in 1982, the NSFNET acceptable-use policy, the limited-liability provisions of Section 230 — the interventions were structurally modest and aimed at removing barriers to private action rather than mandating particular outcomes. Zittrain's proposals tilt toward the more ambitious kind of intervention, the kind that purports to shape technological development in the public interest. The track record of such interventions in fast-moving industries is poor.

What should we do instead? Mostly, what we are already doing. Defend Section 230 and the DMCA's safe harbor provisions, which preserve room for new platforms to emerge without crippling intermediary liability. Resist regulatory capture by incumbent telcos who would prefer to ration network access. Reform software patents, which are a real and growing burden on small developers. Keep antitrust law alive as a backstop against monopolization. And, mostly, trust that the same conditions that produced the generative Internet — low barriers to entry, plural platforms, distributed expertise, and a competitive market — will continue to produce it, so long as we do not over-engineer the rules.

Zittrain's essay reads as if the generative Internet were a delicate plant that could die at any moment unless tended by skilled regulators. I think it is closer to a weed: hardy, opportunistic, and remarkably good at finding cracks in the pavement. We should be careful not to pull it out by trying too hard to help it grow.

The Wikipedia Analogy, and Why It Has to Be Pushed Harder

The most arresting move in Zittrain's essay is the analogy from Wikipedia. Faced with vandalism, copyright infringement, and lies, Wikipedia did not respond by locking its doors. It built community institutions: easy reversion, page histories, watchlists, talk pages, and an evolving normative culture in which a critical mass of editors took responsibility for the encyclopedia's integrity. The result is messy, contested, sometimes wrong, and yet works astonishingly well at scale. Zittrain wants to do for PC security what Wikipedia did for encyclopedia content: build social and technical institutions that distribute responsibility for the platform's health across its users.

I am persuaded by the analogy. I want to argue, though, that Zittrain stops short of its implications. If we take seriously the lesson that distributed community institutions can govern complex, contested resources better than top-down authority, then we should be more ambitious than Herdict and StopBadware suggest. We should be building the institutional plumbing of digital citizenship, and we should be doing it now, while the door is open.

Begin with what Wikipedia actually got right. It is not, despite the romantic narrative, simply that "anyone can edit." Anyone could edit a chalkboard in Times Square; that does not produce an encyclopedia. What Wikipedia got right was the interaction of three things: a low-friction editing interface, a structural commitment to transparency (every change visible, every history navigable), and a community that took ownership of the resource. The first two are technical. The third is sociological, and it is the hardest to manufacture deliberately. Wikipedia's community emerged in part by accident, in part because Jimmy Wales and the early editors made some shrewd cultural choices, and in part because the project had time to incubate before its problems exceeded its volunteers.

The PC security problem does not have that luxury. The botnets are already at scale; the criminal economy is already mature; the population of vulnerable users is already enormous. We cannot afford the decade Wikipedia had to find its footing. So if we want to apply the Wikipedia lesson here, we need to think hard about how to seed and accelerate the community institutions Zittrain rightly identifies as the answer.

Three thoughts on what that might look like.

First, the institutions need to be visible and legitimate, not merely useful. Wikipedia works partly because users see it; they understand they are using something a community built. The StopBadware warning that appears in a Google search result is, by contrast, almost invisible — most users do not know who wrote it or why, and certainly do not see themselves as participants in its construction. If we want the security analogue of Wikipedia, the equivalent of "edit this page" needs to be present in the interface. When my browser warns me that a site is suspicious, there should be a one-click path to seeing why, to contributing my own assessment, and to viewing the deliberations of the community that made the judgment. Without that, we will have built a curated authority service, not a generative civic institution.

Second, the institutions need to be plural. One of Wikipedia's underappreciated features is that there are other Wikipedias — Citizendium, Scholarpedia, Conservapedia, project-specific wikis — competing for editor attention and providing alternatives when Wikipedia gets something wrong. The security space has been dominated by a small handful of antivirus vendors and a small handful of academic projects. Plurality matters because no single institution should be in a position to declare what is "bad code," any more than the Encyclopaedia Britannica should be in a position to declare what counts as knowledge. StopBadware is a good start, but its centralization is a vulnerability. A healthy ecosystem would include several competing or complementary projects with different methodologies, governance structures, and biases. Users could choose, and the choosing itself would be educational.

Third, the institutions need to invest seriously in what I will call "civic onboarding." Wikipedia did not produce its million editors by accident. It produced them by making the entry point unusually shallow: see a typo, fix it, watch your fix become part of an article read by millions, feel the pull of contributing more. Most security tools have no analogous onramp. Herdict, as Zittrain describes it, is admirably designed in this regard — it asks ordinary users to report ordinary observations — but the political and educational work of converting passive Internet users into active netizens has barely begun. This is where Zittrain's call for "artistically and intellectually skilled people of goodwill" lands, and it is the part of his argument that deserves the most amplification. The generative Internet will not be saved by lawyers or by engineers. It will be saved, if at all, by teachers, writers, librarians, and civic organizations that help ordinary people see themselves as stakeholders.

There is a deeper challenge here that Zittrain underplays. Wikipedia governs a resource — encyclopedia content — that does not, in itself, threaten anyone's wallet or safety. When a Wikipedia entry is wrong, the damage is reputational and reversible. When a security judgment is wrong, the damage may be financial, may be permanent, and may attract litigation. The institutional design problem for community-based security is therefore harder than the problem Wikipedia solved. It involves liability, due process, and adversarial actors with resources Wikipedia vandals do not have. We will need not just norms but procedures, not just talk pages but appeals processes, not just bots but auditable governance.

None of this is reason to abandon the Wikipedia analogy. It is reason to take it more seriously than Zittrain does. The right response to the generative Internet's crisis is not to layer community garnishes on top of an architecture still owned by vendors and carriers. It is to build civic institutions of comparable seriousness and durability to the institutions that govern our physical commons — water boards, fire departments, juries — and to embed them in the digital infrastructure as legitimate, contestable, plural, and visible actors. That is a big project. It will take a generation. We should start now, because the alternative Zittrain describes — appliances and tethered services owned by their makers — is not a future the next generation will be able to undo.

We need a fundamental rethinking of who is responsible for the security of the systems that mediate our lives. Zittrain offers a menu of incremental reforms. We need a theory of security liability that actually assigns costs to those who can prevent harms.

Zittrain's essay reads like a eulogy for a world that never quite existed. The generative Internet of his imagination—a space of open experimentation governed by shared norms and civic spirit—was always a minoritarian project. The mass Internet of the 1990s and 2000s was never governed by hacker ethics. It was a commercial space from the beginning, and it worked reasonably well precisely because commercial incentives aligned with user interests in ways that Zittrain's communal vision never could.

The PC revolution was not, primarily, a revolution in openness. It was a revolution in convenience. The Apple II was successful not because it was a blank slate but because it was a finished product that people could buy and use. The spreadsheet made the PC indispensable to businesses. The web browser made it indispensable to everyone else. Openness was a feature of the early PC, but it was never the reason most people bought one.

Zittrain correctly identifies that the three historical controls on malware have weakened. But his diagnosis is incomplete. The hacker ethos weakened not because the Internet community grew too large but because it was never as strong as he remembers. Morris was not a principled rebel—he was a careless graduate student who got lucky. The few genuinely malicious viruses of the 1990s were written by people who operated outside any community norm. The community that Zittrain mourns was always smaller and less influential than he implies.

The real lesson of the past twenty years is that market forces, not communal norms, have delivered the security improvements we have seen. Antivirus software, firewalls, and secure browsing features are products. Their development has been driven by consumer demand and competitive pressure. The malware problem is serious, but it is less serious than it would be in a world without commercial security products.

Zittrain's proposed solutions suffer from a common flaw: they require collective action that the market provides more efficiently. The Herdict project depends on users voluntarily reporting data about their systems' behavior. But users already have antivirus software that does this. They already have automatic updates that patch vulnerabilities. The marginal value of community reporting is small compared to the value of professional security research, which the market funds.

The essay's vision of "netizens" who "identify and belong" to the generative Internet is nostalgia dressed as prophecy. Real Internet users identify with their email providers, their social networks, and their streaming services. They do not identify with the protocol layer, and asking them to do so is not a realistic basis for security policy.

The end-to-end principle should be defended, not qualified. Zittrain suggests that "strict loyalty to end-to-end neutrality should give way to a new principle" that permits ISP filtering. But the reason we have end-to-end neutrality is that centralized control is more dangerous than distributed risk. An ISP that screens viruses also knows what you are doing. That knowledge can be abused by the ISP, by hackers who compromise it, or by governments that subpoena its records. The cure is worse than the disease.

The generative Internet does not need saving by netizens. It needs to be left alone.

Zittrain's essay is resolutely American. Its frame of reference is the Morris worm, the Apple II, and American antitrust law. But the Internet is global, and the security challenges it faces are global too. Any solution that ignores international dimensions is incomplete at best.

The botnet problem is not an American problem. The machines that compose botnets are distributed across every country with Internet connectivity. The command-and-control servers that direct them are often hosted in jurisdictions with weak law enforcement or deliberate non-cooperation. The criminals who profit from them are increasingly based in countries where cybercrime is not a priority. Zittrain's proposed solutions—community-based code assessment, data portability requirements, appliance neutrality mandates—all presuppose a legal and institutional environment that exists primarily in the United States and Western Europe.

Consider the Google/StopBadware initiative that Zittrain praises. It works by identifying hacked websites and warning users not to visit them. This is a reasonable response to a localized problem. But it does nothing against the command-and-control infrastructure of botnets, which is typically hosted in countries like China, Russia, or Romania, where law enforcement either lacks the capacity or lacks the will to take action. The criminal economics of botnets depend on this jurisdictional fragmentation. Zittrain's solutions do not address it.

The same applies to his discussion of "tethered appliances." The iPhone is an American success story, but it exists in an ecosystem that includes millions of Android phones running modified versions of the open-source Android OS, sold by manufacturers in Korea, Taiwan, and China. The regulatory framework for these devices varies enormously across jurisdictions. Data portability requirements, as Zittrain proposes, are meaningful only if they are enforceable across borders. They are not.

The essay's discussion of legal reform is similarly parochial. Zittrain proposes changes to the U.S. statute of limitations for software patent infringement. He notes that the Communications Decency Act shields hosting companies from liability for user-provided content. These are American law questions with American law answers. But the entities that write malware, operate botnets, and host phishing sites are not primarily constrained by American law. They operate in jurisdictions where American law does not apply.

What would an international approach look like? It would begin with recognition that the Internet's security problems are fundamentally jurisdictional problems. The solutions require international cooperation that does not yet exist. The Council of Europe Cybercrime Convention is a start, but it has been ratified by fewer than fifty countries and is toothless against non-parties. We need binding international agreements on cybercrime investigation, on the liability of ISPs for traffic that passes through their networks, and on the responsibility of software vendors for security flaws in their products.

Zittrain is right that "the deciding factor in whether our current infrastructure can endure will be the sum of the perceptions and actions of its users." But users in different countries have different perceptions and face different constraints. An American user who is compromised by malware can buy a new computer. A Vietnamese user who is compromised may have no recourse at all. The security problem is global; the solutions must be global too.

The essay's most poignant passage is its invocation of the "neighborhood watch" as a model for Internet security. I grew up in a neighborhood where the watch existed on paper and in newsletters but never actually deterred crime. The watch worked only when crime was visible, when neighbors knew each other, and when the police were responsive. None of these conditions hold on the Internet.

Zittrain wants us to believe that community-based solutions can substitute for structural reform. They cannot, and the reasons are social rather than technical. The early Internet worked because its users formed a community of sorts—small, self-selected, and united by shared expertise and shared values. This community produced norms that constrained bad behavior. It also produced the Morris worm, which suggests that the norms were never as robust as we remember.

The mass Internet has destroyed the conditions for communal governance. Users are not a community; they are a market. They are connected by commercial relationships rather than social ones. Their participation is mediated by platforms—Google, Facebook, Amazon—that have every incentive to extract value from their attention and no incentive to cultivate their civic capacities. The neighborhood watch required neighbors who knew each other. On the Internet, we are strangers passing in the dark.

Zittrain acknowledges this but offers hope: "tools that allow us to express and live our civic instincts online." The problem is that we do not have civic instincts online, or at least not the kind that Zittrain imagines. We have the instincts of consumers. We want things to work. We want them to be safe. We do not want to contribute our bandwidth and processing power to collective security experiments, because that would require us to understand what we are contributing and why, and most of us cannot.

The essay's vision of "netizens" is a fantasy projected onto users who have never identified as such. Wikipedia works because it offers contributors recognition, intellectual satisfaction, and the occasional ego boost. It does not work because contributors feel a civic duty to the encyclopedia. Zittrain's proposed security tools would offer no such satisfactions. They would ask users to contribute data about their own systems' vulnerabilities for the benefit of strangers. This is not civic participation. It is unpaid labor for an abstract collective good.

The real question is whether we can create institutions that make civic participation attractive rather than obligatory. The answer lies not in tools but in incentives. Zittrain's Herdict project might attract participants if it offered them something—reputation, discounts on security products, social connections. But he presents it as a pure public good, sustained by the generosity of strangers. This is not how public goods work.

We need security institutions that are funded, accountable, and effective. This means commercial security firms, regulated by consumer protection law, competing on the basis of actual protection rather than marketing. It means software vendors held liable for preventable vulnerabilities. It means ISPs that bear some responsibility for the traffic that flows through their networks. These are not glamorous solutions. They do not tap the power of community or express our civic instincts. But they might actually work.
