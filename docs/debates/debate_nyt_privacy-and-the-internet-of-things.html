What are some of the most exciting possibilities for Internet-connected objects, and how can we minimize the risks that come with them?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The promise of the Internet of Things is real, but the discussion so far has tilted toward gee-whiz demos at the expense of hard thinking about who owns the data these devices generate and who is responsible when they fail. Both questions deserve more attention than they're getting.

Start with the possibilities. Networked sensors in the home can cut energy use significantly — the Nest thermostat is only the most visible example. Connected medical devices and pillboxes can keep elderly people in their homes longer and flag dangerous drug interactions before a pharmacist would. Municipal sensors on bridges and water mains could catch failures before they become disasters. Industrial applications — what GE has been calling the "industrial internet" — may matter more in dollar terms than any of the consumer gadgets that grab headlines.

But the recent FTC settlement with TRENDnet, whose insecure baby monitors let strangers peer into hundreds of homes, is a useful warning. The problem isn't that someone built a bad product; it's that the incentives in this market push almost every vendor toward the same mistakes. Shipping a connected dishwasher is a hardware company's first software product, and security is treated as a cost center. Default passwords, unencrypted streams, and firmware that will never be patched are the predictable result.

Three things would help. First, the FTC should make clear — and Congress should ratify — that "reasonable security" is an enforceable obligation for any device that collects or transmits personal data, with safe-harbor guidance for small manufacturers. The agency's recent workshop on the IoT is a start; the principles need teeth. Second, we need a meaningful data-minimization norm. A connected scale does not need to retain every weigh-in on a vendor's servers forever, and a smart meter does not need to share appliance-level usage with third parties without consent. The Fair Information Practice Principles are forty years old and still adequate to the task if anyone bothers to apply them.

Third, and most neglected: we need to think about what happens when the company behind your thermostat or door lock goes bankrupt or simply loses interest. A web service can disappear and you lose your bookmarks; a discontinued IoT device can leave you with a brick on your wall or, worse, a permanent vulnerability on your home network. Some form of escrowed firmware or mandated end-of-life disclosure may be necessary.

None of this requires slowing innovation. It requires treating connected objects as what they are — durable goods that double as surveillance infrastructure — and writing rules that match. The alternative is a decade of TRENDnet stories, followed by the heavy-handed regulation that always arrives after public trust collapses.

**The Promise Is Real, But So Is the Peril**

The Internet of Things will be transformative, and it's worth naming what's actually exciting about it. A pill bottle that reminds an elderly parent to take her medication—and quietly alerts a caregiver when she doesn't—could keep millions of people in their homes longer. A municipal water system that detects leaks in real time could save billions of gallons. Running shoes that track gait could help injured athletes recover. Thermostats that learn your patterns can cut household energy use by double digits. These aren't science-fiction gadgets; many already exist.

But the baby-camera horror story from earlier this year, when a Houston family discovered a stranger shouting at their toddler through a hacked Foscam, illustrates the structural problem. The companies racing to network everything are not, by and large, security companies. They are appliance makers, toy makers, sneaker companies. Many ship products with default passwords, no encryption, and no mechanism for pushing security updates. Once the device is in your home, it's frozen in time—vulnerabilities and all.

Minimizing the risk requires three things. First, the FTC should treat insecure connected devices as an unfair trade practice, as it began doing in the TRENDnet settling earlier this year. That sends a market signal. Second, we need basic baseline standards: forced password changes on setup, encrypted communications, and a commitment to patching for a defined product lifetime. Third, consumers need labeling—something like an Energy Star rating for security—so that the market can reward responsible manufacturers rather than the cheapest.

The deeper privacy question, beyond hackers, is what the manufacturer itself does with the data. A connected dishwasher knows when you're home; a connected car knows where you drive. Without enforceable data-minimization rules, every object becomes a surveillance node operated by someone whose business model you didn't sign up for. That's the conversation we haven't really started having yet.

**Slow Down**

Before we get carried away imagining the wonders of a refrigerator that orders milk, let's ask a simpler question: do we actually want this? The Internet of Things is being sold to us as inevitable, but inevitability is a marketing strategy, not a fact.

Consider what we are being asked to accept. Every object in your home becomes a data-emitting node. Your toothbrush reports your brushing habits to a server in a country you can't name, run by a company you can't sue, protected by a privacy policy you didn't read and couldn't enforce if you had. The dishwasher knows when you eat. The thermostat knows when you sleep. The car knows where you went. Aggregated, this is a portrait of your life more intimate than anything the NSA programs revealed this summer have produced—and it will be held by private actors with even fewer constraints.

The standard response is that consumers can choose. But choice presumes meaningful alternatives, and the trajectory of consumer electronics suggests that within a decade, buying a "dumb" television or a "dumb" car may not be possible. We will be opted in by default.

The standard response is also that regulation can fix the harms. Perhaps. But our regulatory apparatus has not even managed to pass baseline data-protection legislation for the web we already have. The idea that Congress will competently regulate networked toasters strains credulity.

I am not arguing we should ban the Internet of Things. I am arguing we should stop treating each new product launch as an unalloyed good and start asking, product by product, whether the convenience genuinely outweighs the surveillance cost. For a pacemaker that detects arrhythmia and calls 911, yes. For a fork that tweets your dinner, probably not. Discrimination, not enthusiasm, is the virtue we need.

**Treat Connected Devices Like Cars, Not Like Software**

When Microsoft ships a buggy version of Word, the consequence is annoyance. When Ford ships a defective brake system, the consequence is death—and we hold automakers to a standard of strict liability, mandatory recalls, and federal safety regulation. The Internet of Things sits awkwardly between these two regimes, and right now it is being treated as software when it should be treated as a physical product.

This matters because the software industry has been allowed to externalize the costs of its insecurity for decades. End-user license agreements disclaim all warranties. Breaches are met with apologies and credit monitoring. The legal architecture assumes the user accepts the risk. That arrangement was tolerable, barely, when the worst consequence was a hijacked email account. It is intolerable when the device in question is the lock on your front door, the insulin pump in your body, or the camera over your child's crib.

The framework already exists. The Consumer Product Safety Commission regulates dangerous goods. The NHTSA mandates auto recalls. The FDA has begun, tentatively, to assert authority over networked medical devices—witness its draft guidance this June on cybersecurity for medical equipment. Extending product-liability principles to connected consumer goods would shift the incentives dramatically. If a manufacturer were on the hook for foreseeable harms caused by their unpatched device, they would patch it. If they could be forced to recall a vulnerable product, they would design it to be patchable in the first place.

Yes, this raises costs. Yes, it might slow the breathless pace of product launches. Good. The promise of the Internet of Things is genuine, but it will only be realized if consumers can trust that the objects in their lives are not also weapons aimed at them. Liability is how we have always built that trust for physical things. Connected objects are physical things.

**The Killer App Is Healthcare**

Lost in the hand-wringing about hacked baby monitors is the most genuinely revolutionary application of networked objects: continuous, ambient health monitoring. We are within a few years of a world where chronic disease management—the single largest driver of healthcare spending in this country—is fundamentally reorganized around connected devices.

Consider diabetes. A continuous glucose monitor talking to an insulin pump talking to a smartphone, with anomaly detection in the cloud and a clinician alerted on dangerous trends, is not a luxury gadget; it is a functioning artificial pancreas, and it will save lives. Heart failure patients with networked scales and blood-pressure cuffs can be caught days before a decompensation that would have sent them to the ICU. Parkinson's patients wearing motion sensors generate data that lets neurologists tune medications with a precision that ten-minute office visits never permitted. The Pew studies on the "quantified self" movement suggest patients want this; the question is whether the system delivers it well.

The privacy stakes here are obviously high. Health data is the most sensitive category we have, and HIPAA—written in 1996—does not contemplate a world where your shoes know more about your gait than your physician does. The data flowing from a consumer-grade fitness tracker to a third-party app is, in most cases, not covered by HIPAA at all. That is an obvious gap.

But the solution is not to abandon the technology; it is to extend the protections. Health data should be regulated as health data regardless of which device collects it. Patients should own their streams and be able to revoke access. Secondary use—selling fitness data to insurers, for example—should require explicit, unbundled consent.

Get this right and we transform medicine. Get it wrong and we create a discriminatory data economy that punishes the sick. The choice is in front of us, now.

**Security Through Design, Not Through Regulation**

Much of the current debate assumes that the answer to insecure connected devices is more rules from Washington. I'm skeptical. Regulation written today will be obsolete the day it passes, and the FTC's track record on technology issues suggests that any standards it imposes will reflect last year's threat model. The better answer is architectural.

The fundamental problem with most current IoT devices is that they were designed as if they would sit on a trusted home network. They weren't—they sit on the open internet, often with a public IP, often with admin credentials of "admin/admin." This is not a regulatory failure; it is an engineering failure, and it has engineering solutions.

First, default-deny networking. A baby monitor has no business accepting unsolicited connections from arbitrary IP addresses. Manufacturers should design devices that phone home to a controlled cloud service and only accept connections relayed through it, authenticated end to end. This is how modern videoconferencing works and there is no reason a webcam should be any different.

Second, automatic, signed firmware updates. The model where a consumer is expected to log into a router-like web interface and manually flash new firmware is broken. Devices should update themselves, silently, with cryptographically signed code from the manufacturer.

Third, hardware-backed identity. Every device should ship with a unique key pair burned into a secure element at the factory. No shared default passwords. No master keys. Compromise of one device should not compromise a million.

None of this requires legislation. It requires that platform providers—Apple, Google, Amazon—who are building the hubs through which connected devices will operate, impose security requirements on the ecosystem. Apple does this aggressively in its App Store; the same model can work for HomeKit-style frameworks. Market gatekeepers, not regulators, are the most effective enforcers here, because they can iterate at the speed of the threat. Let them.

**The Real Risk Isn't Hackers, It's Companies Operating Legally**

Stories about strangers hijacking baby monitors are vivid, but they distract from the larger and more durable threat to privacy in the Internet of Things: the entirely lawful collection, aggregation, and sale of intimate behavioral data by the companies that make and operate the devices.

Consider the asymmetry. A hacker who breaks into your Foscam commits a federal crime under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. The company that sold you the camera, however, can write a privacy policy granting itself the right to record everything the camera sees, share it with "business partners" undefined, retain it indefinitely, and modify the terms unilaterally—and this is all perfectly legal. The hacker is a felon. The manufacturer is a business.

This is the structure of the surveillance economy as it stands. We extended it from web browsers to smartphones with barely a whimper, and we are now poised to extend it to every physical object in our lives. A connected scale knows your weight. A connected lock knows your comings and goings. A connected vibrator, sold by a Canadian company that made headlines this year, knows things even more personal. Every one of these data streams has commercial value, and in the absence of comprehensive baseline privacy legislation—which the United States, unlike Europe, does not have—there is no default rule preventing its monetization.

The European Data Protection Regulation, now under negotiation, gets the principles right: purpose limitation, data minimization, meaningful consent, a right to deletion. American debates remain stuck on a notice-and-choice model that everyone admits is fictional. No one reads the policies; no one can negotiate them; no one has a meaningful alternative.

If we want a connected future that doesn't also become a panoptic one, the work isn't primarily technical. It's legal. We need a baseline privacy law, and we needed it ten years ago.

**Smart Cities Are the Prize Worth Fighting For**

The most exciting frontier of the Internet of Things isn't in our homes—it's in our streets. Consider what becomes possible when a city's infrastructure becomes sensor-rich and networked. Parking spaces that report their occupancy can eliminate the estimated 30 percent of urban traffic that consists of drivers circling for a spot. Streetlights that dim when nobody is present can cut municipal energy budgets significantly. Trash bins that report when they're full let sanitation crews skip the empty ones. Air-quality sensors at the block level let asthmatic residents plan their commutes. Bridges with strain gauges can warn engineers before they fail.

Cities like Santander in Spain and Songdo in South Korea are already experimenting with this. Closer to home, Chicago's planned array of environmental sensors is a modest version of the same idea. The aggregate gains—in energy, in time, in safety, in quality of life—dwarf anything we will get from a smarter refrigerator.

But cities are also where the privacy stakes become collective rather than individual. You can decline to buy a connected thermostat. You cannot decline to walk down a street that is densely instrumented with cameras, microphones, and Bluetooth sniffers. The data generated by smart-city infrastructure is data about everyone who passes through it, and the governance questions—who holds it, who can subpoena it, how long it is retained, whether it can be combined with face recognition—are public questions that cannot be resolved by clicking "I agree."

This argues for a different kind of approach than we use for consumer products. Smart-city deployments should be subject to mandatory privacy impact assessments before deployment, with public comment. Data retention should be short by default. Identifying data should be discarded at the point of collection wherever possible; aggregate counts, not individual traces, satisfy most legitimate municipal uses.

We can have the efficiency and the freedom both, but only if cities are forced to design for both from the start. Retrofitting privacy onto a surveillance system that already exists has, historically, never worked.
