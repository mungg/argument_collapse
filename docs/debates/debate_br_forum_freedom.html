Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Aziz Rana's essay offers one of the most ambitious recent attempts to explain why "freedom" sounds American only when it sounds conservative — and what it would take to change that. His core claim is structural rather than rhetorical: the language of freedom in the United States has been bent toward a zero-sum, racialized, individualistic key because the material conditions of American life, from settler conquest to postwar hegemony, made that key seem credible to its beneficiaries. This is a powerful frame, and it usefully cuts against the easy liberal hope that the problem is mostly one of Democratic messaging. But I think Rana asks a single causal thread — empire — to bear more weight than it can support, and in doing so he understates both the institutional peculiarities of American politics and the genuine purchase of rhetorical struggle. A reconstructed left politics of freedom needs the institutional program he sketches, but also needs to be honest about what else is doing the work.

Start with the comparative puzzle. If imperial expansion produces individualistic, zero-sum accounts of liberty at home, we ought to find some version of the American syndrome in other modern empires. Britain ruled a quarter of the globe; France pursued an extraordinarily violent colonial project from Algeria to Indochina; the Netherlands, Belgium, and Portugal all extracted on a scale that decisively shaped their political economies. Yet each of those societies developed, in the twentieth century, a robust social-democratic vocabulary in which freedom and collective provision are not antonyms. The British defend the NHS in the language of liberty. French *laïcité* and the French welfare state were built by people perfectly comfortable describing public power as emancipatory. If empire reliably produced the American syndrome, the European postwar settlement should look more like ours. It doesn't. So something distinctively American is doing the heavy lifting, and empire is at most one ingredient.

A more candid story would have to add the constitutional inheritance. The Madisonian design — federalism, separation of powers, a Senate that overrepresents land, a judiciary with extraordinary policy reach, and weak, undisciplined parties — multiplies veto points and makes positive collective action expensive. The institutions that in Europe forged solidarity (programmatic mass parties, parliamentary majorities able to legislate sweeping settlements, encompassing union federations bargaining at the sectoral level) either never developed here or were dismantled early. In a system where it is much easier to block than to build, "freedom from" naturally outcompetes "freedom to," and the political economy of negative liberty thrives. Rana gestures at this when he calls for political reform — voting rights, depoliticized districting — but in his framing these are downstream remedies for an imperial pathology, when they are arguably upstream causes in their own right. The settler experience and the constitutional design reinforce each other, but they are not identical, and conflating them obscures what reform actually has to do.

The Piketty citation illustrates the cost of leaning too hard on empire. Rana writes that nineteenth-century American wealth was less concentrated than European wealth and that "the answer is fairly evident": the frontier. This is plausible but underdetermined. The relative absence of an entailed aristocracy, the early dismantling of primogeniture, partible inheritance, mass primary education, and an enormous flow of European immigrants who were poor on arrival are all part of why the U.S. wealth distribution looked the way it did. Land mattered, conquest mattered, slavery mattered horribly — but so did legal and demographic features that have no direct lineage in settler violence. Reducing "free white capitalism" to expropriation flattens a more textured story and, more importantly, makes the institutional remedies seem like a smaller lift than they are.

I want to push hardest, though, on Rana's near-dismissal of messaging. He says reclaiming freedom "is not chiefly about communication strategy or political messaging. It is a matter of institution building." The "chiefly" does some work, but the binary is wrong. The two New Deal generations were not only an institutional accomplishment; they were a rhetorical accomplishment, and the rhetoric did not merely follow the institutions but helped call them into being. Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech in January 1941 enumerated a concept of freedom — freedom from want, freedom from fear — that reorganized the conceptual field. It made Social Security and the GI Bill and full-employment policy intelligible as freedom rather than as freedom's opposite. King's "I Have a Dream" and "Beyond Vietnam" did similar work. To say that this rhetoric mattered only because it was anchored in unions and churches and Black freedom institutions is true; to say that the institutions would have done the cultural work without the rhetoric is not. The Biden administration's recent and largely unsuccessful attempts to talk about industrial policy, abortion, and unions in the register of freedom show what happens when you try the rhetoric without the institutional anchor. Rana is right that the anchor is missing. But the rhetorical project is not a sideshow; it is one of the things you have to do well while you are rebuilding the anchor.

Where the essay is most persuasive is in its claim that 2023 is an unusually open moment. Here Rana could even be more optimistic than he allows himself to be. The UAW's Stand-Up Strike this fall, conducted in explicitly moral language about dignity and a fair life, won concessions thought impossible a year ago. Starbucks Workers United, the Amazon Labor Union, and the writers' and actors' strikes have made the language of collective self-rule legible to a generation that, on Rana's account, should have no experiential vocabulary for it. The post-Dobbs referenda in Kansas, Kentucky, Michigan, and Ohio show majorities mobilizing under the banner of freedom-as-bodily-self-rule, in red states, against a right that controls the formal "freedom" vocabulary. These are not yet a movement on the scale Rana wants, but they are not nothing, and they show that the institutional path he recommends is not merely hypothetical. People are walking it.

That makes the closing prescriptions more urgent, not less. The Protecting the Right to Organize Act, sectoral bargaining, automatic voter registration, an immigration framework that decriminalizes entry and recognizes the international economic causes of migration, a serious rebalancing of the security state against domestic provision — these are the right items on the list. I would add to them constitutional reform that Rana underplays: ending the filibuster, reforming or expanding the Court, ending partisan gerrymandering through federal standards, and admitting D.C. and (on its terms) Puerto Rico. Without those, the institutional ecosystem he wants cannot be sustained against a counter-majoritarian system that will keep producing minority rule.

Rana's deeper insight is that freedom is always defined against some specific form of domination people actually experience. The question for the left, then, is whether it can name today's dominations — at work, at the doctor's office, at the border, in the algorithmic dark — clearly enough that the answer sounds like liberation rather than like another set of obligations. The institutional work is the precondition. The naming is the work itself.

Aziz Rana offers a powerful and deeply researched account of how the American right came to dominate the language of freedom. His tracing of a zero-sum, exclusionary liberty—rooted in settler colonialism and later reinforced by global empire—is a vital corrective to the simplistic notion that the left simply needs better slogans. Yet for all its historical insight, the essay’s prescription falls short in one crucial respect: it understates the work of cultural and rhetorical reclamation that must accompany institutional change. Rana is right to say this is not just a messaging problem, but the battle over freedom will not be won by new workplaces and voting reforms alone. The right’s hold on the idea persists not only because people lack experience with democratic self-rule, but because they are immersed in a cultural narrative that frames freedom in ways that feel emotionally urgent and morally clear. The left needs to contest that narrative directly, with stories, symbols, and a visceral alternative vision—not just material restructuring.

Rana’s historical scaffolding is convincing. He shows how settler expansion made the experience of individual self-mastery seem not just possible but natural for white Americans, while the state’s coercive power was always a sword pointed outward. This, he argues, created a durable cultural reflex: internal government restraint is tyranny, external force is liberty’s bulwark. The imperial decades after World War II, with the nation’s global economic primacy, gave this reflex a second life by linking domestic prosperity to individualized market entitlements rather than collective provision. The result is a freedom talk that mates rugged individualism with nativist violence, and that—as he notes—is increasingly fragile because it can no longer deliver the material independence it promises.

But Rana’s own characterization of the right’s current weakness may be too sanguine. He calls Trumpism and its “worn-out tropes” fragile, noting that its base increasingly sees corporate interests as divorced from the public good. Yet in the immediate present, polling and political energy tell a more ambiguous story. The freedom to refuse vaccines, to carry guns without permits, to speak without “woke” censure—these continue to mobilize millions. The right’s freedom narrative may be contradictory and often hypocritical, but it is not yet hollow to its adherents. It provides a clear antagonist (the state, the left, the outsider) and a tangible sense of personal agency, even if that agency is frequently performative. The left cannot count on its imminent collapse; it must offer something with equal emotional purchase.

Rana’s corrective is institution-building: sites where ordinary people experience collective self-rule—unions, reformed workplaces, democratic political structures—will organically foster a solidaristic freedom. And because empire has always fed exclusionary liberty, he insists we must also adopt an anti-imperial foreign policy and reimagine immigration as a site of shared freedom. These are worthy, necessary goals. But the idea that changing the everyday experience of work and governance will naturally produce a new common sense about freedom underestimates the autonomy of culture. After all, many labor unions in the mid-twentieth century were themselves complicit in racial exclusion, and even today, strong union identity does not always translate into inclusive solidarity on immigration or foreign policy. Furthermore, the cultural space is filled with media, political entertainment, and religious narratives that constantly re-inscribe the old individualist frame. Institution-building is a generational project; without a parallel effort to reclaim the moral imagination, its fruits may be co-opted or contained by the very narratives we wish to displace.

The deeper challenge is this: the left’s inclusive freedom can sound abstract—as Rana admits—precisely because it lacks a compelling cultural story. The right cluttered the public square with “freedom fries” and “Don’t Tread on Me” for a reason: they connected the idea to immediate gut feelings of defiance, ownership, and identity. The left needs its own resonant symbols and rhetorical frames that make solidaristic freedom feel like a direct answer to real fears. Consider the lived experience of precarity: crushing medical debt, algorithmic surveillance at work, unaffordable housing—these feel like profound unfreedom. They are experienced not merely as missed solidarity but as a direct assault on one’s ability to direct the basic conditions of life. The left can and should speak of freedom in these registers: freedom from wage slavery, freedom from being one illness away from bankruptcy, freedom from the arbitrary whims of a landlord or a scheduling algorithm. This is not a substitute for institutional change, but it gives the project a felt urgency that transcends abstract appeals to self-rule.

The civil rights movement, which Rana rightly invokes, succeeded in transforming the national conversation not only through organizing and institution-building but through an unforgettable moral and rhetorical campaign. King’s “I Have a Dream” speech did not merely describe a legislative agenda; it painted a world in which freedom became a shared, almost tactile experience of dignity. The Freedom Budget, for all its policy specificity, was an attempt to give material heft to that vision. Today’s left needs a similar narrative audacity—one that does not shy away from calling its project freedom, and does so with images and language that can compete with the right’s martial and nostalgic tropes.

This points to another tension in Rana’s argument. He insists that the left’s domestic freedom project requires an anti-imperial reorientation of foreign policy. While the historical linkage is real, tying the reclamation of freedom at home so directly to a wholesale rejection of American global primacy risks turning a broad, popular appeal into an ideological checklist that many ordinary people will find distant and disorienting. Most Americans, including many who are open to stronger unions and a fairer economy, do not immediately connect their own sense of unfreedom to the defense budget or the legacy of colonialism. The left can articulate a freedom vision that prioritizes economic security, community control, and racial justice without making geopolitical transformation a prerequisite. The goal should be to fracture the old settler equation from within, showing that a good life is possible without external violence, not to demand that foreign policy conversion precede domestic hope.

Rana’s essay closes by refusing to abandon freedom talk to the right, and that is exactly the right instinct. But reclaiming freedom is as much a cultural struggle as an institutional one. The right’s version persists not because most Americans actually enjoy robust self-rule, but because the story speaks to a deep desire for agency in a world that feels out of control. The left can answer that desire not only by building spaces of collective agency—though that is essential—but by telling a new story in which freedom looks like a secure, dignified life, unburdened by both corporate domination and atomized isolation. Institution-building will take decades; a compelling freedom narrative can begin to shift the ground now, making those reforms feel like the natural outcome of a widely shared moral vision rather than the top-down projects of policy wonks. Only when the words “freedom” and “solidarity” are as instinctively paired in the American mind as “freedom” and “individual” have been will the left’s project truly take root.

Aziz Rana’s compelling essay provides a necessary corrective to the Democratic Party’s assumption that reclaiming the concept of "freedom" is merely a matter of better messaging. By tracing the right-wing, hyper-individualistic, and exclusionary version of American freedom to its roots in settler colonialism and imperial expansion, Rana convincingly demonstrates that our political vocabulary is downstream of our material history. If freedom in America has historically meant the right of insiders to dominate outsiders—subsidized by expropriated land and global hegemony—then it cannot be "taken back" with a clever speech. As Rana rightfully concludes, redefining freedom as an inclusive, solidaristic practice requires rebuilding the actual institutions, particularly in the workplace, where Americans might experience collective self-rule.

However, while Rana’s diagnosis of domestic institutional decay is acute, his prescription for how to rebuild this solidaristic freedom relies on a perilous political calculation. By inextricably linking the domestic revitalization of labor and democracy to a maximalist agenda of anti-imperialism, defense cuts, and the effective decriminalization of the border, Rana risks alienating the very working-class coalition necessary to build the institutions he champions. In treating the left’s domestic and global projects as an indivisible package, he overlooks the pragmatic, often messy sequencing required to forge solidarity in a fractured society.

Rana is at his strongest when analyzing the American workplace. He correctly identifies that decades of neoliberalism and Reaganite cultural dominance have stripped away the sites where collective agency is practiced. The atomized American worker of 2023, subject to the arbitrary whims of corporate hierarchies and algorithmic scheduling, experiences a profound lack of self-rule. When Rana argues that strengthening unions and passing legislation like the PRO Act are fundamental experiments in linking freedom to democracy, the events of this past year prove him right. The resurgent labor militancy we have witnessed—most notably the United Auto Workers’ historic strike against the Big Three automakers this autumn—demonstrates exactly what solidaristic freedom looks like in practice. Workers standing on picket lines, demanding a fair share of the wealth they create, and securing historic wage increases do not view freedom as a zero-sum competition against their neighbors. They experience it as a shared triumph.

But friction emerges when Rana attempts to map this domestic blueprint onto global geopolitics. He asserts that we cannot expect an inclusive version of freedom at home if the United States pursues imperial projects abroad. Consequently, he calls for a radical reorientation of foreign policy: halving the $900 billion defense budget and instituting "presumptive admission for all" at the border.

The historical precedent Rana uses to support this linkage is selectively optimistic. He points to the pre-New Deal labor movement as a moment when the collapse of the settler frontier forced white Americans to embrace shared responsibility and cross-racial solidarity. Yet, the history of American labor is also a history of fierce protectionism. Many of the unionists who fought bloodily for the eight-hour day and workplace democracy simultaneously lobbied for the Chinese Exclusion Act and subsequent restrictive immigration laws, viewing open borders not as an expression of universal solidarity, but as a weapon wielded by capitalists to flood the labor market, depress wages, and break strikes. Solidarity, historically, has often required defined boundaries.

Today, arguing for the decriminalization of entry and presumptive admission for all ignores the real material anxieties of the domestic working class. In late 2023, as municipalities across the country struggle to manage the logistics and fiscal strains of surging migrant populations, framing open immigration as an essential pillar of working-class freedom is a hard sell. It risks recreating the exact zero-sum mentality Rana critiques, where local communities feel their underfunded schools and social services are placed in direct competition with the needs of newcomers. To ask a precarious American public to embrace a borderless vision of universal solidarity before they have secured the domestic institutions that guarantee their own stability is to put the cart before the horse.

A similar dynamic applies to foreign policy. While Rana rightly points out the disastrous consequences of the post-9/11 wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, and the deep moral tensions of unconditional military support abroad—highlighted acutely by the current devastation in Gaza—he assumes that a rapid American retreat from global primacy will naturally yield domestic prosperity and democratic renewal. But the global economy is deeply entangled. A sudden contraction of American security commitments could trigger supply chain disruptions, energy crises, and economic shocks that would disproportionately hurt the American working class. When people feel materially insecure, they do not turn to the left’s vision of inclusive solidarity; they retreat into the exclusionary, fortress-mentality freedom of the right.

Rana is entirely correct that freedom must be an ongoing practice of collective self-rule, and that the workplace is the most vital arena for this struggle. But by insisting that domestic workplace democracy must be ideologically yoked to a radical dismantling of American geopolitical power and border enforcement, he creates an impossibly steep hill for the left to climb.

If we are to organically integrate an emancipatory vision of freedom into American life, we must begin where people actually live and work. We must prioritize the revitalization of the labor movement and the protection of the ballot box. We need to demonstrate that collective action can deliver higher wages, secure pensions, and democratic control over daily life. Only after Americans have rebuilt the muscle memory of local solidarity, and feel confident in their own material security, can we successfully invite them to extend that solidarity to the rest of the globe. Reclaiming freedom is indeed a project of institution building, but those institutions must be built on the solid ground of domestic security, not the shifting sands of global utopianism.

Aziz Rana is right that the American language of freedom has been warped by conquest, racial exclusion, and empire—and right, too, that the left should not surrender the word to those who define it as tax cuts, gun maximalism, and the right to dominate weaker people. But reclaiming freedom requires more than substituting solidarity for individualism. It also requires taking seriously why many Americans hear progressive invocations of collective power as potential threats to their own agency. A democratic left freedom must be capacious enough to include not only freedom through shared institutions, but freedom from arbitrary power wherever it appears: in markets, in police departments, in employers, in borders, but also in bureaucracies, parties, schools, unions, and states.

Rana’s central historical claim is compelling. The United States has repeatedly offered real freedoms to some by denying them to others. White settlers experienced landownership as independence because Indigenous nations were dispossessed. White workers could imagine themselves as self-made because racialized labor and imperial markets subsidized their security. Cold War prosperity helped many Americans identify private employment, employer-based benefits, and consumer choice with freedom, even as U.S. power constrained self-determination abroad. This history helps explain why the right’s freedom talk has often meant a shield for insiders and a sword for outsiders.

Yet the same history also warns against making “collective self-rule” do too much work on its own. Majorities can dominate. Solidarities can exclude. Institutions built in the name of the people can become insulated from the people. The New Deal, which Rana rightly treats as a moment when freedom and democracy were joined more robustly, also entrenched racial exclusions in agricultural and domestic labor, accommodated segregationist power, and built welfare and labor regimes that often depended on male breadwinner assumptions. Industrial unions were schools of democracy for millions, but some were also hostile to women, Black workers, immigrants, and internal dissent. None of this refutes Rana’s argument. It deepens it: freedom requires institutions of collective power, but also rights, checks, contestation, and exit.

That point matters because the contemporary left’s difficulty with freedom is not only institutional weakness. It is also a credibility problem about coercion. Many Americans experience unfreedom through employers, landlords, medical debt, student loans, algorithmic scheduling, and the fear of losing health insurance with a job. But they also experience unfreedom when public systems seem opaque, punitive, or contemptuous. A parent fighting a school bureaucracy for a disabled child’s services, a small contractor navigating licensing rules written for larger firms, a tenant trapped in a public housing authority’s indifference, or a dissident student worried about ideological conformity may not think “solidarity” captures the remedy they need. They may want voice, but they also want limits on power.

The right exploits these anxieties cynically, often redirecting anger away from corporate domination and toward migrants, teachers, trans people, or racial justice protesters. But the anxieties are not all manufactured. During the pandemic, for example, Americans saw both the necessity of collective action and the costs of institutional mistrust. Public health measures saved lives; they also revealed how unevenly burdens were distributed, how poorly authorities sometimes communicated uncertainty, and how little say many families and workers felt they had in decisions that transformed daily life. A left freedom politics that treats all resistance to public authority as selfish libertarianism will fail. The better answer is to democratize authority, make it accountable, and be honest about tradeoffs.

This is why messaging cannot be so neatly separated from institution building. Rana is surely right that slogans alone cannot create lived experiences of collective agency. But political language is one of the ways people decide which institutions are worth building and which sacrifices are legitimate. The civil rights movement did not merely construct organizations; it named the country’s contradictions in biblical, constitutional, and democratic terms. Labor movements did not only bargain; they taught workers to see the eight-hour day, the weekend, and workplace safety as freedom rather than favors. Feminists changed law and workplaces partly by changing the meaning of privacy, equality, bodily autonomy, and dependence. Words do not substitute for power, but they organize perception. They make some injuries intolerable and some futures imaginable.

A persuasive left account of freedom should therefore speak in several registers at once. It should insist that nobody is free if illness means bankruptcy, if a boss can fire workers for organizing, if police can kill with impunity, if pregnancy is compelled by the state, or if citizenship status makes a person permanently exploitable. It should also insist that people are not free when decisions affecting their neighborhoods, schools, and workplaces are made by distant authorities they cannot influence. And it should defend civil liberties without embarrassment: speech, association, due process, religious liberty, privacy, and protection against arbitrary punishment are not bourgeois distractions from material justice. They are tools the powerless have repeatedly used to organize, dissent, and survive.

Rana’s anti-imperial emphasis is indispensable, especially after Iraq and Afghanistan, and amid the horror in Gaza after October 7. A country habituated to treating other peoples’ self-rule as secondary will struggle to cultivate democratic humility at home. The scale of U.S. military spending also plainly crowds out social investment. Still, an anti-imperial freedom politics must avoid becoming a reflexive inversion of Washington’s preferences. Ukrainians resisting Russian invasion, Taiwanese people facing coercion from Beijing, Iranian women confronting theocratic repression, and Palestinians demanding equal dignity all speak languages of freedom. The question is not whether American power is good or bad in the abstract. It is how to support self-determination without domination, how to use restraint without indifference, and how to distinguish solidarity from geopolitical campism.

The most promising part of Rana’s essay is his call to rebuild everyday sites of agency. Unions matter not only because they raise wages, but because they let people practice democracy where they spend much of their lives. Tenant unions, cooperatives, community land trusts, participatory budgeting, public banking, school boards that are genuinely representative, and immigrant worker centers can all make freedom tangible. So can universal programs—health care, child care, paid leave, housing supports—that reduce dependence on employers and intimate partners. The point is not to replace individual freedom with collective freedom, but to show that secure individuality often depends on collective provision.

The left should reclaim freedom, then, but not by denying the American fear of domination. It should vindicate that fear and redirect it. The problem is not that Americans care too much about being pushed around. The problem is that our politics has taught many to see domination only when government acts, and not when capital, inherited hierarchy, racial violence, or imperial power does. A better freedom would make people less governable by bosses, bigots, monopolies, police, creditors, and unaccountable officials alike. Aziz Rana wants to convince us that the right's monopoly over "freedom talk" cannot be undone by communications strategy, that what is needed instead is a long march through institutions — unions, workplaces, voting systems, immigration regimes — that would make solidaristic freedom an everyday felt experience rather than a rhetorical claim. Begin with the historical record Rana invokes. The New Deal coalition did not assemble itself out of pre-existing institutional facts. Franklin Roosevelt's "Four Freedoms" speech in 1941 — freedom of speech, freedom of worship, freedom from want, freedom from fear — was not a description of conditions Americans already inhabited. It was a rhetorical act that named aspirations and gave a generation of Americans a vocabulary to demand them. The Wagner Act, Social Security, and the GI Bill came after, not before, the discursive reframing. The same is true of the civil rights movement Rana rightly celebrates. The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom did not emerge from already-thriving institutions of interracial solidarity; it summoned those institutions into being through extraordinary acts of public language. King's "I Have a Dream" was speech, not infrastructure. The Freedom Rides were performances, not policies.

Rana's framing — institution-building versus messaging — is a false dichotomy. Institutions do not build themselves, and they do not sustain themselves without stories that explain why they matter. The Protecting the Right to Organize Act he mentions has been stalled in the Senate for years not because workers fail to experience workplace domination, but because the political coalition needed to overcome a filibuster has not been assembled, and assembling it requires persuading marginal voters in marginal states that union freedom is their freedom too. That is a communications problem as much as an organizing one.

There is also a strategic question about audience. Rana writes as if the relevant task is to reach the disaffected working class through transformed daily experience. But American politics is decided at the margins, and at those margins are voters who have been told for forty years that "freedom" means low taxes, gun rights, and getting government off their backs. These voters do not work at sites of imminent unionization. Many of them are retired, or self-employed, or work in small businesses where collective bargaining is structurally impossible. For them, the only available route to a different account of freedom is rhetorical — through churches, talk radio, social media, family conversation, and yes, the speeches of politicians they may or may not vote for. Conceding the language to the right while waiting for institutional transformation to do the persuasive work amounts to unilateral disarmament.

Consider what Democratic politicians have actually done when they take freedom language seriously. Elizabeth Warren's stump speeches in 2019–2020 routinely framed antitrust, financial regulation, and child care as freedom issues. Bernie Sanders has done the same with Medicare for All. More recently, Vice President Harris has framed reproductive rights explicitly in the vocabulary of freedom, and Democratic candidates in 2022 successfully ran ads accusing Republicans of stripping women of their liberty. These were communications choices, made by political professionals, and they moved votes. The 2022 midterms — historically anomalous for a first-term president's party — suggest that the rhetorical battlefield is not lost; it is being fought on, with some success.

Rana might respond that such gains are fragile precisely because they are not anchored in transformed daily experience. Fair enough. But the inverse is also true: transformed daily experience without a vocabulary to interpret it produces, historically, not solidarity but resentment. The white steelworker quoted in Jefferson Cowie's Stayin' Alive whom Rana cites — "if what we lived through in the 1950s was not liberation, then liberation never happens" — inhabited a deeply unionized institutional environment. That experience did not automatically produce solidaristic freedom; it produced, by the late 1970s, Reagan Democrats. What made the difference was the story workers told themselves about what their unions and their prosperity meant, and the right won that story.

There is also a deeper philosophical point about how political identities form. Rana's model assumes a kind of materialist sequencing: change the institutions, and consciousness will follow. But the past half-century of political science suggests something more recursive. People interpret their material conditions through pre-existing narrative frames, and they often interpret identical conditions in opposite ways depending on which frame is culturally available. The same factory closure becomes, for one worker, evidence that capitalism has betrayed them; for another, evidence that immigrants and welfare queens are stealing what is rightfully theirs. The institutions did not determine the interpretation. The language did, working in conjunction with media ecosystems, religious affiliations, and partisan signals.

None of this is to dismiss Rana's institutional program. He is correct that a freedom politics without union density, voting rights, and demilitarized foreign policy is a freedom politics of pure exhortation. The PRO Act matters. So does Puerto Rican self-determination, so does decriminalizing border crossing, so does cutting the Pentagon budget. I would happily sign onto every concrete reform he names. The disagreement is about sequencing and emphasis. Rana treats persuasion as downstream; I think it is constitutive. The institutions he wants to build will not be built by people who lack a shared vocabulary for why they matter, and that vocabulary has to be spoken aloud, repeatedly, by candidates and journalists and organizers, before institutional change becomes politically achievable.

There is one more thing to say about the right's appropriation of freedom. Rana attributes it to structural conditions — settler expansion, then global primacy — that emboldened a zero-sum, racialized account. This is partly true but incomplete. The right's freedom talk also reflects deliberate, generational investment by conservative intellectuals, foundations, think tanks, and media operations. From the Mont Pelerin Society to the Federalist Society to talk radio to Fox News to the manufactured grievance machinery of the Tea Party and Trump, the right built an apparatus designed precisely to seize the language of liberty. That apparatus is institutional, yes, but its primary product is rhetoric. The left does not lack institutions in any absolute sense; it lacks an equivalently coordinated rhetorical apparatus committed to a different vision of freedom. Building that apparatus is messaging work, and it is urgent.

Reclaiming freedom, then, requires both the long institutional project Rana describes and a more aggressive, more immediate rhetorical struggle. To say "this is a matter of institution building, not communication strategy" is to set up a contrast that the historical record does not support. The civil rights movement, the New Deal, the suffragists, the abolitionists — all of them built institutions and spoke a language of freedom simultaneously, each reinforcing the other. We should do the same, and we should start now, in the speeches of the next campaign, not after the institutional revolution has already happened.

Aziz Rana's essay is a sophisticated piece of intellectual history, but its central move is to treat the right's account of freedom as essentially derivative — a cultural residue of settler conquest and imperial primacy, propped up by racial fantasy and now exhausting itself. The right's appeal to freedom is not a fragile relic. It is a response to a real and continuing experience of being governed by institutions one did not choose and cannot meaningfully influence.

Start with the substance of what conservatives mean by freedom. Rana is right that there is a positive and a negative tradition. He prefers the positive — freedom as collective self-rule, as participation in shared institutions. Conservatives, including most American conservatives, hold to a more modest and arguably more defensible vision: freedom as the absence of arbitrary coercion, the security of person and property, the right to associate or not associate, to worship or not worship, to speak or stay silent, to raise one's children according to one's own conscience. This is not "extreme individualism" or "market fundamentalism." It is the classical liberal inheritance, refined through Anglo-American common law, that allowed enormous numbers of people of every background to build lives of dignity over the past two centuries.

Rana repeatedly conflates this tradition with white nationalism. The conflation is rhetorically convenient but historically dishonest. The principles of limited government, due process, freedom of conscience, and economic liberty were not invented by settlers to dispossess Indians. They were imported from a Britain that had no settler colonies, by people whose theoretical commitments — Locke, Sidney, Trenchard, the radical Whig tradition — were forged against monarchy. To say that these ideas became entangled with conquest is true; to say they are reducible to conquest is to throw out the moral inheritance of every dissenter, religious minority, abolitionist, and yes, eventually civil rights leader who ever appealed to them. Frederick Douglass did not appeal to "solidaristic collective self-rule" when he wrote his Fourth of July oration. He appealed to the Constitution and the Declaration, read against their hypocritical practitioners.

He wants a society where freedom means democratic codetermination in workplaces, robust public provision of pensions and health care, decriminalized borders, and dramatically reduced military spending redirected to social investment. This is roughly the program of European social democracy. Whatever its merits — and I would grant it some — let us be honest that it has not delivered greater freedom in any sense an ordinary person would recognize. Western European countries have less innovation, less entrepreneurship, higher youth unemployment, declining religious liberty, increasingly aggressive speech regulation, and demographic trajectories that suggest deep civic exhaustion. Their citizens are more taxed, more surveilled, and increasingly less able to dissent from prevailing elite opinion. The fact that Americans look at this model and prefer their own messier, more dynamic, more contentious freedom is not false consciousness. It is judgment.

The reality is more interesting. The populist right has gained ground precisely because ordinary people across racial lines — Hispanic voters most notably, who have shifted toward Republicans in recent cycles — have correctly perceived that the managerial-progressive class has contempt for their values, their work, their communities, and their religious traditions. When parents object to ideological curricula in their children's schools, they are not reveling in domination; they are exercising the most basic parental freedom. When workers worry about open borders depressing wages, they are not channeling settler racism; they are doing the arithmetic that economists from Borjas to Card have argued over for decades. On this, a genuine populist conservatism and a serious left can find common cause, as figures like Oren Cass and Marco Rubio have begun to articulate. Where we differ from Rana is in believing that the answer is not more administrative state, more federal mandates, and more redefinition of freedom to mean participation in left-approved institutions. It is, rather, restoring the conditions under which families, churches, small communities, and yes small businesses can flourish without being squeezed by either Walmart or Washington.

The right's freedom is not a relic. It is the lived intuition of millions of Americans that they should be left alone to live their lives, and that intuition deserves better than Rana's reduction of it to settler nostalgia.

The Hidden Statism in Rana's "Freedom"

Aziz Rana presents his alternative vision as a more capacious, inclusive understanding of freedom — one that joins liberty to solidarity, democracy, and shared responsibility. It sounds attractive. But beneath the rhetoric is a familiar substitution: the replacement of the individual liberty tradition with collective political authority, dressed up in liberatory language. Anyone who values freedom in its older, more rigorous sense — the absence of coercion, the security of one's person, property, and conscience — should read Rana's essay carefully, because it is precisely the kind of conceptual maneuver that erodes liberty in the name of expanding it.

Consider Rana's working definition: "Above all, to be free was to enjoy a robust experience of self-rule — an experience that combined a mastery over one's own life and a central role in shaping the direction of collective arrangements and institutions." Notice what has happened here. Two distinct ideas have been silently fused. "Mastery over one's own life" is the classical liberal idea — call it autonomy or independence. "A central role in shaping collective arrangements" is something different: political participation, or democratic citizenship. These are both goods, but they are not the same good, and conflating them produces conceptual havoc.

Isaiah Berlin warned against exactly this conflation in his "Two Concepts of Liberty." Negative liberty — being unobstructed by others — and positive liberty — being self-directed, or participating in collective direction — are different things, and the second can be, and historically has been, used to override the first. If "real freedom" requires my participation in shaping collective decisions, then someone who simply wishes to be left alone — to mind his shop, raise his children, attend his church, write his novel — is not, on Rana's account, truly free. He must be made free, drawn into participation, integrated into democratic institutions, taught to experience solidarity. This is freedom with a quotation mark around it, and historically, the projects that have pursued it have not been gentle.

Rana would deny this implication, and he would point to workplace democracy and voting rights as benign examples of his vision. But examine the workplace case. Rana proposes that workers must "enjoy shared control over the decisions that shape the workplace," "regardless of their wealth or status." The word "must" is doing significant work. In Rana's world, the worker who would rather just do his job and go home — who has no interest in attending union meetings, voting on shop-floor governance, or codetermining production schedules — is to be enrolled in this collective freedom whether he wants it or not. The owner who built the business and risked her capital must surrender authority to a collective. This may or may not be a good policy. It is not, however, freedom in any sense the word has historically carried. It is the redistribution of authority.

The same conceptual slippage runs through Rana's treatment of public goods. He laments that in the United States, "jobs, pensions, and health insurance were all private entitlements for workers to negotiate from employers, not public goods entrenched through collective action and government provision." But notice the move: the absence of state provision is presented as a deficit of freedom. By this logic, the freer society is the one with more comprehensive government programs. Yet the citizen of such a society faces higher taxes, more regulation, more dependence on bureaucratic institutions for the necessities of life, and less practical ability to exit, dissent, or arrange his affairs differently. Whatever else one calls this, calling it freedom requires the kind of redefinition that emptied the word of meaning in twentieth-century totalitarian discourse.

Rana is too sophisticated to be confused with a totalitarian, of course. His project is a democratic-socialist one. But the conceptual machinery he deploys has been used, and continues to be used, to justify expansions of state power against which the classical liberal tradition has always cautioned. When Rousseau wrote that citizens might be "forced to be free," he was articulating the same move: the genuine freedom of the individual is identified with the general will, and the general will is identified with the institutional decisions of the collective. There is no recourse against the collective in the name of freedom, because the collective is freedom.

Consider next Rana's history. He traces American individualism to settler expansion and imperial extraction. This is a partial truth made into a totalizing claim. Yes, American agrarian independence depended on land taken from Native peoples. But the principles by which settlers understood their freedom — the common law tradition of property rights, due process, freedom of conscience, jury trial, habeas corpus — were not produced by the conquest. They were imported from England, where there were no Indians to displace. Locke's Two Treatises is, among other things, a defense of property and limited government written against an absolutist monarch on a small island. The reduction of liberal individualism to "settler freedom" is a genetic fallacy: the historical context of an idea's adoption does not exhaust its meaning or validity.

Worse, the genealogy obscures the actual function of individual rights in protecting the marginalized. Frederick Douglass appealed to the Constitution. Ida B. Wells appealed to the right to due process. The civil rights movement won its great victories through litigation under the Fourteenth Amendment and through claims of equal individual liberty. The freedom that mattered most to those at the bottom was not the collective self-rule of a majority that had oppressed them, but the constitutional protection of individuals against majoritarian abuse. Rana mentions King and Hamer, but flattens their thought into a generic communitarianism. King invoked the Declaration and the Constitution — documents of individual rights — as the unfulfilled promissory note America owed.

There is, finally, a practical objection. Rana wants to build institutions that make collective self-rule the everyday experience of Americans. But the historical record of large-scale projects to remake everyday life around collective participation is grim. Even the gentlest versions — the European corporatist arrangements he implicitly admires — have produced sclerotic economies and increasingly censorious public spheres. Real human beings, given a choice, often prefer to be left alone with their families and their projects rather than to attend yet another meeting in which they are invited to codetermine something. This preference is not false consciousness. It is the recognition that the unexamined life may not be worth living, but the over-politicized life is exhausting and corrosive.

Reclaim freedom by all means. But reclaim it as what it actually is: the right of individuals, in their persons and properties and consciences, to be free from coercion, including the coercion of well-meaning collectives. The rest is politics, and politics, however necessary, is not the same as liberty.

Aziz Rana's essay performs a familiar gesture in left-liberal American thought: it acknowledges that the language of freedom in the United States is structured by settler conquest and racial subjugation, then concludes that this language must nonetheless be reclaimed, refurbished, and put to emancipatory work. Rana cites King, Hamer, the Freedom Budget, the Movement for Black Lives. He does not cite Saidiya Hartman, who has shown how the supposed transition from slavery to freedom remade rather than dissolved the structures of unfreedom. He does not cite Frank Wilderson or Jared Sexton, whose work argues that anti-Blackness is not a contingent feature of American liberty but its constitutive shadow. He does not cite Ruth Wilson Gilmore, whose abolitionist geography insists on building something rather than reclaiming something.

This is not a quibble about citations. It points to a deeper problem with Rana's frame. He treats the settler/imperial corruption of American freedom as a historical accident that produced exclusionary outcomes, against which a more inclusive freedom might be politically constructed. But for a substantial current of Black radical thought, the corruption is not accidental. The "free" subject of American political discourse was constituted in and through the unfreedom of others — not just because settlers happened to dispossess Natives and enslave Africans on the way to their own liberty, but because the very category of the free citizen required, conceptually and materially, the production of unfree non-citizens against whom freedom could be measured and felt.

If this is right — and the historical and theoretical case is strong — then Rana's project of "reclaiming" freedom faces a problem he does not adequately confront. You cannot reclaim a concept whose internal structure depends on the production of the unfree. You can only repurpose its language while building something different underneath. The Black radical tradition has increasingly understood this, which is why so much of its most generative recent work uses different vocabulary: abolition, fugitivity, marronage, the undercommons, refusal. These are not synonyms for freedom-as-inclusion-and-solidarity. They name something more radical: a politics that does not seek admission to the house of American liberty but proposes to dismantle the architecture entirely.

Consider the abolitionist current within contemporary movements against policing. Rana mentions "racial justice protests against policing following George Floyd's murder" as an example of potent left invocations of freedom. But "abolish the police" is not a freedom claim in the sense Rana means. It is not a demand to be included in a more democratic policing institution. It is a demand to end policing as a mode of social control and replace it with different infrastructures of care, mutual aid, and conflict resolution. The activists most associated with this current — Mariame Kaba, Patrisse Cullors, Derecka Purnell — have generally been careful to distinguish their project from a reformist freedom politics that would make policing more accountable or more democratic. They are not trying to extend the franchise of liberty. They are trying to build a different world.

The same is true of much contemporary work on reparations. The most rigorous arguments — those of William Darity and Kirsten Mullen, for instance — do not frame reparations as a fulfillment of American freedom's promise to Black people. They frame reparations as the partial repair of a debt produced by centuries of plunder, a debt that is in principle unpayable and that places the moral relationship between the American state and Black descendants of slavery on different terms than the relationship of liberty-bearing citizenship. This is not freedom-talk. It is something else.

Rana might respond that such framings can be folded into his solidaristic vision: the inclusive freedom he wants would, of course, include reparative justice, abolitionist transformation, and the rest. But this folding is precisely the problem. To translate every radical project into the vocabulary of freedom is to refuse to learn from the people who have stopped using that vocabulary because they found it inadequate to what they were trying to say. It is to insist that the master's tools must be reclaimed, when those tools — to recall Audre Lorde — will never dismantle the master's house.

There is also a strategic point. Rana acknowledges that "the most potent left invocations of freedom overwhelmingly involve activists within marginalized communities who are referencing actual direct, immediate, and ongoing threats." He treats this as a partial success of the left freedom project. I read it differently. Those invocations are potent because they are local, specific, and tied to material struggles — against a police killing, against a state abortion ban, against a school-board attack on trans kids. They do not generalize easily into a national freedom narrative, and the attempt to generalize them risks abstracting them from the conditions that make them resonant. The Movement for Black Lives's policy platform, for example, is far more granular than any "freedom for all" framework can capture, and its power lies precisely in that granularity.

What, then, would I recommend instead? Three things.

First, take seriously the possibility that the left does not need a single master-signifier to organize its politics. The conservative movement won its dominance not because it had a better word than "freedom," but because it built durable infrastructure — donor networks, think tanks, judicial pipelines, media — capable of advancing many different policy demands under a shifting array of rhetorical banners. The left could do worse than imitate the infrastructure while letting a hundred vocabularies bloom: abolition here, dignity there, solidarity in another context, care in another.

Second, be honest that the Black radical tradition has produced political vocabularies that are not reducible to liberalism's freedom-talk, and that the white left's persistent attempts to translate these vocabularies back into the language of American liberty have often functioned to defang them. Rana's essay does this politely. The defanging is still defanging.

Third, recognize that Rana's institutional program — voting rights, labor law reform, demilitarization, decriminalized migration — is largely correct and largely separable from his freedom-framing. We can build those institutions whether or not we call the result "freedom." Indeed, calling the result "freedom" may be precisely what allows it to be co-opted, watered down, and absorbed back into the same liberal-imperial frame that Rana otherwise wants to escape. The American experience of "freedom" includes, after all, the Cold War weaponization of the word, the Freedom Fries he mentions, Operation Enduring Freedom, and the long history of empire abroad and prison expansion at home being justified in liberty's name. A movement that wins the institutions but loses the word may end up further ahead than a movement that wins the word but loses the institutions.

Rana wants both. I am skeptical the word can be won. But I am with him on the institutions, and that may be enough.

Where Freedom Is Made: The Workplace Case

Of the many threads in Aziz Rana's rich essay, the one I want to pull is the claim that "precisely because most people spend most of their time at work, the workplace is an essential pillar for reconstituting prevalent cultural narratives." This is the right intuition. It is also an intuition that the American labor movement has been articulating, in different vocabularies, for more than a century — sometimes as "industrial democracy," sometimes as "economic citizenship," sometimes simply as a union contract that gives workers a voice. Rana is correct to place this at the center of any project to revive a solidaristic account of freedom. But the institutional path from where we are to where we need to be is harder than his essay lets on, and it is worth tracing some of that difficulty in more detail than he has space for.

Begin with the empirical landscape. Private-sector union density in the United States is around 6 percent — roughly where it stood in 1900, before the great organizing victories of the New Deal and the immediate postwar period. Public sector union density is higher (around 33 percent) but has been the target of sustained legal and political assault, most prominently in Janus v. AFSCME (2018), which extended right-to-work conditions to all public employment. The legal regime under which unions operate, the National Labor Relations Act, was designed in 1935 for an industrial economy of large workplaces with stable workforces. It is poorly suited to the gig economy, to contracting and franchising arrangements that disperse the employer relationship, and to the cross-border supply chains through which most goods are now produced. Even if every NLRB ruling went the union's way (and most do not), the structural mismatch between the law and the economy would still produce decline.

Rana mentions the Protecting the Right to Organize Act, which would address many of these gaps. The PRO Act would impose meaningful penalties on employers who violate workers' organizing rights, restrict captive-audience meetings, narrow the legal definition of independent contractor, and preempt state right-to-work laws. It is good legislation. It has also been stuck in the Senate for years, blocked first by Democratic conservatives and then by Republican unanimity, and it is not going to pass under current alignments. The honest version of Rana's argument is that the institutional revival he wants depends on prior political victories that the current alignment of voting rights, gerrymandered districts, and rural-state Senate distortions makes very difficult. This is not a reason to abandon the project. It is a reason to recognize that workplace transformation and political transformation are mutually dependent in a way that creates a chicken-and-egg problem the essay does not quite face.

There is also the question of what kind of workplace transformation we are talking about. American labor traditions are largely contract-based: unions bargain with management over wages, benefits, and conditions, but the broader strategic direction of the firm — investment decisions, production locations, technology adoption — remains with management. This is a much weaker form of workplace democracy than what European codetermination provides, where workers sit on supervisory boards and have institutional voice in firm-level strategic decisions. If Rana's vision is that the workplace become a "continuous exercise in shared decision-making," American-style collective bargaining will not, on its own, get us there. We would need something like German Mitbestimmung — and that means rewriting corporate governance law, not just labor law.

Here the political obstacles compound. American corporate law, governed at the state level and dominated by Delaware, holds that directors owe their fiduciary duties to shareholders, not to workers or communities. Senator Elizabeth Warren's Accountable Capitalism Act would have moved in the direction Rana implies, requiring large firms to reserve board seats for worker representatives. It went nowhere. The reason is not just corporate lobbying, though that matters; it is that the American political and legal culture has, over decades, internalized shareholder primacy as a near-natural feature of business organization. To dislodge it requires the kind of long-term ideational and institutional work that Rana correctly says cannot be done by communications strategy alone.

What can be done? Several things, none individually sufficient, all potentially additive.

First, sectoral bargaining. Rather than the firm-by-firm certification election model that has produced the catastrophic decline in union density, sectoral bargaining sets wages and conditions across entire industries. California's recent fast food council legislation is a small experiment in this direction. Scaled up — to logistics, to health care, to retail — sectoral bargaining could rebuild worker voice in ways the existing NLRA framework cannot.

Second, public-option provision of benefits historically tied to employment. As long as health insurance, retirement security, and even paid leave are negotiated firm by firm, workers remain dependent on their employers for the basic conditions of economic security. Decoupling benefits from employment — through Medicare for All, expanded Social Security, universal paid leave — would give workers more freedom to leave bad jobs, organize without fear, and demand more from the employment relationship.

Third, worker ownership. The cooperative sector in the United States is small but growing, and federal policies like the recently expanded employee stock ownership programs could be aggressively scaled. Worker ownership directly addresses Rana's concern that the workplace become a site of collective self-rule rather than employer prerogative.

Fourth, immigration reform that protects rather than excludes immigrant workers. Rana is right that immigrant workers are essential to American economic life and routinely denied the legal protections that would allow them to organize. Decriminalizing entry, providing a path to legal status, and extending labor protections to all workers regardless of status would not just be humane; it would prevent the use of immigration enforcement as a weapon against organizing campaigns, which has been one of the most effective union-busting tools of the past three decades.

None of this happens without political power, and political power, in turn, depends on the very labor movement that the existing institutional landscape has weakened. This is the puzzle. It is also why Rana's emphasis on institution-building is right: there is no shortcut, no rhetorical magic, that allows us to skip the slow work of organizing workplace by workplace, sector by sector, until enough power has been built to change the rules under which organizing happens.

Where I would push Rana further is on the urgency of the unglamorous parts of this work. The PRO Act will not pass under current conditions. But many state-level reforms — California's AB 5, Illinois's Workers' Rights Amendment, sectoral councils, public banking initiatives — are passable and are happening. The federal NLRB under Jennifer Abruzzo has been more aggressive in pro-worker rulings than at any time in decades. New organizing among graduate students, Starbucks workers, Amazon workers, and journalists is reshaping what unionization looks like outside the industrial heartland. These are real victories, occurring now, and they are the building blocks of the freedom Rana describes. Naming them as such, loudly and persistently, is part of how the cultural shift he wants actually happens.

The Limits of the Settler Frame

Aziz Rana's argument rests on a single explanatory engine: American conceptions of freedom were shaped by settler expansion and then by imperial primacy, and the resulting culture of zero-sum, racialized, individualistic liberty is what the left must now overcome. The framework has real force, and it explains things that other frameworks miss. But it is also overextended, and the cost of relying on it as heavily as Rana does is that he ends up obscuring other sources of the American freedom tradition and other reasons for the right's current dominance of freedom-talk.

Consider first the comparative case. Rana implies that the United States is distinctively bad on these matters because of its settler past. But other settler societies — Canada, Australia, New Zealand — have moved in significantly different directions. Australia developed a powerful labor movement and a comprehensive welfare state in the early twentieth century, long before the US New Deal, and despite an arguably more violent and explicit settler ideology. Canadian political culture is famously more communitarian than American, with public health care, more redistributive taxation, and a Charter of Rights and Freedoms that explicitly balances individual and collective claims. New Zealand abolished its constitutional monarchy of property qualifications earlier than most peers and produced an indigenous-settler political settlement (imperfect, contested, but real) that has no American equivalent. If settler expansion produced the American account of freedom, why did parallel settler expansions produce different accounts elsewhere?

One answer is that settler expansion is not, by itself, sufficient. What distinguished the American case was a particular constellation of factors: the absence of an established church, the lateness of universal male suffrage being extended to non-property-holders (though earlier than in Europe), the constitutional structure of federalism that empowered subnational majorities to resist national redistribution, the specific religious culture of evangelical Protestantism with its emphasis on individual conversion, the inheritance of common-law property doctrines from England, and the catastrophic timing of slavery and its abolition. The settler-imperial frame captures some of this constellation but not all of it, and treating it as the master explanation distorts the rest.

Consider, second, the chronology Rana proposes. He argues that the early twentieth century was a moment when "settler expectations" had collapsed and a solidaristic freedom tradition gained ground, only to be overwhelmed again by the consolidation of US empire after World War II. This is suggestive but historically rough. The Progressive Era was not, in most accounts, a triumph of solidaristic freedom; it was a contested period in which technocratic reform, nativist immigration restriction (1924), Jim Crow consolidation, and labor repression coexisted with the genuine social democratic currents Rana highlights. The New Deal itself was a deeply ambivalent achievement, structured by compromises with Southern Democrats that excluded agricultural and domestic workers (largely Black) from Social Security and the Wagner Act. The story of mid-twentieth-century social democracy in America is not a clean tale of solidaristic freedom briefly ascendant.

Conversely, the post-1945 period was not uniformly the triumph of individualistic, imperial freedom Rana suggests. The civil rights movement, Great Society, environmental movement, second-wave feminism, gay liberation, disability rights — all of these arose during and after the consolidation of American global primacy, not in opposition to its decline. If empire's "rents" were so determinative of domestic conservatism, why did the period of maximum imperial extraction produce the period of maximum domestic egalitarian reform?

The answer is that the relationship between foreign policy and domestic ideology is more complicated than Rana's causal story allows. Sometimes empire abroad correlates with reaction at home (the McCarthy era); sometimes it correlates with reform (the Great Society, partly motivated by Cold War competition for the loyalty of decolonizing nations). To pick examples where the correlation supports the thesis and ignore those where it does not is to take the wind out of the argument's empirical sails.

Third, the settler frame can obscure as much as it reveals about contemporary American politics. Take the rural-urban divide, the religious-secular divide, the educational polarization that has restructured American partisan alignment over the past three decades. These do not map cleanly onto a settler/non-settler frame. Many of the most conservative voters today live in places — exurban Texas, the Florida panhandle, the Ohio Valley — whose settler histories are no more recent or vivid than those of liberal regions. Conversely, some of the most progressive coastal cities sit on land taken from Native peoples within living memory of certain settler dynasties. The geography of American conservatism is shaped by economic restructuring, religious affiliation, and media ecology at least as much as by inherited settler ideology.

Fourth, Rana's emphasis on settler conquest can crowd out attention to other deep sources of American individualism. The Protestant religious heritage matters. The peculiar absence of a feudal past matters (as Louis Hartz argued long ago). The constitutional design choices of 1787 — particularly the Senate, the Electoral College, federalism, and judicial review — matter. The specific structure of American immigration in waves matters. Each of these has shaped what "freedom" means in this country in ways that the settler frame, by itself, cannot fully explain.

None of this is to deny what Rana gets right. Settler expansion did shape the early American conception of freedom in ways that have lasting consequences. Race has always been at the center of American struggles over who counts as free. American imperial power abroad has corroded democratic culture at home. These are real points. The question is whether the settler-imperial frame is the best one for explaining contemporary American politics and for guiding contemporary left strategy.

I am not sure it is. A left strategy organized around the recognition that American freedom-talk is structurally settler-imperial may end up emphasizing the wrong reforms and missing potential coalition partners. It can become so focused on dismantling the inherited frame that it neglects the more proximate institutional drivers of inequality and unfreedom: the courts, the filibuster, the corporate consolidation of media, the structure of campaign finance, the absence of universal benefits. These are addressable through reforms that need not be framed in terms of anti-settler or anti-imperial politics, and that may attract broader coalitions when they are not.

There is also a risk that the settler frame becomes an alibi for inaction. If the problem is structural and centuries deep, the work of dismantling it is correspondingly enormous, and the temptation to talk about it more than act on it grows accordingly. The American left has, in my view, often suffered from a surplus of structural diagnosis and a deficit of strategic prioritization. Rana's essay, for all its sophistication, leans toward the former.

Reclaiming freedom may or may not be the right project. But if it is, the path to it runs through specific institutional fights — on labor law, voting rights, social provision, antitrust — that need not be filtered through the settler-imperial frame to be won. Sometimes the directest route is the best.

The Gender Question Rana Doesn't Ask

Aziz Rana has written a thoughtful essay about the racial and imperial corruption of American freedom-talk and the prospects for reclaiming a more solidaristic alternative. But there is a striking lacuna at its center: gender appears almost nowhere. Women's movements are mentioned in passing in the opening paragraph. Reproductive rights surface briefly near the end as an example of marginalized communities deploying freedom-language in the wake of Roe's overturning. Otherwise, the essay's protagonists are male — King, Randolph, Rustin, Debs, Cowie's steelworker, Manley, Nyerere — and its categories of analysis are organized around race, class, and empire. The omission matters, because the relationship between feminism and freedom in the United States is not just an additional case to plug into Rana's framework. It complicates the framework in productive ways and points to resources for reclamation that Rana's settler-imperial story misses.

Begin with the obvious. The single most powerful invocation of freedom in American politics today is the post-Dobbs reproductive freedom movement. State referenda on abortion rights have won in Kansas, Michigan, Ohio, Vermont, California, Montana, and Kentucky — including in conservative states where Democratic candidates routinely lose. The 2022 midterms, which defied historical expectations of first-term presidential losses, were shaped decisively by women voters mobilizing around reproductive freedom. The vocabulary that worked was explicitly the vocabulary of freedom: "freedom to decide," "freedom from government interference in private medical decisions," "the freedom to make our own choices." This was not a niche or marginal use of freedom-language. It was the most successful left invocation of freedom in American politics for at least a generation.

Rana's framework has trouble accounting for this. The reproductive freedom argument that has worked electorally is not primarily solidaristic; it is, in important ways, individualistic. It claims the right of the individual woman, in consultation with her doctor and her conscience, to make decisions about her own body without state intrusion. This is closer to the negative-liberty tradition Rana criticizes than to the positive-liberty, collective-self-rule tradition he champions. And it has worked precisely because it speaks the deep American vocabulary of bodily autonomy and freedom from government intrusion — a vocabulary that Rana attributes to settler individualism but that has now been turned, devastatingly, against the religious right.

This suggests that the right's monopoly on individualist freedom-talk is more fragile, and more recoverable, than Rana's structural account implies. The same vocabulary that the right deployed against the welfare state can be deployed against the religious right's attempts to legislate against women's bodies. The individualist tradition is not inherently right-coded; it depends on what the relevant threats to individual freedom are and who can credibly name them.

A second feminist complication. Rana's emphasis on the workplace as the central site for reconstituting freedom-as-self-rule reflects a long-standing labor tradition that has, historically, undervalued reproductive and care labor. The household, the daycare, the nursing home, the school — these are workplaces too, and they are workplaces where women, disproportionately women of color and immigrant women, perform the labor that makes all other economic activity possible. A workplace-centered freedom project that does not address care work, that does not include domestic workers and home health aides in its vision of labor democracy, that does not grapple with the unequal distribution of unpaid household labor, will reproduce some of the same exclusions that the settler tradition produced.

This is not a small point. The National Domestic Workers Alliance and related organizations have done extraordinary work in recent years to extend labor protections to workers historically excluded from the Wagner Act. The Care Economy organizing that Ai-jen Poo and others have led explicitly frames care work as a freedom issue — both for the workers who perform it and for the families who depend on it. None of this appears in Rana's essay. It should.

A third complication. The American history of women's movements is itself a contested terrain that does not map onto Rana's settler-imperial frame in any simple way. The early women's rights movement was deeply entangled with abolition (Stanton, Anthony, Mott emerged from abolitionist circles), and also with the racism that followed the Fifteenth Amendment's enfranchisement of Black men but not women. First-wave feminism was largely a project of white middle-class women, and its achievements (suffrage, professional access, property rights) were structured by class and racial exclusions. Second-wave feminism emerged partly out of women's experiences within New Left and civil rights organizations, where male leadership often replicated the very hierarchies the movements ostensibly opposed. Third-wave and intersectional feminism have grappled with the limits of universal claims about "women" and "women's freedom" in ways that have produced both productive complication and persistent fragmentation.

This history suggests that "reclaiming freedom" for the left will require more than building unions and demilitarizing foreign policy. It will require grappling with the specifically gendered structures of unfreedom: the ongoing crisis of intimate partner violence, the failure of the legal system to address sexual assault, the persistent wage gap, the rollback of reproductive rights, the targeting of trans youth, the affordability crisis in childcare, the impossibility of work-family balance for most working women. Each of these is a freedom issue. Each requires institutional transformation. None of them are captured by Rana's framework as he has presented it.

A fourth complication. The international dimension of Rana's argument — that domestic freedom rests on global conditions, and that anti-imperialism is essential to any project of domestic emancipation — looks different through a feminist lens. International feminist movements have long pointed out that American foreign policy is not just imperially destructive in general but is destructive in specifically gendered ways. The use of women's rights rhetoric to justify the Afghanistan war (the "liberation" of Afghan women under American bombs) was a particularly grotesque example. The structural adjustment programs imposed by the IMF on Global South countries have disproportionately harmed women, who absorb the costs of cuts to public services through unpaid care labor. The migration patterns Rana mentions are themselves gendered, with women migrating into domestic and care work in wealthy countries while their own children are raised by other women in poorer ones (the "global care chains" Arlie Hochschild and others have documented). A feminist anti-imperialism would be richer than the largely race- and class-coded version Rana offers.

None of this is to dismiss Rana's essay. Its analytical resources are real, and many of its institutional recommendations are ones I support. But the omission of gender is not a small gap to be filled in by adding a sentence or two about reproductive rights. It is a structural absence that, if addressed, would alter both the diagnosis and the prescription. The freedom we most urgently need to reclaim may be the freedom currently being denied to women, queer people, trans youth, and care workers — and reclaiming it may require vocabularies and strategies that Rana's framework, as presently constituted, cannot adequately provide.

Solidarity Begins at the Border

The most arresting move in Aziz Rana's essay is his insistence that domestic freedom and foreign policy are not separate questions. He is right, and he is right at exactly the moment when this connection is most painfully visible. As I write, the United States is providing billions of dollars in military aid and diplomatic cover for an Israeli bombing campaign that has killed something approaching twenty thousand Palestinians in Gaza, the majority of them women and children, in the two months since October 7. The American government calls this a defense of freedom. People across the Global South — and increasingly, young Americans — see it as the continuation of a long imperial pattern in which the United States projects violence abroad while invoking liberty. Rana's framework helps us understand why this dissonance is not accidental but structural.

Let me extend his argument by pressing on three points where I think the implications are even sharper than he allows.

First, the moral economy of American empire. Rana describes how the rents extracted from American global primacy after 1945 fed a domestic culture in which corporate well-being could be experienced as public well-being, and in which individual prosperity could feel like a personal achievement rather than a structurally subsidized outcome. He notes that these rents have been declining for ordinary Americans even as the imperial project continues. What he does not quite say, but should, is that this creates a profound legitimacy crisis for the existing arrangement. American empire is being maintained at enormous cost — fiscal, moral, geopolitical — at precisely the moment when the material payoff to ordinary Americans has shrunk to near nothing. The trillion-dollar defense budget is no longer buying domestic peace, and increasingly it is purchasing only resentment and disillusionment.

This is a political opening, and the anti-war movement that has emerged around Gaza in recent weeks suggests how broad that opening might be. Polling indicates that majorities of Democratic voters and pluralities of Americans overall now favor a ceasefire and disapprove of unconditional support for Israel's military campaign. The generational divide is even more striking: voters under thirty oppose current US policy by overwhelming margins. This is not because young Americans have suddenly become more knowledgeable about international relations. It is because the legitimating story — that American power abroad serves freedom at home — has lost its grip on people who have come of age watching that power produce Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya, and now Gaza, while at home they cannot afford rent or health insurance.

Second, the connection between imperial violence and domestic policing. Rana mentions this in passing but does not develop it. The militarization of American police forces over the past two decades has been driven in large part by surplus military equipment, training, and personnel returning from foreign wars. The 1033 program transferring military equipment to local police, the proliferation of SWAT teams and armored vehicles, the use of counter-insurgency doctrine in urban policing — all of these reflect the seepage of imperial methods back into domestic governance. The Black Lives Matter movement has named this connection repeatedly; activists in Ferguson in 2014 documented military equipment used against protesters and connected it to the war in Iraq. The line from Fallujah to Ferguson is not metaphorical. It is institutional.

This means that the project of demilitarizing American foreign policy and the project of demilitarizing American policing are not separate. They are the same project at different scales. A freedom politics that names this connection — that says ending the global war on terror and ending the militarized occupation of Black communities are part of the same struggle — gains both moral coherence and political power. It also avoids the false choice that establishment Democrats often present, in which one can oppose Republican excess at home while accepting the bipartisan imperial consensus abroad.

Third, the global solidarities that an anti-imperial freedom politics can build. Rana mentions the New International Economic Order, Manley, Nyerere, and the broader Third Worldist project that flourished briefly in the 1960s and 1970s before being crushed by the debt crisis, structural adjustment, and the consolidation of neoliberal globalization. This history matters not just as a historical reference point but as a living tradition. The contemporary Global South is again articulating demands that echo the NIEO — for climate reparations, for debt cancellation, for sovereign control over natural resources, for an international financial system that does not systematically transfer wealth from poor countries to rich ones. South African President Cyril Ramaphosa's recent invocation of the genocide convention against Israel, Brazilian President Lula da Silva's call for a multipolar global order, the BRICS expansion this year, the African Union's accession to the G20 — these are not random developments. They are the emergence of a new round of Global South political assertion, and they create opportunities for American left movements to build solidarities that domestic politics alone cannot generate.

The connection to domestic freedom is direct. If American voters can come to understand that the same Pentagon budget that bankrolls forever wars also crowds out their child care, their health care, their housing — and if they can understand that the same imperial logic that detains migrants at the border also disenfranchises Puerto Ricans and props up dictators in countries those migrants are fleeing — then a coherent left freedom politics becomes available. Without that international dimension, domestic reformism keeps running into the wall of the security state, which absorbs resources and political authority that could otherwise go elsewhere.

Where I would push Rana further is on the question of Israel-Palestine specifically. He mentions it briefly and rightly notes the dissonance between American support for the bombing campaign and the freedom-language Washington deploys. But the Palestinian question is, I would argue, the central test of any contemporary anti-imperial freedom politics. Palestine has functioned for decades as the limit-case of liberal universalism: the place where the language of human rights, self-determination, and freedom is most loudly affirmed in principle and most systematically denied in practice. American politicians who happily invoke Ukraine's right to self-determination cannot bring themselves to affirm Palestinian self-determination on remotely comparable terms. The double standard is not a hypocrisy to be corrected through better messaging; it reflects structural commitments of American foreign policy that no amount of rhetorical adjustment will dissolve.

A left freedom politics worthy of the name has to be willing to break the Palestinian taboo — to insist that Palestinian self-determination, Palestinian sovereignty, and Palestinian freedom from occupation and siege are non-negotiable. This is happening, in the streets, on campuses, in unions that have begun passing ceasefire resolutions, in writers and intellectuals refusing to participate in cultural institutions that whitewash the bombardment. It is the most vital anti-imperial mobilization of this generation, and any project to reclaim freedom-language for the left should begin from it.

Rana's essay points in this direction. The next step is to follow the pointing.

A Democratic Pragmatist's Dissent

Aziz Rana's essay is a thoughtful work of intellectual history grafted onto a political program. The history is interesting. The political program, I want to suggest, is mostly wishful thinking dressed up in academic prose, and Democrats who take it seriously will lose more elections than they win.

Begin with the diagnosis. Rana says the right's hold on freedom-language reflects deep structural features of American history — settler conquest, imperial primacy, the entanglement of racial domination with individualist liberty. As a historical thesis this is partly true. As a guide to current politics it is nearly useless. The voters Democrats need to win — suburban moderates, exurban independents, working-class voters across racial lines — are not making decisions based on Lockean settler ideology. They are making decisions based on inflation, immigration, crime, schools, and whether they trust the person on the ballot to be a reasonable steward of public institutions. To address them with a vocabulary borrowed from postcolonial theory is to lose them before the argument starts.

Consider the recent electoral evidence. In 2022, Democrats overperformed expectations, holding the Senate and minimizing House losses, primarily by running on three issues: defending democracy against election denial, defending reproductive freedom against Dobbs, and casting MAGA Republicans as too extreme for mainstream voters. None of these messages required a settler-imperial reframing of American freedom. They worked because they spoke directly to threats voters could see and feared. Democratic governors elected in red and purple states — Whitmer in Michigan, Shapiro in Pennsylvania, Kelly in Kansas — won by speaking ordinary English, addressing pocketbook concerns, and presenting themselves as reasonable adults. They did not win by reading Adom Getachew.

Rana's program, by contrast, is a wishlist of long-term structural reforms — sectoral bargaining, decriminalized immigration, defense cuts of historic magnitude, voting reforms that would require constitutional amendments or Supreme Court overhauls, reparative policies, presumptive admission for migrants. Each of these is, on its merits, debatable. Few of them command majority support in current polling. Several of them are political poison in the swing districts that decide American elections. Bundling them together as the program of a "freedom" politics does not make them more popular; it makes them more vulnerable to caricature, because it invites opponents to point at the most controversial item in the bundle and ask voters whether they really want that.

Decriminalize border crossings? That is currently the position of approximately ten percent of the American electorate. Defense cuts to fund redistribution? Try running on that in Norfolk or San Diego. Reparations? Polling consistently shows majority opposition, including among substantial minorities of Black voters who find existing proposals inadequate or symbolic. None of these positions are unreasonable as policy debates. As electoral platforms, in the country we actually live in, they are catastrophic.

The pragmatist response to the right's appropriation of freedom-language is simpler and more effective than Rana's structural project. It is to take the language back, item by item, on issues where ordinary Americans already feel their freedom under threat from conservative governance, and to do so without the freight of academic theorizing.

Reproductive freedom: working, demonstrably, in state after state. Keep using it.

Freedom to read, freedom from book bans, freedom from religious imposition in public schools: working in suburban districts where parents have soured on conservative culture-war overreach.

Economic freedom from corporate domination: works in messaging when paired with specific antitrust, labor, and consumer protection enforcement. Lina Khan's FTC has done more for left freedom-talk than a dozen think tank essays.

Freedom from gun violence: working with younger voters and suburban women.

Freedom of the press, freedom of association, freedom from politically motivated prosecution: works when the threat is concretely named (Trump's promises of retribution, state-level censorship laws).

Notice what these have in common. They are individual freedoms threatened by specific, identifiable conservative policies. They speak the negative-liberty vocabulary that ordinary Americans recognize. They do not require a settler-imperial reframing of the entire history of American thought. They just require Democrats to say "freedom" clearly and loudly and on issues where they have the high ground.

Rana objects that this is mere messaging, and that without institutional transformation the rhetoric will not stick. But Democrats winning elections is itself a precondition for institutional transformation. There is no left freedom politics if Republicans control the federal government and pack the Supreme Court for another generation. The institutional reforms Rana wants — voting rights, labor law, public investment — require Democratic governing majorities. Those majorities will not materialize from a freedom politics that begins from the premise that ordinary Americans' intuitions about liberty are settler-imperial residues to be dismantled.

There is a related problem of self-marginalization. Rana writes from the academic left, addresses the academic left, and proposes a politics that the academic left would find compelling. This is not the same as a politics that wins elections in Wisconsin. The academic left has been getting a great deal of intellectual attention in the past decade — through outlets like Boston Review, n+1, Dissent, Jacobin — without translating that attention into electoral power. Indeed, in some cases, the rhetorical style of academic left freedom-talk has actively repelled the working-class voters of all races whom that left ostensibly seeks to represent.

I am not arguing that the left should abandon its analysis or its ambitions. I am arguing that the analysis Rana offers is not actually useful for the political work he says it serves. Better analyses are available, and they are more pragmatic and more electorally productive. They begin from the observation that American voters across demographic lines value freedom, that they have specific and recognizable views about what threats to their freedom are, and that Democrats can win by addressing those threats in language voters already understand.

The right's grip on freedom-language is real but not unshakeable. It is being shaken right now by the post-Dobbs reproductive rights movement, which is winning ballot initiatives in red states by speaking simple, individualistic, negative-liberty freedom-talk. It is being shaken by the growing recognition that conservatives, not progressives, are the ones banning books, restricting medical care, jailing librarians, and threatening political opponents with prosecution. The opening is real. The path through it does not require dismantling the entire American liberal tradition. It requires Democratic politicians to speak plainly, identify their opponents accurately, and run on issues where ordinary Americans already agree with them.

That is a different freedom politics from Rana's. It is also one that might actually win.

Recovering the Republican Tradition

Aziz Rana's essay would be strengthened by more explicit engagement with a particular philosophical tradition that has been quietly enjoying a revival in Anglo-American political theory over the past three decades: civic republicanism, in the sense developed by Quentin Skinner, Philip Pettit, and (in slightly different registers) Michael Sandel, Iseult Honohan, and Frank Lovett. Rana gestures toward this tradition without quite naming it. His core distinction — between freedom as "not being subject to someone else's arbitrary whims" and freedom as "experiencing independence and control over the basic conditions of one's life" — sounds very much like Pettit's distinction between freedom as non-domination and freedom as non-interference. Naming the tradition explicitly would, I think, strengthen Rana's argument and connect it to a body of philosophical work that gives it sharper edges.

The central republican insight is that liberty is undermined not only by actual interference but by exposure to the arbitrary will of another, whether or not that will is exercised against you. A slave whose master happens to be kind is not free; he is dominated, because the master could at any moment exercise his power differently, and the slave has no institutional recourse. Skinner's recovery of this conception in works like Liberty Before Liberalism (1998) showed that the neo-Roman tradition of liberty as non-domination was central to early modern republican thought and was systematically displaced by Hobbes and his successors, who narrowed liberty to non-interference. Pettit's Republicanism (1997) and subsequent work developed this into a contemporary political theory whose policy implications — strong democratic accountability, contestatory institutions, anti-corruption measures, protection against unaccountable private power — overlap substantially with Rana's program.

Why does this matter for Rana's argument? Because the republican tradition offers a vocabulary of freedom that is both indigenous to the American constitutional inheritance and explicitly committed to the institutional and structural commitments Rana wants to advance. American revolutionaries did not think of themselves as Lockean individualists; they thought of themselves as inheritors of a republican tradition stretching from Rome through Machiavelli, Harrington, and the English commonwealthmen. Their concept of liberty was self-government — the capacity of a free people to make their own laws, free from the domination of foreign or domestic tyranny. The transformation of this tradition into the negative-liberty individualism of nineteenth and twentieth century liberalism was a historical accomplishment, not a logical inevitability. Recovering the older meaning is not a foreign import; it is a return to the American republic's own founding self-understanding.

This recovery does work that Rana's settler-imperial framework alone cannot do. It allows the left to make the case for democratic and labor reforms in vocabulary that is recognizable to constitutionally-minded Americans, including conservatives. The argument for labor democracy, for instance, becomes: workers exposed to arbitrary employer power are dominated, even when individual employers happen to be benign. Liberty requires institutional protection against this domination, which is what unions provide. This is a freedom argument, not just a fairness argument, and it draws on resources older and deeper than the welfare state liberalism that the right has spent fifty years discrediting.

Similarly with antitrust. Concentrated corporate power dominates consumers, workers, suppliers, and communities even when particular firms behave reasonably. The republican tradition gives us vocabulary for naming this — the dispersal of power as a condition of liberty — that is much harder for opponents to dismiss as socialism. Senator Elizabeth Warren has occasionally drawn on this tradition; Tim Wu has written explicitly about the antimonopoly tradition as a freedom tradition. There is more here for Rana's project than he has yet drawn upon.

Republican freedom also bears specifically on Rana's foreign policy concerns. The classical republican tradition held that imperial overreach corrupts republican liberty at home — the very thesis Rana wants to defend. Polybius, Sallust, Machiavelli, Montesquieu, and the American antifederalists all worried about how empire abroad produces tyranny at home. This is not a left-wing thesis; it is a deeply conservative one, in the older sense of conserving the institutions of free self-government. Some of the most prescient critics of post-9/11 American foreign policy — Andrew Bacevich, for instance — have written explicitly in this republican tradition. Rana's anti-imperialism would gain coalition partners by being framed in this vocabulary.

Now for a friendly criticism. Rana sometimes blurs the distinction between two different things that republican theory carefully separates: the elimination of domination (a defensible and powerful ideal) and the achievement of full collective self-rule (a more ambitious and more contestable one). Pettit's contemporary republicanism has been careful to argue that non-domination does not require continuous active participation by all citizens in all decisions. It requires institutional structures — separation of powers, judicial review, contestatory procedures, transparency, accountable representation — that protect citizens against arbitrary power without demanding that they spend their lives in political meetings. This is a more politically viable program than the "continuous exercise in participation" Rana sometimes seems to call for, and it answers some of the libertarian objections to positive-liberty traditions.

The distinction matters because many Americans, given a choice, do not want to participate continuously in collective self-government, and a freedom politics that demands they do so will struggle to find them. What they do want is to live in a society where they are not at the mercy of arbitrary power — bosses who can fire them at will, landlords who can evict them on whim, insurance companies that can deny claims for opaque reasons, police who can stop them without cause, governments that can surveil them without restraint. This gendered division of freedom did not quietly disappear as the frontier closed. It was reconfigured through the rise of the family wage, the New Deal’s two-tiered welfare state, and the postwar middle-class order that Rana acknowledges as the mid-twentieth-century “bargain.” The ideal of the male breadwinner who enjoyed corporate benefits, a pension, and healthcare through his employer was the apotheosis of a certain kind of white masculine freedom. But it depended on the unpaid or low-paid care work of women, and it rendered women vulnerable to domestic violence, economic marginalization, and a political system that treated them as secondary. When Rana quotes the son of a steelworker celebrating liberation in the 1950s, we must ask: liberation for whom? For the steelworker’s wife, whose destiny was circumscribed by the kitchen, the nursery, and the expectation of cheerful subordination, that era felt less like liberation and more like a gilded cage.

The right’s contemporary appropriation of freedom is not just a matter of market fundamentalism and white nationalism; it is also a deeply gendered project. The “Freedom Caucus” does not merely oppose government social programs; it opposes reproductive freedom, seeking to compel pregnancy and childbirth as the price of female sexuality. Rana’s framework, for all its critique of exclusion, does not give this reality the centrality it deserves.

Conversely, the most vital left invocations of freedom in recent years have been driven precisely by the struggle against patriarchal control. The movement for reproductive justice, which Rana mentions only in passing, has insisted that true freedom for women requires not merely the negative right to an abortion, but the positive capacity to raise children in safety and dignity. The #MeToo movement reframed the workplace not just as a space of class conflict but as a site of sexual coercion, linking economic precarity to vulnerability to harassment and assault. These movements enact a freedom politics that is at once deeply personal and structurally transformative, and they have succeeded in ways that labor and anti-imperial politics have not in shifting mainstream conversations about what it means to be free. Their relative absence from Rana’s analysis is a missed opportunity, because they demonstrate that institution-building need not wait for a far-off post-neoliberal future; it is already happening in the networks of care, mutual aid, and legal advocacy that these movements have built.

A freedom project that takes gender seriously would have to revise some of Rana’s prescriptions. The workplace democracy he champions remains essential, but it must be paired with a reorganization of care work—through universal childcare, paid family leave, and wage models that value the reproductive labor that sustains all other labor. Immigration reform, too, must account for the ways in which migrant women experience distinct forms of exploitation, from domestic servitude to sexual violence, and ensure that decriminalization does not simply mainstream a low-wage female workforce into the casualized margins. And the anti-imperial dimension must confront the reality that U.S. hegemony has often exported gendered violence abroad, from the toleration of abusive regimes to the direct military targeting of women’s bodies as a tactic of war.

But the silence on gender suggests a lingering blindness to the way domination has always been structured within the very spaces the left seeks to democratize. Reclaiming freedom means not only dismantling the settler-imperial edifice but also tearing down the patriarchal walls that have enclosed the household, the bedroom, and the body. Aziz Rana’s “A Different Freedom” is a serious and learned attempt to understand the peculiar career of “freedom” in American life. Rana’s narrative traces the roots of a corrosive freedom-talk to the settler project: the availability of land—conquered and expropriated—enabled a vision of independence for white men that did not require interdependence with others. The frontier, he argues, acted as a safety valve, smoothing class contradictions and making government regulation feel like an imposition rather than a necessity. Empire in the twentieth century performed a similar function, with U.S. global primacy subsidizing a domestic middle class and entrenching the idea that economic security was a matter of private bargaining rather than public provision. This is an important story, and it rightly points to the material underpinnings of ideology. The very experience of freedom Rana idealizes as a potential alternative—collective self-rule through democratic control of the workplace and society—is systematically foreclosed by the daily operation of the market. Under capitalism, firms that do not prioritize profitability and the extraction of surplus value are disciplined by capital flight, credit ratings, and hostile takeovers. Even the most well-intentioned co-ops and union shops must compete in a global market that compels cost-cutting and labor discipline. It was, in crucial respects, a class compromise that left capitalist property rights essentially intact. When the post-war boom waned and corporate America went on the offensive, that compromise proved reversible. The lesson is not that we need a more robust social democracy buttressed by anti-imperialism; it is that any freedom regime that leaves the investment decisions and profit motive of capital in place is contingent and precarious.

The contemporary moment bears this out starkly. The real content of freedom under capitalism has always been the freedom of capital to move, to invest or disinvest, to hire and fire—a freedom that enforces a despotism over the vast majority. Democrats and social democrats who promise to tame capitalism through regulation and redistribution are not so different from the right-wing libertarian in the grand scheme; both accept the fundamental premise that the major allocative decisions of society should remain in private hands, driven by the imperative of profit. Rana’s call for institution-building is right, but the institutions must be revolutionary, not reformist. Rana wants to replace it with a collectivist, democratic vision of “self-rule” that elevates the group over the individual and mistakes majority participation for liberation. But the record of the twentieth century shows that such visions, when implemented, lead not to a broader freedom but to its dissolution.

Rana’s fundamental move is to collapse the distinction between a principled commitment to limited government and the historical crimes committed by those who invoked liberty while oppressing others. That some settlers used libertarian rhetoric to justify land theft no more discredits the principle of individual rights than King Nyerere’s use of solidarity rhetoric to justify one-party rule discredits the ideal of community. Ideas must be judged on their own merits, not on the worst behavior of those who claim them. The liberal tradition, from Locke to Hayek, has consistently maintained that freedom is fundamentally a condition of non-domination by other persons—the security to live one’s life according to one’s own plans without being subject to the arbitrary will of a master, whether that master is a slaveholder, a bureaucrat, or a democratic majority. This is not “zero-sum” in the way Rana imagines. It is universalizable: my right to be free from coercion is identical to yours. A society ordered around this principle demands that all are equally protected.

Rana might reply that this purely negative freedom is hollow without the “experience of self-rule.” But here he runs headlong into a contradiction. He wants people to enjoy genuine control over their lives, but he simultaneously argues that this can only happen through collective decision-making in workplaces and political institutions. The problem is that collective self-rule is not, for any particular individual, self-rule at all; it is subjection to the group. If I am outvoted by my coworkers on a major question of production, or by my fellow citizens on the allocation of tax dollars, I am exactly in the position of having my life directed by others. Rana may call this “solidarity,” but it is merely a softer name for coercion. The libertarian insight is that true self-mastery is precisely the protected ability to say no to the collective, to opt out, to chart a separate course. That is why the core institutions of a free society are private property, voluntary contract, and a minimal state restricted to protecting those rights. Without them, the individual is absorbed into the mass.

The typical conservative or libertarian voter who wants lower taxes, less regulation, and the right to bear arms is not fantasizing about racial domination. Indeed, the greatest victims of state power have been precisely those Rana wants to shelter: racial minorities, political radicals, the poor.
