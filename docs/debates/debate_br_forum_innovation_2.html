Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Breznitz's essay performs a valuable service by puncturing the cargo-cult mentality that has gripped local economic development since the 1990s. Walk into almost any mayor's office from Columbus to Cairo and you will find the same PowerPoint slides: incubators, accelerators, "innovation districts," tax breaks for venture capital, a science park named after some local notable. The implicit theory is that if you assemble enough of the visible furniture of Silicon Valley, the magic that produced Google and Facebook will somehow materialize. Breznitz is right that this almost never works, and that even when something like it does work—as in Israel—the social results are often dismaying.

Where I want to push back, or at least complicate, is in three areas: the cleanness of the four-stage taxonomy, the political economy of choosing a stage, and the question of what the Silicon Valley model actually is.

First, the four stages. Breznitz presents Shimano, Giant, MediaTek, Brenta footwear, and Shenzhen assembly as exemplars of distinct innovational "stages," each capable of supporting inclusive prosperity if cultivated correctly. The framework is illuminating as a corrective—it forces us to see that Apple's industrial designers and TSMC's process engineers and Foxconn's logistics planners are all doing innovation, not merely executing on Cupertino's ideas. But in practice these stages bleed into one another. Giant did not stay a stage-three improver; its work on carbon fiber frames is, by any reasonable definition, novel product development. MediaTek's reference designs are arguably as inventive as anything Qualcomm produces. The Pearl River Delta's "stage four" prowess increasingly drives stage-one decisions upstream, because firms like DJI emerged precisely because Shenzhen's assembly ecosystem made it cheap to iterate on drone prototypes. The danger of a four-stage taxonomy is that policymakers, who love nothing more than a checklist, may treat the stages as discrete bins to be chosen between rather than as overlapping capabilities that reinforce each other.

Second, the political economy. Breznitz is admirably blunt that to excel at one stage you must forgo others, and that public-private leadership is required to make these choices. But the essay is curiously quiet about who, in a place like Youngstown or Saint-Étienne, is supposed to do the choosing. Taiwan's Industrial Technology Research Institute, the Israeli Innovation Authority, and the Brenta footwear consortium are products of unusual institutional histories: developmental states emerging from existential security threats, regional craft traditions with centuries of accumulated tacit knowledge, post-authoritarian industrial planning capacity. When American cities try to copy these models, they typically run into the same problem they encountered with Silicon Valley emulation—you cannot conjure the institutional substrate by force of will. The U.S. lacks a national innovation agency on the ITRI model; its community colleges are starved of operating funds; its industrial extension service (the Manufacturing Extension Partnership) operates on a budget that would embarrass a mid-sized Taiwanese ministry. Telling Cleveland to "become a stage-three hub" without addressing this institutional deficit risks producing yet another round of disappointment.

Third, on what Silicon Valley actually is. Breznitz frames the Valley as a stage-one VC-and-exit machine, and for the post-1995 Valley this is largely accurate. But the deeper history is more interesting and more supportive of Breznitz's own argument than he acknowledges. The Valley that produced Fairchild, Intel, Hewlett-Packard, and even the early Apple was itself a stage-two-and-three hub: it had fabs, it had production engineering, it had thousands of mid-skill manufacturing jobs, and it had massive sustained public investment via Defense Department procurement. The thing we now call "Silicon Valley"—an asset-light, software-and-finance complex producing extreme inequality—is what the original Silicon Valley turned into after it offshored its production capacity. So the cautionary tale is not just "don't copy Silicon Valley"; it is "don't let your successful stage-one cluster hollow out its connections to the other stages," which is exactly what Schwinn did and what the U.S. semiconductor industry mostly did before TSMC's belated invitation to Arizona.

This sharpens Breznitz's policy conclusions in a useful way. The "tech teens" argument is the strongest part of the essay, and it deserves more emphasis than it gets. Companies in the five-to-fifteen-year range, with revenue, real customers, and local roots, are systematically underserved by both public policy and private capital, which are calibrated to the bimodal distribution of seed-stage moonshots and Fortune 500 giants. The Small Business Innovation Research program, the Manufacturing Extension Partnership, and procurement-based instruments like Operation Warp Speed are tools that can be expanded without waiting for Washington to develop ITRI-grade planning capacity. They are the kind of unglamorous instruments that Breznitz's argument actually calls for.

One last note: Breznitz says little about the labor side of inclusive prosperity. Taiwan and Germany sustain mid-skill jobs not only through stage-three innovation but through vocational training systems, sectoral wage bargaining, and labor-market institutions that the United States has largely dismantled. A community that becomes a stage-three hub without union density or wage standards will discover that "good jobs at all skill levels" do not automatically follow from innovation capacity. Innovation policy and labor policy must be designed together, or the inclusive part of inclusive prosperity will once again go missing.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Dan Breznitz’s sweeping critique of the Silicon Valley model is both timely and necessary. In the wake of COVID-19, as communities scramble to rebuild, his warning against chasing shimmering mirages of high-tech start-ups and venture capital is a vital antidote to the boosterism that has drained local coffers from Kansas to Kazakhstan. His framework—distinguishing innovation that yields inclusive prosperity from the winner-take-all dynamics of stage-one novelty—offers a granular and hopeful map for economic renewal. Yet in his eagerness to debunk myths, Breznitz at times underplays the symbiosis among the four stages he describes, and skates too quickly over the political economy of their implementation. Without addressing these tensions, his otherwise compelling blueprint risks replacing one kind of cargo-cult thinking with another.

Breznitz’s account of Israel’s high-tech miracle is a case in point. He rightly shows that an obsessive focus on NASDAQ exits and foreign VC widened Israel’s gulf between a “geek elite” and the rest, leaving a fifth of households in relative poverty. But the problem was not stage-one innovation itself; it was the way Israel’s innovation agency, for all its foresight, embedded its tech sector almost entirely within U.S. financial circuits. Israeli firms innovated, then handed profits and intellectual property over to overseas investors. That extractive design is a political choice, not an inherent feature of novel-product development. Taiwan, which Breznitz celebrates for its stage-three prowess, has also attracted vast foreign capital into its semiconductor foundries. Yet Taiwan’s model—with state-led research institutes, local ownership structures, and dense supplier networks—kept the gains circulating domestically. The lesson is not to abandon stage one for later stages but to demand that the fruits of any innovation stage be anchored locally through deliberate policy. Tech teens, which Breznitz champions as better rooted in community, could thrive in stage one too, if given patient capital and public procurement, as Operation Warp Speed demonstrated. The SiO2 success in Alabama was a stage-one breakthrough in material science, funded by the government, that simultaneously expanded local employment. The villain is not novelty but the exit-obsessed venture model.

A second strain in Breznitz’s argument is that communities must choose which stage to specialize in, cultivating “particular capacities—to the exclusion of others.” This makes strategic sense in a global division of labor, but it understates how stages feed on one another in the medium term. The ascent of MediaTek, a Taiwanese stage-three champion, relied on stage-one breakthroughs in integrated-circuit design from the wider industry, along with advances from global giants like Qualcomm and ARM, whose early R&D was supported by U.S. public investment. If every region had abandoned stage-one research in favor of incremental improvement, the technological frontier would stall—harming even those who specialize in making existing products better and cheaper. The United States’ belated panic over losing semiconductor fabrication capacity is illustrative: by allowing stage-three capabilities to wither, America jeopardized its own stage-one design firms, which depend on close collaboration with cutting-edge fabs. Regions do need to focus, but the most resilient ecosystems—like Taiwan’s or even Silicon Valley’s own early decades—maintain a strategic mix across stages, often with public institutions bridging the gaps. The binary “pick your stage” framing may mislead local leaders into creating another kind of brittle monoculture.

Then there is the question of what “inclusive prosperity” actually means across Breznitz’s successful examples. He praises the Pearl River Delta’s stage-four innovation for creating millions of jobs and lifting migrants out of rural subsistence. That is no small achievement. But by 2021, the region had become synonymous with grueling working conditions, wage suppression, and rampant labor-rights violations that critics have likened to a new form of exploitation. Shenzhen may have offered a path out of deeper poverty, but the quality of its inclusion is heavily contested. Brenta’s shoe-design ecosystem, by contrast, relies on highly skilled craftspeople and has secured a premium position in global luxury supply chains—yet it serves only a narrow, artisanal labor market. Taiwan’s stage-three model has kept inequality relatively low, but it has done so in a society with a distinct history of state-led development, universal education, and small-firm networks that are not easily replicable. Breznitz is right that stage-two and stage-three innovation can support a wider range of skills, but his case studies hint that context—especially labor protection regimes, education systems, and the political will to redistribute—determines whether “inclusive” means decent wages for many or a slightly larger middle class amid stark divides.

Breznitz is on firmer ground when he calls for robust public goods: collaborative spaces, shared production facilities, and active networks that stitch industries into communities. Yet the political conditions for such investment are fragile. The Biden administration’s early pushes for industrial policy have been beset by partisan gridlock, and the Trump administration’s last-minute plea to TSMC to build fabs in Arizona came with no coherent plan for training workers or developing the supplier base that makes Taiwanese foundries so agile. Brenta’s artisanal revival and Taiwan’s institute-led partnerships each emerged from decades of sustained, often undemocratic, government steering. Today’s local leaders may lack the patience or power to replicate those conditions, especially when they are pressured to show quick wins to taxpayers and bond markets.

Ultimately, Breznitz’s essay succeeds in its primary mission: smashing the myth that Silicon Valley is the one true path. But the response should not be to then elevate stage two, three, or four as magic bullets. The deeper insight—which the essay gestures toward but does not fully explore—is that the governance of innovation trumps its stage. Equity, local ownership, reinvestment of profits, and the creation of high-road employment depend on deliberate institutional design, not just on finding the “right” niche in the global production chain. As communities scarred by deindustrialization and pandemic disruptions weigh their next moves, they would do well to heed Breznitz’s call to look beyond high-tech hype. But they must also resist the lure of any single-stage template, and instead build the messy, adaptive, and deeply political ecosystems that let innovation of many kinds serve the many, not the few.

Dan Breznitz offers a deeply necessary corrective to the most pervasive and destructive economic mythology of our time: the belief that the only path to local prosperity is to become the next Silicon Valley. By decoupling the concept of “innovation” from the narrow realm of venture-backed software startups and financial exits, Breznitz provides a much broader, more realistic map of global economic development. His categorization of innovation into four distinct stages—novelty, design, continuous component innovation, and assembly—rightly highlights the dignity, complexity, and massive economic potential of manufacturing and incremental improvement. However, while his framework is historically illuminating, it underestimates the hierarchical power dynamics of the global value chain and overlooks the glaring vulnerabilities of geographic specialization exposed by the current global crisis.

Breznitz treats his four stages of innovation as a menu of parallel choices for local communities, suggesting that a region can simply choose to specialize in stage two (design), stage three (component innovation), or stage four (assembly) to achieve inclusive prosperity. But these stages do not exist on an equal footing; they are bound together by a ruthless economic hierarchy often described by the "smile curve" of global production. The vast majority of profits, and therefore geopolitical and economic power, are captured at the two ends of the curve: stage one novelty and design, and post-production branding and marketing. The middle stages, particularly stage four assembly, capture the least value.

While Breznitz rightly notes that China’s Pearl River Delta has achieved massive employment through stage four innovation, he also admits these companies operate on "razor-thin profit margins." This is a precarious foundation for inclusive, long-term prosperity. The Foxconns of the world may employ millions, but they remain heavily dependent on the stage one innovators—like Apple—who dictate the terms, control the intellectual property, and absorb the overwhelming lion’s share of the profit. Furthermore, when stage one giants change their mind, the downstream communities suffer instantly. To abandon the pursuit of stage one innovation is to accept a permanent position as a subcontractor in an ecosystem controlled by others. Taiwan’s TSMC is the rare exception where a stage three/four company achieved such absolute monopoly power that it captured top-tier margins, but this required decades of state-backed industrial policy that few other regions could replicate today.

More urgently, Breznitz’s vision of globally dispersed innovation stages rests on an assumption of frictionless, hyper-globalized trade—an assumption that has spectacularly collapsed before our eyes over the last eighteen months. Writing in the fall of 2021, we are witnessing an unprecedented global supply chain crisis. Dozens of cargo ships are currently idling off the ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach. Major American and European automakers are halting production lines not because they forgot how to build cars, but because they cannot source basic semiconductor components from the specialized stage three hubs in East Asia.

The very geographic fragmentation of innovation that Breznitz praises has created crippling global bottlenecks. When Taiwan dominates stage three semiconductor innovation and Shenzhen dominates stage four assembly, a single pandemic, a localized power crisis in China, or a geopolitical flare-up can paralyze the entire global economy. In this light, the Biden administration's push to bring TSMC to the United States is not merely a belated realization of lost stage three innovation, as Breznitz suggests, but a vital matter of national security. The era of optimizing strictly for specialized nodes in a diffuse global supply chain is ending. If local communities are to achieve true resilience moving forward, the conversation must shift from "which single global stage should we specialize in" to "how can we rebuild vertically integrated, regionalized ecosystems."

Finally, Breznitz’s prescriptions for the United States—such as supporting "tech teens" and fostering continuous component innovation—fail to fully account for the structural financial realities of the American market. The United States struggles to emulate the German *Mittelstand* or Taiwan's network of specialized mid-sized manufacturers not simply because local leaders have been seduced by the Silicon Valley myth, but because American capital markets actively punish the slow, capital-intensive work of incremental innovation.

Wall Street and the private equity sector demand rapid scaling, quarterly returns, and high-margin intellectual property. A promising American "tech teen" focused on stage three hardware innovation is incredibly vulnerable in this environment. Long before it can become an anchor for inclusive local prosperity, it is likely to be starved of patient capital, acquired by a larger monopolistic tech giant, or stripped for parts by private equity. Without fundamental reforms to corporate governance, antitrust enforcement, and financial incentives, asking an American city to simply pivot to stage three innovation is like asking a farmer to plant seeds in concrete. The ecosystem Breznitz desires requires a financial architecture that prioritizes long-term stakeholder value over short-term shareholder extraction.

Breznitz is absolutely right that we must abandon the techno-fetishism that worships app developers while ignoring industrial engineers. The path out of the boom-and-bust cycle of the Cobalt silver rush is indeed continuous, multi-tiered innovation. But as the global supply chain fractures and the vulnerabilities of hyper-specialization become undeniable, we cannot rely on the fragmented globalization of the 1990s and 2000s as our guide. The inclusive prosperity of the future will not be found by simply picking a single niche in a brittle global assembly line, but by rebuilding robust, self-sustaining regional economies capable of carrying an idea from invention all the way to the factory floor.

Dan Breznitz is right to insist that innovation is not synonymous with venture-backed invention, and that the Silicon Valley model is a poor guide for places seeking broad-based prosperity. In fact, the most useful part of his argument is not the criticism of Silicon Valley itself, which many people now share, but the disaggregation of innovation into different stages. Once we see innovation as design, engineering, production, maintenance, scaling, and continuous improvement—not only as the launch of a new platform or app—we can begin to imagine economic strategies that are less glamorous but more democratic.

Still, the hardest question is not whether there are many forms of innovation. There are. The harder question is how to make any of them inclusive under contemporary capitalism. The danger is that policymakers will replace one fetish with another: instead of fetishizing startups and venture capital, they may fetishize “advanced manufacturing,” “clusters,” “supply chains,” or “ecosystems” without confronting the institutions that determine who captures the gains. A region can be innovative in production and still generate precarious work, low wages, environmental damage, and political dependency. The lesson to draw from Silicon Valley’s failures is not simply that we should innovate at other stages of production. It is that innovation policy must be joined to labor policy, social policy, competition policy, and democratic planning.

Consider Silicon Valley itself. Its early history was not only a story of entrepreneurs and venture capitalists; it was also a story of federal procurement, Cold War spending, university research, immigration policy, and a dense regional labor market. Public money helped create the technological base, while private firms captured much of the upside. Over time, as the region shifted toward software platforms, network effects, intellectual property, and financialized exits, the distance between winners and everyone else widened. Housing costs exploded, service workers were pushed into punishing commutes, and local governments competed to host firms whose tax strategies often minimized their local contribution. The problem was not innovation as such. The problem was a political economy that allowed innovation to become a mechanism for extraction.

That same risk exists in the alternative models Breznitz describes. Taiwan’s semiconductor ecosystem is rightly admired, and the United States has much to learn from it. But the lesson is not simply “build fabs.” Semiconductor production requires enormous capital investment, patient coordination, technical training, supplier depth, and reliable demand. It also requires decisions about water, energy, land use, and the distribution of public subsidies. If the United States pays foreign or domestic firms billions of dollars to locate plants in Arizona, Ohio, or Texas without demanding strong labor standards, technology diffusion, supplier development, and local training, it may get impressive facilities without durable regional prosperity. A fab can be a cathedral in the desert unless it is embedded in a broader institutional system.

The same is true of stage four production and assembly. Shenzhen’s rise is one of the most important economic stories of the last forty years. Its capacity to prototype, source, assemble, and scale at astonishing speed is a genuine form of innovation. But the Pearl River Delta also grew through migrant labor systems, long hours, dormitory work regimes, and environmental costs that should not be treated as incidental. To call production innovation “inclusive” because it employs millions risks setting the bar too low. Employment matters enormously, especially compared with jobless growth. But inclusion must also mean voice, mobility, safety, bargaining power, and the ability to build a stable life.

This point matters for American regions trying to recover from deindustrialization. Many communities do not just lack startups; they lack institutional power. They have lost anchor firms, union density, vocational systems, local banks, technical schools, and the civic associations that once connected business decisions to community welfare. When a city or state chases the next big employer with tax abatements, it is often negotiating from desperation. The resulting jobs may be better than nothing, but they rarely rebuild the local capacity to shape economic development. Amazon’s HQ2 competition was an extreme example of this dynamic, but smaller versions happen constantly. Public officials offer subsidies; firms promise jobs; the public receives little guarantee of long-term commitment.

Breznitz’s emphasis on “tech teens” is therefore especially promising. Firms that are past the fragile startup phase but not yet dominant are often more rooted in place and more likely to need exactly the kinds of public goods that governments can provide: testing facilities, export assistance, workforce training, procurement contracts, and help meeting regulatory standards. Operation Warp Speed’s support for vaccine-related manufacturing showed that public procurement can do more than buy finished goods; it can create capabilities. The same principle could apply to heat pumps, grid equipment, batteries, medical supplies, public transit, building retrofits, and low-carbon industrial materials. Decarbonization is not merely an environmental imperative. It is a chance to organize demand for useful production.

But public support should come with public obligations. If taxpayers help firms scale, taxpayers should not merely hope that benefits trickle down. Contracts can require domestic or regional production where appropriate, prevailing wages, apprenticeship programs, neutrality toward union organizing, profit-sharing, limits on stock buybacks, and commitments to local supplier development. Public equity stakes or revenue-sharing arrangements may also be justified when the state assumes major technological or market risk. This is not hostility to business. It is recognition that innovation is collective, and that collective investment should produce collective returns.

The United States has some institutional models to build on. Community colleges, land-grant universities, manufacturing extension partnerships, national laboratories, and sectoral training initiatives can all support non-Silicon Valley innovation. Germany’s Fraunhofer Institutes are often invoked for a reason: they help translate research into industrial capability, especially for small and medium-size firms. The American Manufacturing USA institutes created over the last decade point in a similar direction, though they remain too small relative to the scale of the challenge. The problem is not an absence of examples; it is the weakness and fragmentation of the American developmental state.

A further challenge is that local strategy cannot be purely local. Breznitz’s examples are place-based, but they succeed in relation to national and global systems. Taiwan’s achievements depended on national industrial policy, export discipline, public research institutions, and geopolitical positioning. Italian industrial districts depended on craft traditions, regional institutions, and access to high-value European markets. Israeli high tech depended on military research, immigration, and deep integration with U.S. capital markets. Local leaders matter, but they cannot overcome every macroeconomic force. Exchange rates, trade rules, antitrust enforcement, health care costs, infrastructure, and federal research spending all shape what localities can plausibly do.

This is especially important because globalization did not merely “open entry points”; it also created hierarchies. Lead firms often control branding, standards, data, customer relationships, and intellectual property, while suppliers compete on thinner margins. A region specializing in production engineering or components may do well, but it may also become vulnerable to the purchasing power of dominant multinationals. Apple’s supply chain, for example, contains extraordinary manufacturing knowledge, but Apple captures a disproportionate share of profits because it controls design, software, brand, and ecosystem. Any strategy focused on middle stages of innovation must ask how local firms will avoid being permanently squeezed.

That brings us back to competition policy. If a small number of platform companies, pharmaceutical giants, agribusiness firms, or defense contractors dominate markets, local innovation ecosystems will struggle to flourish independently. Antitrust enforcement, open standards, interoperability, limits on exclusionary contracting, and public options in key sectors can all create space for smaller firms and regions. Innovation policy without competition policy may simply subsidize future acquisition targets for incumbents.

The most attractive vision in Breznitz’s essay is of innovation as a collective civic project. That vision is badly needed. For too long, American economic development has oscillated between two inadequate models: laissez-faire faith that entrepreneurs will save us, and subsidy-driven recruitment of footloose corporations. Neither builds the thick local capacities required for shared prosperity. Communities need institutions that can diagnose their strengths, coordinate investment, train workers, share technical knowledge, and adapt over time. They need patience, because capabilities accumulate slowly. And they need humility, because not every place can or should pursue the same niche.

Yet humility should not become resignation. The pandemic exposed the fragility of supply chains built only for cost minimization. It revealed shortages in masks, testing supplies, ventilator components, pharmaceutical inputs, and semiconductors. It also showed that government can move quickly when it chooses to: funding research, guaranteeing markets, coordinating production, and absorbing risk. The question now is whether that emergency capacity can be turned into a durable democratic capacity.

Silicon Valley’s greatest error was not that it celebrated invention. Invention is necessary and often magnificent. Its error was to present one narrow, financialized version of invention as the whole of innovation and then to confuse private fortunes with public success. A better innovation politics would value the machinist improving a production process, the nurse helping redesign care delivery, the engineer making batteries safer, the technician maintaining a water system, and the small manufacturer adapting a component for a new use. But valuing them rhetorically is not enough. We must build institutions that give them power, security, and a share of the gains.

The future of inclusive prosperity will not be found by copying Silicon Valley, but neither will it be found by copying Taiwan, Germany, Italy, Israel, or Shenzhen wholesale. It will require communities to discover where they can contribute to solving real problems—and governments willing to make markets, discipline capital, and protect workers. Innovation can be a road out of Cobalt. But only politics can decide who gets to travel that road, who pays the tolls, and who owns what is built along the way.

It is because the political economy of state and local development policy rewards visible, photogenic interventions: ribbon-cuttings at new Amazon HQ2s, splashy "innovation district" launches, science parks named after sitting governors. A program that quietly capitalizes a hundred mid-sized firms over a decade generates no such payoff for the politicians who fund it. Foxconn in Wisconsin was a fiasco on the merits, but it made perfect political sense. Until we change the incentives facing elected officials, the Cobalt-style boondoggles will keep happening regardless of how persuasive Breznitz's taxonomy is.

The Industrial Technology Research Institute did not appear because someone in Taipei read a good policy memo. It is the product of a developmental state with a particular history, a particular relationship between bureaucratic elites and industry, and a particular tolerance for picking winners. The Brenta shoe cluster reflects centuries of accumulated craft knowledge plus a postwar Italian regional politics of small-firm cooperation that, frankly, we do not have. Telling Cleveland to become Brenta is only marginally less utopian than telling it to become Palo Alto.

I also think Breznitz lets his enthusiasm for stages two through four make stage one sound easier to dispense with than it really is. Yes, novelty is overrated in popular discourse. But novelty is also where the largest rents accrue, and a country that does only second-generation innovation eventually finds itself paying license fees and IP royalties to the country that does the first. Taiwan is a magnificent example of stage three excellence, but it is also a country whose semiconductor preeminence rests on ASML in the Netherlands and on tools and IP from the United States. The strategy works, but it works within a global division of labor that depends on someone doing the front-end work. The United States's mistake was not pursuing stage one innovation; it was pursuing stage one innovation while letting stages two through four atrophy. Both/and, not either/or.

Even if Cleveland successfully reinvented itself as a stage three hub tomorrow, the resulting good jobs would land in a labor market with weak unions, employer-tied insurance, and dramatic regional cost-of-living differentials. The same production strategy can yield very different distributional outcomes depending on the surrounding institutions. Innovation policy is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for inclusive prosperity.

None of this is to dismiss the argument. I think Breznitz is right that the menu of innovation strategies is broader than the Valley evangelists admit, right that incremental innovation is undersung, and right that public-private R&D ecosystems of the Taiwanese kind deserve emulation. What I want to add is that the binding constraints on adopting his program are political and institutional, not cognitive. Policymakers don't pursue the Silicon Valley dream because they haven't heard of the alternatives. They pursue it because it offers them political cover, because the donor class wants it, and because the institutional capacities needed for the alternatives have been allowed to wither. Yes, the gulf between a Google L7 and the contractor who cleans the cafeteria is grotesque. But the principal cause of Bay Area inequality is not the venture capital business model. It is local zoning policy that has refused to permit anything resembling adequate housing construction for forty years. San Francisco's homelessness crisis is reproduced almost as severely in Los Angeles and Seattle, which have very different industrial bases. The pathology Breznitz attributes to stage one innovation is in significant part a pathology of land-use politics. Fixing zoning would do more for inclusive prosperity in the Bay Area than redirecting capital to Taiwanese-style component manufacturing.

Now consider what stage one innovation actually buys us. mRNA vaccines were not the product of stage three incrementalism. They were the product of decades of high-risk basic and translational research, much of it funded through exactly the venture-backed biotech model Breznitz disparages. The COVID-19 response depended on Moderna and BioNTech—both of them VC-backed exit-oriented enterprises that looked, to outside observers in 2018, like the sort of speculative bets that "channel money back to foreign investors." Without that ecosystem, hundreds of thousands more people would be dead. Similarly, the foundational work on lithium-ion batteries, solar photovoltaics, and the machine-learning revolution all came out of research environments and financing structures that include American stage one innovation as a central node.

Breznitz is right that incremental innovation matters. He is wrong to imply that one can substitute for the other. Taiwan's brilliance at semiconductor fabrication is real, but those fabs produce chips designed in California, on architectures developed in California, running software written in Cambridge and Seattle. The global innovation system is genuinely interdependent. Cheerleading for stages two through four while denigrating stage one is like praising the chef while complaining about the farmer.

There is also a question about job quality that Breznitz handles too quickly. He celebrates the Shenzhen production model for creating "jobs to millions of people, many of them immigrants from other parts of China." This is true, and those jobs are an enormous improvement on rural poverty. The stage four employment Breznitz lauds is built on labor regimes—internal migration controls, dormitory labor, suppressed collective bargaining—that no American or European reader would tolerate at home. The comparison flatters the Chinese model in a way that obscures what would actually be required to reproduce it.

Finally, a word on what Breznitz calls "techno-fetishism." I share his irritation with breathless coverage of yet another DTC mattress startup. But the deeper claim—that we mistakenly equate innovation with new products—undersells how much human welfare actually depends on those new products. The smartphone, for all its discontents, has done more to extend the reach of basic information services to poor people globally than any government program in history. The gene-editing revolution is poised to transform agriculture and medicine. The fact that most people working on these technologies will not get rich and that some of their employers will fail does not change the social return. We need both the Taiwanese gear factory and the Cambridge biotech lab.

The right response to Silicon Valley's excesses, in other words, is not to dismiss the model but to fix its housing markets, tax its windfalls more aggressively, and complement it with serious manufacturing policy. Cities that cannot plausibly become hubs of frontier biotech can still aspire to be Brenta or Sakai. That is a real and useful insight. But the framing that pits inclusive prosperity against high-tech entrepreneurship gets the politics wrong and gets the economics half wrong. The frontier still matters. Pretending otherwise is its own form of fetishism.

A Hidden Argument About Finance

What I find most striking about Dan Breznitz's essay is what it almost says but does not quite say. He correctly identifies the venture capital model as a driver of inequality and concentration, and he correctly observes that the Israeli case shows how a country can become a global tech hub without sharing the proceeds. But he frames the problem as one of innovation strategy—choose stage three instead of stage one—when the more direct lesson is about financing structures. The deepest difference between Taiwan and Israel is not which stage of innovation they emphasize. It is who owns the companies, who gets the cash flows, and what time horizons the dominant investors operate on.

Consider what venture capital actually is. It is a financing instrument designed for power-law returns from a portfolio of high-risk bets, with capital recycled through liquidity events—either acquisition or IPO—that return cash to limited partners on a roughly ten-year cycle. Every feature of the model is calibrated to that exit. Founders accept dilution and board control in exchange for the prospect of a windfall. Employees accept below-market salaries plus stock options that vest on a similar timeline. Customers and communities are not parties to the deal. When the exit comes, the value created flows to the LPs, the GPs, the founders, and the early employees, in roughly that order, and very little of it stays with the locality unless those individuals choose to keep living there.

This is not a problem of which industry the model is applied to. It is a problem of the model itself. Apply the same exit-oriented financing to a Brenta-style luxury shoe cluster and you would get the same hollowing-out: roll-up acquisitions by private equity, brand consolidation, gradual offshoring of the lower-margin steps, eventual sale to LVMH. We already know what this looks like because it has happened to many craft industries. The reason Brenta has survived as an ecosystem is partly that its firms have remained closely held, often family-owned, with cash flows reinvested rather than extracted. Taiwan's electronics firms similarly grew under financing arrangements—bank lending, government-directed credit, retained earnings—that allowed them to plow profits back into capacity rather than distributing them to dispersed equity holders.

Once you see this, the policy implications shift. The question is not just "which innovation stage should we pursue" but "what financing instruments do we want to be dominant in our economy." The United States has spent forty years progressively eroding the alternatives to public equity and venture capital. Patient bank lending to mid-sized firms has shrunk as commercial banks consolidated and focused on consumer lending and large corporates. Cooperative financing structures, mutual insurance, and community development financial institutions have been chronically underfunded. The mid-twentieth-century landscape that produced firms like Hewlett-Packard, which could grow for decades on retained earnings before going public, has been replaced by a structure that funnels promising ideas through Y Combinator and out the other end as acquisition targets.

Breznitz's tech teens are precisely the firms that fall through the gap. They are too established for seed funding, too small for the public markets, too unlikely to deliver a ten-bagger to interest a VC. The financing instruments suited to them—long-duration debt, patient equity from family offices and pension funds, cooperative ownership transitions—exist but are vanishingly small parts of the American financial system. If we wanted to fix this, we would not start with another science park. We would start with the tax code, with the regulatory framework around small-business banking, and with the rules governing pension fund investment mandates.

There is also an institutional ownership story to tell. One of the quiet differences between Germany and the United States is the prevalence of family-owned Mittelstand firms with multi-generational time horizons, supported by Sparkassen and Landesbanken that are explicitly mandated to serve regional economies. These firms behave very differently from publicly traded American competitors precisely because they are not under quarterly earnings pressure. The result is exactly the sustained, incremental, locally embedded innovation Breznitz celebrates in his Taiwanese examples. It is not an accident that German manufacturing has held up against globalization while American manufacturing collapsed. The financing structure was different, the ownership structure was different, and the political economy of regional banking was different.

So when Breznitz says we need to invest in a "local innovation ecosystem" including "specialized production facilities" and "collaborative public spaces," I agree, but I want to add: we also need to invest in local financing ecosystems. Capital allocation is at least as important as skill formation, and arguably more so, because no amount of training will save a firm that cannot raise patient capital. The interesting policy experiments of the last decade—the rise of revenue-based financing, employee ownership trusts, Calvert and other mission-oriented investment vehicles, public banks like the Bank of North Dakota—deserve far more attention than they get.

I also want to register a small objection to Breznitz's celebration of Operation Warp Speed and SiO2. The case is real and the contract was well-structured. But singling out one company benefiting from a $143 million directed contract is not yet evidence of a scalable policy. The interesting question is whether the underlying procurement and contracting capabilities of the U.S. government can sustainably channel capital to tech teens at scale, or whether SiO2 will turn out to be a fortunate one-off. The historical track record of American industrial policy is decidedly mixed precisely because we lack the institutional infrastructure—the bureaucratic autonomy, the long-term contracting relationships, the willingness to absorb visible failures—that Taiwan's ITRI or Germany's Fraunhofer Institutes embody.

Innovation strategy is downstream of financing strategy, and financing strategy is downstream of ownership structure, and ownership structure is downstream of political choices about who gets to own what and on what terms. Breznitz's essay points us in the right direction by exposing the inequality embedded in the VC model. But the cure is not just to substitute one innovation stage for another. It is to rebuild the patient-capital ecosystem that once made America's mid-sized industrial firms the envy of the world, and that still makes Germany and Taiwan formidable. Innovation taxonomies are useful. Financial architectures are decisive.

What This Looks Like From the Global South

Dan Breznitz's essay is written from a particular vantage—broadly, from the perspective of mid-income or rich communities deciding among innovation strategies. The cautionary tales are Cleveland, Lyon, the Bay Area. The success stories are Sakai, Taichung, Brenta, Shenzhen, and certain Israeli precincts. The implicit reader is a policymaker in a city with reasonable infrastructure, a functional state, and enough industrial history to plausibly choose among stages two, three, and four. I want to ask what his framework looks like from somewhere else: from Lusaka, from Dhaka, from rural Bolivia, from the parts of the world where the question is not "which innovation stage to specialize in" but "how to escape commodity dependence at all."

Begin with the most important fact about the global economy that Breznitz's framework leaves implicit: the four stages he describes are mostly downstream of access to a particular kind of basic capability. Stage four production in the Pearl River Delta did not arise from nowhere. It rests on decades of state investment in primary and secondary education that produced a workforce capable of operating modern factories; on infrastructure investment that produced ports, roads, and power grids; on macroeconomic management that kept the currency competitive; and on a domestic market large enough to support learning-by-doing before exports took off. Most low-income countries today have none of these preconditions. Telling Zambia to become the next Shenzhen is no more useful than telling Cleveland to become the next Palo Alto.

This matters because there is a real risk that Breznitz's framework, applied uncritically by international development institutions, will substitute one fashionable model for another. We have seen this cycle before. In the 1990s the World Bank pushed export processing zones; in the 2000s, cluster strategies; in the 2010s, innovation hubs and incubators. Each new template promises locally inclusive prosperity. Each tends to flatter the consultants who promote it more than the communities they advise. If we now add "stage two design hubs" and "stage three component innovation" to the menu of recommended strategies, we should at least be honest that almost no country in sub-Saharan Africa is in a position to execute on them in the next decade.

What does work in low-income settings? The honest answer is mostly the same things that worked historically: basic education, basic infrastructure, basic public health, basic state capacity, and selective protection of infant industries long enough for them to develop competitive capabilities. The East Asian developmental states whose successes Breznitz draws on did precisely this. Taiwan did not become a stage three innovator in 1972; it became one after thirty years of patient industrial policy that began with light manufacturing, moved through heavy industry, and only later moved up the value chain. South Korea's path was similar. China's, on a different scale, also. The lesson is sequencing, not stage selection.

The risk of Breznitz's framework for development practitioners is that it can be read as suggesting countries can simply pick an innovation stage and dive in. They cannot. Brenta's design capabilities rest on generations of accumulated craft knowledge in shoemaking. Shimano's gear innovation rests on a Japanese metallurgy tradition that goes back to swords. Even MediaTek, which feels like a more recent phenomenon, is the spinoff of an integrated circuit design capability that the Taiwanese state had been deliberately cultivating since the 1970s. You cannot teleport into stage three. You arrive there by working through stages four and three and back to two, slowly, with a state capable of guiding the process.

I would add a more pointed concern. The picture Breznitz paints of global production is one in which different localities specialize in different stages and all can win. This is a comforting story, but it leaves out the politics of who occupies which slot. The global semiconductor industry has perhaps a half-dozen meaningful nodes. The global luxury shoe industry has perhaps two. The slots are scarce, and getting one of them often involves displacing somebody else. When Schwinn lost its bicycle production capability to Giant, that was not a peaceful complementarity; that was competitive destruction. When MediaTek undercuts Qualcomm, somebody's wages get cut somewhere. The pluralist vision of "many roads to prosperity" is partially true and partially obscures the fact that the global division of labor is zero-sum at any given moment, even if it is positive-sum over time.

For low-income countries, this implies that the available paths are narrower than Breznitz's pluralism suggests. Ethiopia's bid to enter the global apparel value chain is a competitive challenge to Bangladesh's. Bangladesh's was a challenge to China's, which was once a challenge to South Korea's. Each entrant gains because the previous occupant has moved up the chain, but no country gets to skip the queue. The strategy that worked for Taiwan in the 1970s—labor-intensive light manufacturing financed by patient state-directed credit and protected by managed trade—works less reliably today because automation has reduced the labor intensity of much production and because the WTO regime constrains the policy tools that East Asian states once used freely.

So my reading of Breznitz is this. His critique of the Silicon Valley monoculture is welcome, and his stages framework is a useful corrective to "innovation equals startups." But his examples are drawn from middle- and high-income contexts where the basics are already in place, and his framework should not be exported as a development template without serious adjustment. For the poorest countries, the relevant menu is much shorter and much more dependent on getting the boring fundamentals right. For middle-income countries, the strategic question is less which stage to specialize in than how to manage the long climb up the value chain without being trapped on a single rung.

In the global development conversation, what we most need is not new strategy menus but the recovery of policy tools that the post-Bretton Woods consensus took off the table: managed trade, directed credit, capital controls, and the kind of activist industrial policy that East Asia practiced and that the WTO regime has progressively constrained. Without those tools, even the most cleverly designed innovation strategy is unlikely to succeed.

Once you put climate at the center of the analysis, several of Breznitz's framings start to look incomplete. TSMC's fabs consume a meaningful fraction of Taiwan's electricity and have been straining the island's water supply during recent droughts. Shenzhen's assembly economy depends on China's still-coal-heavy grid and on container shipping that is itself a major emitter. The "good jobs at multiple skill levels" Breznitz celebrates are embedded in production systems whose carbon footprint is currently incompatible with a 1.5- or even 2-degree pathway. A model of inclusive local prosperity that ignores its planetary footprint is not really a sustainable model.

This is not just a quibble about externalities. It is a claim that climate fundamentally reorders the strategic landscape Breznitz describes. The next two decades will see the largest reallocation of capital in human history as energy systems, transportation, buildings, agriculture, and industrial processes are rebuilt around low-carbon alternatives. Whichever localities and firms occupy strong positions in that reallocation will define the global economy of mid-century. Conversely, localities that double down on incumbent fossil-dependent specializations—however well-paid the jobs may be today—are setting themselves up to become the Cobalts of the 2040s.

So the most important question for innovation policy in 2021 is not stage one versus stage three. It is whether a community's chosen specialization is positioned for the decarbonization transition. A stage three component innovation hub for internal combustion engines is a dead end. A stage three component innovation hub for battery management systems, heat pumps, or electrolyzers is a once-in-a-century opportunity. A stage four assembly cluster for fast fashion is, climate-wise, a liability; a stage four assembly cluster for solar panels or grid-scale storage is a strategic asset.

Breznitz's framework can accommodate this point, but it does not lead with it, and I think that is a real weakness. The questions he asks—how to embed firms locally, how to build collaborative ecosystems, how to choose stage specializations—are downstream questions. The upstream question is: what should the next economy look like, and how do we navigate the transition from here to there? Innovation strategy without a substantive vision of what we are innovating toward becomes a kind of formalism.

There is also an opportunity here that the essay underplays. Breznitz mentions, almost in passing, that fundamental system innovation requires "the government stepping in as the innovative agent forcing significant changes in multiple sectors." Yes. Decarbonization is exactly such a project, and it is the rare innovation project that has both the moral urgency and the potential coalition support to motivate the kinds of state-led investment Taiwan and Korea made during their developmental periods. Climate policy is potentially the vehicle through which a serious American industrial policy could be built. The Biden administration's Build Back Better proposals, the infrastructure bill, and the climate provisions of various pending legislation gesture in this direction. If they pass in robust form, they could lay the foundation for exactly the kind of public-private R&D and procurement ecosystem Breznitz wants. If they don't pass, or pass in attenuated form, we will have missed a generational opportunity.

Climate also reframes the inequality question that animates Breznitz's essay. The clean energy transition is potentially one of the most powerful tools we have for building broad-based prosperity in regions left behind by globalization. Wind manufacturing, transmission construction, building retrofits, EV component production, hydrogen infrastructure, and so on are all labor-intensive activities that can be sited in places like the Midwestern manufacturing belt or the Appalachian coalfields. They produce union-wage jobs of exactly the kind that disappeared with deindustrialization. The "inclusive prosperity" Breznitz wants is most plausibly built around the climate transition, not in parallel to it.

There are real risks in this framing that I should acknowledge. Industrial policy that picks technology winners can fail expensively; Solyndra is the obligatory cautionary tale. Procurement-based industrial policy can be captured by incumbent suppliers. State-led decarbonization in democracies has to navigate political backlash from communities tied to fossil employment. These are not trivial obstacles, but they are obstacles to be managed, not reasons to default to the laissez-faire stance that has dominated American innovation policy for forty years.

If I were rewriting Breznitz's conclusion, I would say: communities should embrace a wider range of innovation strategies, yes, but they should select among them with an eye to the decarbonization transition. The taxonomy of stages is useful, but it needs to be paired with a substantive view of which technological systems are growing and which are dying. The Brenta of 2040 will not be the Brenta of 2020. The Pearl River Delta of 2040 will be a green-electronics hub or it will be in serious trouble. The Bay Area of 2040 will be more or less consequential depending on whether it pivots toward climate technology or remains dominated by ad-tech and consumer software.

Breznitz's essay is a useful corrective to Silicon Valley triumphalism. I would push it further: the question is not just which stage of innovation to specialize in, but which technological future to bet on. We have run out of time to treat that question as secondary.

The richer body of thought. Breznitz's stages-of-production framework rhymes with a long line of thinking about technological capabilities and the ladder of comparative advantage. List argued in the 1840s that Britain's preaching of free trade to Germany was a kind of "kicking away the ladder"—an attempt to deny to later developers the protective policies Britain itself had used. Fixing it requires changes much further upstream than choosing a different innovation stage to specialize in.

The argument is more powerful when situated within the rich tradition of political-economy thinking about development, and more honest when paired with acknowledgment of how hard the institutional preconditions for his program are to build. I also worry about what the four-stage framework implies for developing countries. The suggestion that countries should pursue "stage four" production and assembly sounds remarkably like the export-oriented industrialization that development economists have critiqued for decades. The World Bank's "getting prices right" orthodoxy led many developing countries to specialize in low-value production while richer nations captured the returns from design and marketing. Breznitz offers a more sophisticated version of this argument—that "all stages offer opportunities"—but the practical effect may be to counsel developing countries to accept their subordinate position in global production networks rather than to challenge it.

This is not to say that Silicon Valley's mythology should go unchallenged. The fetishization of startup culture and the neglect of manufacturing have genuinely harmed American competitiveness. But the alternative Breznitz proposes—a world of specialized regional clusters, each excelling at specific production stages—deserves scrutiny. Such a world may be stable and prosperous for those already at the top of the global hierarchy, but it offers little hope for countries and communities seeking to develop their own innovative capacity and retain the value it creates.

Breznitz has written a compelling corrective to Silicon Valley triumphalism, but I want to suggest that the debate he engages is somewhat beside the point. The real problem is not that Silicon Valley got innovation theory wrong; it is that powerful actors have systematically used Silicon Valley mythology to obscure the policy choices that actually determine economic outcomes.

Consider the history. The American semiconductor industry did not rise through venture capital and garage startups alone. It emerged through massive Pentagon procurement, NASA's electronics purchases, and Defense Department funding for research that became the foundation of Moore's Law. Fairchild Semiconductor, the grandparent of Silicon Valley, was itself spun out from Shockley Laboratories, which received substantial support from the military. The research universities that supply Silicon Valley's technical workforce were built with land-grant funding and continue to receive substantial public subsidies. The Bay Area's physical infrastructure—the highways, the universities, the public research facilities—was built with public investment.

Yet over the past forty years, this history has been rewritten. Policymakers have been taught that government should get out of the way, that markets know best, and that the private sector should lead innovation. The result has been a systematic defunding of the public institutions that made Silicon Valley possible, paired with generous subsidies for the venture capital industry and tech corporations that have captured an ever-larger share of the gains. This is not a failure of theory; it is a deliberate policy choice, obscured by ideology.

Breznitz's framework is useful because it shows that innovation takes many forms and that communities can succeed by excelling at stages other than initial invention. But I worry that this insight could be captured by those who wish to use it for purposes he would not endorse. A development strategy focused on "stage four" production and assembly sounds like a sophisticated version of the export-processing zone model that has failed to generate sustained development across much of the Global South. The difference between "production innovation" and mere contract manufacturing is precisely the kind of technological learning that requires public support—which means that private investors reap the returns while public budgets bear the costs.

What would it mean to take Breznitz's insights seriously? It would require reinvesting in the public institutions that enable innovation: research universities, technical schools, shared manufacturing facilities, infrastructure. It would require industrial policies that retain value locally, through local content requirements, technology transfer agreements, and restrictions on profit repatriation. It would require rejecting the ideology that markets should determine the direction of technological development, and accepting that society must make collective choices about which industries to nurture and which capabilities to build.

In other words, it would require the opposite of the policy direction the United States and other wealthy nations have pursued since the 1980s. Breznitz offers a sophisticated analysis of innovation theory, but the barrier to implementing its lessons is not theoretical ignorance. It is political economy—the power of those who benefit from the current arrangement to resist any changes that would redistribute gains away from them.

The most valuable contribution Breznitz makes is the four-stage framework for understanding innovation, and I want to explore its implications for policymakers who must actually make choices about where to invest scarce public resources.

Stage one innovation—novelty creation—is genuinely important but extraordinarily difficult to support through policy. The history of "picking winners" in technology policy is largely a history of failure, with governments missing transformative innovations while lavishing support on technologies that never achieved commercial viability. The United Kingdom's support for the Ferranti Mark 1 computer, Japan's attempt to dominate the fifth-generation computer project, the European Union's various attempts to build a competitor to GPS—all are cautionary tales. Yet without public support for research that has no immediate commercial application, the pipeline of new technologies will dry up.

The solution, I suggest, is not to fund applied research that duplicates private sector efforts but to fund basic research at universities and public institutions, allowing the private sector to commercialize discoveries that flow from this foundation. The United States has done this reasonably well through the NIH, DARPA, and basic research funding at the National Science Foundation. The problem is that this support has been eroded over decades while resources have been redirected toward corporate welfare and tax breaks for the wealthy.

Stage two innovation—design, prototype development, and production engineering—may be the most promising target for place-based industrial policy. The Italian clusters Breznitz describes show that specialized capabilities in these stages can generate sustained competitive advantage and inclusive employment. But these clusters do not emerge spontaneously; they require institutions that transmit tacit knowledge, that provide specialized training, and that enable collaboration among firms that might otherwise compete rather than cooperate. In the United States, these institutions have atrophied as policy has focused on "innovation" in the abstract rather than on the specific capabilities that enable particular industries to flourish in particular places.

Stage three innovation—second-generation improvement of existing products and components—is where most employment in manufacturing actually occurs, and it is where the United States has suffered the greatest losses. The semiconductor industry is the paradigmatic case: American firms still design cutting-edge chips, but the fabrication has migrated to Taiwan, along with the associated employment and the knowledge that comes from producing complex products. Rebuilding stage three capabilities will require not just investment but institutional change—workforce development programs, apprenticeships, and the kind of public-private collaboration that existed in the mid-twentieth century but has been largely dismantled.

Stage four innovation—production and assembly—is unlikely to return to high-wage economies unless wages are kept low, automation is deployed extensively, or trade policy creates effective barriers to offshoring. Breznitz is right that China has achieved remarkable capabilities in stage four, but replicating these capabilities in the United States would require accepting either low wages, massive public subsidies, or a significant change in trade regime. None of these options is politically easy.

The honest policy conclusion is that different communities should pursue different stages based on their existing capabilities, their institutional strengths, and their political constraints. Some regions will excel at stage one; others will find their comparative advantage in stages two or three. But this diversity of strategy requires a policy environment that supports experimentation and learning rather than imposing a single Silicon Valley template on everyone. That is Breznitz's most important insight, and it deserves to be taken seriously.

Breznitz's framework invites historical comparison, and the comparison is illuminating. The concern that regions can specialize in ways that leave them vulnerable to economic shocks is not new; it is a recurring theme in the history of industrialization.

Manchester in the nineteenth century was the world's leading center of cotton textile manufacturing, dependent on cotton grown in American South and sold in global markets. When the American Civil War disrupted cotton supplies, the resulting "cotton famine" devastated the region, throwing hundreds of thousands of workers into poverty. The lesson seemed clear: over-reliance on single industries and external supply chains creates vulnerability. Yet Manchester eventually recovered, not by replicating the Silicon Valley model but by transitioning to other forms of production and by building diversified service sector employment.

The Rust Belt story is similar but more tragic, because the transition came later and was less successful. American industrial cities did not fail because they lacked innovation; they failed because of specific policy choices: theNAFTA-driven reorientation of production toward Mexico, the failure to invest in workforce transition programs, the decline of labor protections, and the ideologically driven hostility to industrial policy. Cleveland and Detroit did not lack ideas about how to diversify their economies; they lacked the public investment and policy support needed to implement those ideas.

What does this history suggest for Breznitz's framework? First, it suggests that over-specialization is genuinely risky, and that communities should seek diversification within and across innovation stages rather than putting all their eggs in a single basket. Second, it suggests that public investment is essential for transition—that when industries decline, the question is not whether government should intervene but how it should intervene. Third, it suggests that the timeline for successful transition is measured in decades, not years, and that political systems oriented toward short-term electoral cycles will struggle to implement the kind of sustained strategy that successful transition requires.

Breznitz is right that the Cobalt model of resource extraction followed by abandonment is not a path to inclusive prosperity. But the alternative is not simply to make smarter choices about which innovation stage to pursue. It is to build institutions capable of managing ongoing economic change—not because change can be prevented, but because communities that can adapt successfully will survive and those that cannot will decline.

The history of industrialization offers grounds for cautious optimism: most regions that have suffered major economic shocks have eventually recovered, provided they had the institutional capacity and public support to adapt. The challenge for contemporary policymakers is to build those capacities before the shocks arrive, not after.
