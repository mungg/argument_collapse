Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Elaine Scarry has done something rare and valuable: she has taken the temporal arithmetic of September 11 seriously and held it up against the temporal arithmetic that has been used, for half a century, to justify removing the most consequential decisions of war and peace from democratic deliberation. The juxtaposition is devastating. If a fleet of fighter jets, with one hour and twenty-one minutes of warning, could not defend the building that houses our military command, then the casual confidence that we can intercept ICBMs in "a matter of minutes," or that a president must be able to launch nuclear weapons in three minutes without consulting anyone, deserves to be regarded as something closer to liturgy than analysis. I want to affirm the core of her argument while pressing on two places where I think the picture needs complication.

First, the affirmation. The argument from speed has functioned, as Scarry shows, as a kind of trump card in American constitutional debate since 1945. Every constitutional objection to executive war-making, every demand that Congress reclaim its Article I powers, every worry about citizens being reduced to spectators of decisions made on their behalf, has been parried with the same reply: the missiles travel too fast for democracy. September 11 ought to retire that reply. The hijacked planes were slower than missiles, more visible than missiles, accompanied by six layers of corroborating evidence that no incoming warhead would ever generously provide—and still the centralized, top-down defense apparatus could not act in time. If the system cannot meet the easy case, why should we believe its claims about the hard one? Scarry's demand that we re-examine the timetables is not utopian; it is empirical.

Her second move—that the passengers of Flight 93 modeled an alternative, distributed, deliberative form of defense rooted in the original constitutional vision of the militia—is more provocative, and here I want to register both admiration and caution. The reconstruction of the Flight 93 deliberations is genuinely moving, and the structural point is correct: the passengers had what the F-16 pilots did not, namely sensory access to the threat and moral authority over the lives at stake. Informed consent was possible on the plane because the deciders and the dying were the same people. That is exactly the condition that vanishes when the president alone is permitted to authorize the incineration of populations he has never met and who have never consented.

But the analogy between Flight 93 and national defense can be stretched too far. The plane was a self-contained civic unit of forty-odd people facing a single, immediate, sensorily available threat. The polity of 280 million faces threats whose evidence is distributed, classified, ambiguous, and contested. A "town meeting" model for deciding whether to invade Iraq is not impossible—the Constitution in fact prescribes something like it under the name of a congressional declaration of war—but it cannot be expected to deliver the kind of crystalline moral clarity that twenty-three minutes of cell-phone calls produced on Flight 93. Scarry knows this; her short-run prescription is precisely a return to congressional declaration, civil-liberties protections, and citizen access to communications. I would only add that the romance of Flight 93 must not be allowed to obscure how hard genuine democratic deliberation about war actually is, and how easily it can be manipulated when the executive controls the information.

This brings me to the present moment. Scarry is writing while the administration is openly preparing a war against Iraq with no demonstrated connection to the September 11 attacks, while floating doctrines of preemption that—as the West Point speech suggested—seem to leave open the use of nuclear weapons. The congressional resolution now being drafted on the Hill threatens to be a blank check rather than a declaration, the very evasion of deliberation she warns against. If her essay has a single urgent practical implication, it is this: the country is about to repeat, at vastly greater scale, the structural mistake whose smaller version killed 125 people at the Pentagon. The centralized authority is again claiming that speed and secrecy require us to delegate everything; the citizenry is again being asked to sit quietly in its seat.

I would push Scarry's argument one step further. The failure on September 11 was not only a failure of speed but a failure of imagination at the center—a failure that distributed knowledge could have corrected. FAA controllers, airline dispatchers, passengers, and family members all knew pieces of the truth before the Pentagon was hit; what was missing was any structure to aggregate that distributed knowledge into action. A democratic defense is not merely ethically superior; it is epistemically superior, because no centralized authority can match the sensory reach of a citizenry. That is the lesson of Flight 93, and it is the lesson we are now, with terrifying speed, refusing to learn.

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” is a compelling and unsettling essay. She places the two fates of September 11 — the Pentagon strike and the Pennsylvania crash — side by side and asks whether they reveal something deeply flawed about the way we have organized our national defense. Her central claim is that the speed argument, used for fifty years to justify centralized, executive-controlled military power, collapsed on that morning: despite a luxurious amount of time by its own standards, the military could not protect the Pentagon, while a spontaneous assembly of citizen-passengers, acting with informed consent, succeeded in defending their patch of ground. This, she argues, obliges us to rethink our entire defense posture, and to move back toward an egalitarian, democratic model, including the abandonment of nuclear weapons. The elegance of her narrative is seductive, but the conclusions she draws from it deserve a cooler look.

Scarry’s timeline for Flight 77 is indeed arresting. Fifty-five minutes of warning that the plane might be hijacked, twenty minutes of certainty — and yet three F-16s from Langley were scrambled only at 9:24 a.m., reaching Washington after the impact. It is right to be appalled. But was this failure “structurally impossible” for any external defense, as she insists, or was it a catastrophic failure of imagination, communication, and rules of engagement? On September 11, the military’s entire air-defense posture was oriented outward, against threats from abroad; the notion of turning fighters against domestic civilian airliners was so alien that no preexisting protocol existed for doing so. The FAA and NORAD were slow to share information, and the pilots had no authorization to shoot down a passenger plane until it was too late. These are grave and inexcusable failures, but they are not the same as a proof that any external defense is futile. They point to the need for far better coordination and clearer rules — many of which have already begun to be implemented since that day — not to the necessity of replacing standing armies with citizen militias.

The passengers of Flight 93 did something extraordinary, and Scarry is right to honor their collective deliberation and consent. They had what no F-16 pilot could have: direct sensory knowledge of the threat, and the moral and legal standing to sacrifice their own lives. But it is a long leap from the cabin of a hijacked 757 to the broad requirements of national defense. The Flight 93 model worked because the battlefield was the size of an airplane, the threat was a handful of knife-wielding men, and the time horizon was roughly half an hour. It is not obviously transferable to defending against a missile salvo, a biological attack, or a state-sponsored invasion. Scarry acknowledges this when she calls for a return to conventional weapons only, but she never fully confronts the scale problem. A citizen militia armed with rifles and votes is not a substitute for a professional military capable of projecting force or deterring great powers. Even in the narrower domain of aerial terrorism, the Flight 93 precedent is fragile: it required that passengers be able to learn of the World Trade Center attacks, verify them, deliberate, and act — all before the plane reached a target. Had the hijackers chosen a closer objective, or jammed phone signals, the window would have vanished.

Scarry’s constitutional arguments are serious, but they sometimes overreach. She invokes the Second Amendment and the founders’ distrust of standing armies, but the early republic’s militia system was already a failure by the War of 1812, and the modern United States is not a small agrarian society. Moreover, she slides too easily from the failure of a particular centralized response on one morning to the claim that centralized defense in general is unconstitutional and ineffective. The Constitution does not require that every act of defense be performed by a town meeting; it requires that the decision to go to war be made by Congress, and that the military remain under civilian control. Those principles can be upheld without dismantling the professional armed forces. The real scandal she identifies is not the existence of F-16s, but the decades-long erosion of congressional war powers and the rise of an imperial presidency. That is a crisis of democratic governance, not a brief for citizen-soldiers on every airplane.

Perhaps the most troubling leap in Scarry’s essay is her call, in the long run, for the elimination of nuclear weapons as a condition of democratic self-defense. She argues that these “monarchic weapons” give presidents genocidal power without popular consent, and that their speed-arguments are belied by the 9/11 experience. The moral force of the anti-nuclear position is undeniable, but the essay’s own logic undercuts it. If the difficulty of intercepting a single hijacked airliner with a transponder off was so immense, how much more daunting would it be to intercept a mass missile launch, decoys and all? In a world where other nations retain nuclear arsenals, unilateral renunciation would not restore democratic purity; it would simply leave the country defenseless against a threat that — unlike hijacked airplanes — can arrive in minutes and incinerate entire cities. The argument from speed has been abused to justify executive unilateralism, but speed remains a brute fact of nuclear strategy. The way to tame it is not to pretend it away, but to insist, as Scarry herself might agree, that any decision to use such weapons be subjected to the most stringent constitutional checks.

Scarry’s essay is a vital wake-up call. It forces us to recognize that the habits of secrecy and executive power have not made us safer, and that the consent of the governed is not a luxury that can be dispensed with in emergencies. The passengers of Flight 93 demonstrated that ordinary citizens are capable of extraordinary acts of collective self-defense under the most dire circumstances. But the lesson of that day is not that we should dismantle the Pentagon and put our faith in coffee-pot brigades. It is that a democracy must constantly scrutinize the gap between its defense apparatus and its founding ideals, and must refuse to accept that security requires giving up self-governance. The real emergency is not speed; it is the quiet surrender of our own responsibilities.

Elaine Scarry’s examination of the events of September 11 offers a profound and moving tribute to the passengers of United Airlines Flight 93. She rightly identifies their deliberation, vote, and counterattack as a triumph of democratic action in the face of terror. The passengers embodied the deepest civic virtues, reclaiming their hijacked ground and saving an unknown number of lives in Washington. But to extrapolate from the heroic, localized actions of forty passengers a demand to dismantle our centralized military defense, our nuclear deterrence, and our executive command structures is to build a sweeping and dangerous national security doctrine on a foundational false equivalence.

First, we must address the vast difference in scale between a hijacked airliner and a nation-state. Flight 93 was a micro-environment of roughly 600 cubic meters. The passengers possessed absolute, immediate situational awareness: the enemy was visible, the threat was confirmed by loved ones over the phone, and the parameters of the battlefield were entirely self-contained. They did not have to defend the rest of the continent, sort through radar decoys in the stratosphere, or manage a multi-front theater of war. Democracy works beautifully in a town hall, but scaling a town hall to coordinate a real-time defense of a continent against diverse, invisible, or supersonic threats is a physical and logistical impossibility.

Scarry asserts that the military’s failure to intercept American Airlines Flight 77 proves that the necessity of "speed"—long used to justify a top-down military and executive war powers—is an illusion. She meticulously details the 55 to 81 minutes the military had to respond. However, the failure on September 11 was not that centralized defense is inherently too slow, but that it suffered from a catastrophic failure of imagination and bureaucratic coordination. The protocols bridging the FAA and NORAD were designed for Cold War hijackings, where planes were forced to land in Cuba, not for civilian airliners turned into cruise missiles. A procedural blind spot is not an indictment of the physics of modern warfare. When facing a ballistic missile traveling at 6,400 feet per second, there simply is no time for a congressional debate. Speed is a brutal physical reality of the modern era, not merely a rhetorical excuse for executive power.

This brings us to the most glaring impracticality in Scarry’s thesis: the application of a localized, egalitarian defense model to the broader spectrum of modern geopolitical threats. Scarry suggests relying on citizens for defense, evoking the Founders' preference for a militia over a standing army. But how does a citizen militia stop a biological weapon like anthrax, which Scarry herself notes we have spent months struggling to trace? How does an egalitarian vote intercept a chemical attack or deter a rogue state from launching a nuclear weapon? The Constitutional framers feared standing armies, but they also did not face intercontinental ballistic missiles. We delegate defense to centralized authorities precisely because modern threats require specialized, coordinated, and technologically advanced systems that cannot be distributed to the general populace.

Scarry also raises the profound moral question of consent, arguing that military pilots lack the ethical authorization to shoot down fellow citizens, whereas the Flight 93 passengers could consent to their own sacrifice. This is a heavy moral burden, but the social contract of a modern republic inherently involves citizens electing leaders to make agonizing choices to protect the greater good. Ceding the monopoly on violence to a trained, constrained force is the price of living in a complex society. Relying on the mere hope that unarmed passengers will always be able to successfully overcome armed terrorists is not a national security strategy; it is an abdication of the government's primary duty to protect its people.

The tragedy of September 11 demands that we scrutinize our defense arrangements. As we debate the creation of a Department of Homeland Security and the looming possibility of war with Iraq, we must remain vigilant against executive overreach and the erosion of civil liberties. Scarry is absolutely right that we must not passively surrender our constitutional checks and balances in the name of security. However, romanticizing the tragic heroism of Flight 93 to argue against the existence of centralized, rapid-response national defense fundamentally misunderstands both the nature of 21st-century threats and the necessary mechanisms of a modern democratic state. We can honor the citizens of Flight 93 without pretending their desperate, noble sacrifice is a viable template for defending the entire nation.

Elaine Scarry is right to insist that September 11 be treated not only as a wound but as evidence. The day exposed failures of imagination, coordination, and command; it also displayed, in the passengers and crew of Flight 93, a civic courage that deserves to be understood politically and not only memorialized sentimentally. She is also right that “speed” has too often served as a solvent of constitutional government. For half a century presidents have claimed that emergencies arrive too quickly for legislatures, courts, or citizens; and for half a century Congress has often accommodated that claim, sometimes by formal authorization, sometimes by silence. The present drift toward war with Iraq makes her warning urgent: emergency must not become a standing substitute for consent.

But I am less persuaded by the sharp contrast Scarry draws between “external,” centralized defense and “internal,” egalitarian defense, and still less by the conclusion that the latter can provide a general model for national security. Flight 93 shows something indispensable about citizenship in emergency: citizens need information, communication, and the moral authority to act where they are. It does not show that decentralized action can replace professional defense institutions, nor that the failure of air defense on September 11 discredits the entire architecture of modern military command.

The first difficulty is evidentiary. Scarry’s timeline is powerful, but it risks making the day look more intelligible in real time than it was. The phrase “we have some planes” was indeed ominous; the first crash into the World Trade Center was catastrophic; the second made unmistakable that America was under coordinated attack. Still, the inference from “multiple hijackings” to “Flight 77 must be intercepted before Washington” required not merely knowledge but identification, communication across civilian and military agencies, doctrine, and authority. On September 11 those systems failed badly. The Federal Aviation Administration and the North American Aerospace Defense Command were not organized for domestic suicide hijackings. Their habits, procedures, and even mental categories assumed hijackers seeking negotiation or refuge, not using fuel-laden aircraft as guided missiles. That is an indictment, but it is not the same indictment as saying centralized defense is inherently too slow.

Indeed, some of the failures Scarry identifies were not caused by excessive centralization but by fragmentation. Civilian controllers, airline officials, military officers, local authorities, and political leaders did not share a common operating picture. Information existed in pieces, but the pieces did not become usable knowledge quickly enough. The cure for that defect is not simply to push authority downward or outward; it is to build institutions that can transmit accurate information rapidly, assign responsibility clearly, and act under rules known in advance. A democratic state needs competent hierarchy as well as civic participation. Fire departments, air traffic systems, public health agencies, and military commands cannot function as town meetings while buildings burn or aircraft vanish from radar. The issue is how to keep hierarchy accountable, lawful, and bounded—not whether hierarchy can be dispensed with.

Flight 93, for all its nobility, also proves a more limited point than Scarry suggests. The passengers acted because they had three unusual advantages. They were inside the threatened aircraft; they learned from phone calls that other hijacked planes had been turned into weapons; and by the time they acted, the old bargain of hijacking—cooperate and survive—had collapsed. Future terrorists may adapt. They may cut communications, use smaller aircraft, employ explosives sooner, or choose targets too close to takeoff for passenger deliberation to matter. Passenger resistance is now part of the nation’s defense, because citizens have learned that compliance may be fatal. But it is a last line of defense, not a sufficient system.

Scarry’s point about consent is morally serious. Who may authorize the shooting down of a civilian airliner? The question is horrifying precisely because all answers are horrifying. Yet it cannot be avoided by saying only the passengers may decide. Suppose an aircraft with 60 people aboard is minutes from a stadium holding 70,000, and suppose some passengers cannot know the facts, cannot deliberate, or cannot overcome the hijackers. A government that has sworn to protect the population cannot say that because consent from the doomed passengers is unavailable, nothing may be done. The moral burden must be carried by public law: rules debated beforehand, chains of command specified, criteria for action narrowed, and accountability imposed afterward. Such rules will never make the act clean. But democratic authorization sometimes consists not in contemporaneous consent by every person affected, but in prior, public, constitutional settlement of terrible responsibilities.

That distinction matters even more when Scarry turns from aerial terrorism to nuclear weapons and Iraq. I share much of her anxiety. The administration’s language of preemption is dangerous if it becomes a doctrine of discretionary war. Congress should not permit a major war against Iraq by murmur, delegation, or after-the-fact applause. The president should be required to present evidence, explain the connection—if any—between Iraq and the attacks of last September, define the threat posed by weapons of mass destruction, specify aims, and accept limits. A constitutional republic cannot be led into war by insinuation. If Iraq is a grave danger, the case should withstand public argument. If it cannot withstand public argument, it is not yet a case for war.

Nor should nuclear first use be allowed to hover in the background as an unexamined option. Nuclear weapons are not merely larger conventional weapons. Their use would inflict indiscriminate suffering, poison political legitimacy, and weaken the very norm against weapons of mass destruction that the United States rightly seeks to enforce. Any administration contemplating nuclear use except in the most extreme circumstances should be confronted by Congress and the public. Scarry is right that nuclear command has an authoritarian character: the compression of decision into minutes and into one office sits uneasily with republican government.

Still, the analogy between September 11 air defense and nuclear deterrence is imperfect. The failure to intercept hijacked aircraft does not by itself show that nuclear weapons fail to deter states from using nuclear weapons, nor that missile defense is impossible in every form, nor that unilateral American nuclear abolition would persuade Iraq, North Korea, India, Pakistan, Russia, China, Britain, France, or Israel to follow. Nuclear weapons may corrode democracy; they may also have helped prevent direct great-power war. That uncomfortable possibility cannot be wished away. The task is to reduce reliance on them, strengthen arms control, lower alert levels, secure fissile materials, and make first use less likely—not to pretend that the strategic problem can be solved by moral clarity alone.

Scarry’s appeal to the militia tradition is also double-edged. The founders distrusted standing armies, but they also discovered, almost immediately, the limits of militia. The early republic struggled to make citizen-soldiers reliable for national purposes; by the Civil War, the world wars, Korea, Vietnam, and the Gulf War, the United States had repeatedly relied on organized national forces. A draft may indeed force war through the lives of citizens and therefore through politics. But conscription is not inherently democratic; it can distribute sacrifice unjustly, as Vietnam showed, and it can enlarge the state’s claim on the body in ways Scarry herself should distrust. A volunteer military risks separating soldiers from society; a draft risks coercing citizens into wars they have not meaningfully authorized. The better democratic remedy is not necessarily conscription but congressional seriousness, transparent debate, fair taxation for war costs, veterans’ accountability, and a public unwillingness to treat war as spectacle conducted by someone else’s children.

The most valuable lesson of Flight 93 is therefore not that national defense should become “egalitarian” in the sense of dissolving professional authority into citizen action. It is that citizens must not be treated as passive cargo. On airplanes, that means communication, trained crews, hardened cockpit doors, air marshals where useful, and an informed public that knows resistance may be necessary. In cities, it means emergency systems that allow ordinary people, firefighters, police, medical workers, and officials to communicate rather than compete for silence. In foreign policy, it means Congress reclaiming its war powers before war begins, not after troops are deployed. In nuclear policy, it means refusing to let the gravest decision any government can make remain hidden within executive ritual and classified incantation.

Scarry asks, “Why are we sitting quietly in our seats?” We should not be. But neither should we conclude that because passengers once acted with magnificent speed, citizenship consists mainly in spontaneous action at the point of danger. Citizenship also consists in the slower work that precedes emergency: building lawful institutions, demanding reasons, refusing euphemism, and insisting that fear not be converted into blank checks. The passengers of Flight 93 deliberated because they had to; we have the luxury, and therefore the obligation, to deliberate before the next catastrophe narrows our choices. Their example should not make us romantic about decentralized defense. It should make us ashamed of democratic abdication.

The passage of time since Elaine Scarry wrote these words has only deepened the urgency of her central question: what does it mean to defend a democracy, and who should do it? Scarry's analysis of September 11 is compelling in its insistence that we examine the structural assumptions underlying our defense apparatus. Yet while her critique of centralized military authority and her celebration of Flight 93's passengers offer important correctives, her argument raises questions she does not fully address—questions about the limits of the citizen-soldier model, the genuine complexities of democratic deliberation under duress, and the hard choices that defense sometimes demands.

Scarry is right to置疑 the "speed" argument that has justified bypassing constitutional checks on military action. The timeline she reconstructs for September 11 is damning: fifty-five minutes of warning that Flight 77 might be hostile, twenty minutes of certainty, and still no interception. This exposes a fundamental tension in our defense posture. For decades, we have accepted that the presidency must have near-unlimited authority to act within minutes because missiles cross oceans in moments. But September 11 demonstrated that even a much slower threat—a commercial airliner traveling at 500 miles per hour—could not be stopped by the centralized apparatus we had built. Scarry's observation that the passengers of Flight 93 accomplished in twenty-three minutes what the military could not accomplish in an hour is striking, and her broader argument that distributed, democratic defense is both more legitimate and more effective deserves serious consideration.

Yet we must be careful not to romanticize what happened on Flight 93. The passengers' action was heroic, but it was also improvised, desperate, and contingent. Not every group of passengers will have the information, the courage, or the opportunity to respond as they did. Scarry acknowledges this—"I do not mean that passengers in any one case must choose to act, or that—having so chosen—they will be successful"—but she nonetheless builds an enormous edifice of policy recommendation on the premise that the egalitarian model is "our best and only defense against aerial terrorism." This overstates the case. The passengers succeeded because they had cell phones, because they learned about the World Trade Center, because they had the numbers and the resolve. None of these conditions is guaranteed in future attacks.

Moreover, Scarry's celebration of the Flight 93 passengers' deliberations obscures a harder question: what if they had chosen differently? What if, after learning of the towers, they had decided to stay in their seats, hoping the hijackers might change course or land safely? That too would have been a choice made under duress by people whose lives were at stake. Scarry grants the passengers the right to make the decision because they were the ones who would die—but this logic cuts both ways. It affirms individual autonomy in extremis, not some new form of collective defense that can be institutionalized and scaled.

Scarry's critique of nuclear weapons is powerful. She is correct that the confidence we place in missile defense is largely untested and that the decoy problem makes interception of incoming warheads far more difficult than officials acknowledge. Her point that nuclear weapons function as "monarchic weapons"—wholly outside any democratic accountability—is well taken and deserves amplification. The president's unilateral authority to initiate nuclear war, never subjected to a congressional declaration since Hiroshima, represents a structural anomaly that the events of September 11 should prompt us to revisit.

But her prescription—that we must return to a conventional military and "almost certainly" reinstate the draft—requires more scrutiny than she provides. A draft would indeed make war more visible and subject to democratic pressure, but it would also impose enormous costs in liberty and resources. The citizen-militia of the Second Amendment era bore little resemblance to modern warfare; conflating the founders' vision with twenty-first-century conscription elides vast differences in technology, scale, and stakes.

Scarry's opposition to the Iraq war, as she frames it in October 2002, is grounded in her larger critique of presidential unilateralism. She argues that war with Iraq requires congressional authorization and broad public deliberation. This is a defensible constitutional position, though one might note that the authorization debate was already underway and that the case for intervention was being made—however unconvincingly to her—on grounds of weapons of mass destruction and regime change. Her claim that the war "has no visible connection to the events of September 11" reflects one interpretation of the evidence available at the time; others with equal access to the facts reached different conclusions.

What Scarry gets right is that September 11 should have prompted a genuine reckoning with our defense structures rather than a reflexive expansion of executive power. The questions she raises about aerial terrorism, civilian control, and democratic accountability remain urgent. But the answers are harder than her essay sometimes suggests. The Flight 93 passengers did not defeat terrorism; they sacrificed themselves to prevent one more atrocity. The challenge is to build systems of defense that honor their courage without pretending that courage alone can substitute for the harder work of institutional reform.

The Wrong Analogy at the Heart of a Powerful Essay

Elaine Scarry has written something that deserves a careful response, partly because her central observation is so arresting and partly because the argument she builds atop that observation will not bear the weight she places on it. The observation is this: on September 11, the U.S. military had between fifty-five minutes and an hour and twenty-one minutes of warning before Flight 77 struck the Pentagon, and it could not stop the plane, while the passengers of Flight 93, working with twenty-three minutes and a few cell phones, organized themselves into something like a deliberative body and stopped their hijackers cold. From this contrast Scarry derives a sweeping conclusion: that the "speed" argument used to justify presidential first-use of nuclear weapons and the bypassing of congressional war powers has been exposed as hollow, and that we must move toward a "distributed, egalitarian" model of national defense, including, eventually, the abolition of nuclear weapons.

The trouble is that the analogy between Flight 77 and a Soviet ICBM strike — or even between Flight 77 and a contemporary missile threat — does not hold. Scarry knows this; she even anticipates the objection and tries to neutralize it. But her neutralization moves are unpersuasive, and the rest of her argument rests on them.

Consider the differences. A hijacked civilian airliner travels at roughly 500 mph, gives off radar signatures continuously even with its transponder off, can be visually identified, and crucially is full of American citizens whose lives the defender is trying to save. An incoming ballistic missile arrives in roughly thirty minutes from launch to detonation (less for submarine-launched weapons), cannot be visually intercepted, will be accompanied by decoys, and contains no friendly lives whose deaths must be weighed in the calculus. Scarry argues that because Flight 77 was hard to track even with six layers of confirmation, an incoming missile would be impossible to track. This is true and is in fact one of the standard arguments against missile defense — but it is an argument against the *technical feasibility* of interception, not against the *temporal premise* of nuclear command and control. Those are different things. The "matter of minutes" claim about nuclear weapons is not a claim that targets will be easy to identify; it is a claim that *if* a launch is detected, the window between detection and the destruction of one's own retaliatory forces is very short. That problem doesn't go away because passenger jets are hard to intercept.

Second, the consent problem Scarry rightly identifies in shooting down Flight 77 — that an F-16 pilot cannot legitimately decide to kill 200 fellow citizens — does not translate into the missile case. A nuclear command authority responding to an incoming strike is not killing fellow citizens; it is retaliating against an attacking power. One can object to retaliation on many grounds (I have grave doubts about it myself), but the specific Flight 93 moral structure — passengers consenting to their own deaths to spare others — has no analogue in deterrence theory. Scarry's most rhetorically powerful move, the contrast between informed-consent democracy aboard Flight 93 and the silent authoritarianism of nuclear command, simply does not survive the difference in cases.

Third, and most importantly, the lesson Scarry draws from September 11 is the opposite of what most security analysts have drawn, and I think the analysts are closer to right. The failure on September 11 was not a failure of centralization; it was a failure of *integration*. The FAA, the airlines, NORAD, the FBI, and the CIA were operating in stovepipes, with information about "some planes" being taken locked inside one agency while fighters scrambled blindly from another. The fix for stovepiping is not a more "distributed" defense — distribution was precisely the problem — but better lateral communication among existing institutions, and clearer rules of engagement so that the next time a hijacked airliner is heading toward a populated target, the chain of command does not freeze.

That said, Scarry is onto something real, and it would be wrong to dismiss her essay as merely overreaching. She is right that the "speed" argument has been used promiscuously for fifty years to justify the executive's accretion of war powers, and she is right that September 11 ought to puncture some of the more lurid claims about the impossibility of consultation. The country had close to an hour and a half of warning. In that time, surely, *something* could have been routed through the Vice President, the Secretary of Defense, or even relevant committees in Congress. Scarry's deeper point — that the rhetoric of instantaneity has become a kind of incantation that excuses the absence of deliberation — is well taken, and it would be a healthy thing for the country to take it seriously.

But the institutional implication is not the abandonment of nuclear deterrence and a return to the militia. It is the restoration of congressional war powers in the ordinary case. When the Bush administration argues, as it is now arguing, that the President needs no fresh congressional authorization to invade Iraq, that is a moment for citizens to insist on the Constitution. The argument doesn't require us to also believe that Flight 93 is a model for missile defense or that conventional weapons can substitute for the nuclear umbrella that has, for better or worse, kept great-power war off the table since 1945.

I want to register one more discomfort. There is a strain in Scarry's essay that romanticizes the passengers of Flight 93 in a way that is morally moving but politically misleading. She compares them to "a small legislative assembly or town meeting," with their phone calls, their vote, their prayers, their parting words. The comparison is beautiful, and within the closed world of that plane it may even be apt. But the passengers of Flight 93 did not vote on whether to authorize a war; they voted on whether to die in the next ten minutes or the next thirty. The conditions that made their deliberation possible — perfect sensory access to the threat, a shared and unambiguous fate, no time for factional politics — are exactly the conditions that *never obtain* in national defense. A country deciding whether to invade Iraq is not in the position of forty people on a doomed plane. It has time, but it also has divided interests, contested facts, partisan media, and the corrosive influence of fear that Scarry herself has written brilliantly about elsewhere. The town meeting is a stirring image. It is not a defense doctrine.

So: keep Scarry's challenge to the speed argument. Keep her insistence that Congress be heard before the next war. Drop the analogy between hijacked planes and ICBMs, and drop the suggestion that distributed citizen action can substitute for institutional defense. The essay deserves a serious answer, not the indifference it will probably receive in Washington.

Yes, And Further: The Constitutional Case Scarry Doesn't Quite Make

Elaine Scarry's essay is the most important thing written about September 11 in the past year, and almost no one will pay it the attention it deserves. The reason is partly the bluntness of its conclusion — that we should give up nuclear weapons and restore a draft — and partly the difficulty of its premise, which forces the reader to look squarely at a day most Americans would prefer to mythologize rather than analyze. I want to defend her central argument and push it one step further than she takes it.

The central argument, stripped to its frame, is this. For fifty years, the constitutional structure of American war-making has been progressively dismantled in the name of speed. The framers required a congressional declaration of war; we have had none since 1941. The framers rejected a standing executive army in favor of a distributed militia; we now have the largest standing army in the history of the world, available to the president on his sole authority. The framers wanted war-making to be slow, deliberative, public, painful, so that the country would feel the weight of its decisions; we now have war-making that is fast, executive, invisible, anesthetized, and in the nuclear case, genocidal. Each erosion has been excused by the claim that modern weapons move too fast for the Constitution. September 11 ought to disabuse us of that claim. On the day in question, the executive defense apparatus had over an hour to act and could not. A group of citizens with cell phones had twenty-three minutes and succeeded.

This is not, as some will say, an argument from anecdote. It is an argument from structure. Scarry is not claiming that civilians will always succeed and fighter pilots will always fail; she is claiming that a defense apparatus that has detached itself from the population it nominally protects cannot, *in principle*, respond to threats that operate within the population. The Pentagon could not defend the Pentagon because the Pentagon was structurally external to the plane that was about to hit it. The passengers on Flight 93 could defend their plane because they were structurally internal to it. This is the same insight that Madison, Mason, and the other Anti-Federalists pressed in the ratification debates: a defense that floats free of the people becomes both ineffective against the actual threats people face and dangerous to the people themselves.

I want to push beyond Scarry on three points.

First, she is too gentle on the framing of nuclear weapons. She calls them "monarchic weapons" — a useful European term — and notes that they are "wholly external to any powers of consent or dissent." But she stops short of saying what the framing implies: that the existence of presidential first-use authority means that the United States has not, in any meaningful constitutional sense, been a republic since approximately 1947. The capacity to destroy hundreds of millions of human beings on the personal authority of one elected official is incompatible with a Constitution whose central architectural feature is the separation of powers and whose theory of legitimacy rests on the consent of the governed. We have lived for two generations inside a constitutional fiction. September 11 ought to make us notice.

Second, Scarry's draft argument deserves more than the brief mention she gives it. She writes that a return to conventional, distributed defense "will almost certainly mean the return of a draft" and treats this as a cost. It is a cost, but it is also the *mechanism* by which her larger reform would work. A volunteer army recruited disproportionately from the rural poor, fought in countries most Americans cannot find on a map, financed by deficits rather than taxes, and conducted in news cycles that have nothing to do with the experience of the soldiers — this is not an army that Congress will have any difficulty deploying. The whole point of the framers' militia was that war would touch the families of the people who voted for it. Restore that connection, and you restore the friction that makes deliberation possible. The current administration's apparent eagerness for an Iraq war that polls indicate Americans do not particularly want would not survive the prospect that one's own son might be drafted to fight it.

Third, Scarry's argument has implications for the present moment that she only gestures at. As I write, the country is being walked, week by week, into a war against Iraq that has no demonstrated connection to the September 11 attacks, no congressional declaration, no UN authorization, and a doctrinal foundation — "preemption" — that the President announced at West Point this June with language that pointedly did not exclude nuclear weapons. The administration's apparent view is that the events of September 11 have so transformed the security environment that the ordinary constraints of constitutional, international, and prudential deliberation no longer apply. Scarry's essay is a direct rebuke to this view. The events of September 11 did not show that deliberation is impossible. They showed that the executive defense apparatus is fallible and that distributed citizen judgment is sometimes more effective than centralized command. The lesson is the opposite of the one the administration is drawing.

Where I am less sure of Scarry is on the question of what, practically, citizens are supposed to do with this analysis. She calls on us to deliberate, to question, to refuse our seats on the ship that is taking us somewhere we did not buy tickets for. Fine. But the institutional levers of war-making have been steadily removed from citizen reach for half a century. Congressional war powers have atrophied not only because presidents have taken them but because Congress has handed them over, again and again, with the gratitude of an institution relieved of responsibility. The 1973 War Powers Resolution was a feeble effort to claw some back, and even it has been routinely ignored. What would it actually take to restore congressional war powers? Probably a constitutional crisis — a Congress willing to defund a presidential war already underway, a Supreme Court willing to enforce the result. I do not see those conditions being met.

So Scarry's essay leaves me half hopeful and half despairing. Hopeful, because she has named something true that almost no one else is naming: that the architecture of American defense has been disfigured by the cult of speed, and that September 11 exposed the disfigurement. Despairing, because I suspect the country will spend the next decade doubling down on exactly the centralization she warns against, and citing September 11 as the reason. The Pentagon, having failed to defend itself, will now demand the resources to defend everything, and Congress will give them. The lesson of Flight 93 will be reduced to a slogan on a coffee mug.

Still, Scarry has done what an essayist is supposed to do. She has made an argument visible. The rest of us now have the responsibility of acting on it, or of being honest about why we cannot.

What the Pilots Were Up Against: A Defender of the Aircrews Responds

I find myself in a strange position in responding to Elaine Scarry's essay. I agree with about a third of it — the part about congressional war powers, the part about the rhetoric of "minutes" being deployed promiscuously, the part about the need for public deliberation about defense. But I want to register a sharp objection to the way she treats the military performance on September 11, which I think is unfair to the men and women who flew that day and which, more importantly, draws the wrong tactical conclusions from a complicated story.

Scarry's essay rests on a comparison: the military had between fifty-five minutes and an hour and twenty-one minutes and failed; the passengers had twenty-three minutes and succeeded. This comparison is rhetorically devastating and analytically misleading. The two cases are not commensurable in the way the comparison suggests, and the asymmetries cut against her argument rather than for it.

Consider what the F-15 and F-16 pilots actually faced. The North American Aerospace Defense Command on September 11, 2001, was configured for an external threat — bombers and missiles coming over the pole, primarily — and had a grand total of fourteen alert aircraft to defend the entire continental United States. That posture was not the product of executive overreach or of contempt for the citizenry; it was the product of a decade of post–Cold War drawdown that Congress had ratified each year. The hijacking scenarios in the NORAD playbook assumed that hijackers wanted to land somewhere and negotiate, as had been true of every American hijacking in living memory. There was no template for treating a civilian airliner as an inbound weapon. There was no rules-of-engagement framework that authorized shooting one down. There was, until somewhere around 10 a.m. on September 11, no concept of operations for the situation that was unfolding.

The pilots from Otis and Langley took off under those conditions, with vague guidance and partial information, into airspace congested with thousands of civilian aircraft and saturated with reports — most of them wrong — of additional hijackings. The Langley fighters were initially vectored east over the ocean, on standing Cold War procedure, before being turned back. By the time anyone in the system understood what was happening, the Pentagon had been hit. Scarry treats this as evidence of structural failure of centralized defense. I think it is mostly evidence that the system was configured for the last war, that the people inside it improvised under impossible conditions, and that the improvisation was incomplete. Those are different diagnoses with different remedies.

Now to the consent argument, which is the part of Scarry's essay I take most seriously. She argues that even if the F-16s had reached Flight 77, they could not legitimately have shot it down, because no airman has the authority to kill 200 fellow citizens to save some larger number on the ground. She is right that this is a profound problem. She is wrong to think it has a clean democratic solution.

Suppose we accept that the passengers themselves are the only legitimate authorizing body for their own deaths. What then? In the actual case of Flight 77, the passengers did not know what was happening to the World Trade Center towers. They were not in a position to vote. The hijackers had moved them to the back of the plane and (we now believe) had not permitted phone calls. The "informed consent" Scarry celebrates on Flight 93 was a contingent and unrepeatable feature of that particular hijacking. The other three planes did not have it. The next hijacking might not have it either. So we are left with the question of what to do when a passenger plane is being used as a weapon and no consent is possible. Scarry's answer — that nothing should be done from the outside because nothing legitimately *can* be done — is honest but operationally untenable. If a hijacked airliner is heading toward a nuclear power plant or a sports stadium, "let it crash" is not a doctrine any government will adopt, and rightly so.

The post-September 11 answer that has emerged, imperfect as it is, has three components: hardened cockpit doors, so that hijackers cannot take control in the first place; armed federal air marshals on selected flights; and a shoot-down authority that has been formally extended to specific officials below the President. The first two are consistent with Scarry's framework (they keep defense inside the plane). The third is not, but it exists because there are scenarios in which the inside-the-plane defense will fail. I think she should grapple with this rather than waving it away.

I also want to defend, briefly, the much-maligned concept of nuclear deterrence against Scarry's blanket condemnation. I share her revulsion at the weapons. I share her conviction that presidential first-use authority is constitutionally grotesque. I would happily see the United States adopt a no-first-use policy, take its weapons off hair-trigger alert, and pursue much deeper reductions than the current administration is willing to contemplate. But the claim that nuclear weapons "deliver a death blow to our democracy" even when not in use, and that we should give them up unilaterally as a precondition for asking others to do so, runs ahead of what the evidence will support. The historical record since 1945 is, at minimum, ambiguous: great-power war has not recurred, and there are plausible (if not conclusive) reasons to think the weapons had something to do with that. A unilateral American renunciation in the present moment — with Pakistan and India having just tested, with North Korea pursuing a program, with the post-Soviet nuclear inventory still imperfectly secured — strikes me as the kind of bold gesture whose costs would be paid by people not yet born.

What I take from Scarry, then, is not her conclusion but her method. She has insisted on looking at September 11 with timestamps and floor plans, and not just with rhetoric. That is the right way to think about defense. If we applied the same method to the rest of her argument — to nuclear deterrence, to congressional war powers, to the structure of homeland security — we would get a more textured picture than the dichotomy of "egalitarian Flight 93" versus "authoritarian Pentagon" allows. We would notice that the egalitarian model worked on Flight 93 partly because the centralized model failed on the other three flights, in the sense that the passengers learned about the World Trade Center towers from the very communications systems Scarry treats as ancillary. We would notice that the F-16 pilots were as much "the people" as the passengers were, and that treating them as agents of an alien centralized authority gets the sociology wrong.

The pilots flew that day with no good options and made the best decisions they could. They deserve better than to be enrolled as figures in a morality play whose conclusion was written before the facts came in.

The Wrong September 11

Elaine Scarry's essay has the form of an argument from evidence — timetables, mileages, transponder failures — but its conclusions were already in place before the evidence was assembled. She has been writing about the unconstitutionality of nuclear weapons and the distributive logic of the Second Amendment for more than a decade. September 11 is, for her, a confirmation rather than a discovery. This is not a criticism; the best essayists know what they think before they write, and the discipline of the essay is to test that conviction against new material. But it does mean that the reader should be wary about the fit between her evidence and her conclusion. I want to argue that the fit is loose in places and that the September 11 she has analyzed is not quite the September 11 we actually lived through.

Consider first the timeline argument. Scarry makes much of the fifty-five minutes (or eighty-one minutes, depending on how one counts) between the moment the military had information sufficient to be alarmed and the moment the Pentagon was struck. She compares this favorably to the "matter of minutes" rhetoric of nuclear command and control and concludes that the speed argument has been exposed as fraudulent. But this comparison conflates two very different temporal problems. The nuclear "matter of minutes" claim is about *decision time once a threat has been identified*. It is the window between detection and damage. The September 11 timeline includes not just decision time but identification time, classification time, escalation time, communication time, and rules-of-engagement time. The military on September 11 was not sitting on its hands for fifty-five minutes refusing to act; it was struggling to understand what was happening and what its authorities were. The "matter of minutes" claim, whatever its other defects, has never been a claim that all of those processes can happen in minutes. It has been a claim that *if* the President has decided to authorize a launch, the physical execution must happen quickly. That is a different proposition.

Scarry is on stronger ground when she observes that the *rhetoric* of speed has been used to foreclose deliberation in cases where deliberation would have been possible. The Gulf War, the Kosovo intervention, the planned Iraq war — none of these were "matter of minutes" decisions, and the framers' design of congressional deliberation could have been honored in each. The cult of executive war-making in the United States is real and damaging, and Scarry's critique of it deserves to be heard. But when she extends the critique to actual nuclear command and control, she conflates the rhetoric with the reality.

Second, consider the Flight 93 narrative. Scarry presents the passengers as a deliberative body — a "small legislative assembly" — that gathered information, debated, voted, and acted. The narrative is moving and probably substantially accurate in its outlines. But it is also a highly selected story. Three other hijacked planes on September 11 had passengers too, and in those three cases the egalitarian model did not work. Flight 93 was the one where it did, and the reasons it worked there and not elsewhere are contingent: a delayed takeoff that gave passengers time to learn what was happening on the other planes; a particular distribution of athletic and physically capable men in the cabin; a particular cockpit configuration; a particular set of telephones that happened to be working. To generalize from Flight 93 to a doctrine of distributed defense is to commit a sampling error of the most basic kind. We are looking at one positive case and three negative cases, and extracting the lessons of the positive case while ignoring the negative ones.

Third, I want to register skepticism about the framing of the passengers as "citizen-soldiers" enacting a constitutional theory of distributed defense. The passengers themselves did not see what they were doing in these terms. They were ordinary people in a terrifying situation, and they did what people sometimes do in terrifying situations, which is to act with extraordinary courage. The political theory was added later, by Scarry and others, for reasons that are partly about September 11 and partly about long-running debates over the Second Amendment, the standing army, and nuclear weapons. There is nothing illegitimate about this kind of interpretive layering — it is how political traditions are made — but it should be acknowledged for what it is. The passengers were not voting on the Constitution; they were trying to live.

Fourth, the policy conclusions Scarry draws strike me as a non sequitur. From the observation that distributed civilian action averted one disaster on September 11, she concludes that we should give up nuclear weapons, restore the draft, and abandon centralized military command. None of these conclusions follows from the premise. The proposition that civilians on a hijacked plane can sometimes defend their plane better than fighter jets can does not entail any particular view about the proper structure of national defense against state-level adversaries. China is not a hijacked airliner. North Korea is not a hijacked airliner. The threat environment that produced the structure of American nuclear forces is not analogous to the threat environment of September 11, and the lessons of one do not transfer cleanly to the other.

What might be true — and what I think is the genuine insight buried in Scarry's essay — is that the United States has overinvested in capabilities for a fading set of threats and underinvested in capabilities for the emerging set. The Cold War nuclear posture, the carrier battle groups, the heavy armor divisions: these are increasingly disconnected from the actual threats the country faces in the early twenty-first century. The September 11 attackers cost an estimated $500,000 to mount their operation and did damage that will eventually be measured in trillions. No nuclear weapon can deter such an attack and no fighter jet can prevent it; what is needed is intelligence, law enforcement, financial-flow monitoring, and exactly the kind of public alertness that brought down Flight 93 and the shoe bomber. This is a real argument, and it is in some sense Scarry's argument, but it does not require the heavy theoretical apparatus she builds around it.

A final thought. There is a tendency in American letters, after a national trauma, to find in the trauma a confirmation of whatever the writer believed before. Conservative writers found in September 11 a confirmation of the need for unilateral military action. Liberal writers found a confirmation of the importance of international institutions. Religious writers found a confirmation of the importance of religion. Scarry has found a confirmation of the unconstitutionality of nuclear weapons. Some of these confirmations are more grounded than others — Scarry's is more grounded than most, because she actually attends to the evidence — but all of them should be held at a certain skeptical distance. September 11 was an event, not an argument. The arguments came later, and they should be evaluated on their own terms.

The Citizen, the Soldier, and the Things Scarry Does Not Want to Say

Elaine Scarry's essay belongs to a tradition of American political writing that argues, with considerable historical warrant, that a republic and a standing executive military are incompatible over the long run. Madison made this argument; Jefferson made it; the Anti-Federalists made it relentlessly. Scarry's distinctive contribution has been to extend the argument to nuclear weapons, on the grounds that no weapon more thoroughly removes the question of war from the consent of the governed. In this essay, she presses the case again, using September 11 as her lever. I am sympathetic to the tradition and to Scarry's place in it. But I want to raise three difficulties that her essay does not quite confront.

The first difficulty is the Second Amendment. Scarry leans hard on the distributive reading of the Second Amendment — the reading on which the right to bear arms is a structural feature of republican self-defense, embodied in the militia, and not a private right of personal armament. This is the reading associated with Akhil Amar, whom she cites, and with her own earlier work in the *University of Pennsylvania Law Review*. It is a serious scholarly position. But the practical implication, in the contemporary United States, is awkward in ways the essay does not engage. If the Second Amendment is really about distributed self-defense, then the policy program associated with it is not the program of the National Rifle Association — it is something like universal military training, a genuine citizen militia, and the kind of civic-republican defense culture that exists in Switzerland or Israel. Is Scarry recommending this? She gestures at the draft but does not develop the implications. A reader could be forgiven for wondering whether the egalitarian-defense argument, fully cashed out, would lead in directions that the essay's likely readership — left-liberal, urban, professional — would find uncongenial.

The second difficulty is the question of what kind of citizen the egalitarian model assumes. The passengers on Flight 93, as Scarry describes them, were able to deliberate because they had access to multiple independent sources of information, because they could verify what they were told, because they could communicate with people outside the plane, because they could trust each other's reports, because they shared a common situational frame, and because they were able to translate their deliberation rapidly into coordinated action. These are not trivial assumptions. They presuppose a level of cognitive, social, and informational competence that is unevenly distributed across the actual American population. The passengers on Flight 93 were, by demographic happenstance, an unusually capable group — including a businessman, a judo champion, a software executive, a former rugby player, several pilots and engineers. They were not a random sample of the citizenry. The egalitarian defense model, as a general doctrine, has to be able to handle the case where the citizens on the ground are not so well-equipped: panicked, misinformed by partisan media, divided against each other, lacking the trust relations that made the Flight 93 calls possible. What does distributed defense look like in such a population? I do not know that it looks like anything good.

This is the unspoken anxiety of all civic-republican arguments since the founding. The tradition assumes a virtuous, informed, cohesive citizenry. The American citizenry of 2002 is many things, but virtuous, informed, and cohesive are not the first adjectives that come to mind. Television news has spent the past year producing the most reliably misinformed electorate in the developed world. The proportion of Americans who believe that Saddam Hussein was personally involved in the September 11 attacks is, as of this writing, somewhere north of forty percent and rising. If the egalitarian defense model requires that we deliberate together about the use of force, what happens when the deliberation is conducted in this informational environment? The answer, I fear, is that the deliberation produces consent for exactly the kinds of wars Scarry is trying to forestall. The current administration's case for invading Iraq is not being made over the heads of the citizenry; it is being made *to* the citizenry, and the citizenry, by all available polling, is buying it.

The third difficulty concerns gender, which Scarry's essay leaves curiously unexamined. The "citizen-soldier" of the founding tradition was a man, and the militia was a brotherhood. The passengers on Flight 93 were of mixed gender, but the public memory of the event has heavily featured the male passengers — Beamer, Glick, Burnett, Bingham — and Scarry's own narrative, while it includes female names, lingers on the moments of physical action that are coded male. The civic-republican tradition has always struggled with the integration of women, and the slide between "the citizen-soldier as ideal" and "the citizen-soldier as warrior" tends, historically, to reproduce a gendered hierarchy of citizenship that more recent feminist political theory has been working to dismantle. I do not think Scarry intends this — her body of work suggests the opposite — but the essay's rhetoric of armed citizens, militias, and hand-to-hand defense has a masculinist undertow that deserves notice. A fully egalitarian model of defense would have to be more thoroughly egalitarian than the founders managed and more thoroughly than the September 11 mythology has yet been.

These three difficulties do not refute Scarry's argument. They complicate it. The republican tradition she draws on is genuinely valuable, and the critique of presidential war-making and nuclear command authority is more urgent in October 2002 than it has been in many years. With an Iraq war approaching that has not been declared, that has only tenuous international authorization, that may eventually involve weapons whose use the founders could not have imagined, the case for restoring constitutional structures of war-making is overwhelming.

But the restoration cannot be accomplished by invoking Flight 93 as a model. Flight 93 was a moment, not a structure. The structure would have to be built deliberately: through a recovered habit of congressional war declarations, through media institutions capable of supporting actual public deliberation, through educational practices that prepare citizens for the responsibility the egalitarian model assigns them, and through a political culture that does not treat security questions as the exclusive province of credentialed elites. None of this is impossible, but none of it is simple, and none of it is achieved by the essayistic gesture of pointing to a single courageous act and saying: there, that is what democracy looks like.

Scarry's essay is the beginning of a conversation, not its conclusion. The most useful response will be from people who take her seriously enough to argue with her, including from inside her own tradition. I have tried, in these pages, to be one of them.

A Practitioner's Caution

I write as someone who has spent a working life inside the air-traffic and aviation-security systems, and I want to offer a more granular response to Professor Scarry's essay than the ones that have appeared so far in this forum. The essay treats September 11 primarily as a problem in political theory. I think it is also, and perhaps first, a problem in operations. The operational details are not decorative; they materially change the conclusions one ought to draw.

Begin with the timeline. Scarry counts from the moment the FAA controllers heard the phrase "we have some planes" at 8:24 a.m. and treats this as the beginning of the period during which the military could have responded. This is not how the system worked on September 11, and not how it works now. The phrase was heard on an open microphone in the cockpit of Flight 11; it was registered by an air traffic controller in Boston Center who recognized that something was wrong but did not yet have a basis for concluding that multiple aircraft were involved. The information had to propagate from Boston Center to FAA headquarters to NORAD to the alert squadrons, through a chain that had not been exercised for this kind of scenario and that depended on individual judgment at every link. The propagation took time. Was it longer than it should have been? Yes. Could it be shortened by procedural reform? Yes. Does that mean the military had eighty-one minutes of usable warning? No.

The relevant question is not "when did some piece of information about hijacking exist somewhere in the system" but "when did decision-makers with authority to scramble fighters have enough information to act, and to act on what target." By that measure, the military's effective warning time on the Pentagon attack was on the order of ten to fifteen minutes — the time between the Justice Department's awareness of Barbara Olson's call and the impact. The Langley fighters were airborne by then, but vectored away from the actual threat. The procedural and informational failures in this story are real and have been the subject of intense post-mortem within the FAA, the Department of Defense, and the intelligence community. They are not, in my professional judgment, the failures Scarry is identifying.

Second, on the question of what fighters could have done if they had reached Flight 77 in time. Scarry treats the shoot-down problem primarily as a problem of consent — no airman can legitimately decide to kill 200 fellow citizens. I agree that this is a profound problem. But it is overlaid on a set of practical problems she does not address. The F-15s and F-16s scrambled on September 11 were armed with air-to-air missiles designed to engage other military aircraft at distance. Engaging a civilian airliner at low altitude over a populated area with such weapons would risk significant collateral damage from falling debris, and would not necessarily prevent the airliner from continuing on its course long enough to reach its target. The shoot-down option exists in current rules of engagement, but it is not a clean solution, and any honest discussion of it has to grapple with the operational realities.

Third, on the comparison to missile defense. Scarry's analogy between intercepting Flight 77 and intercepting an incoming ballistic missile is rhetorically effective and analytically loose. The intercept problems are different in kind. Flight 77 was a slow-moving, persistent, radar-visible object whose location could be inferred from multiple independent sources over a period of nearly an hour. An ICBM reentry vehicle is a fast-moving, brief, decoy-laden object whose engagement window is measured in seconds. The fact that we failed to intercept Flight 77 does not tell us much, one way or the other, about the feasibility of missile defense. The serious arguments against missile defense — the decoy problem, the cost-exchange problem, the strategic instability problem — are real and important, but they are not the arguments Scarry is making. By yoking the missile-defense critique to the Flight 77 timeline, she risks weakening both arguments.

Fourth, on the lessons for civilian air defense. The actual reforms that have been implemented since September 11 — reinforced cockpit doors, federalized airport screening, expanded air marshal program, improved FAA-NORAD coordination, watchlist sharing — track Scarry's "inside the plane" insight more closely than her essay acknowledges. The current consensus inside the aviation security community is in fact that defense from the outside (fighter intercept) is the option of last resort, and that the primary defenses must be layered upstream: intelligence to identify potential hijackers before they board, screening to prevent them from carrying weapons aboard, cockpit hardening to prevent them from taking control, passenger and crew awareness to interrupt any attempt that does succeed. This is roughly the model Scarry recommends, and it is, with significant exceptions, the model the system is moving toward.

Where Scarry's essay adds genuine value to this operational picture is in its insistence on the consent dimension. The aviation security community has tended to treat the problem as a technical one — better doors, better screening, better intelligence — without adequately confronting the moral and political dimensions of, for instance, the shoot-down authority. Who has it? Under what conditions? Are the public to be informed of the rules of engagement, or are these to be classified? Scarry is right that these are democratic questions and that the public has been excluded from them in ways that should not be tolerated. The publication of clear, public rules of engagement for the shoot-down of civilian aircraft would be a small but meaningful step toward restoring the constitutional architecture she rightly defends.
