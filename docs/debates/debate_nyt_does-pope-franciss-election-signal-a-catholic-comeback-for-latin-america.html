Does Pope Francis’s Election Signal a Catholic Comeback for Latin America?

The world is rapidly reading up on Jorge Mario Bergoglio, the 76-year-old Argentine who is now Pope Francis. What can we deduce from his age, his choice of a papal name, his background as a Jesuit? Most of all: What does it mean that the cardinals chose a candidate from outside Europe? It might signal, in part, that the church is nervous about its thriving rival in Latin America: evangelical Protestantism.

What can the Catholic Church do to compete with the appeal of evangelicalism in Latin America?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The cardinals' decision to look across the Atlantic is itself a kind of admission. Latin America is home to nearly 40 percent of the world's Catholics, and yet the region has been hemorrhaging adherents for decades. In Brazil, the Pew Forum found that the Catholic share of the population fell from roughly 92 percent in 1970 to about 65 percent by 2010, while Pentecostal and other evangelical churches have surged past 22 percent. In Guatemala, evangelicals may already constitute nearly 40 percent of the population. Choosing Jorge Mario Bergoglio is a tacit recognition that Rome cannot keep treating the Western Hemisphere as a stable reserve.

But the symbolism of a Latin American pope will not by itself reverse the trend. The evangelical churches that have taken root in the favelas of Rio, the highlands of Guatemala and the slums of Lima succeed for concrete reasons: lay leadership, a tangible promise of personal transformation, congregational warmth, music, and a willingness to set up shop on the very streets where the institutional Catholic Church has been thin on priests for generations. A neighborhood Assembly of God pastor lives among his flock; the Catholic parish, often understaffed, may serve tens of thousands.

If the Church wants to compete, it will have to learn from what it once dismissed. That means, first, taking the charismatic renewal seriously rather than holding it at arm's length. Catholic charismatic movements already claim tens of millions in Latin America, and figures like Father Marcelo Rossi in Brazil show that liturgical exuberance is not the exclusive property of Protestants. Second, it means expanding lay ministry. The shortage of priests is structural, and base ecclesial communities — once associated with liberation theology — remain one of the few proven models for sustaining Catholic identity without a resident cleric in every village.

Third, the Church will have to decide what to do about the social agenda that gave liberation theology its original appeal. Bergoglio is not a liberationist, but his record in Buenos Aires — riding the bus, washing the feet of AIDS patients, denouncing the indignities of the villas miserias — suggests a "theology of the people" that speaks the language of the poor without the Marxist scaffolding Rome distrusted under John Paul II. That is a more plausible counter to prosperity-gospel evangelicalism than doctrinal retrenchment.

Finally, there is the matter of credibility. The clerical abuse scandals, the Vatileaks affair, and perceptions of an aloof, European-fixated Curia have all corroded the Church's moral authority. A Jesuit pope from the global South, who took the name of the saint of poverty and reform, is well placed to address that. Whether he can translate symbolism into structural change — that is the real test of any Catholic comeback.

The election of an Argentine pope is undoubtedly a dramatic gesture, a symbolic pivot toward a continent that is home to more than 40 percent of the world’s Catholics. But symbolic gestures do not, by themselves, reverse deep institutional trends. To view Francis’s election as a guaranteed “comeback” mistakes recognition for strategy. The Church has finally nodded toward its demographic center of gravity; it has not yet answered why millions of Latin Americans have left.

The primary challenger is Pentecostal and evangelical Protestantism, which has grown explosively in recent decades—most notably in Brazil, Central America, and among the urban poor. These movements succeed not through theology alone but through an intimate, direct pastoral model. They offer tight-knit communities, promises of material and spiritual transformation, and worship that feels emotionally authentic. The Catholic Church, by contrast, has long struggled with a severe priest shortage, leaving many parishes unable to provide the face-to-face engagement that builds loyalty. A pope from Buenos Aires does not instantly generate thousands of locally embedded pastors.

Francis’s own biography suggests he may grasp this. As Archbishop of Buenos Aires, Jorge Mario Bergoglio was known for his austere lifestyle, his focus on the slums, and his critique of priests who refused to baptize children born out of wedlock. That pastoral sensibility, if translated into a global message, could reorient the Church toward a more humble, street-level presence. Yet the Vatican is a complex bureaucracy, and papal priorities are slow to reach the parish pew.

The choice of the name Francis—evoking simplicity and service to the poor—signals an intention to close the gap between the hierarchical institution and the lived experience of the faithful. It is a necessary step, not a finished victory. A Catholic comeback in Latin America is possible, but it will depend less on where the pope was born than on whether his Church can once again become a church of the doorstep and the neighborhood, rather than of the distant cathedral.

The question assumes that evangelical Protestantism's growth in Latin America is primarily a Catholic problem to be solved. It would be more useful to ask why so many Latin Americans, in a single generation, decided that the faith of their grandmothers was no longer adequate for their lives.

The answer is not that the Catholic Church became less appealing. It is that ordinary Latin Americans became different people. They moved from countryside to city. They entered cash economies.
