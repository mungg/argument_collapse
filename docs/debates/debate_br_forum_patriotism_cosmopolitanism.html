Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

There is much to admire in Nussbaum's essay—the seriousness with which she takes our obligations to distant strangers, her insistence that the boundaries of moral concern not coincide with the boundaries of the nation-state, her recovery of the Stoic tradition for contemporary debate. Yet I find myself unpersuaded that the contrast she draws between "cosmopolitan" and "patriotic" education is as clean as she suggests, and worried that her proposal misdescribes both the moral psychology of attachment and the political conditions under which her own ideals might be realized.

Begin with the moral psychology. Nussbaum concedes, following Hierocles, that we may keep our concentric circles of attachment, but she asks us to draw them "toward the center" so that humanity as such commands our first allegiance. The trouble is that "first allegiance" is doing two different jobs in her argument. As a claim about the ultimate source of moral justification—that reasons must in principle be defensible to all rational beings—it is a familiar Kantian thesis and seems right. But as a claim about the proximate objects of civic emotion and identification, it asks something the Stoics themselves had difficulty supplying: a motivational source equal to the demands of justice. Tagore's Nikhil is admirable, but he is also, as Bimala discovers too late, the loser in the political contest the novel depicts. That is not a footnote to the argument; it is the argument. If cosmopolitanism cannot grip the imagination, then teaching it as the central organizing identity of civic life may produce neither cosmopolitans nor citizens, but rather privatized individuals who feel little reason to sacrifice anything for anyone.

This brings me to the institutional question Nussbaum largely sidesteps. Rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness are not floating Kantian abstractions; they are protected, when they are protected at all, by particular states with taxing powers, courts, and armies. The civil rights revolution, the labor movement, the construction of whatever welfare state we possess—each required citizens to recognize obligations to fellow citizens they had never met, and each was carried forward by an argument that ran, in part, "we Americans must live up to what we say we are." That is patriotism doing moral work. To call the nation a "morally arbitrary" boundary is true in the metaphysical sense in which any institution is arbitrary, but false in the sense that matters: the nation is the actually existing site where redistribution, deliberation, and accountability presently occur. Until something replaces it, telling students that their primary allegiance lies elsewhere risks dissolving the only solidarity strong enough to fund the schools in which cosmopolitanism would be taught.

Nussbaum's fourth argument—that we cannot consistently demand mutual respect across ethnic lines inside the nation while withholding it across the national border—has rhetorical force but proves too much. The same logic would dissolve the special obligations she explicitly preserves toward our own children. If giving one's children extra care is justified because, as she puts it, "it is good for children, on the whole, that things should work out this way," then perhaps giving one's fellow citizens extra care is justified on parallel grounds: democratic self-government, mutual accountability, and the cultivation of public virtues all seem to require bounded communities of fate. Rorty's position, as I read it, is not the jingoism Nussbaum fears but precisely this insight—that shame and pride in a shared "we" are the affective preconditions of reform.

What I would urge instead is not a choice between Rorty and Nussbaum but a layered civic education: one that teaches American history with neither piety nor contempt, that situates that history within a genuinely global frame (the slave trade, the world wars, the current ecological predicament Nussbaum rightly emphasizes), and that builds outward from particular loyalties rather than asking students to begin from the species. The Stoics were right that reason recognizes no borders. But Aristotle was also right that we become just by performing just acts in the company of those with whom we share a common life. Cosmopolitanism without a polis to stand on is not a destination; it is a homelessness.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

I appreciate Martha Nussbaum’s eloquent challenge to the complacent nationalism that so often passes for civic education in the United States. Her call to take seriously our obligations to humanity as a whole is a necessary corrective to the inward-looking rhetoric that dominates our public discourse. Yet I am troubled by the stark opposition she draws between patriotism and cosmopolitanism, and by her suggestion that the former is not merely insufficient but “morally dangerous” in itself. In her effort to lift our gaze beyond the nation, she risks dismissing the very attachments that make democratic citizenship possible in the first place—and, paradoxically, that might most effectively advance the universal values she herself champions.

Nussbaum’s central claim is that giving patriotism a primary place in moral and political deliberation subverts the goals of justice and equality by substituting a “colorful idol” for universal right. But this rests on a particular, and I think uncharitable, reading of what patriotism can entail. When Richard Rorty urges Americans to cultivate “national pride,” he is not calling for jingoistic self-congratulation or the uncritical worship of country that Tagore’s Sandip embodies. He is addressing an American left that has, in his view, ceded the language of national identity to the right, thereby forfeiting a powerful instrument for mobilizing solidarity across class and racial lines. The Civil Rights movement, to take an example that Nussbaum does not engage, drew much of its moral force from the insistence that America live up to its own professed ideals—that the promise of equality written into the Declaration and the Constitution be honored not just for white citizens but for all. That movement was patriotic in the deepest sense, and its achievements depended in part on the emotional resonance of a shared national story, flawed as that story was. To dismiss such patriotism as inherently tribal is to overlook how universal norms can be embedded in, and advanced through, particular loyalties.

Nussbaum’s cosmopolitan alternative, for all its philosophical pedigree, faces a motivational obstacle that she acknowledges but then sets aside. She admits that becoming a citizen of the world is “often a lonely business,” a kind of exile from the warmth of local belonging. Yet she treats this as an existential challenge that can be overcome by Stoic discipline and a vivid education. But political action does not run on reason alone; it requires the kind of emotional investment that comes from sharing a concrete fate with others. The nation, whatever its moral arbitrariness, is for most people the largest unit within which they can realistically hope to shape collective decisions democratically. It provides the institutional machinery of lawmaking, taxation, and redistribution that makes justice something more than a philosophical aspiration. A cosmopolitanism that asks us to regard our own country as merely one temporal power among many may inadvertently weaken the citizen’s sense of responsibility for what that government does—both at home and abroad. If I am first and foremost a citizen of the world, why should I hold my own country especially accountable for its foreign policy, its environmental impact, or its trade practices? A robust patriotic attachment, by contrast, can fuel precisely the kind of critical engagement that Nussbaum desires: it is because I identify with this nation that I am ashamed when it acts unjustly, and determined to change its course.

This is not to deny that patriotism can slide into chauvinism or xenophobia. The examples of the former Yugoslavia and Rwanda, fresh in our minds, show the horror that ethnic nationalism can unleash. But these are not arguments against patriotism per se; they are arguments against a particular kind of blood-and-soil nationalism that defines the nation by exclusion. The American tradition, at its best, has aspired to a civic patriotism grounded in shared political principles rather than shared descent. That tradition is imperfectly realized, to be sure, but its very imperfection offers the critical patriot a foothold for reform. Nussbaum’s fear that patriotism inevitably leads to the fragmentation she sees in Tagore’s novel—from “Indian first” to “Hindu first” to “upper-caste landlord first”—assumes that the nation must be defined in ethnic or religious terms. But a nation can also be defined by a commitment to constitutional democracy and the ongoing project of making that democracy more inclusive. In that case, patriotism and cosmopolitanism need not be enemies: a citizen can hold that her primary political obligations lie with the fellow members of her national community, while simultaneously recognizing that the values animating that community—human dignity, equal rights—are universal in scope.

Nussbaum’s Stoic metaphor of concentric circles is a helpful corrective to the view that we must choose between local and global loyalties. But she then undermines its subtlety by insisting that the outermost circle of humanity must command our “first allegiance.” The Stoics themselves, including Hierocles whom she cites, did not demand that we abolish the special concerns due to those nearest us; they urged a gradual extension of our sympathy, a drawing of the circles closer, so that we treat fellow citizens with something of the care we reserve for family, and foreigners with something of the care we reserve for fellow citizens. This is a process, not a zero-sum choice. A cosmopolitan education rightly understood would teach students about the histories and struggles of people in other lands, not in order to dissolve their attachment to America, but to enrich it—to help them see their own nation as one among many, interconnected in ways that generate mutual obligations. It would cultivate the habit of seeing from the perspective of others, as Marcus Aurelius advised, precisely so that one could be a better citizen of one’s own place, capable of judging when patriotism becomes parochialism.

The real educational challenge, then, is not to replace patriotism with cosmopolitanism but to shape a patriotic tradition that is self-critical and outward-looking. The American curriculum Nussbaum rightly criticizes for its ignorance of the wider world does not suffer from an excess of genuine patriotism; it suffers from a shallow chauvinism that mistakes slogans for understanding. A deeper study of our own history, including its moments of shame and its debts to other cultures, would itself be an exercise in cosmopolitanism. And a deeper study of other nations’ attempts to realize decent government would give students the comparative perspective they need to assess their own country’s performance—not from a stance of detached neutrality, but from the engaged standpoint of one who cares enough about the American experiment to want it to succeed on terms worthy of universal respect.

In the end, the dichotomy Nussbaum offers may be more a reflection of academic debate than of lived political experience. The landlord Nikhil’s tragedy is not that he was a cosmopolitan, but that he failed to connect his universal ideals to the legitimate desire for collective self-expression that moved his wife and his countrymen. The lesson of Tagore’s novel is not that patriotism is a curse, but that a patriotism unmoored from justice becomes idolatry, while a cosmopolitanism unmoored from concrete political community becomes ineffectual. We need both: a patriotism chastened by the knowledge that our nation is not the exclusive repository of value, and a cosmopolitanism rooted in the real work of building just institutions where we actually live. That, it seems to me, is the education our times demand.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Martha Nussbaum mounts an eloquent and morally ambitious defense of the Stoic ideal of the *kosmou politês*, warning us against the exclusionary dangers of national pride. Writing in a year when the horrors of ethnic nationalism are vividly apparent in Rwanda and the Balkans, her call for cosmopolitan education resonates deeply. Yet, in her eagerness to dismiss Richard Rorty’s plea for shared national identity, Nussbaum underestimates the psychological and political necessity of the very boundaries she seeks to transcend.

Nussbaum invokes the Stoic metaphor of concentric circles, arguing we must "draw the circles somehow toward the center." But human affection and moral motivation are inherently radial; they diminish in intensity as they expand outward. To love humanity in the abstract is easy, but it is often practically inert. Rorty’s defense of patriotism is not an embrace of jingoism, as Nussbaum fears, but a pragmatic recognition that democratic solidarity requires a localized, emotionally resonant "we." The welfare state, civil rights legislation, and domestic justice are achieved precisely because citizens feel a special, bounded obligation to one another.

Furthermore, global problems—such as the ecological crises Nussbaum correctly identifies—are currently solvable only through the concerted actions of strong nation-states. An education that skips over national allegiance in favor of global citizenship risks cultivating a generation of detached observers, equipped with universal ideals but lacking the grounded political machinery to implement them.

We should indeed educate students about the diverse cultures, families, and crises of the wider world, as Nussbaum suggests. However, a healthy patriotism is not the enemy of global responsibility; it is its prerequisite. We learn to love the world by first learning to take responsibility for our own particular corner of it.

Martha Nussbaum is right to warn against the worship of country. The century now drawing to a close has provided too many examples of nations made into gods: Europe’s fascisms, ethnic cleansing in the former Yugoslavia, the poisonous politics of religious nationalism in South Asia, and many lesser forms of swaggering indifference. She is also right that American education remains scandalously provincial. A student can graduate from a good school knowing little of Africa beyond famine, of Islam beyond oil politics, of India beyond mysticism and poverty, or of Latin America beyond immigration and debt. Such ignorance is not merely discourteous; it is politically dangerous in a world of nuclear weapons, global markets, epidemics, ecological interdependence, and vast inequalities.

But I do not think the remedy is to make world citizenship the “primary” allegiance and national citizenship secondary. The better answer is a patriotism chastened by cosmopolitan morality: a love of one’s country that is neither ultimate nor innocent, but still indispensable. Nussbaum’s target is patriotic idolatry, and against that she is eloquent. Yet she too quickly treats patriotism itself as though it naturally slides into jingoism, exclusion, and faction. It can do so. But it can also be one of the chief ways universal principles acquire practical force.

Consider the American examples Nussbaum herself has in view. The most powerful critics of American injustice have often spoken not as exiles from American identity but as claimants to its betrayed promises. Frederick Douglass’s Fourth of July oration, Lincoln’s appeal to the Declaration, the abolitionists’ invocation of natural rights, and Martin Luther King Jr.’s insistence that America honor its “promissory note” all drew energy from a patriotic moral vocabulary. King’s language was not merely tribal; it was universalist Christianity and natural-rights liberalism. But it mattered that he addressed Americans as Americans, recalling them to a shared story they could not easily disown. A purely cosmopolitan rebuke might have been morally correct; it would not necessarily have moved the conscience of Birmingham, Washington, or Selma.

This suggests that patriotism need not compete with universal justice. At its best, it is a historically situated commitment to make one political community more just, more decent, and more answerable to the claims of humanity. We do not love our country because it is morally supreme. We love it because it is ours to repair. The possessive pronoun here is not metaphysical; it is practical. These are the institutions through which we vote, tax, litigate, educate, punish, defend, and redistribute. These are the fellow citizens with whom we must make binding decisions. If we abandon the language of common membership, we may weaken the very solidarities that make democratic justice possible.

Nussbaum rightly invokes concentric circles, but her argument gives insufficient weight to the inner political circle of citizenship. Families are not morally ultimate; nevertheless, children are ordinarily best cared for when particular adults bear special responsibility for them. So too with political communities. A citizen of the United States has obligations to hungry children in Bangladesh and Sudan, but she also has distinctive obligations to the public schools of Chicago, the prisons of Texas, the wages of farmworkers in California, and the constitutional rights of unpopular minorities in her own polity. These obligations are not grounded in the superior worth of Americans. They arise from participation in a coercive scheme of law and collective power. I help authorize, benefit from, and am represented by American institutions. That gives me special duties to reform them.

Nor is cosmopolitanism free from dangers of its own. It can become an elegant abstraction, more comfortable in seminars than in neighborhoods. To say “I am a citizen of the world” may enlarge sympathy; it may also release one from the more tedious burdens of actual citizenship: attending meetings, compromising with disagreeable compatriots, paying taxes, serving on juries, learning local history, accepting responsibility for national crimes. The world citizen has no world legislature to vote in, no world welfare state to sustain, no world public school board to confront. We should build stronger international institutions, and we should teach students to care about them. But in 1994 the effective instruments of democratic accountability remain overwhelmingly national and local. Moral imagination may be global; political responsibility is still mediated.

Nussbaum asks why the values that bind citizens together should “lose steam” at the border. They should not. But it does not follow that borders are morally meaningless. They are not magical lines transforming strangers into persons; they are jurisdictional lines marking shared institutions. A Chinese immigrant in New York and a Chinese citizen in Shanghai are both entitled to human respect. But the former is also a participant, actual or prospective, in our constitutional order. We owe both justice; we owe the former the additional duties generated by common citizenship. The distinction is not between full humanity and lesser humanity. It is between universal moral regard and special political relationship.

The educational implication is not patriotic indoctrination. Schools should not teach children to “rejoice” uncritically in America, still less to rank American lives above others. They should teach the crimes as well as the achievements: slavery, conquest of native peoples, exclusion of immigrants, Hiroshima, support for dictatorships, as well as constitutionalism, emancipation, labor struggles, civil liberties, and democratic reform. They should also teach world history, comparative religion, foreign languages, global ecology, colonialism, development, and the unequal distribution of resources. On this central curricular point Nussbaum’s case is compelling.

But the student should learn to say not simply, “I am above all a citizen of the world,” nor simply, “I am above all an American.” She should learn a harder sentence: “I am an American citizen whose country is answerable to the world.” That formula preserves what Nussbaum most wants—universal moral scrutiny—while retaining what democratic politics most needs: a sense of shared fate and mutual obligation among citizens.

Tagore’s Nikhil is right that Right is greater than country. But it does not follow that country is negligible. The task is to love without worshiping, to criticize without disowning, to belong without excluding, and to remember that the nation is not a god but a responsibility.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Nussbaum's case for cosmopolitan education deserves serious engagement, but her critique of patriotism ultimately proves too sweeping. She rightly identifies the dangers of nationalism elevated to worship, yet her alternative demands more than human psychology and political reality can sustain.

Her first argument—that cosmopolitan education yields self-knowledge—rests on a false dichotomy. One can study other cultures and one's own simultaneously without abandoning national identity. American students already learn about Indian families, Bolivian economics, and Nigerian history alongside their own. The question is not whether to look outward but whether that looking must replace rather than supplement patriotic education.

The second argument addresses genuine problems. Environmental degradation does indeed cross borders, and international cooperation is necessary. But Nussbaum conflates the need for cooperation with the sufficiency of cosmopolitan identity. The Kyoto Protocol, the Montreal Protocol—these agreements emerged from nations acting in concert, not from world citizens. Political cooperation typically requires some degree of particular loyalty, some "we" willing to sacrifice for a "them."

Her third argument proves most compelling: Americans do enjoy standards that cannot be universalized without ecological catastrophe. This moral tension deserves serious attention. Yet her solution—teaching children to see themselves as citizens of the world first—may inadvertently undermine the very moral seriousness she seeks. Political motivation typically requires concrete ties. We donate to save children in our town before children abroad, not because we value local children more but because solidarity begins at home. Cosmopolitan abstractions risk producing not moral engagement but paralysis.

The fourth argument—that patriotism undercuts multicultural respect within nations—is clever but mistaken. Rorty's defense of national pride need not exclude global concern. One can take pride in American commitments to equality while recognizing that other nations share and sometimes surpass those commitments. The civil rights movement, women's suffrage, the defeat of fascism—these achievements merit patriotic pride precisely because they demonstrate moral progress, not because they are uniquely American.

Nussbaum's Stoic vision is beautiful and her Tagore invocation moving. But she underestimates what she herself concedes: that concentric circles of loyalty can coexist, that special care for one's own is justifiable in universalist terms. The genuine question is not whether to be citizens of the world but how to balance world citizenship with the particular loyalties that make political life possible. A pedagogy that neglects the latter may produce neither good cosmopolitans nor effective patriots.

The Thin Comfort of the World

Martha Nussbaum's essay is graceful, learned, and in many places admirable. Who would not wish for an America less ignorant of the world, for children who can find Bolivia on a map and who feel some pang of conscience about the air that drifts northward from a Mexican smelter? The trouble is that almost none of the practical and educational conclusions she draws actually require the cosmopolitan metaphysics she invokes. And the metaphysics, when pressed, turns out to be more an evasion than a foundation.

Consider Nussbaum's most powerful argument: that we Americans cannot live at our present standard if the rest of humanity follows suit, and we ought to be troubled by this. Quite right. But the moral force of that observation does not depend on our regarding ourselves as citizens of the world before being citizens of the United States. It depends on the much more modest premise that our actions affect others and that the others count. One can grant the full Kantian premise—that every human being deserves respect—without abandoning the claim that I have special and differentiated obligations to the particular people with whom I have made a political life. A parent who feeds her child first does not deny the moral standing of other children; she discharges a role-specific duty that, as Nussbaum herself concedes, the abstract universalist must in the end justify.

The concession is fatal to the rhetorical shape of her essay. Once you admit, as Nussbaum does in section three, that "politics, like child care, will be poorly done if each thinks herself equally responsible for all," the question between her and Rorty becomes empirical and pedagogical rather than foundational. How much weight should the local get? What proportion of the curriculum? Which obligations are owed to whom? These are interesting questions, but they are not settled by quoting Marcus Aurelius. Indeed Marcus, governing an empire, is a curious patron saint for a politics meant to be skeptical of grand civilizational pretensions.

Nussbaum claims that to concede moral weight to the "morally arbitrary" boundary of the nation undermines our case for crossing barriers of race and gender within it. This seems to me precisely backwards. The reason Americans have, over the last half-century, been persuaded—imperfectly, painfully—to extend full citizenship to black Americans, to women, to immigrants, is not that they were convinced of a thin Stoic universalism. It is that they came to see these neighbors as full members of a shared American story: as fellow citizens whose claims on the polity were the same as their own. Patriotism, broadened and chastened, did the work. Abstract humanitarianism, historically, has done remarkably little. The great cosmopolitan empires of the nineteenth century were not noticeably kinder to their subjects than the bounded republics that succeeded them.

There is also something curious in Nussbaum's choice of Tagore against Gandhi, of the cool landlord Nikhil against the imagined Sandip. The independence movement that actually freed India was not a movement of detached Stoics; it was a movement saturated with the language of Mother India, with hymns and symbols and the deep particularism of swaraj. Gandhi was a patriot. So was King. So was Havel. The great moral movements of our century have almost all spoken the language of "we, this people"—and have appealed to universal principles through the medium of a particular loyalty, not over its head.

None of this is to defend jingoism, which Nussbaum rightly fears. But the answer to bad patriotism is better patriotism, not its abolition in favor of a citizenship to which no actual institution corresponds. Diogenes could say he was a citizen of the world because he had renounced the duties of an Athenian; the world owed him nothing and he owed it nothing in particular. That is not a model for democratic education. It is a model for the philosophical tramp.

Teach children about India and Bolivia and Norway, by all means. Teach them that the air does not stop at the border. Teach them that universal principles bind us. But teach them, too, that they are heirs to a particular and improvable inheritance, and that the work of justice is done, in this world, mostly through the messy institutions of nations. That is not idolatry. It is realism about where moral agency lives.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

A View from Outside the West

Reading Professor Nussbaum's essay from a vantage point outside the comfortable precincts of the American academy, one is struck by how much of its cosmopolitanism remains, despite itself, a local product. The Stoics are summoned; Kant is invoked; Tagore is read—but Tagore is read selectively, as if his quarrel with nationalism could be detached from his equally fierce quarrel with the universalism preached by European empires. The same Tagore who scorned Bande Mataram also wrote that the West, in its claim to speak for humanity, had built its temple upon the bones of subject peoples. The cosmopolitan ideal, in his hands, was not the smooth extension of Enlightenment reason to the globe. It was a critique of that extension.

Nussbaum's cosmopolitanism, by contrast, is unmistakably an American article. It speaks confidently of "the values on which Americans may most justly pride themselves" as being "in a deep sense, Stoic values"—a sentence which manages to claim Marcus Aurelius for Philadelphia. It assumes that the universal language in which world citizens will converse is, more or less, the language already spoken in the seminar rooms of Chicago and Providence. And it treats "humanity" as a transparent category, when in fact every actual exercise of cosmopolitan reasoning—from the Roman Stoa to the United Nations Development Programme where she has worked—has had to decide whose conception of the human shall stand for the species.

This is not an idle worry. Many of us in the formerly colonized world have been told before that we are citizens of the world. We were told it by missionaries who came to free us from local superstition. We were told it by colonial administrators who came to bring us into the universal market. We are told it now by structural adjustment programs that demand the dismantling of particular national economies in the name of a borderless human prosperity. When Professor Nussbaum's children are taught to see Bolivians and Nigerians as fellow members of the human community, that is good. When they are taught that the standards by which Bolivia and Nigeria are to be assessed—standards of development, of rights, of family structure, of women's work—are simply the standards of universal reason, that is something else.

I do not say this to defend ethno-nationalism, which in my own region has done its full share of damage. Nussbaum is right that the slide from "I am a citizen of country X" to "I am a member of community Y within country X" is short and ugly, and that the secular Indian state is under threat from forces that would do well to be tempered by some Stoic cool. But the answer to bad nationalism is not the dissolution of nations. For most peoples of the world, the nation is the only instrument they have ever possessed for resisting the universal claims of others. Take away the nation, and you do not arrive at a global community of equals; you arrive at a global market in which the strong continue to speak the language of humanity while the weak are told to be reasonable.

Nussbaum's four arguments are unobjectionable as far as they go. Of course American students should know more about the world. Of course pollution does not respect borders. Of course one cannot universalize one's standard of living. But these are arguments for better internationalism, not for the demotion of national citizenship to a "morally arbitrary" accident. The nation is morally arbitrary only if one has already decided that the proper unit of moral concern is the species. And that decision is itself a particular tradition's choice, not the view from nowhere.

What I would urge, against both Rorty's nation-first patriotism and Nussbaum's species-first cosmopolitanism, is a pluralism of moral attachments in which neither layer claims final priority. The nation is one of the circles Hierocles drew. So is humanity. Neither swallows the other. To teach a child that she is "above all" a citizen of the world is no improvement over teaching her that she is "above all" an American; both formulations demand a ranking that life does not in fact require. Better to teach her that she stands within many concentric obligations, and that wisdom consists in knowing, in each situation, which circle is being addressed.

That is closer, I think, to what Tagore actually believed. He founded Visva-Bharati not to dissolve Bengal into humanity but to let Bengal converse with the world on terms of dignity. The conversation, not the dissolution, is the cosmopolitan thing.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Patriotism Is Not the Enemy

Professor Nussbaum has written a beautiful essay about an ideal she calls cosmopolitanism, but she has not, I think, written a fair essay about the thing she calls patriotism. The patriotism she attacks is a straw figure: a worship of country as god, a Sandip-like intoxication with symbols, a refusal to look beyond one's borders. No one I know defends that. Certainly Richard Rorty, the proximate target of her essay, does not. What Rorty defended in his Times piece was something much more modest and much more defensible: the proposition that a left which cannot bring itself to feel any pride in the country it seeks to reform will not be able to reform it.

This is not a metaphysical claim about the priority of the nation over humanity. It is a sociological observation about how political movements actually mobilize. The civil rights movement appealed not to abstract humanity but to the promises of the Declaration of Independence. The labor movement appealed to American traditions of dignity and republican self-rule. The suffragists invoked the Constitution. Each of these movements expanded the circle of moral concern, often dramatically, and each did so by speaking in the dialect of national membership rather than against it. To call this jingoism is to mistake an instrument for an idolatry.

Nussbaum's argument has, at its heart, what philosophers sometimes call a category error. She treats patriotism and cosmopolitanism as rival answers to the same question—"To which community do I owe my first allegiance?"—and concludes that the cosmopolitan answer is more defensible. But patriotism, as practiced by sane people, is not a candidate answer to that question at all. It is not a claim about ultimate moral priority. It is an affective relation to particular institutions, places, and histories, accompanied by a sense of responsibility for their improvement. One can have such a relation while also believing, with full Kantian conviction, that every human being is of equal worth. One can love one's country without thinking it the best, just as one can love one's spouse without thinking her the most beautiful woman in the world.

Indeed, the comparison is illuminating. Suppose someone told us that marital love was morally dangerous, because it elevates a "morally arbitrary" person—the one you happen to have married—above the rest of humanity, who are equally deserving of love. Suppose she added that we should rather love human beings as such, and that the special affection given to spouses is at best a concession to practical necessity. We would, I hope, regard this as a misunderstanding of what love is. Marital love is not a ranking; it is a relation. Patriotism, in its decent form, is similar. It is the relation a citizen has to the polity she helps to constitute and is constituted by, and the demand that she repudiate it in favor of a relation to humanity at large is a demand that she become, morally speaking, no one in particular.

Nussbaum will reply that humanity is not "no one in particular" but the most morally significant community of all. Yet humanity is not a community. It does not deliberate. It does not have institutions through which obligations can be discharged and disputes adjudicated. It has no laws, no taxes, no schools, no army, and no court whose writ runs unimpeded across borders. The "community of human argument and aspiration" of which Seneca spoke is a metaphor, and a noble one, but it cannot do the work that actual political communities do. To owe one's first allegiance to it is to owe one's first allegiance to an abstraction—and abstractions, as the bloody history of this century has shown, can become idols as easily as flags can.

There is a further point. Nussbaum worries that defending the nation's moral weight undermines arguments for crossing internal barriers of race and gender. I would say the opposite. The moral logic that integrated the American workplace and the American school was the logic of equal citizenship: these are our fellow Americans, and they must be treated as such. That argument has bite precisely because "American" names a real community with real obligations of mutual treatment. Replace it with "fellow human being" and the argument loses traction—not because human beings do not deserve respect, but because the institutional vehicle for delivering that respect is unclear. Civil rights are claims against a state. They have to be made by people who consider themselves entitled to make them, against a state they consider their own.

I share Nussbaum's hope that American students learn more geography, more history, more languages, more about the texture of lives lived elsewhere. I share her alarm at the smug provincialism that has too often passed for civic education here. But these are arguments for cosmopolitan content within a patriotic frame, not for the dissolution of the frame. The child who learns about India in an American school is being prepared to be a better American citizen of a world her country shares with others. That is not a betrayal of the cosmopolitan ideal. It is, I would argue, the only way the ideal has ever made any practical headway at all.

Two Cheers for Nussbaum

Martha Nussbaum has done something genuinely important: she has reopened, in a public forum, a question that has lain quiet in American political philosophy for too long. The question is whether the moral cosmopolitanism that almost all of us profess—the conviction that every human being is of equal worth—can be reconciled with the political nationalism that almost all of us practice. Her answer, broadly, is that it cannot, and that the cosmopolitan side should win. I want to give two cheers for her position and explain why I withhold the third.

The first cheer is for her diagnosis. The recent vogue, on both right and left, for what is sometimes called "communitarianism" has produced some impressive scholarship and a great deal of mush. Among its mushier products has been the suggestion that liberal political philosophy has erred by treating individuals as abstracted from their thick particular attachments, and that the remedy lies in restoring the moral weight of nation, locality, tradition. Rorty's piece is a relatively sophisticated specimen of the type, but the genre as a whole has tended to forget that one of liberalism's central achievements was precisely to limit the moral claims of inherited collective identity. Nussbaum is right to insist that the cosmopolitan tradition—Stoic, Kantian, Enlightenment—is not some bloodless rationalism but a continuous moral protest against the idolatry of the local. We forget this at our peril.

The second cheer is for her educational proposals. The provincialism of American schooling is a national embarrassment. Our students learn next to nothing about the histories, languages, religions, and political experiences of the four-fifths of humanity that does not live in Western Europe or North America. The result is not only ignorance but a kind of moral disability: a citizenry incapable of imagining itself into the situations of those whose lives are affected by its government's choices. When the United States debates trade policy with Mexico, or aid to sub-Saharan Africa, or intervention in the Balkans, it does so in a fog of unfamiliarity that no democracy worthy of the name should tolerate. Nussbaum's call for a curriculum that treats the world as the proper object of civic study is exactly right, and I would go further than she does in pressing it.

The withheld third cheer concerns the philosophical frame. Nussbaum wants to say not merely that we have substantial obligations to non-citizens, and not merely that national borders are morally arbitrary in some fundamental sense, but that our "first allegiance" should be to humanity as such rather than to the nation. This formulation strikes me as both unnecessary for her practical conclusions and damaging to their political prospects.

It is unnecessary because everything Nussbaum wants to say about pollution, hunger, child-rearing, and the universal scope of Kantian respect can be said without the ranking. One can hold that obligations to non-citizens are morally weighty, often weightier than ordinary patriotic discourse acknowledges, without claiming that they always trump obligations to one's compatriots. The image she borrows from Hierocles—of concentric circles to be drawn "somehow toward the center"—is in fact a non-ranking image. It does not say that the outer circle is most important; it says that all circles deserve moral attention and that the outer ones are systematically neglected. That, I take it, is the operative point, and it does not require the further claim that humanity comes "first."

It is damaging because the further claim invites a response Nussbaum cannot afford: that her position is philosophically tone-deaf to the actual phenomenology of moral life. People do not, in fact, encounter humanity as such. They encounter their children, their neighbors, their coreligionists, their countrymen, and—at increasing remove—the rest. A moral theory that tells them that their first allegiance is to an abstraction will not be believed, and what is not believed cannot guide. The genius of the Stoic tradition, when it was at its best, was to recognize that universal reason had to be lived out through particular attachments, not against them. Marcus Aurelius did not stop being a Roman emperor when he meditated on the unity of the human limb-and-part. He governed Rome better, perhaps, but he governed Rome.

Nussbaum knows all this; her concessions in section three acknowledge much of it. But the rhetorical thrust of her essay—and especially its framing against Rorty—obscures the concessions. Readers will come away thinking she has argued for the demotion of patriotism, when what she has actually argued for is its chastening. The chastening I support without reservation. The demotion I think a philosophical error and a tactical mistake.

So: two cheers. The third is owed not to Nussbaum but to whoever can reconcile her substantive program—a humane, knowledgeable, world-aware citizenship—with the patriotic emotional grammar through which Americans actually conduct their political lives. That synthesis is the real work, and the lead essay, for all its eloquence, points toward it more than it accomplishes it.

What Cosmopolitanism Forgets About Power

There is a kind of essay that gets written every generation by humane and learned people who would like the world to be more just, and who suppose that the path to justice lies through the broadening of moral imagination. Professor Nussbaum's essay is a distinguished example of the genre. Its premises are decent; its tone is admirable; its references are well chosen. And yet, reading it, one cannot escape the suspicion that it is addressed to a world that does not exist, and that its prescriptions, if adopted, would leave the actual world more or less as it is.

The world that does not exist is one in which moral persuasion does the heavy lifting of politics. In that world, if American students were taught more about Bolivia, they would feel more solidarity with Bolivians, and this feeling would translate into policy choices more favorable to Bolivia. In that world, if the cosmopolitan Stoic image of humanity as a single body could be imparted in the schools, the trade regime, the pattern of capital flows, the structure of military alliances, and the distribution of ecological harms would all begin to shift. In that world, in short, the explanation for the suffering of the global poor is that the global rich do not yet sufficiently understand them. Better curricula will fix it.

The actual world is otherwise. The relation between the United States and Bolivia is not principally constituted by the sentiments of American students. It is constituted by the operations of mining companies, by the conditions attached to loans from international financial institutions, by the policies of the United States Trade Representative, by the demands of the war on drugs. American ignorance of Bolivia is a real problem, but it is downstream of, not upstream of, the structural arrangements that determine how Bolivia is treated. A maximally cosmopolitan curriculum, taught by saints, would do little to disturb those arrangements unless it were accompanied by something Nussbaum's essay almost entirely lacks: an analysis of who benefits from them and how their power is to be confronted.

This is, I think, the deepest difference between the cosmopolitan tradition Nussbaum invokes and the internationalist tradition of the labor and socialist movements. Both traditions speak of humanity, but they speak of it differently. The Stoic cosmopolitan says: recognize humanity in the foreigner, attend to her dignity, deliberate with her. The socialist internationalist says: the workers of Bolivia and the workers of Pittsburgh share a common antagonist, and only by acting together can either be free. The first is an ethical stance; the second is a political strategy. The first can be adopted by a humanities professor at the University of Chicago without disturbing anyone's interests; the second cannot be adopted by anyone without disturbing the interests of a great many powerful people. That is not an accident.

I do not mean to suggest that Nussbaum is unaware of power. Her work in development economics has obviously brought her into contact with it. But the essay before us treats the obstacle to a more just world as essentially educational and imaginative, and it locates the cosmopolitan tradition almost entirely in literature, philosophy, and the curriculum. The political vehicles by which a more cosmopolitan order might actually be brought into being—international labor solidarity, the non-aligned movement, the various campaigns against the new debt regime, the indigenous mobilizations now stirring across Latin America—do not appear. Nor does the question of why those vehicles have been so systematically obstructed, and by whom.

There is, additionally, a class question internal to the cosmopolitan ideal as Nussbaum presents it. To be a citizen of the world, in the relevant sense, requires a certain detachment from one's local circumstances. Diogenes could afford it because he had renounced property. Marcus Aurelius could afford it because he owned an empire. The university professor can afford it because her labor is portable and her income secure. The auto worker in Flint cannot afford it, and when she is told that her attachment to her nation, her neighborhood, her union local is a parochialism to be overcome in favor of allegiance to humanity as such, she correctly suspects that the proposal is being made on behalf of someone who would like her to stop making expensive claims on her national polity. Cosmopolitanism has too often been the ideology of those for whom borders are an inconvenience, addressed to those for whom borders are a protection.

None of this is to defend American chauvinism, which is a sickness and which Rorty's essay underestimates. Nor is it to deny that internationalism—real internationalism, the kind that organizes across borders rather than dissolving them—is desperately needed. It is to insist that internationalism is a political project, with allies and enemies, and that it cannot be conducted as a seminar on the Stoics. Tagore's Nikhil, whom Nussbaum admires, is in the novel a landlord; his cosmopolitan virtue is, among other things, a function of his position. Sandip is a demagogue, and Nussbaum is right to deplore him, but the appeal of demagogues to people who have been condescended to by cosmopolitan landlords is a phenomenon she might have addressed more directly. The defeat of cosmopolitanism in the novel is not merely a tragedy of mistaken passion; it is a tragedy of a class that mistook its own decency for politics.

What is wanted, then, is not less cosmopolitanism but a cosmopolitanism with teeth: one that names the structures by which the global rich extract wealth from the global poor, one that allies itself with the movements of the poor rather than improving their image among the rich, one that understands that the question "to what community do I owe allegiance?" is less important than the question "with whom must I act, and against whom?" Nussbaum's essay gives us most of the materials for such a position. It just does not, in the end, build it.

The Curriculum Is the Question

Whatever one thinks of the philosophical contest between Professor Nussbaum and Professor Rorty, the practical question their exchange raises is one we cannot avoid: what should American children actually be taught? On this, I find myself in considerable agreement with Nussbaum, even where I am uncertain about the larger Stoic frame.

The facts about American education are not in serious dispute. Our students leave high school with a startlingly thin grasp of the geography, history, and contemporary politics of the world outside North America and Western Europe. Surveys regularly find that substantial fractions of American eighteen-year-olds cannot locate major countries on a map, identify the basic facts of the Cold War's principal theaters, or name the religions practiced by a majority of the world's population. This is not a problem of "values"; it is a problem of information. And it cannot be addressed by debates about whether children should feel themselves first as Americans or first as humans. It can be addressed only by changing what is taught.

What Nussbaum's essay usefully provides, beneath its philosophical apparatus, is a rationale for that change. Her four arguments—self-knowledge through comparison, the necessity of international cooperation, the moral obligations attaching to global interdependence, and the consistency of universalist principle—are exactly the rationales that ought to be put before school boards, textbook committees, and state education officials when they ask why their students should spend time on India and Nigeria and Bolivia. Each argument is independently strong. Together they make a case that, in my experience of curriculum debates, is more persuasive than any appeal to mere global awareness.

I would only add a fifth argument, more prosaic than Nussbaum's but politically potent: American economic and security interests now depend on a citizenry able to think competently about the wider world. The graduates of our high schools will be voting, in the next several decades, on matters ranging from trade with East Asia to humanitarian intervention to climate to immigration. They will do so badly if their mental map of the world remains as parochial as it currently is. This is not a cosmopolitan argument in Nussbaum's strict sense; it is a national-interest argument. But it points to the same curricular conclusions, and it has the advantage of being persuasive to people who do not share her philosophical premises.

This suggests, finally, that the Nussbaum–Rorty disagreement may be less consequential at the level of schools than it appears at the level of journals. A patriotic American who wants her country to flourish in a complex world, and a cosmopolitan who wants her students to honor the dignity of humanity as such, will find themselves agreeing on almost every concrete curricular question. Both will want more world history, more languages, more geography, more comparative study of institutions, more attention to the global dimensions of ecology and economy. Both will want less of the narrow civics that treats the United States as a closed system. The metaphysical disagreement about first allegiance need not be resolved in order to act.

If I have a worry about Nussbaum's essay, it is that by framing the practical question in such grand philosophical terms, she risks alienating allies whom she does not need to alienate. Rorty, for all his rhetorical pugnacity, is not an opponent of teaching children about the world; he is an opponent of teaching them to be ashamed of their country. These are different propositions, and a curriculum can easily be designed that does the first without doing the second. Nussbaum's own concessions—that special concern for one's own sphere is justifiable, that local identifications need not be given up, that education may "reflect those special concerns"—suggest she would agree. The fight over "first allegiance" is, I suspect, a fight more interesting to philosophers than to teachers.

Let us, then, take what is most valuable in Nussbaum's essay—its eloquent reminder that ignorance of the world is a moral as well as an intellectual failing—and put it to work where it can do good: in the design of curricula, in the training of teachers, in the standards adopted by states. The philosophical question can continue to be debated in journals where it belongs. Children, meanwhile, have a world to learn.

National honor is a dangerous phrase, but not always a bad one. It can mean swagger, but it can also mean shame at betrayal. That shame is patriotic as well as cosmopolitan: it says that this country, with its professed ideals, must not behave so.

Second, serious knowledge of other societies: without that, foreign policy becomes ignorance armed. Third, commitment to universal human rights: without that, national interest becomes an excuse for brutality.

Nussbaum fears that patriotism leads to idolatry. Sometimes it does. But cosmopolitan sentiment without institutional discipline can lead to posturing, overreach, or despair. The task is not to choose between country and world. It is to build citizens who understand that their country is one actor in a morally common world—and who are prepared to make that actor behave responsibly.

Nussbaum cannot learn this lesson because she has defined politics down to the point where it is merely a subset of moral reasoning.

The American commitment to equality—of the Declaration's proposition that all are created equal, of Reconstruction's unfinished promise—was articulated through patriotic appeals. Frederick Douglass did not invoke world citizenship to denounce slavery; he invoked the nation's own founding ideals against its actual practice. Lincoln's Gettysburg Address appealed not to humanity in general but to "the proposition that all men are created equal." This is patriotic rhetoric, and it did moral work that cosmopolitan detachment could never have accomplished.

But one can study foreign cultures without cosmopolitan ideology; most educated people do. The claim that one needs "world citizenship" to learn about families in other nations is simply false.

Second, she argues cosmopolitanism enables international cooperation on problems like ecology. But international cooperation does not require cosmopolitanism; it requires institutions, treaties, and the political will that national governments provide. The Montreal Protocol worked not because signatories adopted cosmopolitan consciousness but because nations found common interests. Nussbaum confuses the instrument with the ideology.

Third, she insists we recognize moral obligations to the rest of the world. True, but these obligations are better recognized through religious teaching, human rights law, and practical empathy than through cosmopolitan philosophy. Most people who care about global poverty care because of moral intuitions formed in their communities, not because they read Diogenes.

Americans who believe in universal human rights can already ground respect for foreigners in their own tradition. They do not need Stoic cosmopolitanism.

Patriotism creates communities of people willing to sacrifice for one another across lines of difference—willing to pay taxes, serve in wars, and share burdens that cosmopolitan reason alone cannot generate. A nation of world citizens, contemplating global humanity, might rationally agree that cooperation is desirable; they would not thereby be moved to act. Motivation requires the emotional density that only particular attachments can provide.

Nussbaum ends with the happy story of Crates and Hipparchia. But this is a love story between two philosophers, not a model for civic education. Nussbaum's engagement with the Stoic tradition is thoughtful, but philosophers will notice significant distortions in her reading. Understanding these misreadings matters because her entire argument depends on claiming the authority of ancient wisdom for a modern position that the Stoics would have found alien.

The central misreading concerns the relationship between cosmopolitanism and political engagement. Nussbaum presents Diogenes as a proto-cosmopolitan and then claims the Stoics developed his insight into a full philosophy of world citizenship. But this elides a crucial development. The Cynic Diogenes was an anti-political philosopher; his "citizen of the world" remark expressed contempt for all political communities, not engagement with them. The Stoics, by contrast, were deeply political philosophers who argued that the wise man should participate in political life for the benefit of the community. Seneca's "De Otio," from which Nussbaum quotes, is precisely about when the philosopher may withdraw from politics—not a manifesto for cosmopolitan detachment but a meditation on political withdrawal that even Seneca considered unusual.

Nussbaum quotes Hierocles on drawing circles toward the center, but she omits his full argument. Hierocles held that our obligations to nearer circles are stronger than to distant ones; we should love our immediate family more than strangers, our fellow citizens more than foreigners. The cosmopolitan aspiration was not to equalize all circles but to extend the warmth of familial feeling outward—never denying that family comes first but arguing that we should not be cold to those outside it. This is quite different from Nussbaum's implication that our "primary allegiance" should be to humanity.

The image of the body politic that Nussbaum finds in Marcus Aurelius is also more ambivalent than she acknowledges. Marcus used this image to think through imperial governance—how to treat conquered peoples, how to understand his role as emperor. He was not developing a democratic educational philosophy. The "limb" metaphor has authoritarian potential: just as the body does not deliberate with its hand, citizens in an empire may be called to obey. Nussbaum recognizes the "abuse" potential of the organic model but does not adequately reckon with how thoroughly the Stoic framework was shaped by autocracy.

Perhaps most significantly, Nussbaum claims that Stoic cosmopolitanism "is in this way the ancestor and source of Kant's idea of the 'kingdom of ends.'" This is historically questionable. Kant was influenced by Rousseau and by rationalist metaphysics more than by Stoicism. More importantly, Kant's kingdom of ends is a regulative ideal for rational agents who recognize each other as legislators of universal law—a deeply modern conception that owes more to Protestant conscience and Enlightenment rationalism than to Stoic nature philosophy.

What should we conclude from these misreadings? Not that Nussbaum's arguments are wrong, but that they stand on their own feet rather than on Stoic authority. Her case for global education, for international cooperation, for recognizing moral obligations to distant strangers—all of this can be argued on its merits without claiming ancient wisdom that the ancient philosophers would not have recognized. If anything, the Stoic emphasis on political participation and the priority of natural communities suggests a position closer to civic republicanism than to cosmopolitan detachment.

Nussbaum's essay offers a valuable corrective to unreflective patriotism, but I want to propose a more synthetic position that preserves what is valuable in both her cosmopolitanism and in legitimate forms of patriotic attachment.

The core of Nussbaum's argument is correct: national borders are morally arbitrary in important respects, and we have genuine obligations to distant strangers that cannot be captured by patriotism alone. The American living standard is not morally self-entiting; the ecological consequences of our choices affect people far beyond our borders; global cooperation on shared problems is urgent and unavoidable. On these points, Nussbaum is persuasive.

But her argument goes further, suggesting that patriotism itself is "morally dangerous" and that our "primary allegiance" should be to "the community of human beings in the entire world." This overstates the case. Patriotism, at its best, is not the worship of nation as god that Nussbaum rightly condemns; it is a particular form of moral commitment to a community of fate—people who share a territory, a history, and institutional structures for collective self-governance. Such commitment is not merely psychologically useful for motivation (as Rorty argues) but morally significant in its own right.

Consider why democratic government exists at all. The nation-state remains the primary locus of legitimate political authority and democratic accountability. Global governance, where it exists, is typically technocratic, distant from citizens, and undemocratic. If we cultivate a generation of students who identify primarily as world citizens, we may produce individuals who feel morally responsible for humanity in general but insufficiently attached to any particular polity to participate in its governance. The cure for jingoism is not rootless cosmopolitanism but patriotic participation in democratic deliberation—citizens who take pride in their nation's best ideals while subjecting its actual practices to scrutiny.

Nussbaum's four arguments for cosmopolitan education can be reframed as arguments for global literacy within a patriotic civic education. We learn about other cultures to understand ourselves better—not as world citizens but as Americans who need to know where we fit in the world. We pursue international cooperation through our national governments, which remain the primary vehicles for global engagement. We recognize obligations to others not as abstract cosmopolitan duties but as concrete commitments that our democracy can actually fulfill. We develop a coherent moral position by rooting universal principles in our own tradition while subjecting that tradition to critical examination.

The Tagore story that Nussbaum retells supports this more modest reading. Nikhil was right to resist Sandip's nationalism; he was wrong to think moral wisdom required detachment from political engagement. The tragedy was not that patriotism triumphed over cosmopolitanism but that both failed to generate effective political action. In our world, we need both: the moral clarity that cosmopolitan universalism provides and the political motivation that patriotic commitment generates. Education should cultivate both, not by choosing between them but by teaching citizens to hold them together.
