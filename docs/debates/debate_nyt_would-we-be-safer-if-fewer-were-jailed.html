Yes, and the evidence we already have in 2015 makes the case fairly strongly. The American jail population has roughly tripled since 1983, but that growth has not been driven by a surge in dangerous offenders. According to the Vera Institute's recent "Incarceration's Front Door" report, nearly three-quarters of people in local jails on any given day are charged with nonviolent offenses — traffic violations, drug possession, low-level property crimes — and the majority have not been convicted of anything. They are sitting in a cell because they cannot post bail, often for amounts under $2,500.

This is where reform can reduce numbers without raising risk. Money bail is a particularly poor sorting mechanism: it detains poor people who would show up to court and releases wealthier people who might not. Washington, D.C., which essentially abolished cash bail decades ago in favor of risk-based release, manages appearance rates above 85 percent while detaining far fewer pretrial defendants than comparable cities. Kentucky and New Jersey are moving in similar directions. Validated risk-assessment tools, supervised release, text-message court reminders, and day reporting all reduce failure-to-appear and rearrest rates at a fraction of the cost of detention — and at far less human cost, since even a few days in jail measurably increases the likelihood that a defendant will later be convicted, lose a job, or reoffend.

The second large category — people with serious mental illness — is even clearer. Rikers Island and the Cook County Jail are now, by default, the largest mental health facilities in their states. Jail is a terrible treatment setting and an expensive one. Cities that have invested in crisis intervention training, mental health courts, and diversion programs (Memphis, Miami-Dade, San Antonio) have reduced both jail admissions and police use of force without measurable increases in crime.

None of this means emptying the jails. Violent and high-risk defendants should still be detained, and reform must be paired with adequate community treatment capacity. But the current jail population reflects budgetary inertia and habit more than public-safety necessity. Smarter sorting would make us safer, not less safe.

The fundamental flaw in our debate over jail reform is the reflexive assumption that more cages equal more safety. The evidence, however, points in a starkly different direction. Our jails are packed not with master criminals but with the broken, the poor, and the mentally ill—people whose brief, brutal stays behind bars often transform them from manageable risks into future liabilities. We can absolutely reduce our jail populations without endangering society; in fact, a strategic reduction would likely make us safer.

Start with who is actually in jail. On any given day, roughly 60 percent of the people in America’s jails are not convicted of anything; they are legally innocent and merely too poor to post bail for often low-level, nonviolent offenses. For a person with a job, a family, and a stable address, even a few days in a cell can sever those vital ties, leading to job loss and housing instability—the very conditions that drive recidivism. Simultaneously, our jails have become de facto psychiatric wards. The three largest mental health providers in the nation are the Los Angeles County Jail, Rikers Island, and Cook County Jail. Locking up a person in psychotic crisis for stealing a sandwich doesn’t protect a neighborhood; it merely cycles a sick human being through a system utterly unequipped to heal them, all at a staggering public cost.

The criminogenic effect of short-term incarceration is well-documented by 2015. A study from the Arnold Foundation found that low-risk defendants held just 2-3 days before trial were significantly more likely to commit new crimes down the road than similar defendants released within 24 hours. The jail stay itself acts as a crime-school, packing nonviolent offenders alongside hardened detainees, while the stain of even a brief incarceration makes legal employment harder to find. We are literally manufacturing the risk we claim to be averting.

The fiscal argument underscores the folly. Housing an inmate in a New York City jail costs well over $100,000 annually per bed. Diverting that money toward supervised release programs, mental health courts, and community-based treatment yields far better public safety returns. Washington, D.C.’s pretrial services agency, for example, has demonstrated that rigorous, non-monetary release with reminders and check-ins ensures high court-appearance rates and low re-arrest rates without the destruction of detention. For the severely mentally ill, assertive community treatment and crisis intervention training for police keep fragile people stable and connected, preventing the kind of desperate, low-level crimes that so often lead to jail.

The false choice between liberty and safety ignores how jails often accelerate the very dysfunction they purport to contain. A reformed system—one that reserves a jail cell for the genuinely dangerous and violent while treating addiction, poverty, and illness as health issues rather than crimes—would save billions, preserve families, and ultimately produce fewer victims. That is the very definition of a safer society.

The current crisis of overcrowding and brutality in our nation’s jails—most visibly exposed by recent federal investigations into Rikers Island—forces us to confront a fundamental misconception: the belief that higher incarceration rates automatically equate to safer streets. In reality, significantly reducing our local jail populations, particularly among pretrial defendants and those with mental illness, would not only maintain public safety but actively enhance it.

When we incarcerate low-level, nonviolent defendants simply because they cannot afford a few hundred dollars for bail, we trigger a cascade of destabilizing events. A few days in jail frequently leads to lost employment, evicted families, and severed community ties. Criminological research demonstrates that this severe destabilization actually increases the likelihood of future criminal behavior. By keeping these individuals out of jail, we preserve the very community anchors that prevent crime.

Institutions like Rikers Island and Cook County Jail are filled with individuals suffering from severe mental illnesses and substance abuse disorders. Jails are structurally incapable of providing therapeutic care; instead, the traumatic, chaotic, and often violent environment of a cell block inevitably exacerbates mental health crises. When these individuals are eventually released, they return to society in a far more volatile state than when they entered. Subjecting someone arrested for a minor offense to an institution characterized by gang violence, extortion, and systemic brutality hardens them. Shrinking our jail populations is not a reckless gamble with public safety; it is a vital public safety strategy. Reformers bear the burden of demonstrating that alternatives work, and the data, while not definitive, suggests we are meeting that burden.

Los Angeles County Jail, Cook County Jail, and Rikers each house more people with serious mental illness than any hospital in the country. Correctional officers are not clinicians; cells are not treatment settings. We are paying premium prices to make sick people sicker, then releasing them with no continuity of care.

(where most defendants are released non-financially and appearance rates remain high) all target the low-risk population specifically. The goal isn't to empty jails indiscriminately; it's to stop using them as warehouses for poverty, addiction, and untreated illness.

Fewer jailed, more carefully chosen — yes, we would be safer. The evidence on this point is no longer seriously contested among researchers. What's contested is whether we have the political will to act on it.

The fashionable answer right now is that mass incarceration has gone too far, that we are jailing people who don't need to be jailed, and that decarceration would leave us no less safe — and possibly safer. There's truth in parts of this story, but the confident version being marketed by reformers deserves more skepticism than it has been getting.

We ran a massive natural experiment over thirty years: we locked up far more people, and crime fell to lows that earlier generations would have found astonishing. To now reverse course on the assumption that none of that mattered is a gamble, not a settled finding.

Yes, jails hold pretrial defendants, and yes, many of those defendants are accused of nonviolent crimes. But "nonviolent" is a slippery category. It includes serial burglars, drug dealers operating at scale, domestic abusers whose charges got pleaded down, and people with long records of failure to appear. Risk-assessment instruments help, but they are far from perfect, and their errors fall on victims, who are disproportionately poor and minority themselves.

The mental health argument is the strongest one for reform, and I largely accept it. Jails are terrible psychiatric facilities, and diverting seriously mentally ill defendants to treatment makes sense — provided the treatment infrastructure actually exists. The catch is that it usually doesn't. The deinstitutionalization movement of the 1960s and 70s was supposed to be paired with robust community mental health services. Those services never materialized at scale, which is precisely how jails became the default. Decarceration without that infrastructure just relocates the problem onto the streets and emergency rooms.

Rikers has real problems — brutality, mismanagement, lengthy pretrial detention — but those are management and bail-system failures, not arguments against jailing people generally. Fix bail. A sensible reform agenda is narrower than the current rhetoric: bail reform for genuinely low-risk defendants, mental-health diversion where treatment capacity exists, and faster case resolution. That agenda is defensible. The maximalist version — that decarceration is essentially a free lunch — is not. Crime victims have a stake in this debate too, and their interests rarely show up in the op-ed pages.

We should stop framing this debate as a tradeoff between safety and decarceration, because the framing concedes too much. The real question is what "safety" means and whom it is for.

For a middle-class commentator, safety usually means low probability of victimization by a stranger. Jail, on this view, is a tool for incapacitating threats. But for residents of the neighborhoods that produce most of our jail population, safety means something more textured: it includes not having your brother disappear into Rikers for six months over a subway turnstile jump, not losing your apartment because your mother was held on bail she couldn't pay, not watching your children grow up with a father shuttled in and out of custody for offenses that never quite rise to harm but never quite fall away either.

These are safety concerns. They just don't register in the conventional crime-policy vocabulary, which counts arrests and convictions but not the cascading harms of misdemeanor justice. When sociologists like Issa Kohler-Hausmann describe the lower courts as engines of "managerial" social control over poor communities of color, they are pointing at a system whose primary product isn't public safety but disciplined marginality. That product comes at enormous cost — fiscal, human, and democratic.

So would we be safer if fewer were jailed? It depends on whom you count. If you count only potential victims of stranger crime, the answer is uncertain and depends on which people you stop jailing. If you count the people churned through jails themselves, their families, and their neighborhoods, the answer is almost certainly yes — because the current scale of jailing is itself a source of insecurity for them.

This reframing has practical implications. It means we shouldn't evaluate bail reform purely on whether rearrest rates tick up or down a percentage point. We should also ask whether jobs are preserved, whether children stay housed, whether people with mental illness reach treatment, whether communities feel less occupied. A reform that produces a modest increase in some minor offenses but a major decrease in collateral devastation could still be a net safety gain — properly understood.

None of this means opening the gates. Violent and predatory offenders need to be incapacitated, and any serious reformer admits as much. But the population at issue here — pretrial nonviolent defendants, the mentally ill, people too poor to post small bail — is precisely the group for whom incarceration produces the least safety and the most harm. Releasing them isn't a concession to disorder. It's a recognition that our current definition of safety has been suspiciously selective about whose security counts.

Let me approach this as a fiscal question, because that's the angle most likely to produce durable reform across the political spectrum.

The United States spends roughly $80 billion a year on corrections. Local jails account for a substantial share of that, with New York City alone spending around $167,000 per inmate per year at Rikers — more than Harvard tuition, room, and board. For that price, taxpayers are buying outcomes that, on any reasonable cost-benefit accounting, are dismal. Recidivism rates hover around two-thirds within three years of release. Pretrial detention destabilizes employment and housing, increasing the likelihood of future criminal justice involvement. Mental illness goes untreated or actively worsens in custody.

If a private contractor delivered results this poor at this price, no serious government would renew the contract. Yet we keep funding the jail system at current levels largely out of inertia and political squeamishness about appearing soft.

A conservative case for reducing the jail population rests on three propositions. First, government should not spend money on programs that don't work, and the evidence that short-term jailing of low-risk defendants reduces crime is thin to nonexistent. Second, government should not expand its coercive footprint over citizens beyond what public safety strictly requires; pretrial detention of presumptively innocent people is among the most aggressive things government does. Third, family and work are the institutions that actually produce law-abiding behavior, and jail systematically disrupts both.

This is why states like Texas, Georgia, and South Carolina — hardly bastions of progressive criminology — have been quietly leading reform. They've expanded drug courts, mental-health diversion, and probation alternatives, and they've seen both prison populations and crime rates fall. The Right on Crime coalition has made these arguments effectively, and conservative governors have responded.

The jail-level version of this agenda includes risk-based pretrial release, eliminating cash bail for low-level offenses, mental health crisis diversion (the Memphis CIT model is a good template), and aggressive case processing to reduce the time defendants wait. None of these are radical proposals. They are basic management reforms applied to a system that has been running on autopilot.

Would we be safer? At minimum, we would be no less safe in any way the evidence can detect — and we would have billions of dollars freed up for things that actually prevent crime: better policing, treatment, education, and economic development in high-crime neighborhoods. Even if you are skeptical that decarceration itself improves safety, the opportunity cost of the status quo is enormous. That ought to be enough for any taxpayer to demand reform, regardless of their politics.

Domestic violence victims often want their abusers held — that's frequently the only window of safety they get. Crime victims in poor neighborhoods, who are disproportionately black and Hispanic, consistently tell pollsters they want more police presence and more accountability for repeat offenders, not less.

Reformers who treat it as a baseline guaranteed to continue regardless of policy choices may discover, painfully, that it wasn't.

Caution is not callousness. In criminal justice, the people who pay for policy errors are rarely the people who made them.

The question "would we be safer if fewer were jailed?" invites a yes-or-no answer that the underlying issue doesn't really support. The honest answer is: it depends entirely on which fewer, replaced by what.

Consider three different decarceration scenarios.

Scenario one: release pretrial detainees held on bail under $1,000 who have no violent priors. The research here is reasonably solid. Studies from the Vera Institute, the Laura and John Arnold Foundation, and others suggest these defendants pose modest flight and rearrest risks, and that pretrial detention itself increases their future criminality by disrupting employment and family ties. Net effect on safety: likely positive, or at minimum neutral, while saving substantial money.

Scenario two: divert seriously mentally ill defendants from jail to community treatment. The catch is everything. If "treatment" means a robust assertive community treatment team with medication management, supportive housing, and 24/7 crisis response, the evidence shows excellent outcomes — both for the patients and for public safety. If "treatment" means a referral to an underfunded community clinic with a six-week wait, the outcome will be revolving-door street homelessness and more crises. The reform is only as good as the infrastructure behind it.

Scenario three: significantly reduce sentences for drug offenses and other nonviolent felonies. Here the evidence is more contested. Some drug offenders are casual users caught up in aggressive enforcement; many others are deeply embedded in violent illicit markets. California's Proposition 47 just downgraded many such offenses in November; we don't yet know the effects. Caution is warranted.

What these scenarios share is the recognition that decarceration is not a single policy but a family of policies with very different risk-benefit profiles. The political conversation tends to collapse this distinction, with reformers presenting all reductions as obviously beneficial and opponents presenting all reductions as dangerous. Both are wrong.

A few principles should guide the work. First, individualized risk assessment beats categorical rules; some "nonviolent" defendants are quite dangerous, and some defendants with violent histories have aged out of risk. Second, decarceration without alternative infrastructure is just abandonment. Third, transparency and data matter — jurisdictions that pilot reforms should be required to track outcomes rigorously, including effects on victims. Fourth, the goal is right-sizing, not maximizing reduction for its own sake.

If reform is pursued thoughtfully, with attention to which populations to release and what to replace incarceration with, we can probably reduce jail populations significantly without compromising safety, and quite possibly improve it. If it is pursued sloppily, as a moral cause indifferent to operational detail, we will get bad outcomes and a political backlash that sets the cause back a generation. Reformers should choose carefully.
