The F.D.A.'s deeming rule is well-intentioned but poorly calibrated. By requiring every e-cigarette product on the market after February 2007 to undergo a costly premarket tobacco application — likely running into the hundreds of thousands of dollars per product — the agency has effectively guaranteed that the small and mid-sized vapor companies driving innovation will be wiped out. What will survive are the cigalike products owned by the major tobacco companies: the very products that ex-smokers have largely found inadequate as substitutes for combustible cigarettes.

This is a serious problem because the public health case for e-cigarettes, as the Royal College of Physicians recently concluded, rests on their being a meaningfully less harmful nicotine delivery system than burning tobacco. Smoke, not nicotine, is what kills smokers. If adult smokers lose access to the open-tank systems and varied flavors that surveys suggest help them switch, some will return to Marlboros.

That said, the alarm should be tempered. The rule does not ban e-cigarettes, and youth uptake — much of it by never-smokers — is a legitimate concern worth addressing through age verification and marketing restrictions. The right fix is legislative: move the predicate date forward so existing products aren't pulled, and scale review costs to risk. Otherwise the F.D.A. will have protected combustible cigarettes from their most promising competitor.

The FDA’s new e‑cigarette regulations, announced on May 5, 2016, extend its tobacco authority to virtually all vaping products — a step many public health advocates have long demanded. But by requiring manufacturers to submit costly premarket applications for every product introduced after February 2007, the rules risk dismantling a nascent harm‑reduction tool just as evidence for its usefulness in smoking cessation is solidifying. While the agency’s core aim — keeping e‑cigarettes out of adolescents’ hands — is irreproachable, the regulatory architecture it has chosen could make it harder, not easier, for adult smokers to quit combustibles.

The central tension lies in the premarket review process. Under the new rule, any e‑cigarette not marketed before the predicate date must undergo a burdensome application demonstrating that it is “appropriate for the protection of public health.” The cost, estimated in the millions per product, is prohibitive for the small and medium‑sized enterprises that drove the innovation behind today’s most popular vaping devices and nicotine e‑liquids. If those manufacturers exit the market rather than navigate the review, consumers will be left with a far narrower range of products — likely those owned by large tobacco conglomerates with the resources to comply. This consolidation could erode the very diversity of flavors and device types that many ex‑smokers credit for helping them switch. Research has consistently shown that smokers who quit via vaping frequently gravitate toward non‑tobacco flavors, which make the experience feel less like a cigarette and more like a distinct, appealing alternative. A less varied marketplace may dissuade the curious smoker from even trying.

The harm‑reduction argument is no longer fringe. In April 2016, the Royal College of Physicians released a landmark report concluding that e‑cigarettes are at least 95% less harmful than traditional smoking and that they can be an effective aid to cessation. Public Health England has been actively promoting the “smoke or vape, but not both” message. At a time when U.S. smoking rates have stalled around 15%, a tool that offers nicotine without the tar and carbon monoxide of combustion deserves a regulatory pathway that distinguishes between products that perpetuate addiction and kill users, and those that merely sustain addiction while dramatically reducing risk. But the FDA’s rule treats a vape pen and a pack of menthol cigarettes with nearly identical stringency under the premarket review, signaling that the agency sees the products as morally equivalent threats. This sends a confusing, potentially dangerous signal: that smokers have little to gain by switching.

No one disputes that youth uptake must be reversed. The monitoring data show e‑cigarette use among high‑schoolers tripled from 2013 to 2014, a trend that demands action. But the FDA’s ban on sales to minors and on vending machines, combined with mandated age verification, directly addresses that problem without extinguishing adult access. The heavier, innovation‑stifling component — retroactive premarket review — was a choice, not a necessity. And there is reason to worry it will backfire. If fewer e‑cigarette options are available, if prices rise because of compliance costs, and if the products are stripped of the features that make them attractive substitutes, then smokers who might otherwise quit will be pushed back toward combustible cigarettes or toward an unregulated black market.

The existing clinical evidence on e‑cigarettes and cessation is, admittedly, mixed. A 2016 Cochrane Review found moderate‑certainty evidence that vaping with nicotine helps smokers quit in the short term, but the long‑term data are still immature. In the absence of certainty, regulators must weigh the potential benefits — millions of smokers converting to a far less deadly form of nicotine delivery — against the precautionary fear that e‑cigarettes will act as a gateway. So far, the observational data do not bear out the gateway narrative for adults; instead, they suggest that e‑cigarettes are primarily used by current or former smokers. Over‑regulating on the basis of an unproven risk while undermining a proven harm‑reduction pathway is a high‑stakes gamble.

Ultimately, the FDA’s rule risks sacrificing the tool’s smoking‑cessation potential on the altar of pre‑market purity. Without room for the regulatory agility that would allow newer, safer products to reach smokers, the very population that bears the heaviest burden of tobacco‑related disease could lose a lifeline. Protecting kids is essential; making it harder for addicted adults to stop breathing smoke is not a price we should have to pay.

The Food and Drug Administration’s newly finalized “deeming” regulations on electronic cigarettes represent a well-intentioned effort to protect adolescents from nicotine addiction. However, by imposing an incredibly burdensome pre-market approval process on all vapor products, the F.D.A. risks dealing a severe blow to the most effective harm-reduction tool currently available to adult smokers. Ultimately, these rules may lead to more smoking-related deaths, not fewer.

To understand the danger of the F.D.A.’s approach, we must look at how e-cigarettes are viewed across the Atlantic. Just last month, the prestigious Royal College of Physicians released a comprehensive report concluding that e-cigarettes are likely to be at least 95 percent less harmful than traditional combustible cigarettes. The British medical establishment actively encourages smokers to switch to vaping, recognizing it as a massive public health opportunity to transition users away from deadly tar and carbon monoxide. The F.D.A., in contrast, is treating e-cigarettes with the same heavy-handedness as the lethal combustible products they replace.

The core issue is not the ban on sales to minors or the requirement for warning labels—measures the vaping industry broadly supports. The genuine threat to public health lies in the retroactive Pre-Market Tobacco Applications (PMTAs) required for nearly every vaping product introduced after February 2007. Completing a PMTA is a massive undertaking, estimated to cost hundreds of thousands, if not millions, of dollars per product, including every variation of flavor and nicotine strength.

Small and medium-sized businesses, which drive the innovation in open-tank systems that many heavy smokers rely on to gradually reduce their nicotine intake, simply cannot afford this regulatory hurdle. This financial barrier will effectively decimate the independent vaping industry. The market will quickly consolidate into the hands of traditional Big Tobacco companies, which possess the capital to navigate the F.D.A. bureaucracy and will likely dominate the shelves with less effective, closed-system “cigalikes.”

If the diverse, evolving marketplace of vapor products is essentially outlawed, the consequences are highly predictable. Faced with limited, expensive, and less satisfying vaping options, many adult ex-smokers will inevitably return to easily accessible combustible cigarettes. We must protect youth from nicotine, but the F.D.A. must find a regulatory middle ground that achieves this without sacrificing the millions of adult smokers who are successfully using vapor products to save their own lives.

The F.D.A.’s decision will help anti-smoking efforts in one important respect and hurt them in another. Keeping e-cigarettes out of the hands of minors is plainly legitimate. Youth use has risen sharply, nicotine is not harmless for adolescents, and a market that can sell candy-flavored nicotine with little oversight invites abuse. Age verification, child-resistant packaging, ingredient disclosure, manufacturing standards and penalties for false claims are sensible public-health measures.

But the central question is not whether e-cigarettes should be regulated. It is whether they should be regulated as though they pose the same public-health problem as combustible cigarettes. They do not. The overwhelming danger from smoking comes from inhaling burned tobacco: tar, carbon monoxide and thousands of combustion products. E-cigarettes deliver nicotine without burning tobacco. They are not risk-free, and long-term data are still limited, but the available evidence strongly suggests they are far less dangerous than cigarettes. That is why the Royal College of Physicians has urged that smokers who cannot or will not quit nicotine should be encouraged to switch to lower-risk products.

The F.D.A.’s rules risk getting this balance wrong. Because the regulations treat nearly all current vapor products as “new” tobacco products, manufacturers may have to go through an expensive premarket approval process for products already being used by smokers. Large tobacco companies may be able to absorb that burden; small vapor businesses often cannot. The likely result is fewer products, less innovation and less competition. That matters because smokers vary: some quit with patches or gum, some with medication, some by stopping abruptly, and some by experimenting with devices, nicotine strengths and flavors until cigarettes become less appealing.

The evidence that e-cigarettes help people quit is not yet definitive. Randomized trials have been small, and observational studies are mixed, partly because many smokers try e-cigarettes only after failing with other methods. Still, millions report using them to reduce or stop smoking, and Britain’s more permissive harm-reduction approach deserves attention. If regulation makes e-cigarettes harder to obtain, less satisfying or more expensive than cigarettes, some smokers will simply keep smoking.

A better policy would regulate according to risk: strict rules against youth sales and deceptive marketing, product safety standards, accurate nicotine labeling, surveillance of health effects, and permission for truthful comparative-risk claims. Anti-smoking policy should not protect cigarettes from their most promising competitors. The goal should be fewer smokers, not fewer nicotine users at any cost.

The FDA's New Rules Will Cost Lives

The FDA's "deeming" rule is a public health disaster dressed up as consumer protection. By requiring every e-cigarette product introduced after February 15, 2007 to go through a Premarket Tobacco Application process — at an estimated cost of hundreds of thousands to over a million dollars per product — the agency has effectively guaranteed that the small and mid-sized vapor companies that drive most innovation in this space will be wiped out. The survivors will be the cigarette companies, who can afford the paperwork and who have every incentive to keep their combustible products dominant.

This matters because the evidence increasingly suggests that e-cigarettes are dramatically less harmful than smoked tobacco. Public Health England last year concluded that vaping is around 95 percent safer than smoking, and the Royal College of Physicians has just reached a similar conclusion, explicitly endorsing e-cigarettes as a harm-reduction tool. Smokers who switch completely escape the combustion byproducts — tar, carbon monoxide, the cocktail of carcinogens — that actually kill the roughly 480,000 Americans who die annually from tobacco-related disease.

There is no comparable mortality threat from nicotine itself, and no plausible scenario in which kids vaping at current rates produces anything like that toll. Yet the FDA has decided to treat a product that helps smokers quit as if it were as dangerous as the product killing them. The predictable result: fewer choices, higher prices, less innovation, more smokers staying with Marlboros because the alternative just got harder to find.

If the FDA were serious about reducing smoking deaths, it would have created a streamlined approval pathway for vapor products, regulated marketing to minors aggressively, and set sensible manufacturing standards — without imposing a regulatory regime that only Big Tobacco can survive. Instead, it has handed the cigarette industry a gift. Some smokers will still quit; many who would have switched to vaping will not. That is a measurable cost in human lives, and the agency owns it.

Regulation Is Overdue, and Quit Rates Will Be Fine

The panic over the FDA's new e-cigarette rules misreads both the regulations and the evidence. For nearly a decade, vapor products have been sold in the United States with essentially no federal oversight of their ingredients, manufacturing quality, or marketing. The result has been a Wild West market in which products of wildly varying quality have been pitched, often with cartoon-friendly flavors and slick social media campaigns, to anyone with a credit card — including, surveys consistently show, a rapidly growing number of teenagers. The CDC reported that e-cigarette use among high school students more than tripled between 2013 and 2014. That is not a trend a serious public health agency can ignore.

The FDA's rule does not ban e-cigarettes. It requires age verification, prohibits sales to minors, mandates ingredient disclosure, and brings new products through a premarket review. These are the kinds of basic standards we already apply to virtually every other consumable product on the market. The claim that ordinary product regulation will somehow prevent smokers from quitting deserves skepticism.

It is also worth scrutinizing the cessation narrative itself. The evidence that e-cigarettes help people quit is suggestive but mixed. Several observational studies have actually associated e-cigarette use with lower quit rates among smokers, and randomized trial evidence remains thin. The Royal College of Physicians' optimism is not universally shared — the World Health Organization and the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force have been considerably more cautious, declining to recommend e-cigarettes as a cessation aid.

Smokers who genuinely want to quit will continue to have FDA-approved options: patches, gum, lozenges, varenicline, bupropion, counseling, and yes, e-cigarettes themselves, which will remain on shelves. What they will no longer have is an unregulated market in which a teenager can order a mango-flavored device online with no questions asked. That trade-off looks reasonable, not catastrophic.

Public health policy should not be held hostage to the worst-case projections of an industry that, for years, has resisted any oversight at all. Regulation and harm reduction are not opposites; competent regulation is the precondition for harm reduction we can trust.

Right Goal, Wrong Instrument

There is a sensible regulatory regime for e-cigarettes, and there is the one the FDA just issued. They are not the same.

Almost everyone outside the vapor industry agrees that some rules are necessary. Restricting sales to minors, requiring ingredient disclosures, setting manufacturing standards, and curbing marketing aimed at youth are all defensible and overdue. Teen vaping rates have climbed sharply, and the long-term effects of inhaling flavored aerosols are genuinely unknown. A regulator that did nothing would be failing its mandate.

But the specific architecture the FDA has chosen — applying the full Premarket Tobacco Application framework to virtually every existing vapor product — is poorly matched to the public health goal. The grandfather date of February 15, 2007 predates the existence of nearly every e-cigarette currently on the market. That means essentially the entire industry must now file expensive applications to keep selling products they have already been selling for years. Industry estimates of per-product compliance costs run into six and seven figures. Whatever one thinks of those estimates, the direction is clear: small manufacturers will exit, product variety will collapse, and the surviving players will disproportionately be the large tobacco companies that own the leading "cigalike" brands.

That is a strange outcome for a rule meant to protect public health. If e-cigarettes are even modestly effective at displacing combustible tobacco — and the Royal College of Physicians' recent report suggests they are substantially so — then policies that reduce vaping options without reducing cigarette options will at the margin push some smokers back toward, or keep them on, the deadlier product.

A better approach would have been to move the predicate date forward, to create a proportional review pathway for lower-risk products, and to pair these with strict youth-access and marketing restrictions. The European Union's Tobacco Products Directive, whatever its flaws, at least tries to draw these distinctions.

The FDA's instinct to regulate is right. Its execution risks reproducing the worst feature of American tobacco policy: protecting incumbents while squeezing out the alternatives that might actually save lives. Congress or the courts may yet force a course correction. They should.

Don't Confuse a Marketing Story With a Cessation Strategy

Lost in the outcry over the FDA's new rules is a basic question that deserves more skepticism than it has received: are e-cigarettes actually helping Americans quit smoking? The assumption underlying much of the debate — that any regulation of vapor products is a blow to smoking cessation — treats a contested empirical claim as if it were established fact.

It isn't. The Royal College of Physicians' recent endorsement is notable, but it is hardly the consensus. A 2016 meta-analysis in The Lancet Respiratory Medicine found that e-cigarette users were, on average, less likely to quit smoking than non-users. The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force has explicitly declined to recommend vaping as a cessation tool, citing insufficient evidence. Most American vapers are "dual users" who continue to smoke combustible cigarettes alongside their e-cigs — which is not what cessation looks like.

Meanwhile, the harms are not zero and not fully characterized. Nicotine itself is a potent addictive substance with cardiovascular effects, and there is growing evidence that adolescent nicotine exposure affects brain development. Teen vaping rates have exploded, and a meaningful share of those teens were not previously smokers. If e-cigarettes are recruiting a new generation into nicotine addiction while only marginally helping current smokers quit, the public health math looks very different from the harm-reduction narrative.

In that context, the FDA's regulations are not an attack on quitters. They are a belated effort to apply ordinary scrutiny to a product category whose health claims have outrun the evidence. Genuine smoking cessation products — nicotine replacement therapy, varenicline, behavioral counseling — have been through rigorous trials and remain available. Smokers who want to quit have options that don't depend on the marketing assurances of a young and self-interested industry.

We may eventually conclude that e-cigarettes belong in the cessation toolkit. But that conclusion should be earned through evidence, not assumed as a premise that makes any regulation seem like sabotage.

The question of whether the FDA’s e-cigarette regulations will hurt smoking cessation has no simple yes or no answer. The final verdict depends almost entirely on how the agency balances two legitimate but competing public health goals: preventing youth nicotine addiction and offering adult smokers a less deadly alternative.

The parts of the deeming rule that directly target young people — the nationwide ban on sales to minors, mandatory age verification for online purchases, and restrictions on free samples — are overdue and should strengthen anti-smoking efforts over the long term. The danger lies in the premarket review requirements. If the agency uses its new power to define a distinct, reasonable route for reduced-risk products, regulation could actually boost cessation by creating a trusted, standardized category of alternatives. The real story will be written not in the press releases, but in the detailed guidances and enforcement policies that follow. Whether the FDA’s decision helps or harms cessation is an open question — one that depends on a regulatory wisdom that has yet to be demonstrated.
