Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Gilens has assembled an empirical case that is hard to dismiss: across decades and thousands of survey items, the preferences of low- and middle-income Americans bear little independent relationship to what government actually does. The finding deserves to unsettle anyone who takes the small-d democratic premise seriously. But I want to press on three points where the evidence, as Gilens presents it, may be doing less work than the conclusions suggest.

First, there is a measurement problem lurking in the gap between the 90th percentile and the truly rich. Gilens admits this himself: when affluent and middle-class preferences diverge, we cannot tell whether the operative actor is the dentist earning $150,000 or the hedge-fund manager earning $50 million. This matters enormously for diagnosis. If the merely affluent are setting policy, then expanded turnout and small-donor campaigns might genuinely move the needle. If the top 0.1 percent is doing the work — through campaign finance, lobbying outlays, and the revolving door — then the reforms Gilens gestures toward (nonpartisan districting, GOTV drives) are unlikely to be adequate. Page and Bartels's forthcoming work on top-1-percent preferences should be treated as a precondition for prescription, not a footnote to it.

Second, congruence between affluent preferences and policy outcomes does not by itself establish causation. The affluent are, on average, better informed about policy detail and more attentive to elite discourse. If both policymakers and affluent citizens are responding to a shared informational environment — economists' near-consensus on trade liberalization through the 1990s, for instance — then we might be observing parallel updating rather than disproportionate influence. Gilens's protectionism example illustrates the difficulty: poor and middle-class opposition to NAFTA may reflect rational economic interest, but it may also reflect less exposure to the arguments that persuaded experts. I don't think this fully rescues the system from the charge of plutocracy, but it does complicate the inferential leap from correlation to influence.

Third, Gilens's own data contain a result worth dwelling on: under unified Democratic control in the Johnson years, policy tracked no one's preferences — not the poor's, not the affluent's. This suggests that the binding constraint on responsiveness is sometimes simply that elected officials pursue their own programmatic visions when given the chance. That is not necessarily a failure of democracy; it is what representative government, as distinct from plebiscitary government, is partly designed to do. The normative weight of "responsiveness" depends on whether we think representation should mirror current opinion or shape it.

None of this dissolves Gilens's central worry. But the path from his findings to reform proposals runs through questions his data cannot yet answer.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Martin Gilens offers a sobering empirical portrait of American democracy. By tracking the match between policy outcomes and the preferences of different income groups, he shows that the affluent have substantial influence while the poor and middle class have virtually none when their views diverge from the rich. The finding that electoral competition modestly reduces this inequality is a glimmer of hope, but the overall diagnosis is damning. Yet for all the power of this evidence, we should hesitate before concluding that money directly purchases policy. Gilens’s measure—correlation between survey responses and subsequent policy change—may confound elite influence with elite alignment with technocratic consensus. Free trade, for example, was embraced by economic experts across the ideological spectrum for decades; its adoption despite broad public opposition might reflect deference to perceived expertise rather than a simple triumph of wealth. This alternative does not exonerate the system—policy should be democratically responsive—but it complicates the causal story.

More troubling is Gilens’s reliance on political competition as the primary remedy. His historical analysis shows that closely divided government forces parties to court the median voter, boosting responsiveness. But the logic assumes that parties are still capable of such pivots. In our current era of deep partisan polarization, divided government often yields gridlock that punishes the most vulnerable. The debt-ceiling standoff of 2011, for instance, imposed austerity that a majority of Americans, and especially lower-income groups, opposed. When competition becomes synonymous with obstruction, the filtering effect Gilens describes—blocking unpopular policies—can also prevent urgently needed action, from extending unemployment benefits to investing in infrastructure. Competition may not save us if it is weaponized by an anti-government faction.

Gilens’s pragmatic suggestion—to prioritize policies with cross-class support—carries its own dangers. He notes that majorities of all income levels favor minimum-wage hikes, education spending, and Social Security. While such consensus-building is politically shrewd, it implicitly accepts the veto power of the affluent over any policy they do oppose. Genuinely equal representation would mean that a majority-supported increase in top marginal tax rates or a robust financial transactions tax could become law even if the rich objected. By ceding the agenda to those issues the wealthy already accept, we risk confining the ambitions of the non-rich to a narrow, affluent-approved strip of possibilities. Structural economic reforms—the kind that might genuinely reduce inequality—often require confronting elite interests head-on, not just finding common ground.

Finally, the reform levers Gilens identifies—campaign finance changes, nonpartisan districting—are themselves subject to the very dynamics he describes. Citizens United and the subsequent explosion of super PAC spending show how concentrated wealth shapes the legal rules of the game. Meaningful campaign finance reform would require overcoming that same elite influence. The protests in Wisconsin in 2011, where mass mobilization temporarily countered corporate-backed austerity, hint at a different path: political power may also respond to large-scale collective action that disrupts business as usual. If the affluent dominate through donations, then expanding the organizational capacity of ordinary people—through unions, community groups, and social movements—might offer a counterweight that procedural tweaks alone cannot. Gilens rightly insists that our political destiny is not predetermined. But the road to a more equal democracy probably runs not through elite accommodation but through a revival of contentious, broad-based counter-power.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Martin Gilens provides a sobering, empirical confirmation of a long-held democratic anxiety: when the policy preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the poor and middle class, the affluent win. His data convincingly maps the scope of this representational inequality. However, by pointing to campaign donations as the primary mechanism for this disparity, Gilens relies on a diagnosis that underestimates the deeper structural and institutional advantages enjoyed by the top ten percent.

Campaign contributions undoubtedly matter, but they are only the most visible currency of influence. The affluent dominate not just because they fund campaigns, but because they possess disproportionate social and cultural capital. Lawmakers, their staffs, and the affluent live in overlapping networks; they share educational backgrounds, professional assumptions, and an ideological vocabulary. Even if the Supreme Court were to reverse *Citizens United* tomorrow and campaign spending were magically equalized, the affluent would still maintain privileged access to the daily, unseen machinery of government.

This structural advantage extends to Gilens’s dismissal of interest groups. He notes that organizations like the AARP and AFL-CIO frequently align with middle- or low-income preferences. But influence in Washington is rarely decided by broad policy alignments alone; it is won in the technical minutiae of legislation and regulation. During the recent debates over the Affordable Care Act and the Dodd-Frank financial reforms, corporate and affluent-aligned interest groups did not merely lobby—they provided the highly technical expertise required to draft the rules. This intellectual capture ensures that even when a popular reform is passed, its implementation is routinely tailored to elite interests. The poor do not have think tanks or corporate lawyers translating their broad polling preferences into statutory language.

Furthermore, Gilens’s suggestion that political gridlock serves as a filter that protects the middle class by thwarting unpopular policies is deeply troubling. In our current era of intense polarization and divided government, gridlock fundamentally preserves the status quo. When the status quo already features widening income disparities, a weakened labor movement, and systemic economic vulnerabilities, legislative paralysis inherently benefits the privileged. The affluent can afford a government that does nothing; the poor and middle class rely on government action for economic mobility and protection. Treating gridlock as a democratic safeguard ignores the fact that inaction is often a policy choice that favors the powerful.

Gilens has delivered an invaluable, data-driven indictment of American democracy. Yet, if we are to take his findings seriously, we must recognize that campaign finance reform and competitive elections are insufficient cures. Rebalancing influence requires rebuilding the civic institutions—particularly organized labor—that can systematically translate middle-class preferences into persistent legislative pressure, ensuring the public is heard not just on Election Day, but every day.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Martin Gilens’s evidence should unsettle anyone who takes political equality seriously. If the preferences of citizens at the bottom and middle of the income distribution predict federal policy only when they coincide with the preferences of the affluent, then the formal equality of one person, one vote is doing far less democratic work than we like to imagine. The finding is especially important because it cuts through the comforting thought that the problem is simply Republican government, or conservative ideology, or a few bad Supreme Court decisions. The pattern is broader: money, attention, access, and organization tilt the whole system.

Still, I would put somewhat more weight than Gilens does on the institutions that translate preferences into power. The poor are not merely “ignored” as isolated individuals; they are weakly organized as a class. Mid-twentieth-century American politics gave working- and lower-middle-class citizens influence not only because they voted, but because unions, urban machines, civil-rights organizations, churches, and mass-membership associations could bargain, threaten, educate, and mobilize. The decline of private-sector unionism, the weakening of local parties, and the professionalization of advocacy have left many citizens represented, if at all, by organizations speaking for them rather than organizations composed of them.

This matters because public opinion is not a fixed input that government either obeys or disobeys. Citizens often hold broad values rather than detailed policy views, and elites help define which policies seem connected to those values. The 2001 and 2003 tax cuts, for example, may have had majority support across income groups, but that support depended on how the issue was framed: as tax relief, as stimulus, as fairness, or as a distributional choice favoring high earners. Likewise, hostility to “welfare” can coexist with support for Social Security, Medicare, unemployment insurance, and aid to education. Unequal influence operates not only after preferences are measured; it operates before then, in agenda-setting, framing, and the production of political common sense.

That qualification does not weaken Gilens’s case. It strengthens it. If affluent Americans and business interests have disproportionate capacity to shape the agenda, fund campaigns, subsidize think tanks, lobby continuously, and define what counts as “serious” policy, then unequal responsiveness measured at the end of the process understates the democratic problem. By the time a proposal appears in a survey, many alternatives have already been excluded.

Nor should we be too reassured by political competition. Close elections can make politicians more attentive to public opinion, but they can also make them more dependent on donors, consultants, and negative advertising. Competition in a low-turnout, high-money system may sharpen responsiveness to swing voters while leaving nonvoters and safe-district residents invisible. Gridlock may block unpopular overreach, but it also protects the status quo—a status quo already shaped by prior inequalities.

The most promising remedy is therefore not simply cleaner campaign finance, though public financing and disclosure are essential. It is rebuilding countervailing power. Easier voter registration, protection against voter suppression, competitive districts, small-donor matching systems, and stronger labor rights all belong together. So do institutions that help ordinary citizens deliberate and act collectively rather than merely answer poll questions.

Gilens is right that American democracy has been worse and has improved. But improvement has rarely come from benign competition alone. It has come when excluded citizens organized enough power that politicians could no longer afford to ignore them.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Martin Gilens presents a sobering portrait of American democracy, but I think his analysis, while compelling, understates both the depth of the problem and the complexity of its solutions. His core finding—that policy responsiveness tracks income rather than population—is well-supported and significant. Yet the normative weight he assigns to "representational equality" deserves more scrutiny than his essay provides.

Gilens is careful to note that perfect responsiveness might not be desirable; majorities can be shortsighted, and minority rights matter. But he doesn't fully grapple with what this concession implies. If we accept that policymakers should sometimes resist public preferences, we immediately face the question of who should exercise that resisting judgment. Gilens assumes that deviations from majority will should reflect something like expert wisdom or protecting minority interests. But his own data suggests that deviations instead reflect the preferences of the affluent—which may be no more legitimate a guide than majoritarian caprice.

The implication is troubling: Gilens documents a system where the wealthy effectively veto popular preferences, yet his proposed remedies—campaign finance reform, enhanced competition—don't address the deeper problem. Even if we achieved more competitive elections, why would politicians court low-income voters whose turnout remains low and whose donations remain negligible? The competitive pressure Gilens identifies operates primarily by making politicians more attentive to the median voter or the swing voter, not by empowering those with the lowest rates of political participation.

This points to a deeper issue Gilens mentions only in passing: the cultural and civic dimensions of political inequality. Voting rates among the poor have declined substantially since the 1960s, a trend Gilens doesn't examine in detail. Bridging representational inequality requires not just structural reforms but addressing the alienation and disengagement that leave low-income citizens on the sidelines of political life.

Finally, Gilens acknowledges that the affluent don't always push policy in a conservative direction—on moral issues, wealthier Americans tend to be more liberal. This point deserves more emphasis. It suggests that income-based inequality in influence doesn't map neatly onto ideological conflict, and that the stakes of representational inequality are more complex than a simple story of rich versus poor.

Gilens is right that American democracy falls short of its ideals. But diagnosing the problem is easier than prescribing lasting remedies, and the structural sources of representational inequality are more deeply embedded than his analysis acknowledges.

**The Measurement Problem at the Heart of Gilens's Argument**

Martin Gilens has assembled an impressive dataset and his core finding—that policy outcomes correlate more tightly with affluent preferences than with the preferences of the poor—deserves serious engagement. But before we accept the dramatic conclusion that American democracy is failing the non-affluent, we should think harder about what his correlations can and cannot tell us.

Consider what Gilens's methodology actually measures. He takes survey questions about proposed policy changes, sorts respondents by income, and asks whether policy ended up matching the preferences of each group. When the affluent want X and the poor want Y, and X happens, the affluent are "influential." But correlation is not causation, and there's a troubling alternative explanation for many of his findings: on issues where preferences diverge, the affluent tend to be better informed about the technical content and likely consequences of the policies in question.

Take trade policy, one of Gilens's signature examples. Yes, lower-income Americans have consistently opposed NAFTA-style agreements while affluent Americans have supported them. But virtually every academic economist—across the ideological spectrum—agrees that liberalized trade produces aggregate welfare gains. If policymakers follow expert consensus rather than diffuse public opinion on technical matters, the resulting policy will correlate with affluent preferences simply because the affluent are more likely to hold views consistent with that expert consensus. The same pattern likely applies to immigration reform (which Gilens notes was opposed by 90 percent of the poor in 1965) and to deregulation in particular industries where the case for reform rested on technical economic arguments.

This is not to dismiss the inequality concern. It is to say that Gilens's correlations are consistent with at least three stories: (1) the affluent buy disproportionate influence through donations and access; (2) the affluent happen to have better-informed views that policymakers reasonably weight more heavily; (3) the affluent's views proxy for the views of expert and elite communities (journalists, economists, civil servants) that genuinely do shape policy. These mechanisms have very different normative implications, and Gilens's data cannot distinguish among them.

Gilens himself acknowledges some of this when he notes that interest groups don't cleanly track the affluent-poor divide—the AARP and AFL-CIO are powerful, and industry groups often align with the poor on spending issues. This is a major concession. If organized interests, which actually do the lobbying and the campaign contributing, don't map onto the income gradient, then the "money buys influence" story he gestures toward in his conclusion becomes harder to sustain. He also concedes that wealthy members of Congress don't vote differently from less wealthy ones, knocking out another obvious mechanism.

What's left? Gilens points to campaign contributions as the most plausible channel. But the empirical literature on campaign finance effects is famously inconclusive. Ansolabehere, de Figueiredo, and Snyder's much-cited piece "Why Is There So Little Money in U.S. Politics?" found that contributions are better understood as consumption than investment, and that the actual policy returns to contributions are surprisingly hard to detect. If money were as decisive as the Gilens narrative implies, we'd expect much more of it sloshing through the system than we actually observe.

There's also a selection problem in the policy questions themselves. Survey researchers don't ask about every conceivable policy; they ask about salient ones, often ones that elites are already debating. This means the universe of "proposed policy changes" Gilens studies is filtered through an elite agenda-setting process. The policies poor Americans might most want—say, a federal jobs guarantee, or aggressive industrial policy—may never get asked about because they're not on the table.

None of this means we should be complacent about inequalities of political voice. Voter turnout differences by income are real and troubling; the disproportionate role of large donors is troubling; the post–Citizens United landscape is troubling. But the leap from "policy correlates better with affluent preferences" to "the affluent dominate American democracy" requires causal claims that Gilens's data cannot quite bear. A more modest framing would be: on contested issues where preferences diverge by income, the policy outcomes we observe are more consistent with affluent than with poor preferences, for reasons that include—but are not limited to—differential political influence. That's still worth worrying about. But it's a more honest summary of what the evidence shows.

**Gilens Understates His Own Case**

Reading Martin Gilens's careful, measured essay, one is struck by how restrained his conclusions are given the data he has assembled. He documents that across four decades of policymaking, the preferences of poor and middle-class Americans have had essentially zero independent effect on what their government does. None. Zero. And yet he concludes with hopeful gestures toward campaign finance reform, nonpartisan redistricting, and identifying issues where cross-class coalitions might work. This is like discovering the foundations of your house are rotted through and recommending fresh paint.

If Gilens's findings are correct—and I think his methodology is sound—then the implications are far more radical than he is willing to state. The United States does not have a democracy in any meaningful sense of that word. It has an electoral oligarchy in which competing factions of the affluent contest for control while the rest of the population is offered ritualized participation that does not translate into representation. The procedural trappings—elections, parties, free press—are real, but their substantive output bears no relationship to majority preference except where majority preference happens to coincide with elite preference.

Consider what Gilens shows about the Democratic Party. The party traditionally understood as the vehicle for working-class interests has, over the past several decades, "shifted strongly in a free-market, anti-regulation, free-trade direction—a shift more consistent with the preferences of the affluent than of the less well off." Under Clinton, the Democrats delivered NAFTA, welfare reform, financial deregulation, and the repeal of Glass-Steagall. Under Obama, after running on a platform of pushing back against Wall Street excess, the administration delivered TARP without serious conditions, prosecuted no major banking executives for the conduct that produced the 2008 crisis, and passed a health care reform built around private insurers rather than the public option majorities of voters supported. The pattern is consistent: when the affluent and the rest disagree, the affluent win, regardless of which party holds power.

Gilens's "bright spot"—that political competition increases responsiveness—deserves harder scrutiny. He acknowledges that even under maximally favorable conditions, "competition is rarely strong enough to create anything remotely resembling equal representation." So the best the system can offer is somewhat-less-extreme inequality of voice. This is grading on a curve so steep it's nearly vertical.

I want to push particularly hard on his treatment of campaign finance. Gilens treats Citizens United as an unfortunate setback that future courts might reverse. But Citizens United is not an aberration; it is the logical culmination of a constitutional framework that has, since Buckley v. Valeo in 1976, treated political spending as protected speech. Within that framework, modest reforms—matching funds, contribution limits, disclosure requirements—are perpetually vulnerable to being eroded by the next inventive workaround. Super PACs have made nominal contribution limits nearly meaningless within two years of being authorized. The plutocratization of American politics is not a problem that can be solved by tweaking the existing system; it requires structural change of a kind that the existing system makes nearly impossible to enact, because those who would lose power under reform are precisely those whose support reform requires.

Gilens also underplays the international comparison. Other wealthy democracies have not seen the same explosion of inequality the United States has, and they have not seen the same hollowing out of policy responsiveness. The contrast suggests that the American pattern reflects specific institutional choices—weak parties, candidate-centered elections, privately financed campaigns, a constitutional structure that requires extraordinary supermajorities for change—rather than some universal logic of capitalism or globalization. Gilens treats American institutions as the given background; they are in fact the foreground problem.

What would a serious response to Gilens's findings look like? It would include public financing of elections that genuinely crowds out private money, not as a supplement to it. It would include rebuilding the institutional infrastructure—unions, mass-membership organizations, local party structures—that historically gave non-affluent citizens organized voice. It would include constitutional remedies to Buckley and Citizens United. It would include automatic voter registration and mandatory voting on the Australian model, since Gilens identifies turnout differentials as one channel of influence inequality.

Gilens has produced what should be a five-alarm fire of a finding. He himself rings the bell too softly. The data say American democracy has failed; the question is whether we can build something that deserves the name.

**What Gilens Misses About Majoritarianism**

Martin Gilens's research is valuable, and his evidence that policy tracks affluent preferences more closely than poor preferences is hard to dismiss. But Gilens frames his findings within a particular theory of democracy—a roughly majoritarian one in which the legitimacy of policy turns on its correspondence to citizen preferences—and that framing obscures as much as it reveals. Before we lament representational inequality, we should ask whether equal representation of preferences is actually what democratic theory recommends or what American institutions are designed to produce.

Start with what Gilens's own data show but he passes over quickly. Many of the policy reversals that occurred during the period he studies were not popular at the time but are now broadly accepted: civil rights legislation, immigration reform, the loosening of restrictions on stem cell research, expanded protections against discrimination. Gilens notes that the 1965 immigration reform was opposed by 90 percent of poor Americans and three-quarters of middle-class and affluent ones. By his criterion, this was a profound failure of democratic responsiveness. By almost any other criterion, it was one of the great achievements of postwar American governance. The same is true of a great deal of civil rights policy, which moved ahead of public opinion and helped pull opinion forward.

This is the classic Madisonian observation: pure responsiveness to popular preference is not a virtue but a danger. The American constitutional order was deliberately designed to filter, slow, and sometimes frustrate majority will, particularly when that will threatens minority rights or reflects passion rather than considered judgment. Gilens nods to this concern in a paragraph near the end ("minority rights are important too, and majorities are sometimes shortsighted or misguided") but does not let it disturb his framework. It should disturb it more.

There is also a problem with treating survey responses as the gold standard of "what the public wants." Survey questions present policy proposals stripped of context, tradeoffs, and budgetary implications. Respondents often hold contradictory preferences—they want lower taxes and more spending, more services and smaller government. Their views shift dramatically based on question wording and the salience of different considerations. When Gilens finds that policy fails to match poll responses, the failure may sometimes lie not with policymakers but with the polls' ability to capture preferences that could plausibly guide actual governance.

Consider Gilens's example of trade policy. He treats the move toward freer trade as evidence of policymakers ignoring the poor and middle class in favor of the affluent. But the public's apparent protectionism in polls coexists with persistent consumer behavior that has rewarded cheaper imported goods, and with the broad bipartisan judgment among economists, foreign policy professionals, and labor economists alike that the overall welfare effects of liberalized trade are positive even when distributional effects are uneven. A policymaker who decides that aggregate welfare considerations and expert judgment should outweigh diffuse poll opposition is not necessarily failing democracy; she may be exercising the kind of considered judgment that representative (as opposed to direct) democracy is designed to enable.

Now, none of this is a blanket defense of how American politics actually works. Gilens is right that the influence of large donors raises serious concerns, that voter turnout disparities are troubling, and that organized economic power can distort policy in ways that go well beyond legitimate expert judgment. The growing concentration of wealth at the top is a real political-economic problem, and the explosion of money in elections post–Citizens United deserves the worry Gilens directs at it.

But the diagnostic frame matters. If we believe the problem is that policy fails to match poll majorities, we will be drawn toward more populist, plebiscitary reforms—national referenda, term limits, weakening of "elite" institutions. If we believe the problem is more specifically the disproportionate role of concentrated wealth in shaping elite deliberation, we will be drawn toward campaign finance reform, anti-corruption measures, and strengthening countervailing institutions like unions. These are different reform agendas, and Gilens's framing pushes us toward the first when the second is probably what his evidence actually supports.

American democracy has serious problems. But the problem is not that the public doesn't get its way often enough. It is that some channels of influence have grown too powerful relative to others, distorting the deliberation through which representative government is supposed to operate. That is a narrower diagnosis than Gilens offers, but a more accurate one.

Diversified DeepSeek V4 Pro

Martin Gilens has assembled a devastating empirical case for what many Americans have long suspected: our democracy is not just imperfect, but is, for large stretches and on crucial issues, a sham. His painstaking analysis of decades of survey data and policy outcomes shows that when the preferences of the rich diverge from those of everyone else, the rich almost always win. The poor and the middle class are, in effect, disenfranchised between elections. That this pattern holds even when Democrats control Washington shatters the comforting illusion that the party of the New Deal still serves as the voice of the working class. The implications are nothing short of a crisis for democratic legitimacy.

Gilens’s finding that the influence of the affluent has steadily increased over the past four decades should alarm anyone who believes that government must rest on the consent of the governed. The data track the same decades that saw a massive upward redistribution of wealth, the decline of labor unions, and the transformation of political campaigns into money-guzzling machines. The coincidence is not accidental. As Gilens notes, the one form of civic participation that matches the pattern of unequal influence is the writing of large checks. When candidates must raise millions to compete, they inevitably become attentive to the donor class. Even the mitigating effect of partisan competition is fleeting; once one party consolidates control, as the Republicans did after 2004, the public’s voice vanishes entirely.

The danger is not simply that the rich get the policies they want while others do not. It is that the very legitimacy of the state erodes when people perceive—correctly—that their views carry no weight. The Occupy movement, whatever its organizational shortcomings, gave voice to a widespread sense that the game is rigged. If the public comes to believe that elections cannot discipline the powerful, disillusionment will deepen and could eventually turn into civil unrest or support for authoritarian alternatives. Gilens’s essay should be read as a fire alarm.

What can be done? The reforms Gilens mentions—campaign finance regulation, nonpartisan districting—are necessary but insufficient. The Supreme Court’s decision in *Citizens United* was a body blow, but even before that ruling, private money dominated politics. A constitutional amendment to overturn that decision and to enable effective limits on campaign spending is the only structural solution that can break the link between concentrated wealth and political power. Coupled with robust public financing of elections, which frees candidates from dependence on the donor class, such an amendment could begin to restore the equal representation that democracy demands. In addition, aggressive antitrust enforcement, a higher minimum wage, and measures to strengthen unions would not only reduce economic inequality but also build countervailing organizational power. Gilens notes that unions sometimes align with the poor and middle class; their near-collapse is part of the reason the affluent face so little organized opposition.

Some will argue that the rich have a right to participate, and that restricting money is an infringement of speech. But when the speech of a few drowns out the speech of the many, the First Amendment is being perverted to destroy the very political equality it was meant to protect. The Framers, for all their flaws, feared concentrated power and designed a system of checks and balances. They did not anticipate a world in which corporations and billionaires could purchase the government wholesale. This is why social movements matter. The civil-rights movement did not merely reveal majority preferences; it transformed them. The labor movement did not wait for benevolent elites to discover working-class opinion; it forced new issues onto the agenda. Occupy Wall Street, whatever its limitations, has already changed political language by making inequality and the “1 percent” central to national debate. Whether that language becomes policy depends on whether protest can become organization.

Gilens ends with guarded hope, and he is right to do so. American democracy has repeatedly been widened by people who were told that the system was too fixed to change. But the route to equal influence does not run only through closer elections. It runs through stronger collective institutions for those who currently enter politics alone. The affluent are powerful not just because they are rich, but because their wealth is organized. The democratic answer must be to organize everyone else.

Gilens has assembled an impressive body of evidence, but I worry that the survey-based methodology conflates two distinct phenomena: stated preferences and effective political influence. When a low-income American tells a pollster she supports raising the minimum wage, that's one datum. But whether she would vote against a congressman who opposed such a raise, donate to a campaign supporting it, or join a picket line in its defense—these are questions the surveys cannot answer. The very act of expressing a preference in a survey context, divorced from any real political agency, may inflate apparent support for policies that most people don't care enough about to act on.

This matters because the gap between expressed preferences and actual political behavior is precisely where class inequality manifests most sharply. The affluent don't just have different policy preferences—they mobilize differently, they communicate differently with representatives, and they withdraw their participation when ignored. A middle-class voter who tells a surveyor she supports environmental regulation but never attends a town hall meeting, never writes a letter to her congressman, and never joins an environmental group exercises almost no political influence regardless of what she tells a pollster.

Gilens acknowledges that voting and volunteering patterns track income, but he underemphasizes the informational dimension. The wealthy are more likely to understand how policy actually works, to track legislative developments, and to identify which votes and which issues truly matter. A waitress who supports "health care reform" has expressed a preference that is almost meaningless in the policy space—she may oppose the specific form that reform eventually takes, but she lacks the knowledge and resources to fight for her preferred version or to punish legislators who betrayed her.

The solution, then, is not primarily campaign finance reform, though that matters. It's something more fundamental: rebuilding the political knowledge and civic infrastructure that once made American democracy more participatory. Unions once bridged the knowledge gap; their decline has been catastrophic for working-class political efficacy. Community organizations, civic education, and local news all contribute to citizens who can translate preferences into influence. Gilens is right to be concerned about the wealthy Few shaping policy, but the solution runs deeper than regulating their campaign contributions.

I confess to finding Gilens's normative framework confused. He treats equal influence over policy as a precondition for democracy, but this has never been what democracy meant. Democracy is rule by the people, and "the people" have always included the wealthy, the educated, the organized, and the numerous. The Founders were explicit that they were creating a republic, not a direct democracy, precisely because they distrusted mob rule and wanted institutions that would filter public opinion through deliberation and compromise.

More importantly, Gilens conflates two different questions: whether the wealthy have disproportionate influence, and whether democracy is thereby threatened. The wealthy have always had disproportionate influence—they fund campaigns, they hire lobbyists, they organize effectively. This is not a bug in democracy; it's how interest aggregation works. The question is whether the resulting policies serve the broad public interest, and here Gilens's evidence is actually more ambiguous than his alarmist tone suggests.

Consider what actually changes when influence becomes more equal. Gilens acknowledges that we would see "greater restrictions on abortion," "tighter limits on stem cell research," and "more support for school prayer." Is this really a better democracy? The affluent tend to be more secular and more supportive of individual freedom on social issues, while working-class Americans—whose preferences would gain weight under Gilens's reforms—tend toward social conservatism. If we care about representing the actual values of ordinary Americans, not just their pocketbook interests, then the current system may be getting some things right.

Moreover, Gilens's own evidence contains seeds of hope. Competitive elections, divided government, and political gridlock all increase responsiveness to public preferences. This suggests that American democracy has built-in corrective mechanisms—that when politicians go too far astray, voters punish them. The Bush years offer a case study: Republican overreach on Social Security privatization and the Iraq War helped produce the 2006 and 2008 electoral corrections. This is not a system in crisis; it's a system that, imperfectly and messily, still responds to popular pressures.

Rather than worrying about whether the wealthy have too much influence, we should focus on strengthening the mechanisms—competitive elections, an independent press, robust civil liberties—that keep democracy functional even amid inequality.

Gilens's findings are damning but his proposed remedies are timid. He suggests campaign finance reform, nonpartisan districting, and get-out-the-vote drives. These are reasonable incremental improvements, but they leave the fundamental structure untouched. We are asked to believe that tweaking the rules of political participation can offset the effects of a economy that increasingly rewards capital over labor and concentrates wealth in ways that make 1910 look like an egalitarian paradise.

The real problem is structural. American democracy was designed for an era of relative economic equality, when even modest citizen participation could counterbalance elite influence. We no longer live in that era, and reforms that don't address economic concentration will at best produce cosmetic improvements. The 2008 financial crisis demonstrated this: despite massive public anger at Wall Street, the subsequent regulatory response was modest at best, precisely because the financial industry retained its political influence even as its reputation suffered.

What would genuine reform look like? Mandatory voting, as they have in Australia, would dramatically increase the political voice of low-income citizens who currently sit out elections. Proportional representation in state legislatures would make third parties viable and break the duopoly that currently channels all political competition through donor-dependent parties. A wealth tax would both reduce economic inequality and generate resources for civic engagement programs.

Gilens is correct that political competition provides "room for optimism." But he underestimates how much structural reform would be required to achieve anything like equal representation. The obstacles are not merely political, though they are that too; they are ideological. Americans have been taught to believe that any inequality in outcomes must reflect corresponding inequalities in merit, effort, or talent. Until we challenge this ideology—which serves the affluent as well as any campaign contribution—we will not build the political will for reforms that matter.

The data are clear: American democracy is failing its poorest citizens. The only question is whether we have the courage to make the changes necessary to save it.
