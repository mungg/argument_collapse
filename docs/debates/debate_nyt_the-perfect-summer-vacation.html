Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The perfect summer vacation, for me, has nothing to do with destinations. It has to do with the particular shape a day takes when nothing is required of you before noon. I have been on cruises and to writers' colonies, to rented cottages on lakes whose names I can no longer pronounce, and I have learned that the geography matters far less than the geometry of the hours.

My most memorable summer was spent, improbably, in a borrowed apartment above a bakery in a small town in northern Portugal. A friend of a friend was leaving for six weeks to care for her mother, and I was leaving a marriage, and we made an arrangement that suited us both: I would water her geraniums and feed a tortoise named Beatriz, and in return I would have a desk by a window and the smell of bread rising through the floorboards at four every morning.

What I remember now is not the Atlantic, though I went to it most afternoons, nor the wine, though there was a great deal of it. What I remember is the rhythm. I woke when the ovens started. I wrote until the heat made writing impossible, usually around eleven. Then I walked, ate something cold, swam if I was near water, slept, and read in the long blue evenings until I could not see the page. I finished a draft of a novel I had been failing to write for three years. I also, I think, finished mourning something I had not realized I was mourning.

This is what I have come to believe about summer: it is the one season that gives you permission to be unproductive in the open, and paradoxically that is when most of the real work of a life gets done. The great American summer mythology — the road trip, the lake house, the screened porch — is really a mythology about consent to drift. You cannot force the kind of thinking that summer makes possible. You can only clear a space for it and wait.

So my advice, for whatever it is worth in mid-August, when the season is already tipping toward its end: do not over-plan. Pick one window. Pick one book you have been afraid of. Find a body of water, or failing that, a fan. Allow yourself to be bored long enough to become interesting to yourself again. The tortoise is optional, though I do recommend her.

The very premise of a “perfect summer vacation” feels like a high-wire act, a performance of leisure that is destined to collapse under its own curated weight. By August 2015, our social feeds are a relentless scroll of other people’s apparently flawless holidays: the precisely angled hammock shot with just enough turquoise sea in the background, the rustic-chic farm-to-table plate captured seconds before being eaten, the unsmiling, love-bitten couple artfully disheveled against a Roman ruin. These images, which increasingly shape our aspirations, sell a fantasy of escape that is entirely frictionless. A truly memorable vacation, in my experience, has never been frictionless. The ones that stick, that insinuate themselves into the architecture of who you are, are the ones marked by a glorious, unplanned unraveling.

I think of a trip to the coast of Maine not long ago, which had none of the aspirational gloss of a magazine spread. The plan was simple: a week of reading, lobster, and long walks. The reality included a third night when a nor’easter’s tail end whipped through, knocking out the power and plunging the small cottage into a profound, primitive darkness. The bright, plot-driven novel I’d brought was useless. Instead, by a flickering candle that threw distorted shadows against the walls, I found a crumpled collection of Mary Oliver poems left by a previous tenant. The storm’s percussive rage against the windows made the lines feel urgent and raw, not like a contemplative exercise. The perfect calm I had sought was shattered, and in its place was something sharper: a feeling of utter presence, huddled against the elements, reading with a focus that felt like survival.

This is the real secret the glossy catalogs and curated Instagram grids never reveal. The perfect summer isn’t about the flawless execution of a consumer experience; it’s about the suspension of control and the derailing of intention. We pursue a beach vacation to empty our minds, but the mind doesn’t truly reset through passive lounging. It resets when it is jolted out of its routine thought patterns by the unforeseen—a downpour that traps you in a conversation with a stranger, a wrong turn that leads to an unnameable cove, a power outage that forces you to read poetry by candlelight. These are not inconveniences marring an ideal; they are the very substance of recollection.

Our obsession with the "good summer read" is a parallel longing for a frictionless mind, a desire for a book that is simultaneously absorbing and undemanding, a literary beach blanket. But the most memorable reading experiences, like the most memorable journeys, disrupt rather than lull. By 2015, we are so saturated with performance and so anxious about optimizing our time off that we have forgotten how to simply be somewhere, and how to be with a book that doesn't double as a social signifier. The perfect vacation is the one that, like a good book, leaves a small, indelible mark because it forced you to see a sliver of the world, or just the corner of a rain-lashed room, with a clarity you hadn’t planned for at all.

In our hyper-connected era, the concept of a summer vacation has become paradoxically stressful. We pack our bags, board planes, and cross oceans, only to spend our days looking at the same glowing rectangles we stare at back home. The modern vacation too often devolves into a desperate search for international data roaming and the perfect social media filter to prove to our peers that we are, in fact, relaxing. But as a writer, I’ve found that the perfect summer escape has very little to do with exotic geography and everything to do with intentional disconnection.

My ideal summer vacation takes place somewhere with notoriously terrible cell reception. Picture a weathered, cedar-shingled cabin on the coast of Maine or a quiet lake in the Adirondacks. There is no itinerary, no morning alarm, and absolutely no Wi-Fi. The perfection of the trip is defined not by what is scheduled, but by the vast, unbroken stretches of empty time. When you remove the constant, frantic thrum of emails and breaking news alerts—a thrum that has only grown louder and more demanding in recent years—your brain slowly unclenches. You remember how to let your mind wander, which is the very state required to fully inhabit a good book.

A few Augusts ago, I experienced a deeply memorable iteration of this ideal. I rented a small house on an island in the Pacific Northwest, accessible only by a slow-moving ferry. For a week, my universe shrank to a wooden porch, a tall stack of battered paperbacks, and the rhythm of the tides. Without the internet to distract me, reading ceased to be a fractured, fragmented exercise squeezed between pressing tasks. I spent entire afternoons lost in the pages of sprawling, ambitious novels, achieving the kind of immersive reading I usually only associate with childhood summers. The biggest daily dilemma was deciding whether to switch from fiction to history before or after lunch.

Ultimately, the perfect summer vacation is a return to a simpler, slower cognitive rhythm. It is a quiet rebellion against the demand to be perpetually available and constantly productive. Whether you are writing a book or simply reading one on a quiet beach, you need that mental spaciousness to let the words breathe. The best summer getaways don’t just offer a change of scenery; they offer a temporary change in how we experience time itself, trading the frantic rush of the modern world for the slow, satisfying turn of a page.

The perfect summer vacation is not the one with the most perfect weather, the cleanest beach or the most enviable photographs. It is the one that interrupts the tyranny of the schedule. By August, many Americans are not merely tired; they are trained out of rest. We carry work in our pockets, answer email from Adirondack chairs and treat idleness as a character flaw. A good vacation has to do more than change the scenery. It has to change the tempo.

My most memorable summer vacation was modest: a rented house near a cold lake, a stack of books, bad cell service and no itinerary more ambitious than swimming before lunch. Nothing “happened,” which is precisely why I remember it. We cooked, walked, read, lost track of the day of the week. There was time for conversation to wander past logistics and into memory, gossip, politics, old regrets, family stories. The absence of novelty made room for attention.

That is why I am suspicious of the fantasy vacation as a checklist: three countries, four museums, six restaurants, every hour optimized. Travel can enlarge the mind, of course, and some of my happiest days have been spent in unfamiliar cities. But the summer holiday has a different moral purpose. It should restore our capacity to notice: the smell of sunscreen on a towel, the patience required to finish a long novel, the small comedy of relatives forced to share one kitchen.

A perfect vacation also needs some imperfection. Rain gives permission to stay inside. A wrong turn becomes a story. Children get bored and then, if adults can endure the complaining, inventive. The best summer days are not frictionless; they are spacious.

So my ideal vacation is simple: near water, with people I love, enough books, fewer plans, and a phone that mostly stays off. Perfection, in summer, is not escape from life. It is life returned to human scale.

The perfect summer vacation, I've come to believe, is less about exotic destinations than about the quality of attention we bring to our time away. In an era when leisure is increasingly crowded with productivity optimization and Instagram documentation, the vacations that linger in memory are the ones that allowed genuine emptiness—the rare gift of unstructured hours.

My most memorable summer came unexpectedly. A decade ago, I rented a small cottage on a lake in northern Michigan with no WiFi and a dock that extended into water so clear I could count the rocks beneath. For the first two days, I fidgeted. I had brought books I'd never read, a journal I intended to fill, plans for long walks that would somehow make me more myself. None of it worked. The silence felt accusatory.

Then something shifted. I stopped trying to use the time and simply inhabited it. I swam in the mornings when the lake was mirror-still. I cooked simple meals from local produce. I sat on the dock in the evenings and watched the sky perform its slow fade into darkness. The days became elastic in a way that weekdays never are, stretching to hold whatever I placed in them.

What I learned is that summer vacation at its best offers something increasingly scarce: the experience of time moving at human pace. We've built economies premised on relentless motion, yet the body and mind require periods of genuine rest—not the "active relaxation" of scheduled wellness retreats or curated experiences, but the deeper quiet that comes only when we stop performing recovery and simply allow it to happen.

The perfect summer vacation, ultimately, is any stretch of days that restores our capacity to be present. Whether it unfolds on a lake or a beach, in a foreign city or a familiar backyard, its measure is the same: we return having remembered who we are when we're not rushing.

The perfect summer vacation, for me, has nothing to do with destination and everything to do with the slow dissolution of obligation. I have come to believe that we ruin our summers by treating them as projects — itineraries to be optimized, photographs to be staged, "experiences" to be collected like baseball cards. The vacations I remember most fondly are the ones in which almost nothing happened.

One August, I rented a small cottage on a lake in Maine with a screened porch, a stack of paperbacks I had been meaning to read for a decade, and a refrigerator I stocked exactly once. I had no Wi-Fi. I had a transistor radio that picked up a single classical station and, on certain nights when the weather was right, a Red Sox game broadcast from somewhere across the water. For two weeks I read in the morning, swam at noon, napped in the heat of the afternoon, and ate tomatoes with salt for dinner. By the end of the first week I had stopped checking the time. By the end of the second, I had stopped checking the date.

What I noticed, when I returned, was how much of my ordinary life consisted of low-grade vigilance. The phone, the inbox, the calendar — these are not neutral instruments. They keep a part of the mind permanently on patrol. A real vacation is not a change of scenery; it is a temporary dismantling of that patrol. Scenery helps, of course. A lake helps. But a person who travels to Provence with a smartphone full of work email has merely relocated the patrol to a prettier outpost.

I would urge any reader contemplating a summer break to be ruthless about this. Pick somewhere you can plausibly defend as "out of range." Tell colleagues you will be unreachable, and mean it. Bring more books than you think you can finish; the surplus is the point, because the perfect summer book is the one you didn't plan to read. Cook simply or not at all. Walk somewhere every day without a destination.

The cruel paradox of modern leisure is that we have more of it than any prior generation and feel less restored by it. The fault is not in our days off but in our refusal to truly take them. A perfect summer vacation, in the end, is one from which you return slightly bored, slightly browner, and unable to remember what was so urgent the week before you left.

I am suspicious of the phrase "perfect summer vacation." It carries the same whiff of commercial fantasy as "dream wedding" or "forever home" — a phrase engineered to sell you something, usually an airline ticket or a linen sundress. The vacations that have meant the most to me were not perfect. They were, in fact, often disasters, and that is precisely why they have stayed with me.

There was the summer my husband and I drove from Chicago to the Badlands in a car whose air conditioner gave out somewhere in Iowa. There was the rented house in Greece that turned out to overlook not the sea but a parking lot, and where the shower drained directly onto the kitchen floor. There was the camping trip in the Sierras that coincided with the worst mosquito hatch in living memory, during which my then-six-year-old daughter wept for two hours and then, somewhere around hour three, began to laugh hysterically and would not stop. She still talks about that trip. She does not talk about the well-planned Disney visit that followed it the next year.

A trip that goes exactly as you imagined is a trip that produces no stories. You return home and discover, sheepishly, that you have nothing to say about it beyond the predictable adjectives. Whereas the broken-down car, the misrepresented villa, the biblical insects — these are the raw material of narrative, and narrative is how we metabolize our lives.

I think this is especially worth saying in 2015, when so much of vacation has become performance. We curate our trips for Instagram before we have even unpacked. We rehearse the captions in our heads while standing in front of the cathedral. The "perfect" vacation, in this idiom, is one that photographs well. But a photograph is not a memory; it is a substitute for one, and often a worse one.

If I were to recommend anything, it would be this: plan less. Book the flight and the first night's lodging and then leave room for the place itself to assert its character, which it will, often inconveniently. Be willing to be uncomfortable, or at least mildly inconvenienced. Travel with people who can laugh when things go sideways, and avoid those who cannot. Bring a notebook rather than a camera.

The perfect summer vacation, I suspect, does not exist and should not. What exists, if you are lucky, is the memorable one — and memory, as any novelist will tell you, prefers a little friction.

I will be unfashionable and admit it: my perfect summer vacation is a city. Not a beach, not a mountain, not a lake house with a dock — a hot, loud, slightly smelly city, ideally one I have never been to before, in the middle of a heat wave its residents will spend the entire month complaining about.

But this is exactly what makes a city in summer such an interesting place. It is depopulated of its usual self. The locals who remain are the ones who couldn't get away or didn't want to, and they are often the most interesting locals. The restaurants that stay open are the ones with something to prove. The museums are emptier. You can get into the opera.

My most memorable summer trip was two weeks in Lisbon in August, when the temperature hovered around 100 and the trams ran half-empty and every plaza had a single ancient man drinking a single small beer. I walked twelve miles a day. I ate grilled sardines in a tiled room with no other customers. I read Pessoa on a bench above the Tagus and felt, with that strange clarity heat induces, that I had stumbled into someone else's life and was, for the moment, welcome there.

What I am defending is a certain idea of vacation as encounter rather than retreat. The dominant American conception of summer travel is restorative — you go somewhere quiet to recover from your noisy life. There is nothing wrong with this, and at certain ages and stages it is essential. But there is another model, older and I think undervalued, in which travel is supposed to disturb you rather than soothe you, to drop you into a foreign rhythm and let you fumble around in it until something shifts.

You cannot do this from a beach chair. You can barely do this from a guided tour. You can do it best by walking, alone or nearly so, through a city in which you do not speak the language, getting lost, getting hot, getting tired, and getting fed by people who have no particular reason to be kind to you and are anyway.

That, to me, is the perfect summer vacation. It is also, not coincidentally, where novels come from.

My perfect summer vacation has a single, non-negotiable feature: my children are in it, and they are not on screens. Everything else — the location, the weather, the food, the lodging — is incidental and negotiable. Anyone who has tried to parent in 2015 will understand why this sounds both modest and impossible.

I am not one of those parents who believes technology is poison. My kids use iPads; I am writing this on a laptop; we are not Luddites. But during the school year our family operates as four separate satellites orbiting four separate devices, occasionally intersecting at the dinner table to exchange status updates. Summer is the one chance we have to remember that we actually like each other, and that liking requires proximity and boredom and a certain amount of forced cohabitation.

So the vacations I treasure are the ones structured to make screens inconvenient or unavailable. A canoe trip in the Boundary Waters. A week in a cabin without cell service. A long drive across a stretch of country where the radio gives out and someone has to actually look at the map. These are not glamorous trips. They involve bug spray and freeze-dried meals and the particular smell of damp synthetic fabric. But they produce something nothing else in our lives produces: hours of uninterrupted, unmediated time with my own children.

What happens in those hours is not always pleasant. My teenager is, frankly, a more difficult companion without his headphones; my younger one asks approximately one question every nine seconds. But by day three or four, something shifts. The questions become better. The teenager starts telling stories I have never heard before. We notice the same hawk. We argue about something real, like whether to take the longer trail, instead of something fake, like screen time limits.

I am aware that this sounds sentimental, and possibly prescriptive in the way that parents of a certain class are prone to being prescriptive. I do not mean to suggest that every family must paddle a canoe to be whole. Let me dissent from the entire premise. The perfect summer vacation is a fiction invented by people who can afford one, and the question itself is a kind of soft cruelty in a country where, according to surveys I have seen recently, something like a quarter of workers get no paid vacation at all and many who do are afraid to use it. Asked to describe the perfect summer vacation, I find myself wanting to ask instead why so many Americans have no real summer at all.

I grew up in a household where vacation meant my father's one week off from the plant, during which we drove to my aunt's house and slept on the floor. My mother packed sandwiches for the road because restaurants were a luxury. I did not see the ocean until I was nineteen. None of this is a tragedy — we were fine, we were loved, the sandwiches were good — but it does mean I notice the class assumptions baked into the very framing of this conversation. "Where do you go in August?" is a question that, in much of the world and in much of this country, simply has no answer.

The writers being asked here are writers, which is to say people whose work travels with them and whose summers are, in a sense, infinitely portable. We should be honest that this is a privileged position. The waitress serving us our lobster roll on the wharf does not have a perfect summer vacation. She has a double shift, and she is grateful for the tips, and her kids are at her sister's house watching television because summer camp costs more than she makes in a week.

I do not say this to scold anyone for enjoying their time off. Enjoy it; you have earned it; the world is hard and small pleasures are not theft. But when magazines and newspapers run features about perfect summers, they participate, however gently, in a culture that treats leisure as an individual achievement rather than a collective entitlement. In France, vacation is a right. In the United States, it is a perk, distributed like all perks — unevenly, and often to those who need it least.

So my answer to the question is a counter-question. What would it take for the perfect summer vacation to be something more Americans could actually have? Mandated paid leave, certainly. Affordable childcare in the summer months. Public beaches and parks adequately funded. Until then, the perfect summer vacation is, for too many people, a magazine article they read on their lunch break.

A perfect summer vacation, in my experience, has remarkably little in common with perfection. It is not restful. It is not scenic, at least not in a postcard sense. It is a blister on your heel, the weight of a pack cinched too tight, breath that turns ragged above 12,000 feet, and a mind that has run out of distractions and must finally sit with itself. My most memorable summer unfolded in the Cordillera Blanca of Peru, a solo traverse that was less a vacation and more a confrontation. I went there seeking the opposite of a beach read; I wanted something that would strip away the varnish of everyday life.

The argument for the strenuous journey is ancient but easily forgotten in an age of curated comfort. When we push our bodies into physically demanding and unfamiliar terrain, we rediscover a basic animal alertness that cubicle life suppresses. I remember crouching beside a glacial stream, filling a bottle, and realizing that my entire world had collapsed to the immediate concerns of navigation, shelter, and the next sip of water. That narrowing was a liberation. The mental static of career, mortgage, and social obligation simply had no air to breathe up there. In their place came a luminous clarity, a sensation that every step was a negotiation between will and gravity, and that I was, for the first time in years, fully present.

Some dismiss this as an adrenaline addiction or a form of escapism. But the strenuous vacation is not about running from life; it’s about running into a more honest version of it. Physical challenge dissolves pretense. It exposes the fault lines of a relationship if you’re traveling with a partner, and it magnifies your own flaws when you’re alone. I discovered on that trek that I was less resilient than I imagined, and also far more capable of pushing through discomfort once I accepted it as part of the experience. The perfect vacation, then, is one that refuses to coddle you. It leaves you sore, sunburned, and humbled. Months later, when you sit at your desk in the stale office air, you can still feel the phantom pull of that altitude in your lungs, and you understand that you return not restored in the soft sense, but resharpened—a tool honed by rock and thin air.
