Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Sypnowich is right to be impatient with the slogan of "equal opportunity." In its thinnest form — the non-discrimination version invoked by politicians from Biden to Trudeau — it has become a kind of alibi, allowing societies with grotesque disparities in wealth and life expectancy to congratulate themselves on their fairness. And she is right that even the more demanding philosophical versions, from Rawlsian fair equality of opportunity to Dworkinian equality of resources, are compatible with inequalities most of us would find indefensible if we encountered them in the flesh. The move from opportunity to outcomes — or, in her preferred vocabulary, flourishing — is a corrective worth taking seriously.

But I want to push back on two features of her argument. The first concerns whether "flourishing" can really do the work she asks of it without smuggling in the perfectionist commitments liberal egalitarians have been right to worry about. The second concerns whether her diagnosis of liberal egalitarianism is fair, or whether she has set up an unnecessarily sharp opposition between opportunity and outcomes.

Begin with the perfectionism worry. Sypnowich is admirably candid: she wants the state to "take a stand on how people should live," to disincentivize some pursuits, and even to impose compulsory arts education "of the right kind." She insists this is not coercion in any objectionable sense, since every polity already shapes preferences through education and incentives. That much is true — Rawlsian neutrality has always been neutrality of justification, not of effect — but her examples actually understate the worry. The state's encouragement of literacy or its discouragement of smoking proceeds from claims about which there is broad reflective convergence: that being able to read is a prerequisite for almost any conception of the good, that lung cancer is bad. Compulsory music education "of the right kind" is a different beast. Whose right kind? Decided how? Sypnowich gestures toward "legitimate, democratic debate about what counts as flourishing," but democratic majorities have historically been quite enthusiastic about enforcing their views of the good life on dissenting minorities — religious nonconformists, sexual minorities, the irreligious, the avant-garde. The liberal egalitarian's neutralism is not a piece of bad sociology, as she suggests; it is a response to the historical record of what happens when polities decide they know what flourishing looks like.

She anticipates this with the reassurance that flourishing "comes in many forms." But once we add this pluralism back in, the distance from sophisticated equality-of-opportunity views shrinks considerably. Nussbaum's capabilities approach already tries to identify a plural list of substantive functionings while leaving people free to function or not; Sen's framework foregrounds substantive freedoms; Rawls's primary goods are designed precisely to be the conditions of pursuing diverse conceptions of the good. If Sypnowich's flourishing must accommodate plural valuable lives and must not be imposed by force, then the apparatus she rejects — capabilities, primary goods, opportunity sets — starts to look like the practical machinery any flourishing-oriented state would need to deploy. The disagreement becomes one of emphasis and policy ambition, not of fundamental commitment.

Now to the second worry. Sypnowich treats luck egalitarianism as paradigmatic of liberal egalitarian thought, and Dworkin's option-luck framework as its emblem. But this overstates the consensus. Anderson's "What Is the Point of Equality?" — to which Sypnowich nods — already mounts a devastating relational critique of luck egalitarianism from within the liberal egalitarian camp, arguing that equality concerns the standing of persons in relations of mutual respect, not the metaphysics of choice. Scheffler, Wolff, and others have piled on. The "relational egalitarian" tradition, which insists that equality is about how people stand to one another rather than the distributive shape of a final pattern, is hardly less concerned with community, solidarity, and the dignity of the worst-off than Sypnowich's socialism is. To collapse contemporary liberal egalitarianism into its Dworkinian wing is to fight the battles of 1990 rather than 2023.

This matters because Sypnowich's most powerful arguments — that choice is shaped by circumstance all the way down, that "from each according to ability, to each according to need" cannot be reconstructed in luck-egalitarian terms, that solidarity is constitutive of the egalitarian ideal — are widely shared on the liberal left. They do not by themselves vindicate the leap from opportunity to flourishing as the operative metric of justice. One can accept all of her sociology of choice and still think that the appropriate political response is to expand and equalize the substantive conditions under which people exercise agency, rather than to evaluate the agency-exercises themselves and intervene when their outcomes diverge.

There is also a strategic point worth raising. At a moment when egalitarians face well-funded opposition that already caricatures progressive politics as paternalistic and culturally domineering, a public-facing case for equality framed around "the egalitarian community taking a stand on how people should live" hands ammunition to opponents that the opportunity framing, for all its limitations, withholds. This is not a knockdown objection — sometimes the truth is politically inconvenient — but it suggests that the rhetorical case for outcome-talk should be made with more caution than Sypnowich allows.

The flourishing horizon is the right one. But getting there will require more of the liberal egalitarian toolkit, and less confidence in the state's capacity to identify the valuable, than her essay suggests.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Christine Sypnowich’s lead essay is a powerful indictment of the thin gruel that passes for egalitarianism in much contemporary political discourse. She is right that the rhetoric of “equal opportunity” too often serves as a fig leaf for staggering inequalities of income, wealth, and power, and that the dominant liberal egalitarian philosophies have been unduly preoccupied with personal responsibility and the formal availability of chances. Yet her recommended alternative—a politics of “equal flourishing” that abandons liberal neutrality and embraces socialism—is both philosophically overambitious and politically hazardous. A reformed and deepened liberal egalitarianism, rather than a leap beyond it, is the more plausible and liberatory path.

Sypnowich’s critique of the opportunity paradigm is most persuasive when she shows how the distinction between choice and circumstance is blurred to the point of uselessness. As she notes, choices are always made in a social context; disadvantage shapes decision-making, and one bad choice can foreclose future options. The luck egalitarian insistence that those who suffer from their own poor decisions have no claim of justice can indeed seem “soulless,” as Jonathan Wolff has argued. But Sypnowich understates the extent to which liberal egalitarians themselves have moved beyond a crude responsibility–cut. John Rawls’s difference principle, which requires inequalities to work to the benefit of the least well-off, is an outcome-focused constraint. Amartya Sen’s capability approach, though officially concerned with capabilities rather than functionings, is driven by a moral urgency to ensure that people are actually well-nourished, sheltered, and educated. In practice, even liberal egalitarians endorse policies that secure outcomes, not just opportunities, for these basic goods. The line Sypnowich draws between a supposedly opportunity-obsessed liberalism and her own outcome-focused radicalism is, therefore, overdrawn.

More worrying is Sypnowich’s positive vision. She calls for an egalitarian state that actively promotes valuable ways of life and disincentivizes others, with the aim of equalizing human flourishing. This is an unabashed perfectionism. Sypnowich preempts the charge of coercion by arguing that all states inevitably shape values, so the only question is whether we do so transparently and democratically. But there is a vast difference between a state that funds public libraries and one that takes responsibility for ensuring that each citizen actually achieves a certain level of flourishing in multiple dimensions. Once the state is held accountable for equal outcomes in subjective well-being, the pressure to override dissenting individual choices—through compulsory education in the arts, for example, or the stigmatization of non-approved lifestyles—becomes difficult to resist. Sypnowich insists that flourishing takes many forms and that the state should be open to revising its conception of the good. Yet in a pluralistic society, deep disagreements about what constitutes a valuable life are inevitable. Entrusting the state with the task of equalizing flourishing gives it a license to impose a particular, however contested, vision of the good. The historical experience of twentieth-century socialism, much of which devolved into authoritarian attempts to engineer the “new man,” should give us pause.

Sypnowich’s emphasis on community and solidarity is welcome and echoes the best of the socialist tradition. But she too quickly identifies these values with the abolition of markets and the supersession of liberal rights. One can cultivate an egalitarian ethos without dismantling the institutional architecture of liberal democracy. The Nordic countries, for all their imperfections, demonstrate that generous universal welfare states, strong collective bargaining, and high levels of social trust can coexist with market economies and robust civil liberties. They have not eliminated inequality of outcome, but they have dramatically reduced the kind of poverty, insecurity, and status hierarchies that most undermine human flourishing. That seems a more tangible and defensible goal than equal flourishing per se.

Finally, Sypnowich’s near-total rejection of personal responsibility risks infantilizing citizens. While it is true that social conditions constrain agency, an emancipatory politics should aim to expand people’s capacity to make meaningful choices and to take ownership of their lives, not to relieve them of accountability altogether. An egalitarian society should provide a generous floor below which no one is allowed to fall, regardless of the reasons for their misfortune. But it need not guarantee that everyone ends up equally happy or fulfilled—an aim that is both unattainable and, in a free society, undesirable.

Sypnowich’s essay performs a vital service by exposing the poverty of opportunity talk and reminding us that equality is, at bottom, about how people actually live. Yet the proper response is not to abandon liberal egalitarianism but to radicalize it: to demand, as the late Erik Olin Wright urged, “real utopias” that equalize power and resources while preserving individual freedom. That means fighting for universal basic services, a job guarantee, a progressive wealth tax, and deep democratic reform—measures that bring outcomes much closer to equal without requiring the state to dictate what it means to flourish. Equality of opportunity may not be enough, but the pursuit of equal flourishing must be tempered by a liberal respect for the plurality of human goods.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Christine Sypnowich offers a provocative and timely critique of the contemporary political obsession with “equality of opportunity.” She rightly points out that in the rhetoric of many modern politicians, opportunity talk frequently functions as a convenient alibi. It allows leaders to accept staggering wealth gaps and systemic deprivation, so long as they can point to a theoretically open starting line. Furthermore, Sypnowich’s diagnosis of “luck egalitarianism” hits its mark: a political philosophy that leaves individuals to suffer the catastrophic consequences of their “bad choices” ignores the reality that choices are invariably shaped by unjust social structures.

However, in her rush to discard the opportunity framework altogether, Sypnowich embraces an alternative that carries its own profound risks. By arguing that egalitarianism must abandon state neutrality and actively engineer “equal flourishing” and equal outcomes, she advocates a paternalistic vision of the state that threatens the pluralism at the heart of free societies. Liberal egalitarianism’s commitment to choice and neutrality is not, as Sypnowich suggests, merely a concession to right-wing market logic. Rather, it is a crucial safeguard against the authoritarian potential of a state empowered to dictate the definition of a good life.

The most troubling aspect of Sypnowich’s argument is her dismissal of liberal neutrality—the idea that the state should not rank or impose specific conceptions of how to live. She argues that because individuals are already influenced by unaccountable market forces, the state should step in to purposefully “encourage people to see the value of one choice over another.” To illustrate this, she uses the highly benign example of mandatory music lessons. But the power to define what constitutes a "flourishing" life rarely stops at the arts.

Historically, when governments have abandoned neutrality to promote substantive visions of the good life, the results have frequently oppressed those who deviate from the majority’s norms. Conceptions of flourishing are inherently subjective and culturally bound. What looks like "flourishing" to a dominant cultural group might look like forced assimilation to an Indigenous community, or compulsory heteronormativity to LGBTQ+ individuals. When the state takes it upon itself to correct “cultures of fatalism” or “limited horizons,” it too often pathologizes minority lifestyles, neurodivergence, and unconventional family structures. Liberal egalitarianism insists on neutrality precisely to protect individuals from a majority that might otherwise impose its own moral or cultural preferences under the guise of egalitarian care.

Furthermore, Sypnowich severely underestimates the radical redistributive potential already present within the liberal egalitarian tradition. She claims that liberal philosophy "washes its hands of how people are actually faring." But this is a mischaracterization of thinkers like John Rawls and Amartya Sen. Rawls’s “difference principle” is entirely concerned with outcomes: it dictates that social and economic inequalities are only justified if they maximize the absolute position of the worst-off members of society. This is not a philosophy that abandons people to the market; it is one that demands continuous, structural redistribution.

Similarly, Sen and Martha Nussbaum’s capability approach was specifically developed to bridge the gap between abstract opportunity and actual well-being. Sypnowich criticizes Nussbaum for focusing on the "capability to function, not functioning per se." Yet this distinction is vital. The state should ensure that every citizen is fully nourished, educated, and housed—providing the absolute capability to participate in society. But if a citizen chooses to fast for religious reasons, the state should not force-feed them to ensure the “outcome” of equal nourishment. Ensuring the *capability* to flourish is a matter of profound material justice; mandating the *act* of flourishing is a violation of human agency.

Sypnowich is certainly correct that the line between choice and circumstance is deeply blurred. Our tastes, ambitions, and even our mistakes are molded by our environments. But entirely absorbing choice into circumstance, as she suggests, effectively strips individuals of their agency. If we assume that bad choices are merely symptoms of a diseased social environment, we risk treating citizens as passive patients to be cured by the state, rather than as autonomous agents to be respected. The proper liberal egalitarian response to the blurred line between choice and circumstance is not to abandon personal responsibility, but to ensure a robust, unconditional social safety net. We can believe that people are responsible for their choices while simultaneously believing that no choice, no matter how imprudent, should result in homelessness, starvation, or a lack of medical care.

Finally, Sypnowich points to non-material deficits, such as alienation and loneliness, as outcomes that the egalitarian state must rectify. While it is true that modern capitalism exacerbates social isolation, positioning friendship and love as matters of state-enforced egalitarian justice invites an alarming level of government intrusion into intimate life. A society can foster community by building public parks, funding community centers, and shortening the workweek, but it cannot and should not attempt to equalize human relationships.

Ultimately, Sypnowich is right that superficial "equality of opportunity"—the mere removal of legal discrimination—is drastically insufficient. The playing field is not level, and the gates of opportunity remain closed to millions. But the solution to this crisis is to demand that opportunity be made truly structural, material, and fair, rather than discarding the concept entirely. By ensuring unconditional basic needs, aggressively redistributing wealth, and protecting democratic pluralism, we can provide everyone with the genuine capability to flourish, while still trusting them with the freedom to decide what that flourishing looks like.

Christine Sypnowich is right that “equal opportunity” often functions as a moral solvent: pour it over entrenched hierarchy and the hard edges of inequality seem to disappear. If the poor had a fair shot, if the labor market were color-blind, if schools were adequately open, then the fact that some live in precarity while others accumulate dynastic wealth can be redescribed as the residue of choice. This is politically convenient and philosophically misleading. In societies where family wealth buys neighborhood, school, safety, confidence, social networks, internships, test preparation, legal help, and time, opportunity cannot be cleanly separated from outcome. The child’s “opportunity” is made out of the parents’ outcomes.

But I hesitate before Sypnowich’s proposed replacement: equality of flourishing. The hesitation is not because outcomes do not matter. They matter enormously. It is because “equal flourishing” is too ambitious in one sense and not political enough in another. It risks asking public institutions to equalize what they cannot and should not fully control, while leaving somewhat underdescribed the more concrete egalitarian aim: to build social relations and institutions in which no one is dominated, abandoned, humiliated, or forced to sell their life chances cheaply.

The strongest part of Sypnowich’s argument is her attack on the choice/circumstance distinction. Contemporary inequality is not a track meet in which runners arrive with different shoes but the same map. It is a system that produces capacities, expectations, and desires. A young person who “chooses” not to apply to university because no one in her family has done so, because debt seems terrifying, because her school has few counselors, and because paid work is immediately necessary has not simply revealed a preference. She has adapted to a horizon. Likewise, “bad choices” about health, work, money, or relationships are often made under stress, scarcity, trauma, and social isolation. Behavioral economics has confirmed what socialists and social workers have long known: scarcity taxes attention and narrows planning. To treat the resulting inequalities as morally deserved is cruel.

Sypnowich is also right that neutrality is often fictitious. The liberal state already promotes forms of life: homeownership through tax policy, employment through welfare rules, marriage through law, consumption through infrastructure, fossil fuel use through subsidy and design, and education through compulsion. Markets are not neutral forums in which autonomous selves disclose their values. They are engines for shaping wants, attaching status to commodities, and rewarding activities whose social value may be dubious. A society that leaves children’s cultural formation to advertising, streaming platforms, unequal families, and local tax bases is not respecting freedom; it is privatizing influence.

Still, the language of flourishing should make egalitarians cautious. Flourishing is plural, partly subjective, and partly tragic. Some people flourish in solitude; others in dense association. Some find meaning in ambition, others in care, devotion, craft, worship, play, or contemplation. Disability rights movements have rightly challenged outside judgments about which lives are “less flourishing.” Feminists have shown how ideals of the good life can smuggle in gendered expectations about family, dependency, and service. Religious and cultural minorities have reason to fear public authorities that confidently rank ways of living. Even when coercion is disavowed, the state’s power to “incentivize” and “educate” can become a power to normalize.

The problem is not that public judgment can be avoided. It cannot. The problem is how to discipline it. Appeals to flourishing need democratic, rights-protecting, and anti-domination constraints. We should be especially wary when elites define the valuable pursuits of others. Music education is a benign example, and a persuasive one: compulsory schooling should introduce children to arts, sciences, physical activity, civic life, and practical skills they might not otherwise encounter. But the same rationale could be used less benignly—to stigmatize working-class leisure, pressure women into approved forms of “empowerment,” or treat some communities as culturally deficient. A politics of flourishing must therefore be participatory, not merely perfectionist. People must have power in defining the conditions of their own flourishing.

For that reason, I would put relational equality at the center. The question is not whether everyone achieves the same level of well-being, an impossible and perhaps undesirable goal. It is whether our institutions secure for all the material and social bases of standing as equals. That includes outcomes: income, wealth, housing, health, education, leisure, safety, political voice, and access to care. But the point of equalizing them is not to produce identical lives. It is to prevent caste, dependency, and domination.

This emphasis yields a demanding political program. It supports universal health care, including mental health care; strong unions and sectoral bargaining; a generous child allowance; paid leave; public childcare and eldercare; social housing; desegregated and well-funded schools; free or low-cost higher and vocational education; disability supports; public transit; progressive taxation of income, inheritance, and wealth; and democratic constraints on corporate power. It also supports public goods that are not merely redistributive in the narrow sense: libraries, parks, arts institutions, community centers, safe streets, and time away from paid labor. These policies care about outcomes, not just access. Their success is measured by whether people are actually housed, healthy, educated, secure, and able to participate.

Such a program also avoids one weakness of opportunity rhetoric: its obsession with mobility. A society should not ask whether a few children born poor can become rich; it should ask why being born poor is so damaging in the first place. The point is not to diversify the elite while leaving the hierarchy intact. It is to compress the hierarchy and make the lower rungs decent, powerful, and secure. Equal opportunity can coexist with a brutal competition for scarce goods. Egalitarianism should reduce the brutality and the scarcity.

Sypnowich’s socialist conclusion is therefore compelling if socialism means democratic control over the economic conditions that shape our lives, decommodification of basic needs, and solidaristic provision beyond market desert. But socialism should not promise equal flourishing in too total a sense. It should promise something at once more modest and more radical: that no one’s life prospects will depend on parental wealth, market vulnerability, racial status, gendered subordination, disability, or sheer abandonment; that all will have the resources and standing to help shape the common world; and that inequalities will be judged by what they do to people’s real lives, not by whether they can be laundered through the language of choice.

Equal opportunity is not enough. But the alternative is not a society that guarantees that all flourish equally. It is a society that takes responsibility for the conditions under which flourishing becomes broadly and genuinely possible—and that refuses to excuse misery because, somewhere along the way, a formal gate was open.

Sypnowich's essay effectively diagnoses why opportunity-focused egalitarianism falls short. Her critique of luck egalitarianism's sharp distinction between choice and circumstance is compelling: if our preferences, dispositions, and even what we take to be our authentic choices are shaped by social conditions, then the idea that individuals should bear full responsibility for their outcomes becomes difficult to defend. The flourishing perspective she advocates offers a more humane measure of equality—one that asks not whether people had formal chances but whether they actually live well.

Yet her argument requires greater specification in at least two respects. First, the flourishing approach must clarify what genuine equal flourishing actually demands. Sypnowich rightly insists that flourishing comes in many forms and need not be imposed coercively. But she also argues that we should "not be squeamish about an outcomes approach that takes a stand on how people should live." The tension here needs resolution: if flourishing is pluralistic yet we nonetheless take stands on how people should live, who determines where to draw the line? The danger is not necessarily强制 but rather a subtle creeping toward a conception of flourishing shaped by particular class or cultural assumptions—what counts as a valuable pursuit may reflect the preferences of those who write the social policies. An account of flourishing-based equality needs robust mechanisms for democratic deliberation that genuinely incorporate diverse perspectives rather than simply importing elite assumptions about the good life.

Second, Sypnowich gestures toward cultivating responsibility and encouraging people to become "better choosers," yet if choices are thoroughly shaped by social context, the meaning of responsibility becomes elusive. The essay would benefit from distinguishing among different senses of responsibility—moral, practical, and political—rather than conflating them. We might reasonably hold people to commitments they actually make while still recognizing that those commitments arise from social circumstances we can and should address. This would provide a more defensible foundation for the responsibility-attention that Sypnowich rightly retrieves from the luck egalitarian tradition without endorsing its harshness.

Sypnowich's emphasis on solidarity offers the most promising path forward. Her observation that opportunity-talk sidesteps "the responsibility members of an egalitarian society may have to their fellows" names a genuine gap in liberal egalitarianism. The Rawlsian framework, for all its sophistication, tends to treat justice as a matter of institutions rather than relationships. Yet moving from the recognition that we are social beings to the claim that "the egalitarian ideal requires socialism" requires more argument than the essay provides. One can accept that equality involves community and solidarity without concluding that these require the abolition of private property or the central planning associated with historical socialism. Democratic socialism, market socialism, and robust welfare state approaches all incorporate solidarity while differing on institutional design. The flourishing ideal would benefit from engaging more directly with these institutional questions rather than treating socialism as a conclusion reached once we reject opportunity-talk.

Finally, the flourishing approach raises questions about political strategy. Sypnowich is surely right that a polity committed to genuine equality cannot simply present people with options and wash its hands of what follows. But the history of progressive reform suggests that such commitments often prove vulnerable to capture by expert administrators who define flourishing in technocratic terms disconnected from democratic contestation. A flourishing-based egalitarianism needs to specify not merely that people should flourish but who gets to decide what flourishing means and through what democratic processes those determinations can be revised. Without such mechanisms, the flourishing ideal risks becoming another guise for expert-driven paternalism, however benevolently intended.

In the end, Sypnowich is right that equal opportunity is insufficient. Her essay should prompt further inquiry into what an equality of flourishing would actually require—including difficult questions about pluralism, responsibility, institutional design, and democratic accountability that the flourishing perspective must address if it is to replace rather than merely supplement opportunity-focused egalitarianism.

**The Radical Promise of Liberal Egalitarianism**

Christine Sypnowich's case for an outcome-focused, flourishing-based egalitarianism is bracing, but it rests on a caricature of the tradition it sets out to displace. Read generously, liberal egalitarianism is not a thin doctrine of removing barriers and stepping back; it is a demanding theory whose implementation would transform the economic and social structures of contemporary capitalism beyond recognition. Sypnowich's argument lands only if we accept her relatively impoverished portrait of what Rawls, Dworkin, and their successors have actually been arguing about.

Consider Rawls. His two principles do not amount to a program of equal opportunity in the colloquial sense. Fair equality of opportunity is the *second* principle's first clause, and it is itself qualified by the difference principle. The first principle, lexically prior, guarantees a set of basic liberties whose fair value must be maintained — meaning that wealth cannot be permitted to corrupt political equality. Taken seriously, Rawls's framework rules out private financing of elections, requires very substantial limits on inheritance, and probably requires what he later called a "property-owning democracy" or liberal socialism. The difference principle itself demands that inequalities benefit the worst-off; in a world where, as Sypnowich rightly observes, vast disparities of wealth coexist with stagnant mobility, a Rawlsian audit of existing institutions would call for enormous redistribution. The fact that Rawls "permits" inequalities in principle does not mean his theory permits the inequalities we actually have. It almost certainly does not.

The same applies to Dworkin. The hypothetical insurance market that grounds his equality of resources is a thought experiment whose policy implications include something like a universal basic capital endowment, robust public provision against disability and unemployment, and steeply progressive taxation. Dworkin is not a defender of laissez-faire markets; he is a defender of *counterfactual* markets corrected for the unequal starting points and unchosen contingencies that real markets enshrine.

Sypnowich treats these positions as compatible with significant inequality and therefore as wanting. But "significant inequality" relative to a fully-implemented Rawlsian or Dworkinian society would mean differences in resources and opportunities so much smaller than current ones that the political distance between liberal egalitarianism and her preferred socialism is considerably narrower than her essay suggests. The disagreement is real, but it is not the disagreement between a complacent liberalism that washes its hands and a radical egalitarianism that gets serious about outcomes.

A second concern is that Sypnowich underestimates the moral significance of what she calls "neutrality." Liberal egalitarians did not arrive at neutrality through carelessness about flourishing; they arrived at it through hard reflection on what happens when states get into the business of ranking ways of life. The track record is grim. States that have undertaken to promote "flourishing" have promoted particular sectarian, class, and gender visions of it. They have done so with confidence that they were liberating their citizens from "cultures of fatalism, low expectations, self-abnegation, or limited horizons" — phrases that, with all due respect, could have been lifted from any number of paternalist tracts of the past two centuries directed at the poor, at women, at colonized peoples, at religious minorities. Sypnowich gives us a footnote to the worry, conceding plurality, but does not seriously engage with the historical sociology of perfectionist government.

Her example — compulsory music education — is revealing precisely because it is so charming and so seemingly innocuous. Of course music is valuable. But who decides which music? The Western art tradition that European education ministries have historically promoted? The "great works" that, as it happens, correlate with bourgeois cultural capital and were used as a sorting mechanism for educational and occupational stratification? The flourishing view at this level is no longer simply about equality; it is about authorizing a community to identify and embed substantive ideals of the good in coercive institutions. We may be willing to accept that authorization in some cases — public health is the obvious one — but we ought to be vigilant about extending it, and the appeal to "transparent and democratic" methods is not, by itself, a sufficient safeguard. Democracies promote bad conceptions of flourishing all the time.

Third, Sypnowich's repudiation of luck egalitarianism is too quick. The point of distinguishing choice from circumstance is not to underwrite a sink-or-swim ethic; it is to identify what egalitarian justice *requires* as distinct from what humanitarianism, solidarity, or community spirit also support. Dworkin and Cohen were not denying that we should care about people who suffer because of their own choices. They were saying that there is a particular kind of unfairness — being worse off through no fault of one's own — that justice in the strict sense addresses, and that other kinds of suffering are addressed by other moral resources. Collapsing these distinctions does not give us more equality; it gives us a less articulated moral vocabulary. We need to be able to say both that a person who has gambled away her savings is owed something by her community *and* that this is not the same kind of claim as that of a person born with a debilitating illness.

Fourth, the flourishing framework as Sypnowich describes it is curiously thin where it should be thick. We are told that the egalitarian community will be "proactive," using "incentives and supporting institutions," that it will "revisit our policies" when outcomes diverge, that it will involve "compulsory arts and music education, of the right kind." But the hardest questions in political philosophy are: which institutions, on what authority, accountable to whom, and what happens when citizens, exercising their democratic agency, reject the community's verdicts about what flourishing requires? Liberal egalitarianism's commitment to neutrality is not a refusal to think about these questions; it is a considered answer to them. One can disagree with that answer — many do — but it cannot be set aside by gesturing at solidarity and Marx.

Finally, I want to register a methodological complaint. Sypnowich invokes Marx, Tawney, Morris, and the socialist tradition as the source of a more generous egalitarian vision. But the strongest socialist arguments against liberal egalitarianism have generally targeted its *structural* assumptions — the persistence of wage labor, the commodification of labor power, the political domination of capital — rather than its *attitudinal* failure to insist on outcome equality. G. A. Cohen's late work is a case in point: his critique of Rawls is largely about the difference principle's complicity with incentive-driven inequality, not about its agnosticism regarding flourishing. There is a robust socialist argument to be made against contemporary liberal egalitarianism, but it is principally an argument about political economy. By relocating the disagreement to the terrain of opportunity-versus-outcome and neutrality-versus-perfectionism, Sypnowich obscures rather than illuminates what is really at stake.

None of this is to defend the cramped, mobility-talk version of equal opportunity that pervades centrist political rhetoric. Sypnowich is entirely right that Bernstein's and Trudeau's invocations of opportunity are anemic, and that they often serve to license the inequalities they purport to address. But the answer is not to abandon opportunity as a category; it is to take the more demanding versions of equal opportunity seriously, push their institutional implications all the way down, and recognize that doing so requires confronting the structural inequalities of capitalist economies. Liberal egalitarianism, fully realized, is a far more radical doctrine than its critics — and, frankly, many of its defenders — have allowed. We should not leave its radical potential on the table.

**Flourishing for Whom?**

Christine Sypnowich is right that the dominant rhetoric of equal opportunity functions today as an alibi for inequality. She is also right that political philosophy in the Rawlsian tradition has, in important ways, conceded too much ground to a particular notion of responsibility that flatters the well-off and disciplines the disadvantaged. But her proposed alternative — equality of flourishing, with the state authorized to cultivate citizens' capacities and inculcate worthwhile pursuits — should give democratic socialists pause. The trouble is not that flourishing is the wrong goal. The trouble is the agent she imagines bringing it about.

Let me put the worry directly. Sypnowich repeatedly invokes "the egalitarian community" as the entity that proactively promotes the valuable, judges what counts as a worthwhile pursuit, intervenes when people fail to take up opportunities to flourish, and revises its conception of flourishing through "legitimate, democratic debate." This is, at best, a wager. In the world we actually live in, "the community" that would be empowered to identify flourishing and intervene to secure it is the existing state — capitalist, racialized, patriarchal, and shot through with the very class divisions that Sypnowich elsewhere blames for warping citizens' choices. Why would such a state, if granted perfectionist powers, suddenly become a vehicle for liberation rather than a more efficient instrument for the reproduction of existing hierarchies?

History offers some salutary cautions. Compulsory education has been used to flourish children, yes, but also to break Indigenous languages, to instill industrial discipline, and to enforce gender norms. Public arts funding has subsidized the canon of dominant cultures while marginalizing the expressive forms of subordinated groups. Welfare states have promoted the "deserving poor" by requiring particular family arrangements, particular work patterns, particular respectabilities. Each of these projects was justified, in its day, by appeal to flourishing — to lifting the masses out of cultural privation, equipping them for civilized life, opening their horizons. We should not pretend that the perfectionist impulse is innocent simply because we hold it.

Sypnowich's response, I take it, would be that *her* community would do better — that it would be democratic, transparent, pluralistic, attentive to a diversity of forms of flourishing. Fine. But this raises the obvious question: what institutional design makes a perfectionist state more, rather than less, likely to slip into the patterns just described? And here her essay is almost completely silent. We get music education as an example. We do not get a serious discussion of who decides, by what procedure, with what protections for dissenters, and what happens when democratic majorities turn out to favor conceptions of flourishing that exclude or coerce minorities.

This is not a small omission. Liberal neutrality was not born of indifference; it was born of bitter experience with sectarian and ideological states. When Rawls insists that the political community should not weigh in on rival conceptions of the good, he is drawing a lesson from religious warfare, from the policing of sexuality, from the long history of states using the language of virtue to dominate their subjects. We may have left the worst of that behind, but the impulse to use coercive institutions to "improve" people is alive and well — visible in everything from welfare conditionality to anti-trans legislation justified in the name of child welfare to anti-immigration rhetoric about cultural integrity. The neutrality principle is a fragile but precious gain. To toss it aside in the name of solidarity, without specifying what will replace it, is reckless.

There is a deeper issue here. Sypnowich's argument operates at a level of abstraction that obscures the political question of whose conception of flourishing wins. She tells us that flourishing comes in many forms, that we should disincentivize "other forms of life," that we should "rule out promoting" certain things. Which? Subsistence agriculture? Religious orthodoxy? Voluntary poverty? Choosing not to participate in the labor market? Hobbies that produce nothing of recognized cultural value? Forms of relationship that the dominant culture finds inscrutable? In the absence of a worked-out account, the answers will be filled in by whoever holds power. And the empirical track record suggests they will be filled in to disadvantage exactly those groups that egalitarians claim to care about.

A different version of this worry concerns the disabled. The flourishing framework, particularly in its Aristotelian and Marxian variants, tends to privilege the active, productive, contributing life. Sypnowich endorses this: "one's life goes better if one is contributing to the community"; well-being involves the life of "all-round development" extolled by Marx. Disability theorists have spent decades pushing back against precisely this kind of ranking, which has historically been used to justify warehousing, sterilization, and worse. A flourishing framework that confidently ranks ways of life — that says some are valuable and others are not, that envisions a community proactively cultivating the productive virtues — sits uneasily with the recognition that there are radically different ways of being human and that the egalitarian project must accommodate all of them.

I want to suggest that the real lesson of Sypnowich's critique cuts in a different direction than she proposes. The problem with opportunity talk in actually existing politics is not that it focuses on opportunity rather than outcomes; it is that the "opportunities" supposedly equalized are nothing of the sort. Schools are unequally funded. Health care is unequally available. Housing is unequally secure. Wealth is grotesquely concentrated. Political power tracks wealth. Labor markets are structured by deeply unequal bargaining power. When centrist politicians invoke equal opportunity, they typically mean something like formal non-discrimination, which is wholly inadequate to dealing with these substantive inequalities. The remedy is not to switch metrics from opportunity to flourishing; it is to redistribute the material conditions of opportunity until they actually become equal in any meaningful sense.

Doing so would, in fact, require something like socialism — but a socialism organized around democratic ownership, workplace democracy, decommodified provision of housing, health, education, and so on, rather than around a perfectionist state that identifies and inculcates the right ways to live. The flourishing that resulted would emerge from the choices of free and equal citizens, materially empowered to pursue lives of their own design. That is, I think, what most people who use the language of flourishing actually want. The danger in Sypnowich's framing is that it makes the cultivating state, rather than the redistributive economy, the central instrument of egalitarian transformation.

To put it sharply: I want the means of subsistence and the conditions of meaningful participation to be unconditionally guaranteed, and I want the state to keep its opinions about my hobbies to itself. This is not a flight from solidarity; it is a recognition that solidarity is best expressed through shared material institutions, not through collective judgments about whose music lessons matter. Sypnowich has identified a real failing in contemporary egalitarian thought. But the answer is more, and more militant, equality of resources — not the substitution of opportunity by a state-led project of cultivating the good life.

**The Sociology Is Where the Argument Should Be**

Reading Sypnowich's essay, I kept wanting to reach across the page and pull her toward the empirical territory she keeps gesturing at but never quite occupies. She is right that opportunity-talk has become an ideological lubricant for inequality. She is right that the choice/circumstance distinction is blurry. She is right that the social production of preferences should make us skeptical of "from-the-inside" defenses of unequal outcomes. But the argument that follows from these correct observations is not principally a philosophical argument about flourishing versus opportunity. It is a sociological argument about what equality of opportunity, even minimally construed, would actually require — and the answer turns out to be enormous.

Consider what we know about social mobility in the United States and the United Kingdom. The data of the past fifteen years, much of it produced by Raj Chetty's team and by economists in Europe, has demonstrated with extraordinary granularity that the place a child is born — the neighborhood, the school catchment, the labor market they enter — predicts their later income, health, marriage rates, and life expectancy with disturbing precision. The Great Gatsby Curve shows that more unequal countries have less intergenerational mobility. Studies of "moving to opportunity" experiments show that children who move from high-poverty to lower-poverty neighborhoods at young enough ages see substantial gains in adult outcomes. The conclusion that emerges, again and again, is that *opportunity itself is not equalized*, in any serious sense, in contemporary rich democracies. Children of similar talents face radically different prospects depending on the accidents of their birth.

This matters for Sypnowich's argument in two ways. First, it suggests that the contrast she draws between "equality of opportunity" and "equality of flourishing" is, in practice, much narrower than the philosophical literature implies. Genuinely equalizing opportunity would require interventions of breathtaking scope: housing desegregation, comprehensive early childhood education, equal school funding regardless of geography, universal health care, robust labor unions, restrictions on inherited wealth, financial security adequate to allow children to be raised without chronic stress, and the elimination of the parental income premium in college admissions and labor markets. If we did all of this — and we are nowhere close — we would have a vastly more equal society than we have now. The fact that residual differences in flourishing would remain is, at this point, an extremely abstract worry. We are not in danger of running out of equality-of-opportunity work to do.

Second, the empirical literature complicates Sypnowich's central claim that an outcome-focused approach is what is really needed. The most successful egalitarian policies of the postwar period — the GI Bill, the expansion of public higher education, the National Health Service, social democratic housing programs in Scandinavia — were predominantly framed and justified in opportunity-and-resource terms, not in flourishing terms. They produced substantial gains in equality of life outcomes. Conversely, the policies that have most explicitly invoked flourishing — temperance movements, certain education-reform crusades, parts of the welfare-to-work agenda — have often been the most paternalistic and have frequently failed on their own terms. There is a pragmatic case for sticking with opportunity and resource language even if one is, deep down, a flourishing theorist.

Sypnowich would likely respond that what we need is the strong version of opportunity *plus* a substantive concern with how people actually live. Fair enough. But notice how much of the argumentative work is then being done by the "strong version of opportunity" part, and how little by the additional flourishing component. The flourishing component, when she develops it, comes down to a small number of examples: music education, mental health, friendship and love, the William Morris point about beauty. These are real and important. But they are not, in current political conditions, where the action is. The action is in the basic distribution of income, wealth, housing, health, and education — and in the political power that tracks them.

There is a related issue about how flourishing should be measured. Sypnowich does not propose a metric, but the candidates in the literature — capabilities, subjective well-being, objective lists of valuable functionings — each face well-known difficulties. Capabilities, as Nussbaum's own version concedes, tends to converge with opportunity-talk once we take seriously the distinction between functioning and the capability to function. Subjective well-being is famously plastic: adaptive preferences and hedonic adaptation mean that people often report being satisfied with conditions we should not accept. Objective list approaches require an account of which goods belong on the list, which puts us back in the perfectionist thicket. None of these are knock-down problems, but they suggest that the operationalization of "equal flourishing" as a guide to policy is harder than the philosophical argument lets on.

I also want to push back on Sypnowich's claim that opportunity-talk "sidelines community." This may be true of certain libertarian uses of the concept, but it is not a feature of the more substantive variants. Consider Nordic social democracy, the most successful approximation of equal opportunity in modern history. It rests on extraordinarily high levels of social trust, dense civic institutions, strong labor movements, and a thick sense of collective responsibility. The Nordic countries are not perfectionist in Sypnowich's sense — they do not generally try to inculcate particular conceptions of the good — and yet they are paradigmatically solidaristic. Solidarity, it turns out, does not require that the state get into the business of ranking ways of life. It requires shared institutions that materially express collective concern.

Where Sypnowich's argument does land, I think, is on the specific question of mental health, loneliness, and non-material flourishing. She is right that these are pressing problems and that conventional opportunity frameworks do not address them well. But I am not sure that the solution lies in shifting to a flourishing metric. It lies, rather, in expanding what we count as social infrastructure: libraries, parks, community centers, mental health services, time off from work, walkable neighborhoods. These are material provisions whose effects include the cultivation of non-material goods. They can be defended in opportunity-and-resource terms (people lack the resources to flourish without them) just as well as in flourishing terms.

There is, in the end, a temperamental difference here. Sypnowich is drawn to the bold philosophical claim — that we must abandon opportunity and embrace outcomes — because it sounds like a serious rupture with the politics of the center-left. I am inclined to think that what the center-left needs is less of a philosophical rupture and more of an empirical and political confrontation with how non-equal opportunity actually is, and with what it would actually take to equalize it. The slogan of the next decade should not be "equality of flourishing" rather than "equality of opportunity." It should be: "equality of opportunity — and we mean it this time."

This is not the boldest answer, but I think it is the right one. Sypnowich's diagnosis of the rhetoric is sharp. Her sociological observations are largely correct. Her prescriptions, however, lead us away from where the political and moral leverage actually is. The case for socialism, if it is to be made, will not be made by displacing opportunity with flourishing. It will be made by showing that capitalist societies cannot deliver on the equality of opportunity they purport to value.

**A Defense of Responsibility, Even Now**

I find myself in the awkward position of a sympathetic skeptic. I share Sypnowich's view that contemporary inequality is morally intolerable and that the dominant political rhetoric of opportunity is largely a sham. But her wholesale dismissal of choice and responsibility as ethical categories goes further than the evidence warrants, and I think it misjudges both the philosophical terrain and the political moment. Responsibility belongs in any plausible egalitarianism, including a socialist one, and the trick is to integrate it rightly, not to dissolve it into circumstance.

Begin with the philosophical issue. Sypnowich's argument against the choice/circumstance distinction proceeds by emphasizing how circumstances shape choices. This is true and important. But it does not show what she seems to think it shows. From the fact that our choices are conditioned by social factors, it does not follow that they cease to be ours, or that ascriptions of responsibility are mistaken. The compatibilist tradition in philosophy — Hume, Mill, Frankfurt, Dennett, and many others — has spent centuries explaining how meaningful agency and responsibility coexist with causal determination. Our actions are produced by our characters, which are in turn produced by our upbringings and circumstances. So what? The same is true of every conscious being. If this counts as undermining responsibility, then responsibility is undermined everywhere and the concept does no work anywhere — including in Sypnowich's own characterization of what an egalitarian community will and will not do.

Note that her essay actually relies on responsibility throughout. She wants us to "enable people to choose well, to become better choosers, to take responsibility." She wants the lucky rich to take responsibility for their fellow citizens. She wants citizens to participate in democratic deliberation about what counts as flourishing. None of this makes sense unless agents are capable of being held to standards, of being addressed as the authors of their conduct. The point is not that her view secretly contradicts itself; the point is that responsibility is ineliminable from the moral vocabulary, and the question is not whether to use it but how.

The luck egalitarian tradition has a real insight here, even if it has sometimes been pressed into ungenerous service. The insight is that a society of equals is not one in which every disparity is dissolved into compensation. It is one in which people are treated as agents — given the resources, opportunities, and respect appropriate to beings who direct their own lives. To refuse, on principle, to recognize anyone as responsible for their outcomes is to refuse to take them seriously as agents. It is, paradoxically, anti-egalitarian: it treats some people as moral patients rather than as moral peers. This was Anderson's point about the demeaning character of luck egalitarianism in some of its forms, but it cuts both ways. Anderson's "democratic equality" still insists that citizens are responsible for their choices; she just thinks egalitarian justice should secure the social bases of agency rather than tracking choices in fine detail.

Sypnowich's flourishing approach, taken to its logical conclusion, threatens to occupy the unattractive end of this spectrum. If we say that unequal outcomes always signal a need for remedy, we have committed ourselves to treating people's actual choices as failures of community provision. The person who chooses a life of contemplation over a life of productivity, the person who declines to take up music lessons, the person who pursues a relationship the community thinks unwise — all of these become projects for egalitarian intervention. This is not respect; it is supervision.

This brings me to the political worry. Sypnowich's argument lands in a moment when democratic publics in the rich world are deeply suspicious of elite paternalism. The right has weaponized this suspicion to devastating effect. To respond by embracing a philosophical framework that explicitly authorizes the state to identify and promote correct conceptions of flourishing — and to do so against people's expressed preferences — is to walk straight into a political trap. The point is not that public authorities should never promote anything substantive. They obviously must and do. The point is that the more such promotion can be defended in opportunity-and-resource terms, the more politically robust it will be. Universal music lessons are easy to defend as the equalization of access; compulsory music education aimed at correcting "limited horizons" is much harder. The first is a gift; the second is a sneer.

There is a deeper conceptual problem too. Sypnowich's account of agency is, in places, oddly thin. She says that "personhood is a dynamic process in which nurture and nature, circumstance and choice, are mutually constitutive," which is true but also tells us almost nothing about how to think about responsibility in practice. The genuine philosophical question is not whether the self is socially constituted — of course it is — but how socially constituted agents can be the bearers of normative claims, expectations, and reciprocal demands. The luck egalitarians, for all their faults, have tried to give answers. Sypnowich gestures at the question and moves on.

I want to suggest that the better response to the inadequacies of luck egalitarianism is not to abandon responsibility but to socialize it. By this I mean: we should ask what kind of society makes the exercise of responsibility actually possible and meaningful. A person crushed by precarity, lacking time, health, education, or security, is not exercising responsibility when she makes a poor financial choice; she is being acted upon by structures. The way to honor responsibility is to build the conditions under which it can be genuinely exercised: secure housing, decent work, accessible education, real political voice. In such a society, what people choose with the time and resources they have is genuinely their own, and the residual differences in their outcomes can be largely accepted by the community without injustice.

This is not so different from what some progressive luck egalitarians have argued, but it inflects the project differently. It places the emphasis on what we owe each other in the way of preconditions of agency, rather than on either compensating for past misfortunes or cultivating particular forms of life. It allows for substantial inequalities in outcome — some people will work harder, take different risks, pursue different goods — while ensuring that those inequalities track genuine choices rather than systemic disadvantage. And it does not require the state to take a position on whether music is more valuable than gardening or whether contribution to community is more flourishing than solitary contemplation.

Finally, I want to register a worry about the rhetoric of "the lucky rich." Sypnowich is right that egalitarian discussions have paid too little attention to the obligations of the well-off. But framing this in terms of luck imports the wrong frame. The wealthy in contemporary societies are not lucky; they are largely the beneficiaries of structures that systematically funnel resources upward — tax systems, property regimes, financial institutions, political processes. The critique should be structural, not moral. The rich do not owe the rest a portion of their luck; they owe the rest the structural rents they are extracting. That is a stronger claim, with better political legs, than the gentler charge that they should share their good fortune.

So: responsibility yes, with the conditions of its meaningful exercise placed at the center of the egalitarian project. Flourishing yes, as the diffuse aim that animates the project. But state cultivation of flourishing, with responsibility relegated to a sociologically conditioned afterthought? I cannot follow Sypnowich there. The moral and political risks are too high, and the philosophical case is weaker than she makes out.

**The Missing Politics of Production**

Christine Sypnowich's essay reaches its rousing conclusion with the assertion that "the egalitarian ideal requires socialism." I agree — but the essay arriving at that conclusion has surprisingly little to say about the one thing socialists have historically taken to be central, which is the organization of production. The argument turns almost entirely on consumption and cultivation: what people have, what they do with what they have, what the community helps them become. The site of work, of class relations, of who controls the means of production — these appear only glancingly. This is a curious omission in a socialist argument, and I think it leads Sypnowich into a vision of egalitarianism that is at once too statist and too detached from the social struggles that have historically driven egalitarian gains.

Consider the question of where flourishing actually comes from. For most people in capitalist societies, the largest single block of waking time is spent at work. The texture of that work — whether it is autonomous or supervised, varied or repetitive, secure or precarious, dignified or humiliating, well-paid or impoverishing — is the most consequential single determinant of their flourishing or lack of it. A society in which workers are powerless, surveilled, and casualized will not be a flourishing society no matter what music lessons the state provides. Conversely, a society in which workers exercise meaningful control over their working lives generates flourishing as a by-product of its basic structures, without requiring perfectionist intervention.

This is the socialist insight that goes back at least to Marx's account of alienation: that the central question for human flourishing is not what consumption goods or cultural opportunities the state provides, but whether human beings can recognize their own activity as their own — whether they relate to their labor as self-expression rather than as compulsion. To the extent that capitalism systematically denies most workers this relation, no amount of compensatory provision in the consumption sphere can produce equal flourishing. Conversely, transforming the relations of production toward workers' control, cooperative ownership, and democratic management would, at a stroke, address many of the non-material shortfalls Sypnowich identifies — alienation, loneliness, lack of meaningful contribution — far more effectively than perfectionist cultivation could.

I want to suggest, then, that the real socialist critique of liberal egalitarianism is not principally that it focuses on opportunity rather than outcomes. It is that liberal egalitarianism, even in its most ambitious forms, accepts as given a basic separation between politics and economics, between citizenship and worker-hood, that allows a redistributive state to coexist with capitalist relations of production. The result is a society in which redistributive politics is permanently embattled by the structural power of capital and in which the basic experience of work for most people remains unfree. Outcome equality, in this institutional setting, can only be approached through ever-more-elaborate state interventions — including, perhaps, the perfectionist interventions Sypnowich describes. The deeper alternative is to transform the relations of production so that egalitarian outcomes emerge from the structure itself rather than being imposed upon it.

This shift in framing has several advantages over Sypnowich's. First, it does not require the egalitarian community to take positions on which conceptions of the good life are correct. Worker control of enterprises does not entail any view about whether one should take music lessons. It just transforms the conditions under which people decide for themselves. Second, it identifies a concrete political agent — the working class, broadly construed, including all those who must sell their labor to live — rather than the abstract "egalitarian community" whose composition and accountability Sypnowich leaves vague. Third, it connects egalitarianism to the historical struggles that have actually produced egalitarian gains: trade unionism, cooperative movements, public ownership campaigns, the welfare state. These struggles have been about power and resources, not about the cultivation of taste.

Sypnowich's framework, by contrast, places enormous weight on the state's role as cultivator. The "egalitarian community" must promote valuable pursuits, intervene against fatalism and low horizons, revisit its own conceptions of flourishing, judge that some forms of life fall short. This is a great deal of work for a state to do, and it requires that the state be both very capable and very trustworthy. In actually existing capitalism, the state is neither. It is dependent on capital for tax revenue, constrained by international economic competition, captured at multiple points by organized wealth, and burdened with the policing of class relations rather than their transformation. To ask such a state to undertake the cultivation of flourishing is to ask the wrong agent to do the wrong job.

There is also a more specifically Marxist worry. The cultivation of flourishing is the kind of project that historically has appealed to socialist movements *in lieu* of structural transformation. The Fabians, the social democratic parties of the postwar period, and many strands of European Christian democracy all embraced something like a flourishing agenda — improving the moral and cultural condition of the working class, expanding access to high culture, promoting healthy lifestyles — in ways that often coexisted comfortably with capitalist property relations. The result was a partial humanization of capitalism rather than its supersession. This is not a criticism of those movements, which secured many real gains, but it is a caution against thinking that flourishing-talk is somehow more radical than redistributive talk. In some respects, it is less.

I am also struck by what Sypnowich's framework leaves out of the conception of human flourishing itself. She mentions music, friendship, love, William Morris's beautiful world, all-around development. These are real and important. But the most distinctive form of flourishing that a socialist tradition can offer is the experience of collective self-determination: of belonging to a community that has actually taken control of the conditions of its own life, of participating in the deliberate shaping of social institutions. This is the flourishing that emerges from democratic agency rather than being delivered by a cultivating state. It is what the Paris Commune, the Spanish anarchist collectives, the Yugoslav self-management experiments, and a hundred lesser experiments in cooperative and democratic organization were trying to produce. It is also what is most thoroughly missing from contemporary politics, including the politics of liberal egalitarianism.

So my response to Sypnowich is: yes to flourishing, yes to socialism, but let us locate the project where socialist tradition has long located it — in the transformation of the relations of production and in the expansion of democratic self-determination, not in the cultivating activities of a benevolent state. The shift in question is not from opportunity to outcomes; it is from a politics of redistribution within capitalism to a politics of democratic transformation of capitalism. Those are different things, and the conflation of them has been one of the persistent confusions of the recent egalitarian literature.

If the egalitarian community is to do its proactive work, the first piece of that work should be the constitution of the community itself as a self-governing political body capable of acting on the economy. That is the precondition of everything else. Without it, the choice between flourishing and opportunity is a debate about which kind of paternalism we prefer.

**The Care That Liberal Egalitarianism Forgets**

Christine Sypnowich's essay does important work by drawing attention to the non-material dimensions of inequality — loneliness, mental ill-health, the lack of friendship and love that material redistribution alone cannot address. But she does not push this insight as far as it can go, and in failing to push it she leaves intact a set of assumptions that feminist scholarship has been challenging for decades. The deepest failing of the opportunity framework is not its neglect of flourishing in general; it is its neglect of the relational, dependent, and care-laden nature of human life. To put it sharply: equality of opportunity is built around a fictional figure — the unencumbered, productive, choosing adult — and no amount of outcome-talk can fix the problem unless we also rebuild the framework around the actual human beings the fiction excludes.

The fiction in question is well-known. Liberal political philosophy, from Locke to Rawls to Dworkin, has tended to take as its model citizen an adult capable of full economic and political participation: someone with the time, capacities, and freedom from dependency-work to enter the public sphere as an equal. This figure is, of course, historically the bourgeois man, made possible by the unwaged caring labor of women, servants, and slaves. Feminist theorists from Carole Pateman to Susan Moller Okin to Eva Kittay have documented how this exclusion shapes the entire architecture of liberal theory, including its conceptions of equality. Equality of opportunity, in particular, has tended to mean the equal chance to compete in spheres — labor markets, public office, professional careers — that are themselves organized around the assumption that competitors do not have substantial caring responsibilities. Women's gains under this framework have come largely by becoming able to act more like the bourgeois man, often by offloading care onto other, more marginalized women.

Sypnowich is broadly sympathetic to feminist concerns, I believe. But her flourishing framework does not quite confront this issue. She wants the egalitarian community to cultivate valuable pursuits and promote contribution. The implicit model of contribution is, again, productive activity — work, art, music, "all-round development." Care, however, is barely visible. Yet care is the indispensable form of human activity without which no flourishing of any kind is possible. Children must be cared for. The sick and disabled must be cared for. The elderly must be cared for. We must care for each other in countless quotidian ways. And the people doing this work — disproportionately women, disproportionately poor, disproportionately racialized — are precisely those whose flourishing is most systematically compromised by both capitalism and the state's organization of welfare.

A flourishing-focused egalitarianism that took care seriously would look quite different from the one Sypnowich sketches. It would not focus principally on what the community provides to individuals (music lessons, employment opportunities, cultural goods) but on how the community organizes the basic work of human reproduction. It would ask: how is caring labor distributed across genders, classes, races? How is it compensated, valued, and supported? How does the structure of work, the housing market, the school day, the welfare system either enable or undermine the practices of care on which everyone depends? These questions cut across the opportunity/outcome distinction in interesting ways. They are not principally about distributing means to ends; they are about the social organization of dependency.

Joan Tronto and others have argued that care should be seen as a fundamental political category — that a just society is one organized around the responsibility to care for, and be cared for by, others. From this perspective, both equality of opportunity and equality of flourishing, as typically formulated, miss the point. Opportunity-talk assumes a competitive sphere with care as a private background; flourishing-talk asks what the community provides for its members but tends to leave the care-relations themselves unexamined. The richer alternative is to ask how the basic political community can organize itself so that care is justly distributed, well-supported, and recognized as constitutive of citizenship.

This has practical implications that I do not see in Sypnowich's framework. A genuinely egalitarian society would have things like universal child care; long, well-paid, gender-equal parental leave; a shorter standard work week to make room for care responsibilities; public support for elder care that does not pauperize families; recognition and compensation for care work performed in the home. It would treat the demand to care as a right shared by all citizens, not an obligation imposed on some. These policies do not flow naturally from either the opportunity framework or the flourishing framework as Sypnowich articulates them; they flow from an analysis of care as a primary site of inequality.

There is another aspect of this that I want to bring out. Sypnowich emphasizes solidarity and community, which is welcome. But the solidarity she invokes is somewhat abstract — citizens of an egalitarian community recognizing their stake in each other's flourishing, members of a polity caring about each other's lives. This is fine as far as it goes. The relations that actually constitute people's lives, however, are typically more particular: family relations, neighborhood relations, friendship networks, religious congregations, workplace solidarities. These are the sites where care happens, where flourishing is sustained or undermined, where loneliness is alleviated or deepened. An egalitarian politics that does not strengthen and democratize these intermediate relations will not produce equal flourishing, no matter how much state cultivation it engages in.

This is not an argument for retreating from politics into communitarianism. It is an argument for a politics that takes the social ecology of care seriously — that asks what kind of policy framework allows the dense, particular, relational sources of human flourishing to thrive across class and gender lines, rather than being eroded by market pressure or undermined by paternalist intervention. The dichotomy between liberal individualism and perfectionist statism that structures Sypnowich's essay misses the territory in between, which is where most actual care, and most actual flourishing, happens.

I want to flag one more point. Sypnowich's example of compulsory arts education is meant to be charming and obvious. From a feminist perspective, it is neither. Decisions about what counts as worthwhile cultivation have historically been deeply gendered. The "valuable pursuits" promoted in nineteenth- and twentieth-century educational systems — classical music, certain forms of literature, particular sports — were the cultural capital of bourgeois masculinity. Domestic crafts, oral traditions, women's spiritual practices, and forms of expression rooted in subordinated communities were systematically devalued, often explicitly so. A flourishing framework that licenses the community to identify the valuable runs a serious risk of reinscribing these hierarchies under egalitarian colors. The history of "improving" the working class and "civilizing" women looms behind every confident assertion that we know which pursuits are worth promoting.

So: yes to attention to non-material flourishing, yes to a deep critique of the productive, choice-centered citizen of liberal egalitarianism, yes to a recognition that loneliness and care matter for justice. But the project these "yeses" point to is not principally one of state cultivation of valuable pursuits. It is one of reconstructing the basic political community around the recognition of human interdependence — around care as a shared responsibility and a basic source of dignity. That project does not require us to choose between opportunity and outcomes. It requires us to question the picture of the citizen on which both frameworks have implicitly relied.

If we want equal flourishing, in any sense that does not reproduce existing exclusions, we need to start by asking who is doing the work that makes anyone's flourishing possible, and how that work might be justly organized. That question is not on Sypnowich's agenda. It should be on ours.

**Against the Cultivating State**

Christine Sypnowich's argument has the shape of an irresistible philosophical inference: since choices are socially conditioned, choices cannot ground responsibility; since opportunity is realized only in actual flourishing, equality must be measured by flourishing rather than by opportunity; since neutrality is a fiction, the state must take a substantive view of the good. From these moves, she concludes that the egalitarian community must proactively cultivate the flourishing of its members, intervening against limited horizons, fatalism, and unworthy pursuits. The conclusion follows from the premises, more or less, but the premises are doing more work than meets the eye. Each of them sounds harmless in isolation. Together, they license a cultivating state whose powers and ambitions go well beyond what any liberal democracy currently authorizes, and well beyond what the historical record suggests is wise.

I want to defend, against this drift, a deflated but I hope still serious commitment to something like state neutrality — not because I have any illusion that neutrality is fully achievable or that any state is wholly innocent of substantive judgments about the good, but because the demand for neutrality is a *constraint* that disciplines the use of state power in ways the cultivating state will lack.

Begin with the choice/circumstance argument. It is true that the line between chosen and unchosen is blurry. But blurry lines do not authorize the abolition of the categories they separate. The line between adulthood and childhood is also blurry, but no one concludes from this that the distinction should be abandoned. We use blurry distinctions productively all the time: we make our best judgments in clear cases, treat marginal cases with appropriate humility, and accept that errors will sometimes be made in both directions. The choice/circumstance distinction can be used the same way. Most of us have a reasonably good sense, in our own lives and in the lives of those we know, of when someone has been let down by circumstance and when they have made choices that they themselves would acknowledge as their own. The philosophical impossibility of drawing the line with mathematical precision does not undermine the moral and political utility of the distinction.

Sypnowich's argument here, taken at face value, would license a degree of paternalism that goes well beyond what most defenders of the flourishing approach would endorse. If choices are not really choices because they are socially conditioned, then the state is licensed to override them whenever the state's judgment about flourishing diverges from the citizen's. There is no principled limit to this, since by hypothesis the citizen's preferences are themselves products of social conditioning. The only question is which conditioning we prefer: the unaccountable conditioning of markets and culture, or the deliberate conditioning of democratic institutions. Sypnowich plumps for the latter, but she does not really argue for it; she assumes that "democratic" institutions will produce wiser judgments than the alternatives. Why should we believe this? Democratic politics has produced its share of catastrophes, and a democratic majority that has decided to override the supposedly conditioned preferences of a minority is not obviously a more attractive prospect than the market.

The next move concerns neutrality. Sypnowich is right that the state cannot be perfectly neutral. The state's tax code, education system, criminal law, and zoning regulations all encode substantive judgments about valuable activities. Fine. The question is whether, given the impossibility of perfect neutrality, the state should aspire to as much neutrality as practicable, or should embrace its substantive role with vigor. The deflated neutralist position holds that the state should make its substantive judgments narrow, transparent, and revisable, and should refrain from extending them into areas (religious belief, intimate relationships, aesthetic preferences, broad life-plans) where citizens have legitimate claims to authority over their own lives. The cultivating state Sypnowich describes seems to lack this constraint. It will weigh in on the value of various enterprises, promote some pursuits and disincentivize others, override expressed preferences when they reflect fatalism or limited horizons. Where, exactly, does the cultivating state stop?

This is not a hypothetical worry. Liberal democracies routinely face questions about how far to push their substantive judgments about citizens' lives. Should the state discourage certain religious practices it considers oppressive? Should it intervene in family structures it considers unhealthy? Should it require certain dietary practices on health grounds? Should it discourage certain leisure pursuits it considers degrading? Liberal neutrality, even in its limited form, provides a default against such interventions, requiring a high burden of justification before they are undertaken. The cultivating state inverts this default. It begins from the premise that the community has a stake in citizens' flourishing and proceeds to ask only what means are appropriate to advance that stake. The result is a politics in which the burden of justification falls on the citizen who wishes to be left alone, rather than on the state that wishes to intervene.

Sypnowich's response is that the cultivating state will use only legitimate, non-coercive means: incentives, education, supporting institutions. But this is not a real constraint. Almost all state power operates through incentives, education, and supporting institutions; the question is what the state may do with these instruments. A tax system designed to discourage certain pursuits is coercive in any meaningful sense, even if it does not involve police powers. An education system that compels children to take certain forms of cultivation is coercive of those children's parents. An institutional landscape that systematically privileges certain forms of life over others is shaping the choices available to citizens in ways that are not, on Sypnowich's own analysis, easy to consent to. The distinction between coercion and incentive is much less clean than the essay implies.

There is also a question about who, in actual political practice, will operate the cultivating state. Sypnowich gestures at democratic deliberation, but the heavy lifting will be done by educational bureaucracies, cultural institutions, public health authorities, and welfare agencies — bodies staffed by professionals with their own class and cultural backgrounds, their own conceptions of valuable pursuits, their own blind spots. The cultivating state, in practice, will reflect the substantive judgments of the educated middle class. Its compulsory music lessons will be lessons in the music the educated middle class values; its incentives against fatalism and low horizons will be incentives toward the aspirations the educated middle class endorses. We can call this "democratic" if we like, but it is a particular kind of democratic, in which some citizens' conceptions of flourishing become the standard by which others' lives are judged and shaped.

For these reasons, I think the cultivating state should be approached with great caution. The fact that opportunity-talk does not deliver equal flourishing is a problem, but it is not the worst problem in egalitarian politics. The worst problems involve states that have decided they know what their citizens need. The history of those states is mostly grim.

The better path, I think, is to push redistribution and the equalization of resources further than current liberal egalitarianism does, while preserving as much of the neutrality presumption as possible. Give citizens robust material security, broad access to a wide range of cultural and educational opportunities, real political voice, freedom from domination at work and in their relationships. Then let them work out for themselves what flourishing means. They will not always get it right, in the eyes of those who would have preferred different choices. But the alternative — a state confident enough in its own conception of flourishing to override those of its citizens — is worse than the residual inequalities of taste and aspiration it would correct.

Sypnowich diagnoses a real disease. The cure she proposes risks being worse than the disease.

**Flourishing, Disability, and the Limits of Outcome Equality**

I want to enter this discussion from an angle that Sypnowich's essay does not quite address but that I think tests her framework severely: the question of disability. The history of egalitarian thought about disability has been instructive precisely because it forces clarity about what we are equalizing and why. Sypnowich's commitment to equality of flourishing, as she elaborates it, is in some ways better suited to the realities of disability than competing approaches; in other ways, it sits uneasily with what disability rights movements have demanded over the past several decades. Working through this tension can help us see both the strengths and the limits of her position.

The strength first. Sypnowich is right that purely opportunity-focused frameworks, taken on their own terms, struggle with disability. If equality means simply that the same opportunities are formally available to everyone, then a person with significant disabilities is entitled only to the same opportunities everyone else has, which she may be unable to take up. Substantive frameworks like the capabilities approach try to address this by asking whether people are actually able to do the things that count, rather than whether they have been offered the formal chance. Sen and Nussbaum, on Sypnowich's reading, hedge this insight by retreating to "capabilities" as opportunities to function rather than as functioning itself; but the basic move toward substantive evaluation of how people are actually faring is a real advance, and Sypnowich's flourishing approach extends it. To know whether a society is treating disabled citizens as equals, we have to know how they are actually living, not just what formal options they enjoy. That seems right.

Moreover, the flourishing approach allows us to identify forms of inequality that resource-based and opportunity-based approaches miss. Consider the experience of social isolation, the way disabled people are systematically excluded from many forms of public life, the lack of accommodation in workplaces and public spaces. These are not principally failures to provide resources; they are failures in the basic organization of social life to recognize disabled people as full participants. The flourishing approach has the conceptual resources to register these as injustices in a way that, say, Dworkin's equality of resources does not naturally do.

But here the tension begins. The flourishing approach, in Sypnowich's formulation, commits us to substantive judgments about what counts as a good life. It commits us to the view that "one's life goes better if one is contributing to the community," that flourishing involves "all-round development," that we should disincentivize forms of life that fall short. The disability rights movement has spent decades pushing back against precisely this kind of ranking. The history of disability has been a history of forms of life being judged by abled standards and found wanting: deaf culture devalued by hearing norms, neurodivergent ways of being treated as deficient versions of neurotypical functioning, lives shaped by chronic illness or impairment judged less worthwhile than lives shaped by health. To embrace a flourishing framework with substantive content is to enter territory that has been the site of profound injustice toward disabled people.

The deaf community example is particularly sharp. For decades, mainstream educational and medical institutions treated deafness as a deficit to be corrected by oralism, lip-reading instruction, and later cochlear implants. Deaf activists argued, and continue to argue, that deafness is not a deficit but a different way of being, with its own language, culture, and community. The flourishing framework, if applied unreflectively, would have sided with the institutions: deaf children should be cultivated toward the more flourishing condition of hearing, since hearing makes for a richer life. The community, deploying its democratic authority over what counts as flourishing, would have justified the cultural destruction of deaf communities.

Sypnowich would, I am sure, resist this implication. She acknowledges "the plurality of ways of living well" and insists that the outcome approach can accommodate diverse forms of flourishing. But the structure of her argument keeps pulling against this pluralism. She wants the community to identify the valuable, to promote certain pursuits over others, to intervene against limited horizons. The plurality she defends is a plurality of valuable forms, but the determination of what counts as valuable is the community's. In a world where most people are not disabled and most institutions are organized around abled norms, the community's determinations will tend to encode those norms. The flourishing framework provides no internal mechanism to resist this; it provides only the (correct but insufficient) injunction to be inclusive.

The disability case suggests, I think, that the egalitarian project needs a different kind of resource: a strong principle of self-determination, including the right of communities of difference to define their own flourishing. This is not quite the liberal principle of neutrality, but it borrows from it. It says that there are forms of life whose internal logic should be respected by the wider polity, even when the wider polity finds them puzzling or thinks they fall short of full flourishing. The wider polity owes such communities resources, accommodation, and political recognition; it does not get to substitute its judgment about flourishing for theirs.

This is a constraint on the cultivating state that I do not see clearly articulated in Sypnowich's essay. It is also, I want to suggest, a constraint that disability politics has developed with particular sophistication, but that applies more broadly. Religious minorities, cultural minorities, queer communities, neurodivergent communities, and many others have made similar claims: that the appropriate egalitarian response is to recognize and resource their forms of life, not to fold them into the majority's conception of flourishing.

Where does this leave the broader argument? I think it suggests that the flourishing approach is right at the level of its general orientation — equality is about how people actually live, not just about what they are formally offered — but wrong at the level of its institutional implications when it authorizes the community to identify and promote the valuable in a strong sense. The better path is something like: substantive concern with people's actual lives, strong principles of self-determination both individual and collective, and a commitment to providing the material and institutional conditions under which different forms of flourishing can be pursued by those who endorse them.

This is more pluralist than Sypnowich's framework but more substantively committed than liberal neutrality. It might be called something like "flourishing without paternalism," or "substantive equality with strong rights of self-definition." It would involve, in policy terms: universal provision of the material conditions of flourishing (income, health, housing, education); strong protections for forms of life that majorities find puzzling; democratic processes of deliberation about what social institutions should provide, with particular voice for affected minorities; and a humility about the limits of the polity's competence to judge whose lives are going well.

One last point. Sypnowich worries about leveling down — about removing measures of flourishing that cannot be enjoyed equally. Disability politics has its own version of this worry. There is a tradition of arguing that we should be willing to accept lower aggregate flourishing in order to achieve greater equality, including by withholding resources from those whose disabilities make them harder to bring up to baseline. This is monstrous, and Sypnowich rightly resists it. But the same logic applies to subtler cases: the demand that disabled people aspire to abled forms of flourishing in order to maximize their lives by the community's standards. The egalitarian project should make space for forms of life that do not maximize on community-endorsed metrics, while still ensuring that those who pursue them have the resources to do so.

Sypnowich's essay is a useful provocation. Its instincts are largely right. But the project it sketches needs more guardrails than it provides, and disability is one of the places where those guardrails most urgently need to be built.

Sypnowich’s central charge is that liberal egalitarians, from Rawls to Dworkin to Sen, focus on opportunity and thereby tolerate massive inequalities of outcome. She laments that “liberal egalitarians do not even aim for equality per se” and that the luck egalitarian distinction between choice and circumstance leads to a “sink-or-swim” mentality. But this ignores the full architecture of Rawls’s justice as fairness. Rawls’s first principle guarantees equal basic liberties, but his second principle contains two parts: fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle. The latter explicitly requires that social and economic inequalities be arranged so that they are to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged. This is not a mere opportunity floor; it is an ongoing outcome-sensitive criterion that governs the basic structure of society. Under the difference principle, if the worst-off group is not doing well—if they lack adequate income, wealth, access to meaningful work, and the social bases of self-respect—the society is unjust, regardless of whether they had an “equal start.”

Moreover, Rawls’s vision of a property-owning democracy (or liberal socialism) involves dispersing wealth and capital widely, so that over time, the fair value of political liberties and fair equality of opportunity are preserved. These institutional arrangements are designed to prevent the kind of dynastic inequality and social sclerosis that Sypnowich rightly condemns. The “washing of hands” is nowhere to be found: the basic structure continuously corrects for the cumulative effects of the market and family background. As for Dworkin’s equality of resources, his hypothetical insurance scheme is explicitly outcome-oriented: it asks what level of insurance rational people would buy behind a veil of ignorance against the risk of low talents or disability. The resulting social minimum would be substantial and would guarantee a dignified life. Sypnowich complains that Dworkin allows inequalities to persist, but only those that result from genuine choice, and after compensation for bad brute luck. Even in an ideal society, people will have different temperaments, desires, and metabolisms, and no amount of redistribution can ensure that everyone experiences the same level of “flourishing.” As Sen noted, converting resources into well-being varies from person to person. A just society should ensure that everyone has a fair share of resources and opportunities to live a life they have reason to value, but it cannot promise identical outcomes in happiness or fulfillment. That would be to overpromise and invite the very leveling down she rightly rejects.

We do not need to abandon opportunity for outcomes; we need to realize the full implications of fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle. That would be radical enough.

In doing so, she casually dismisses the very engines of human progress—free markets, individual choice, and the epistemic humility of liberal neutrality. From a Hayekian and classical liberal perspective, her vision is not only economically ruinous, but deeply threatening to human liberty.

Sypnowich’s fundamental grievance is with the "choice as circumstance" problem. She argues that because individuals are shaped by their social environments, their choices are never truly their own. Therefore, it is "harsh" to hold them responsible for the outcomes of those choices. This deterministic view of humanity is the fatal flaw in her argument. While it is obviously true that we are influenced by our environment, erasing the concept of individual agency destroys the basis for any functional society. If individuals are not responsible for their choices, then they cannot be trusted with freedom.

And indeed, Sypnowich does not trust them with freedom. Because she believes people are constrained by "unaccountable markets" and "cultures of fatalism," she demands that "representative political institutions" take over the job of "guiding by incentives, education, and supporting institutions that offer pursuits of value." What Sypnowich fails to grasp is the knowledge problem. How does the state know what constitutes flourishing for 330 million diverse individuals? The market allows individuals to define their own flourishing and coordinate with others to achieve it. Sypnowich views market outcomes as "deleterious" because people often choose things she, as an academic, does not value. When the state forces distribution regardless of effort, the result is not warm comradeship; it is resentment, stagnation, and the inevitable rise of an authoritarian bureaucracy needed to enforce the distribution.

We must reject the socialist temptation and defend the only equality that matters: the equal right of every individual to be free from state coercion.

Predistribution—shaping wages, ownership, bargaining power, and corporate governance before income is distributed—is essential.

First markets allocate rewards and risks; then philosophers ask which losers deserve compensation. Economic democracy asks why the allocation process is organized so undemocratically to begin with. If workers collectively governed firms, if communities shaped investment, if social rights insulated everyone from desperation, the question “was your poverty your fault?” would lose much of its grip.

A workplace might be democratic and well-paid, yet some workers find more meaning in family, religion, art, or leisure than in work. It is that no one should be structurally subordinated or denied the material bases of a decent life.

The choice is not market influence versus state influence. It is oligarchic influence versus democratic influence.

People do not need the state to make their lives equally fulfilled. They need, together, to govern the workplaces, resources, technologies, and public goods that make fulfillment possible. Liberals face a similar challenge with their account of opportunity, but they have at least a procedural response: let individuals define their own conceptions of the good and provide the means for pursuing those conceptions. Sypnowich acknowledges that perfect equality of outcome is impossible and that some outcome disparities reflect legitimate differences in talent, temperament, and choice. Sen's central insight remains indispensable: the appropriate focus of egalitarian concern is not resources, not utility, but what people are actually able to do and be—their capabilities. A capability is the substantive freedom to achieve valuable functionings. Sypnowich argues that capability theory is too similar to opportunity-based approaches because it measures capability to function rather than actual functioning. The capabilities approach does not require us to rank comprehensive conceptions of flourishing against each other. The approach is explicitly political in the sense that it involves public deliberation about what capabilities are essential and how they should be secured. The difference is that such deliberation proceeds in terms of actual capabilities—the real freedoms people possess—rather than abstract flourishing.

If we specify capabilities adequately, we can ask whether individuals have the genuine opportunity to function well before concluding that their actual functioning is their own responsibility. The question is not whether choices are "free" in some absolute sense but whether they are made under conditions of adequate capability.

Capabilities are inherently relational: affiliation, for instance, requires relationships with others and social institutions that support meaningful participation in community life. But these are challenges to be addressed, not insuperable objections. The capabilities framework provides the most promising synthesis of the insights Sypnowich develops—attention to actual well-being, rejection of narrow proceduralism, concern with community—while avoiding the pitfalls of comprehensive flourishing egalitarianism.

Global solidarity would require not only that members of a community care for each other but that they extend this care to all human beings, regardless of nationality or location. This is a demanding conclusion, but it follows from the premises of the flourishing approach: if flourishing is the metric and all persons have equal claim to it, then the barriers of national sovereignty cannot legitimately block the flow of resources and assistance that solidarity requires.

Sypnowich might resist this extension on grounds that solidarity must be rooted in shared institutions and history to be meaningful. But this response would concede too much to particularism. The flourishing approach seeks to articulate universal standards of justice that apply to all human beings; it cannot quietly limit those standards to those who share our national citizenship or cultural heritage. Global solidarity is not merely an optional extension of domestic solidarity; it is a test of whether the solidarity argument can bear the weight that Sypnowich places on it.

A second limitation of the solidarity argument concerns its relationship to freedom. Sypnowich argues that solidarity requires the flourishing of all, not merely the equality of formal opportunity. But it is not clear how solidarity relates to the freedom of individuals to pursue their own conceptions of the good. If solidarity demands that everyone flourish, it might also demand that individuals be guided toward flourishing—prevented from making choices that lead to floundering. This is the paternalism problem that I raised earlier, but it appears here in a different form. Solidarity does not merely require institutions that enable flourishing; it requires that individuals actually flourish, which seems to require interference with their liberty.

Perhaps solidarity is compatible with freedom if it is understood not as a demand that individuals flourish but as a disposition of mutual care that provides the background against which individual flourishing occurs. On this interpretation, solidarity does not justify paternalism but rather explains why we care about equality in the first place: not because of abstract principles but because we care about others and want them to thrive. This is plausible, but it raises the question of what to do when individuals do not want to thrive in ways that solidarity would require. Must they accept guidance, or can they opt out of solidarity's demands?

These are not rhetorical objections but genuine puzzles that any solidarity-based approach must address. Sypnowich has opened an important line of inquiry; it remains to be fully developed.

Sypnowich's argument focuses on domestic equality—the inequalities within wealthy capitalist societies like the United States, the United Kingdom, and Canada. But her framework has profound implications for global justice that she does not adequately explore. I want to argue that a flourishing-based egalitarianism, taken seriously, requires us to prioritize global inequality over domestic inequality, and that doing so would transform our understanding of justice.

The scale of global inequality is staggering. The World Bank estimates that roughly 700 million people live in extreme poverty, surviving on less than $2.15 per day. Life expectancy in some sub-Saharan African countries is less than fifty years, compared to over eighty in Japan and Switzerland. These disparities are not natural disasters; they are products of political and economic decisions made by powerful actors within the global order. Colonialism, debt extraction, trade regimes that favor wealthy countries, intellectual property regimes that prevent technology transfer, and corporate practices that extract resources while leaving little value behind—all these have contributed to global inequality in ways that are not accidental.

Sypnowich argues that we should focus on outcomes, not opportunities. But the outcomes of global inequality are manifest: billions of people live lives marked by preventable disease, premature death, and material deprivation that is simply incompatible with human flourishing as she defines it. The opportunities these people possess are also shaped by global structures: their governments face debt constraints that prevent investment in health and education; their economies are integrated into global supply chains that extract value rather than creating it; their citizens cannot migrate to wealthy countries that might offer better opportunities.

If flourishing is our metric, we cannot be indifferent to this global dimension. The flourishment of a median American—whose life expectancy exceeds seventy-five years, whose income is many times the global median, whose access to education and culture is relatively robust—is already at a level that billions of people cannot approach. This does not mean that domestic inequality is irrelevant; it means that it cannot be the sole or even primary focus of egalitarian concern.

What would a global flourishing approach require? At a minimum, it would require significant transfers from wealthy to poor countries—greater than anything currently contemplated by development assistance or international institutions. It would require reform of trade and financial regimes to prevent the extraction of value from poor countries. It would require the cancellation of odious debts that burden governments in the global South. It would require technology transfer and intellectual property reform to enable poorer countries to build their own productive capacities.

Some of these requirements are controversial among egalitarian theorists. The consensus view in the global justice literature holds that wealthy countries have strong duties of justice to the global poor, but there is little agreement on the content of those duties. Singer's pond thought experiment establishes that distance does not eliminate duties, but it does not determine their scope. Pogge's argument about positive duties to address structural global injustice is compelling, but critics question whether existing inequalities can be traced to identifiable causal pathways that implicate the wealthy.

Sypnowich might resist this extension on grounds that the flourishing approach is embedded in particular communities with their own histories and solidarity bonds. The global poor are too distant, too diverse, too unfamiliar for genuine solidarity to develop. This is a serious objection, and it reveals a tension in Sypnowich's argument between her universalist aspirations and her communitarian leanings.

But the tension can be resolved. The flourishing approach does not require local solidarity to be effective; it requires that flourishing be the metric. If global inequality prevents the global poor from flourishing, then it is unjust regardless of whether wealthy country citizens feel solidarity with them. The political mechanism for addressing global inequality need not be solidarity; it can be justice, rights, or the extension of the institutional structures that enable flourishing. The European Union does not require solidarity among all its citizens to justify redistributive policies; it requires recognition that all members have equal claim to the benefits of membership.

Sypnowich writes that "the egalitarian ideal requires socialism." I would add that it requires global socialism—not a particular political program but a commitment to ensuring that all persons, wherever they live, possess the conditions for flourishing. This is a demanding conclusion, but it follows from taking equality seriously. If we care about flourishing, we cannot ignore the most severe deprivations of flourishing anywhere in the world. Domestic equality matters, but it cannot be the measure of an adequate egalitarian theory.

The practical implications are radical. Immigration restrictions that prevent the global poor from accessing better opportunities would be difficult to justify under a flourishing framework. Trade policies that perpetuate poverty in the global South would be subject to searching scrutiny. The allocation of resources for climate change adaptation and mitigation would be guided by the needs of the most vulnerable, not the interests of the wealthy. None of this is politically feasible in the current moment, but political feasibility is not the same as moral requirement. Sypnowich is right that equality requires outcomes; I am arguing that it requires global outcomes.
