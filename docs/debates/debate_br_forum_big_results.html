Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The case Glennerster and Kremer make is genuinely exciting, and the accumulated evidence from the J-PAL network and its collaborators over the past fifteen years has changed how serious people think about anti-poverty programs. They are right that the "you don't value what you don't pay for" intuition has been embarrassed repeatedly, and right that small frictions — a thirty-cent fee, a 3.5-minute walk, an unmet need for cash on the day school fees are due — have effects that no plausible rational-agent model would predict. The lentils-for-immunization result from Banerjee and Duflo's Udaipur work, the deworming cascade, the Progresa/Oportunidades evaluations: these are now anchor points in development economics for good reason.

But I want to push back on three places where the essay glides past harder problems.

First, the leap from "behavioral frictions matter at the margin" to "small changes drive big results" risks confusing point estimates with program design. Consider the chlorine dispenser. It is a beautiful example of behaviorally-informed design — visible reminder, default quantity, peer monitoring, free at point of use. But the dispenser only works because someone refills it, maintains it, and replaces broken parts. The two-dollars-per-case-of-diarrhea figure rests on the dispenser actually functioning at steady state, which in turn rests on an institutional supply chain whose costs are notoriously hard to capture in a controlled trial. The same caveat applies to deworming campaigns, which depend on teachers actually distributing pills, and to conditional cash transfers, which depend on a state capable of verifying school attendance and moving money. Mexico in 1997 had that state; Sierra Leone, the essay's other example, did not. "Small changes" is sometimes shorthand for "we held the institutional scaffolding constant and varied one input." Once you try to scale, the scaffolding becomes the binding constraint.

Second, I think the essay is too quick to dismiss the external-validity critics. It is true that hundreds of RCTs are now finding family resemblances — price-sensitivity for preventive health, present bias, peer effects. But "family resemblance" is doing a lot of work. The Malawi finding that the smallest CCT achieved the average effect, the Kenyan finding that uniforms reduced teen pregnancy, the Indian call-center result on girls' schooling — each of these depends on local elasticities that we have no theoretical reason to expect to be stable across settings. Angus Deaton's recent critique deserves more engagement than the essay gives it. A randomized trial tells you what happened in this village, with this NGO, under this implementation. The inferential step from there to "people procrastinate, so put deadlines on chlorine adoption everywhere" is not itself randomized. It is an act of theoretical interpretation, and behavioral economics is still a thin enough theoretical structure that competing interpretations are easy to construct. Is low chlorine uptake about present bias, or about (rational) skepticism that the water is actually unsafe, or about distrust of outside products, or about taste? Different stories imply different policies.

Third, the essay's treatment of user fees is one-sided in a way that should make us nervous. Yes, removing fees raises uptake dramatically; the Cohen-Dupas bednet study is convincing on that. But Glennerster and Kremer concede, almost in passing, that fees may motivate frontline workers to show up and may provide flexible funding where governments fail to deliver budgets. These are not small concessions. In much of sub-Saharan Africa, the binding constraint on health delivery is not consumer demand but absentee providers and stockouts. If abolishing fees produces a surge of patients arriving at clinics where the nurse is absent and the drug shelf is empty, we may have moved the bottleneck rather than removed it. The essay's own line — "we don't know the impact of fees on the supply side" — is, I think, the most important sentence in the piece, and it deserves to be at the center rather than the periphery of the argument.

None of this is an argument against the experimental turn in development economics. It is the most intellectually honest thing that has happened to the field in a generation, and the discipline it imposes — go look, measure, replicate — is exactly what the macro-development debates of the 1990s lacked. But I'd urge a sharper distinction between two claims that the essay sometimes runs together. One claim is methodological: randomized evaluation is a powerful tool for learning what works in specific contexts. That is now beyond serious dispute. The other claim is substantive: there exists a portable behavioral toolkit — small incentives, default options, convenience, deadlines — that yields large welfare gains across the developing world. This second claim is plausible and partly evidenced, but it is also exactly the kind of "big-think" generalization that the experimental approach was supposed to make us suspicious of. The honest answer, in 2011, is that we are at the beginning of knowing, not the end.

The lead essay by Glennerster and Kremer offers a compelling narrative: that small, behaviorally informed tweaks to policy can yield large improvements in health and education outcomes for the world’s poor. Drawing on a growing body of randomized evaluations, they argue that modest incentives, free provision of preventive goods, and minor adjustments in timing and convenience can overcome procrastination and cognitive biases that prevent poor households from investing optimally in their own well-being. This is an important message, and the evidence they marshal is impressive. But as an agenda for development, the “small changes” approach risks elevating micro-level fixes over the deeper structural transformations that ultimately determine living standards.

The authors are right to celebrate the experimental turn in development economics. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) have brought a welcome dose of empirical rigor to a field long dominated by grand theory and casual empiricism. The demonstration that a free school uniform can boost attendance by six percentage points, or that a kilogram of lentils can triple immunization rates, is both striking and actionable. These are precisely the kinds of concrete, cost-effective interventions that donors and governments can scale up with measurable impact. And by showing how seemingly trivial barriers—a bit of walking, a tiny fee—disproportionately deter beneficial behaviors, the research reveals a consistency in human psychology that cuts across cultures.

Yet there is a danger that this focus on the low-hanging fruit of individual behavior change distracts from the harder graft of institutional reform. The essay acknowledges that “the details of institutional circumstance can matter a lot,” but it largely sidesteps the question of why public health and education systems are so dysfunctional in the first place. In many countries, clinics lack drugs, teachers are chronically absent, and corruption diverts resources. A chlorine dispenser at a communal water point is a clever fix, but if the local water authority fails to maintain the borehole, the dispenser is of little use. Small incentives can get children into classrooms, but they do little to improve the quality of teaching once they arrive. The “small changes” paradigm risks seeking silver bullets that bypass, rather than repair, broken systems.

Moreover, the enthusiastic extrapolation of behavioral regularities across contexts may be premature. The essay’s claim that “strikingly similar patterns of behavior” hold “across different sectors, products, and continents” comes mainly from two countries, Kenya and India. Even within those settings, the studies are typically run in partnership with committed NGOs, under the watchful eye of researchers. Whether such effects survive national scale-up with weak government capacity is an open question. Scalability involves navigating political economy and bureaucratic incentives that small pilots can sidestep.

A related concern is that behavioral economics, for all its insights, can adopt a subtly paternalistic framing. The essay speaks of the poor as procrastinators who fail to make “the best decisions,” echoing the rational-agent model’s assumption that there is a single, knowable optimal choice. But what looks like procrastination may reflect a rational weighing of risks and constraints. A family that declines to buy a mosquito net at a subsidized price might have competing immediate needs (food, medical emergencies) that are invisible to researchers. Their apparent neglect of preventive health could stem from deep-seated mistrust of health providers, or from a realistic assessment that the net will not be properly maintained. The behavioral lens, if applied uncritically, risks treating poverty as a problem of faulty cognition rather than a problem of poverty itself.

These cautions are not an argument against the intelligent use of behavioral insights. The authors’ chlorine dispenser example is a model of how thoughtful design—reducing packaging costs, combining steps, providing visual cues—can align convenience with public health goals. And the broader message that we should take people as we find them, with all their cognitive quirks, is a vital corrective to the simplistic rational-actor model. But small changes should be seen as complements to, not substitutes for, larger efforts to strengthen public services, promote inclusive economic growth, and empower the poor to demand accountability. In the end, the biggest results—the kind that transform societies—are likely to come from structural change, not just from the clever calibration of carrots and lentils.

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer offer a compelling and optimistic vision of poverty alleviation. By applying the insights of behavioral economics through Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), they demonstrate how human idiosyncrasies—procrastination, present bias, and sensitivity to minor inconveniences—can be harnessed for good. The empirical triumphs they cite, from removing user fees for school uniforms to offering bags of lentils to boost immunization rates, are undeniable. They have successfully proven that development policy need not always rely on massive, top-down capital injections; sometimes, small, well-designed adjustments can yield tremendous improvements in human welfare.

Yet, in our enthusiasm for these "small changes," we must be careful not to lose sight of the broader structural landscape. The risk of the behavioral revolution in development economics is that it threatens to reduce the monumental, systemic challenge of poverty to a series of technocratic tweaks. While human psychology may be relatively universal, the institutional and political environments in which people operate are not. Development is not merely about overcoming individual behavioral bottlenecks; it requires building the physical, economic, and institutional infrastructure that makes sustained prosperity possible.

Consider the authors’ reliance on the success of conditional cash transfers (CCTs), such as Mexico’s PROGRESA. The authors rightfully note that school enrollment rose when families received financial incentives. But PROGRESA succeeded not just because of the behavioral incentive it provided to families, but because Mexico had the necessary state capacity to identify beneficiaries, track daily attendance, and securely disburse funds on a massive scale. In many of the world's poorest nations, this administrative apparatus simply does not exist. A behavioral nudge is only as effective as the bureaucratic machinery required to deliver it.

Furthermore, Glennerster and Kremer focus heavily on the demand side of the equation—getting children into uniforms and mothers into clinics. But what happens when artificially stimulated demand collides with a broken supply side? It is a well-documented reality that public service delivery in developing countries is plagued by systemic failures. High rates of absenteeism among teachers and health workers mean that a child incentivized to walk to school may arrive to find an empty classroom. A mother who walks to a clinic for her bag of lentils may find that the vaccines are out of stock. If small changes increase demand without simultaneous, large-scale reforms to supply and accountability, the resulting frustration could undermine trust in public institutions entirely.

The macroeconomic context is equally vital. The authors note that rural Indian families began sending girls to school more frequently when recruiters for call centers arrived in their villages. But the arrival of those call centers was not the result of a micro-economic nudge; it was the product of massive, macro-economic liberalization, infrastructure investment, and global trade dynamics. If structural economic transformation does not create formal-sector jobs, the long-term returns to incentivized education will eventually crater.

This highlights a significant limitation of the RCT framework: the problem of external validity. As economists like Angus Deaton have recently argued, knowing that a specific intervention works in one village does not automatically tell us *why* it works or guarantee it will scale elsewhere. A chlorine dispenser next to a communal water source is a brilliant, cost-effective intervention, but it is ultimately a coping mechanism for the absence of a municipal water-treatment system. We must ensure that policymakers do not become so enamored with funding highly measurable, randomized micro-interventions that they neglect the messier, harder-to-measure necessities of development: building roads, securing property rights, expanding power grids, and fostering trade.

None of this is to say that the behavioral approach is without immense value. Glennerster and Kremer are entirely correct that the rational-agent model is insufficient for designing good policy. When governments or NGOs distribute health products, they absolutely should use RCTs to optimize delivery and make adoption as frictionless as possible.

However, we must view behavioral economics as a powerful diagnostic tool and a tactical asset, not a comprehensive theory of economic development. Small changes can indeed bring big results for specific health and education metrics, saving millions of lives in the process. But elevating entire nations out of poverty remains a macro-level challenge. We cannot nudge our way around the fundamental need for capable states and robust economic institutions.

Glennerster and Kremer are right to insist that development policy should take seriously the mundane frictions that keep people from using valuable services. A school uniform, a chlorine bottle, a walk to a clinic, or the timing of a fee can look trivial from an office in Washington or Nairobi; for a household managing irregular income, illness, debt, and daily labor, such details can be decisive. The great virtue of the experimental work they describe is that it has made these frictions visible. It has also punctured several durable myths: that charging small fees reliably screens for “serious” users, that free goods are necessarily wasted, and that information campaigns alone can overcome practical barriers.

But I worry that the lesson “small changes, big results” can be too easily misread. It is true that small design changes sometimes produce large improvements. It does not follow that poverty is mainly a problem of bad choice architecture, or that the most important obstacles to health and education are behavioral quirks shared by rich and poor alike. The poor may procrastinate, as all humans do, but they also face constraints that are sharper, more dangerous, and more political than the analogy to American retirement savings suggests.

Consider user fees. The evidence that even modest charges reduce use of preventive services is persuasive and important. But the reason to abolish fees is not only that households are present-biased. It is also that primary education, immunization, clean water, and malaria prevention are public goods, or at least goods with large social spillovers. They are part of the basic social minimum that governments should provide. A family that does not buy chlorine may be misjudging the future, but it may also be making a reasonable decision under scarcity: today’s coins buy food, transport, kerosene, or repayment to a lender; tomorrow’s diarrhea is uncertain. Calling this procrastination risks making an intelligible response to insecurity look like a cognitive defect.

The same point applies to schooling. Conditional cash transfers, scholarships, uniforms, and information about returns can raise enrollment, and that is welcome. Yet enrollment is not education. Many developing countries have achieved impressive gains in attendance while children learn little. In India, surveys by Pratham have repeatedly shown that many children in school cannot read simple texts or do basic arithmetic at grade level. In parts of Africa, teacher absenteeism and weak accountability remain severe. A free uniform can get a child into the classroom, but it cannot by itself ensure that a teacher is present, that the curriculum is appropriate, or that the child learns enough to alter her life chances. On the supply side, institutions are not small details.

This is where randomized trials are both powerful and limited. They can tell us whether a particular incentive raised immunization in Rajasthan, whether deworming reduced absenteeism in western Kenya, or whether a chlorine dispenser increased water treatment at communal sources. Such findings are far better than wishful thinking. But many of the most important development questions concern systems: how to build a competent civil service, how to finance universal health care, how to regulate private providers, how to create jobs for educated youth, how to make politicians answerable for service delivery. These questions can sometimes be studied experimentally at the margin, but they cannot be reduced to a sequence of marginal tweaks.

Nor is scaling up merely a technical exercise. An NGO can distribute incentives or monitor a program closely in a pilot. A ministry must operate through budgets, procurement rules, unions, local brokers, and electoral pressures. A program that is cheap in an experiment may become expensive when extended nationally; a behavior induced by novelty may fade; a subsidy may be captured; informal fees may replace formal ones. Glennerster and Kremer acknowledge these issues, especially on the supply side, but their essay’s emphasis falls on the elegance of design rather than the messiness of power.

Still, the behavioral evidence should change policy. Governments and donors should be much more skeptical of fees for basic preventive health and primary education. They should pay attention to timing: school costs due in a lump sum at the beginning of term will exclude children even when annual costs appear low. They should design services around actual routines: chlorinate water at the source, bring immunization closer to villages, make forms simpler, reduce waiting times. These are not second-order concerns. For people with little slack, convenience is substance.

The best conclusion, then, is not that small interventions are enough, but that justice often depends on small implementation choices. A right to education means less if a uniform fee blocks entry. A right to health means less if the clinic is open only when mothers cannot come, or if a nurse demands an unofficial payment. Behavioral economics helps us see these barriers without romanticizing the poor as perfect calculators or condemning them as irrational.

We should welcome randomized evaluations as a discipline of humility. They force experts to test assumptions and to notice when the poor reject programs designed for them. But humility cuts both ways. The success of a lentil incentive or a chlorine dispenser should not tempt us into a new technocratic confidence that development can be achieved by clever nudges alone. Small changes can produce big results when they are attached to larger commitments: public financing, reliable delivery, accountable institutions, and respect for the people whose behavior is being studied.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The essay by Glennerster and Kremer presents a compelling case for applying behavioral economics to development problems, and the evidence from randomized evaluations is genuinely persuasive. Yet several tensions in their argument deserve closer examination, particularly regarding the sustainability of incentive-based programs and the broader political economy in which they operate.

The authors rightly challenge the assumption that the poor are simply optimizing according to classical economic models. Their evidence that small fees dramatically reduce adoption of beneficial products—from chlorine to school uniforms—poses a genuine problem for standard theory. However, the essay somewhat understates the difficulty of sustaining programs that rely on continuous external subsidies or incentives. The Rajasthan immunization program, which raised rates from 5 to 38 percent using lentils and plates, worked brilliantly as an evaluation. But converting such pilots into permanent public health infrastructure requires institutional capacity that many developing-country governments simply lack. The authors acknowledge this problem when they mention informal fees creeping back after formal abolition, but they treat it as an implementation detail rather than a fundamental challenge to their approach.

The chlorine dispenser project offers perhaps the most impressive cost-effectiveness claim in the essay—at under two dollars per case of diarrhea averted. Yet even this relatively simple technology raises questions the authors do not fully address. Who maintains the dispensers? How are communities selected for implementation? The randomized evaluation can tell us whether dispensers work when installed, but it cannot tell us whether governments will scale such programs, or whether donors will continue funding them as enthusiasm shifts to the next innovation. The history of development is littered with effective pilot projects that never reached scale, and behavioral solutions are not immune to this pattern.

The authors are right to push back against "context is everything" relativism, but they may be somewhat overconfident about generalizability. Much of their strongest evidence comes from Kenya and India, as they acknowledge. Whether the finding that small convenience costs suppress adoption holds equally in, say, Bangladesh or Brazil remains an empirical question. More fundamentally, the essay does not address how cultural factors beyond simple cost-benefit calculations might shape responses to interventions. The peer effects they document in Mexico and Colombia are fascinating, but the mechanisms remain somewhat opaque. Are children attending school more because their peers are there, or because conditional cash transfers change household bargaining dynamics in ways that favor children's education?

Perhaps most significantly, the essay largely sidesteps questions of political power. The behavioral interventions it describes operate on individuals, encouraging them to make better choices given existing constraints. But many of those constraints—from inadequate public health infrastructure to corrupt local officials—are themselves products of political decisions. If people in developing countries are systematically prevented from making optimal choices by institutional failures, then micro-level interventions may achieve less than the authors suggest. The chlorine dispenser works partly because it requires minimal institutional support, but most development challenges cannot be solved through such elegant technical fixes.

That said, Glennerster and Kremer have identified a genuinely important set of phenomena. The fact that small incentives can have large effects, and that small costs can deter beneficial behavior, represents a robust finding that should influence policy. The authors are correct that development economics needed this empirical grounding, and randomized evaluations have delivered it. The remaining task is to understand when and how such findings can be translated into lasting institutional change rather than temporary behavioral nudges.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**The Quiet Revolution Deserves a Louder Reception**

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer have written a careful, almost understated, manifesto for what is genuinely the most important methodological shift in development economics in a generation. Reading their essay, one is struck not by the boldness of any single claim, but by the cumulative force of dozens of small, well-documented findings: that a six-dollar uniform changes a girl's life trajectory; that a lentil bribe quintuples immunization rates; that a chlorine dispenser at a spring outperforms years of social-marketing campaigns. The temptation, having read this, is to nod politely and move on. That would be a mistake. The implications are larger than the authors let on.

For half a century, development economics oscillated between grand theories. Rostow's stages, Prebisch's structuralism, the Washington Consensus, the "Big Push" revival under Sachs—each was sold as the master key, and each, in turn, was discredited by the embarrassing variance of actual experience. The reason these debates never closed is that they could not. Cross-country regressions, however clever the instrument, will never settle whether aid works or whether institutions cause growth, because the units of observation are too few, too heterogeneous, and too endogenously linked. We have argued in circles because the evidence permitted nothing better.

Randomized evaluations break that circle in one specific but vital domain: the design of programs. We may never know, with certainty, whether Ghana should liberalize its capital account. But we now know, with something close to certainty, that deworming pills in western Kenya raised school attendance dramatically at a cost of pennies per child. That is not a small thing. It is the kind of knowledge on which a profession can build.

The critics will say—indeed they have said, loudly—that all this micro-knowledge tells us little about the truly large questions of growth, structural transformation, and political order. There is something to this. No one has yet randomized a constitution. But the critics overstate the dichotomy. Glennerster and Kremer's essay quietly demonstrates how the accumulation of small findings can produce something resembling a general theory. The pattern that emerges—that the poor, like the rich, are sensitive to small upfront costs, that procrastination and present bias are not features of affluence but of humanity, that convenience and default options matter everywhere—is not a trivial observation. It is the foundation of a more honest behavioral picture of poverty than anything Sachs or Easterly has offered in their dueling polemics.

I would push the authors further on one point, however. They are too modest about what their findings imply for ideology. The user-fees debate, which they treat almost as a technical matter, is in fact one of the most consequential policy fights of the past twenty years. The World Bank and the IMF, through structural adjustment, pushed user fees on health and education across sub-Saharan Africa during the 1980s and 1990s on the explicit theory that prices ration scarce resources toward those who value them most. Glennerster and Kremer have evidence that this theory is straightforwardly wrong for preventative health and basic schooling. Those who pay are not those who use; those who use are not those who benefit; and even modest fees collapse demand. This is not a marginal refinement. It is a refutation, and one with millions of lives in the balance. The authors might have said so more plainly.

A second area where they could be bolder concerns the supply side. Their essay focuses, naturally, on consumer behavior, because that is where most of the early experimental work has been done. But the same logic—that small incentives, defaults, and information shifts produce large behavioral changes—surely applies to the providers of health and education as well. Teacher absenteeism in India, which Kremer himself has studied, is not a mystery of culture but a predictable response to incentive structures. The same camera-monitoring experiment that increased teacher attendance could, in principle, be replicated and refined across dozens of bureaucratic contexts. The agenda is not exhausted; it has barely begun.

There is one critique of the randomista program I do find compelling, and which Glennerster and Kremer address only obliquely: the risk of measuring what is measurable rather than what matters. Diarrhea cases averted are countable; civic capacity, political voice, and the long-run trajectory of an institution are not. If we only fund what we can evaluate, we will accumulate evidence about the things at the bottom of Maslow's hierarchy and remain agnostic about everything above. That is a real cost, and the field needs to wrestle with it more openly. But it is a reason to expand the methodological toolkit, not to retreat from rigor.

Finally, a word on the Stellaluna ending, which I confess I find more powerful than its sentimentality should allow. Development economics has long carried a residue of orientalism—an assumption that the behavior of poor people in poor countries is governed by exotic cultural variables that "we" must learn to respect. There is a kinder version of this view and a crueler one, but both share the premise of fundamental difference. The accumulated evidence Glennerster and Kremer summarize suggests something less flattering to the field and more humane in implication: the Kenyan farmer who wants a commitment device looks rather like the American graduate student who pays for a gym membership she will not use. Both procrastinate, both respond to defaults, both undervalue future health relative to present convenience. We are not so different. Recognizing this should make development policy less paternalistic in tone and more behaviorally serious in design.

The randomista revolution is not the answer to every question in development. But it is finally producing knowledge that accumulates rather than dissipates, that survives the next ideological cycle, and that translates directly into programs that save lives at startling cost-effectiveness. That is more than the field has accomplished in any previous decade. Glennerster and Kremer deserve credit for describing it modestly; the rest of us should be willing to celebrate it loudly.

**The Limits of the Petri Dish**

Glennerster and Kremer's essay is admirably clear, but its clarity is purchased at the price of evading the central methodological question their movement has raised: do the findings of randomized controlled trials generalize, and if not, what exactly have we learned? The authors gesture at this issue and then declare it "empirical" — to be settled by accumulating still more trials. I want to suggest that the question is not merely empirical, and that the accumulation strategy is less reassuring than it sounds.

Begin with what an RCT actually estimates. In its purest form, it produces an unbiased estimate of the average treatment effect of a specific intervention, delivered by a specific implementer, in a specific population, at a specific time, under conditions partly shaped by the experiment itself. That is a precise and valuable thing. But it is not, on its own, a parameter of human nature. When the Mexican PROGRESA program raised secondary school enrollment among girls by 14.8 percentage points, that number is a fact about Mexico in the late 1990s, with its particular labor market, its existing school infrastructure, its specific cash transfer amount relative to local wages, and a competent federal bureaucracy capable of delivering the transfers reliably. Take any of these away and the number changes — perhaps slightly, perhaps drastically. There is no way to know in advance which.

Glennerster and Kremer counter this concern by pointing to patterns: across multiple trials in multiple countries, small fees reduce uptake, small incentives increase it, convenience matters, present bias is pervasive. These are real patterns, and they are valuable. But notice what has happened in the argument. The defense of the experimental program is no longer that any individual RCT tells us what will happen elsewhere — that defense was abandoned long ago — but that the accumulated body of RCTs reveals stable behavioral regularities. This is, in fact, the kind of inductive generalization that the randomista movement was supposed to improve upon. When we say "small fees reduce uptake of preventative health products," we are no longer in the world of clean causal identification. We are in the world of meta-analysis, narrative review, and judgment about which contexts resemble which — exactly the kinds of inferential leaps the RCT was meant to discipline.

This does not make the leaps wrong. It makes them ordinary social science, with all of its attendant uncertainty. The randomistas have produced excellent natural-history specimens; the question of which specimens describe a general phenomenon remains as hard as it ever was.

Consider also the selection of questions. Lant Pritchett has been pointing out for some years that the topics amenable to randomization tend to be narrow, project-shaped interventions: deworming pills, bed nets, conditional cash transfers, information campaigns. The questions that have historically driven large changes in human welfare — industrialization, urbanization, the building of effective states, the demographic transition — are not amenable to randomization at all. The countries that have lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty in the past thirty years (China most spectacularly) did so through policy choices that no RCT could have validated in advance. If our knowledge becomes increasingly weighted toward what we can randomize, we risk producing a development economics that knows a great deal about how to deliver chlorine and very little about how a society develops.

The user-fees finding is a useful illustration. Glennerster and Kremer report convincingly that even small fees collapse demand for preventative health products. Granted. But the policy question is not "does demand drop?" It is "what is the best use of scarce public budget?" The trial tells us about the demand response; it does not tell us about the opportunity cost of free provision, the supply-side incentives created when clinics lose their flexible cash, the political economy of who captures free goods in distribution chains, or the long-run consequences for the relationship between citizens and providers. The authors acknowledge some of these complications, but only in passing, and the headline message — "abolish small fees" — has already escaped into policy circles with the imprimatur of "evidence-based."

I am also uneasy with the framing of behavioral findings. Glennerster and Kremer move smoothly from "the poor are sensitive to small upfront costs" to the implication that policy should provide small upfront incentives. There is a paternalist undertone here that deserves attention. We have decided, on the basis of our own theory of what is good for the poor (more immunizations, more schooling), that we will design choice architectures to nudge them toward our preferred outcomes. This may be defensible — most policy is paternalist in some sense — but it is a different argument from the one the authors are making. They present their findings as if the optimal behavior were known and only the behavioral hurdles remained. Yet the parents who delay immunization may know things about provider quality, side effects, and local circumstances that the visiting researcher does not. Treating their behavior as a procrastination problem rather than a rational response to bad providers begs the question.

None of this is to suggest that the experimental program should be abandoned. The careful causal evidence Glennerster and Kremer summarize is genuinely better than what came before, and the discipline it has imposed on the field is welcome. But the proper attitude toward this body of work is more cautious than its champions suggest. We have learned a great deal about how particular interventions worked in particular places. We have learned somewhat less about why. We have learned still less about whether the same interventions will work elsewhere or at scale. And we have learned almost nothing, by this method, about the questions that drive the long arc of development.

The petri dish is real, and what it grows in is real. But a petri dish is not an ecosystem.

**Missing the Politics**

There is much to admire in Glennerster and Kremer's careful synthesis of a decade's worth of experimental development research. The findings are real, the methodology is rigorous, and the policy implications — particularly on user fees — are humane. And yet, reading the essay, I could not shake the feeling that something enormous was missing from the picture. That something is politics.

Notice what the essay does not contain. It does not mention the state, except as a vague provider of services or remover of fees. It does not discuss why some governments deliver public goods competently and others do not. It does not ask why, after sixty years of independence, parts of sub-Saharan Africa still lack functional primary health systems while others have built them. It does not consider that the chlorine dispenser at the Kenyan spring exists in a country whose chronic underinvestment in rural water infrastructure is itself a political fact, the product of decisions about who counts and who does not. The dispenser is a clever workaround for a political failure, and the authors describe it as if politics had nothing to do with it.

This silence is not accidental. It is a feature of the experimental method. RCTs work best when the intervention is bounded, the implementer is reliable, and the political context can be held constant by design. NGOs make ideal partners precisely because they are flexible, project-shaped, and uninterested in confronting the larger structures around them. The randomista program has, in effect, carved out the depoliticized portion of development and studied it intensively. What it has learned in that bounded space is valuable. But the bounded space is not where the action is.

Consider the conditional cash transfer literature, which the authors rightly celebrate. PROGRESA in Mexico worked. So have its imitators in Brazil, Colombia, and elsewhere. But none of these programs is primarily an "intervention." They are pieces of a broader political project — typically associated with center-left governments responding to mobilized constituencies, building electoral coalitions among the poor, and constructing the administrative capacity to deliver benefits at scale. The cash transfer is the visible tip; the iceberg is a state capable of identifying poor families, transferring money to them reliably, monitoring conditions, and absorbing the political backlash from those who object. When other countries try to copy the cash transfer without the underlying political infrastructure, they often fail. The "treatment effect" is not portable because what is being transferred is not just a policy but a state capacity.

Or consider user fees. The authors present the evidence against fees as a triumph of behavioral economics over a flawed economic model. But the user-fee regime was never the product of an honest economic argument. It was imposed on dozens of African and Latin American countries during the 1980s and 1990s by the World Bank and the IMF as part of structural adjustment, against the wishes of national health ministries and despite warnings from many economists at the time. The reason fees persisted for decades was not that economists believed they worked. It was that they were embedded in a global political structure that constrained what indebted governments could do. Showing in a trial that fees reduce uptake is useful, but it is responding to a political imposition with a technocratic finding. The political imposition is the deeper story.

The same pattern shows up in education. Glennerster and Kremer report that providing information about returns to schooling, or recruiters visiting villages, raises enrollment. Excellent. But why do families underestimate returns in the first place? Often because the labor markets they observe really do have low returns to education — because the formal sector is small, because credentialing is rationed by ethnicity or politics, because the schools available to the rural poor are so bad that the returns to attending them are genuinely low. These are political facts about how labor markets and school systems are organized. An information campaign can shift beliefs, but if it shifts beliefs that were tracking reality, the effect will fade.

I do not mean to suggest that any of this invalidates the experimental findings. It does not. But the framing in which "small changes" produce "big results" can become misleading when readers conclude that the small changes alone are the explanation. The chlorine dispenser produces big results because it is solving a real problem of water contamination — a problem created by the political failure to provide safe piped water. The conditional cash transfer produces big results because it is delivering resources to households that have been politically excluded from the gains of growth. The deworming pill produces big results because the parasites have been allowed to flourish in the absence of adequate public health systems. Each "small change" is, in a sense, an admission of a large failure.

A more candid framing would acknowledge this. The experimental literature has identified a class of cheap, effective workarounds for the failures of weak states and ill-functioning markets. These workarounds are worth pursuing. They save lives. But pursued in isolation, they risk substituting for the harder political project of building states that can provide clean water, decent schools, and functional clinics without requiring an NGO with a randomized trial to figure out the optimal incentive structure.

Albert Hirschman warned long ago about the seduction of small, technical projects in development, and the way they could displace attention from the larger institutional questions. Glennerster and Kremer's program is not guilty of Hirschman's worst fears — the rigor is real and the humility is genuine. But the displacement effect is worth worrying about. We are producing a generation of development economists exquisitely trained to evaluate interventions and barely trained to think about politics, the state, or power. The "small changes" agenda needs to be paired with a serious account of where the big changes come from. Otherwise we will keep learning, with ever greater precision, how to cope with structures we have given up trying to change.

**Yes, Small Changes — But Whose Small Changes?**

I want to take Glennerster and Kremer at their word: that small adjustments to defaults, incentives, and convenience can produce dramatic changes in behavior, and that this is one of the most robust findings of the experimental development literature. I think they are right. The evidence is strong. What I want to press them on is a question they do not directly engage: who decides which small changes to make, and on whose authority?

Behavioral economics, in its developed-world applications, has always sat uneasily on a paternalist edge. Thaler and Sunstein tried to defuse the worry with the phrase "libertarian paternalism" — the idea that choice architectures inevitably exist, so we may as well design them to promote welfare, while leaving people free to opt out. Even in the U.S. context, this framing has been contested. Cass Sunstein, defending it, has had to argue carefully that the chooser remains sovereign. But when we move from automatic 401(k) enrollment for middle-class Americans to lentil bribes for Rajasthani mothers, the power asymmetries become much sharper, and the question of who is nudging whom becomes much harder to wave away.

Consider the Rajasthan immunization study Glennerster and Kremer cite as a triumph. Full immunization rates rose from 5 percent to 38 percent when mothers received a kilogram of lentils per visit and a set of plates at the end. The authors present this as a behavioral solution: present-biased mothers procrastinate on immunization; small incentives overcome the procrastination; everyone wins. But notice what is implicit. We — the researchers, the NGO, the development economists — have decided that the right behavior is full immunization. We have decided that the deviation from this behavior is a defect to be corrected. We have designed an incentive to correct it. And we have done so for some of the poorest, least politically powerful people on earth, who were not consulted about the choice architecture being designed for them.

The proximate justification is that immunization saves lives, and the mothers themselves, on reflection, would presumably endorse it. Perhaps. But this justification depends on a particular theory of what their behavior reveals. The behavioral story is that their non-vaccination expresses present bias — a failure to act on preferences they themselves would endorse. An alternative story is that their non-vaccination expresses rational distrust of unreliable public clinics, doubts about side effects, or rival demands on their scarce time. The lentil incentive does not distinguish between these stories. It just changes the behavior. If the behavior was rational on the original information set, the incentive has manipulated rather than empowered.

This may seem like an overly philosophical objection to a program that is, after all, saving children's lives. I am not arguing against immunization. I am arguing for explicitness about what we are doing. The randomista literature has, almost unintentionally, become a powerful technology of behavioral modification aimed at the world's poor. It works. The question is what kind of accountability accompanies its use.

In the U.S. case, automatic 401(k) enrollment passes through Congress, employer benefit committees, and individual opt-out provisions. In the Rajasthan case, the choice architecture is designed by economists at a Cambridge or Boston institution, implemented through an Indian NGO, and presented to mothers who have effectively no voice in its design. We tell ourselves that we are correcting behavioral biases. We could equally well say we are exploiting them: identifying the small interventions that produce large behavioral changes is, after all, the same skill set whether one's goals are benign or otherwise. The current generation of randomistas has, by and large, benevolent intentions. The next generation, working for governments or corporations with less benevolent goals, will inherit the same toolkit.

There is also the question of which behaviors get targeted. Glennerster and Kremer's examples are mostly uncontroversially good: get children vaccinated, get girls in school, get water chlorinated. But the methodology is agnostic about goals. It will work equally well to nudge consumers into buying products that are not in their interest, to nudge voters into electoral choices they would otherwise reject, or to nudge populations into demographic behaviors deemed desirable by elites. The Chinese government has been a careful consumer of behavioral economics. So have political consultancies in many countries. The randomista community has, as far as I can tell, given little thought to how its methods might be appropriated by less benign actors.

I would urge the authors, and their colleagues, to take three steps. First, develop a more explicit ethical framework for the design of choice architectures targeting poor populations. The IRB process, which governs much of this research, is concerned mostly with informed consent during the experimental phase. It says little about the ethics of permanent policy changes that emerge from the research. Second, build in mechanisms of voice. The mothers in Rajasthan should not just be objects of intervention. They should, where possible, be participants in deciding which behaviors are problems and which incentives are acceptable. This is harder than running an RCT, but it is essential to the legitimacy of the project. Third, be more candid about the limits of "welfare" as a justification. When the researcher's theory of welfare diverges from the subject's revealed preferences, that divergence is a moral claim, not just an empirical one, and it deserves defense.

Glennerster and Kremer have produced a body of evidence that genuinely improves our ability to help the global poor. That is a serious achievement. But the very effectiveness of the methods makes it urgent to think carefully about their governance. Small changes produce big results. They also produce big responsibilities. The discipline has not yet caught up with the implications of its own power.

**From the Field: What the RCTs Don't See**

I have spent the better part of twenty years implementing health and education programs in East and West Africa, much of that time working closely with researchers running randomized evaluations. I read Glennerster and Kremer's essay with appreciation for its clarity and some concern about what it leaves out. Their findings are real. The complications I want to describe are also real, and they are visible mostly from inside the operations of programs rather than from the analyst's desk.

Take the chlorine dispenser, which Glennerster and Kremer present as a model of behaviorally-informed design. It is. I have seen them work, and the cost-effectiveness numbers are genuinely impressive. But I have also seen what happens between the evaluation phase and the scale-up phase, and the gap is larger than the published numbers suggest.

During the trial, dispensers are installed by careful field teams. They are maintained on a defined schedule. When one breaks, it is replaced quickly, because the integrity of the study depends on continuity. The chlorine supply chain is managed by people who understand that the experiment depends on it. The local promoters who encourage use are trained, paid, and supervised. Under these conditions, the dispenser is a beautiful intervention.

At scale, almost none of these conditions hold. Dispensers break and are not replaced for months. Chlorine runs out because the district health office did not place its order in time, or because the order was placed but the truck did not arrive. Promoters, if they exist, are unpaid volunteers whose enthusiasm wanes after the first year. The visual reminder still sits on the spring, but it is now an empty plastic box, and the social-norm effect runs in reverse: a broken dispenser becomes a symbol of donor projects that come and go. I have watched this happen in three different countries.

This is not a criticism of the chlorine-dispenser approach. It works when it works. It is a criticism of the inference from "this intervention had a large effect in the trial" to "this intervention should be the policy." The trial measures the efficacy of the intervention under near-optimal implementation conditions. Policy needs to know its effectiveness under the actually-available implementation conditions, which are usually much worse. The two numbers can differ by a factor of five or more. I have seen it.

The authors gesture at this issue when they note that "the details of institutional circumstance can matter a lot" for supply-side reforms. But they understate how pervasive the issue is. Even consumer-side interventions like the chlorine dispenser have crucial supply-side components that are invisible to the demand-side trial. If the system cannot keep chlorine in the dispenser, the demand findings are moot.

A second observation from the field: the same intervention often produces very different effects depending on the implementing organization. A free uniform distribution program run by a high-capacity NGO with deep community relationships produces different results than the same program run by a government ministry under political pressure to distribute uniforms by election day. Both are "the same intervention" in the language of the trial. They are different in practice. The RCT cannot easily distinguish them, and the published effect size depends, often invisibly, on which kind of implementer was randomized.

This matters for how findings should be communicated. When Glennerster and Kremer report that a Kenyan free uniform program raised attendance by 6.4 percentage points, the policy reader hears: free uniforms work. The field practitioner hears: free uniforms worked when distributed by this particular partner under these particular conditions to this particular population. The gap between these two readings is where many "evidence-based" programs go to die.

Third, I want to push back on the framing of behavioral barriers. The authors describe parents who delay immunization as procrastinators whom small incentives can mobilize. In my experience, parents who delay immunization in the villages I have worked in have specific, often well-founded reasons: the clinic is often closed when they arrive; the nurse may be rude; their last child had a bad reaction; the rumor mill has been activated by some local controversy. The lentil incentive may overcome these reasons by raising the expected payoff of attending. But it does not address the reasons themselves. A program that fixes the clinic — that ensures it is open, that the nurse is present and respectful, that side effects are explained honestly — may produce equally large effects without bribing parents to overlook the real problems. We do not know, because the experimental literature has not, on the whole, randomized clinic quality with the same intensity it has randomized incentive structures. The result is a literature that emphasizes demand-side fixes for what are often supply-side failures.

Fourth: scale. The published RCTs tend to operate on populations of thousands or tens of thousands. National programs operate on populations of millions. Almost everything changes between these scales: the quality of staffing, the political visibility of the program, the capacity of monitoring systems, the integrity of supply chains, the temptations of corruption. Glennerster and Kremer treat scale-up as a relatively unproblematic extension of the trial. In practice, scale-up is a different operation entirely, and many cost-effective interventions in trials become mediocre or worse at national scale.

None of this means the experimental program is wrong. It means it is incomplete. The findings are real but they are inputs to design, not blueprints. The field needs more humility about the gap between trial and policy, more attention to implementation systems, and more partnership with practitioners who can see what the data alone cannot. Glennerster and Kremer would, I think, agree with most of this. I wish their essay had said it more loudly. "Small changes, big results" is a wonderful slogan. "Small changes, big results, sometimes, under conditions we do not fully understand, when implemented by partners we cannot always replicate" would be more accurate. It would also be more honest about what we have learned.

**The Behavioral Turn and the Macroeconomic Vacuum**

Glennerster and Kremer have produced an elegant summary of what randomized evaluations have taught us about individual decision-making among the global poor. The findings they describe — that small fees deter, that small incentives mobilize, that convenience matters more than the rational model predicts — are valuable contributions to the microeconomic understanding of poverty. I want to use this response to argue, however, that the very prominence of these findings is symptomatic of a worrying drift in development economics: away from the macroeconomic questions that actually determine the trajectory of nations and toward the microeconomic puzzles that lend themselves to clean identification.

Consider what we know about the past sixty years of development. The countries that have most dramatically reduced poverty — South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, more recently China, and to varying degrees Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia, and others — did so primarily through sustained economic growth driven by structural transformation: the movement of labor from low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity manufacturing and services. The policies that supported this transformation were not designed by behavioral economists. They involved exchange rate management, industrial policy, infrastructure investment, land reform, education systems geared toward an industrializing economy, and the painful construction of effective state institutions. None of these were tested in randomized trials. Many of them violated the contemporary best practice as understood by Western economists at the time.

By contrast, the countries that have remained mired in poverty — much of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Latin America, Haiti, Yemen — have generally failed at this macroeconomic transformation. They have remained dependent on commodity exports, have not built domestic manufacturing capacity, have suffered from chronic state weakness, and have seen their educated youth emigrate. Behavioral interventions can do little against these structural constraints. A chlorine dispenser does not industrialize a country.

This is the elephant in the room of the randomista program. Its central findings are about how individuals in poor countries can be helped to make marginally better decisions about specific health and education investments. They tell us little about why some countries grow rich and others do not. They tell us nothing about the speed of structural transformation. They cannot evaluate trade policy, exchange rate regimes, financial sector reform, or industrial strategy. And yet these are the policies that, historically, have determined whether nations enter modernity or remain trapped at its margin.

Glennerster and Kremer might respond that no one is claiming RCTs answer every question, and that the program is properly modest about its scope. Fair enough. But the discipline does not always behave with the modesty the authors recommend. Behavioral and experimental findings are increasingly setting the agenda for development funding, for graduate training, for the choice of dissertation topics, and for the policy advice donor agencies provide. A young economist today, choosing a research path, faces strong incentives to work on questions amenable to randomization and strong disincentives to work on questions of macroeconomic policy or political economy where causal identification is harder. Over time, the discipline becomes shaped by what its methods can study, and the questions its methods cannot study become marginalized.

There is also a quieter intellectual cost. The randomista literature has produced a stylized image of the poor — present-biased, sensitive to small incentives, easily mobilized by clever choice architecture — that has begun to displace earlier images, including the image of the poor as economic actors making rational decisions under harsh constraints. Both images contain truth. The earlier image was important because it pushed back against patronizing assumptions of cultural deficiency. The newer image is important because it pushes back against simplistic assumptions of perfect rationality. The question is which image is more useful for which purpose. For the design of small projects, the behavioral image works well. For the design of national strategy, the rational-actor image, with its emphasis on aggregate incentives and structural constraints, is often more illuminating. The current intellectual climate, in which behavioral findings are everywhere and macroeconomic analysis is recessed, suggests we have lost the balance.

Consider, finally, the user-fee debate that Glennerster and Kremer treat as a behavioral question. From a macroeconomic perspective, the question is different: what are the implications of free public health and education provision for the size of the state, for tax capacity, for the relationship between citizens and the fiscal contract? Free provision requires either taxation or external aid. Taxation requires state capacity, which is built over decades of political development. External aid creates dependencies and distorts political accountability. The behavioral finding — that user fees deter use — is one input into this larger question, not its answer. A country that abolishes fees on paper but cannot fund free provision adequately may end up with worse services than one that charged small fees and used the revenue to maintain quality. The trial does not settle this.

I do not want to be misunderstood. The randomista program has produced genuine knowledge, and the specific findings Glennerster and Kremer summarize are useful. I would happily endorse most of the policy implications they draw. But the development field needs to maintain a sense of proportion. Curing one child of worms is good. Building a state that no longer has worm epidemics is better. The first is achievable by a project; the second is the work of a generation. The current emphasis on the first risks teaching us how to manage poverty more effectively rather than how to end it.

The Stellaluna analogy with which the authors close — "How can we feel so different and be so much alike?" — is touching but misleading as a guide to policy. The relevant question for development is not whether Kenyans and Americans share psychological dispositions. They do. The relevant question is why one country has the institutions to make those dispositions productive and the other does not. The answer is not behavioral. It is structural, political, and historical, and the experimental program has, by design, almost nothing to say about it.

**Iteration as a Public Good**

Most of the commentary on the randomized-evaluation movement, including some of the criticism the movement has earned, focuses on what individual trials can or cannot prove. I want to suggest that this framing misses the most important contribution of the work Glennerster and Kremer describe. The deepest payoff of the experimental turn is not any particular finding. It is the construction of a cumulative, iterative research apparatus — something development economics has not previously possessed, and something that may, over time, matter more than any of the specific results it produces.

To see the point, consider the counterfactual. Before the experimental wave, development knowledge accumulated in a peculiar pattern. Big ideas — import substitution, structural adjustment, microfinance, the millennium villages — would arrive with great enthusiasm, get implemented at scale, generate ambiguous results, and eventually be displaced by the next big idea, often without anyone establishing definitively whether the previous one had worked. The pattern repeated every decade or two. Each cycle left behind some adherents, some skeptics, a great deal of folklore, and very little settled knowledge. Disagreements about whether the prior idea had failed because it was wrong in principle or wrong in execution were unresolvable, and so the field oscillated between fashions rather than converging on understanding.

The experimental turn breaks this pattern, not primarily because any single trial proves anything, but because the methodology supports incremental refinement. A study finds that free bed nets are taken up more than partially subsidized ones. A follow-up study examines whether the free recipients actually use them, and whether their use of free nets predicts later willingness to pay. A third study tests whether the social-norm effects spill over to neighbors. A fourth tries a delivery mechanism that combines free distribution with reminders. Each study is narrowly designed, each is open to critique, but the sequence is producing something that did not exist before: a structured body of knowledge in which subsequent investigation builds on, and corrects, prior findings.

This is what mature sciences do. Medicine reached this stage roughly a century ago. Agricultural science reached it earlier. Development economics, despite its long history, did not — not because its practitioners were less capable, but because its methods did not permit accumulation. The experimental turn is, finally, providing the missing infrastructure. The chlorine-dispenser program Glennerster and Kremer describe is itself a product of this iteration: an intervention designed in light of prior findings about price sensitivity, convenience, social norms, and waste, then tested rigorously, then refined. This is not a one-off study. It is the visible output of a research community that has learned how to learn.

This iterative capacity has implications for how we should think about external validity, the standard critique of the program. Critics ask: does a finding from Kenya transfer to Bangladesh? The honest answer is sometimes yes and sometimes no, and we cannot always tell in advance. But the iterative apparatus is itself the response. If we have built a research infrastructure capable of running a careful trial in Bangladesh next year, the question of transfer becomes empirically tractable in a way it was not before. The complaint that "you cannot generalize from one context" loses much of its force when the same methodology can be deployed across many contexts, with each new deployment refining our understanding of which features of context matter. We are building a map. Each trial is a survey point. The criticism that any one point fails to describe the whole territory is correct but beside the point.

The contrast with earlier development debates is striking. Consider the perennial argument about whether aid works in the aggregate. Sachs says yes, Easterly says no, decades pass, neither side moves. Cross-country regressions, sensitive to specification, cannot resolve the question. The aid effectiveness literature has consumed extraordinary intellectual resources and produced very little knowledge that anyone trusts. By contrast, the experimental literature on, say, conditional cash transfers has produced robust knowledge in less than two decades: we know that CCTs increase school enrollment, we have good estimates of the magnitudes, we understand how the effects vary with program design, and we can compare cost-effectiveness across delivery mechanisms. This is real progress. It does not answer the aggregate aid question, but it answers a specific operational question with a confidence that cross-country regressions can never provide.

I would push the authors further on one implication of all this. The infrastructure of the experimental program — the partnerships with NGOs, the trained field staff, the data systems, the protocols for IRB approval, the platforms like J-PAL and IPA — is itself a global public good. Maintaining it requires sustained investment, and the case for that investment is stronger than the case for any individual study. Donors and governments often think about evaluation as an add-on to programs. They should think about it as core infrastructure, like a statistical agency, that requires ongoing support whether or not any particular trial produces a politically convenient finding.

I would also urge more attention to the meta-question of how the field synthesizes its findings. As the number of trials grows, the bottleneck shifts from running trials to interpreting them collectively. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses become essential. So does a culture that treats null findings and failed replications as valuable rather than as failures. The experimental movement has not yet fully developed this culture; it remains too easy to publish positive results and too hard to publish their qualifications.

Glennerster and Kremer's essay is admirably free of triumphalism. They acknowledge what they do not know. They emphasize iteration and humility. I would only add that the iteration itself is the achievement worth defending most fiercely, against critics from the left who want grander narratives and critics from the right who want simpler verdicts. Knowledge that compounds is rare in social science. We have begun to build it in development. That is worth more than any of its individual products.
