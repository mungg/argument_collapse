Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's case for a twenty-first-century "two-step" politics is bracing and largely persuasive in its diagnosis. He is right that the state cannot be wished away, right that fossil capital represents a structural impediment whose defeat is a precondition for almost any serious progressive project, and right to insist on a strategic—rather than merely moral—calculus when ordering our priorities. Where I want to push, gently, is on whether the two-step framing itself can carry the weight he places on it.

The stagism analogy is more cautionary than Táíwò allows. The Russian Marxists he invokes are not just an instructive precedent; they are a warning. The "bourgeois revolution first, socialist revolution later" sequence produced, in the actual twentieth century, regimes that arrested at the first step and then mobilized the language of the second to suppress those who pressed on it. National independence movements in Asia and Africa likewise won the political step and then found—because the world economy did not pause to wait for them—that the socioeconomic step receded into the indefinite future. Samir Amin, whom Táíwò cites approvingly, spent much of his career documenting exactly this disappointment. The pattern is consistent enough that any contemporary stagism owes its proponents an account of why the second step would not, again, be deferred indefinitely once the first is won.

This worry sharpens when we ask what "dethroning fossil capital" actually denotes. Táíwò sometimes treats fossil capital as a discrete fraction of capital whose defeat would open political space for broader transformation. But the financial actors he singles out—BlackRock, Vanguard, sovereign wealth funds—are not fossil-specialist firms. They are diversified asset managers whose exposure to hydrocarbons is contingent and adjustable. They would happily pivot to "green capital" the moment doing so promised superior risk-adjusted returns, and they already issue ESG products on that premise. A successful energy transition that leaves the asset-manager society intact is entirely imaginable—indeed Daniela Gabor's "big green state" critique, which Táíwò endorses, describes its early shape. The danger is that we win the first step in a form that forecloses the second: a decarbonized but still privatized, financialized, and extractive economy that has simply migrated from oil rents to lithium, cobalt, and rare-earth rents.

There is a related concern about the suggestion that "racial justice, say, simply cannot be won in a world where fossil capital still reigns." This is offered as a possibility, not a thesis, but it matters because it underwrites the temporal hierarchy of the argument. The historical record is at best ambiguous. Most of the gains of the U.S. civil rights movement, of postwar feminisms, of decolonization, of disability and LGBTQ liberation, were won within the high noon of fossil capital. None of these victories were complete; none abolished the conditions that produced the injustices they targeted. But the suggestion that nothing meaningful can be done on these axes until fossil capital is defeated does not match the experience of the movements that produced those gains. Strategically, it also risks asking people whose lives are imperiled now to wait on a transition whose timeline is, by Táíwò's own account, civilizational.

What I think Táíwò's argument actually needs—and what it sometimes shows but does not articulate—is something less like sequencing and more like load-bearing parallelism. The Build Public Renewables Act in New York, the LADWP renewable target, the energy democracy project in Arkansas: these were not won by movements that bracketed racial, labor, and gender justice for a later stage. They were won by coalitions in which "gold-standard labor language," public ownership, and racial equity were constitutive of the climate demand itself. The political conditions under which fossil capital can actually be defeated turn out to be the conditions in which a broader justice politics is already operating. The two "steps" are entangled at the level of coalition-building, even if they are analytically distinguishable as outcomes.

The other place where the lead underspecifies is scale. Táíwò is admirably clear that climate is planetary while political agency is largely state-bound. But the move from "use the state" to "delink the world economy from fossil fuels" leaps over the absence of any institution capable of coordinating that delinking. The Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty is admirable but currently toothless; debt cancellation requires creditor consent the IMF and private bondholders are not eager to give. Without a more concrete account of how state-level victories aggregate into planetary delinking—against the grain of the very inter-state competition Táíwò names as a driver of emissions—the strategy rests on a hope that subnational and national wins will harmonize internationally.

None of this is reason to abandon the two-step. It is reason to hold the framing loosely: to treat it as a heuristic about where to apply pressure rather than as a temporal map, and to insist that the coalition that defeats fossil capital will be the coalition that has already begun building the world after it. Otherwise we will find, as our predecessors did, that step two has a way of never quite arriving.

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s call for a twenty-first-century “two-step” strategy—first dethrone fossil capital, then transform the world—is a necessary corrective to climate movements that either fetishize state power or retreat into a politics of pure moral witness. His central insight, that the climate crisis now constitutes the political condition of possibility for all other justice struggles, shifts the debate in a generative direction. I share his urgency, and I agree wholeheartedly that the organized political power of oil, gas, and coal interests must be decisively broken. But his prescription hinges on a wager: that the state, despite its deep entanglement with fossil capital, remains the likeliest path forward. That bet, as I’ll argue, underestimates the constitutive character of the state’s relationship to fossil capital and, by extension, the likelihood that state-centric strategies will deliver the required rupture.

Táíwò is careful to note that states have institutional interests in maintaining fossil fuel extraction, and he acknowledges the structural constraints that bind state managers to capitalist imperatives. Yet ultimately he returns to the state’s “creative” capacities—its ability to plan, direct investment, and provide public alternatives—as the decisive instruments for energy transition. The examples he offers, from an Arkansas school district’s solar panels to New York’s Build Public Renewables Act, are indeed inspiring. They demonstrate that public power can, in specific contexts, beat back private interests. But these victories remain fragile, localized, and embedded within a broader political economy that is actively hostile to their scaling. The same state that can install a municipal solar project today can be captured tomorrow by a fossil-friendly administration that repeals the legislation, privatizes the assets, and locks in another generation of extraction. The structural power of fossil capital does not rest on the fortunes of individual politicians or policies alone; it is woven into the fiscal architecture of the state, from sovereign wealth funds to tax codes to national security doctrines. Táíwò himself notes that even Norway’s progressive reputation cannot shield its sovereign wealth fund from the gravitational pull of oil revenues. If the state’s very financial stability depends on continued extraction, can we reasonably expect it to become the agent of that extraction’s defeat?

The deeper problem is not merely strategic but analytical. The modern state system was born alongside the expansion of fossil-fueled industrial capitalism, and its capacities for planning and coordination have been shaped by the demand to facilitate accumulation and manage its crises. Táíwò’s “two-step” logic treats the state as an instrument that can be turned against a fraction of capital—as if the state were a neutral vessel that progressive forces could simply seize and repurpose. But the state is not a container that can be filled with different content; it is itself a set of relationships that evolved to secure the conditions for capital accumulation, of which fossil capital is an integral part. This is not to say that state power is irrelevant—only that we should be sober about the scale of transformation needed to align state interests with a genuine energy transition. Public ownership without deep democratic redesign can reproduce the same extractive logics, just under different management. The Zapatista and Rojavan experiments that Táíwò acknowledges demonstrate that meaningful decommodification and delinking can also emerge from autonomous, counter-hegemonic spaces that explicitly refuse the state form, building alternative energy and economic systems from the ground up. While their atmospheric impact may be small in isolation, they prefigure a different planetary politics—one that does not require states as intermediaries.

This raises a related concern about temporal sequencing. Táíwò insists that we must not confuse the moral parity of different justice struggles with their temporal priority: first defeat fossil capital, and only then can racial, gender, and class justice become winnable. But this sequencing risks repeating the stagist mistakes he otherwise critiques. The fossil fuel industry and the carceral state, for example, are mutually reinforcing systems; pipelines and prisons are both built on stolen Indigenous land, and communities of color are disproportionately burdened by both extraction and policing. To ask movements for racial justice to postpone their demands until after fossil capital is politically defeated is to misunderstand the entangled nature of these systems. In practice, the most promising climate justice initiatives are those that refuse such artificial sequencing. The Build Public Renewables Act in New York paired renewable targets with “gold-standard labor language” and union jobs; the Los Angeles Department of Water and Power’s renewable goal emerged from coalitional work involving climate, labor, and racial justice groups. In other words, the first step is not a single, monolithic battle against a neatly defined fossil capital but an ongoing, multifaceted struggle that integrates multiple fronts simultaneously.

Finally, Táíwò’s emphasis on state-led delinking at the national level leaves the international dimension underspecified. He rightly points to the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty and debt cancellation as vital, but these multilateral efforts remain trapped within an interstate system that is structurally rigged in favor of wealthy, high-emitting nations. The same state that might champion a just transition at home can, through development finance and trade agreements, offshore its emissions and extractive footprint. A twenty-first-century delinking must be genuinely planetary, but it cannot rely solely on states whose sovereignty is inextricable from the fossilized world order. Here, transnational movement-building—from the Cochabamba process to Indigenous-led global networks—offers a more promising model for building counter-power across borders.

None of this is to dismiss state-oriented tactics outright. Phasing out fossil fuels will indeed require legislative and regulatory intervention at multiple scales, from municipal public power to national decarbonization mandates. But elevating the state as the strategic linchpin of a two-step politics risks reinforcing the very infrastructure that has stalled progress for decades. The task before us is not simply to dethrone fossil capital and then transform the world, but to begin transforming the political forms we inherit—including the state—even as we fight to dismantle the fossil economy. A more pluralistic strategy, one that combines autonomous institution-building, direct action, and selective engagement with state institutions while maintaining an oppositional stance toward state power itself, might just prove more resilient than a single-minded drive to win state power for the next step. In the end, the question is not whether the state has a role to play, but whether we can afford to let the state define the game.

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s compelling defense of a twenty-first-century “two-step” strategy offers a much-needed strategic anchor for the climate left. Facing an escalating planetary crisis, we do not have the luxury of time to first build a utopian society from scratch, nor can we rely solely on localized, autonomous zones to dismantle the global hegemony of fossil capital. The emissions of ExxonMobil and the US military, as Táíwò rightly notes, respect no borders. Thus, the state—with its unparalleled capacity to coordinate production, mandate phaseouts, and channel vast resources—must be the primary vehicle for achieving step one: the total political defeat of the fossil fuel industry. Only once that existential threat is neutralized, the argument goes, can we realistically proceed to step two: transforming the world along broader emancipatory lines.

While Táíwò’s diagnosis of the necessity of state power is unassailable, his sequential division of labor—first dethrone fossil capital, then transform the world—carries a profound historical and structural risk. The state is not a neutral vessel waiting to be filled with the right administrative directives. It is a deeply path-dependent institution, historically engineered to facilitate capital accumulation, manage borders, and secure resources through coercion. If the climate left treats emancipatory transformation as a secondary project to be deferred until after the energy transition is secured, we risk allowing the means of our survival to foreclose the possibility of our liberation.

The central danger of a sequential approach lies in what happens when the state is tasked with a massive industrial reorganization without being simultaneously democratized and reoriented away from its extractive logic. If the imperative is merely to delink the world economy from fossil fuels, the modern capitalist state will default to the path of least resistance: partnering with, and empowering, non-fossil fractions of capital. We can already see this dynamic unfolding in real time. The flagship climate policies of the Global North, such as the Inflation Reduction Act in the United States, do not seek to overcome the logic of private accumulation; they merely attempt to redirect it. By showering “green” capital with subsidies and de-risking private investment, the state is engineering a transition that keeps the underlying architecture of economic power intact.

Replacing fossil capital with “green” capital is not a defeat of the extractive system, but a mere changing of the guard. The rush to secure the critical minerals necessary for a renewable energy transition—lithium, cobalt, copper, and nickel—has already triggered a new wave of neocolonial extraction in the Global South. From the lithium triangle of South America to the mining belts of Central Africa, the imperatives of “energy security” for developed states are resulting in land grabs, ecological degradation, and the violent displacement of Indigenous and frontline communities. If our first step relies on the state to rapidly scale up clean energy without fundamentally altering its relationship to the Global South, we will simply replace a petro-state imperial order with a green-imperial one. A transition that sacrifices the periphery to decarbonize the core is not a stepping stone to justice; it is a reinscription of the very colonial dynamics Táíwò warns against.

Therefore, the battle to transform the state cannot wait until after fossil capital is defeated. The two steps must be compressed into one. The state’s creative capacities must be leveraged, but this requires an active, simultaneous struggle to dismantle its commitments to capital accumulation and imperial border enforcement.

Táíwò’s own examples point toward this necessity. He highlights the New York Build Public Renewables Act as a model of using the state to phase out fossil fuels while building public capacity. But what makes this legislation so powerful is precisely that it refused to separate the energy transition from broader emancipatory demands. By mandating gold-standard labor language and focusing on public ownership, the organizers did not just seek to replace natural gas with solar; they sought to decommodify power and democratize the workplace. They did not accept a two-step compromise where a private green developer would build the infrastructure now, with the promise of unionizing later. The utopian demand—public control and worker empowerment—was the very mechanism through which the climate victory was won.

Furthermore, deferring broader struggles for justice until after the energy transition ignores the political reality that marginalized communities are the very engines of the climate movement. As Táíwò notes, climate change directly affects the working conditions of farmworkers and drives mass displacement. But to ask these frontline communities to subordinate their immediate struggles against racialized state violence, border imperialism, or economic exploitation in the name of a sequenced victory against fossil capital is both politically unviable and morally fraught. A state that is empowered to execute a top-down energy transition while its carceral and militaristic apparatuses remain fully intact is a state primed for eco-authoritarianism. In an era of increasing climate migration, border militarization and xenophobic nationalism are already merging with narratives of resource scarcity.

The defeat of fossil capital is indeed the material precondition for a livable future. Táíwò is absolutely correct that abdication of the state is a strategic dead end. But history has repeatedly demonstrated that the architecture we build to win a crisis becomes the architecture we must live within. If we construct a massive, green-capitalist security state to solve the emissions crisis, we may find that step two has become permanently out of reach. We must build the world we want through the very process of saving it. Delinking from fossil fuels must be inextricably bound up with delinking from the logics of empire, extraction, and exploitation. There is no waiting for the other side of the crisis; the transformation must be the transition itself.

Táíwò is right that climate politics cannot be reduced to “more renewables, please.” Fossil fuels are not merely an energy source; they are a political bloc, a revenue stream, a geopolitical architecture, a set of sunk assets, and a habit of statecraft. Any climate strategy that treats oil, gas, and coal as technical problems rather than organized power will be defeated by organized power. The last decade has made this plain. Solar and wind have become astonishingly cheap, electric vehicles have moved from fantasy to mass market, and yet fossil fuel production has not obediently withered. In many places it has expanded alongside renewables. “All of the above” is not confusion; it is the governing compromise between climate concern and fossil power.

But I worry that the formula “first dethrone fossil capital; then transform the world” risks preserving too much of the old two-step problem even as it updates the target. The danger is not that it takes the state too seriously. It should. The danger is that it imagines the political defeat of fossil capital as separable from the broader transformations that would make such a defeat durable, legitimate, and materially possible. Fossil capital will not be dethroned first so that justice can later arrive. It will be dethroned, if at all, through partial and contested forms of justice that build the coalition, infrastructure, and state capacity necessary to keep going.

The distinction matters because fossil fuels are embedded in ordinary life as well as elite balance sheets. Of course ExxonMobil, Saudi Aramco, Gazprom, and the coal lobby have interests that must be broken. But the political power of fossil capital does not rest only on lobbying, campaign donations, and disinformation. It also rests on the fact that millions of people heat homes, commute to work, depend on freight systems, and hold jobs in economies built around fossil energy. When climate policy appears as scarcity, punishment, or technocratic moralism, the right gains an opening. The Yellow Vest protests in France were not simply an anti-climate revolt, but they did show what happens when decarbonization is experienced as a tax on rural and working-class mobility while wealth remains visibly protected. A politics that dethrones fossil capital must therefore be redistributive from the beginning, not after the main battle has been won.

This is why the strongest parts of Táíwò’s argument are the examples of public power and energy democracy. The Arkansas school solar project, New York’s Build Public Renewables Act, and municipal renewable targets are not just “climate” measures. They are experiments in making decarbonization socially legible as better wages, public revenue, cleaner air, and democratic control. They begin to answer the question that haunts every Green New Deal proposal: who, concretely, benefits before the atmospheric benefits arrive? The answer cannot be only “future generations” or “humanity.” It must also be teachers, tenants, bus riders, nurses, construction workers, farmers, and communities living next to refineries and highways.

The state is indispensable here, but not because it is inherently more public-spirited than markets. States built the fossil world. They licensed extraction, subsidized highways, protected shipping lanes, invaded oil-producing regions, insured pipelines, and criminalized protest. They also own or control much of the world’s fossil production. The obstacle is therefore not simply private fossil capital capturing an otherwise neutral state. Often the state is a fossil actor in its own right. Petrostates, coal-dependent provinces, militaries, and public utilities can be as committed to carbon-intensive development as private firms. A strategy centered on the state must therefore be a strategy for changing the state, not merely using it.

That requires movements capable of imposing new interests on public institutions. The Inflation Reduction Act in the United States illustrates both the promise and the limit. It mobilizes large-scale public incentives for clean energy manufacturing, batteries, and electrification, but it largely does so by trying to induce private investment rather than by planning a public energy transition. It also coexists with new oil and gas leasing, liquefied natural gas export politics, and a geopolitical scramble over critical minerals. Still, it has helped create constituencies for decarbonization: workers in battery plants, states seeking manufacturing investment, households installing heat pumps, local governments chasing federal funds. These constituencies are not automatically egalitarian or anti-fossil. But they are part of the terrain on which fossil capital can be weakened.

The same is true globally. China’s vast investment in solar, wind, batteries, transmission, and electric vehicles has changed the material possibilities of climate politics for everyone. It has lowered costs and accelerated deployment. Yet China remains deeply dependent on coal, in part because state planners prioritize industrial stability, energy security, and provincial growth. Europe has expanded renewables while scrambling for gas after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. India faces the moral and practical problem of development under carbon constraint: hundreds of millions need more reliable energy, while coal remains politically powerful and renewables increasingly attractive. These cases suggest that “dethroning fossil capital” cannot mean one identical sequence everywhere. It must mean constructing reliable, affordable, low-carbon systems fast enough that fossil incumbency becomes unnecessary as well as illegitimate.

That brings us to the question of time. Táíwò is right that climate change is now eroding the conditions for emancipatory politics. Heat, crop failures, storms, migration pressures, insurance retreat, and fiscal shocks all strengthen authoritarian temptations. But this urgency cuts both ways. It means we cannot wait for a purified anti-fossil state before transforming housing, transport, agriculture, disaster response, and care. Adaptation is not secondary to mitigation for the people already living through climate disruption. Nor is it politically optional. A state that asks for sacrifice in the name of decarbonization while failing to protect people from fires, floods, heat, and displacement will not maintain consent for long.

The most persuasive climate politics, then, is not exactly two-step. It is iterative. Build public goods that reduce dependence on fossil fuels. Use those public goods to create constituencies for stronger phaseout. Use stronger phaseout to weaken fossil capital’s fiscal and ideological grip. Use that weakened grip to democratize investment, expand social provision, and reduce wasteful consumption by the rich. Repeat under worsening climatic conditions, with internationalism wherever possible and unilateral action where necessary.

This does not soften the confrontation with fossil capital. On the contrary, it clarifies how such confrontation can be won. Bans on new fossil infrastructure, public renewable buildout, debt cancellation, green industrial policy, free and electrified public transit, building retrofits, heat protections for workers, and public ownership are not separate items on a progressive wish list. They are mutually reinforcing ways of changing what people need, what states can do, and what capital can demand.

The old two-step failed when the first step became an excuse to postpone liberation. A climate two-step will fail if “dethroning fossil capital” becomes too narrow, too national, or too indifferent to the social content of transition. The task is not to choose between seizing state power and building democratic alternatives, between emissions cuts and justice, between realism and utopia. The task is to make each the condition of the other. Fossil capital must be defeated. But the only force strong enough to defeat it is a transformed society already struggling to be born.

The essay's central argument—that dethroning fossil capital is the prerequisite for any broader transformative politics—rests on a compelling diagnosis of our present moment. Táíwò is correct that the structural power of fossil fuel interests represents a formidable obstacle to climate justice, and that any strategy indifferent to this reality is likely to fail. Yet the essay's prescription for a twenty-first-century "two-step" politics warrants closer scrutiny, particularly regarding the assumption that state capture by fossil capital can be reversed through conventional political contestation.

Táíwò acknowledges the historical record of state-aligned fossil fuel expansion, noting that even ostensibly progressive governments like Norway's have struggled to delink from extractive logics. This is not merely a failure of political will but reflects something deeper: the structural position of petrostates within global commodity chains creates powerful material interests that no electoral outcome alone can dismantle. These are not steps toward seizing state power so much as steps toward building public capacity that remains answerable to popular constituencies.

The Zapatistas and Rojava experiments are mentioned as examples of withdrawal rather than engagement, yet their emphasis on prefigurative politics—building the world you want to see within the cracks of the present—offers a supplementary logic rather than a rival strategy.

Táíwò argues that justice struggles must be sequenced such that fossil capital's defeat becomes the enabling condition for progress on race, gender, and class. But the climate crisis does not respect sequencing. A strategy that subordinates present struggles to an anticipated political opening risks abandoning those most vulnerable to climate harm in the interim.

What the essay gets right is that fossil capital's structural power cannot be overcome by moral suasion or market mechanisms alone. But the response to this reality need not be a teleological "two-step" that centralizes state power as the agent of transformation. Instead, climate politics might proceed through what the essay itself gestures toward: coalitions that build public renewable capacity while embedding it in accountability structures, international initiatives like the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty that constrain fossil expansion through treaty mechanisms rather than individual state capture, and movements that maintain pressure from below even as they contest state power above.

Táíwò concludes that "enough steps in the right direction get you to where you're going." This is true. But the direction matters as much as the steps, and a strategy that centralizes state power without robust mechanisms for popular accountability risks taking steps that lead toward state socialism's twentieth-century disappointments rather than toward genuine emancipation. The climate crisis demands audacity. It also demands humility about what state power alone can accomplish.

**The State Will Not Save Us: An Anarchist Reply to Táíwò**

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò has written an elegant and serious essay, and its seriousness deserves a serious answer. His argument can be summarized briskly: fossil capital is the load-bearing wall of the present world, and only state power is heavy enough to pull it down. Therefore, whatever our long-term suspicions of the state, we must in the short and medium term contest for it. First dethrone fossil capital; then transform the world.

The argument is appealing because it is honest about timelines. Greenhouse gases are a stock; the atmosphere does not wait for our prefigurative experiments to mature. But the argument is also dangerous in a way Táíwò himself half-acknowledges before steering past. Every twentieth-century version of "first take state power, then transform" produced its own permanent first stage. The "workers' state" became the state. The anticolonial state became the postcolonial bourgeoisie's instrument. The Bolivarian state became a petrostate. Why should we believe the climate variant will end differently?

Táíwò's response is essentially: because the alternative is worse, and because the state is the only entity with the scale of organized coercion necessary to break the oil majors. This is the same argument made by every reformer who ever picked up the master's tools. It has the virtue of plausibility and the vice of being wrong in a particular way: it consistently underestimates how thoroughly the tools shape the user.

Consider what "dethroning fossil capital" via the state would actually require. It would require a state apparatus capable of expropriating the largest, best-defended fraction of global capital in human history. Such an apparatus does not currently exist anywhere. To build it, one would need either (a) to capture an existing state and then radically expand its repressive and planning capacities, or (b) to construct new state forms from movements. Option (a) is the Chinese path Táíwò himself notes is exceptional. Option (b) requires the very prefigurative, dual-power, autonomous organizing that the "two-step" essay treats as a charming distraction from the main event in Chiapas and Rojava.

The Zapatistas and the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria are not, in fact, side dishes to the real meal of state climate politics. They are existence proofs that a different relation between human beings, land, and energy is possible at scale, under fire, without the mediation of a sovereign apparatus. Táíwò asks the skeptics of state politics to "explain how that abdication fits into a political trajectory that ends in the political defeat of fossil capital on a planetary scale." This is the wrong question. The right question is: what kind of political subjects must exist in order for fossil capital to be defeated and stay defeated? A passive electorate that votes correctly once every few years will never sustain that defeat. Only organized communities with material capacity to live differently will.

Here the anarchist tradition has an empirical, not merely ideological, point to make. Look at what has actually slowed fossil extraction over the past fifteen years. It is not the COP process. It is not the Inflation Reduction Act, which is a subsidy regime for capital with fossil carve-outs baked in. It is Standing Rock. It is the ZAD at Notre-Dame-des-Landes. It is the Wet'suwet'en land defenders. It is Lützerath. It is the dozens of pipeline projects, LNG terminals, and coal mines cancelled or delayed because people put their bodies between extraction and capital. These movements draw on traditions of direct action that explicitly refuse the legitimacy of the state's claim to mediate the relationship between humans and the earth.

Táíwò will respond that these are tactical victories that buy time but do not amount to "delinking the world economy from fossil fuels." Fair enough. But neither does any actually-existing state policy. The choice is not between effective state action and ineffective movement action. It is between two incomplete strategies, and the question is which one builds the capacities required for the next phase rather than dissipating them.

State-centric climate politics tends to dissipate movement capacity. It professionalizes activism into lobbying. It teaches movements to ask rather than to take. It binds the time horizons of organizers to electoral cycles. When the favored party loses—as it inevitably does, eventually—the movement finds it has built no autonomous infrastructure to fall back on. This is the story of the U.S. climate movement after 2016 and the British climate movement after 2019. The "two-step" turns out to have only one step, repeated indefinitely: lose elections, mourn, organize for the next one.

There is also a more uncomfortable point. Táíwò's framing presumes that the state's interests can be aligned with the people's against capital. But the modern state was constituted in and through fossil capital. Timothy Mitchell's *Carbon Democracy* made this case more than a decade ago: mass democratic politics in the twentieth-century North was made possible by the choke points coal workers could seize, and was then defanged by the transition to oil, which routed energy flows around any possible point of labor leverage. The state form we inherit is an oil state. Asking it to dethrone its own metabolic basis is asking it to commit suicide. Some states might do this. Most will not.

What follows? Not quietism. Not pure withdrawal. The anarchist position, properly understood, is not that we must refuse all engagement with state institutions but that we must never confuse such engagement with the building of liberatory power. Where a state policy will materially reduce emissions or protect frontline communities, support it; where state repression will fall on land defenders, defend them. But the strategic center of gravity must remain with the construction of autonomous, federated, place-based capacities to feed, house, power, and defend ourselves outside the wage-and-state nexus. Those capacities are what survive the next authoritarian turn, the next financial crisis, the next state retreat from climate commitments under fiscal pressure.

Táíwò ends his essay by saying that aligning state interests with the public's would be "a giant step—in itself a world-historical achievement, easily the task of a generation." I agree. That is precisely why I would rather we spent that generation on something that can actually outlast a single electoral cycle. The state is too brittle and too compromised a vessel for what we need. The atmosphere is patient enough to wait for us to notice this, and impatient enough to punish us if we do not.

**Yes, And: A Friendly Amendment from the Degrowth Left**

Táíwò's essay is the best short statement I have read of a position I largely share: that the climate crisis is, in the first instance, a political problem about the organized power of fossil capital, and that defeating that power requires using, rather than abandoning, the state. I want to push the argument further in a direction Táíwò gestures toward but does not fully embrace: the energy transition cannot be a like-for-like substitution of clean kilowatt-hours for dirty ones. It must involve a substantial reduction in aggregate energy and material throughput in the rich world. Without that, the "two-step" politics he proposes will fail not because it loses the political fight but because it wins the wrong one.

Start with the arithmetic. Global primary energy consumption in 2023 was around 620 exajoules, of which roughly 80% was fossil. The remaining 20% includes hydro, nuclear, biomass, and the still-modest renewable share. To replace the fossil share at current consumption levels with wind and solar would require building several times the entire existing renewable fleet, plus storage, plus transmission, plus the mining capacity to source the lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earths required, plus the industrial capacity to manufacture and install the panels, turbines, batteries, and HVDC lines. The IEA's own scenarios concede that some of these material requirements have no precedent. Some categories—copper especially—face physical depletion of high-grade ores within a few decades.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a counsel of honesty. The energy transition that is physically possible in the next two decades is one in which absolute energy demand in the rich world falls substantially while clean supply ramps up. "Green growth" at current global GDP growth rates is not a serious proposal; it is a marketing slogan attached to the same growth model that produced the crisis.

Táíwò knows this. He cites the degrowth literature respectfully. But his strategic argument tilts away from its implications. The "two-step" formulation suggests that once fossil capital is defeated, we will have the political space to debate growth, distribution, and ecological limits. I worry this gets the sequencing backwards. The political coalitions capable of defeating fossil capital are precisely those that will be unwilling to accept a stationary or contracting economy. The labor unions that Táíwò rightly centers, the social-democratic parties that might build "big green states," the renewable-energy capitalists who could be peeled off from the oil majors—all of these constituencies have a material interest in a high-throughput green economy. None has an interest in a low-throughput one.

What follows is that "first dethrone fossil capital, then transform the world" risks producing a transformation that consolidates rather than dissolves the social relations of capitalism. We get electric SUVs, AI data centers running on solar, and lithium extracted from Chilean salt flats and Indonesian rainforests at colonial terms of trade. Carbon dioxide concentrations stabilize, but every other planetary boundary keeps blowing through its threshold. This is not a hypothetical. This is the trajectory the existing "green industrial policy" of the U.S., E.U., and China is on.

The honest answer is that we need a "two-and-a-half-step" politics: dethrone fossil capital; simultaneously contest the material composition of the replacement; then transform the rest. The middle step cannot be deferred because the infrastructure being built now will lock in patterns of consumption for forty to sixty years. A solar-powered colonial extraction regime is a different and slightly less terrible thing than an oil-powered one, but it is not what climate justice means.

Where I do agree with Táíwò, emphatically, is on the centrality of state power to this project. Degrowth has too often been articulated as a politics of personal renunciation or local self-sufficiency. That tendency is rightly criticized as politically empty. You cannot localize semiconductor supply chains. You cannot prefigure a high-speed rail network. You cannot, by lifestyle choice alone, decommission a coal plant. The reduction of throughput must be planned, coordinated, and democratically authorized—which is to say, it must be statist, even as it expands democratic control over what states do.

This is why I think the most important debates in climate politics are not between degrowthers and Green New Dealers but between both camps and the financialized "transition" agenda that dominates official policy. Daniela Gabor's work, which Táíwò cites, is essential here. The "de-risking state" is the actual political form of the energy transition under current conditions, and it is structurally incapable of either rapid decarbonization or just distribution. Public power—as both ownership and ethos—is the alternative.

One last point. Táíwò argues that fossil capital must be defeated because it is the structural barrier to other justice projects. I would invert the framing slightly: fossil capital must be defeated, but defeating it is not sufficient to achieve any of those other projects, including the ecological project. The same political-economic logic that produced fossil capital—endless accumulation organized through commodified labor and nature—will produce its successor monster if not itself dismantled. Lithium capital, data-center capital, geoengineering capital. The names change; the function does not.

What this means practically is that the movements building toward the first step have to keep one eye on the second. A worker-led nationalization of an oil company is good; a worker-led nationalization that maintains the firm's drilling schedule under public branding is bad. A green industrial policy that creates union jobs is good; a green industrial policy that locks in suburban sprawl, private car dependence, and tropical mineral extraction is mostly bad. The criteria for evaluating "two-step" tactics cannot be reduced to "does this hurt fossil capital." They must include "does this build the political and material capacity for the second step."

Táíwò's essay opens this conversation rather than closes it. The strategic clarity he insists on—that we must concentrate fire on the fossil bloc rather than dispersing it across every justice front—is correct, and the left is much weaker than it admits for failing to internalize it. I want only to add: concentration of fire is not the same as narrowing of vision. We can know what we are aiming at and still know why we are aiming.

**The Two-Step Has Been Tried**

I read Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's lead essay with the strange feeling of reading a manifesto for something that has already happened. The "two-step" politics he proposes—first defeat the dominant fraction of capital using state power, then proceed to broader transformation—is not a novel strategic proposal awaiting our adoption. It is, more or less, the program of every social-democratic and developmentalist government of the past hundred years. The question is not whether to try it. The question is why every previous attempt has failed at the second step.

Take Norway, which Táíwò mentions with the appropriate ambivalence. Norway is a state that has, by most measures, achieved the precondition Táíwò describes: a strong democratic state with substantial public ownership in the energy sector, a sovereign wealth fund that gives it macroeconomic independence from global capital markets, a labor movement deeply integrated into governance, and a citizenry that consistently votes for parties of the left. If any state in the world is institutionally positioned to "dethrone fossil capital and then transform the world," it is Norway. Yet Norway is one of the largest per-capita oil and gas producers on earth, has approved new exploration in the Arctic as recently as this year, and has structured its entire welfare state around the continuation of hydrocarbon revenues.

The Norwegian case is not a story about the difficulty of step one. Norway already controls its fossil sector. It is a story about the impossibility, under current conditions, of using that control against the sector's logic. The state has been thoroughly metabolized by the industry it nominally directs. The pension funds of Norwegian workers depend on continued extraction. The fiscal base of Norwegian social democracy depends on continued extraction. Norwegian voters know perfectly well what the climate science says and elect governments that continue extraction anyway.

If the "two-step" strategy fails in Norway—where every supposed precondition holds—why would it succeed elsewhere? Táíwò's answer would presumably be that Norway has not yet been gripped by a movement of sufficient force to make the second step politically actionable. Perhaps. But this restates the problem rather than solving it. The forces that would compel a Norwegian-style state to act against its own fossil base are precisely the forces that the "two-step" strategy proposes to build through the first step. The strategy is circular: we need the state to defeat fossil capital, but the state will not defeat fossil capital until we have built movements strong enough to do it ourselves, at which point we did not need the two-step in the first place.

The same circularity haunts the British case. The Corbyn project Táíwò invokes had a serious climate platform—the Green New Deal motion at Labour conference in 2019, the £250 billion National Investment Bank, public ownership of energy. It lost, and it lost in part because the structural power of finance and the media—both of which are intricately bound up with fossil capital—mobilized to make sure it lost. The lesson is not that the next attempt should be more committed to electoral state-seizure. The lesson is that the channels through which state power must be won are themselves shaped by the interests that state power is supposed to confront. You cannot win an election against fossil capital while operating in a media-financial-political ecosystem that fossil capital substantially owns.

Latin America offers a fuller case study. The "pink tide" governments of the 2000s and 2010s came closer to the two-step model than anything outside of historical state-socialism. Lula, Morales, Correa, Chávez—each used commodity rents to expand redistribution, sometimes dramatically. Poverty fell. Health and education indicators improved. And yet not one of these governments managed even to slow the expansion of its country's extractive frontier, let alone reverse it. Morales, the most ideologically committed to indigenous ecological politics, presided over a doubling of Bolivian hydrocarbon output. Correa pursued the YasunĂ­-ITT initiative and then drilled the YasunĂ­ anyway. Lula's current government is auctioning offshore oil blocks near the mouth of the Amazon. The reason is simple and devastating: the redistributive politics that secured the political base of these governments was funded by extraction. They could not finance the social spending that kept them in office without continuing the extraction that destroyed the planet their constituents depend on.

This is the trap Táíwò's essay does not adequately confront. The states most likely to be captured by movements opposed to fossil capital are also the states most fiscally dependent on fossil revenues. The richest, most fiscally diversified states could in principle afford the transition but are also the ones most thoroughly integrated into global capital markets and least susceptible to the kind of leftward swing the strategy requires. The geographic distribution of the political opportunity and the geographic distribution of the political necessity do not overlap.

What would actually break this trap? I suspect the honest answer is: international transfers on a scale that has never been politically achievable. The fossil-dependent states of the Global South cannot transition without massive concessional finance from the North. The fossil-rich states of the North cannot mobilize politically to provide it without a degree of internationalist solidarity their populations have never shown. The "two-step" strategy, even if pursued nationally, runs aground on this distributional problem at the planetary scale. Cochabamba pointed to it. So did the loss and damage debate at Sharm el-Sheikh. So does the abject inadequacy of the $100 billion climate finance pledge.

I do not say this to counsel despair. I say it because I think Táíwò's framework, by emphasizing the state as the unit of strategy, accidentally encourages a methodological nationalism that the problem does not permit. The carbon cycle is planetary. The state system, even at its best, is not. Climate politics has to be conducted both at the level of state-building and at the level of building cross-border solidarities that can compel transfers between states. The latter is much weaker, much harder, and much less theorized than the former. It is also the part the twentieth-century two-step strategists never figured out, which is one reason their step two so often shrank to step one.

I do not have a tidy alternative. I have a worry: that the "two-step" framing reassures us that we know what we are doing strategically when in fact we do not, and that this reassurance allows us to defer the harder conceptual work of imagining a politics adequate to a planetary problem. Táíwò's essay is brave for being so clear about strategy. It would be braver still to admit that the clarity may be premature.

**Fossil Capital Is a Symptom, Not the Disease**

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's essay is a clarifying contribution to climate strategy debates, and I want to register both my appreciation and my disagreement with what I take to be its central premise: that "fossil capital" names the specific antagonist whose political defeat constitutes the strategic objective of our era. I think this framing, though it has the considerable virtue of focusing the mind, mistakes a symptom for the disease, and that the strategic implications of getting this wrong are nontrivial.

What is "fossil capital"? In Andreas Malm's formulation, from which Táíwò draws, it is the fraction of capital whose accumulation is bound up with the extraction, processing, and combustion of fossil hydrocarbons. The category is meant to do explanatory work: to identify a specific set of firms, financiers, and political actors whose material interests run against decarbonization and who can therefore be targeted as the proximate political enemy. This is analytically helpful and politically motivating.

But it is also misleading in two important ways.

First, "fossil capital" is not a coherent class fraction with stable interests. The oil majors are diversifying into petrochemicals, hydrogen, and—erratically—renewables. Saudi Aramco is investing in Korean battery firms. BP and Shell oscillate between net-zero pledges and walking them back. The asset managers that own large stakes in the oil sector—BlackRock, Vanguard, State Street—also own large stakes in the renewable energy sector, the automotive sector, the utility sector, and basically everything else. Their interest is not in oil per se but in the maintenance of returns across a diversified portfolio. If the rate of return on renewables exceeds that on oil for long enough, capital will rotate. It is already doing so at the margin.

This matters because it changes what "defeating" fossil capital would mean. You cannot expropriate "fossil capital" as a discrete entity because, as a discrete entity, it barely exists outside polemical convenience. You can shut down particular fields, refineries, and pipelines. You can revoke leases. You can tax windfalls. But you are not confronting a unified bloc; you are confronting moves on a chessboard played by capital in general, which has multiple pieces and many openings.

Second, and more importantly: even if fossil capital were a coherent enemy and even if we defeated it, we would not have addressed the structural drivers of climate breakdown. Those drivers are the imperatives of accumulation under capitalism—the pressure to grow output, to externalize costs, to convert anything into a commodity if there is a margin to be made. These imperatives produced the fossil economy because fossil energy was the cheapest way to power industrial accumulation. Remove fossils and the imperatives remain. They will find new substrates: lithium, cobalt, biomass, oceanic mining, atmospheric carbon removal as a tradeable commodity, biodiversity offsets, water markets.

I worry that "two-step" politics, by naming fossil capital as the specific antagonist, underwrites the conclusion that a post-fossil capitalism would be a habitable planet. It would not. The IPCC's own scenarios for staying within 1.5°C or 2°C of warming require, in addition to fossil phaseout, dramatic reductions in deforestation, methane from agriculture, nitrous oxide from fertilizers, and the energy intensity of consumption itself. None of these is reducible to defeating the oil and gas companies. Some of them—industrial agriculture, for instance—involve capital fractions that are politically allied with the climate-friendly center-left in many countries.

The deeper issue is that the planetary crisis is plural. Climate is one of nine planetary boundaries that the Stockholm Resilience Centre has identified, and we have already crossed six. Biodiversity, biogeochemical flows, freshwater, land system change, novel entities (including microplastics and forever chemicals), and climate. A "two-step" strategy focused solely on the energy transition leaves five of these boundaries unaddressed and, in some scenarios, accelerates the violation of them. A massive global buildout of renewable capacity within current accumulation logics will worsen the mining frontier, freshwater stress in semi-arid regions, and biodiversity loss in tropical forests cleared for biomass and minerals.

What would a strategy that took seriously the plurality of crises look like? At minimum, it would refuse the singular framing of "fossil capital" as the strategic enemy. It would instead identify the social relation of accumulation as the strategic object, with fossil energy as a particularly urgent expression rather than a freestanding antagonist. This is the classical ecosocialist position—the position of John Bellamy Foster, of Jason Moore, of the more rigorous degrowth theorists—and it generates different tactical guidance.

For instance: a "two-step" climate politics naturally allies with green capital against fossil capital. An ecosocialist politics is more skeptical of green capital, because green capital's interests are continuous with the accumulation logic that will produce the next ecological catastrophe. The two strategies converge on opposing the Keystone XL pipeline; they diverge on a lithium mine in Nevada. The "two-step" strategist supports it (substitutes for fossil inputs); the ecosocialist asks who owns it, what its water use is, whether the labor is unionized, whether the local Indigenous community consented, and whether the lithium will go to electrify private cars or expand public transit.

Táíwò might respond that this is precisely the work of "step two" and that we should keep our eye on the prize in step one. But the infrastructure built in step one will determine the political economy of step two. The lithium mines, the data centers, the offshore wind farms with corporate purchasers, the carbon capture pipelines—each is a node in a future political economy that will resist reorganization once built. There is no clean break between the two steps. The build-out is itself a politics.

I want to be careful not to caricature Táíwò's position. He acknowledges, in the essay's later sections, that the state we win would not in itself deliver justice and that the work of broader transformation continues. My point is that this acknowledgment sits uneasily with the strategic prioritization the essay also advances. If we genuinely believe that fossil defeat does not deliver the broader transformation, then our strategy in the first phase has to be one that builds the constituencies and infrastructures for the second phase. That is not a "two-step" strategy. It is a continuous one in which the rhetoric of stages distracts from the unitary problem of accumulation.

The diagnostic shift I am proposing is small, but its consequences are large. It changes the answer to the question: who is the enemy? Not "fossil capital" but capital, in its current ecological phase. It also changes the answer to: who is the friend? Not "green capital" but the working classes—including the workers of the oil patch and the workers of the solar fields—whose material interests in habitability and dignified work cut across the green-fossil divide. The two-step framing accidentally narrows our coalition by widening our enemy list within capital and narrowing it within labor. The reverse is what we need.

**View from the Petrostate**

I am writing from Lagos, and I want to register a Global South perspective on Táíwò's essay—one that takes seriously both his West African background and the very different political conditions under which his "two-step" strategy would have to operate in the countries that most concretely embody the dilemmas he describes.

Nigeria is a petrostate. Roughly 80% of federal revenues and the overwhelming majority of foreign exchange earnings come from oil and gas. Yet Nigeria is also a country where the local consequences of fossil dependency are catastrophic: the Niger Delta is one of the most polluted environments on earth, gas flaring contributes more local atmospheric harm than legitimate energy use, and the political economy of oil rent has produced a state that is both fiscally bloated and developmentally hollow. We have all the disadvantages of the fossil economy and few of its benefits. Per capita electricity consumption is among the lowest in the world. Most Nigerians experience the fossil economy primarily through diesel generators, petrol scarcity, and the politics of fuel subsidy.

From this vantage point, several aspects of Táíwò's framework look different than they do from Boston Review.

First, the "two-step" sequencing has a particular tragic resonance in petrostates. The proposition that we must first dethrone fossil capital before proceeding to broader transformation runs immediately into the question: what replaces the rents? The Nigerian state, with all its failures, is held together by oil revenues. Strip those away and the social compact—such as it is—dissolves. The result is not transformation but state collapse, which historically has been catastrophic for the poor rather than liberating.

This is not a hypothetical concern. The fuel subsidy debate of 2012, which produced the Occupy Nigeria movement, was already a microcosm of the dilemma: removing the subsidy was framed by neoliberal advisors as fiscally rational and developmentally progressive, but its immediate effect was to raise the price of transport, food, and electricity for the urban poor, who responded with weeks of protest. Subsidy removal happened again in 2023 under President Tinubu, with predictably devastating consequences for living standards. The same logic applies to fossil phaseout writ large. There is no version of step one that does not, in the short term, deepen the immiseration of the populations that most need step two.

What follows is that the "two-step" strategy, exported to petrostates, cannot consist of fossil phaseout alone. It must consist of the simultaneous construction of alternative fiscal and economic bases. Without that, "first dethrone fossil capital" is just a more sophisticated version of structural adjustment, with the IMF's clothes traded for a green vest.

Second, the global character of the climate crisis means that climate justice has to grapple seriously with international transfers. Táíwò mentions Cochabamba and the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty, but the essay's central strategic frame is national: it is about states using their own apparatuses to confront the fossil bloc. This framing tracks the political imagination of the Northern left more than the actual structure of the problem. Nigeria can phase out its own oil and gas tomorrow without making a meaningful dent in global emissions, but the resulting fiscal hole cannot be filled by Nigerian state action. It can only be filled by international finance—either market-rate, which would deepen our debt crisis, or concessional, which would require political mobilization in donor countries that does not currently exist.

The Bridgetown Initiative led by Mia Mottley has been the most serious recent attempt to articulate this. Special drawing rights reallocation, multilateral development bank reform, debt-for-climate swaps, loss and damage finance: these are the institutional levers through which the Global South can be enabled to transition. They are also the levers most starkly absent from the actually-existing climate politics of the North. Even the Inflation Reduction Act—the largest climate spending bill in U.S. history—contains nothing for the rest of the world and includes provisions explicitly designed to undermine industrial development in trade partner countries.

A two-step strategy that does not center this international dimension is not, in fact, a two-step strategy. It is a Northern strategy with a Southern rhetorical wrapper. To make it real, the political work in Northern capitals would have to be at least as much about reshaping international finance and trade as about domestic energy transition. I am not sure Táíwò would disagree, but the essay does not center this work, and its absence reflects a broader tendency in Northern climate writing to treat the rest of the world as either victim or backdrop.

Third—and this is more sympathetic to Táíwò's position—the experience of African states under coup belt conditions illustrates exactly his point about the climate crisis eating the conditions of possibility for progressive politics. The coups in Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Guinea, and Gabon have multiple drivers, but among them are the slow-burning effects of agricultural collapse driven by Sahelian climate change, the displacement of populations and the consequent strain on governance, and the resource competition over uranium, gold, and increasingly oil. The breakdown of the West African political order is not separable from the ecological breakdown of the territories on which that order rests. By the time the climate has hollowed out a state, no strategy of "contesting state power" is meaningful, because there is no functional state power to contest.

This is the strongest version of Táíwò's argument: the time horizon on which a coherent state-building politics is even possible is itself a function of the climate trajectory. Every year of delay narrows the window. I appreciate Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's essay as a serious effort to think about climate strategy from the left, and I appreciate especially his attention to the labor dimension—the gold-standard labor language in New York's Build Public Renewables Act, the references to unions and tenant organizations, the emphasis on public power as both ownership and ethos. The first thing to say is that the two-step framework, as Táíwò presents it, is much closer to actual labor traditions than the impatient maximalism that dominates much climate discourse. Trade unions have always thought in stages: contract by contract, sector by sector, beachhead by beachhead. We know that strategy means choosing what to fight for now and what to defer. We know that solidarity has to be constructed and that "the workers" are not a unified subject who will simply act on their abstract interests if those interests are correctly described. From this perspective, Táíwò's argument is welcome, because much of the climate movement has been working with a model of social change that bears no relation to how social change actually occurs—which is slowly, through institutions, through built relationships, through compromises that prefigurative politics finds unpalatable.

That said, I want to flag three problems that Táíwò's essay touches on but does not fully address.

The first is the actual position of fossil workers in the transition. The U.S. has roughly 150,000 direct workers in oil and gas extraction, plus another 500,000 or so in pipeline, refining, and distribution, plus several million in jobs adjacent to or dependent on fossil-energy systems—gas station attendants, truck mechanics, petrochemical workers, plastics workers. In coal country, the number is smaller but the geographic concentration is severe. These workers are not abstractions. They are union members in some cases, would-be union members in others, and they vote.

A "two-step" politics that proposes to dethrone fossil capital has to have a serious answer for what happens to these workers, their families, and their communities. The honest version of this answer is not "they will be retrained for jobs in solar," because solar jobs do not pay what oil jobs pay, are not located where oil jobs are located, and require different skills. The honest answer is some combination of (a) generous early retirement, (b) wage insurance for displaced workers that protects their living standards for a substantial period, (c) place-based investment in fossil-dependent communities that may or may not produce comparable employment, and (d) honest acknowledgment that some communities will not recover and that those communities deserve substantial transfers regardless of whether new economic activity materializes.

This is what a "just transition" actually requires, and it is expensive—much more expensive than the climate left typically budgets for. The German coal exit, for instance, included tens of billions of euros for affected regions. The U.S. equivalent would be in the hundreds of billions. Without this kind of commitment, the political math of fossil phaseout simply does not work, because the workers and communities affected will fight any phaseout with everything they have, and they have considerable political power, particularly in U.S. swing states. The history of trade liberalization tells us what happens when displaced workers are promised retraining and not given material security: they become the political base for reactionary movements, and the next progressive coalition has to spend a generation winning them back.

The second problem is the labor politics of the green sector itself. Táíwò celebrates the union jobs mandated in New York's Build Public Renewables Act, and rightly so. But the broader green economy in the U.S. is largely non-union and increasingly so. Solar installation, in particular, has been a notoriously low-wage, transient, dangerous occupation, with high turnover and contractor-based employment that defeats most organizing efforts. Battery and EV manufacturing is unionized at the Big Three but increasingly not at the new entrants—Tesla, Rivian, the Korean and Japanese plants in the South. The IRA's labor provisions are real but limited and contested.

If the green transition produces millions of new jobs but those jobs are worse than the fossil jobs they replace, the working-class politics of the transition will fail. Not because workers are short-sighted but because they correctly recognize a worsening of their material position. The "two-step" strategy has to confront this directly: the first step is not merely to dethrone fossil capital but to build the green economy on labor terms that make it materially preferable to the fossil economy, which means substantial public-sector employment, strong labor law reform, prevailing-wage requirements, and project labor agreements as norms rather than exceptions.

The third problem is the international dimension of green industrial policy. The IRA, the Inflation Reduction Act, has aspects I support and aspects I do not. I support its scale, its labor provisions, and its commitment to public investment. I am more skeptical of its protectionist features and what they mean for solidarity with workers in Mexico, Korea, China, and elsewhere. A climate strategy that succeeds in the U.S. by exporting the costs of decarbonization to workers in other countries is not a climate strategy that the international labor movement can endorse.

The trade union tradition has historically been internationalist in rhetoric and nationalist in practice. The climate crisis demands that we close that gap, and we are not close to doing so. Industrial policy that benefits American workers at the expense of Mexican workers is not the model. Industrial policy coordinated across North America—or, better, across the hemisphere—on shared labor and environmental standards, would be. We are not building toward that, and Táíwò's essay does not push us toward it.

Where does this leave the two-step strategy? Where any honest strategy leaves it: as a framework that has to be filled in with concrete commitments that the climate movement has not yet adequately made. The labor movement is ready to be a serious partner in this work, but readiness is conditional on the climate left's willingness to internalize what serious labor politics actually requires: massive material commitments to displaced workers, a non-negotiable commitment to high-wage union jobs in the new sectors, and international solidarity that does not collapse the moment domestic political pressures intensify.

Táíwò invokes the New York legislation as a model, and it is. But it is a model that took years of patient coalition work, that required compromises that purists in the climate movement opposed, and that even now is being implemented at a pace and scale that falls short of what the climate emergency demands. The "two-step" strategy is correct in principle. The labor movement's caution is about whether the climate left is prepared for the actual work of step one—which is not a clever slogan but the long, unglamorous labor of building a coalition that can survive the political reactions our victories will provoke.

**The Land Beneath the State**

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's essay argues that the strategic priority of our era is the political defeat of fossil capital, and that this defeat must be pursued primarily through state power. He treats the autonomist experiments of the Zapatistas and the Autonomous Administration of North and East Syria with respectful skepticism, asking how they could possibly scale to atmospheric relevance. I want to take the opposite tack: to argue that the question of land—who controls it, who lives on it, who decides what is extracted from it—is more fundamental to climate politics than the question of state power, and that the autonomist tradition has been getting this right while the statist left has been getting it wrong.

Begin with a fact that climate strategists often skip past. Indigenous peoples constitute about 5% of the global population but steward roughly 25% of the world's land surface, including an estimated 80% of remaining biodiversity. Lands under Indigenous management have, on average, lower deforestation rates, higher carbon sequestration, and more intact ecosystems than lands managed by states or private capital. The most rigorous comparative studies—from the Amazon to the boreal—show that Indigenous tenure outperforms state-protected areas at conservation outcomes, often by significant margins.

This is not a romantic claim. It is a structural one. Indigenous communities are tied to specific places across long time horizons. They cannot externalize ecological damage because they have nowhere else to go. State agencies and private firms, by contrast, can and do externalize: ministers rotate, corporations dissolve, capital flees. The land is the only actor with permanent skin in the game, and Indigenous governance systems are uniquely structured to internalize the land's interests.

What follows for climate strategy? Quite a lot, actually. The most effective single intervention in slowing the climate crisis would be the recognition of Indigenous land rights at scale, combined with the resourcing necessary to defend those rights against extractive incursion. This is not speculation. It is documented in the IPCC's working group reports, in the Land Gap Report, in the work of the Rights and Resources Initiative. Yet it occupies a marginal position in most strategic discussions on the climate left, including Táíwò's.

Why? Because the framework of "state power confronts fossil capital" has no comfortable place for actors who refuse the legitimacy of state power and contest the territorial premise of capital. Indigenous land defense is not a state strategy. It is, in many cases, an explicitly anti-state strategy—or at least a strategy that subordinates state institutions to prior, place-based forms of governance. The Mapuche, the Wet'suwet'en, the Sarayaku, the Munduruku, the Adivasi communities resisting bauxite mining in Odisha—these movements are not waiting for the state to wield its capacities on their behalf. They are exercising sovereignty over their own territories against both state and capital, which often function as a joint extractive apparatus.

Táíwò's essay treats this kind of politics as admirable but insufficient: "the emissions of ExxonMobil and the Pentagon poison the air and raise the temperature in Mexico and Syria too." This is true, but it cuts the other way as well. The carbon sequestered in the Yasuní rainforest is sequestered for the atmosphere of New York and Lagos and Beijing. The Indigenous defense of the YasunĂ­ against oil extraction—which prevailed in a national referendum last year, against the wishes of both major political parties—was a planetary climate victory achieved through a politics that the statist left would categorize as "merely defensive." It kept several hundred million barrels of oil in the ground. There is no policy at the federal level in any rich country with comparable confirmed climate benefit.

The implication is that "scale" is the wrong metric for evaluating climate politics. What matters is not whether a movement is national or international but whether it can prevent specific molecules of carbon from reaching the atmosphere or remove them from it. A network of Indigenous-managed territories across the Amazon, the Congo Basin, the boreal, and the tropical Asia-Pacific would, in aggregate, do more for the climate than any plausible state-level energy transition policy in the rich world. The aggregate exists. The political support for it does not.

Why doesn't it? Partly because the international left has not done the conceptual work of integrating land-based politics into its strategic frameworks. Marxists have historically treated the peasantry and Indigenous communities as residual categories—important sometimes, but not the strategic subject. The climate movement has inherited this prejudice. The strategic subject is assumed to be the urban working class, organized through unions and parties, contending for state power. Land defenders are auxiliary.

But the actual carbon arithmetic does not work this way. Roughly 23% of global greenhouse gas emissions come from land use, land-use change, and forestry. Roughly 30% of the carbon removal capacity that any 1.5°C pathway requires comes from "nature-based solutions," which is to say, from the maintenance and restoration of forests, peatlands, mangroves, and grasslands. The political subjects who actually control these capacities are not unions, parties, or states. They are land-based communities, many of them Indigenous, who have been doing the work of keeping carbon in the ground and in living biomass for generations without recognition or resources.

A climate strategy that took this seriously would look different from Táíwò's. It would still include energy transition—I am not arguing against decarbonizing electricity systems—but it would not treat energy transition as the master frame. It would instead treat the question "who controls the land?" as the central political question of the era, and would evaluate every tactical move by whether it strengthens or weakens land-based sovereignty against state-capital extractive alliances.

Some implications. First, in the rich countries, climate politics would prioritize the return of land to Indigenous nations as both moral imperative and climate strategy. Land Back is not a slogan to be supported in principle and ignored in practice; it is a climate policy. Second, in the Global South, climate politics would prioritize the legal recognition and material resourcing of Indigenous, Afro-descendant, and peasant land tenure, including in conflicts with progressive governments that often see Indigenous territoriality as an obstacle to their developmental projects. Third, in international climate finance, the priority would shift from project-based mechanisms (REDD+, carbon markets) that often dispossess land-based communities to direct transfers that strengthen their governance capacities.

None of this requires the abandonment of state politics. It does require subordinating state-level strategies to a more fundamental question that the statist left has not adequately posed: what is the relationship between human governance and the land that sustains it? The state form, as we know it, presumes a relationship in which the state is sovereign over land and grants conditional access to its inhabitants. This presumption is the political precondition for extraction, including fossil extraction. As long as it is unchallenged, dethroning fossil capital just means handing the same sovereign relation to a different capital.

The autonomist experiments Táíwò treats as marginal are, in this light, not romantic distractions but laboratories for the political form that climate stability requires. Chiapas is not relevant because it might scale to a national alternative; it is relevant because it demonstrates that a different relation between people and land is possible, and that this different relation has ecological consequences that the state form cannot match. The same applies, in different inflections, to Rojava, to the Cherán experience in Michoacán, to the various territorial defense movements across the global South.

I am not arguing against the energy transition. I am arguing that the energy transition, conceived as a state project against fossil capital, is necessary but not sufficient, and that focusing on it as the master strategy obscures the politics of land that is more fundamental and that already has political subjects willing and able to undertake it. The atmosphere does not know whether the carbon it accumulates came from a Chevron well or a Brazilian soy farm. The climate movement should know, and should organize accordingly.

Climate change is the first truly global commons problem: it can only be solved through coordinated action at the planetary scale, yet the institutions capable of such coordination do not exist and face structural barriers to their creation.
