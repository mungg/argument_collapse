If it wants to be a health care company, what else should CVS stop selling?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

CVS's decision to drop tobacco is genuinely bold — roughly $2 billion in annual revenue walked out the door — but the logic, if taken seriously, points well beyond cigarettes. The question is how far a pharmacy can credibly extend the "health care" frame without becoming either preachy or empty.

The strongest next target is sugar-sweetened beverages and the candy racks that line every checkout aisle. The evidence linking sugary drinks to obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular disease is by now about as solid as the evidence on tobacco was in the 1970s. If CVS won't sell a product whose label warns it causes cancer, it's hard to justify selling 32-ounce sodas next to the diabetes test strips.

A second category is homeopathic remedies and dubious supplements. These aren't "unhealthy" in the calorie sense, but they undermine the pharmacy's claim to scientific authority. Selling sugar pills marketed as flu cures alongside FDA-approved drugs blurs exactly the line a health care company should sharpen.

I'd be more cautious about alcohol, processed snacks, or tanning products. Moderate alcohol use isn't clearly harmful, and a slippery slope toward policing every Doritos bag turns CVS into a scold and probably an unprofitable one.

The honest test is consistency with the pharmacist's counter. If a CVS pharmacist would warn a customer against a product, the store probably shouldn't be stocking it three aisles over.

A pharmacy should be a sanctuary for wellness, not a convenience store for disease. By removing sugary drinks, CVS would not only align its shelves with its white coats, it would send a powerful, science-backed message: we refuse to profit from the slow, sweetened path to illness. The tobacco ban was brave; following it with a ban on liquid sugar is the necessary next step toward becoming a true health care provider.

CVS Caremark’s decision to ban tobacco products from its stores is a watershed moment for corporate public health. It rightly recognizes that selling the leading cause of preventable death fundamentally contradicts the mission of a healthcare provider. CVS does not need to become a sterile clinic, and removing every marginally unhealthy item is impractical. CVS should not turn itself into a monastery. Pharmacies sell aspirin, ice cream, cough syrup, sunscreen, candy and cosmetics because customers live ordinary lives, not because every item is a medical endorsement. But if CVS wants to be treated as a health care company, it should draw a bright line around products that are dangerous when used exactly as intended, or that trade on medical credibility without evidence.

That puts all tobacco products first: cigarettes, cigars, smokeless tobacco and the new nicotine devices that are not approved as smoking-cessation aids. E-cigarettes may prove less harmful than cigarettes, but in 2014 their contents, marketing and long-term effects remain too uncertain for a pharmacy counter. A reasonable compromise would be to stop discounting and advertising alcohol, and phase it out where local economics allow.

The harm is not only chemical; it is substitution. A customer who buys magic instead of treatment may get sicker.

Candy and soda are different. They contribute to obesity and diabetes, but they are not inevitably harmful in ordinary use. CVS should shrink, not sanctify, that aisle. Health care requires judgment, not puritanism.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

CVS's decision to drop tobacco was a principled move, but extending that logic too far risks crossing from corporate conscience into corporate paternalism. Tobacco stands apart: it's the only legal consumer product that, when used as intended, kills half its long-term users. No similar argument applies to alcohol, sugary snacks, or lottery tickets, all of which CVS and virtually every other retailer sell.

That said, CVS could rationally go further without hypocrisy. Alcohol shares tobacco's addictive properties and contributes meaningfully to disease, car accidents, and social harm. If CVS genuinely wants to align its product mix with its health brand, phasing out alcohol sales—particularly in locations near MinuteClinics—would be defensible.

But there's a harder question lurking beneath this debate: whether retailers have any obligation to curate our choices at all. CVS is a private company free to stock whatever it likes. Its tobacco decision was admirable precisely because it sacrificed short-term revenue for long-term credibility. Demanding it go further implies an obligation that doesn't exist. Consumers who want healthier retailers can shop elsewhere; those who don't can stay.

The real legacy of CVS's announcement may not be what else it stops selling, but whether it sparks a broader conversation about how retail and health intersect—and whether that conversation leads to better consumer information rather than narrower product availability.

CVS deserves applause for the tobacco decision, but let's not turn this into a moral crusade that empties half its shelves. The company is a pharmacy, not a wellness ashram. Tobacco is uniquely indefensible because there is no safe dose — every cigarette causes harm, and the product kills roughly half its long-term users. That's a categorical distinction, not a continuum.

The slippery-slope arguments now flooding the conversation — "next they should ban soda, candy, chips, alcohol, sugary cereals" — confuse two very different ideas. Sugar and salt in moderation are part of a normal diet. Tobacco in moderation is still poison. CVS can reasonably stock a Snickers bar at the checkout counter without contradicting its mission, just as a hospital cafeteria can serve a hamburger.

That said, there are two product categories where CVS should follow the logic of its own decision. The first is homeopathic remedies and "detox" cleanses, which the chain currently sells right alongside genuine medications. These products are not merely useless; they actively undermine the pharmacist's role by suggesting that sugar pills and FDA-approved drugs deserve equal shelf space. A health care company should not legitimize pseudoscience for profit.

The second is electronic cigarettes. If CVS is removing combustible tobacco on health grounds, it should be consistent and not simply migrate smokers to a nicotine product whose long-term effects are unknown and whose marketing is increasingly aimed at adolescents. Pulling e-cigarettes would close an obvious loophole.

Beyond that, CVS should resist the temptation to play nanny. The danger of dramatic gestures is that they invite endless escalation, and pharmacies that try to police every customer's diet will lose the trust of the customers they need to reach. Keep the focus narrow: products with no legitimate use, no safe dose, or no scientific basis. That's a defensible line.

CVS has opened a door it cannot easily close. By declaring that selling tobacco is incompatible with being a "health care provider," the company has invited the public to audit the rest of its inventory against that standard. Fair enough — let's audit.

I don't expect CVS to do any of this, because it would gut the front-of-store business model. But that is precisely the point. Asking what else CVS should stop selling assumes that the right response to unhealthy products is removal from shelves. I want to push back on the premise. The most consequential health hazard at CVS isn't sold in the candy aisle or the cigarette case — it's behind the pharmacy counter.

Consider opioids. Prescription painkillers now kill more Americans each year than heroin and cocaine combined, and pharmacy chains are central nodes in their distribution. The DEA has repeatedly cited CVS and Walgreens for failing to flag suspicious orders at individual stores; in 2012, CVS paid $75 million to settle claims that its Florida outlets had become pill-mill conduits. If CVS is serious about being a health care company, the meaningful reform is tightening dispensing protocols, training pharmacists to spot red flags, and refusing to fill prescriptions from known problem prescribers — not bagging up the Marlboros.

Consider also antibiotic stewardship. Inappropriate antibiotic prescribing, often filled without question at retail pharmacies, drives resistance that the CDC has labeled one of the top public health threats of our era. A pharmacy chain with real ambition could pioneer pharmacist-led review of antibiotic prescriptions for short-course infections.

Consider, finally, the MinuteClinic model itself. These walk-in clinics are a genuine innovation for a country with too few primary care doctors. Expanding their scope to include vaccination drives, blood pressure monitoring, and diabetes screening would do more for population health than any number of symbolic product removals.

The tobacco decision is welcome, but it is the easy version of being a health care company. The hard version involves changing how the pharmacy itself operates — its prescribing practices, its clinical services, its relationship with prescribers. Debating whether soda or chips should be the next casualty is a distraction from the much larger health footprint CVS already has, and largely fails to manage well.
