Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Bonica and Grumbach have written the most thorough recent critique of Democratic "popularism," and they are persuasive on a great deal. They are right that the median voter theorem describes a political world that no longer exists, right that nationalization has flattened candidate-level differentiation, and right that the case-study evidence trotted out for moderation—Manchin, Golden, Sinema—proves something narrower than its boosters claim. Their basic methodological point, that comparing PAC-endorsed candidates to "everyone else" is not science, deserves to land. But having dismantled one set of false certainties, they spend the back half of the essay building another. The anti-corruption, anti-establishment, turnout-maximizing strategy they recommend is offered with a confidence that their own evidence does not earn, and it papers over hard problems that any serious anti-authoritarian politics has to solve.

Start with their causal evidence against moderation. The headline statistic—that twenty-one of twenty-two Democratic incumbents who lost competitive seats from 2016 to 2024 were moderates—is striking until you remember that moderates are who Democrats run in competitive seats. Of course nearly all the losers were moderates: nearly all the competitive-district incumbents were moderates. The denominator is missing. Their cleaner identification strategy—looking at narrow primary wins between moderates and progressives—is more credible, but the null result there is also consistent with moderation mattering exactly as much as ideologues claim, just buried under noise from a sample of a few dozen close primaries. Andrew Gelman's warning about WAR estimates cuts both ways: if Split Ticket's positive estimates are too uncertain to trust, the authors' null estimates are too uncertain to license sweeping pronouncements about a "scholarly consensus." A small, context-dependent moderation premium is exactly what we would expect, and a small premium is not nothing when elections are decided by tens of thousands of votes across three states.

A second problem is that the essay's positive program is itself a form of popularism, just one its authors like. The argument for an anti-corruption frame is supported by—of all things—polling: a Yale/George Mason survey showing Americans are "very worried" about corruption, a YouGov survey on bribery, the popularity of a congressional stock-trading ban. This is precisely the move Ezra Klein recommends: find what voters tell pollsters they care about and talk about that. There is nothing wrong with the move; in fact it suggests that popularism, properly understood, is not the cartoon of pandering Bonica and Grumbach paint. The disagreement is really about which popular things to emphasize, and whether "corruption" is a more mobilizing frame than "cost of living." That is a real and interesting debate, but it is not a debate between empiricists and idealists. It is a debate within empiricism.

Third, the anti-establishment-populism prescription understates how hard it is to wield from inside the institutions voters distrust. Trump's authority to denounce "the swamp" rests on his being personally external to the political class, however implausibly. Democratic candidates running on anti-corruption in 2026 will be running as members of the party that controls many of the institutions—universities, prosecutors' offices, regulatory agencies, blue-state governments—that the right has spent a decade coding as "the establishment." The European cases Bonica and Grumbach cite cut against them here, too: where left parties have successfully run anti-corruption campaigns against incumbent authoritarians (Poland's Civic Coalition, for instance), they did so as a coalition that included plenty of moderates and conservatives, organized around defending institutions, not attacking them. The model is closer to "anti-Trump united front" than to "left populist insurgency," and the former requires exactly the kind of broad-tent coalition-building that progressive purity tests tend to obstruct. Sherrod Brown and Jon Tester—populist, working-class-coded Democrats with reform credentials—both lost their 2024 Senate races. If economic populism plus authentic biography plus anti-establishment branding were a winning combination, they should be senators today.

Fourth, the turnout-versus-persuasion framing is too clean. The authors emphasize the partisan turnout gap in 2024, and they are correct that it matters. But the 2024 election was not solely or even mostly a turnout story; it was also a story of voters who showed up and switched. Trump improved his margins among Latino, young, and working-class voters who voted. Catalist and Pew validation data both point to real persuasion, not just differential demobilization. A strategy built entirely around mobilizing the base will leave on the table exactly the voters Democrats need to keep from defecting. The honest position is that mobilization and persuasion are both necessary, that neither is sufficient, and that there is no algorithm—popularist or progressive—that tells you the right mix in advance.

Where I think Bonica and Grumbach are most valuable is in pressing the deeper point that the strategic frame matters more than any particular issue position. If you concede that elections are nationalized, that voters follow leaders rather than the reverse, and that authenticity dominates triangulation, then the question of whether a candidate is two clicks left or right of the median is the wrong question. The right question is whether the party offers a coherent story about who it fights for and against. On that, "the system is rigged and we will un-rig it" is at least a story. The Harris campaign's careful avoidance of any such story—its retreat into joy, its embrace of Liz Cheney, its silence on Gaza, its refusal to break with Biden on anything—was not really moderation in the substantive sense. It was the absence of a frame. That absence is what cost the campaign, more than any specific position.

So the most useful reading of this essay is not as a vindication of progressive strategy over moderate strategy, but as a call for any strategy with a backbone. Their three lessons—reframe the battlefield, build anti-establishment credibility, boost turnout—are reasonable, and the first two are largely independent of left-right positioning. A moderate Democrat could run on banning congressional stock trading, refusing corporate PAC money, and breaking up monopolies; an economic progressive could run on hawkish border enforcement. The point is to have something to say that isn't downstream of a focus group. Where I'd push the authors is to admit that their own prescription is also a bet, also poll-informed, and also unproven against an opponent who has spent a decade convincing half the country that he is the anti-corruption candidate. Humility is warranted on all sides. The "old rules are dead" is a useful slogan; "we know what the new rules are" is not yet a defensible claim.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Bonica and Grumbach have written a bracing, meticulously argued piece that deserves a wide audience. Their challenge to the stale conventional wisdom that moderation is the key to Democratic electoral success is timely and, in many respects, persuasive. They are right to point out that the median voter theorem belongs to a different era, that nationalization and polarization have eroded the old advantages of centrism, and that the popularist obsession with polling often mistakes measurement for leadership. Yet for all its rigor, the essay overcorrects. In dismissing moderation as exhausted and urging the party to embrace an anti-corruption mobilization strategy, it risks trading one oversimplification for another. The evidence on moderation's decline is more mixed than they acknowledge, and the proposed alternative, while intuitively appealing, faces serious obstacles that the authors glide past.

The core empirical claim is that moderation no longer provides a consistent electoral benefit. But the studies they cite, including their own well-designed primary analysis, mostly show that the effect is small and context-dependent—not zero. That distinction matters. In a polarized environment, even a one-point swing can decide a presidential election or a Senate race. Moreover, the very studies they invoke frequently find that moderation still helps in certain conditions, such as when a candidate has a strong personal brand or when the district is especially cross-pressured. To say that "the moderation advantage has largely disappeared" is a reasonable interpretation, but it's not the same as saying it never matters. Their own example of Joe Manchin—whose personal brand allowed him to survive until retirement—illustrates that moderate positioning, when credible, can still be powerful. The problem for Democrats is not that moderation is futile; it is that it is no longer sufficient on its own, and that insincere triangulation backfires. The essay's rhetorical move from "small and inconsistent" to "don't bother" is too hasty.

The 2024 election is the centerpiece of their timing. Harris’s loss is presented as a failure of the moderation playbook. But as political scientists have long noted, presidential elections are blunt instruments for testing candidate ideology. Harris was weighed down by profound headwinds: an unpopular incumbent, inflation, and a global anti-incumbent mood. Her move to the center may not have saved her, but that does not mean it cost her the election. It is at least as plausible that her moderate pivot prevented a worse down-ballot collapse, especially in swing districts where Democratic House candidates outperformed the top of the ticket. Bonica and Grumbach’s own data show that twenty-one of twenty-two Democratic incumbents who lost between 2016 and 2024 were moderates, but that fact conflates selection with effect: moderates are the ones who hold competitive seats, so they are the ones most exposed to partisan waves. Progressives, by contrast, often sit in safely blue districts and rarely face the same general-election vulnerability. The statistic does not prove that moderation caused their defeats.

The Turnout Gambit and the Swing Voter Blind Spot

The essay’s strategic pivot is to reframe the fight around corruption, build anti-establishment credibility, and boost turnout. This has a certain electric clarity, but it ignores the uncomfortable math of American geography. Democrats need to win in states and districts that are considerably more conservative than the national median—precisely the places where a full-throated anti-corruption, anti-establishment message may repel the very voters who decide control of the Senate and Electoral College. The authors cite polls showing that Americans are deeply worried about corruption, and indeed they are. But corruption as a valence issue is easily co-opted by populists of any stripe; Donald Trump rode to power in 2016 promising to "drain the swamp." A generic anti-corruption frame is not ideologically owned, and it is far from clear that it mobilizes low-turnout Democratic constituencies more effectively than it energizes angry conservatives who see the "deep state" as the corrupt elite. The authors’ own favorite example of successful anti-authoritarian coalitions abroad—often in parliamentary systems with proportional representation—do not map neatly onto a two-party, first-past-the-post system with an Electoral College that magnifies rural, conservative power.

Similarly, the emphasis on turnout as the silver bullet downplays the evidence that base mobilization is not cost-free. Running a more strident anti-establishment campaign can alienate the suburban moderates who have powered Democratic gains in Arizona, Georgia, and Pennsylvania. In 2018 and 2020, Democrats won by assembling a coalition that included both high turnout from the base and defections among college-educated Republicans repelled by Trump’s chaos. The authors’ own research shows that progressive candidates slightly boost turnout among registered Democrats, but the net electoral effect depends on whether that gain exceeds the loss among independents and wavering Republicans. In many pivotal races, the math remains precarious. If the strategy is to bet the house on creating a "new mobilized electorate," one must grapple with the sobering reality that similar efforts in the past—from George McGovern to Jeremy Corbyn—have often produced landslide defeats when the newly mobilized fell short of the newly antagonized.

Authenticity Over Ideology

The essay is at its strongest when it argues that authenticity and leadership matter more than precise ideological positioning. Voters follow leaders, not platforms. Yet this insight pulls against the prescription. If authenticity is what counts, then the imperative is not to adopt a uniform anti-corruption frame but to allow candidates to find their own credible voice—which in some districts will be sharply progressive, and in others will sound like the moderate Jared Golden before his brand collapsed. The problem with the popularist approach is not that moderation is inherently bad, but that the top-down, poll-driven enforcement of a moderate script looks fake. A party that genuinely wants to experiment should stop imposing a single strategic line from Washington and instead resource a diverse bench of candidates who can authentically connect with their own electorates. Some of those candidates will run to the left, some to the center. The evidence suggests that, once incumbency and district lean are accounted for, the difference between them in general election performance is marginal. Let a thousand flowers bloom indeed—but that requires not dismissing the flower of moderation when it grows organically in a tough district.

The Existential Stakes and the Risk of Certainty

The authors close with a stirring call to treat the moment as existential and to abandon old rules. The emotional gravity is understandable. But existential threats can also encourage the very certainty they rightly critique in popularists. There is no scientifically secure path to defeating authoritarianism, and the history of democratic resilience offers no single formula. Anti-corruption frames have worked in some contexts but flopped in others; mass mobilization has sometimes saved democracy and sometimes triggered a violent backlash. If we are to embrace experimentation, as the essay urges, we must be open to the possibility that some moderation—especially in candidates’ personal style and capacity to build broad coalitions—remains an important arrow in the quiver. The false choice between left and center does need to be retired, but not by dismissing the center altogether. The real divide is between a Democratic Party that fights with strategic pluralism and one that, in its rush to junk the old playbook, imposes a new orthodoxy. Bonica and Grumbach have performed a vital service by exposing the shakiness of the moderation dogma. But the lesson of their own evidence is not "run left, fight corruption, and turn out the base," but rather that there are no shortcuts, and that the party's vitality depends on a messy, plural, often contradictory set of approaches—including, in some places, the very centrism they are ready to bury.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Adam Bonica and Jake Grumbach’s post-mortem of the 2024 election captures the profound frustration of a political coalition that played by the conventional rules and still lost to an authoritarian movement. Their autopsy of “popularism” and the median voter theorem is, in many respects, sharply observed. They are right that politics in the 2020s is dominated by nationalized media, hyper-polarization, and negative partisanship. They are also correct that mechanically triangulating on a left-right axis to chase an increasingly mythical “swing voter” often yields an uninspiring, incoherent platform that fails to resonate.

However, in their eagerness to bury moderation, Bonica and Grumbach draw a deeply flawed roadmap for the future. Their proposed alternative—abandoning the center in favor of a left-populist, anti-establishment crusade centered on “anti-corruption” and base mobilization—relies on a misdiagnosis of why Democrats lost the 2024 election. Worse, it prescribes a cure that would likely accelerate the decay of the very democratic institutions they seek to save. You cannot defeat a right-wing authoritarian populist movement by simply offering a left-wing mirror image of its anti-establishment grievances.

To understand the fatal flaw in their prescription, we must first examine their proposed silver bullet: the “anti-corruption” frame. Bonica and Grumbach argue that corruption reorganizes politics around a fundamental question of legitimacy, pointing to polling from 2025 showing deep American anxiety about a “rigged” system. They cite Clarence Thomas’s undisclosed gifts and congressional stock trading as the fuel for a new, unifying moral outrage.

But this assumes “corruption” is an ideologically neutral blank slate. In our hyper-fragmented media environment, it is not. To a progressive, corruption means billionaires buying Supreme Court seats or corporate price-gouging. To a right-wing voter, or a disaffected independent immersed in populist media ecosystems, corruption means the “Deep State,” the mainstream media, diversity initiatives, the Biden family’s foreign business dealings, and the “weaponization” of the justice system against Donald Trump.

By leaning into an overarching narrative that “the system is fundamentally rigged,” Democrats would be fighting on authoritarianism’s home turf. Donald Trump and his movement have spent a decade cultivating a monopoly on anti-system grievance. When a pro-democracy party validates the premise that the nation’s core institutions are irreparably corrupt, it inadvertently does the authoritarian’s work for them. Authoritarians thrive on cynicism; they rely on the electorate believing that everyone is dirty, so you might as well elect the strongman who admits it. Banning congressional stock trading is a fine policy, but the notion that it will single-handedly break the authoritarian fever and win back the working class is a dangerous fantasy.

This brings us to the authors’ second major recommendation: prioritizing base turnout over persuasion. Bonica and Grumbach correctly note the devastating turnout gap in 2024, observing that the drop-off in participation among Black, Latino, and young voters was fatal to Kamala Harris’s campaign. From this, they conclude that moderation dampens enthusiasm and that Democrats must instead offer a “cause larger than any single candidate” to mobilize the disengaged.

But this interpretation fundamentally misreads the nature of the voters who stayed home or defected to the GOP in 2024. The working-class voters who abandoned the Democratic coalition were not holding out for a more aggressive progressive agenda or a crusade against corporate PACs. Exhaustive post-election data shows they were driven away by material and cultural alienation. They punished the incumbent party for a punishing cost-of-living crisis, perceived chaos at the southern border, and a cultural aesthetic that struck them as out of touch with mainstream values.

Bonica and Grumbach dismiss this as the “folk theory” of policy preference, suggesting that voters don’t actually care about granular policy positioning. While it is true that voters do not act as rigid ideological accountants, they absolutely use a party’s handling of highly salient issues to judge its basic governing competence and common sense. When Ezra Klein and other proponents of popularism suggest Democrats should “talk about the popular stuff and shut up about the unpopular stuff,” they are not advocating for soulless pandering. They are advocating for political survival. Ignoring voters' visceral anxieties about inflation and public safety to lecture them about institutional reform is a recipe for further alienation, not mobilization.

Furthermore, the authors’ critique of moderation metrics, while statistically sophisticated, relies on selective interpretations of recent political history. They cite the collapse of Jared Golden’s polling in Maine by late 2025 as proof that moderation is a dead end. But this gets the causality backward. Maine’s 2nd district is deeply conservative territory that voted heavily for Trump. The fact that Golden, a Democrat, survived there as long as he did—winning in 2018, 2020, and 2022—was an astonishing testament to the power of his independent, moderate brand. His eventual struggles in the face of an overwhelming 2024 national partisan tide do not prove that moderation failed; they prove that gravity eventually catches up to everyone in a hyper-polarized era. Had Democrats run a progressive firebrand in Maine’s 2nd district in 2018, that seat would have been lost half a decade earlier.

Similarly, Bonica and Grumbach invoke the “Follow the Leader” theory, noting that Donald Trump can adopt flatly contradictory policy positions—such as shifting stances on abortion or the minimum wage—and his supporters will simply adjust their views to match his. They argue that this proves voters crave “authenticity and disruption,” not careful policy calibration.

But this dynamic is entirely asymmetric. Trump possesses a unique, cult-of-personality charisma within a right-wing ecosystem primed for strongman leadership. Democrats structurally cannot replicate this. The Democratic Party is inherently a coalition of highly educated professionals, minority voters, and suburban moderates. It is the party of institutions: the civil service, the universities, the scientific community, and the organized labor establishment. Demanding that Democratic candidates adopt the posture of "disruptive," anti-establishment outsiders requires them to fake a populist persona that voters will immediately recognize as inauthentic. When a right-wing populist attacks the establishment, voters believe him, because he is attacking the very institutions he wants to dismantle. When a Democrat attacks the establishment, they are attacking the institutions their own party relies on to govern.

Bonica and Grumbach urge us to look at other democracies that have successfully fought off democratic backsliding. But if we look at recent international examples up to early 2026, the lesson is exactly the opposite of the one they draw. In Poland, Donald Tusk did not defeat the authoritarian Law and Justice (PiS) party by running an anti-establishment crusade. He won by assembling a broad, disciplined coalition that explicitly included moderates and conservatives who were exhausted by institutional chaos. In Brazil, Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva narrowly defeated Jair Bolsonaro not by pivoting to the radical left, but by choosing a center-right rival as his running mate and promising a return to institutional normalcy and economic stability.

Successful anti-authoritarian movements do not win by creating a new, radical axis of polarization. They win by becoming the big tent. They win by making the authoritarian look like a chaotic, exhausting aberration.

The authors dismiss this approach as “1990s rules,” claiming that the existential threat of authoritarianism requires us to dispense with the “altar of caution.” But recognizing the stakes of a crisis does not excuse a party from the basic laws of electoral physics. If the threat is truly existential, then coalition-building is more imperative than ever. A strategy that deliberately alienates swing voters and moderate defectors in the hope of materializing a dormant, anti-establishment progressive base is a staggering gamble to take with the fate of a republic.

Democrats do not need to choose between soulless, poll-tested pandering and radical anti-establishment mobilization. The path forward requires a synthesis that Bonica and Grumbach overlook: relentless focus on material delivery combined with a robust defense of institutional integrity. Voters want a system that works, and they want leaders who seem capable of managing it without talking down to them.

The 2024 election was a traumatic defeat for the pro-democracy coalition, and it is entirely natural for political scientists and strategists to want to burn the old playbooks. But the solution to an authoritarian movement that feeds on institutional distrust is not to adopt its tactics. It is to demonstrate that democratic governance can actually deliver. Discarding the center in favor of left-populist purity is not a bold new strategy for defeating authoritarianism; it is simply a different way to lose.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Bonica and Grumbach are right about the most important thing: “move to the center” is not a strategy adequate to the crisis. It is a reflex, and often a stale one. In a nationalized, polarized, low-trust politics, the image of a candidate sliding along a left-right line until she reaches the median voter is much too simple. It cannot explain Donald Trump’s hold on his coalition, the weakness of many painstakingly calibrated Democratic candidates, or the fact that voters routinely reward authenticity over ideological tidiness. Nor can it answer the central challenge of an authoritarian movement: how to generate the urgency, solidarity, and institutional courage needed to defend self-government.

But if the old moderation gospel is inadequate, Democrats should be wary of replacing it with an equally simple counter-gospel. The lesson of the last decade is not that moderation never matters, or that persuasion is a distraction, or that the party can mobilize its way out of every defeat by becoming more confrontational. The lesson is that “moderation” has been asked to do too much conceptual work. Sometimes it means ideological centrism. Sometimes it means cultural reassurance. Sometimes it means procedural restraint, coalition-building, or a refusal to indulge activist jargon. Sometimes it means little more than donor comfort. These are not the same thing, and anti-authoritarian politics needs some of them even as it rejects others.

The strongest part of Bonica and Grumbach’s argument is their critique of candidate-level moderation as a magic electoral variable. It is plainly true that individual Democratic candidates have less room than they once did to outrun the national party brand. A Democrat in rural Maine, a Democrat in the Central Valley, and a Democrat in suburban Atlanta are all now interpreted through nationalized media, partisan identity, and social conflict. Ticket-splitting has declined. Local newspapers have weakened. Voters know less about their House candidates and more about the latest national outrage. Under those conditions, the marginal benefit of a candidate’s carefully curated ideological positioning is likely to be small, inconsistent, and easily overwhelmed by presidential approval, inflation, immigration, Dobbs, or the behavior of national party figures.

Yet that finding does not settle the broader question. Candidate ideology and party reputation are related but distinct. A moderate nominee in a swing district may not gain much from a slightly more centrist roll-call profile, but the national party can still pay a price when voters come to associate it with unpopular views, institutional arrogance, or indifference to their sense of disorder. The fact that Jared Golden or Kyrsten Sinema could not save themselves through triangulation does not prove that cultural trust, issue salience, and party image are unimportant. It proves that individualized distancing is often too weak to overcome a damaged brand.

This is where some versions of anti-popularism go too far. Polls are not commandments, and politics is not consumer marketing. But democratic leadership is not the opposite of responsiveness. It requires listening to citizens as they are, not merely summoning them to become the electorate one wishes existed. Calling popularism “pandering” captures a real danger—the hollow politician who says whatever tests well—but it can also become an excuse for dismissing genuine public concern. Voters who worry about border disorder, crime, inflation, school disruption, or ideological conformity are not necessarily dupes of right-wing media. Some are describing real experiences. Others are registering anxieties that any successful democratic coalition must address rather than shame.

The most promising anti-authoritarian politics would therefore combine moral clarity with democratic humility. It would say: Trumpism is corrupt and dangerous; political violence, election subversion, and government by personal loyalty are unacceptable; and also the public is entitled to demand competence, order, affordability, and respect. That is not the politics of Liz Cheney photo opportunities. It is the politics of building a majority large enough to isolate authoritarians rather than merely denounce them.

The turnout-versus-persuasion debate is another place where the lead essay usefully corrects a consultant cliché but risks creating a false choice. Democrats do have a turnout problem. The decline in Black participation, youth participation, and Democratic registration advantages in 2024 was catastrophic. A party that depends on irregular voters cannot win by speaking only to high-propensity suburban moderates. Year-round organizing, labor revival, campus and community infrastructure, and candidates rooted in working-class institutions matter enormously.

But nonvoters are not a hidden army of left-progressives waiting for a sufficiently bold message. Many are alienated, cross-pressured, cynical, socially conservative, economically populist, inattentive, or simply exhausted. Some lean Democratic; some lean Republican; many dislike both parties. Mobilizing them requires more than ideological intensity. It requires trust. And trust is built through presence, performance, and respect—showing up before the campaign, delivering material benefits, solving local problems, and speaking in a language that does not sound imported from elite nonprofits or cable panels.

That is why anti-corruption is such a valuable frame. Bonica and Grumbach are right that corruption can reorganize politics around legitimacy rather than left-right placement. It connects democracy to daily life: the billionaire’s gift to a justice, the stock trade made by a member of Congress, the defense contractor’s revolving door, the private equity roll-up of a hospital, the crypto donor’s access, the corporation that converts supply shocks into permanent price hikes. It explains why government seems remote and rigged. It can appeal to progressives, independents, and conservatives who believe the powerful play by different rules.

Still, anti-corruption is not self-executing. In a low-trust environment, “they are corrupt” can quickly become “everyone is corrupt,” which benefits the most shameless actor. Trump has survived scandal in part because his supporters view accusations against him as part of a corrupt establishment’s war on them. To pierce that shield, Democrats need more than an indictment of elite impunity. They need visible sacrifice and institutional consistency: ban congressional stock trading; impose real Supreme Court ethics rules; strengthen disclosure; police conflicts of interest in both parties; reform permitting, procurement, and contracting so government can actually build; reject the fundraising gimmicks that treat small donors like marks; and stop pretending that every problem can be solved by a tax credit designed for a stakeholder coalition.

Anti-establishment credibility also cannot mean anti-institutional nihilism. The democratic opposition must distinguish between captured institutions and institutions as such. Authoritarians want citizens to believe that courts, elections, civil servants, scientists, universities, and the press are merely factions in disguise. Democrats should expose corruption within institutions while defending the possibility of impartial rules. That is a harder message than “burn it down,” but it is essential. A democracy cannot be saved by teaching people that all authority is fake.

Comparative experience reinforces this point. Successful anti-authoritarian coalitions are usually broad, disciplined, and ideologically mixed. In Brazil, Lula defeated Jair Bolsonaro not by running as the maximal version of the left but by forming a democratic front that included Geraldo Alckmin, his former center-right rival. In Poland, the 2023 opposition victory depended on cooperation among liberals, centrists, agrarians, and the left, all focused on restoring rule of law after years of democratic erosion. In Turkey’s municipal contests, opposition gains came through pragmatic coalition-building, local competence, and candidates who could speak beyond the already converted. These movements did not win by “moderating” in the empty consultant sense. But neither did they treat breadth as betrayal. They understood that the first task in an authoritarian crisis is to split the regime’s coalition and unite everyone who still wants competitive politics to survive.

That has implications for the Democratic Party. It should not ask every candidate to sound the same, hold the same positions, or appease the same donor class. But it should impose a different kind of discipline: do not gratuitously alienate people who might join an anti-authoritarian majority. Defend transgender Americans’ dignity without forcing every local candidate into the least popular activist formulation. Support immigration and asylum while insisting that borders and legal processes must be orderly. Demand police accountability while taking public safety seriously. Pursue climate action through abundance—cheaper clean energy, faster building, reliable infrastructure—not only sacrifice and regulation. Stand with labor without implying that educational achievement or entrepreneurship are suspect. None of this requires surrendering principle. It requires translating principle into a majority language.

The same applies to “kitchen-table issues.” Bonica and Grumbach are right that a thin list of poll-tested affordability proposals cannot meet a democratic emergency. But democracy and material life should not be separated. Authoritarianism is not only a threat to norms; it is a mechanism of theft. When public power becomes personal power, ordinary people pay—in higher prices, worse services, arbitrary enforcement, crony contracts, and rights dependent on loyalty. Democrats should not choose between saying “Trump is a danger to the republic” and “your rent is too high.” They should explain that a rigged republic produces rigged markets, rigged courts, and rigged opportunities.

The party also needs to be honest about governance. Voters do not reward democracy’s defenders merely for having better values. They want proof that democratic government can deliver. The Biden years contained major legislative achievements: infrastructure, clean energy investment, industrial policy, prescription drug reforms. Yet many benefits were delayed, diffuse, or invisible, while inflation was immediate and punishing. A renewed Democratic strategy must therefore treat implementation as politics. Build faster. Make benefits legible. Reduce bureaucratic friction. Govern in ways that people can feel before the next election, not in white papers released after the loss.

Bonica and Grumbach’s call for experimentation is exactly right, but experimentation should be institutionalized rather than romanticized. Let candidates adapt to their states and districts. Invest in county parties, union halls, churches, community colleges, veterans’ networks, and local media. Recruit nurses, electricians, teachers, small-business owners, veterans, and parents who have fought school closures or hospital mergers—not as props, but as leaders with their own authority. Test messages, but do not let testing replace judgment. Study losses without assuming the answer in advance. Above all, stop treating campaigns as episodic advertising operations and start treating them as membership-building organizations.

The deepest problem with “moderation” as conventionally preached is that it often asks Democrats to become less vivid. It counsels caution when the moment calls for definition. But the deepest problem with some anti-moderation rhetoric is that it can mistake vividness for reach. A politics capable of defeating authoritarianism must be sharp enough to name the threat and generous enough to welcome latecomers, skeptics, and former opponents. It must be insurgent against corruption and conservative about constitutional democracy. It must mobilize the base and persuade the uneasy. It must be angry at impunity without becoming contemptuous of the public.

Democrats will not save democracy by sanding every edge off their agenda. Nor will they save it by assuming that every demand for reassurance is cowardice. The question is not whether to move left or center on an abstract line. The question is how to build a pro-democracy majority in a country that distrusts elites, fears disorder, resents unfairness, and still contains many millions of people who can be reached. Anti-corruption can be the bridge, but only if joined to competence, pluralism, and a willingness to share the coalition with people who disagree on much else.

The old rules are not dead so much as insufficient. Elections are nationalized, but candidates still embody trust. Turnout is crucial, but persuasion still matters. Polls are limited, but public opinion is real. Moral clarity is indispensable, but so is coalition breadth. Authoritarians thrive when their opponents choose between purity and caution. They are defeated when a democratic coalition becomes at once broader, tougher, cleaner, and more credible than the regime it seeks to replace.

The authors rightly diagnose what's broken about popularism. The median voter theorem assumes voters with stable, freestanding preferences who can be appealed to through careful positioning. That assumption collapses when party identification drives vote choice more than policy nuance, when media environments amplify partisan cues beyond any candidate's control, and when turnout itself becomes the variable that determines outcomes. These are genuine transformations, not statistical complexities to be dismissed.

But if the problem is turnout—getting your voters to the polls rather than triangulating toward the median—then the proposed solution of anti-corruption framing requires more careful examination than the essay provides.

The anti-corruption frame is seductive because it seems to transcend left-right divisions. The Yale/George Mason poll finding that Americans worry more about corruption than the economy taps into genuine and widespread disillusionment. And the authors are correct that corruption offers a moral clarity that "kitchen table" messaging cannot. It can mean aggressive prosecution of corporate malfeasance or it can mean nativist complaints about foreign corruption. Elon Musk's DOGE crusade, for example, has weaponized anti-corruption rhetoric for purposes that the authors would surely oppose. Democrats spent years demonstrating opposition to Wall Street excess only to see the phrase "both parties" land with equal force against them. Anti-corruption can become another version of the "difference without distinction" narrative that has plagued center-left parties globally.

The authors note that accommodating right-wing positions on immigration brought no electoral benefit to European center-left parties, and may have drove voters toward the far right. This is a valuable warning. But the parallel cuts the other way too: anti-corruption appeals that lack clear progressive content can be absorbed by more populist forces. The energy around the Epstein files, as the authors acknowledge, pointed toward suspicion of "a club of powerful insiders acting with impunity." That energy could be channeled toward accountability and structural reform—or it could be captured by whoever arrives first with the simplest narrative.

The essay's strongest insight is that successful anti-authoritarian movements create new political axes rather than competing on the enemy's terrain. This is historically accurate. But the mechanism by which such axes emerge remains underspecified. Leadership matters enormously here, and the essay says relatively little about what kind of candidate can carry this message. The authors criticize the moderation thesis partly on the grounds that being Manchin beats being a generic moderate, but building Manchin-level credibility in diverse districts requires years of authentic positioning that cannot be manufactured by consultants.

The turnout data the authors present is sobering. The ten-point gap in Black voter participation represents a structural crisis that no single frame will resolve. Yet mobilization is not simply a matter of finding the right message. It requires organizing infrastructure, candidate recruitment, long-term relationship building, and resources. The popularist approach may be intellectually bankrupt, but fixing the ground game requires investment that operates on different timescales than electoral strategy.

Perhaps the most important question the essay raises is whether the existential framing itself is correct: whether the threat from authoritarianism is so severe that it should override all other strategic calculations. The authors argue that it is. This may be right, but it also poses a challenge for the anti-corruption frame. Anti-corruption is a means to the end of democratic mobilization, not the end itself. If the threat is truly existential, then the measure of any strategy is whether it contributes to building the kind of coalition and consciousness capable of meeting that threat. By that metric, anti-corruption is promising because it offers moral clarity and a clear enemy. But clarity alone is insufficient; the clarity must point toward action.

The authors close by noting that history will judge Democrats on whether they fought hard enough. This is correct. But history will also judge political scientists for the quality of the advice they offer. The moderation thesis deserved the critique it received. The alternative prescription deserves equal scrutiny before it becomes the next conventional wisdom.

The Authors Are Right About the Symptom, Wrong About the Cure

Bonica and Grumbach have written the most persuasive obituary yet for the median voter theorem as applied to contemporary American politics. The empirical core of their argument—that the moderation premium has collapsed, that nationalization has swallowed local variance, that the Manchin example is being mistaken for a generalizable lesson—deserves to be taken seriously by anyone honest about the data. I would go further than they do in some places. Their list of twenty-one moderate losses out of twenty-two competitive Democratic defeats from 2016 to 2024 should, on its own, end the popularist debate as currently constructed.

But I want to register a serious objection to the prescriptive half of their essay. Having dismantled the false certainty of popularism, they reach for a different false certainty: that anti-corruption framing, combined with anti-establishment credibility and a mobilization strategy, will produce the breakthrough Democrats need. This is asserted with more confidence than the evidence supports, and it makes the same category error popularism makes—treating a frame as a strategy.

Consider the anti-corruption case. The authors cite that 73 percent of Americans believe a member of Congress would accept a bribe, and that more Americans worry about corruption than the cost of living. Fine. But there is a glaring problem: the corruption issue has been salient for at least fifteen years, and the party that has consistently won the framing battle on it is the Republican Party, even as its standard-bearer was convicted of thirty-four felonies, took foreign money through his businesses, and pardoned half the white-collar criminals of the last decade. If Trump can run as the anti-corruption candidate against Hillary Clinton, against Joe Biden, and against Kamala Harris—and win twice on something resembling that frame—then "corruption" is not a stable substantive position. It is a vibe attached to whoever is currently perceived as the outsider. Democrats holding the presidency, the bureaucracy, and most major institutions are not structurally positioned to capture it.

Brazil's Lava Jato investigation, ostensibly an anti-corruption crusade, became the launching pad for Bolsonaro. India's anti-corruption movement of the early 2010s produced Modi. The 2014 Maidan revolution in Ukraine was anti-corruption in self-description and produced a series of governments that struggled to deliver on it. Anti-corruption movements have a deeply ambiguous historical relationship to democracy. They have toppled democratic governments at least as often as they have saved them. The frame is not a partisan asset; it is a solvent that dissolves whoever holds power.

This connects to a deeper problem in the essay. Bonica and Grumbach correctly identify that voters increasingly follow leaders rather than platforms, and that authenticity matters more than positioning. But then they recommend that Democrats authentically reposition around an anti-corruption agenda. You cannot strategize your way into authenticity; that is the whole point of the concept. A Democratic Party that decides in 2026 that anti-corruption is now its mobilizing frame will be doing exactly what the authors criticize popularists for: chasing a polled-up message rather than building a durable identity.

The honest version of the authors' argument is that Democrats need a different kind of party—one that is structurally not beholden to the donor class, that recruits genuinely working-class candidates, that runs on costly material reforms—and that this transformation takes time and conflict, including internal conflict the current leadership is unwilling to provoke. I agree with all of that. But this is a ten-year project of party rebuilding, not a 2026 messaging pivot. The authors' essay slides between these two timescales in ways that obscure how difficult their prescription really is.

There is also a tension between two of their claims that they do not resolve. On the one hand, they argue that voters follow leaders, not platforms, citing Trump's contradictions on vaccines, abortion, and the minimum wage. On the other hand, they argue that Democrats need substantive, costly reforms to build anti-establishment credibility. But if leaders shape followers, then a charismatic Democrat who simply asserts anti-establishment credibility while taking corporate money might do just as well as one who actually rejects it. Bernie Sanders rejected corporate money and lost two primaries. Trump took corporate money openly and won. The "follow the leader" theory and the "substantive reform" prescription point in different directions, and the authors should have grappled with that.

None of this is to defend popularism, which the authors have largely demolished. It is to say that the alternative they sketch is underdeveloped. The honest answer to "what should Democrats do" in February 2026 may be: nobody knows, experimentation is warranted, and any strategist claiming certainty—popularist or populist—is selling something.

If I had to name what is missing from the essay, it is an acknowledgment that the most important variable is probably not ideology, framing, or even mobilization, but candidate quality and biographical fit. The Democrats who consistently overperform—Sherrod Brown when he won, Jon Tester when he won, John Fetterman in his Pennsylvania run, even AOC in her primary upset—did so through a combination of personal story, communication style, and willingness to break with the party's professional class on selective issues. None of this is reducible to a left/center axis, and none of it is reducible to anti-corruption messaging. The authors gesture at this when they say "let a thousand flowers bloom," but that gesture is in tension with the prescriptive certainty of the rest of the piece.

Democrats should reject popularism. They should also reject the next thing that sounds equally certain. The lesson of the data the authors have assembled is humility, not a new orthodoxy.

In Defense of the Despised Median Voter

Bonica and Grumbach have produced a vigorous, data-rich argument that I find ultimately unconvincing, and I want to explain why someone who has read the same political science literature can reach the opposite conclusion. The essay's central empirical claim—that the moderation advantage has disappeared in the Trump era—rests on a methodological move that deserves more scrutiny than it has received.

Consider their closely-contested-primaries design. The logic is sound: in primaries decided by tiny margins, the winner is essentially randomly assigned, so we can compare the general-election performance of moderates versus progressives who won "coin-flip" primaries and isolate the causal effect of candidate ideology. They find no significant general-election difference. Therefore, moderation provides no benefit. QED.

But notice what this design measures. It compares moderates and progressives who survived a competitive primary in the same district. By construction, both candidates are viable enough to win a Democratic primary. Both have run effective campaigns. Both have raised money. The design is excellent for isolating ideology while holding district and viability constant, but it tells us almost nothing about the cases that worry moderates most: districts where progressives never make it through the primary because they cannot raise money, mobilize voters, or attract endorsements in the first place. The design conditions on selection into the competitive set, which is precisely where the moderation advantage is most likely to operate.

A related problem haunts their twenty-one-out-of-twenty-two statistic. Of course twenty-one Democratic incumbents who lost competitive seats from 2016 to 2024 were moderates. Almost every Democrat representing a competitive seat is a moderate. The denominator matters. If 95 percent of Democrats in competitive districts are moderates and 95 percent of losses are moderates, that is a finding of zero ideological signal, not a finding against moderation. The authors know this. But the way the statistic is deployed in the essay invites the reader to draw a stronger inference than the data support.

There is a more fundamental issue. The authors' argument implicitly treats "moderation" as a single variable—a position on a left-right axis. But the popularist critique of progressive Democrats has rarely been about left-right placement in the abstract. It has been about a specific cluster of culturally coded positions: "defund the police," opposition to enforcement at the border, "Latinx," the language and posture associated with the post-2020 left. Whether one approves or disapproves of these positions, the empirical question is whether they specifically harm Democratic candidates. There is substantial evidence that they do—not because voters carefully evaluate policy platforms, but because these positions become symbolic markers that travel through national media and attach to the party brand regardless of what local candidates say.

David Shor's analyses, whatever their methodological imperfections, consistently identify a specific subset of progressive positions that perform poorly. The authors largely ignore this granularity, treating "moderation" as a monolithic variable they can statistically dispatch. But the popularist argument that they need to refute is not "candidates should be more centrist on every issue." It is "candidates and the party should stop letting a small number of culturally unpopular positions define them." On that narrower claim, the evidence is much stronger than the authors let on.

I also want to push back on their treatment of turnout. They argue that Democrats' problem is not persuading moderates but mobilizing the base, and that progressive candidates are "slightly more effective" at mobilizing registered Democrats. Two problems. First, "slightly" is doing a lot of work. The base-mobilization effect they identify, even taken at face value, is small relative to the swings produced by national conditions and partisan tides. Second, the 2024 election presents an awkward problem for the mobilization theory: Harris lost in part because nonvoters in key states leaned Trump. The disengaged were not progressive young people waiting for a clarion call. Many were working-class voters of all races who had drifted right on cultural and economic issues. A more aggressively progressive nominee in 2024 would likely have lost by more, not less, in the Sun Belt and the industrial Midwest. The base-mobilization story works for some elections and fails for others. It is not the general theory the authors imply.

Finally, the anti-corruption pivot. I am sympathetic in principle. Democrats should ban congressional stock trading; they should reject corporate PAC money where possible; they should recruit working-class candidates. But the strategic theory behind the prescription is weaker than it looks. Anti-corruption framing has been Trump's frame, not the Democrats', for a decade. Voters who are persuadable on corruption have already chosen their corruption-fighter. The Democratic anti-corruption pitch has the disadvantage of being directed at an opponent whose voters have spent ten years deciding that "corrupt" means "the establishment that the establishment media defends," which they read as the Democrats.

What would I recommend instead? Roughly what the popularists recommend, with corrections. Democrats should run candidates who match their districts. They should de-emphasize the small set of culturally polarizing positions that have become unforced errors. They should defend tangible material achievements when they have them and propose tangible material improvements when they do not. They should mobilize the base where mobilization is the binding constraint and persuade swing voters where persuasion is. This is unglamorous, varies by context, and lacks the moral grandeur of the authors' prescription. But it is closer to how elections are actually won.

The authors close by warning that "history won't judge Democrats on whether they were moderate enough." Perhaps not. But history will judge them on whether they won. And the path to winning, in the absence of any reliable evidence that aggressive populism produces consistent gains, runs through the boring discipline of meeting voters where they are.

The Missing Variable Is Class, Not Corruption

Bonica and Grumbach's essay is the most ambitious anti-popularist intervention to date, and it is right about almost everything until the last third. Where it falters is in its diagnosis of what has actually shifted in the American electorate—a shift that the corruption frame, however appealing, can only partially capture.

The single most important change in American politics over the past twenty years is the realignment of educational polarization. Working-class voters of every race have moved right, faster among whites, more recently among Latinos, and now perceptibly among Black men. College-educated voters of every race have moved left. This is not a marginal trend. It is the largest demographic reshuffling of partisan loyalties since the New Deal coalition formed and dissolved. And it has produced a Democratic Party that wins the votes of doctors, lawyers, professors, and tech workers while losing carpenters, truckers, line cooks, and nurses.

The authors gesture at this when they call for recruiting "nurses, teachers, veterans." But they treat candidate recruitment as a presentation strategy, a way to demonstrate that the party "is not beholden to the same professional class." This understates the depth of the problem. The professional-class orientation of the Democratic Party is not a branding issue; it is a substantive issue. It shapes which policies get prioritized, which industries get protected, which constituencies get heard in primary debates, and which cultural inflections become the party's default mode.

A Democratic Party whose donor base is concentrated in finance, tech, and entertainment, whose congressional staff is drawn from the top fifty universities, and whose policy intelligentsia is professionally rewarded for sophisticated takes that play well on Twitter is going to produce policy outputs and rhetorical postures that read as foreign to the median noncollege voter. No amount of anti-corruption messaging fixes this if the messaging is delivered by people who manifestly do not share the cultural world of the people they are trying to mobilize.

This is what Trump understood and what Sanders also understood, in very different ways. Trump's appeal to working-class voters was never primarily about policy; it was about cultural alignment and a posture of contempt for the credentialed class. Sanders's appeal was about substantive economic positions that named the donor class as the enemy. Both succeeded because they offered a structural account of who had power and who was getting screwed. Neither succeeded by triangulating, and neither succeeded primarily by complaining about corruption in the abstract.

Bonica and Grumbach's anti-corruption frame is in some ways a sophisticated dodge. It allows the party to position itself against "the system" without taking on its own donor base, its own credentialed staff, or the cultural posture that alienates working-class voters. Banning congressional stock trading is fine. So is rejecting corporate PAC money. But these are symbolic gestures relative to the actual structural problem: the Democratic Party has become the party of the professional managerial class, and it cannot become a winning coalition party without changing that.

What would change look like? It would mean policies that visibly redistribute toward wage workers, even at the expense of educated professionals: aggressive antitrust against industries professionals work in, tariffs that protect manufacturing even when economists frown, immigration policy that takes seriously the wage effects on incumbent workers, housing policy that breaks the asset interests of homeowners (many of whom are Democratic voters). It would mean breaking with the cultural priors of the credentialed left when those priors conflict with the material interests or expressed preferences of the working class.

This is harder than anti-corruption messaging, and it is harder than turnout maximization. It requires the party to lose some of its current voters in order to gain others—the explicit logic of realignment, which the authors avoid because their model is additive (mobilize more base, redraw the battlefield, win) rather than zero-sum (give up some current supporters to gain different ones).

Their turnout argument also bears more scrutiny in light of class realignment. The authors cite the 10.9-point Black-white turnout gap and the decline in youth turnout. But the implicit assumption is that closing these gaps benefits Democrats proportionally. This is increasingly suspect. Young men of all races shifted toward Trump in 2024. Black men shifted toward Trump. Latino men shifted toward Trump. The marginal nonvoter in many key demographics is no longer a reliable Democrat-in-waiting. Higher turnout among 18-29-year-old men in Nevada in 2024 would have helped Trump. The "mobilize the base" theory is increasingly anachronistic because the base itself has changed.

The political theory that the authors really need is one that takes class realignment seriously and asks: what coalition can Democrats actually assemble in 2026 and 2028? Two possibilities exist. The first is to lean fully into the educated coalition, accepting that this means a smaller but more cohesive party that competes hard for suburban moderates of all parties. This is roughly the popularist program, and it has a coherent logic. The second is to attempt a class-realignment counterattack, building back working-class support through substantive policy and cultural realignment, even at the cost of the credentialed liberals who currently dominate the party's funding and staffing.

The authors want a third option that bypasses this choice: mobilize the base through anti-corruption framing, redraw the battlefield, transcend the moderate-progressive divide. I do not think this option exists. The class divide is real, and any winning strategy has to confront it directly rather than route around it.

Where I agree with the authors completely: the popularist theory of politics is empirically bankrupt. Where I depart: their replacement theory underestimates the structural depth of what has gone wrong. American politics is realigning along class and educational lines, and Democrats are losing the larger side of that realignment. No frame, however clever, substitutes for the work of becoming a different kind of party.

A Defense of Boring

I want to defend a position that is unfashionable in this debate: that the most likely cause of Democrats' difficulties is not too much moderation, not too little populism, and not the wrong frame, but the simple fact of incumbency during a period of global inflation, and that most of the strategic prescriptions on offer—including those of Bonica and Grumbach—are overconfident attempts to extract strategy lessons from what is mostly noise.

Consider what happened to incumbent parties around the world in 2024. The British Conservatives were routed. The French government lost its majority. The Japanese LDP suffered historic losses. The German governing coalition collapsed. The South African ANC lost its majority for the first time in thirty years. The South Korean ruling party suffered a serious defeat. Across the entire democratic world, ruling parties of every ideological coloration—left, right, technocratic, populist—were punished by voters. The single best predictor of 2024 electoral outcomes was not ideological positioning. It was whether your party held power during the post-COVID inflation.

The Democrats lost the 2024 presidential election by 1.5 points in the popular vote. This is not a catastrophic defeat that demands radical strategic rethinking. It is approximately the result you would predict from a model that knew only (1) inflation had been running above normal for three years, (2) the incumbent president was unpopular, and (3) the Democratic candidate had nine weeks to introduce herself after a chaotic switch. In other words: the result was overdetermined by fundamentals, and arguments about whether moderation cost Harris the election, or whether more progressive messaging would have saved her, are largely unmoored from how presidential elections actually work.

Bonica and Grumbach acknowledge nationalization but use it mostly to argue against moderation. The fuller implication of nationalization is more sobering: candidate ideology, candidate messaging, and candidate strategy explain a shrinking share of variance in outcomes. The factors that do matter—national economic conditions, incumbent approval, the broader media environment—are largely outside any campaign's control. This is humbling for strategists of all ideological persuasions, and it implies that most claims about what "Democrats need to do" are overstated.

This is not an argument against thinking about strategy. It is an argument against the genre conventions that have developed around political strategy writing, in which writers attribute electoral outcomes to whatever they were already predisposed to recommend. Yglesias attributes Democratic losses to insufficient moderation. Bonica and Grumbach attribute them to popularist timidity and corruption tolerance. Progressives attribute them to corporate capture. Centrists attribute them to "the squad." All of these accounts treat a noisy, fundamentals-driven outcome as if it were a clean test of strategic hypotheses. None of them grapple seriously with the possibility that the election was mostly about eggs and gasoline.

What follows from this? A few things. First, humility about strategy claims. The 2024 result is not a referendum on popularism, and the next election will not be a referendum on whatever new strategy emerges from this debate. Second, attention to fundamentals: keeping inflation low, presiding over visible economic improvement, maintaining presidential approval. These are not glamorous, and they are partly outside party control, but they swamp everything else. Third, structural reforms that change the playing field: voting rights, electoral reform, anti-gerrymandering measures, ending the filibuster when possible. These pay dividends across many elections, not just one.

I should also defend boring at the level of candidates. Bonica and Grumbach are right that "being Manchin wins" rather than "being moderate wins." The implication is that biography and brand matter more than ideology. The candidates who consistently overperform are those who have built durable local identities over time, often through unglamorous service in lower offices, often through long association with local institutions like unions or veterans' groups or churches. The cult of the breakthrough national-profile candidate—the AOC or the Mamdani—is overrated as a model for swing-district politics. Most winning Democrats in tough districts will continue to be people whose names voters know from school board meetings and chamber of commerce breakfasts, not people who went viral.

This is also why I am skeptical of the authors' anti-corruption frame as a national strategy. National frames matter, but they matter through national figures with national reach. Below the presidential level, what matters is whether voters trust their specific candidate. Trust is built locally and slowly. It is not built by a national strategic pivot.

There is one place where I agree with the authors strongly: the popularist tendency to dress strategic claims in scientific authority is bad practice. Polling can tell you what voters say they think; it cannot tell you how messages will land in a contested information environment, or what would happen if a candidate took a contrary position. The Split Ticket WAR metric, as the authors show, contains far less empirical substance than its presentation implies. Democrats should be wary of any consultant or analyst who claims that they have decoded the electorate.

But the same wariness should extend to the authors' own confident prescriptions. Anti-corruption framing, anti-establishment positioning, base mobilization—these may help on the margin, in some races, with some candidates. They are not a winning national formula, because there is no winning national formula. There are only patient, candidate-by-candidate, district-by-district efforts to assemble enough wins to govern.

What Democrats most need is not a new strategy. It is a willingness to wait out the post-incumbency punishment, hold the line on the institutional commitments that matter, develop a deep bench of plausible candidates for the next two cycles, and stop expecting any one frame or messaging strategy to overcome the structural headwinds that the global inflation produced. By 2028, the inflation memory will have faded, and the same Democrats whose moderation supposedly failed in 2024 will be plausibly competitive again—not because their strategy has improved, but because they will no longer be carrying the incumbency burden.

This is a less exciting story than either the popularist or the anti-corruption frame, and that is precisely its virtue.

The Mamdani Lesson Is Not the One You Think

Bonica and Grumbach mention Zohran Mamdani only briefly, as a foil to popularist caution. But the Mamdani victory in New York City in 2025 is, in my view, the single most important data point in American Democratic politics in the last five years, and it deserves more serious analysis than it has received from either popularists who dismiss it as a one-off urban anomaly or progressives who treat it as a portable template.

What Mamdani actually showed is something the authors' framework half-grasps but does not fully articulate. He did not win because he was further left, or because of anti-corruption messaging, or because of base mobilization in the narrow sense. He won because he ran a campaign with a coherent theory of communication, organized field operation, a charismatic candidate who could metabolize hostile media without being defensive, and a small number of clear, materially salient promises that voters could repeat.

That combination is exceedingly rare. It is rare not because Democrats are timid or corrupt, but because the Democratic Party's candidate-recruitment pipeline is not designed to produce it. The party recruits former prosecutors, military officers, and credentialed policy people. It does not, by and large, recruit organizers, communicators, or people with the rhetorical training to win an argument on hostile turf. The Mamdani campaign was anomalous because Mamdani himself is anomalous: a former rapper turned state assemblyman with deep ties to organized constituencies and a willingness to do extended interviews with conservative podcasters without being neutralized.

This points to a different diagnosis than Bonica and Grumbach offer. Their story is that the popularist program failed because it cannot mobilize the base or generate moral clarity. My story is that the popularist program failed because it was being implemented by candidates and consultants who were chosen precisely for their unsuitability to the present media environment. Kamala Harris did not lose because her positioning was wrong. She lost because she was not, on the evidence of the campaign, capable of unscripted communication at the level the moment required. Joe Biden was withdrawn because his diminishment had finally become undeniable, but he had been a marginal communicator for the entire administration. The Democratic communications problem is a personnel problem before it is a strategy problem.

I want to push this further. The center of gravity of Democratic talent production has been law school and policy school. The center of gravity of Republican talent production has been—increasingly over the last decade—media, podcasting, and right-wing institutional networks that train people to argue on camera, take hostile questions, and use humor. The asymmetry between Democratic and Republican communicators in 2025 was not an ideological asymmetry. It was a talent-pipeline asymmetry. Republicans had Trump, Vance, Ramaswamy, Crenshaw, Cotton, and dozens of younger figures who could perform competently on long-form audio. Democrats had Newsom, Buttigieg, AOC, and... a steep dropoff. The bench is thin not because moderates are too cautious but because the party has not invested in developing communicators.

This is connected to Bonica and Grumbach's anti-corruption argument in a way they do not develop. The reason anti-corruption framing fails for most Democrats is that it requires a communicator who can credibly inhabit it. Mamdani can credibly run against the New York real estate machine because he is a young immigrant whose biography vibes with the message. A Senate Democrat in his fourth term whose largest donors are biotech executives cannot credibly run on anti-corruption no matter how many press releases he issues about congressional stock trading. The frame requires the right messenger, and most Democratic messengers cannot wear it.

If this is right, then the prescription is different from either popularism or anti-corruption populism. The prescription is: recruit and develop candidates whose biographies and communication skills fit the current information environment, give them substantive platforms they can credibly embody, and let them figure out the framing in their specific contexts. This is closer to the "let a thousand flowers bloom" gesture in the authors' essay, but it is more demanding than they acknowledge. It requires the party to substantially reorganize its recruitment, training, and funding infrastructure.

It also requires accepting that some of these candidates will be progressive in a way the popularists hate, some will be moderate in a way the progressives hate, and the cohesion of the party will come not from ideological discipline but from a shared willingness to fight on the current battlefield. Mamdani is not a template for everyone, but the meta-lesson—communicate effectively, organize relentlessly, make materially salient promises, do not be defensive—is portable.

I will add one note of caution. Mamdani's victory occurred in New York City, where the Democratic primary effectively decides the election and the median voter is deeply progressive. Replicating his model in a swing district is not impossible, but it requires translation that the most enthusiastic progressives often refuse to do. The portable lesson is the candidate-quality lesson, not the ideological-positioning lesson. Anyone telling Democrats that Mamdani proves the party should run on rent freezes and city-owned grocery stores in Bucks County is not paying attention to either New York or Bucks County.

So I take the authors' larger point: moderation as the universal strategic prescription is dead, killed by nationalization, polarization, and the collapse of split-ticket voting. But the alternative is not a new universal prescription. It is a recognition that the only durable Democratic strategy is to develop better candidates and trust them to find their own paths in their own contexts. Everything else is consultant noise.

What Comparative Politics Actually Teaches

Bonica and Grumbach invoke "what has worked in other countries that have faced democratic backsliding" and draw three lessons from this comparative experience: reframe the battlefield around anti-corruption, build anti-establishment credibility, boost turnout. As a comparativist, I want to register that the actual comparative record is considerably messier than they suggest, and that the lessons are not as clean as the essay implies.

Start with the cases. Where, in the recent comparative record, have democratic oppositions successfully defeated authoritarian or proto-authoritarian incumbents? The most-cited cases are Poland's Tusk-led coalition in 2023, the various opposition wins against Erdoğan at the municipal level (most prominently in Istanbul), the Brazilian opposition's defeat of Bolsonaro in 2022, and the partial successes of opposition movements in Hungary's 2022 election (which the opposition lost but compressed Orbán's margin in some places) and the 2025 South Korean response to Yoon's impeachment crisis.

What do these cases actually have in common? Not anti-corruption framing as a primary strategy. Tusk ran on a complicated coalition of issues including European integration, abortion rights, judicial independence, and economic management. The Brazilian opposition coalesced around Lula, a figure who had himself been imprisoned on corruption charges (later annulled) and who could not credibly run a clean-hands campaign. The Istanbul opposition won by running a charismatic mayoral candidate, İmamoğlu, who emphasized service delivery and citywide identity rather than anti-corruption as such.

What these cases do have in common is something different: they featured broad pro-democratic coalitions that explicitly subordinated ideological disagreement to the defense of democratic institutions. Tusk's coalition stretched from the center-right Civic Platform to the agrarians to the progressive Left, united by what they were against more than by what they were for. Brazilian opposition stretched from genuine leftists to former military officers who had served under the dictatorship. The Korean opposition, including conservative legislators, voted to impeach Yoon when the moment demanded it.

This points to a lesson Bonica and Grumbach gesture at but do not fully embrace: defeating authoritarian movements requires broad coalitions that explicitly include people the activist base does not like. It requires center-right pro-democracy figures who can speak to disaffected conservatives. It requires institutional alliances with parts of civil society—business, religious organizations, the military, established media—that may have nothing else in common with the progressive left. The authors' framework, with its emphasis on anti-establishment credibility and class realignment, is in some tension with this lesson, because broad pro-democracy coalitions are establishment coalitions almost by definition. They bring together the institutional sectors that have a stake in democratic continuity.

Consider the Harris campaign's much-criticized embrace of Liz Cheney. The authors treat this as a failed popularist gesture. But Cheney coalitions are precisely the comparative model that has worked elsewhere. The problem with the Cheney outreach was not that it was attempted; it was that it was attempted thinly, late, and without the substantive concessions that would make it real. A serious pro-democracy coalition would have included not just Cheney but a cadre of former Republicans in visible roles, not just rhetorical appeals to "moderates" but actual policy compromises that demonstrated the seriousness of the coalition.

This brings me to the deeper disagreement. Bonica and Grumbach treat the choice as between popularist moderation and a populist anti-corruption mobilization. But the comparative record suggests a third path that they do not consider: institutional pro-democracy coalition politics. This path involves explicitly subordinating progressive policy goals to the defense of democratic institutions, building coalitions that include reluctant conservatives, accepting that this means making peace with constituencies the progressive base dislikes, and treating elections as referenda on regime survival rather than as opportunities for ideological progress.

This is, frankly, what the popularist strategy was at its best, even if its proponents often described it in different terms. Klein and Yglesias and others have been arguing for a version of this for years—a politics of broad anti-Trump coalition that explicitly downplays progressive policy ambitions in order to defend the institutional baseline. Bonica and Grumbach treat this as failed, but the comparative cases they implicitly invoke are mostly cases that look more like the popularist model than like their populist alternative.

What about anti-corruption specifically? The record is genuinely mixed. Some opposition movements have used anti-corruption framing effectively, but as I noted at the start, anti-corruption mobilizations have at least as often produced new authoritarian regimes as defeated existing ones. The Italian Mani Pulite investigations of the 1990s collapsed the old party system and created the political space for Berlusconi—the original right-populist media oligarch. The Brazilian Lava Jato investigations contributed directly to Bolsonaro's rise. India's 2011 anti-corruption movement produced Modi. The frame is structurally unstable in ways the authors do not address.

The honest comparative lesson is this: there is no single strategy that reliably defeats backsliding incumbents. What there is, in the cases of success, is some combination of (1) a broad pro-democracy coalition that explicitly sets aside other disagreements, (2) candidates with personal credibility and effective communication skills, (3) institutional alliances that span the ideological spectrum, and (4) sustained civil society mobilization that goes beyond electoral politics. The authors capture some of (4) and gesture at (2). They miss or actively oppose (1) and (3), which the comparative record suggests are actually the most important.

If Democrats want to learn from successful pro-democracy coalitions abroad, the lesson is not to become more populist. It is to become more capacious—building real coalitions with the parts of American conservatism that still believe in constitutional order, even when this requires making peace with policy positions the progressive base finds intolerable. This is hard, and it is unsatisfying to activists, but it is what the comparative cases the authors implicitly invoke actually show.

What the Authors Get Right About the Information Environment, and What They Miss

The strongest part of Bonica and Grumbach's essay is the argument that elections have nationalized in ways that make traditional candidate positioning largely irrelevant. They are right that AOC and Jared Golden rise and fall with the same partisan tides, that voters increasingly choose Team Red or Team Blue rather than evaluating individual candidates, and that the "all politics is local" frame is no longer accurate. This is well-documented in political science and worth taking seriously.

But the authors do not follow through on the implications of their own argument. If elections are now national referenda mediated by an information environment that overwhelms individual candidate signals, then the central strategic question is not what candidates should do but how the information environment itself can be shaped. And on this question—the central question—the essay has very little to say.

Consider the actual mechanism by which "national tides" reach voters. Voters do not receive raw partisan information directly from Washington. They receive it through specific media channels: cable news, social media platforms, podcasts, local television (declining but still significant in some markets), partisan websites, and increasingly, algorithmic content streams curated by platforms whose incentives have nothing to do with civic deliberation. The character of these channels has changed dramatically over the last decade in ways that systematically advantage the right.

The right has built a media ecosystem—Fox News, the broader Sinclair empire, the major right-wing podcast network anchored by Rogan, Carlson, and the Daily Wire ecosystem, the political talk-radio infrastructure that has been operating since the 1990s, the relatively coordinated activist Twitter/X presence amplified by the platform's owner—that the left has nothing approaching. The Democratic-aligned media environment is fractured, undercapitalized, and structurally less able to coordinate national messaging. MSNBC reaches a fraction of Fox's audience. There is no left-wing equivalent of the Daily Wire. The most successful left-leaning podcasts (Pod Save America, Chapo, Klein's show) have audiences orders of magnitude smaller than Rogan's.

This asymmetry has electoral consequences that swamp anything the authors discuss. When the authors note that messaging is "drowned out by partisan tides," what they are describing is the operation of a media environment that systematically delivers right-wing framings to a much larger audience than left-wing framings reach. The 2024 election was decided in part because, in the closing months, the right-wing media ecosystem could coordinate sustained attacks on Harris on a small number of themes (the border, "she/her" pronoun videos, inflation), while no equivalent coordinated attack on Trump existed in the media diet of low-engagement voters.

The authors' anti-corruption frame, however appealing on the merits, runs into this structural problem immediately. A Democratic anti-corruption message has to travel through a media environment in which (a) the right-wing ecosystem will reframe it as a Democratic attack on the working class, the police, traditional values, etc., and (b) the centrist mainstream media will sanewash both sides. Even if Democrats had a perfect anti-corruption messenger and a perfect anti-corruption agenda, the message would not reach the voters who need it without a media infrastructure capable of carrying it.

What follows? Two things. First, Democrats and Democratic-aligned philanthropists should be investing in media infrastructure at a scale far beyond anything currently contemplated. This is not glamorous, it has long time horizons, and it does not produce wins in the next election cycle, but it is the most important structural intervention available. The right understood this in the 1980s and built the institutions over decades. The left has been comparatively absent, partly because of a misplaced faith that mainstream institutions would carry the necessary information, and partly because liberal donors prefer to fund issue advocacy with measurable outputs.

Second, the most strategically important Democratic figures in the next decade may not be politicians at all. They may be the people who build durable left-leaning media platforms—not the boutique podcasts that talk to the converted, but the genuinely cross-pressured platforms that can reach voters who are not already Democrats. This is the model of Joe Rogan, who began as a comedian and ended up shaping the information diet of millions of low-engagement male voters. There is no Democratic equivalent. Producing one is a more important strategic priority than any messaging shift.

The authors gesture at this when they discuss authenticity and the "tells it like it is" appeal, but they treat it as a feature of candidate positioning rather than as a feature of the platform on which candidates appear. The platform shapes what authenticity looks like. Trump succeeded in part because he came up through media (The Apprentice, Twitter, cable news appearances) and was fluent in its forms. Most Democratic politicians come up through politics and are not fluent. This is not fixable by repositioning; it requires a different recruitment model and a different media infrastructure.

I would push the authors' argument in a more structural direction. Yes, the median voter theorem is dead. Yes, moderation has lost its premium. But these are downstream effects of a deeper change: the information environment in which voters form opinions has been restructured in ways that systematically advantage one side. Until Democrats grapple with this at the level of infrastructure rather than at the level of messaging, no strategic pivot—popularist, populist, or otherwise—will produce sustained gains.

Anti-corruption framing is fine. Recruiting working-class candidates is fine. Mobilizing the base is fine. But the binding constraint on Democratic success is upstream of all of these, in the architecture of how political information moves through American society. That is where the next major Democratic project must be located if the authors' larger ambition—defending democracy against an authoritarian movement—is to be plausible at all.

Methodological Notes from a Skeptic

I want to take Bonica and Grumbach's empirical claims seriously enough to disagree with them on the methods. The essay leans heavily on the authority of "research designs that can distinguish causal effects from correlations" and "comprehensive measures of ideology" to claim that the moderation effect has disappeared. As someone who works in this corner of political science, I think both the popularist analyses they critique and their own counter-analyses are weaker than presented, and that the resulting debate has more heat than light.

Begin with measurement. The authors say they built ideology measures combining "over a dozen distinct metrics" including voting records, campaign finance data (DW-NOMINATE and CFscores), candidate websites, and voter evaluations. This is a sophisticated approach, but it conceals a real problem: these measures correlate imperfectly with each other and produce different rankings of candidates. CFscores in particular—which Bonica himself has done foundational work developing—measure something specific: the ideological composition of a candidate's donor network. A candidate who raises money from progressive donors will score progressive on CFscores even if their voting record is moderate. The reverse is also true. Combining these measures via principal components or similar techniques can mask substantial cross-measure disagreement about who is "moderate."

This matters because the empirical claim that "moderation does not help" is sensitive to how moderation is defined. Define it via DW-NOMINATE (roll-call votes), and you are measuring something downstream of the legislator's actual behavior. Define it via CFscores, and you are measuring donor composition. Define it via candidate websites, and you are measuring strategic self-presentation. These can diverge sharply, and analyses that combine them may produce stable averages with high internal variance that disappears in the aggregate.

The closely-contested-primary design has its own problems. It is, as I noted, an excellent design for isolating ideology in a specific subpopulation, but the inferential reach is limited. Close primary contests between viable moderates and viable progressives are themselves selected events. They occur in districts where the Democratic primary electorate is plausibly divided, where both candidates can raise money, and where the institutional pressures from national groups have not foreclosed one side. The estimates from this design tell us about ideology's marginal effect in this specific population, not about the general effect of running progressives in arbitrary districts.

The authors' headline claim about the twenty-one out of twenty-two competitive losses being moderates is also methodologically weaker than presented. As critics have noted, the denominator is overwhelmingly moderate; the ratio is approximately what you would expect from a null effect of ideology on outcomes. The authors know this; the essay's framing does not.

I am being hard on the authors not because I disagree with their conclusion that moderation's premium has shrunk—I think this is probably true—but because the certainty with which they present it is overstated, in a way that mirrors the popularist certainty they criticize. The honest scholarly position is that the moderation premium has likely declined, that the magnitude of decline is genuinely uncertain, and that context-dependent effects probably remain in specific races. This is not the rhetorical posture of the essay.

The same caution applies to their turnout claims. The finding that progressive candidates are "slightly more effective at mobilizing their base" is presented as the central empirical alternative to the popularist focus on swing voters. But the magnitude of "slightly" is doing significant work. The authors do not, in this essay, give a clear point estimate. If the base-mobilization effect of running progressives is one percentage point and the swing-voter persuasion effect is zero, then their argument holds. If the base-mobilization effect is one percentage point and the swing-voter effect is one percentage point in the opposite direction, then it does not. Without the magnitudes, the policy implication is indeterminate.

Their description of the Manchin/Elliott contrast is also worth scrutinizing. Manchin won; Elliott, also a moderate, lost by 40 points. The authors conclude that "being Manchin wins, not being moderate." This is too clean. The 2024 environment in West Virginia was vastly different from any previous Manchin election. Trump was on the ballot, the state had continued its rightward trend, and the open seat was always going to be much harder than any Manchin contest had been. Attributing the entire 40-point margin to "not being Manchin" overlooks the political environment in which Elliott ran. A Manchin clone in 2024 might have lost by twenty-five points instead of forty—a substantial difference that still falls short of a win, but a difference nonetheless. The contrast does not establish what the authors want it to establish.

What is my own view? I think the moderation premium has declined significantly, particularly in high-information races where partisan tides dominate. I think there are still races—lower-profile state legislative races, races involving incumbent personal brands, races in states with strong split-ticket traditions like Maine or Alaska—where it matters. I think the popularist program has overpromised on the basis of analyses that have not adequately addressed selection effects and have over-relied on opaque proprietary metrics. I also think the authors' counter-program overpromises in the opposite direction, on the basis of analyses that have their own limitations.

The honest message is one the authors articulate at one point and then walk back: "nobody knows for certain." Their preferred prescriptions—anti-corruption framing, anti-establishment credibility, turnout maximization—deserve consideration as plausible directions, but they should be tested with the same skepticism the authors apply to popularist claims. There is no demonstrated alternative strategy that reliably outperforms the moderation strategy whose decline they document. The next decade of American politics will probably involve a lot of experimentation, much of which will fail, and the strategists who insist they have already figured it out are the ones to be most suspicious of, on either side of this debate.

A View from the Districts the Authors Are Talking About

I have worked on House races in the kinds of districts Bonica and Grumbach are theorizing about—the rural Pennsylvania seats, the Central Valley California seats, the upstate New York seats. I want to push back, gently but firmly, on the gap between their theoretical argument and the operational reality of those campaigns.

The first thing to know about a campaign in a swing rural or exurban district is that the candidate is not running a national strategy. They are running against a specific opponent in a specific media market with specific local issues that voters care about. The "anti-corruption" frame the authors recommend is invisible at the local level. Voters in a swing district care about whether their local Democrat is going to make their lives noticeably worse on the small number of issues that have salience for them: typically taxes, the cost of living, a specific industry that matters in the district (farming, manufacturing, energy), and—increasingly—immigration and cultural issues that get nationalized through right-wing media.

The most successful Democratic House candidates in these districts—people like Mary Peltola, who won and then lost in Alaska, like Pat Ryan in upstate New York, like Marcy Kaptur in Ohio—do not win by adopting a national populist anti-corruption frame. They win by being credibly local, by demonstrating specific competence on issues that matter in their district, by visibly representing the cultural identity of the district, and by carefully avoiding association with the most polarizing national-Democratic positions. This is not "moderation" in the abstract sense the authors critique; it is something more like cultural and biographical fit.

The authors lump this into the "being Manchin wins" category and treat it as not generalizable. But it is generalizable, in the sense that what successful swing-district Democrats share is precisely the willingness to position themselves on local terms rather than national terms. This is what popularism, at its best, has been advocating: not "be more moderate on a left-right axis," but "be more attentive to local political conditions and less captured by national-progressive cultural posture."

The authors' anti-corruption frame would be a disaster in many of these districts. Why? Because the corruption the right-wing media ecosystem talks about—the Biden family's financial dealings, the FBI's alleged political bias, the deep state—is not the corruption the authors mean. In the lived informational environment of a swing-district voter, "anti-corruption" cues right-wing framings, not progressive framings. Democrats running on anti-corruption would find themselves immediately on the defensive about their own party's actual or alleged scandals, and they would lose the framing battle in the local media.

This points to a deeper problem with the authors' argument. They write as if there is a single national strategic conversation in which Democrats can collectively choose a frame and deploy it nationally. But the national-strategic conversation is precisely what the authors elsewhere argue has nationalized politics in destructive ways. If candidates must rise and fall with national tides, then the question is: what national posture should the party project? And the answer to that is not the same as the answer to the question: what should a candidate in PA-7 actually do?

The authors collapse these two questions in a way that obscures the operational dilemma. A national Democratic posture of populist anti-corruption mobilization may, plausibly, energize some segments of the base and produce mobilization gains. It may also produce a national party brand that swing-district candidates find unworkable. The right-wing media will translate the populist frame into "Democrats want to defund the police, dismantle the border, and confiscate your guns," and the swing-district candidate has to run against that translation. A more careful, less ideologically distinctive national posture—which is what the popularists have been advocating—gives swing-district candidates more room to operate locally.

This is the part of the popularist case the authors do not adequately rebut. The popularists are not arguing that moderation as an individual candidate strategy is a silver bullet. They are arguing that the national party brand has costs for swing-district candidates that the activist base does not adequately appreciate, and that a more disciplined national posture would give those candidates more room. This is closer to a true claim than the authors allow.

I will say something about turnout. The authors emphasize the turnout gap and argue that Democrats should focus on mobilizing the base. But the mobilization theory is much weaker in the kinds of swing districts I have worked in than it is in deep-blue cities. Democratic primary electorates in swing districts are small, frequently more moderate than the national party, and dominated by older voters. The "base" the authors imagine—young, diverse, mobilizable through populist messaging—is largely absent from these districts. The marginal voter in a swing-district House race is more likely to be a registered independent in their fifties who voted for both Obama and Trump than to be a 25-year-old non-voter waiting for inspiration.

The authors are theorizing about national strategy from a perspective that overweights the dynamics of national-presidential and major-urban races and underweights the operational reality of the swing districts that determine which party governs. The kinds of districts that swung in 2024—the suburban and exurban districts that decide House majorities—are not won by progressive populist mobilization. They are won, when they are won, by candidates who can credibly represent the cultural identity of the district while delivering material wins. That is what popularism actually means in practice, and it is much closer to right than the authors allow.

I conclude with one concession. The popularist case has been overstated in some places—the Split Ticket WAR analysis the authors critique is genuinely flawed, and the certainty with which some popularists have presented their conclusions has been undeserved. But the operational program—run candidates who fit their districts, avoid the cultural posture of the national progressive movement when it conflicts with local conditions, be disciplined about national messaging—remains the only program that has any track record in the kinds of districts Democrats actually need to win to govern. The authors' alternative is theoretically interesting and operationally undertested. Until I see it work in PA-7, I will keep running the playbook that has at least sometimes worked there.

The Authors Are Too Generous to Electoral Politics

I find myself in the unusual position of agreeing with Bonica and Grumbach about almost every diagnostic point while disagreeing fundamentally with the implicit faith their essay places in electoral politics as the primary terrain on which democratic backsliding will be defeated or accommodated. The essay treats the question "how do Democrats win elections" as roughly equivalent to the question "how is democracy defended." This conflation is mistaken, and it is the most consequential mistake in the piece.

Consider the comparative cases of authoritarian consolidation and democratic resistance. Where authoritarian governments have been resisted successfully, electoral politics has been one important arena, but rarely the decisive one. Poland 2023 came after years of judicial resistance, EU institutional pressure, sustained protest mobilization (especially around abortion), and the building of civil society infrastructure that could survive electoral defeat. Brazil 2022 came after Bolsonaro had been weakened by pandemic catastrophe, judicial resistance, and a long civil-society effort that operated mostly outside formal politics. The Korean impeachment of Yoon required massive street mobilization within days of his martial law declaration—mobilization that had been organized over years by labor, religious, and civic networks.

Where authoritarian movements have consolidated, by contrast, the opposition often won occasional elections without being able to stop the broader institutional capture. Orbán's opponents have won occasional contests; the regime continues. Erdoğan's opponents have won occasional contests; the regime continues. Modi has lost seats; the regime continues. Why? Because authoritarian consolidation operates through capture of the judiciary, the regulatory state, the security services, the media, and increasingly the platforms through which civic life occurs. Defeating the consolidation requires resistance in all of these arenas, not just at the ballot box. An opposition that focuses primarily on winning the next election while ignoring these other arenas tends to lose them all.

Where is this in the authors' essay? Not entirely absent—they note "institutional coordination, civil society mobilization, and the political courage to name and confront the authoritarian threat"—but treated as a coda to what is fundamentally an essay about electoral strategy. The proportion of the essay devoted to congressional vote-share analysis, ideology measurement, and turnout statistics dwarfs the attention given to non-electoral resistance.

This matters because the operational implications are very different. If the question is "how do Democrats win 2026," then the answer involves messaging, candidate recruitment, turnout operations, and so on. If the question is "how is the slide into competitive authoritarianism arrested," then the answer involves a much wider set of activities: organizing within the federal civil service to maintain professional norms, building legal infrastructure that can sustain extensive litigation against executive overreach, developing alternative media platforms that can survive coordinated pressure, creating mutual-aid networks that can support targeted communities under official harassment, building international alliances with foreign governments and institutions that retain commitments to democratic norms, and preparing for the possibility of extra-electoral pressure including general strikes and mass mobilization.

The American left and center-left have invested much less in these capacities than the comparative cases suggest is necessary. The federal civil service entered 2025 unprepared for the systematic loyalty-test purges that followed. Major media institutions entered the Trump-2 era without robust plans for resisting subpoena pressure, FCC harassment, or owner capitulation. The progressive legal infrastructure (ACLU, Lawyers for Civil Rights, etc.) is well-developed but undermanned for the scale of the litigation actually being demanded. Civil-society organizations that could mobilize street resistance—labor unions, faith communities, immigrant-rights organizations—are mostly weaker than their counterparts in successful resistance cases.

I worry that an essay framed primarily as "what Democrats should do electorally" implicitly reinforces the idea that electoral victory in 2026 or 2028 will reverse the consolidation. It will not, on its own. Even a sweeping Democratic victory in 2026 would face a Trump administration that has spent two years installing loyalists across the executive branch, packing the federal judiciary, capturing regulatory bodies, and intimidating civil society. Reversing this would require sustained action across many fronts, of which electoral politics is only one.

What would a more comprehensive framing look like? It would treat the next two years as a period of resistance and rebuilding rather than as a campaign cycle. It would prioritize building durable institutional capacities that can survive multiple electoral cycles. It would recognize that the most important political work is often the work that does not show up in vote totals: the organizing that sustains communities, the legal work that draws lines the regime cannot cross without enormous cost, the documentation work that preserves the historical record of what is being done. It would, frankly, take seriously the possibility that the 2028 election may not be free and fair in the ways previous American elections have been, and prepare for that contingency as well as for the contingency that it will be.

The authors' anti-corruption frame is one piece of this. Anti-corruption framing can do important work in delegitimizing an authoritarian regime—witness its role in the Brazilian and Polish cases, both as motivator of supporters and as a basis for international pressure. But anti-corruption framing as an electoral strategy is much narrower than anti-corruption work as a multi-front resistance program. The latter includes investigative journalism, prosecutorial action, civil society documentation, international pressure (anti-money-laundering sanctions, for example), and academic and professional accountability of officials who participate in abuses.

I do not mean to dismiss the authors' contribution. The empirical demolition of popularist confidence is valuable, and the gestures toward broader resistance are welcome. But I would urge readers to take the broader gesture more seriously than the electoral analysis. The defense of democracy is not coextensive with the strategy of the Democratic Party. It is a wider project, and it will require capacities that no electoral strategy by itself can produce.

The Frame Is the Problem, Not the Solution

Bonica and Grumbach end their essay with a recommendation that Democrats reorient their politics around anti-corruption. I want to argue that this recommendation, however well-intentioned, repeats in different language the same error they spend most of the essay diagnosing: it treats a frame as if it could substitute for substantive coalition-building, and it underestimates how the frame would be received and translated in the actual information environment.

Consider what "anti-corruption" actually does as a political frame. It identifies an enemy (corrupt insiders), a victim (the people), and a remedy (root out the corruption). This is a populist structure, and populism is genuinely powerful as a mobilizing form. But the structure does not specify who counts as a corrupt insider, and the answer is supplied by the broader media environment rather than by the candidate who deploys the frame. In contemporary American politics, the answer that the right-wing media ecosystem has been training voters to give for ten years is: the Democratic Party, the federal civil service, professional journalists, university faculty, and so on. The "corrupt insiders" are the credentialed institutions Democrats are aligned with.

This means that when a Democrat takes up the anti-corruption frame, they are operating on terrain that has already been shaped by their opponents. A Democratic senator who calls for banning congressional stock trading is met with: "What about Pelosi? What about Hunter Biden? What about the Clinton Foundation?" The right-wing response weaponizes the same frame more effectively than the Democrat can wield it, because the frame's content—who counts as corrupt—has been pre-loaded with right-wing answers.

The authors' best counter-example is Mamdani in New York City, whose campaign successfully ran against real-estate insider corruption. But Mamdani succeeded because (a) the real-estate industry in New York is a concrete, locally hated villain with specific identifiable actors, and (b) Mamdani himself was credible as an outsider, with a biography that did not invite countercharges of insider corruption. Neither condition generalizes well to most Democratic candidates. Most Democratic senators do not have a specific local-villain industry to run against, and most have biographies that look like exactly the kind of credentialed insider profile the right-wing media has been training voters to distrust.

There is also a deeper problem with anti-corruption populism as a defense of democracy. Anti-corruption frames, in their pure populist form, are anti-institutional. They treat existing institutions as captured by corrupt elites and demand their cleansing or replacement. This is the same logical structure that authoritarian movements use to delegitimize the institutional constraints on their power. Trump's case against the FBI, the Department of Justice, the intelligence community, the federal civil service, and the courts has been an anti-corruption case throughout: these institutions are corrupt, captured by his enemies, and need to be cleansed or replaced. The anti-corruption frame is the operating logic of the authoritarian movement, not its antithesis.

Democrats who try to compete on this terrain are competing on terms set by their opponents. Worse, they risk further delegitimizing the institutional constraints that are, at the moment, the actual line of defense against further consolidation. If the Democratic posture in 2026 is "the whole system is corrupt and needs to be torn down," they are making the case the regime needs them to make. The pro-democratic case in an authoritarian moment is, awkwardly, often an institutional case: the courts, the civil service, the independent agencies, the constitutional order. Anti-corruption populism cuts against this case.

The successful authoritarian-resistance movements abroad have largely understood this. Tusk did not run on tearing down Polish institutions; he ran on rebuilding them, restoring judicial independence, recovering EU funding, rejoining the European mainstream. The Brazilian opposition to Bolsonaro emphasized institutional restoration, not anti-corruption populism. The Korean response to Yoon was a strict institutional response—impeachment, constitutional court review—not a populist anti-corruption mobilization. The comparative record favors pro-institutional democratic restoration, not populist anti-corruption mobilization.

What about the polling that finds Americans worried about corruption? The authors cite this as evidence of an opening. But polling on abstract concerns like "corruption" or "the system being rigged" tells us very little about what voters will actually respond to in a campaign. Voters tell pollsters they are worried about corruption because it is an unobjectionable thing to be worried about. The same voters, asked about specific cases, divide along partisan lines about who the corrupt actors are. The polling does not establish an actionable opening; it establishes a vague valence that both parties will compete to claim, and that the better-resourced and more disciplined party (currently the right) will probably claim more effectively.

What would I recommend instead? Not popularist moderation as conventionally understood, but a different kind of moderation: an explicit pro-institutional, pro-rule-of-law, pro-competence framing that defends specific institutions against specific attacks and demonstrates specific results from competent governance. This is not exciting. It does not mobilize the activist base. But it has the advantage of being aligned with the actual defensive posture democracy requires, and of being credible from the kinds of candidates Democrats actually field.

This framing has costs. It cedes the populist register to the right. It requires the party to defend institutions that many of its own voters are skeptical of. It does not produce the moral clarity the authors rightly note is missing. But it has one decisive virtue: it is the frame under which the institutional constraints on authoritarianism can actually operate. Anti-corruption populism delegitimizes those constraints; pro-institutional defense reinforces them.

The authors close their essay by warning that history will judge Democrats on whether they fought hard enough. I agree. But fighting hard does not require fighting on the terrain the opponent has prepared. It can require, instead, fighting on the terrain the opponent has tried to discredit—the boring, institutional, rule-of-law terrain that is the actual battlefield of democratic survival. This is unglamorous, and it will not win any messaging awards. It may, however, be the only fight worth winning.

What the Authors Get Right

Most of the responses to Bonica and Grumbach's essay will, I expect, focus on what it gets wrong. I want to focus on what it gets right, because I think the central argument has been more dismissed than refuted in the early reactions, and the dismissals do not engage with what the authors have actually shown.

The central argument is this: the empirical foundation on which popularism rests—that strategic moderation produces consistent electoral gains—has eroded in the Trump era, to the point where its boosters are increasingly relying on opaque proprietary metrics and selection-biased comparisons to sustain a claim that the more transparent academic literature does not support.

This is a significant claim, and the authors marshal substantial evidence for it. The closely-contested-primaries design is among the cleanest causal designs available in observational political science, and its null finding for ideology effects in general elections is hard to wave away. The breakdown of the Split Ticket WAR metric is a real contribution; the demonstration that less than a third of WAR scores reflect actual candidate overperformance is methodologically devastating to the most-cited popularist data source. The replication failures of the Split Ticket model across multiple reasonable alternative specifications confirm that the headline number is fragile.

These findings deserve to be confronted directly by anyone defending popularism. The honest popularist response would be: yes, the magnitude of the moderation effect has been overstated; yes, the proprietary models have not been adequately scrutinized; yes, the academic literature now finds smaller and more context-dependent effects than the consultants have claimed; but here is why we still think moderate positioning has a meaningful effect in the specific cases that matter. I have not seen this response made carefully. What I have seen instead is dismissal of the academic findings as "too complex" or assertions that the case for moderation is intuitive enough not to require empirical support. Neither response is satisfying.

The authors are also right about Manchin. The Manchin example has been so overworked in popularist commentary that it deserves the specific deflation the authors give it. Manchin won because Manchin was a uniquely embedded political brand in West Virginia, built over decades of state-level office and personal relationships. He is not a generalizable case for moderation; he is a generalizable case for incumbency, personal brand, and biographical fit. The Elliott loss in 2024 confirms this directly: a generic moderate replacement in the same state, in the same year, lost by forty points. The lesson is not "moderation does not work in West Virginia"; it is "Manchin does not generalize."

The authors are also right that the median voter theorem, as commonly invoked in political strategy commentary, no longer fits American political reality. The theorem assumes single-dimensional ideological space, voters who evaluate candidates on policy positions, low partisan attachment, and elections that respond elastically to candidate positioning. None of these assumptions describes contemporary American electoral politics. The theorem has not been definitively refuted in formal political theory; it remains useful in some applications. But its application as a strategic-political prescription—"move to the center, win the median voter"—is obsolete.

The authors are also right that the turnout question deserves more weight than popularist commentary has given it. In a nationalized, polarized electoral environment where vote choice is largely determined by partisan identification, the binding constraint on electoral success is often turnout rather than persuasion. Democrats have a documented turnout deficit, and the magnitude of this deficit in 2024 was decisive in key states. Closing it requires strategies that popularist commentary has tended to under-emphasize: investing in field organizations, year-round mobilization in low-engagement communities, and substantive promises that give marginal voters reason to participate.

The authors are also right about the corruption opportunity, though I disagreed with elements of their treatment in another response I drafted. The general point—that anti-corruption framing has been a powerful axis of political mobilization throughout democratic history, that the current American public is unusually receptive to it, and that the Democratic Party has not fully exploited the available terrain—is correct, even if the implementation is more complicated than the essay allows.

What follows from accepting these points? Several things.

First, the popularist intellectual coalition needs to update. The most-cited popularist sources—Yglesias, Klein, Teixeira, the Split Ticket analysts—have been working from analyses whose empirical foundations are weaker than they have publicly acknowledged. Their prescriptions may still be partially right (I think some of them are), but the certainty with which they have been delivered is unjustified. A more humble popularist program would acknowledge that the moderation premium has shrunk substantially, that the cases where it remains operative are narrower than previously thought, and that complementary strategies—including base mobilization—are necessary even within a popularist framework.

Second, the popularist political coalition—the Democratic consultants, donors, and elected officials who have organized around this strategy—needs to confront the gap between the public confidence of their intellectual leadership and the actual state of the empirical case. This is uncomfortable. The 2024 election was, in many respects, a clean test of the popularist program; the Harris campaign implemented something close to a textbook popularist strategy, and the result was a Democratic loss across the board. The popularist response to this has mostly been to argue that the moderation was insufficient, not to consider that the framework itself is failing. This is the move that I find least defensible in current commentary.

Third, alternatives deserve more serious consideration than they have received. This does not mean the authors' anti-corruption populism is the right alternative. It means that experimentation is warranted, that different candidates should be running different strategies in different districts, and that no single approach should be presented as the demonstrated path forward.

I have my own disagreements with the authors, which I have aired elsewhere. But I want to say plainly that the empirical core of their argument is stronger than its critics have generally allowed, and that the popularist response has so far been more rhetorical than substantive. The next phase of this debate should engage with the evidence rather than asserting authority over it. If the popularist case can be reformulated to take the evidence seriously, it would be a stronger position. If it cannot, then it deserves to lose the argument.

The Race-Turnout Problem Is Larger Than the Essay Allows

Bonica and Grumbach note that the 2024 Black-white turnout gap of 10.9 percentage points was the widest in over three decades, and that Black turnout dropped more sharply than white turnout. They treat this as evidence for their broader mobilization argument. I want to argue that the racial dimensions of the turnout collapse deserve closer attention than they receive in the essay, and that the standard mobilization framework—on either popularist or progressive-populist versions—does not adequately address what happened.

What actually happened in 2024 among Black voters is more complicated than "Democrats failed to mobilize." Black turnout did fall, particularly among Black men, and particularly among younger Black voters. But the Democrats' Black vote share also declined; Trump improved his share among Black voters from roughly 8 percent in 2020 to roughly 13 percent in 2024, with larger gains among Black men under fifty. The combination of lower turnout and lower vote share among Black voters means that the standard mobilization story—"if we had just turned out our base, we would have won"—is incomplete. The base itself is changing.

This is part of a broader pattern of dealignment among voters of color. Latino voters have shifted toward Republicans across multiple cycles, with particularly large gains for Trump in 2020 and 2024. Asian American voters have shifted somewhat. Black voters, the most durable Democratic constituency for fifty years, are beginning to dealign at the margins. These shifts are concentrated among working-class voters of color, particularly men, and they are happening even in places where Democratic field operations are well-developed.

The authors' framework has trouble with this because it treats Democratic-base voters as a stable demographic that simply needs better mobilization. The "mobilize the base" theory works if the base is what it was in 2008 or 2012. It does not work if the base is itself unstable, with significant portions drifting toward the opposing party. Higher turnout among Latino men or Black men in 2024 would not necessarily have produced more Democratic votes; depending on the specific marginal voter, it might have produced more Trump votes.

What is driving the dealignment? Several factors that the standard mobilization framework does not capture. First, the perception that the Democratic Party has become the party of the credentialed and white-collar professional class, rather than of working-class people of color. This perception is partially accurate—the party's voter coalition has shifted in this direction even as its policy commitments have shifted left—and it does not respond well to mobilization messaging if mobilization is delivered by exactly the credentialed professionals voters are distrustful of.

Second, the cultural cleavages that have opened up over the last decade. Many working-class voters of color hold socially conservative views on immigration, crime, gender, and education that put them in tension with the Democratic Party's contemporary cultural positioning. These voters have not moved on economic questions; they have moved because the party has moved on cultural questions, in ways that read as foreign to their communities. This is precisely what the popularist critique of progressive cultural posture has been arguing, and it is a real phenomenon that should not be dismissed.

Third, the specific failure of the Democratic Party to deliver on its 2020 promises to Black voters. The George Floyd policy agenda largely did not happen; the John Lewis Voting Rights Act failed; the police reform agenda failed. Whether or not one supports the original agenda, the failure to deliver on it produced cynicism among the voters who had been promised it. Cynicism reduces turnout, and it also reduces the marginal commitment of voters who do turn out.

What does this mean for the authors' framework? It means that the mobilization theory needs to grapple with the question of which mobilization. Mobilizing the 2008 base is not the same as mobilizing the 2024 base. The marginal Black voter who did not turn out in 2024 is not the same person as the marginal Black voter who turned out for Obama in 2008. Demographic categories are not stable political categories.

It also means that the anti-corruption frame the authors propose is a more uncertain bet with these specific voters. Black and Latino working-class voters who have been drifting away from the Democratic Party have not generally given "the party isn't fighting corruption hard enough" as the reason. They have given reasons related to economics (the cost of living, immigration's effects on wages, housing costs), to cultural distance (the party feeling foreign to their communities), and to specific policy failures (crime, schools, broken promises on policing). An anti-corruption pivot would not obviously speak to these grievances; it might speak past them.

What would speak to them? My honest answer is: I am not sure, and I do not think anyone is sure. But the elements that seem most promising are substantive policy commitments on cost-of-living issues, an explicit pivot away from the cultural posture of the credentialed left on a small set of high-salience issues, and candidate recruitment that puts authentic working-class voices of color at the center of the party's identity. This is closer to popularism than to populism, although it is a populism-flavored popularism that is willing to break with donor preferences in ways the established popularists have often not been willing to.

I will offer one more observation. The Democratic Party has, over the past few cycles, increasingly relied on a coalition of white college-educated voters and voters of color. This coalition is internally tense, because the cultural priors of the white college-educated wing are not the cultural priors of most working-class voters of color. The popularist case has often been about preserving the coalition by being more disciplined about the cultural posture of the credentialed wing. The progressive case has often been about preserving the coalition by being more militant about racial-justice politics that activists believe will mobilize voters of color. Neither has worked. The coalition is fracturing because the underlying alignment is unstable.

The most honest version of this analysis would be that the Democratic Party may need to choose. It can be the party of the credentialed left, which means accepting smaller margins among working-class voters of color but possibly more durable support among educated suburbanites. Or it can be the party of working-class voters across racial lines, which means substantial cultural realignment that the educated wing will hate. The authors' essay implies that a synthesis is possible through anti-corruption mobilization; I am not sure the synthesis exists.

The race-turnout question is, ultimately, the most important political question of the next decade. It deserves more careful treatment than either the popularist or the populist frameworks have given it.

The Authoritarianism Frame Is Doing Too Much Work

I want to interrogate something in Bonica and Grumbach's essay that has gone largely unexamined: the framing of the moment as an existential battle against authoritarianism, and the strategic implications they draw from that framing. The framing is not obviously wrong, but it is not obviously right either, and the strategic conclusions depend heavily on whether one accepts it.

The authors treat the authoritarianism diagnosis as settled. They cite Brownstein's distinction between those who see Trump as a distraction from kitchen-table issues and those who see authoritarianism as the existential crisis defining all other questions, and they place themselves squarely in the second camp. From this premise, they argue that the moment requires "mobilization, not positioning... purpose, not just messaging." The scale of the threat demands a scale of response that conventional electoral strategy cannot provide.

This is a coherent position, and it may well be correct. But it deserves more interrogation than the essay provides. What does it mean to describe the current American moment as "authoritarian"? The Trump administration has done genuinely concerning things: politicized prosecutions, civil-service purges, attacks on independent agencies, intimidation of media and law firms, deployment of federal force against domestic political opponents. These are the predicates for democratic backsliding. They are also, however, not yet at the level of consolidated authoritarianism in the comparative sense. Elections are still being held; opposition parties still operate; media still publish critical coverage; courts still issue adverse rulings. The current state is somewhere between healthy democracy and consolidated authoritarianism, in a gray zone that political scientists have variously called "competitive authoritarianism," "hybrid regimes," or simply democratic decline.

Why does this matter for strategy? Because different points on this spectrum imply different strategic responses. If the regime is on the path to consolidated authoritarianism, then the authors' argument for emergency mobilization and movement politics has substantial force; conventional electoral strategy in a closing system has well-known limits. But if the regime is in a contested period where conventional electoral politics still has real purchase—which the 2026 midterms will likely demonstrate one way or the other—then the case for tearing up the existing playbook is weaker.

The authors implicitly assume the more pessimistic scenario. This may be empirically correct. But the practical implication is that they are recommending high-risk strategic departures (anti-corruption populism, abandonment of moderation, base-mobilization focus) on the basis of a diagnosis that is not yet fully confirmed. If the diagnosis is right, the high-risk strategy may be necessary. If the diagnosis is wrong, the high-risk strategy may produce worse outcomes than the conventional approach would have produced.

I want to think about the asymmetric costs here. Suppose the authoritarianism diagnosis is correct and Democrats adopt the conventional popularist approach. The result is plausibly that Democrats win some elections, lose others, and the regime continues to consolidate beneath the level of electoral politics. The conventional approach proves inadequate to the actual threat, and democracy slides further. This is the scenario the authors warn about, and it is genuinely concerning.

Suppose the authoritarianism diagnosis is overstated and Democrats adopt the populist mobilization approach. The result is plausibly that Democrats lose elections they could have won with a more disciplined posture, polarize the electorate in ways that benefit Trump's successors, and find themselves out of power for longer than they otherwise would have been. The result is worse outcomes on the issues the activist base cares about, more years of conservative governance, and no enhanced defense of democratic norms because no defense was needed.

The asymmetric-costs argument cuts both ways depending on one's diagnosis. Under the high-threat diagnosis, the conventional approach is reckless. Under the lower-threat diagnosis, the populist approach is reckless. The strategic question reduces, in important ways, to the empirical question of how to characterize the current threat level.

I want to suggest that the threat level is genuinely uncertain, and that strategic prescriptions that depend heavily on resolving this uncertainty are over-confident. The authors' essay treats the high-threat diagnosis as a premise. But a careful reader should notice that the diagnosis is doing significant work in the argument, and that alternative diagnoses are tenable.

Consider three plausible scenarios for the next two years. In the first, the Trump administration continues its aggressive consolidation but is checked by courts, civil society, and electoral pressure, with the result that the 2028 election occurs under conditions similar to 2024—competitive, contested, but recognizably democratic. In the second, the administration successfully consolidates control over key institutions, with the result that the 2028 election occurs under conditions of substantially compromised democracy—a competitive-authoritarian environment in which incumbent advantages have grown significantly. In the third, the administration overreaches, faces sustained domestic and international backlash, and either pulls back or is constrained by internal Republican defections, with the result that the 2026 midterms operate as a strong corrective.

Each of these scenarios implies a different optimal Democratic strategy. The first calls for something like the popularist program with strong civil-society backing. The second calls for emergency politics of the kind the authors recommend, plus extensive non-electoral resistance. The third calls for disciplined opposition that lets Republican overreach do the political work.

I do not know which scenario is most likely. I suspect the authors do not know either, despite the confidence of their argument. The honest strategic posture is one of contingency planning: prepare for the multiple scenarios, maintain capacities that work across more than one, and avoid making large strategic bets that pay off only in a single scenario.

The authors' anti-corruption populism is, in my view, a large strategic bet that pays off primarily in the second scenario—the genuine-consolidation scenario—and may be counterproductive in the first and third. If the regime is being constrained by ordinary political pressure, then a more disciplined opposition that lets Republican failures speak for themselves may produce better electoral outcomes than aggressive populist mobilization. If the regime is consolidating, then populist mobilization is necessary but probably not sufficient.

The thoughtful version of the authors' argument would acknowledge this contingency. It would say: under the worst plausible scenarios, the conventional playbook is inadequate, and Democrats need to develop capacities that work in those scenarios even if they have not yet been forced upon us. This is a reasonable position. The essay as written takes a stronger position—that the worst scenario is essentially our current reality—and that stronger position is less defensible than the contingent version would have been.

History will judge Democrats on how they responded to actually occurring events, not on whether they correctly anticipated the worst plausible scenario. Strategic flexibility, capacity-building across multiple contingencies, and willingness to update as the threat becomes clearer are all virtues. Strategic certainty about which scenario we are in is the failure mode the authors most need to avoid, even though their argument is structured around it.

A Modest Defense of Doing Nothing in Particular

After reading the various confident strategic prescriptions on offer in this debate—the popularist confidence that moderation wins, the populist confidence that anti-corruption mobilization wins, the proceduralist confidence that institutional restoration wins, the realignment-class confidence that economic populism wins—I find myself wanting to defend a position no one else is defending. The position is: nobody knows what works, the best evidence suggests that strategic positioning has small effects relative to fundamentals and candidate quality, and the most reliable path forward is probably to do the boring unglamorous work of running good candidates locally and waiting for the political environment to shift.

This is not a satisfying position. It does not feel commensurate with the stakes. It produces no memorable slogans. It is exactly the kind of advice that gets you uninvited from Democratic strategy podcasts. But I think it is closer to true than any of the more ambitious alternatives, and I want to make the case for it briefly.

Start with what we actually know about election outcomes. The single best predictor of vote shares in American elections, over the past several decades, is the partisan composition of the electorate combined with national conditions, particularly economic conditions and presidential approval. Candidate-specific factors—ideology, message, biography, personal characteristics—matter at the margin, but they are dwarfed by fundamentals in most cases. This is what the authors' own evidence implies when they note that AOC and Jared Golden "rise and fall with the same partisan tides."

If this is true, then the strategic value of choosing the right frame, the right positioning, or the right messaging is genuinely small relative to the strategic value of waiting for fundamentals to favor your party. Democrats lost in 2024 not primarily because of moderation, not primarily because of insufficient populism, not primarily because of corruption tolerance, but because they were the incumbent party during a period of high inflation following a global pandemic. The same Democrats with the same strategy would likely have won in 2018 or 2020 conditions, and will likely win again when conditions next favor them. The strategic question is mostly: what is the variance you can capture beyond the fundamentals? And the honest answer is: not much.

This implies that the major strategy debates of the moment—popularist versus populist, moderate versus progressive, anti-corruption versus institutionalist—are arguments about a small slice of variance, dressed up as arguments about the entire game. Both sides invest the debate with stakes that the evidence does not actually support. The differences in expected outcomes between optimal popularist and optimal populist strategies are probably on the order of one to two percentage points in any given race, with substantial variance across contexts. Sometimes the gap is bigger; sometimes the gap is zero; in many cases, the gap is so small relative to other factors that it is empirically indistinguishable from noise.

What does this imply for action? Several things.

First, lower the temperature. Strategic disagreements between popularists and populists are not the existential questions they are sometimes presented as. Both factions are arguing over relatively small effects. Treating these arguments as if they were determinative of democratic survival is misleading and probably damaging to the broader coalition.

Second, focus on what does have larger effects. Candidate quality, biographical fit, communication ability, organizational competence, and incumbent advantage produce larger effects than ideological positioning. The party should invest more in recruiting and developing strong candidates and less in arguing about what those candidates should say. Mamdani won not because of his ideology but because he was a uniquely talented communicator running an unusually well-organized campaign in a favorable context. The lesson is not "be more like Mamdani's ideology" but "develop more candidates with his level of skill."

Third, accept that the political environment will shift. Trump's coalition has internal contradictions that will produce political costs as his administration governs. The realignment that has hurt Democrats with working-class voters of color is not necessarily permanent; coalitions shift, and the next two years of Republican governance will likely produce new opportunities for Democrats among constituencies the right has not actually served. Patience is a strategic virtue.

Fourth, do the structural work. The biggest gains available to Democrats over the next decade are probably not in messaging or positioning but in structural reforms that change the playing field: voting rights, anti-gerrymandering, ending the filibuster when possible, reforming the Senate's apportionment to the extent the Constitution allows. These pay dividends across multiple cycles and many candidates, regardless of ideological positioning.

Fifth, build the capacities the authors gesture at without overcommitting to their specific framing. Civil society infrastructure, legal infrastructure, media infrastructure, organizational infrastructure—these are valuable across many strategic scenarios. They are also expensive and unglamorous, which is why they have been underinvested in for decades.

The authors' essay is right that the popularist program has been oversold and that the moderation premium has shrunk. It is wrong, in my view, to substitute a different overconfident strategic prescription in its place. The honest message is that the levers strategists have available are smaller than they claim, that the most important variables are fundamentals and candidate quality rather than positioning, and that the best Democratic response to the current moment is probably a combination of patient capacity-building and disciplined opposition.

This is admittedly an unsatisfying place to end. The moment feels like it demands something more dramatic. But political strategy is in significant part the work of resisting the temptation to do something dramatic when something disciplined would serve better. Democrats have made plenty of expensive strategic mistakes by overlearning the lessons of recent defeats. The popularist program is one such overlearning. An anti-corruption populist pivot would likely be another.

What I would say to anyone who feels the urgency of the moment and wants to act: act in the arenas where the levers are largest. Run for office. Volunteer for campaigns. Support local journalism. Join an organization that does sustained civil-society work in your community. Donate to legal defense funds. None of this is strategy in the high-flown sense the authors invoke, and none of it produces the kind of dramatic narrative arc that strategic essays demand. It is the actual work of democratic maintenance, which is what defending democracy mostly consists of, year over year, decade over decade.

The authors and the popularists are both convinced that they have identified the strategic key to the current moment. I think they are both wrong. I think there is no strategic key. There is only the work, and the patience, and the willingness to keep doing the work when no single act feels commensurate with the threat. That is what democratic citizenship is, in normal times and in difficult ones. It is enough.

Once the street protests end and a new administration takes office, the energy dissipates unless the movement delivers tangible improvements in people’s lives—wages, housing, healthcare, dignity. The risk is that an anti-corruption platform without a strong economic core will sound to many like one more elite promise—Democrats wagging their fingers about Supreme Court ethics while the party’s own ties to Wall Street and Silicon Valley remain intact. Bonica and Grumbach offer a welcome antidote to overconfident punditry, but their empirical claims should be read with more caution than their rhetoric allows. The case for moderation is often sloppy; the case against moderation can become sloppy in the opposite direction. Candidate ideology may have smaller effects than it once did, and nationalization certainly constrains local differentiation. Start with close-primary designs. Comparing general-election outcomes after narrowly decided primaries is one of the better tools political scientists have for estimating causal effects. If a moderate barely beats a progressive, and a progressive barely beats a moderate elsewhere, the winners may be treated as almost randomly assigned. That is elegant. But the design estimates a local effect: the effect of nominating the kind of moderate or progressive who appears in a close primary, in the kinds of districts where such primaries occur, during the periods studied. It does not necessarily tell us what would happen if a national party became strongly associated with positions voters dislike, or if presidential candidates adopted highly salient stances on immigration, crime, or cultural issues.

Measurement is also difficult. Ideology is not one thing. Roll-call votes, donor networks, campaign websites, interest-group endorsements, expert ratings, and voter perceptions each capture different dimensions. A candidate may be economically left and culturally moderate, or vice versa. A candidate may hold progressive positions but communicate them in nonthreatening language. Another may be moderate on paper but symbolically tied to unpopular national groups. If ideology is measured imprecisely, estimated effects will shrink. The absence of large measurable effects may reflect both a real decline and the limits of our instruments.

The authors criticize Split Ticket’s WAR model for opacity and excessive certainty. Fair enough. Proprietary political metrics should not be treated as scientific authority. But the same humility should apply to academic models. Transparent uncertainty is better than hidden assumptions, but it remains uncertainty. Elections are low-N, high-noise events influenced by incumbency, fundraising, district partisanship, national mood, candidate quality, scandals, turnout, advertising, local media, and random shocks. The effect of moderation may be small relative to these factors and still decisive in close races.

Consider the statement that of twenty-two Democratic incumbents who lost competitive seats between 2016 and 2024, twenty-one were moderates. This sounds damning, but it may mostly reflect selection. Democrats tend to nominate moderates in the most competitive or Republican-leaning districts. Progressives more often represent safer blue seats. If the battlefield is populated by moderates, most casualties will be moderates. The authors know this and offer more sophisticated evidence elsewhere, but rhetorically the statistic invites a misleading inference.

A national nominee is not merely one candidate among many; she defines the party brand. Harris’s 2024 campaign did attempt moderation, but voters evaluated her through years of prior party positioning, the Biden administration’s record, inflation, immigration pressures, foreign crises, and her own 2019 primary statements. A late pivot to kitchen-table rhetoric does not constitute a clean test of popularism. Conversely, a bold position on a low-salience or cross-cutting issue may help by signaling conviction. The authors correctly note that candidate ideology matters less than it once did and that elections are nationalized referendums. But they do not fully explore why this is true. The answer lies in the transformation of American media. When voters consumed local newspapers and local television, they encountered candidate-specific information. Now they consume national media—cable news, social media, podcasts—that frames everything through a national lens.

If Democrats can shift the national narrative in their direction, down-ballot candidates benefit automatically.

This suggests that the strategic emphasis on candidate positioning—whether moderate or progressive—is somewhat misplaced. The real leverage point is the national narrative. Which party is seen as representing the future? Which party is seen as having solutions to the problems voters care about? The anti-corruption frame the authors propose is promising precisely because it can travel across media environments. Corruption is a compelling narrative that works on cable news, social media, and conversation alike. It offers a villain (the corrupt insider) and a hero (the reformer) that voters can immediately understand.

This means investing in surrogates, commentators, and messengers who can amplify the chosen frame. It means disciplined messaging that does not allow the opposition to set terms.

The authors are right that experimentation is needed. I would add: experimentation at the narrative level, not just the policy level. Democrats have spent too much time debating which policies to emphasize and too little time building the capacity to make a narrative dominant.

The next best approach is to compare similar candidates with similar resources across many elections. The authors do this with their presidential-coattail analysis and find small effects. I am not defending the strong version of the moderation thesis, which the authors correctly reject. I am suggesting that the evidence is more ambiguous than the confident tone suggests.
