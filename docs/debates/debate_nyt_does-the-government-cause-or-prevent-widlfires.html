Has the government played a role in increasing the risk of these fires? Can government policies prevent them?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

For most of the twentieth century, the U.S. Forest Service operated under the "10 a.m. policy," which committed it to extinguishing every fire by the morning after detection. That doctrine, born of the catastrophic 1910 "Big Burn," made sense as a property-protection measure but ignored the ecological reality that ponderosa pine, lodgepole, and chaparral forests evolved with regular, often low-intensity fire. A century of suppression has produced what fire ecologists like Stephen Pyne have long warned about: dense stands of small-diameter trees, ladder fuels reaching into the canopy, and thick litter on the forest floor. When ignition does come — from lightning, a downed power line, or a careless camper — the fire that results behaves nothing like the historical norm. It crowns, races, and resists suppression. In that sense, yes, federal policy has helped cause the megafires now consuming Colorado.

But blaming "the government" stops the analysis too early. Congress has repeatedly underfunded the hazardous-fuels reduction and prescribed-burn programs that agencies themselves recommend, while simultaneously demanding aggressive suppression once fires start — a fiscal structure that guarantees the worst of both worlds. The 2009 FLAME Act tried to fix the budget-borrowing problem, but the Forest Service still spends roughly half its budget fighting fires, leaving thinning projects perennially behind schedule. Meanwhile, local governments have permitted explosive growth in the wildland-urban interface; the Colorado Springs subdivisions consumed by the Waldo Canyon fire sit in country that has always burned.

Can policy prevent the next disaster? Not entirely — especially if the climate projections about hotter, drier Western summers prove correct. But government can substantially reduce the damage. That means funding mechanical thinning and prescribed fire at the scale the landscape actually requires, letting more remote backcountry fires burn under monitoring rather than throwing crews at every smoke, pricing federal fire protection into insurance and building codes for homes in the interface, and requiring defensible space and ignition-resistant roofing as a condition of that protection. The choice is not whether to pay for fire, but whether to pay in advance or in ashes.

The relationship between government and wildfire is paradoxical: federal policy helped create today’s megafires, yet only government can marshal the resources to mitigate them. Since the 1910 Big Burn, the U.S. Forest Service aggressively suppressed all fires—a stance solidified by Smokey Bear. This succeeded in the short term but turned many Western forests into tinderboxes. Forests that once burned lightly now carry a century’s fuel load. In that sense, government inadvertently caused the explosive fire behavior seen in Colorado and across the West.

Government also enables risk through land-use decisions. Permitting homes in fire-prone wildland-urban interface areas without mandatory defensible space and fire-resistant building codes puts property and lives at ever greater risk. Subsidized insurance and disaster relief further mask the true cost of building in hazardous terrain, encouraging more development where natural fires would once have burned.

Yet government is also the chief actor in prevention. Federal and state agencies thin forests, conduct prescribed burns, and fund community wildfire protection plans. The Firewise program teaches homeowners to create defensible space. These efforts are straitjacketed by tight budgets, air-quality rules that limit burn windows, and a public conditioned by decades of suppression to fear any flame. The recent heat-wave study heightens the urgency: climate change is loading the dice for longer fire seasons and more extreme burning conditions. Government can be neither wholly blamed nor absolved; its policies built the fuel ladder, and now it must help dismantle it.

Government both increases and reduces wildfire risk, but not in the simple way the question is often framed. Fire is not an alien invader in the West; it is part of many Western ecosystems. The mistake has been treating every fire as a disaster to be extinguished immediately, especially in forests that historically burned often at low intensity. A century of aggressive suppression, symbolized by Smokey Bear and backed by federal land agencies, has left many dry forests with dense small trees and heavy fuels. When drought, heat and wind arrive, those fuels can help turn a natural fire into a severe one.

But it would be wrong to blame government alone. Climate conditions matter enormously, and the recent heat and drought across the Rockies are exactly the kind of stress scientists have warned will become more likely as the planet warms. People also start many fires, and more people now live in fire-prone foothills and forests. Local zoning decisions, subsidized roads and utilities, and the expectation that public firefighters will defend every house have encouraged building in the wildland-urban interface. That makes fires more expensive and more dangerous, even when the landscape itself is not unusually flammable.

Government policy can help, though it cannot “prevent” wildfire in the absolute sense. The smarter goal is to prevent catastrophic losses. That means more prescribed burning and carefully targeted thinning near communities and in forests adapted to frequent fire. It means allowing some remote fires to burn under safe conditions. It also means enforcing defensible-space rules, requiring fire-resistant building materials, and making development decisions reflect real fire risk.

There is no single villain and no single cure. More logging will not solve climate-driven fire weather, and more suppression will only postpone the problem. The best public policy would accept fire as inevitable, reduce accumulated fuels where science supports it, stop encouraging reckless development, and address the warming trend that is loading the dice toward longer, hotter fire seasons.

The relationship between government policy and wildfire risk is complex, but the evidence through mid-2012 strongly suggests that federal land management practices have contributed significantly to the fuel accumulation that makes modern wildfires so severe—while also acknowledging that government programs have reduced risks in important ways.

For much of the twentieth century, the U.S. Forest Service pursued aggressive fire suppression, a policy rooted in the false assumption that all fires should be extinguished. This approach allowed forests to become unnaturally dense, leaving enormous quantities of dead fuels that now feed catastrophic burns. The 1995 Federal Fire Policy acknowledged this mistake, but implementation has been slow. Budget pressures have compounded the problem: by 2012, fire suppression was consuming an ever-larger share of agency budgets, leaving fewer resources for prescribed burns and forest thinning. The result is a growing backlog of hazardous fuels on federal lands.

At the same time, government does prevent wildfires. National firefighting capacity has grown more sophisticated, and coordination among agencies has improved. Building codes, public education campaigns, and residential fire mitigation programs in fire-prone areas have reduced losses.

The most defensible answer is that government policy contributed to today's fire risk through decades of well-intentioned but ultimately harmful suppression, while simultaneously working to reduce that risk through prevention programs. The challenge is that prevention remains chronically underfunded while suppression demands ever-greater resources—a structural problem that explains why summers like 2012 continue to devastate the West. The policy prescription is clear: redirect resources toward forest management before fires occur, rather than simply fighting the consequences.

For nearly a century, the federal government has operated under what amounts to a war on fire. The Forest Service's infamous "10 a.m. policy," instituted after the catastrophic 1910 Big Burn, required that every wildfire be suppressed by the morning after its discovery. Smokey Bear taught generations of Americans that all fire is bad. The result has been an ecological catastrophe in slow motion.

Ponderosa pine forests of the interior West evolved with frequent, low-intensity ground fires that cleared underbrush every 5 to 25 years. By suppressing those fires, the government allowed fuel loads to accumulate to levels never seen in the historical record. Dense thickets of small trees now choke forests that once held widely spaced old-growth giants. When fires ignite today—and they will ignite, whether from lightning or carelessness—they burn hotter, faster, and more destructively than the fires the policy was meant to prevent.

So yes, the government caused this. Not by malice, but by a well-intentioned crusade that ignored fire ecology. The Cerro Grande fire in 2000, ironically begun as a federal prescribed burn gone wrong, and the High Park and Waldo Canyon fires in Colorado this summer, are symptoms of a forest structure the government engineered.

The way out is more fire, not less—prescribed burns, mechanical thinning, and a willingness to let some lightning-ignited backcountry fires run their course. The 1995 Federal Wildland Fire Policy nominally endorsed this approach, but funding remains skewed massively toward suppression. Every dollar spent fighting a fire is a dollar not spent preventing the next one through fuel treatment. Until Congress restructures Forest Service budgets to reward prevention over heroic suppression theater, we will keep watching our forests, and increasingly our towns, burn.

The framing of this debate—government as cause or cure—misses the more uncomfortable culprit: us. Americans have moved in enormous numbers into what foresters call the wildland-urban interface, the zone where subdivisions push into flammable forests and chaparral. Colorado Springs, where the Waldo Canyon fire just consumed 346 homes, has tripled in population since 1970, and much of that growth has crept up canyons and onto ridgelines that have always burned.

Government policy enables this in ways both visible and invisible. Federal flood and fire insurance programs, federally subsidized mortgages, and local zoning that permits construction in obvious fire corridors all send a signal: build where you like, and the rest of us will help you rebuild. When the fires come, federal firefighters risk their lives—and federal taxpayers spend roughly $3 billion a year—protecting structures that arguably should never have been permitted.

Compare this to how we handle other natural hazards. We have building codes for earthquakes and hurricanes. We have flood maps that constrain development. But there is no federal wildfire map that meaningfully limits where one can build, and state and county governments in the West have been politically unwilling to draw such lines.

Real prevention would mean requiring defensible space, fire-resistant roofing and siding, and ember-resistant venting—and refusing to permit homes that can't meet those standards. It would mean shifting suppression costs onto local jurisdictions that approve risky development, so they internalize the consequences of sprawl. None of this is glamorous, and none of it stops fires from burning forests. But it can stop fires from becoming disasters. The government's role isn't really about the flames in the trees; it's about the houses we keep putting in their path.

Let's not lose sight of the elephant in the smoke-filled room: climate change. The scientific evidence is now overwhelming that the American West is hotter and drier than it was a generation ago, that snowpack is melting earlier, and that fire seasons have lengthened by more than two months since the 1980s. A widely cited 2006 study in Science by Westerling and colleagues documented a fourfold increase in large wildfires in western forests, with the trend tracking spring and summer temperatures more closely than any other variable.

Forest management matters, and the wildland-urban interface matters. But pretending that a century of fire suppression alone explains what we are watching this summer requires ignoring the climate signal. Bark beetles have killed tens of millions of acres of western conifers precisely because winters are no longer cold enough to kill the larvae. Reservoirs are drawing down earlier. The atmospheric thirst that pulls moisture from vegetation has grown measurably.

If we ask "has the government played a role in increasing the risk of these fires?", the honest answer includes decades of federal inaction on greenhouse gas emissions. The failure of cap-and-trade legislation in 2010, the lack of a carbon price, and the continued subsidization of fossil fuel extraction are wildfire policy, whether we want to admit it or not. Conversely, government can prevent future fires—not the ones burning today, but those of 2050 and 2080—by treating decarbonization as the long-term fire prevention strategy it actually is.

It is tempting, and politically convenient, to talk only about thinning projects and defensible space. Those are necessary. But a hotter planet will overwhelm any forest management regime we devise. The most important wildfire policy in Washington isn't at the Forest Service; it's at the EPA.

Government doesn't start wildfires—lightning and careless campers do. Government doesn't grow forests—nature does. The question of whether government "causes" wildfires is mostly a distraction from a practical conversation about what works.

What works is straightforward, even if expensive: prescribed burning, mechanical thinning around communities, robust early detection, aggressive initial attack on fires that threaten lives and property, defensible space requirements, and fire-hardened construction. We know these things work because we have decades of data showing they work. The problem is not knowledge; it is implementation at scale and across jurisdictions.

The federal government owns roughly half the land in the West. That ownership creates an obligation to manage actively. The Forest Service and BLM have made progress under the National Fire Plan and the Healthy Forests Restoration Act, but treatment rates remain a small fraction of what landscape-scale restoration would require. Litigation, NEPA delays, and inconsistent funding all slow things down. None of these are insurmountable, but they require political will.

States and counties have their own obligations: enforceable building codes in fire-prone areas, defensible-space inspections, and honest hazard mapping. Insurance companies can drive a lot of this by pricing risk accurately, but they often can't because of state rate regulation.

So can government prevent wildfires? It cannot prevent ignitions, and shouldn't try to prevent every fire from burning—fire is part of these ecosystems. But it can substantially reduce the catastrophic, home-destroying megafires that have become the new normal. Doing so will cost money up front and produce benefits that are diffuse and statistical rather than visible. That is exactly the kind of problem democracies handle poorly, which is why we keep having this debate after each fire season instead of before the next one.

A common narrative blames the Forest Service for a century of "fire exclusion" and credits restoration of natural fire regimes as the solution. There's truth in this, but it has hardened into a slogan that obscures important distinctions.

Fire exclusion did dramatically increase fuel loads in dry, low-elevation ponderosa pine and mixed-conifer forests that historically burned frequently at low intensity. In those ecosystems, thinning and prescribed fire genuinely can restore something like the pre-suppression structure and reduce catastrophic fire risk.

But not all western forests are like that. Lodgepole pine forests in the high Rockies, including much of what is now burning in Colorado, historically burned in infrequent, high-severity, stand-replacing fires every 100 to 300 years. The big crown fires we see in lodgepole are not the result of fire suppression; they are how these forests have always worked. You cannot "fix" a lodgepole forest by thinning it, and the recent beetle epidemic is part of a natural cycle, even if exacerbated by warmer winters.

This matters for policy. Treating every forest like a fire-suppressed ponderosa stand will waste money and damage ecosystems that don't need thinning. Treatments should be concentrated near communities, where they demonstrably protect lives and structures, rather than spread thinly across vast backcountry where they accomplish little.

The government's role, then, is not to "prevent wildfires" as a category. It is to recognize the ecological diversity of western landscapes and tailor interventions accordingly: aggressive fuel treatment in fire-suppressed dry forests, defensible space and home hardening in the WUI, and acceptance that some fires—including big, scary ones—are part of how the West works. Pretending otherwise sets up unrealistic expectations that no agency can meet.

Government causes wildfires the same way it causes most disasters: by socializing the cost of risky behavior. The economic logic is simple. When the federal government bears the cost of fighting fires and rebuilding homes, while local governments capture the tax revenue from new development and individuals enjoy their mountain views, the result is predictable. More homes get built in fire-prone places than would otherwise be rational, and fewer precautions get taken than would otherwise be prudent.

Fix the incentives and you fix much of the problem. Charge property owners in high-hazard zones the actuarial cost of fire protection. Require local jurisdictions that approve WUI development to pay a substantial share of suppression costs. Let insurance markets price risk without rate caps that force flatland policyholders to subsidize forest-edge mansions. Stop providing federally guaranteed mortgages for homes that fail basic fire-resistance standards.

None of this requires new science or new technology. It requires acknowledging that the current system functions as a subsidy for development in some of the most dangerous places in America, and that the subsidy is paid in dollars by taxpayers and in lives by firefighters. Nineteen wildland firefighters have died on the job already this year, many protecting structures that were built in defiance of obvious hazard.

Government can also do useful direct work: thinning, prescribed fire, and fuel breaks around communities. But absent better incentives, every dollar spent on these treatments is partially undone by the next subdivision approved in the next canyon. You cannot outspend bad policy. The debate over wildfires is ultimately a debate over whether we are willing to make the people who benefit from living in the woods bear a fair share of the costs.

Wildfire risk assessment reveals that government intervention has genuinely ambiguous effects on fire outcomes. Agencies that use prescribed fire credibly claim prevention benefits. Yet prescribed burns occasionally escape control, as occurred in New Mexico in 2000, generating property damage and political backlash that chilled broader application.

There is no policy choice without trade-offs. The "fire suppression" policy of the twentieth century offered one set of trade-offs; the emerging "fire restoration" paradigm offers another. Neither eliminates wildfire—rather, each shapes fire behavior in different ways.

Better understanding of fire ecology, improved modeling of long-term fuel dynamics, and more sophisticated risk assessment can guide intervention choices. What government cannot do is eliminate fire from fire-prone landscapes. Acceptance of this reality should lead to policies that shape fire rather than futile attempts to abolish it. The goal should be reducing damage while maintaining ecological benefits that fire provides. Government that pursues this realistic aim serves the public interest.

The most striking aspect of wildfire politics is its resistance to evidence-based reform. Decades of forest ecology research demonstrate that fire-suppression-only policies create dangerous fuel accumulation. Yet programmatic reform proceeds slowly despite this consensus. Why?

Political economy explains much. Rural communities fear prescribed burns and mechanical thinning as threats to their sense of landscape. Environmental groups litigate fuel treatments that might affect rare species habitat. Property rights advocates resist restrictions on interface development. The result is policy paralysis dressed as careful deliberation.

Federal land management budgets reveal the priorities: suppression spending grows steadily while prevention investment stagnates. This reflects political incentives. Suppression is visible—firefighters on television, visible flames engaged—that generates support. Prevention is invisible—healthy forests, reduced fuel loads, avoided catastrophe—making it difficult to defend in appropriations hearings.

The government doesn't cause wildfires, but political structures do prevent the prevention work that would reduce wildfire damage.
