Hiring a Woman for Her Womb

People unable to bear children have increasingly turned to women who bear children for them, often by transferring an embryo created by in-vitro fertilization. Because legal and social views on surrogacy vary from nation to nation (and even state to state), prospective parents often engage surrogates in the United States and in developing countries. Controversy has clouded this issue.

What can be done to ensure that birth surrogacy is safe, ethical and protective of both the birth mother and the intended parents?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Surrogacy will not be made safe and ethical by good intentions alone. It requires a legal architecture that recognizes what is actually happening: a woman is being paid to undertake a medically risky labor on behalf of others. Pretending otherwise — through the polite fiction of "altruistic compensation" or, worse, through unregulated commercial markets in places like Anand, India — has produced most of the abuses now in the headlines, from the recent Baby Gammy case in Thailand to reports of surrogates housed in dormitories and barred from seeing their families.

Three reforms would help. First, contracts should be enforceable only when negotiated with independent legal counsel for the surrogate, paid for by the intended parents but chosen by her. The asymmetry of information and bargaining power is the central problem; standardize the floor.

Second, medical decisions during pregnancy — selective reduction, cesarean, abortion in case of fetal anomaly — must remain the surrogate's, as bodily autonomy is non-negotiable. Intended parents take that risk knowingly. Parentage, however, should vest in the commissioning parents at birth by pre-birth order, sparing everyone the custody battles that haunted Baby M.

Third, international surrogacy needs treaty-level coordination, akin to the Hague Adoption Convention. Otherwise wealthy clients will continue arbitraging jurisdictions, and poor women will bear the externalities.

Banning surrogacy, as France and Germany have, simply exports it. Regulating it candidly — acknowledging it as paid labor deserving labor protections, health coverage, and exit rights — is the honest path.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The global surrogacy landscape is a patchwork of laws and norms, leaving both surrogates and intended parents vulnerable to exploitation and heartbreak. To navigate this ethically treacherous terrain, we must move beyond piecemeal approaches and establish a robust framework rooted in transparency, autonomy, and equal protection. The central challenge is balancing the profound desire for a genetically related child with the imperative to safeguard the women who make that possible, particularly when economic disparities cross international borders.

First, any effective solution must mandate independent legal counsel for both the surrogate and the intended parents from the earliest stages. Too often, agencies or attorneys represent only the commissioning couple, leaving the surrogate without a true advocate to explain complex contractual terms, medical risks, and long-term psychological implications. This imbalance of power is especially acute in transnational arrangements, where a surrogate in a low-income country may not fully comprehend the legal waivers she is signing in a foreign language. A “cooling-off” period after legal consultation, irrespective of contract signing, should be required to ensure consent is never coerced by financial pressure or time constraints.

Second, standardized, enforceable medical and psychological screening protocols are non-negotiable. While physical health evaluations are routine, comprehensive psychological assessments for all parties are often overlooked. Surrogates must be screened not merely for stability, but for a nuanced understanding of the relational boundaries that will be required; intended parents must be assessed for the emotional readiness to parent a child born through a third party. These screenings must be paired with mandatory independent life-insurance policies and health-coverage guarantees for the surrogate, extending well beyond the postpartum period to cover any complications arising from the pregnancy.

Finally, the transnational dimension demands a concerted international effort. By 2014, the Hague Conference on Private International Law was already exploring a convention on surrogacy and parentage, and this work must be accelerated. Binding agreements could establish a floor of protections—including transparent compensation structures that guard against exploitation, a clear recognition of legal parentage from birth, and robust record-keeping to preserve a child’s right to know their origins. Any framework must also explicitly ban the practice of holding a newborn’s travel documents contingent on the surrogate’s waiver of her rights. Without such guardrails, the promise of creating families will remain tainted by the risk of treating a woman’s body as a commodified instrument, rather than honoring it as the source of a profound, life-giving gift.

The recent controversies surrounding international surrogacy, most notably the heartbreaking "Baby Gammy" case in Thailand, have starkly illuminated the ethical perils of an unregulated global surrogacy market. When reproductive outsourcing spans borders without robust legal frameworks, it leaves both surrogate mothers and the resulting children deeply vulnerable to exploitation and abandonment.

Crucially, it must ensure that women in developing nations are not coerced by poverty, guaranteeing them independent legal representation, fair compensation, and comprehensive medical care before, during, and after the pregnancy.

Furthermore, mandatory psychological screening and independent counseling must be required for all parties involved, ensuring expectations and boundaries are clearly managed.

Ultimately, surrogacy cannot be treated merely as a commercial transaction or a free-market commodity. By implementing rigorous, globally recognized legal and medical protections, we can safeguard the bodily autonomy and dignity of surrogate mothers while providing a safe, ethical, and compassionate pathway for people desperately hoping to build a family.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Surrogacy should not be treated as either a miracle beyond regulation or a market so tainted that prohibition is the only moral answer. Bans tend to drive the practice abroad or underground, where the women with the least bargaining power bear the greatest risk. The better answer is a tightly regulated system that recognizes surrogacy as serious reproductive labor and protects all parties before pregnancy begins.

First, every arrangement should require independent legal counsel for the surrogate and the intended parents, paid for by the intended parents but chosen freely by each side. Contracts should be written in plain language and reviewed before any medical procedure occurs. They should specify compensation, medical expenses, insurance, risks, procedures for miscarriage or multiple pregnancy, and parentage after birth. Payment should be placed in escrow, not controlled by a broker or by the intended parents, so the surrogate is not dependent on goodwill.

Second, the surrogate’s medical autonomy must be nonnegotiable. Intended parents may have preferences, but no contract should force a woman to undergo an abortion, selective reduction, cesarean section or other medical intervention against her will. At the same time, intended parents need security that, absent fraud or coercion, they will be recognized as the child’s legal parents. Courts should allow pre-birth or prompt post-birth parentage orders so children are not left in legal limbo.

Third, agencies and fertility clinics should be licensed and audited. They should screen intended parents and surrogates, provide psychological counseling, limit dangerous embryo transfers, require comprehensive health and life insurance for the surrogate, and disclose success rates and fees. Advertising should be monitored to prevent misleading promises to poor women or desperate would-be parents.

Finally, cross-border surrogacy demands special caution. Cases in India and Thailand have shown how easily poverty, citizenship law and weak oversight can combine to make babies and women vulnerable. Countries should not allow brokers to exploit gaps between legal systems. If international arrangements continue, they should meet minimum standards: informed consent, enforceable parentage, medical care after birth, and no abandonment of disabled children.

Ethical surrogacy is possible only when the woman is treated not as a womb for hire, but as a person whose rights survive the contract.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The question of how to regulate surrogacy arrangements requires balancing respect for reproductive autonomy with protections against exploitation and harm. Several reforms could help achieve this balance.

First, comprehensive informed consent protocols must be established. Surrogates should receive independent legal counsel and thorough medical counseling about the physical and psychological risks involved, including the possibility of multifetal pregnancy, gestational complications, and the emotional weight of carrying a child for another family. These protections should not be merely formal but genuinely accessible.

Second, fair compensation structures deserve careful attention. Compensation should be sufficient to avoid exploitation while avoiding payments so large that they create problematic incentives or obscure the human dimensions of the arrangement. Some have proposed establishing non-profit agencies or using escrow systems to prevent exploitation on either side.

Third, clear legal frameworks should establish parental rights before birth. Too many Intended Parents and surrogates have faced costly legal battles due to ambiguous contracts or conflicting state laws. Federal guidance or model legislation could reduce this uncertainty while respecting state autonomy.

Fourth, international arrangements require particular scrutiny. While cross-border surrogacy serves families who cannot otherwise afford treatment, it can also circumvent more protective regulations. Encouraging the development of international ethical standards and protecting the citizenship and rights of children born through these arrangements should be priorities.

Ultimately, no regulatory framework can eliminate all risks, but thoughtful oversight can make surrogacy safer and more ethical for all involved.

The patchwork governing surrogacy in the United States is a national embarrassment. In California, surrogacy contracts are enforceable and intended parents can secure pre-birth orders establishing their parentage. In Michigan, the same contract is a criminal offense. In New York, paid surrogacy is void but unpunished. Families cross state lines not for medical reasons but to find a legal regime that will recognize them. This makes no sense.

Congress should pass a federal Uniform Surrogacy Act that does several things at once. First, it should distinguish gestational surrogacy (where the carrier has no genetic relationship to the child) from traditional surrogacy, and treat them differently. Gestational arrangements present fewer custody disputes and should be permitted under clear contractual terms. Traditional surrogacy, where the carrier is also the genetic mother, deserves stricter scrutiny and a guaranteed revocation window.

Second, the law should mandate independent legal counsel for the surrogate paid by the intended parents, separate medical and psychological evaluation, and a written compensation schedule that pays the surrogate for her labor and risk regardless of whether the pregnancy results in a live birth. Too many current contracts pay only on delivery, which essentially shifts all miscarriage and medical risk onto the woman.

Third, parentage should be established by a single, streamlined court order before birth so that hospitals, insurers, and schools are not navigating ambiguous documents months later. The Baby M case taught us that murky parentage hurts everyone, especially the child.

Fourth, the federal floor should ban what is currently a quiet scandal: brokers who recruit low-income military spouses and immigrants with TRICARE or other coverage, run shoestring matching agencies with no escrow accounts, and disappear when complications arise. License agencies. Require bonded escrow. Audit them.

Finally, a federal framework would give the United States moral standing to push for international standards through the Hague Conference, which has been studying this since 2011. As long as we tolerate Mississippi shipping babies one way and California shipping them another, we cannot credibly tell India or Thailand how to regulate their clinics.

Surrogacy is not going away. The intended parents are infertile couples, gay men, cancer survivors, women born without uteruses — people with a deeply human longing that medicine can now answer. The surrogates, in the better arrangements, describe the experience as meaningful work. What we owe everyone involved is a legal regime that treats them as adults entering a serious relationship, not as parties to an underground transaction.

Let us be honest about what commercial surrogacy is: the rental of a woman's body and the sale of the child she produces. No amount of euphemism — "gestational carrier," "compensated journey," "gift of life" — changes the underlying transaction. And once we admit that, the ethical problems become unavoidable.

Begin with who the surrogates are. In the United States, recruiting materials target women on military bases, in economically depressed regions, and within tight-knit religious communities where having babies is normalized and well-paying work is scarce. In India, where the industry has exploded since the 2002 legalization, the women housed in surrogacy hostels in Anand and Mumbai are overwhelmingly poor, often illiterate, and paid a fraction of what American carriers receive — though it is still many years of local wages. This is not a market of equals. It is a global pipeline running from poor wombs to rich nurseries.

Then consider the contracts. Surrogates routinely sign away their right to refuse cesarean sections, to decline selective reduction, to choose what they eat or where they sleep. They agree in advance to relinquish a child they may have come to love. We would not enforce such terms in any other context. We would call them unconscionable.

The standard response is that women are autonomous and can decide what to do with their bodies. I agree. But autonomy arguments cut both ways. We do not permit people to sell their kidneys, even though they own them, because we have judged that a kidney market would prey on the desperate. The same logic applies here, with the added complication that a child — not just tissue — is the product.

The path forward is the one taken by France, Germany, and much of Europe: prohibit commercial surrogacy, permit altruistic surrogacy between known parties with judicial oversight, and refuse to recognize parentage obtained through foreign commercial arrangements. This is not cruelty toward infertile couples. Adoption exists. Foster care exists. Childlessness, though painful, is not a harm requiring that another woman's body be drafted into service.

If we are unwilling to go that far, then at the very least we should outlaw the cross-border trade, set a national minimum compensation floor that prices out the most exploitative recruiting, and give every surrogate an absolute, non-waivable right to keep the child for a defined period after birth. Anything less treats women as means and children as merchandise.

The framing of the debate around "safety" and "ethics" obscures a simpler point: adult women are capable of deciding whether to carry a pregnancy for someone else, and the law's job is to enforce the agreements they make, not to second-guess their choices.

I find the paternalism in much of the surrogacy debate striking. The same commentators who insist that a woman has the absolute right to terminate a pregnancy suddenly discover, when money is involved, that she cannot possibly understand what she is consenting to. The same advocates who celebrate sex work decriminalization as bodily autonomy worry that compensated gestation is coercion. The inconsistency is telling. What unsettles people about surrogacy is not exploitation but commerce — the idea that something as sacred as childbirth might have a price.

But everything has a price. Obstetricians charge for delivery. Egg donors are paid. Adoption attorneys bill by the hour. Hospitals profit handsomely from labor and delivery. Only the woman doing the actual work is supposed to act from pure altruism. That double standard does not protect women; it transfers value away from them.

What a sensible regime looks like is not hard to describe. Enforceable contracts, negotiated by attorneys for both parties, with full medical and psychological disclosure. Compensation set by the market, paid through a bonded escrow account so the surrogate is not left holding the bag if intended parents default. Clear pre-birth parentage orders. Mandatory health insurance funded by the intended parents. A licensing regime for agencies that weeds out the bad actors who give the industry a reputation it does not deserve.

On international surrogacy, I would resist the impulse to ban. An Indian woman earning ten years' wages by carrying a pregnancy for an American couple is not a victim of trafficking; she is a worker who has found, in her own assessment, the best option available to her. If we want to improve her situation, we should push for minimum compensation standards, independent counsel, and real oversight of clinics — not shut down her livelihood and congratulate ourselves on our morality.

The Baby M era is over. Modern gestational surrogacy, where the carrier shares no DNA with the child, has produced thousands of healthy families and very few horror stories. The horror stories that exist almost all involve unregulated markets — exactly the markets that prohibition creates. Light, not darkness, is what protects everyone here.

The most urgent reforms are not philosophical but practical, and they concern the cross-border trade. Whatever one thinks about surrogacy in principle, the system as it currently operates between rich countries and poor ones is producing predictable and preventable harms.

Consider what happened in Thailand earlier this year. An Australian couple commissioned twins through a Bangkok surrogate, learned that one had Down syndrome, and reportedly took home only the healthy child, leaving baby Gammy with the surrogate. The story prompted Thailand to begin shutting the industry down. Around the same time, an Australian man was discovered to have commissioned a child in Thailand whom he subsequently abused, raising questions no agency had thought to ask. India, the largest market, has been tightening rules and may soon bar foreign single people and same-sex couples entirely. Nepal, Mexico, and Georgia are emerging as new hubs, each less regulated than the last.

The pattern is clear. Wherever commercial surrogacy goes underground or operates in jurisdictions with weak oversight, the same problems recur: surrogates who do not fully understand the contracts they sign, payment structures that put all risk on the carrier, no screening of intended parents, abandoned children, citizenship limbo, and clinics that cut medical corners because their clients have already left the country.

A serious response requires international coordination, not just national rules. The Hague Conference on Private International Law has been working since 2011 on a framework analogous to the inter-country adoption convention. That work should be accelerated and adopted. At minimum, such a convention should require: background checks on intended parents, including criminal and child welfare screening; independent legal representation for the surrogate; minimum compensation tied to local cost of living; medical standards including limits on embryo transfer to prevent dangerous multiple pregnancies; and a guarantee of citizenship for the child in the intended parents' country before the pregnancy begins.

Receiving countries — the United States, Australia, the United Kingdom, Israel — have the most leverage here. They can refuse to recognize parentage or grant passports for arrangements that did not meet international standards. That alone would push the market toward compliance.

None of this answers the deeper question of whether wombs should be for hire at all. But while that debate continues, real women and real children are being processed through an industry that no one is regulating in earnest. The first task is to stop the bleeding. The philosophical reckoning can follow.

As a physician who has cared for surrogates and intended parents, I want to shift the discussion from contracts and ideology toward something that often gets lost: the medicine.

Gestational surrogacy is not a benign procedure. It typically involves hormonal preparation, the transfer of one or more embryos, often a higher-than-average risk of multiple pregnancy, and all the ordinary risks of pregnancy and delivery — hypertension, gestational diabetes, hemorrhage, cesarean section, postpartum depression, and, rarely, death. A surrogate undertakes these risks on someone else's behalf. We owe her, at the very least, the standard of care we would want for ourselves.

Yet current practice falls short in several specific ways. First, single embryo transfer should be the default. Many intended parents, eager to maximize success per cycle and minimize cost, push for double transfers. Twin pregnancies carry several times the maternal and neonatal risk of singletons. The American Society for Reproductive Medicine has issued guidelines, but they are routinely ignored. Make them binding.

Second, mental health support is treated as a formality when it should be central. Carrying a child and relinquishing her is psychologically complex even when the surrogate is committed and well-prepared. Pre-screening should be rigorous, and post-partum counseling should be guaranteed and paid for, not offered as an afterthought.

Third, surrogates need their own physicians, not the intended parents' fertility specialist managing the pregnancy. The conflicts of interest are obvious. A clinic that has been paid by the intended parents has every incentive to recommend interventions — selective reduction, scheduled cesarean — that serve the contract rather than the patient in the room.

Fourth, we should set medical eligibility standards. Many programs allow women to serve as surrogates after multiple prior cesareans, or with conditions that raise the risk of complications. A national medical registry would let us track outcomes and identify practices that are unsafe.

Fifth, the intended parents' medical history should be screened too — both for genetic factors relevant to the embryo and for capacity to parent the child who results. We screen adoptive parents. We do not screen the people commissioning a pregnancy. That is strange.

The ethical debates about commodification will continue, and they should. But while philosophers argue, the women in our clinics are pregnant now. Treating them as patients first — with the autonomy, information, and clinical advocacy any patient deserves — is the most concrete thing the medical profession can offer this debate.

Any effort to make surrogacy “safe and ethical” must begin by recognizing it for what it is: a profoundly personal contract between consenting adults. The quickest way to endanger both the birth mother and the intended parents is to layer the process with heavy-handed regulations that drive it underground or push it into jurisdictions with no oversight at all. The best protection is a rigorously enforced legal framework built on transparency, informed consent, and the unwavering right of a woman to manage her own body and labor.

We already have a model for such a contractual relationship: the fully informed, willingly undertaken work governed by civil law. Prospective surrogates should receive independent legal counsel before signing any agreement, and all parties should have crystal clarity on issues like selective reduction, compensation for medical risks, and the intended parents’ obligation to accept the child regardless of health. Standardized, enforceable contracts—subject to ordinary contract law remedies, not criminal penalty—would weed out the bad actors that tarnish the practice. An escrow system for payments, mandatory health insurance covering pregnancy complications, and life insurance for the rare catastrophic event are simple, market-driven safeguards that protect the surrogate without treating her as a victim incapable of consent.

Calls to ban commercial surrogacy entirely are not merely paternalistic; they are dangerous. When women in the United States are barred from receiving compensation, those desperate to build a family will turn to unregulated overseas markets, where exploitation flourishes precisely because there is no binding contract law and no legal recourse. If we insist that a woman cannot be trusted to rent out her womb, we deny her the dignity of choice and inevitably create the very black markets we claim to fear. The ethical path forward is not to police motives but to insist that every surrogacy arrangement meets strict, legally guaranteed standards of informed consent and full financial protection. In a free society, a woman’s womb is hers to govern, and the state’s sole legitimate role is to ensure her decisions are free, fully knowledgeable, and backed by the full force of the law.

To ask how to make surrogacy “safe and ethical” is to ask the wrong question. The practice is inherently exploitative, and no regulatory tweak can erase the fact that commercial surrogacy turns a woman’s body, and the child she carries, into commodities for sale. The most protective measure—for the birth mother, for children, and for global equality—is a clear international ban on for-profit surrogacy contracts, with only strictly limited altruistic arrangements permitted under exceptional circumstances.

Proponents like to paint surrogacy as a triumph of reproductive choice, but choice operates within crushing economic constraints. In the United States, agencies aggressively recruit women in financial distress, promising money that can pay off debt or a semester’s tuition. The contract virtually erases the surrogate’s bodily autonomy by requiring her to submit to invasive medical procedures, to terminate the pregnancy on demand, and to relinquish all legal claims before the child is born. That is not a free contract between equals; it is a purchase of intimate labor under duress. Globally, the asymmetry is even more stark. In India, where commercial surrogacy boomed before its 2015 restrictions, women in poverty became the reproductive assembly line for affluent Westerners, often forfeiting any meaningful consent in languages they did not fully read.

Some suggest we can fix this with counseling, insurance, and price floors. But these cosmetic fixes miss the point. So long as a third party can buy gestation itself, the power differential will always place the birth mother’s health and emotional reality as a cost to be managed rather than a priority. The only truly protective stance is to treat the bearing of a child as a non-commercial, relational act. Altruistic surrogacy—where a sister or close friend carries a child without payment beyond medical expenses—resituates the arrangement within a framework of care rather than commerce. Such arrangements need careful legal oversight to confirm parentage, but they are not a market. For everyone else, the answer is not safer surrogacy but its abolition. Only then can we stop asking how to polish a transaction that, at its core, sells a woman’s reproductive capacity.

The hard truth is that surrogacy is here to stay. When celebrities announce the birth of a child via a gestational carrier, and when clinics in states like California have built a profitable, international clientele, no outright ban is politically feasible—or desirable. The challenge is to craft a regulatory regime that treats surrogacy not as baby-selling, but as a deeply consequential, heavily intermediated service demanding public health–level safeguards. The solution lies in licensing, professionalized support, and clear rules about parentage that are finalized only after birth.

First, all surrogacy agencies and fertility clinics involved should be licensed by a state health authority and subject to unannounced inspections. Every surrogate must have independent legal representation paid for by the intended parents, and every party must undergo rigorous psychological screening, with a focus on the surrogate’s understanding of the emotional complexity of relinquishment. Mandatory counseling should continue through the pregnancy and for at least six months postpartum, because the risk of psychological harm does not end at delivery. Second, we need standardized insurance requirements that cover all medical costs, complications, and lost wages during bed rest without dispute. An escrow account for the full compensation, managed by a neutral third party, would end the horror stories of intended parents walking away from a child with a disability while stiffing the surrogate.

The most difficult piece of the puzzle is the legal parentage order. The turmoil of the Baby M case in the 1980s taught us that pre-birth contracts that strip a birth mother of parental rights are a recipe for tragedy. A sound statute would establish that the birth mother is the legal mother until she freely reaffirms her consent 48 hours after birth, when she can transfer parentage without coercion or the fog of labor drugs. Intended parents, for their part, must be legally bound to accept the child regardless of health, excepting only an agreement about severe fetal anomalies signed before embryo transfer. This model—rooted in licensed oversight, continuous support, and post-birth finalization—does not romanticize the market, but it acknowledges that the only realistic way to protect both the woman and the family is to surround them with the sturdiest ethical guardrails a law can build.

The debate over surrogacy is too often a shouting match between those who see a sacred bond and those who see a labor contract. Both miss the most urgent failing: the international regime, or lack of one, that allows wealthy families to exploit poverty across borders. We do not need to ban surrogacy; we need a binding global treaty that forces every cross-border arrangement to put the birth mother’s rights and citizenship first.

Today’s transnational surrogacy is a legal and ethical disaster. An Israeli couple can commission a Thai surrogate, conceive with a Danish embryo, and then find themselves stateless for months because no country will issue the newborn a passport. The surrogate, often a migrant with few legal protections, can be abandoned at a hospital with a child no intended parent will claim. We have seen the grotesque spectacles in India and Nepal, where clinics treat women like egg-laying hens. The first line of defense must be international agreement. This dual-compliance rule would stop clinics from forum-shopping to the least protective jurisdiction. Critics will say that such rules will simply push the market further into the shadows. But the current system already thrives in a shadow world of unenforceable contracts and sprawling inequality. A genuine international framework, backed by sanctions, would at last give legal standing to the most vulnerable person in the equation: the woman doing the enormously risky work of gestation. Until we stop pretending that surrogacy is a purely private choice and instead demand that it respect the surrogate’s fundamental rights as a worker, mother, and citizen, no amount of domestic regulation will make it ethical.

To protect women and children, we must abandon the naive assumption that surrogacy can be made safe through better contracts. The practice is morally wrong because it deliberately severs the natural maternal bond, treating a child as a product to be designed and delivered and a woman as a vessel for the desires of others. The only truly protective approach is to discourage surrogacy in all its forms and to channel the deep longing for children into adoption, a path that serves a child already in need of a home rather than creating one through a commercialized embryo transfer.

Proponents claim that the gestational surrogate feels no biological connection because the child is not genetically hers. But the physiological reality is that pregnancy rewires a woman’s body and brain: hormone cascades, the rhythms of fetal movement, and the trauma of birth all forge a profound attachment. Our law wisely presumes that a birth mother is the legal mother because that presumption reflects a truth about human nature. Pre-birth surrogacy contracts ask a woman to override that natural tie and to promise, before she has ever felt a kick, that she will hand over the infant as if it were a parcel. When the moment arrives and grief overwhelms her—as it did for Mary Beth Whitehead—the courts and agencies treat her as in breach of contract, not as a mother in anguish. Third, contractual provisions should address the full range of contingencies: selective reduction, pregnancy complications, and the always-possible change of heart that can devastate everyone involved.

Perhaps most importantly, we need national uniformity in a country where surrogacy arrangements currently depend on which state you happen to live in. The current patchwork creates perverse incentives for prospective parents to hunt for the most permissive jurisdiction—or to go abroad entirely, where oversight may be minimal. A federal floor of protections would not dictate state policy but would ensure that some baseline safeguards travel with every American surrogacy arrangement.

Critics will argue that no contractual framework can truly capture the complexity of what it means to carry a child for someone else. They are partly right. But the alternative—operating in legal grey zones, exploiting women in developing nations, leaving children in legal limbo—is demonstrably worse. Thoughtful regulation is not the enemy of ethical surrogacy; it is a prerequisite for it.

Behind the clinical language of "birth surrogacy" lies a practice that, in too many instances, amounts to the commodification of women's bodies. When wealthy intended parents—often from developed nations—hire women, frequently from poorer communities or countries, to gestate children for them, we should be honest about what is happening: economic desperation is doing the recruiting.

The language of "choice" rings hollow when the alternative is untenable. A woman in Mumbai or Los Angeles who agrees to become a surrogate because she needs money to feed her existing children is not exercising the same kind of autonomy as someone choosing between consumer goods. Material need constrains choice, and contracts written by parties with unequal bargaining power are not genuine expressions of individual liberty.

Beyond exploitation, there are health concerns that the industry routinely downplays. The fertility drugs required for IVF are not trivial interventions. Multiple pregnancies carry genuine risks. And the psychological weight of carrying a child to term only to hand it over—often described by surrogates as "relinquishment grief"—deserves far more attention than it typically receives.

What would it mean to take women's welfare seriously? It would mean questioning whether surrogacy arrangements should exist at all, or at minimum treating them as a last resort rather than a growing industry. It would mean recognizing that creating children is not the same as providing other services, and that gestation involves a uniquely intimate relationship between a woman and the life growing within her.

The desire of people who cannot otherwise have children is understandable. But that desire does not create a right to hire someone else's body.

In a free society, competent adults should be able to enter into voluntary contracts without government second-guessing their decisions. Surrogacy arrangements, when entered into freely by all parties, are exactly the kind of private transaction that minimal legitimate government should respect.

The arguments against contractual surrogacy often rest on paternalism or on intuitions about the sacredness of reproduction that do not survive scrutiny. If we accept that people have the right to donate organs to strangers—which we do, with appropriate safeguards—why should gestating a child for another family be categorically different? The gestational surrogate assumes risks and accepts compensation in exchange for helping another family achieve something precious to them. This is labor, and labor contracts deserve legal protection, not prohibition.

What about concerns for the child? Here again, the evidence cuts against restriction. Children born through surrogacy generally fare no worse—and often better—than children born through other assisted reproductive technologies. The real problems with surrogacy today stem not from the practice itself but from legal uncertainty and inadequate contract enforcement. When agreements are unenforceable or when one party can unilaterally exploit the ambiguity, everyone suffers. The solution is clearer legal frameworks that enforce contracts as written, not abolishing the practice. Let consenting adults make their own reproductive choices and hold them to their agreements.

Much of the surrogacy debate focuses on the adults involved—the intended parents who want children and the surrogates who carry them. But we should redirect attention to a group whose interests are often overlooked: the children themselves.

Children born through surrogacy have a stake in clear legal parentage from birth. Too often, the legal status of these children remains uncertain for weeks or months, creating anxiety for intended parents and potential vulnerability for the child. The right of a child to know their family situation should not depend on navigating a legal grey area.

Beyond parentage, there are identity concerns that merit serious attention. Surrogacy, like adoption, creates questions about origin and belonging that shape a child's development. Research on adopted children consistently shows that access to information about biological heritage matters for identity formation. Children conceived through donated gametes or gestated by surrogates deserve similar consideration. At minimum, this means ensuring that records are maintained in ways that allow individuals, once they reach adulthood, to understand their biological origins if they wish.

The international dimension compounds these concerns. When children are born in one country to a surrogate from another, they may carry citizenship or immigration complications that follow them for years. A child should not start life as a legal ambiguity because their parents wanted them badly enough to engage a surrogate abroad.

Regulation, from this perspective, should be child-centered: ensuring clear legal recognition, maintaining records for future reference, and minimizing the circumstances in which children begin their lives in legal limbo. The adults' desires matter, but the child's interests should come first.

Surrogacy has become a genuinely global phenomenon, with prospective parents routinely crossing borders to engage surrogates in other countries. This reality demands an international legal framework that currently does not exist—and urgently needs to be built.

The current situation is untenable. India, which emerged as a major surrogacy destination, has moved toward restricting commercial surrogacy for foreigners amid concerns about exploitation. Thailand, after high-profile cases involving same-sex parents and other arrangements that caught international headlines, has similarly tightened rules. The Ukraine has become a destination for others, while the United States operates a patchwork of state laws with no federal coherence. Intended parents find themselves in a dizzying landscape where the legality of their arrangement depends entirely on where they happen to be.

This creates concrete harms. Children born through international surrogacy arrangements sometimes face citizenship gaps or immigration complications. Surrogates in developing countries often lack basic legal protections. And the absence of consistent standards allows the worst actors to operate with impunity.

What would international coordination look like? At minimum, nations should agree on baseline protections for surrogates—requirements for informed consent, medical standards, and prohibition of the most exploitative practices. Agreements on parentage recognition would prevent children from becoming legal non-entities while their parents litigate jurisdiction questions. And mechanisms for cross-border enforcement would give all parties certainty about their rights and obligations.

The Hague Conference on Private International Law has begun work on some of these issues, but progress is slow. What is needed is a serious, sustained diplomatic effort to develop a comprehensive international convention on surrogacy. The children, surrogates, and intended parents caught in this legal vacuum deserve better.
