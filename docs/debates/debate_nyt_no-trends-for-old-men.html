Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The premise of the Details article—that men past forty should retreat into a tasteful uniform of dark suits, cashmere, and sensible loafers—mistakes conformity for elegance. It assumes that style is a youth phenomenon, something one ages out of like skateboarding or all-night drinking. But fashion, properly understood, is not a privilege of the young; it's a form of self-presentation that gets more interesting, not less, as a person accumulates a sense of who he actually is.

The men who look best in their fifties and sixties are almost never the ones playing it safe. Think of Paul Smith, Nick Wooster, Glenn O'Brien, or Doonan himself—men whose pleasure in clothes is visible and undimmed by the calendar. What they share isn't trend-chasing so much as a confident relationship to their own taste. They wear bright socks, slim trousers, unusual eyewear, and the occasional questionable hat because they enjoy it, and that enjoyment reads on them. By contrast, the forty-five-year-old who has been frightened into navy blazers and pleated khakis because a magazine told him neon sneakers are undignified looks exactly like what he is: someone taking orders.

The real rule isn't about age but about fit—both literal and figurative. A sixty-year-old in a sprayed-on Hedi Slimane suit looks uncomfortable; so does a twenty-five-year-old, frankly. Trends are best treated as a vocabulary to borrow from, not a costume to wear whole. An older man can absolutely pick up this season's wider trouser, textured knit, or boot silhouette; he just shouldn't dress head-to-toe like a Topman mannequin. That advice, incidentally, applies to the twenty-two-year-old too.

What the Details piece really betrays is the menswear industry's anxiety about its own customer. After the explosion of #menswear blogs, Pitti Uomo peacocking, and the rise of brands like Engineered Garments and Band of Outsiders, older men are spending and experimenting more than they have in decades. Telling them to sit down and behave isn't sound advice. It's a failure of nerve.

To tell a man over 40 he must avoid trendy looks is insulting and reductive. The Details article dismissing Simon Doonan as a “fashion don’t” conflates age with irrelevance. Doonan’s bold ensembles prove personal flair has no expiration. The real question is how, not whether, older men can wear trends authentically.

Blanket bans assume all trends are equal. A slim suit, patterned shirt, or vibrant sock can elevate at any age. The failure comes from bad fit or lack of confidence, not birth year. A mature man in skinny jeans might look awkward, but that’s execution, not principle. Integrate a contemporary piece into a lived-in wardrobe and the effect can be magnetic—think Paul Newman, who borrowed from the moment without losing himself.

The idea that 40 is a style cliff is arbitrary. Today’s 40-somethings know the difference between chasing youth and expressing personality. Doonan’s crime was being visible and having fun, a sin only to rulebook-thumpers.

Self-awareness is key. Chasing every micro-fad looks desperate at any age. Curation is everything: borrow from the zeitgeist but filter through taste and lifestyle. A leather jacket in a classic cut? Yes. Drop-crotch trousers? Only if performance art. The line is coherence, not age.

Fashion tells a story. Silencing someone past an arbitrary birthday impoverishes that narrative. Doonan’s story is far richer than a sea of beige khakis. Should men over 40 avoid trendy looks? Only if they want to signal they’ve quit caring. For the rest, the rule holds: wear it like you mean it.

Men over 40 should not avoid trendy looks; they should avoid being bullied by them. That is a different matter. The problem with the Details premise is not that clothes have no age, but that it treats age as a costume violation rather than one factor among many: body, profession, personality, setting, taste and confidence.

Fashion has always depended on borrowing across generations. The modern suit was once a youth rebellion against aristocratic dress. Jeans, sneakers, leather jackets and military coats have all migrated from subculture or utility into adult wardrobes. If every man put down a garment the moment it became associated with younger people, the adult closet would be a museum of navy blazers and apology.

Still, “wear whatever you want” is too easy. Clothes communicate. A 48-year-old banker in a neon tank top and drop-crotch sweatpants at a client meeting is not being brave; he is ignoring context. But the same man in well-cut trousers, serious shoes and a sharply chosen printed shirt may look more alive and more appropriate than someone hiding in anonymous khakis. Trendiness fails when it looks like panic — when a man appears to be auditioning for an age he no longer is, rather than dressing as the person he has become.

That is why Simon Doonan is a poor target for fashion scolding. His style is not a desperate reach for youth; it is consistent, witty and legible. He has earned the right to eccentricity because it appears integrated into his identity. The same outfit on someone else might read as costume. That distinction matters more than the birthday.

The better rule for men over 40 is not “avoid trends,” but “translate them.” A trend can be adopted in proportion: slimmer tailoring without sausage-casing oneself; color without clownishness; patterned socks, interesting frames, a modern jacket, better denim. Hair, grooming and fit matter more with age, not because older men must be invisible, but because sloppiness is less easily mistaken for cool.

The fear beneath articles like this is that older men will look foolish. But the opposite danger is just as real: that they will look resigned. Fashion should not be a youth preserve. Men over 40 should dress with judgment, not obedience — and judgment sometimes includes a trend.

The Details piece, however well-intentioned, codifies an outdated premise: that aging automatically means fashion retreat. What men over 40 actually deserve is not permission to dress down, but encouragement to dress according to their own evolving aesthetic judgment.

The Details article and the resulting hand-wringing miss what's actually changed in menswear over the past decade. We are living through the most genuinely democratic moment in men's fashion in a century. The old hierarchies—business attire by day, age-appropriate leisurewear by evening, sneakers only for the gym—have collapsed. Tech billionaires wear hoodies to board meetings. In this environment, the question "is he too old for that?" is a category error. The relevant question is whether the clothes fit, whether they suit the occasion, and whether the wearer looks comfortable in them. Age is one variable among many, and not the dominant one.

What Details is really registering, I suspect, is a generational discomfort within the fashion press itself. For decades, men's magazines existed to teach younger men how to dress like older, more established men. The arrow pointed up the age ladder. Now it points down: dad rock, dad jeans, and dad style are all pejoratives, while youth aesthetics dominate. Editors trained in the old system don't know what to do with a sixty-year-old man in slim jeans and a bomber, because their entire framework assumed he'd be in a sack suit by now.

Let's be honest about what "trend" means here, because the conversation keeps sliding between two very different things.

Trend, in the narrow sense, means whatever look the fashion industry is currently promoting—this season's silhouette, this season's color, this season's must-have piece. In that sense, no one over forty should pay much attention, but neither should anyone under forty. Following trends in this narrow industry sense is a mug's game, a treadmill engineered to make you feel perpetually behind. It's bad advice at any age.

Trend, in the broader cultural sense, means the gradual evolution of how people actually dress: slimmer fits replacing baggy ones over the 2000s, sneakers migrating from the gym to the street, beards returning, tattoos becoming unremarkable. These shifts aren't optional in the same way; they're the visual vocabulary of contemporary life. A man over forty who refuses to participate in them at all ends up looking like he's frozen in 1994, which is its own form of costume.

The Details article conflates these. By telling men over forty to back off from "trendy" looks, it's effectively telling them to look older than they are, to opt out of the present. That's a different and more reactionary message than "don't chase every microtrend on the runway."

The right approach is to engage with the broader cultural drift while ignoring the narrow industrial cycle. Adjust your fit when fits shift. Update your shoes occasionally. Notice what younger people are doing and decide which elements work for you. But don't buy a peplum jacket because GQ said so, and don't dye your hair to match.

Doonan, for what it's worth, has always operated on instinct rather than trend-following, which is why his look reads as personal rather than dated. That's the real lesson, and it has nothing to do with turning forty.

The either/or framing of this debate does a disservice to men who simply want to dress well. “Should men over 40 avoid trendy looks?” is the wrong question. The right one is: “How should men over 40 engage with trends?” The answer is not a blanket yes or no but a nuanced, case-by-case evaluation that accounts for the specific trend, the man’s body and context, and, critically, the manner of execution.

Not all trends are created equal. A skinny jean that clings like a second skin is a high-risk proposition for a man past 40, not because of his birth certificate but because few older bodies have the proportions to carry it off without looking squeezed. But a slim-straight jean in a dark wash—a clear descendant of the skinny trend—can look modern and flattering. Similarly, a graphic hoodie from a hyped streetwear brand screams “teenager,” while a refined sweatshirt in a luxurious fabric nods to the athleisure wave without pretending you’re headed to a skate park. The goal is translation, not direct imitation.

Context is everything. A creative director in a relaxed industry can get away with far more adventurous fashion than a litigator in a white-shoe firm. But even the lawyer can incorporate a subtle trend: a tie with an unusual texture, a pocket square in a color lifted from the runway, a slightly cropped trouser. These details signal that you’re still curious about the world without suggesting a midlife crisis. The real failure is wearing a trend unthinkingly, without adapting it to your life. The older you get, the more crucial fit becomes; a trendy item that hangs perfectly on a 25-year-old model will look tragic if it pulls, sags, or constricts on a mature frame.

Ultimately, men over 40 should neither avoid trends entirely nor chase them slavishly. They should approach fashion with the same discernment they bring to other pleasures: good wine, good music, good design. You sample, you edit, you keep what resonates and discard the rest. That’s not age-appropriate dressing; it’s just good taste.
