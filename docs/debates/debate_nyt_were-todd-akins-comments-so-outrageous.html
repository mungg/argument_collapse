Were Todd Akin’s Comments So Outrageous?

Republicans and Democrats expressed outrage after Todd Akin, the G.O.P. Senate candidate in Missouri, said he opposed all abortions, even after rape, because in cases of “legitimate rape,” women rarely get pregnant.

But Akin’s position is not unusual; many opponents of abortion want a ban without exceptions. A draft of the Republican platform essentially calls for that. An early version of a House bill banning federal money for abortions, co-sponsored by the Republican vice presidential candidate Paul Ryan, refined the rape exception so it would deal only with “forcible rape.”

Given all this like-minded thought, why are Akin’s comments considered so outrageous?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The outrage at Todd Akin is real, but it is mostly outrage at the wrong thing. His policy view — that abortion should be banned even in cases of rape — is held, openly, by a substantial bloc of the anti-abortion movement, by the drafters of the Republican platform now being finalized in Tampa, and by Paul Ryan, who co-sponsored legislation with Akin that sought to narrow the federal rape exception to "forcible rape." If holding that position were itself disqualifying, a great many sitting members of Congress would be disqualified too. The pretense that Akin has wandered off the reservation is, in that sense, a convenient fiction for a party that would rather not defend its platform in a general election.

What was genuinely outrageous was the factual claim wrapped around the policy: that women's bodies have some mechanism to "shut that whole thing down" during a "legitimate rape," and the phrase "legitimate rape" itself. The first is a piece of folk pseudoscience — a version of which has circulated in anti-abortion literature for decades, traceable to physicians like John Willke — that any obstetrician will tell you is false. Rape produces pregnancy at roughly the same rate as consensual intercourse; the studies put the figure around 5 percent per incident among women of reproductive age. The second is worse, because "legitimate" implies its opposite: that some large category of rape claims are illegitimate, exaggerated, or invented. That is an old and ugly idea, and it is the premise that makes a no-exceptions abortion policy emotionally tolerable to its advocates. If most rape isn't really rape, then denying those women abortions costs less on the conscience.

So Akin did his party a backhanded favor: he said out loud the assumption that usually stays implicit. Mitt Romney, Reince Priebus, and Senate Republicans are scrambling not because they disagree with Akin's conclusion — many of them have voted for it — but because he exposed the reasoning. Voters who are comfortable with a rape exception are not necessarily comfortable with the proposition that rape victims are mostly lying. That is why the comments are treated as outrageous, and why, on that narrower ground, they deserve to be.

The outrage over Representative Todd Akin’s comments is not rooted in the extremity of his policy prescription—banning abortion without a rape exception—but in the callous and pseudoscientific rationale he offered to justify it. Many abortion opponents, including prominent Republicans, advocate for exactly that ban, and the party’s platform draft reflects a hard-line stance. What transforms Akin’s remarks from a predictable shift rightward into a moment of scandal is the linguistic and factual framework he deployed: the concept of “legitimate rape” and the medically fraudulent assertion that such trauma rarely results in pregnancy. This language does not simply advance a political goal; it resurrects a deeply misogynistic tradition of scrutinizing and discrediting female victims of sexual violence.

First, the phrase “legitimate rape” injects into public discourse the toxic implication that there is a category of illegitimate, or false, rape. This distinction feeds the pernicious myth that many women fabricate or invite sexual assault, pivoting the moral burden away from the perpetrator and onto the victim’s credibility. By suggesting that only certain rapes are “legitimate,” Akin questions the veracity and character of rape survivors, a stance that medical, legal, and social-service communities have systematically rejected for decades. Rape is defined by the absence of consent, not by the degree of physical force or the relationship between victim and aggressor. The attempt to narrow the definition to “forcible rape,” as seen in earlier legislative language co-authored by Representative Paul Ryan, reveals a similar impulse to parse victims into worthy and unworthy categories. Akin’s comments stripped away the coded legalism to expose the raw, judgmental core of that logic, and the public recoiled from seeing it so plainly.

However, the most floridly outrageous element is his use of junk science to serve a moral agenda. The claim that a woman’s body has biological ways to “shut that whole thing down” during a rape is entirely unsupported by medical evidence. Emergency rooms and public-health research demonstrate that rape results in pregnancy at rates comparable to, or even higher than, consensual intercourse, due to factors like ovulation timing. By asserting otherwise, Akin not only perpetuates a dangerous falsehood but erases the lived reality of the thousands of women who conceive from rape each year. The implication is a form of double trauma: first the assault, then the political dismissal of its consequences based on fabricated biology. The bipartisan revulsion was, at its core, a defense of basic human dignity against an ideologue willing to invent facts to impose his conscience on the bodies of others. The outrage is not a reaction to a stylistic gaffe; it is a condemnation of a worldview that sees women’s experiences as subject to political revisionism.

While it is true that Representative Todd Akin’s underlying policy stance—opposing abortion without exceptions for rape or incest—aligns with a significant wing of the Republican Party and its draft platform, the intense, bipartisan outrage directed at him is not strictly about his legislative goals. Rather, the backlash stems from the profound medical ignorance of his statement and the deeply offensive terminology he used to categorize sexual assault.

First, Akin did not merely defend a strict anti-abortion position on moral or religious grounds; he attempted to justify it using biological pseudoscience. By claiming that "the female body has ways to try to shut that whole thing down" to prevent pregnancy during a rape, he promoted a medically baseless myth. This assertion is not only factually incorrect but carries a devastating implication for victims of sexual assault: if a woman does become pregnant from an assault, her body must not have "shut it down," suggesting she secretly consented or that the assault was not genuine. This transforms a policy debate into an exercise in victim-blaming.

Second, the phrase "legitimate rape" strikes at the heart of how society views victims of sexual violence. While some lawmakers previously attempted to use the term "forcible rape" to narrow federal funding parameters—a move that also faced severe pushback—Akin’s phrasing was far more visceral. It suggested a worldview in which women routinely fabricate claims of rape to bypass abortion restrictions. Categorizing rape into "legitimate" and, by implication, "illegitimate" forms minimizes the trauma of survivors and reinforces harmful stigmas that advocates have spent decades trying to dismantle.

Finally, the political context of August 2012 cannot be ignored. The Republican Party is entering the final stretch of a tight presidential race, attempting to focus the national dialogue on economic recovery and government spending. Mitt Romney’s recent selection of Paul Ryan as his running mate was intended to pivot the conversation toward fiscal policy. Akin’s comments abruptly derailed this strategy, handing Democrats potent ammunition to reinforce their campaign narratives. The immediate, forceful condemnation from Republican leadership—including calls for Akin to step down from the Missouri Senate race—reflects an urgent need to distance the national ticket from this specific, highly toxic rhetoric before the upcoming convention.

Ultimately, Americans routinely debate the morality and legality of abortion bans in cases of rape. However, attempting to justify those bans by redefining sexual assault and inventing human biology crosses the line from a difficult policy debate into an undeniable source of outrage.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Todd Akin’s comments are outrageous not because he opposes abortion after rape. That position, however severe, is a familiar and internally consistent one among some abortion opponents: if one believes abortion is the taking of innocent human life, then the circumstances of conception do not change the fetus’s moral status. Voters can judge that view on its merits.

What made Akin’s statement different was the way he tried to make the rape exception disappear. He did not simply say, “I believe abortion is wrong even in cases of rape.” He said that in cases of “legitimate rape,” pregnancy is rare because “the female body has ways to try to shut that whole thing down.” That is not a moral argument. It is a false biological claim used to soften the apparent harshness of a political position.

The phrase “legitimate rape” is itself a serious problem. It suggests that some reported rapes are not real enough to count, and that women seeking abortions after rape may need to pass a test of credibility before their suffering is recognized. This is not an academic concern. Rape is already among the most underreported crimes, in part because victims fear disbelief, scrutiny and humiliation. When a Senate candidate casually invokes a category of “legitimate” rape, he reinforces precisely the suspicion that keeps many victims silent.

The medical claim is also indefensible. Women can and do become pregnant from rape. Studies before 2012 have estimated thousands of rape-related pregnancies in the United States each year. Stress, trauma and violence do not create a reliable contraceptive mechanism. To say otherwise is not merely a gaffe; it is an attempt to use junk science to justify public policy.

That is why the comparison to the Republican platform and to the “forcible rape” language in the House bill cuts both ways. Akin’s remarks are not an isolated meteor from nowhere. They reveal the assumptions embedded in efforts to narrow rape exceptions: that government can distinguish “real” rape from lesser rape, that some victims are suspect, and that abortion law should turn on invasive judgments about a woman’s experience of violence. The outrage is partly because Akin said bluntly what more polished language often conceals.

There is also a political reason for the reaction. Parties prefer abstractions: “human life amendment,” “no taxpayer funding,” “exceptions only in cases of rape.” Akin forced the public to confront what enforcement would mean. If rape exceptions are narrowed, who decides whether the rape was forcible, legitimate or credible? Police? Doctors? Prosecutors? Legislators? And what happens to the woman who cannot prove enough, quickly enough?

So yes, Akin’s comments were outrageous. Not because they were wholly alien to current anti-abortion politics, but because they combined bad science, suspicion of rape victims and a policy agenda that would give those suspicions legal force. That combination deserves outrage.

He confirmed the fears of those who suspect that the endgame of the anti-abortion movement is control without compromise, and he did so at the worst possible moment, in the most damaging possible context. Political movements survive on ambiguity; Akin stripped it away.

The outrage is not really about the policy. It is about the biology.

Todd Akin did not just say he opposed abortion in cases of rape. Plenty of politicians say that, including his party's vice presidential nominee, and the position has a coherent (if to my mind wrong) moral logic behind it: if the fetus is a person, the circumstances of conception do not change that. You can disagree without thinking the person who holds the view is a crank.

What Akin did was different. He invoked a folk myth — that a woman's body can somehow "shut that whole thing down" during a "legitimate rape" — to argue that the rape exception is essentially unnecessary because rape rarely produces pregnancy. This is false. The best epidemiological estimates put rape-related pregnancy at roughly five percent per assault among reproductive-age women, which extrapolates to tens of thousands of pregnancies a year in the United States. Holly Smith and others published this work in the 1990s; it is not obscure.

So Akin offered a policy position propped up by a fabricated physiological mechanism, and in doing so he implied something even uglier: that if a woman did get pregnant, perhaps her rape was not "legitimate." The phrase functions as a back-door way of doubting rape victims wholesale. That is why women's groups exploded, and that is why Republican strategists from Karl Rove on down spent Monday trying to push him out of the race before sundown.

The question posed here — why is Akin singled out when his policy is in the GOP mainstream? — confuses two distinct things. The platform plank banning abortion without exceptions is a position one can argue about within the normal range of democratic disagreement. Akin's statement is a piece of misinformation about women's bodies, repeated by a sitting member of the House Science Committee, used to dismiss the experience of rape victims. Those are not equivalent.

That said, the controversy does have a useful side effect. It is forcing the public to notice that the no-exceptions position — long treated as a fringe view — is in fact written into the draft Republican platform and reflected in Paul Ryan's legislative record on "forcible rape." Akin made the subtext text. If voters now ask Mitt Romney and Ryan how their stated positions differ from Akin's in substance rather than tone, that is a debate worth having.

The pseudoscience is what made Akin radioactive. The policy is what should make him newsworthy.

Let us try, for a moment, to take Todd Akin's comments in the spirit they were probably offered, rather than the spirit in which they have been received.

Akin, asked a gotcha question on a local television show, fumbled for a way to defend a position he sincerely holds: that human life begins at conception and deserves protection regardless of how it was conceived. Reaching, awkwardly, for something he had apparently heard from a sympathetic doctor years ago, he claimed that pregnancies from rape are rare because the female body has defenses against them. He used the phrase "legitimate rape" — clearly meaning, in context, "actual forcible rape as distinguished from statutory or falsely alleged rape," a distinction that exists in the criminal law itself.

He was wrong on the biology, and he has said so. He apologized within twenty-four hours, acknowledged that rape causes pregnancy, and asked forgiveness. In any other context that would count for something. Instead the entire political class, including most of his own party, demanded his immediate withdrawal from a Senate race he had legitimately won in a contested primary.

The disproportion is striking. Vice President Biden told an audience of African-Americans this same month that Republicans were going to "put y'all back in chains," and the response was a shrug and a news cycle. Akin says something foolish about reproductive biology and is treated as beyond the moral pale.

What the episode really reveals is the impossibility, in our current discourse, of a serious public conversation about abortion absent exceptions. The position that an unborn child should not be put to death for the crime of his or her father is not eccentric. It has been the position of the Catholic Church for centuries, of most evangelical denominations, and, as the question itself notes, of the current Republican platform. Roughly one-fifth of Americans tell pollsters they oppose abortion in all circumstances. They include rape survivors who chose life and now advocate for the same choice for others.

One can disagree with that position — many thoughtful people do — without pretending it is unspeakable. The genuinely outrageous element in Akin's remarks was the pseudoscience, and he has retracted it. The underlying moral conviction is shared by millions of his constituents. They are entitled to a candidate who represents it, and to a press that engages it rather than running it out of town on a rail.

The most revealing word in Todd Akin's now-infamous sentence was not "rape." It was "legitimate."
