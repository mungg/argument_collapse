Can Farms Survive Without Drying Up California?

California’s first mandatory water restrictions ordered by Gov. Jerry Brown, in response to a four-year drought, did not affect agriculture because, Brown said, farmers had sacrificed enough.

But as irrigation, which uses as much as 80 percent of California’s surface water, has begun to dry up even the water beneath the surface of the state’s fertile valleys, what can be done to limit the agricultural use of water in California?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

California's drought lays bare a pricing problem more than a scarcity problem. Agriculture consumes roughly 80 percent of developed surface water to produce about 2 percent of state GDP, and much of that water is delivered at prices set decades ago under federal and state contracts that bear no relationship to current scarcity. When water is effectively free at the margin, farmers plant almonds, pistachios and alfalfa — thirsty perennials and low-value forage crops, much of the latter exported to China — and pump groundwater to make up shortfalls. The result is the slow-motion collapse of Central Valley aquifers, with subsidence already damaging canals and well casings.

The first step is to actually enforce the 2014 Sustainable Groundwater Management Act now, not on its leisurely 2040 timeline. Aquifers are common-pool resources, and unmetered pumping is the textbook tragedy. Every significant well should be metered, and extractions capped within each basin.

Second, build real water markets. Allow farmers with senior rights to sell or lease water to higher-value uses, including environmental flows, with transparent pricing. This rewards conservation without expropriating rights.

Third, retire the most marginal irrigated acreage — the salt-damaged west side of the San Joaquin Valley, drainage-impaired lands that should never have been irrigated under the Central Valley Project. Targeted buyouts would be cheaper than the tunnels and desalination plants otherwise required.

Cities tearing out lawns is reasonable symbolism, but until alfalfa and almond growers face the true cost of water, California will keep mining its future.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

California’s farms are not doomed, but their survival hinges on abandoning the myth that they can keep consuming water as if drought were an aberration rather than the new normal. The governor’s decision to exempt agriculture from mandatory cutbacks may have felt politically necessary, yet it ignored a blunt reality: using 80 percent of the state’s developed surface water for irrigation while groundwater tables plummet is a trajectory toward collapse, not resilience.

The path forward demands a rapid, widespread shift to precision irrigation—drip systems, soil-moisture sensors, and micro-sprinklers—which can reduce on-farm water use by a quarter or more while maintaining yields. Equally important is crop selection. It makes little sense to expand water-thirsty nut orchards on marginal land when deficit irrigation strategies for wine grapes or switching to drought-tolerant feed crops could stretch supplies. Markets can help: properly priced water trading already allows municipalities to lease water from rice farmers in wetter years, but those mechanisms must be made permanent and flexible enough to keep high-value perennial crops alive during severe shortages.

Groundwater regulation, long the third rail of California water politics, is finally beginning to take shape under the 2014 Sustainable Groundwater Management Act. That law provides the framework for local agencies to cap pumping at sustainable levels, but its success depends on forcing hard trade-offs soon, not postponing them for two decades. Farms can survive, even prosper, but only if the state and agricultural leaders treat water as the finite, shared resource it actually is, not a birthright to be drained until it’s gone.

California can keep farming, but not by pretending that every acre and every crop has an equal claim on a shrinking commons. The drought has exposed a system built for a wetter century: subsidized surface deliveries where available, largely unmetered groundwater where not, and weak signals about scarcity. When rivers fail, pumping shifts the damage underground, lowering aquifers, collapsing land and leaving poorer rural communities with dry wells.

The first priority is to make groundwater management real, not symbolic. California’s new groundwater law is an important start, but its deadlines are too leisurely for basins already in overdraft. Pumping should be measured and reported, and critically overdrafted basins should face interim caps tied to recharge, with protections for drinking-water wells and ecosystems.

Second, water should be priced and traded more honestly. Farmers who save water should be able to sell or lease it, but only after accounting for third-party effects: return flows, aquifer depletion and harm to fish and downstream users. Markets can move water from low-value uses to higher-value crops without simply rewarding those who have historically held the strongest rights.

Third, conservation must go beyond drip irrigation. Drip can raise yields but also encourage more planting and reduce water returning to streams or aquifers. The real measure is net consumption. In some places, that means fallowing marginal land, shifting away from the thirstiest low-value crops, and reconsidering permanent orchards that demand water even in the worst years.

Cities should conserve too, but urban cutbacks alone cannot solve an agricultural water problem. Farms can survive if California manages scarcity openly, measures use accurately and accepts that some land should not be irrigated in every year.

The premise embedded in this question deserves scrutiny. It assumes an inherent zero-sum conflict between agricultural viability and California's water future, but this framing obscures the real policy choices ahead. Farms can survive — and California can preserve more of its water — but only if we abandon the assumption that current practices are either fixed or optimal.

By 2015, agricultural water efficiency had improved dramatically in some sectors while remaining abysses in others. Drip irrigation, once a luxury, had become standard for many permanent crops. Yet flood irrigation still dominated significant acreage, and water pricing rarely reflected true scarcity. The political calculus that exempted agriculture from Brown's restrictions reflected more than fairness claims; it exposed how thoroughly California's water governance had been captured by agribusiness interests over generations.

Survival for agriculture doesn't require maintaining the status quo. It requires adaptation: shifting toward higher-value, lower-water crops in appropriate regions; investing in storage and recycling infrastructure; accepting groundwater management that previous generations rejected. The Central Valley's political class has resisted these changes partly because the current system enriches large landowners at the expense of both small farmers and environmental sustainability.

California's farms will survive some version of water constraint. The question is whether they'll adapt proactively — guided by policy that prices water honestly and regulates extraction fairly — or reactively, through crisis and collapse. The latter outcome isn't "farms drying up California"; it's shortsighted governance making that outcome more likely. The drought offers an opportunity to build smarter water policy. Whether California's political institutions can seize it remains the genuine uncertainty.

Price the Water, and the Rest Will Follow

The reason California agriculture uses 80 percent of the state's developed water while contributing roughly 2 percent of its GDP is not a mystery: water is grossly underpriced, and in many cases effectively free. Senior water-rights holders pay pennies for acre-feet that urban users would pay hundreds of dollars for. Groundwater, until last year's Sustainable Groundwater Management Act, was a literal free-for-all. When you give a resource away, people use too much of it. Economics 101.

Governor Brown's decision to exempt agriculture from mandatory cuts is politically understandable but analytically indefensible. Lawns in Beverly Hills are an easy target, but a Beverly Hills lawn uses a rounding error of what a single almond orchard does. If we are serious about matching demand to a shrinking supply, the price signal has to reach the people who control the demand.

The cleanest mechanism is a robust water market. Establish clear, tradable rights, cap total withdrawals at sustainable levels, and let farmers decide whether it makes more sense to grow alfalfa for export to China or to sell their allocation to a city or a higher-value crop. Australia's Murray-Darling basin reforms, imperfect as they are, demonstrate the basic model works. Farmers who can grow tomatoes or pistachios with drip irrigation will bid water away from those still flood-irrigating cotton in the desert. Marginal acreage will fallow, as it should.

Critics will say markets are heartless, that water is a human right, that family farms will lose. But the current system is not preserving family farms — it is subsidizing Wall Street-backed almond conglomerates whose groundwater pumping is collapsing the aquifer beneath everyone else. A properly designed market, with carve-outs for drinking water and ecosystem flows, is more equitable than the existing regime of first-in-time, first-in-right.

California's drought is a preview of its climate future. The question is not whether agriculture will use less water — physics will eventually impose that — but whether the adjustment happens through deliberate policy or through aquifer collapse and rural ruin.

Don't Blame the Almond

It has become fashionable to point fingers at California farmers, and especially at almonds, as if a single nut were responsible for the state's predicament. This is convenient scapegoating that lets urban Californians off the hook and ignores the genuine value of what the Central Valley produces.

California grows roughly half of the fruits, vegetables, and nuts consumed in the United States. It is not optional infrastructure; it is the reason American families can afford fresh produce year-round. The same Mediterranean climate that draws people to live in coastal cities is what makes the valley agriculturally unique on the continent. If we drive farming out of California with punitive water policy, that production does not vanish — it moves to Chile, Mexico, or simply disappears from American plates at affordable prices.

The 80 percent figure that gets repeated endlessly is also misleading. It refers to "developed" water, excluding the roughly half of the state's water that flows to environmental purposes. Once you count environmental flows — much of which serves legitimate ecological goals but some of which arguably does not — agriculture's share looks closer to 40 percent. That changes the moral arithmetic considerably.

Farmers have, in fact, sacrificed. Hundreds of thousands of acres were fallowed last year. Surface water deliveries from the federal Central Valley Project to many farms were zero. Workers in Mendota and Huron are unemployed. Brown was not being soft on agriculture; he was acknowledging reality on the ground.

Yes, groundwater overdraft is a serious problem and the new groundwater law was overdue. Yes, drip irrigation should be universal. Yes, some crops are poorly matched to the climate we now have. But the answer is not to dismantle California agriculture in a panic. It is to invest in storage, recycling, and conveyance; to fund the technology transition; and to let farmers adapt as they have always done. The almond is not the enemy.

Fix Groundwater First, Everything Else Second

The most alarming sentence in the prompt is the offhand observation that irrigation has begun "drying up even the water beneath the surface." That is the real emergency. Surface droughts come and go. Aquifer depletion is, on human timescales, permanent. Subsidence in the San Joaquin Valley is now measured in feet per year in some areas, collapsing the very pore space that once held water. Wells are going dry, forcing rural communities to truck in drinking water.

California passed the Sustainable Groundwater Management Act last September, and it is a meaningful step. But its timeline is glacial: local agencies have until 2017 to form, until 2020 or 2022 to write plans, and until 2040 to bring basins into balance. Twenty-five years is the blink of an eye for hydrologists and an eternity for a depleting aquifer. The pumping will continue, accelerated by the drought, and by the time enforcement bites, irreplaceable storage will be gone.

Sacramento should compress that timeline aggressively, at least in critically overdrafted basins. Require metering of all agricultural wells now — astonishingly, most are unmetered. Cap withdrawals in the worst basins on an emergency basis, the way fisheries are closed when stocks collapse. Provide state cost-sharing for managed aquifer recharge, which can turn flood years into bank deposits.

Surface water restrictions are theater compared to this. You can take shorter showers and rip out your lawn, and you will not save the Tulare basin. The basin is being mined by a relatively small number of large operators with deep wells and deeper pockets, and the cost of their pumping is being externalized onto neighbors whose shallower wells run dry first.

This is a classic tragedy of the commons, and the solution is classic too: define rights, measure use, enforce limits. Everything else — markets, crop choices, efficiency mandates — is downstream of getting the groundwater accounting right.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Rethink What We Grow

Behind the water debate lies a question California has avoided for a century: what should this land actually produce? The cropping pattern in the Central Valley is not a natural outcome; it is the product of subsidies, export markets, and water rights that bear little relationship to scarcity. As long as that is true, no efficiency gain will be enough — because farmers will simply use the saved water to plant more thirsty crops.

Consider the math. Alfalfa is California's largest water-using crop, much of it exported to dairies in China and the Gulf states. We are, in effect, exporting California water in baled form. Cotton, another desert-incompatible crop, still occupies meaningful acreage in the Tulare basin. Almonds and pistachios have exploded over the past decade because their returns are high — and unlike row crops, they cannot be fallowed in a dry year without killing the investment, which locks in demand for water regardless of conditions.

This is a portfolio problem. A state facing chronic water scarcity should be biased toward annual crops that can flex with conditions, toward crops with high value per gallon, and away from permanent plantings that require water no matter what the sky does. Yet policy has done the opposite: cheap water and weak groundwater rules have encouraged precisely the most rigid and thirsty cropping patterns.

The solution does not require Soviet-style crop assignments. It requires removing the distortions. Charge realistic prices for water. Enforce groundwater limits. Let permanent plantings compete on equal terms with row crops, and watch some of those almond orchards never get replanted when their productive life ends. Tie federal crop insurance and disaster aid to water-realistic crops.

California will still be an agricultural powerhouse. It will simply grow different things, and grow them where they make sense — tomatoes and lettuce on the coast, perhaps, and a leaner footprint of high-value perennials where groundwater can sustain them. That is not the end of farming. It is its maturation.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The Drought Is a Symptom; Climate Change Is the Disease

Discussions of California's water crisis too often treat the current drought as a discrete event — bad luck that will end when the rains return. The science suggests otherwise. Tree-ring studies indicate that the 20th century, the century during which California's water system was designed, was unusually wet by the standards of the past two thousand years. Megadroughts of decades-long duration are part of the region's natural variability, and climate change is loading the dice toward more frequent and severe dry periods, less snowpack, and earlier runoff.

If that framing is right, then asking how to "limit agricultural water use" in the drought is the wrong question. The right question is how to redesign California's agricultural and water system for a climate that no longer matches the assumptions of 1913 or 1960. That means assuming, as a planning baseline, that there will be substantially less water available on average — perhaps 20 to 30 percent less Sierra runoff by mid-century — and structurally less in dry years.

Practically, this implies several things at once. Storage strategy must shift from surface reservoirs (which depend on snowpack we will not have) toward aquifer recharge (which captures the more episodic, flashy precipitation we will get). Agricultural acreage in the most water-stressed basins must contract, and the contraction should be planned rather than chaotic. Water rights, frozen in their 19th-century form, must be reformed to allow adjustment to a moving baseline. And — politically hardest — Californians must accept that the population, agricultural output, and lawns supported by 20th-century hydrology cannot all be supported by 21st-century hydrology.

Brown's emergency measures are appropriate as immediate crisis response. But they are not a strategy. A strategy would acknowledge that the drought is not an interruption of normal. It is the new normal, or the early stages of it, and California's farms — like its cities — need to be redesigned accordingly.

Let Farmers Lead, With the Right Incentives

Most commentary on this topic, including some of what surrounds it on this page, assumes that farmers are the problem and that bureaucrats and economists have the solution. As someone who has spent time in the Central Valley, I would suggest the opposite is closer to the truth. Farmers know their land, their crops, and their water better than anyone in Sacramento. The question is not how to constrain them but how to give them the right incentives and tools.

Consider what has already happened, largely without mandates. Drip and micro-sprinkler irrigation now cover roughly 40 percent of California cropland, up from near zero a generation ago. Precision agriculture, soil moisture sensors, regulated deficit irrigation in orchards — these have all been adopted by farmers responding to higher water costs and tighter supplies. California agriculture is, by global standards, remarkably efficient.

Where the policy environment matters is in three places. First, water transfers: it remains shockingly difficult to move water from a willing seller to a willing buyer because of legal and infrastructure barriers. Streamline transfers, and farmers will move water to its highest use without bureaucrats picking winners. Second, groundwater: clear, enforceable, tradable rights would convert the current race-to-the-bottom into a sustainable regime, and most farmers I know would welcome it, because they suffer from their neighbors' pumping too. Third, infrastructure: California has not built significant water storage or conveyance in decades, even as its population has doubled. That is a policy failure, not a farmer failure.

Mandates on what to plant, where, and how would be a disaster. They would politicize every cropping decision and create a regulatory thicket that benefits the largest, most lawyered-up operators. Get the property rights right, fix the infrastructure, and let the people who actually grow food make the decisions. They will surprise you.

Cities, Stop Pretending This Isn't About You

The conversation about California water has settled into a comfortable shape: agriculture uses 80 percent, so agriculture must be where the cuts come. That arithmetic is correct but the conclusion is too easy. It lets urban and suburban California — the political majority — treat itself as a bystander to a farm problem.

Consider who eats the food. Almonds get pilloried, but they are not consumed mainly by farmers. They are consumed by the same Bay Area and Southern California residents who are outraged by the drought. The dairy that drinks the alfalfa produces the milk in urban refrigerators. If Californians want their state's agriculture to shrink dramatically, fine — but they should be honest that this means importing food from places with their own water and labor problems, not that it means a free lunch.

Meanwhile, urban water use is not as innocent as the comparison to agriculture suggests. Per-capita residential use in much of California remains among the highest in the developed world, driven by lawns, pools, and decorative landscaping that produce no food and little ecological value. Brown's 25 percent urban cut is welcome but overdue, and modest by international standards. Israel, facing comparable scarcity, achieves residential use roughly a third of California's, while reusing some 85 percent of wastewater.

A serious response to the drought would treat farms and cities as a coupled system. Urban water recycling and stormwater capture could free up Delta supplies for agriculture or environment. Eliminating ornamental turf in California's hot interior cities would save quantities of water that would be agriculturally meaningful. Pricing reform in cities — most of which still have absurdly low marginal water prices — would do for urban demand what it has only patchily done for agriculture.

The honest answer to the question posed is: yes, farms can survive without drying up California, but only if cities accept that they too must change. The drought is not somebody else's problem.
