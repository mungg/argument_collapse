Should the multibillion-dollar state pension industry be more transparent and less prone to influence and corruption?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Public pension funds manage trillions of dollars on behalf of teachers, firefighters, and other public servants. Beneficiaries and the taxpayers who backstop these plans have an unambiguous right to know how that money is invested, what it costs, and who is profiting from the arrangements. The case for radical transparency is overwhelming, and the standard objections to it do not survive scrutiny.

The usual defense of secrecy is that hedge funds, private equity firms, and other "alternative" managers demand confidentiality to protect proprietary strategies, and that pensions will be excluded from the best deals if they disclose terms. But the recent reporting by Pando and others, building on earlier work by the SEC's Andrew Bowden — who in a notable May speech described widespread fee abuse in private equity — suggests the opposite problem. Secrecy is not protecting trade secrets; it is concealing 2-and-20 fee structures, undisclosed monitoring fees, side letters favoring connected investors, and performance that, net of fees, often trails a plain index. CalPERS' own research has questioned whether hedge fund allocations earn their keep. If a manager truly cannot bear sunlight on basic terms, a fiduciary should ask why.

There is also a democratic dimension. State treasurers and pension trustees are often elected or politically appointed, and the flow of campaign contributions from placement agents and fund principals to those officials — documented in the New York and New Mexico pay-to-play scandals — shows how confidentiality clauses can shade into corruption. SEC pay-to-play Rule 206(4)-5 was a start, but enforcement remains thin.

A reasonable disclosure regime would require publication of every fund's fees, carried interest terms, net-of-fee returns, and the identity of any placement agents and their compensation. Genuinely proprietary positions can be reported on a lag. None of this prevents pensions from investing in alternatives; it merely forces them to do so on terms they can defend in daylight. Public money demands public accounting.

The fiscal strain on public pensions is undeniable. With aging workforces and lackluster returns from traditional bonds, fund managers were under intense pressure to seek higher yields. Shifting assets into hedge funds and private equity, which promise enhanced returns and diversification, was a rational response to a low-interest-rate world. However, the condition of strict confidentiality that often accompanies these “alternative investments” has opened the door to a troubling erosion of public trust. When pension funds demand that fee structures and partnership terms remain hidden, they remove the most basic check on mismanagement: public scrutiny. Without transparency, it is impossible for taxpayers and retirees to know whether millions are being siphoned off in placement agent fees to individuals who have cultivated cozy relationships with board members. The pay-to-play scandals that have roiled funds like CalPERS are not anomalies; they are the logical outcome of a system where dealmakers can operate in the shadows. The Securities and Exchange Commission’s 2010 pay-to-play rule was a necessary response, but it only addresses a narrow slice of the problem. Real accountability requires that investment contracts—including the true cost of fees and the identities of all intermediaries—be open records, not proprietary secrets.

A pension fund’s fiduciary duty is to its beneficiaries, not to the convenience of Wall Street. When citizens suspect that their contributions are being funneled to politically connected insiders, support for funding these obligations evaporates. Transparency is not merely a safeguard against corruption; it is the foundation for preserving the social compact that underpins defined-benefit pensions in the first place. The multibillion-dollar state pension industry must choose: honor its public mission with open books, or continue to erode confidence one secret deal at a time.

Sunlight Is the Only Defense Beneficiaries Have

Public pension funds exist for one reason: to pay retirement benefits to teachers, firefighters, police officers, and other public servants. They are not venture capital vehicles for politically connected financiers, and they are not slush funds for governors who want to reward campaign donors with lucrative management contracts. Yet that, increasingly, is what they have become. The recent reporting by David Sirota at Pando and by others at the Times and ProPublica has documented a disturbing pattern: trustees signing nondisclosure agreements that prevent the public — and sometimes even the beneficiaries themselves — from learning what fees are being paid, what returns are being earned, and who is collecting the carry.

This is indefensible. Public money demands public accounting. When CalPERS or the New Jersey Division of Investment or the Rhode Island state treasury hands a billion dollars to a hedge fund or private equity firm, the citizens of that state are the limited partners, not some abstract institution. They are entitled to know the terms. The standard industry rebuttal — that disclosure would compromise proprietary strategies and scare off the best managers — is largely a bluff. The "best managers" have, on average, underperformed cheap index funds since the financial crisis once their 2-and-20 fee structures are accounted for. If a fund manager refuses to accept public money on transparent terms, the appropriate response is to send the money elsewhere, not to launder away the public's right to know.

The corruption angle is even more troubling. Pay-to-play scandals in New York under Alan Hevesi, in New Mexico, in Illinois, and elsewhere have shown that the placement-agent ecosystem around pension allocations is a magnet for self-dealing. Secrecy is what makes that ecosystem possible. When a placement agent earns millions for "introducing" a state treasurer to a fund that the treasurer was already going to choose, the only thing standing between the public and outrage is the contractual gag order.

Reforms are not complicated. States should require: full disclosure of fees, both management and performance, on a net and gross basis; disclosure of placement agents and their compensation; public reporting of fund-by-fund returns benchmarked against passive alternatives; and a prohibition on confidentiality clauses that override state public-records laws. Several states have moved in this direction, and the sky has not fallen on their investment programs.

The pension funding crisis is real, and the temptation to chase yield through opaque alternatives is understandable. But trading transparency for the promise of alpha has, on the evidence, delivered neither. Beneficiaries deserve better stewardship, and stewardship begins with sunlight.

Transparency Is a Slogan, Not a Strategy

Everyone is in favor of transparency in the abstract, in the same way everyone is in favor of efficiency and good government. But the current campaign against "secret pension deals," driven largely by activists who oppose alternative investments on ideological grounds, conflates several distinct issues and proposes a cure that may be worse than the disease.

Start with the basic economics. Most large public pension funds are underfunded, and they carry assumed rates of return — typically 7.5 to 8 percent — that cannot plausibly be hit with a traditional 60/40 portfolio in an era when investment-grade bonds yield 2 to 3 percent. Trustees have a fiduciary duty to try to close that gap. That means moving up the risk curve, and it means accessing asset classes — private equity, real estate, distressed credit, certain hedge fund strategies — where the best performers do not need or want public capital and will simply refuse to take it on terms that broadcast their positions to competitors.

The confidentiality demanded by these managers is not a sinister cover-up; it is a commercial reality. A private equity firm that publishes its quarterly portfolio company valuations hands a roadmap to competitors and counterparties. A hedge fund that discloses its positions in real time invites front-running. When state legislatures have tried to mandate granular disclosure — Texas and California have wrestled with this — top-quartile managers have responded by capping or excluding public investors. The result: state pensions end up stuck with the second-tier funds that will accept the disclosure terms, which is precisely the opposite of what beneficiaries need.

This does not mean anything goes. There is a meaningful distinction between disclosure to the public and disclosure to the right oversight bodies. Fees paid, aggregate returns, the identity of managers, conflicts of interest involving trustees, and the use of placement agents should all be public. Position-level holdings and proprietary terms in side letters generally should not be. Pay-to-play abuses, where they occur, are already illegal under SEC Rule 206(4)-5 and state ethics laws; the answer is enforcement, not blanket disclosure regimes that punish legitimate managers along with the bad actors.

The Pando reporting has done a service by raising questions, but it has also tended to paint with too broad a brush, treating any alternative investment as suspect by definition. The real debate is not transparency versus secrecy. It is how to design oversight regimes that catch corruption without crippling the ability of pension trustees to do the job their beneficiaries are counting on them to do.

The Problem Isn't Secrecy. It's the Promises.

The transparency debate around public pension investments, while not unimportant, is a sideshow that lets the real culprits off the hook. State and local pension funds did not start chasing hedge funds and private equity because trustees suddenly developed exotic tastes. They did it because legislators and governors, over decades, promised retirement benefits they had no intention of funding honestly, and assumed rates of return high enough to paper over the gap on paper.

Look at the arithmetic. Across the fifty states, the unfunded liability is estimated at somewhere between $1 trillion (using the funds' own assumptions) and $3-plus trillion (using risk-free discount rates favored by financial economists). New Jersey has skipped or shorted its pension contributions almost every year since the Whitman administration. Illinois has effectively run its pension system as a credit card for general operating expenses. Kentucky, Connecticut, and Rhode Island are not far behind. When you are this far underwater and you cannot raise taxes or cut benefits politically, the only remaining lever is to bet the portfolio. Hence the rush into "alternatives."

Once you understand that, the transparency conversation looks different. Yes, the secrecy around alternative investment terms hides excessive fees, mediocre returns, and sometimes outright corruption. All of those should be exposed. But even if every fee were posted on a public website tomorrow, the underlying problem — that pensions are structurally insolvent and management has been pushed into Hail Mary investing — would remain. You can disclose your way to a fairer pricing of hedge fund services. You cannot disclose your way out of a $1 trillion funding hole.

There is also a perverse dynamic at work. Greater transparency, by exposing how lousy a deal alternatives often are, will likely accelerate calls to abandon those strategies. Fine. But then trustees still have to hit 7.5 percent somehow. If they retreat to bonds, the funding gap widens immediately on an actuarial basis, forcing larger contributions from strapped state budgets or, more likely, benefit cuts for retirees. The political class would much rather have an opaque hedge fund to blame than admit the math.

So by all means, end the gag orders, ban placement agent payola, publish the fees. But understand that transparency is a hygiene measure, not a solution. The real reform agenda is honest discount rates, ironclad funding requirements that politicians cannot skip, and an adult conversation with public workers about what their plans can actually deliver. Until that happens, we will keep arguing about the wrapping paper while the package rots.

A Modest Proposal: Treat Pension Trustees Like the Fiduciaries They Claim to Be

Lost in the heat of the transparency debate is a simple legal point. Trustees of public pension funds owe an undivided duty of loyalty to beneficiaries. That duty, articulated under ERISA-analog state laws and in centuries of trust jurisprudence, is not a vague aspiration. It requires trustees to act solely in the interest of plan participants, to avoid self-dealing, to monitor agents prudently, and — critically — to be able to document and defend every material decision they make.

A trustee who signs a nondisclosure agreement so restrictive that he or she cannot explain to beneficiaries what fees are being paid, what benchmark is being used, or who is profiting from a placement arrangement is arguably breaching that duty on its face. The "we can't tell you, it's confidential" defense would not fly in any private trust context. It should not fly here.

That suggests a different reform framing than the one we usually hear. Rather than legislate disclosure in granular detail — which invites endless lobbying and gamesmanship — strengthen fiduciary standards and enforce them. Specifically: (1) Require trustees to certify annually, under penalty of personal liability, that all material terms of every investment, including fees, side letters, and placement agent arrangements, have been reviewed and are prudent. (2) Create a private right of action for beneficiaries against trustees who execute confidentiality provisions that prevent meaningful oversight. (3) Empower state inspectors general or independent auditors — not the trustees themselves — to be the recipients of confidential commercial information, with statutory authority to report aggregate findings publicly.

This approach finesses the legitimate concerns about commercial sensitivity raised by alternative managers. The hedge fund that genuinely needs to keep its positions confidential can disclose to an independent auditor under a protective order, the way litigants do in court every day. What it cannot do is contract around the trustee's underlying duty of care and loyalty.

There is precedent for this. Several states have moved in this direction in fits and starts — New York after the Hevesi scandal, New Mexico after its placement agent debacle. The pattern is clear: scandal, brief reform, gradual backsliding as memories fade. Embedding the reforms in fiduciary law, rather than in transparency statutes that legislatures can quietly amend, makes the gains harder to reverse.

The pension system is a trust, in the deepest legal and moral sense. Trusts run on accountability, not on the goodwill of those who manage them. Restoring that accountability — not just publishing more documents — is the work that needs doing.

The instinct to demand full transparency from public pension funds is understandable. Revelations of hidden fees and political favoritism rightly outrage the public. The uncomfortable answer is that some strategic opacity is not just defensible but necessary, and the cure could be worse than the disease.

If a public pension fund were forced to disclose its commitments to a particular fund in real time, or reveal the specific positions that fund holds, it would give rivals an open book. Already, some elite funds, such as Sequoia Capital, have limited or avoided public pension capital precisely because of disclosure requirements. Pension boards, often stacked with political appointees and lacking financial expertise, negotiate complex contracts that they don’t fully understand. The result is exactly what critics decry: a culture where influence peddlers and fee-maximizing intermediaries thrive.

This body would have the power to blow the whistle publicly if abuses are found, but it would also be able to negotiate with top managers on equal footing.

Transparency is a means, not an end. The end is maximizing risk-adjusted returns for retirees, free from corruption. We can achieve that without adopting a simplistic “post everything online” policy that would drive away the very investment partners we need most.
