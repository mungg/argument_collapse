Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Singer's case for effective altruism is bracing precisely because it refuses to let us hide behind the warm glow. Matt Wage's calculation—that a moderately paid professional, tithing to bed-net distribution, can save roughly a hundred lives over a career—is the kind of arithmetic most donors carefully avoid performing. Singer is right that performing it should change behavior, and right that the gap between Make-A-Wish and the Against Malaria Foundation is not a matter of taste but of moral weight. Still, several features of the movement as Singer presents it deserve more pressure than he applies.

First, the epistemic foundations are shakier than the confident comparisons suggest. GiveWell itself, in its public reflections, has revised its cost-per-life-saved estimates repeatedly and warned against treating its numbers as precise. The figure of roughly $3,400 per life saved through the Against Malaria Foundation that circulates in effective altruist writing depends on assumptions about counterfactual funding, net usage, mortality baselines, and insecticide resistance, all of which are contested. This does not vindicate Make-A-Wish, but it should temper the rhetorical move from "we can measure" to "we know." A movement built on evidence and reason ought to wear its uncertainty more visibly than Singer does here.

Second, the "earning to give" path that Wage exemplifies is presented as a personal triumph, but it raises systemic questions Singer skirts. If arbitrage trading is itself a zero-sum or socially marginal activity, the altruist's donations are partly recycled from harms or rents imposed elsewhere—on counterparties, on a financial system whose 2008 crisis is still fresh. The calculation of net good has to include what one does for forty hours a week, not just where the paycheck ends up. Singer's brief response to the factory-owner case shows he understands this in principle; he does not apply the same scrutiny to Wall Street.

Third, Singer's dismissal of arts philanthropy and donations to elite universities is too quick. He concedes the Princeton-and-Yale point with a joke about endowments, but the deeper issue is that "doing the most good" tends, in practice, to privilege interventions whose outputs are countable—lives, QALYs, dollars of income. Justice, institutional reform, political organizing, and cultural change resist that metric and therefore quietly drop down the priority list. A framework that systematically undervalues what it cannot measure is not neutral evidence-following; it is a substantive ethical stance that deserves defense, not assertion.

Finally, Singer's framing of a "minimally acceptable ethical life" as requiring substantial donation is more demanding than he lets on. Telling readers that ordinary affluent decency falls below the moral minimum may be true, but it risks the very guilt-driven disengagement he says effective altruists avoid. The movement's most valuable contribution may be modest: making giving more thoughtful, not redefining what it means to be a decent person.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Peter Singer’s case for effective altruism is compelling in its clarity and moral urgency. The idea that we should use evidence and reason to do the most good possible with our spare resources seems hard to dispute. Who could argue against saving lives efficiently? Yet the movement, as Singer describes it, reveals a narrowness that worries me—not because it does nothing, but because of what it may leave undone and the political implications it quietly carries.

Effective altruism reimagines ethical living as a personal optimization problem. Matt Wage’s choice to work on Wall Street in order to donate more is praised as a rational trade-off. But this logic treats existing economic structures as neutral terrain rather than as forces that themselves cause vast suffering. A trading firm may generate profits by exploiting loopholes, fueling speculative bubbles, or deepening inequality—effects that could harm many more people than Wage’s donations help. Even if his particular work is harmless, the unquestioning acceptance of high finance as a mere platform for earning-to-give sends a powerful signal that career choices need not challenge unjust systems, only redirect some of their spoils. The danger is that effective altruism becomes a sophisticated way for privileged people to feel better about remaining within extractive institutions, so long as they write a check.

Singers’s discussion of Make-A-Wish versus malaria nets sharpens the concern. He contrasts the $7,500 cost of fulfilling a child’s wish with the number of lives that could be saved by bed nets. The calculation is emotionally stark, but it strips away dimensions of value that are not easily quantified. The wish of a dying child is not just a “warm glow” for donors; it can affirm the child’s humanity, bring joy to a family in distress, and strengthen community solidarity. Effective altruism’s metric of “lives saved” or “suffering averted” tends to flatten moral experience into a single currency. This may lead us to systematically underestimate the importance of dignity, relationships, and local agency—the very goods that make a life worth living in the first place.

Singer acknowledges that effective altruists are “not saints” and can give priority to their own children, but the concession sits awkwardly with the movement’s core demand to do the most good. If it is permissible to disproportionately benefit one’s own children, why not one’s own community or country? Once partiality is admitted, the logic of maximization weakens, and the door opens to a much thicker moral world where local ties and shared histories count. Effective altruism offers no principled way to weigh these competing loyalties except by appealing to a utilitarian calculus that, in other hands, has been used to justify terrible things, a history Singer himself warns against.

The movement also places enormous faith in the power of rational argument to reshape mass giving. Yet decades of research in moral psychology suggest that emotional engagement is not merely a bias to be overcome but a precondition for sustained ethical commitment. People who give to a child they see on television may also, over time, become more broadly empathetic citizens. By dismissing “warm glow” giving as less valuable, effective altruism risks alienating potential allies and creating a moral hierarchy where only the most data-driven donors count. This could narrow the philanthropic ecosystem and reduce the very diversity of approaches that long-term progress requires.

Finally, effective altruism largely ignores the role of collective political action. The most effective “charity” might be funding campaigns to reform tax codes, close tax havens, or demand structural changes that poor communities themselves identify. Such work rarely offers the clean cost-per-life-saved figure that GiveWell prizes, but it addresses root causes. Without a complementary focus on justice and democratic mobilization, effective altruism risks becoming a movement of well-meaning elites who alleviate symptoms while leaving the disease intact. Singer’s vision is an important corrective to thoughtless generosity, but left on its own, it offers a dangerously incomplete picture of what it means to make the world better.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Peter Singer’s articulation of effective altruism offers a necessary corrective to the often self-indulgent world of traditional philanthropy. He is entirely right that choosing to save three lives from malaria is objectively better than spending the same amount of money to fulfill one child’s wish to be Batman for a day. By demanding that altruism be guided by reason and rigorous evidence rather than mere "warm glow" emotional impulses, Singer provides a compelling ethical framework for individuals in the affluent world.

However, the logic of effective altruism, as Singer presents it, suffers from a profound blind spot regarding the structural and political roots of global poverty. By focusing almost exclusively on quantifiable, individualistic interventions, the movement risks treating the symptoms of global suffering while ignoring—or even becoming complicit in—the systemic forces that generate that suffering in the first place.

This structural blindness is most evident in Singer’s celebration of "earning to give." He praises Matt Wage for taking a lucrative job at a Wall Street arbitrage trading firm so that he can donate a large percentage of his income to effective charities. But this calculus evaluates the morality of a career solely by its philanthropic output, completely ignoring the economic footprint of the career itself. The global financial system is not a neutral wealth-generating machine; it is deeply implicated in the structural inequalities, debt crises, and resource exploitations that keep the developing world impoverished. If effective altruists flock to industries that extract wealth or exacerbate inequality, the harm they participate in systemically may far outweigh the localized good they achieve through their charitable donations. A Robin Hood ethic is insufficient if it requires propping up the Sheriff of Nottingham's economic system.

Furthermore, effective altruism’s heavy reliance on highly measurable, evidence-based interventions introduces a severe "measurement bias" into philanthropy. Organizations like GiveWell rightfully praise the Against Malaria Foundation because the cost-effectiveness of a bed net is easily quantified. But the most transformative changes in human history—the abolition of slavery, the civil rights movement, the establishment of the welfare state, the fight against systemic corruption—were messy, politically fraught, and impossible to evaluate through a randomized controlled trial. As scholars like Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson have compellingly argued, the primary driver of national prosperity and poverty alleviation is the development of inclusive political and economic institutions. By steering money only toward interventions with immediate, quantifiable metrics, effective altruism systematically under-values political advocacy, labor organizing, and structural reform, which are the actual engines of long-term human flourishing.

Finally, Singer’s framework borders on a kind of technocratic paternalism. It assumes that the best way to improve the world is for intelligent, wealthy individuals in the West to use their excess capital to buy solutions for the global poor, rather than supporting the poor in building their own democratic power. It treats the victims of extreme poverty as passive recipients of our optimized charity rather than as political agents who need structural empowerment.

Effective altruism is undoubtedly a step forward from giving based entirely on emotional resonance. Buying bed nets is a good and necessary thing to do. But if millennials want to do the "most good," they cannot settle for simply redistributing the spoils of an unequal global economy. They must also engage in the far more difficult, unquantifiable work of reforming the economic and political structures that make such charity necessary in the first place.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Peter Singer is right to insist that philanthropy should be answerable to evidence. If a donor can prevent blindness, malaria, or parasitic infection for a modest sum, it is morally evasive to pretend that every charitable gift is merely an expression of personal taste. The comparison between Make-A-Wish and bed nets is uncomfortable because it should be. Sentiment often tracks proximity, publicity, and national identity rather than need. Effective altruism usefully interrupts that bias.

But the phrase “the most good we can” does more work than Singer admits. It suggests a clean maximizing problem where the hardest questions are technical: which charity saves the most lives per dollar? Sometimes they are. Yet many of the deepest causes of suffering are political, institutional, and historical. Malaria nets matter enormously, but so do public health systems, land rights, tax policy, corruption, trade rules, patent regimes, climate policy, and war. These are harder to measure, and the causal chains are longer, but they are not therefore less important. A movement that strongly favors what can be quantified may end up mistaking measurability for moral significance.

Singer knows this danger, and effective altruists often discuss it. Still, the movement’s public face can make charity appear as the central arena of ethics for the affluent. That is too narrow. A hedge fund employee who gives half his income may save many lives, and that is admirable. But we should also ask what financial institutions do to the societies in which they operate. Some forms of arbitrage may add liquidity and efficiency; others may extract value, amplify volatility, or reward talents that could have been used elsewhere. “Earning to give” cannot be assessed only by the size of the donation. It must include the social effects of the earning.

There is also a democratic worry. Effective altruism often imagines the donor as a rational allocator of resources, surveying global needs from above. That perspective can correct parochialism, but it can also sideline the agency of the people being helped. A community may need vaccines, but it may also need political voice, reliable institutions, and freedom from domination. Those goods are not easily captured by cost-per-life-saved calculations. Nor are they simply “warm glow” preferences. Justice is not merely a feeling donors have; it is a claim people make on one another.

This matters especially because philanthropy is already shaped by inequality. When wealthy individuals decide which problems deserve attention, they exercise power without the accountability that accompanies democratic taxation and public spending. Singer is persuasive that giving to Princeton or the opera is often hard to defend while children die cheaply preventable deaths. But a world in which billionaires redirect their gifts from museums to bed nets is still a world in which billionaires set priorities. Effective altruism should therefore be paired with a politics that asks why such private discretion is so large in the first place.

None of this refutes Singer’s central challenge. It is not an argument for giving badly, or for retreating into local loyalties, or for indulging every charitable impulse as equally valid. On the contrary, anyone who gives should welcome scrutiny. Donors should ask whether a program works, whether the need is urgent, whether the beneficiaries are among the worst off, and whether another use of the money would do more.

The best version of effective altruism would combine rigor with humility. It would fund proven health interventions while also supporting careful efforts at institutional reform. It would count animal suffering and future risks without pretending that all values can be reduced to a single spreadsheet. It would encourage personal generosity without letting governments, corporations, and citizens evade structural responsibilities.

Singer’s essay is powerful because it restores moral seriousness to choices affluent people prefer to regard as optional. Its weakness is that it sometimes makes ethics look too much like optimization by donors. We should keep the demand for evidence, the cosmopolitan concern, and the impatience with vanity giving. But doing the most good will require not only better charity. It will require better politics, better institutions, and a fuller account of justice.

Peter Singer's defense of effective altruism is rigorous and largely persuasive, yet it reveals a tension at the heart of the movement between its empirical ambitions and its philosophical foundations. While Singer is right to insist that reason and evidence should guide our charitable giving, effective altruism risks becoming another form of utilitarian calculus that abstracts away dimensions of human flourishing that matter deeply.

Singer frames effective altruism as the logical extension of caring about others, yet the emphasis on maximizing impact can paradoxically narrow our moral imagination. When we fixate on lives saved as the metric of moral success, we may undervalue forms of help that are harder to quantify: the friendship that prevents despair, the community organization that builds resilience, the art that sustains hope across generations. Singer dismisses giving to opera houses, but the humanities have shaped moral progress in ways that no bed net distribution can replicate. The question is not merely how to reduce suffering most efficiently, but what kind of world we are trying to build, and that requires more than arithmetic.

There is also something troubling about the framing of Matt Wage's career choice. Singer celebrates Wage's decision to work on Wall Street so he can donate half his income. Yet this positions financial speculation—often disconnected from productive enterprise—as a morally praiseworthy path, provided the earnings go to good causes. This seems to sidestep questions about whether some ways of earning money are themselves problematic regardless of how the proceeds are used. Effective altruism should grapple with the ethics of its own fundraising.

Singer's handling of partiality toward family is thoughtful, but it raises further questions about the limits of impartialist ethics. He argues that parents can prioritize their children without being morally negligent, yet effective altruism remains committed to the demanding ideal that strangers' needs should outweigh the preferences of the giver. This creates an asymmetry: the movement asks individuals to subordinate their own projects and relationships to global optimization, while institutional structures—governments, corporations—face no such expectation. If governments consistently failed to prioritize global poverty, would Singer fault citizens for not pressuring them harder rather than donating individually? The movement's focus on individual giving may inadvertently let collective actors off the hook.

Finally, effective altruism's emphasis on measurable outcomes privileges causes we can count. This biases giving toward health interventions over structural reforms, toward emergency relief over long-term political change. Singer acknowledges that justice and freedom matter but treats them as instrumental to reducing suffering. Some philosophers would argue that justice is valuable in its own right, not merely as a means to happier lives.

These concerns do not undermine Singer's core insight: that we should use evidence to make our giving more effective, and that the fortunate have moral obligations to the less fortunate. But a fully adequate ethics of giving must make room for values that effective altruism, in its current form, struggles to accommodate.

**The Bed Net Is Not Enough**

Peter Singer's case for effective altruism rests on a vivid moral image: a young man on Wall Street who, by donating half his salary to the Against Malaria Foundation, will save more lives than a firefighter could pull from a burning building. It's a powerful picture, and like all powerful pictures it deserves scrutiny. As someone who has spent years in development work, my worry about effective altruism is not its altruism but its theory of what makes interventions effective.

The bed net is the movement's totem because GiveWell can put a price on a life saved with reasonable confidence. But the very features that make bed nets evaluable — discreteness, short causal chains, easily counted outputs — also mark out a narrow slice of what actually drives human development. The dramatic reductions in child mortality over the past forty years owe more to broad processes: rising incomes, female education, urbanization, vaccine programs run by national health ministries, and the slow grind of state capacity building. None of these would have scored well on a 2007 GiveWell scorecard. Indeed, programs like oral rehydration therapy, which has saved tens of millions, looked unimpressive in pilot RCTs before national systems took them up.

Singer concedes that effective altruists debate cause prioritization, and he gestures toward "systemic change" being part of the conversation. But the movement's epistemology has a built-in tilt. When the test for a good intervention is "evidence and reason" operationalized as randomized trials and cost-per-DALY calculations, you end up favoring whatever can be measured cleanly over what cannot. Funding a malaria net is measurable. Funding a campaign to reform a country's tax-collection capacity, or to support a journalists' union, or to push rich-country governments to change agricultural subsidies that depress African farm incomes, is not. The latter may matter more. We don't know — and that is precisely the problem. "Don't know" gets coded as "don't fund."

There is a deeper issue. Singer presents the choice as Make-A-Wish versus the Against Malaria Foundation, framed so that any reasonable person must pick AMF. But the truly contested comparison in global poverty is not Batkid versus bed nets; it is bed nets versus, say, GiveDirectly's cash transfers, or versus advocacy for migration liberalization (which Michael Clemens has estimated could generate trillions in welfare gains), or versus investment in long-run scientific research. On these comparisons, effective altruism's confident hierarchies fray fast. A movement that markets itself on the certainty of "doing the most good" is forced, in practice, to admit it is making bets under deep uncertainty — bets shaped as much by what is legible to spreadsheets as by what is actually consequential.

I also want to register a worry about the "earning to give" pathway that Matt Wage embodies. Singer treats it as a near-miraculous arbitrage: take a high-paying job, donate the surplus, multiply your impact. The argument assumes the financial-sector job is morally neutral — that Wage's arbitrage trading itself does no harm and displaces no good. But finance is not a neutral conduit. Some activity in those firms is socially valuable; some is rent extraction; some, as we saw in 2008, can metastasize into systemic harm. If we are being rigorous about consequences, we have to count the expected impact of the work itself, not just the donations it funds. The fact that effective altruists rarely run those numbers suggests the framework's quantification has limits it doesn't acknowledge.

None of this is a reason to dismiss effective altruism. Singer is right that the average American giver donates emotionally, that charity is under-evaluated, that GiveWell has done real work in pulling tens of millions of dollars toward demonstrably effective programs. A movement of young people willing to give 10 or 50 percent of their income is a genuinely heartening development in a culture that more often celebrates conspicuous consumption.

But the movement would be stronger if it were humbler. The right slogan is not "do the most good you can" — a phrase that promises an optimization most of us cannot perform — but something closer to "give more, give thoughtfully, and stay open to evidence that the leaderboard is wrong." The danger of Singer's framing is that it converts ethics into a problem of execution rather than of judgment. Real moral life, including real philanthropy, requires judgment in the face of irreducible uncertainty about what helps and what hurts. Bed nets are good. They are not the whole of doing good.

**Earning to Give, or Laundering a System?**

Peter Singer's essay is a recruiting pitch wrapped in a syllogism. The syllogism: suffering is bad, you can prevent some of it for a few thousand dollars, therefore you ought to. The recruiting pitch: come to Wall Street, become a quant, send the surplus to the Against Malaria Foundation, and you too can be a hero. I want to take the pitch seriously, because I think it is doing real harm to the moral imagination of a generation of bright young people.

Start with the figure of Matt Wage. Singer presents him as a triumph: a philosophy major who turned down Oxford to take an arbitrage job and now donates six figures a year to charity. But what is arbitrage? It is the extraction of small price differentials in financial markets. The most generous defense is that it makes markets more efficient. The less generous, and probably more accurate, description is that it is a zero-sum activity in which clever people armed with capital and infrastructure transfer money from less clever people to themselves. Whatever its merits, it is not the work of a humanitarian. To treat the income as morally clean — as if the only ethical question were what to do with the proceeds — is to accept a strange premise: that the economic system that produced the surplus is beyond moral evaluation, and only the disposal of the surplus is up for debate.

This is not a small philosophical quibble. It is the central political question of our time. The same global economy that allows an American twenty-five-year-old to "save a hundred lives" with his bonus is the economy whose trade rules, tax havens, intellectual property regimes, and capital flows make it cheaper to die of malaria in Lilongwe than to live. Effective altruism treats poverty as if it were a natural disaster — a hurricane that happens to children in distant countries, which we good people must mop up after with our donations. It is not a hurricane. It is a structure, and the structure has beneficiaries. Some of those beneficiaries are now writing checks to GiveWell.

I am not the first to make this point, and Singer would no doubt reply that effective altruists are perfectly free to give to structural causes too. But notice what is conspicuously missing from his essay: any mention of labor unions, of tax justice campaigns, of land reform, of movements to lower the cost of essential medicines, of organizing among the poor themselves. The examples are all of donors and recipients, of givers and gratitude. The poor appear in Singer's argument as objects of rescue, never as agents of their own liberation. This is the oldest pathology of philanthropy, dressed up in the language of evidence-based optimization.

There is also a more subtle ideological move. Singer wants to reorient ethics around the question: what should the privileged individual do with his or her surplus? But the prior question, the one that political philosophy used to take seriously, is why some people have such enormous surpluses while others have none. By making the surplus a starting point rather than a problem, effective altruism converts a question of justice into a question of consumer choice. It treats the structure of wealth as given, and asks only how generously to redistribute its scraps. Lenin called this "bourgeois philanthropy" a century ago, and the term still fits.

I will grant Singer this: the warm-glow critique is real. Americans really do give too little, and what they give they often give thoughtlessly. **What Effective Altruism Misses About Being a Person**

Peter Singer's essay has the curious quality of presenting a vision of the moral life that almost no one, including Singer himself, actually lives, and treating this as a feature rather than a bug. "Living a fully ethical life involves doing the most good we can." A "minimally acceptable ethical life" requires donating a substantial portion of one's spare resources. The rest is concession to human frailty: of course we cannot really be perfect, of course we may love our own children more, of course we leave ourselves "time and resources to relax." But the bar is set, and our lives are measured against it, and we are all by definition falling short.

I want to push back on the picture of ethics this implies. Singer treats morality as fundamentally a matter of maximizing impersonal goodness, with our particular attachments, projects, and identities appearing as obstacles to be managed — partiality "accepted" only because it would be "unrealistic" to require otherwise. But this gets the structure of ethical life almost exactly backwards. Our particular attachments are not concessions wrung from a more demanding impartial morality; they are the substance out of which moral life is built. To be a person at all is to stand in relations — to parents, children, friends, neighbors, fellow citizens, colleagues — that come with their own obligations, intelligible only from within them. The view from nowhere, from which all suffering counts equally and the question is simply how to minimize the aggregate, is not the deepest moral standpoint. It is, in many ways, a refusal of the moral standpoint, because it refuses the particularity that makes obligations recognizable.

Bernard Williams made this point decades ago in his criticism of utilitarianism, and the point still stands. A man who calculates whether saving his drowning wife is permitted by impartial morality has, as Williams put it, "one thought too many." He is not a better husband for the calculation; he has failed at being a husband, and at being a person, in a basic way. Singer's framework, despite all its qualifications about loving one's own children, cannot really accommodate this. Within his system the love is always provisional, always answerable upward to the demand to do the most good. The "spare" resources are whatever is left after our partialities have taken their permitted bite. But what counts as "spare" is precisely the question, and it cannot be answered without a substantive account of what a human life is for.

The arts question is a useful case. Singer says David Geffen could have found better uses for his $100 million than renovating a concert hall. On a narrowly consequentialist calculus, this is obvious — the marginal dollar spent on bed nets prevents more suffering than the marginal dollar spent on Lincoln Center acoustics. But why should the question be settled by that calculus? Music, scholarship, scientific curiosity, civic monuments, sacred architecture — these are among the things that make a human civilization rather than a managed welfare zone. A world that had eliminated malaria but built no concert halls and wrote no symphonies would not be a flourishing world. It would be a kept world. Singer concedes that the arts will be worthy "in a world that had overcome extreme poverty," as if culture were a luxury to be unlocked only after triage. This is exactly the philistinism that great moral traditions — religious and secular — have always pushed back against. There has never been a society so prosperous that suffering was absent, and on Singer's logic there never will be one in which it is permissible to build a cathedral.

I do not want to deny what is right in effective altruism. Most middle-class Westerners give too little and too thoughtlessly. We could do enormous good with money we currently spend on trivialities. A movement that nudges us toward giving more, and toward giving more carefully, is on balance a gift. But this is a moral revival, not a moral revolution. It does not require us to accept that ethics is fundamentally a maximization problem, or that our local loves are concessions to weakness, or that the value of a human life can be cashed out in dollars per QALY. We can give generously and remain people, embedded in the rich particularities that make giving — and being given to — meaningful in the first place. Singer's framework cannot quite see why this matters. Most of us can, and should hold onto the sight.

**Yes, And: Where Singer's Argument Needs to Go Next**

Peter Singer's essay is, in many ways, the public face we want: clear, generous, recruiting. But it is also, I think, a slightly out-of-date face. The version of EA Singer describes is the version of five years ago, before the movement's center of gravity began to shift. A response essay is a chance to say what is changing and why it matters.

The clearest shift is away from global health and toward two other priorities: animal welfare and the long-term future. Singer mentions both in passing. He notes that animal suffering counts, and he lists "reducing risks of human extinction" as a cause that effective altruists debate. But the order of presentation in his essay — bed nets first, the rest as footnote — no longer matches the order of arguments inside the movement. Many of the people doing the most rigorous cause-prioritization work, at organizations like the Open Philanthropy Project, the Centre for Effective Altruism, and the Future of Humanity Institute, have concluded that on expected-value grounds, work to reduce risks from advanced artificial intelligence, engineered pandemics, or nuclear war may dominate global health by orders of magnitude, because the future contains enormously many potential lives. Whether one finds this argument compelling or troubling, it is now central, and any essay introducing EA that doesn't grapple with it is doing readers a disservice.

The marginal cost of preventing severe suffering through corporate cage-free campaigns, broiler welfare reforms, or alternative-protein research appears, on current estimates, lower than the marginal cost of preventing equivalent human suffering through global health. The movement has earned the right to a harder sell.

The movement's own career-advice organization, 80,000 Hours, has been increasingly explicit about this: most high-impact people should not earn to give, because there are not enough good direct-work people to fill the roles. The first is hard to deny. The second is much harder to defend than we sometimes admit. It is at its worst when it talks as if the leaderboard is settled.

If effective altruism continues to mature — taking animal suffering seriously, taking the long-term future seriously, and taking its own uncertainty seriously — it has a chance to become more than a giving guide. **The Calculus and the Care**

What strikes me first about Peter Singer's essay is its emotional register. The argument is supposed to be about caring more, about expanding our circle of concern, about the moral seriousness of suffering. And yet the prose itself is curiously cool. Children die; we should run the numbers; this charity beats that one by a factor of three; David Geffen has misallocated. The world Singer wants us into is the world seen through the spreadsheet. I do not say this to dismiss him. I say it because I think this register is doing a great deal of moral work that the essay does not acknowledge, and that women in particular have reason to be wary of it.

The feminist tradition of care ethics — Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings, Virginia Held, Joan Tronto, and others — has been arguing for forty years that mainstream moral philosophy has systematically misdescribed the moral life by abstracting away from the concrete relationships in which moral demands actually arise. We do not first encounter "aggregate suffering" and then derive duties from it; we first encounter this child crying, this neighbor sick, this mother overwhelmed, and our moral capacities develop out of responsiveness to particulars. Singer's framework reverses this. Particular relationships appear in his account only as exceptions, "special responsibilities" grudgingly conceded, things effective altruists "leave themselves time for" between bouts of optimization. The view from nowhere is the default; care has to argue its way back in.

This matters, practically, because most of the work of keeping the world livable is care work, and most of it is done by women, and most of it is exactly the kind of work that resists Singer-style measurement. The mother feeding her family, the daughter looking after her elderly father, the community health worker who knows everyone's name in her village, the nurse who stays an extra hour — none of this scores well on dollars-per-DALY. Some of it doesn't involve money at all. But the world without it is unrecognizable. A philosophy of giving that systematically discounts what cannot be tabulated will, sooner or later, systematically discount the people whose contributions are hardest to tabulate, and those people have historically been women.

There is a related concern about what effective altruism does to the moral psychology of its adherents. Singer dismisses the "warm glow" giver who sends ten dollars to five charities, contrasting her with the rational maximizer who sends a substantial check to one. Empirically, he is right that small donations are inefficient and emotionally driven. But the warm glow is not nothing. It is part of the affective infrastructure that keeps human beings open to one another. Cultivating a moral life that refuses warm-glow giving in favor of cool-eyed calculation may produce more units of well-being per donated dollar in the short run. Over decades, in a whole person's life, I am less sure. The capacities for compassion and identification get trained or atrophied by what we practice. A practice that always overrides our identification with the visible child in favor of the statistical child elsewhere may not, in the end, leave us with the moral selves we want to have. (And it may not even leave us giving more in the long run; sustained giving seems to depend on emotional engagement.)

I want to give Singer credit where it is due. His Make-A-Wish example has a genuine bite. A society in which we spend tens of thousands on a single child's wish while children elsewhere die for want of bed nets has its priorities tangled, and the bedrock moral intuition Singer is pressing — that distance and visibility should not determine moral worth — is correct. I am not arguing for parochialism, or for sentimentalism over evidence. I am arguing that the dichotomy between calculation and care is false, and that the effective altruist tendency to side with calculation against care purchases its clarity at too high a price.

What would a better synthesis look like? Probably something like: care is the basic moral mode; reason serves care by helping us see what would actually help; emotional engagement with particular cases is what keeps us motivated; structural and quantitative thinking is what keeps our compassion from being captured by whoever has the best PR. The point is not to rank these against each other. The point is that ethics needs all of them, and a movement that elevates one at the expense of the others — even in the name of "doing the most good" — has misunderstood what it is to be good.

**Charity Begins at Home, and So Does Solidarity**

Peter Singer is one of the most influential moral philosophers alive, and he has spent his career arguing that we owe distant strangers what we owe our own. The argument has the force of pure logic on its side: a child is a child, suffering is suffering, geography is morally arbitrary. I want to suggest, with some hesitation, that the argument is wrong — or at least that it proves much less than Singer thinks it does, and that the movement it has spawned is shallower than it appears.

The communitarian tradition, from Aristotle through Burke to MacIntyre and Sandel, has insisted that human beings are not free-floating moral atoms but creatures situated in particular communities — families, neighborhoods, religious congregations, nations — and that our obligations are differentiated by these situations. A father owes his children what he does not owe his neighbor's children. A citizen owes his fellow citizens what he does not owe foreigners. A parishioner owes the elderly widow down the pew what he does not owe an equally needy stranger across the world. This is not parochialism dressed up as principle. It is the recognition that moral obligations track the relationships that make us who we are, and that to deny these obligations is to deny something essential about human personhood.

Singer waves at this concern with a brief paragraph about why one's own children may take priority — but only, he says, because it would be "unrealistic" to require otherwise and because children "thrive in close, loving families." Notice the structure of the concession. Partiality is permitted only on consequentialist grounds; it is a tactic in service of the impartial aggregate. The communitarian view is the opposite: partiality is the basic moral fact, and impartial concern is a derivative virtue, real and important but always embedded in and constrained by particular obligations.

Why does this matter for effective altruism? Because the movement, in its purer forms, ends up corroding precisely the local institutions and loyalties that make sustained moral life possible. Consider Singer's stricture against giving to one's alma mater or local church or community arts center, on the grounds that the marginal dollar would do more good distributed as bed nets in Africa. By this logic, virtually every form of giving that builds and sustains a community — the local food bank, the parish school, the synagogue building fund, the volunteer fire department, the friends of the public library — is suboptimal and should be redirected. But these institutions are the connective tissue of civil society. They are how, in Tocqueville's phrase, Americans have historically formed themselves into a people capable of self-government and mutual care. To hollow them out in the name of impartial maximization is to trade a durable, locally rooted ethical culture for a thin globalist transfer regime.

Religious traditions have understood this. The tithe, which Singer mentions approvingly, was historically given to the church or the local poor — that is, to the community of which one was a part. The "10 percent" was not a calculation of aggregate utility; it was an act of belonging. To recast it as a payment into an optimizing fund that distributes anonymously to wherever the spreadsheet points is to misunderstand what the practice was for. Religious giving builds the church that visits the sick, buries the dead, raises the children, and holds the community together. EA-style giving does some good things, but it does not do that, and it cannot.

There is also a political dimension. A society in which the privileged discharge their moral obligations by writing checks to faraway causes, rather than by participating in the messy, frustrating work of their own polity, is a society that is hollowing out from the middle. Singer's young people are encouraged to take high-paying finance jobs and donate to AMF. They are not encouraged to coach Little League, sit on the school board, run for city council, or take care of their aging parents — though every one of these matters more, in the texture of an actual life and a livable society, than a donation that they will never see at work.

I do not deny that we have obligations to distant strangers. Of course we do. But these obligations are added to, not substituted for, our particular ones. The trouble with Singer's framework is that it forgets this, and the trouble with effective altruism is that it institutionalizes the forgetting. A better ethics begins at home — and from there, reaches outward.

Buried in Peter Singer's essay is a sentence that should provoke more outrage than it has: David Geffen, by donating $100 million to renovate Lincoln Center's concert hall, "could have found better things to do with his money." Singer says this casually, almost as an aside, on the way to a more general claim that "donating to opera houses and museums isn't likely to be doing the most good you can." What, after all, is the implicit argument? It runs: the same $100 million could have saved tens of thousands of lives from malaria; therefore concert halls lose to bed nets; therefore Geffen has misallocated his philanthropy. The logic is so simple it feels invincible. But the strength of the argument depends on a premise that Singer never defends — namely, that the only morally serious goods are the prevention of suffering and the extension of life. Once you let in any other good — the development of human capacities, the transmission of cultural inheritance across generations, the cultivation of beauty, the creation of public spaces in which a city can know itself — the argument loses its grip, because these goods are not commensurable in QALYs and cannot lose a comparison they were never entered in.

A society capable of producing and sustaining a great orchestra is a remarkable human achievement. So is a great university, a great museum, a great library. These institutions are how civilizations remember themselves, how they educate the imaginations of their young, how they continue to produce people capable of, among other things, recognizing that distant suffering matters and that something should be done about it. The very moral sensibility that Singer trades on — the capacity to see one's own comfort against the backdrop of another's distress, to feel the call of impartial concern — is itself a cultural inheritance, transmitted through institutions that include the ones he wants to defund.

Singer offers a fallback: in a world that had eliminated extreme poverty, the arts would be a worthy cause. Until then, triage. But this concession reveals the depth of the problem rather than resolving it. Extreme poverty has been part of the human condition for as long as there has been a human condition, and on no plausible projection does it disappear in any of our lifetimes. The Singerian permission to fund the symphony is therefore deferred forever. We are to live, and our children are to live, and our grandchildren are to live, in a culture that systematically starves its non-instrumental institutions because suffering somewhere else has not yet been eliminated. This is not an ethics. It is a permanent state of emergency.

Compare, for a moment, the actual history of philanthropy that Singer is implicitly criticizing. The great American foundations of the twentieth century — Carnegie, Rockefeller, Ford, Mellon — funded libraries, universities, medical schools, scientific research, museums, music conservatories. They also funded public health: the eradication of hookworm in the American South, the development of yellow fever vaccines, the green revolution. Their philanthropy was pluralistic, betting on multiple goods on the theory that human flourishing is multidimensional and we don't know in advance which investments will pay out in which currency. That pluralism produced the institutional bedrock of modern American civic life. It would not have survived a GiveWell evaluation.

I am not arguing that Geffen made the optimal gift. Maybe he should have given more to public health, or to climate research, or to refugee resettlement. Reasonable people can argue. What I am arguing is that the question "did he do the most good?" is not the right question to settle the matter, because it presupposes a totalizing view of value that no thoughtful person actually holds when they think about their own lives. None of us live by maximizing one good against all others. We try to be decent in many directions at once. Our philanthropy can, and should, reflect this.

The effective altruist will respond that giving to one's preferred art form is just self-indulgence dressed up as ethics. Sometimes it is. But the answer is not to abolish the category of philanthropy that supports culture; it is to give more, give thoughtfully, and recognize that the human good is more textured than a spreadsheet can hold. Geffen Hall will outlast a generation of bed nets, and the people who hear music in it will be better for it. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, quite a lot.

**What the Psychologists Know That Singer Doesn't**

Peter Singer's essay leans, at a crucial moment, on a piece of psychological research: the distinction between donors who give substantially to one or two evaluated charities and "warm glow givers" who scatter small amounts widely. He cites this approvingly, as evidence that the effective altruist style of giving is the rational one and that ordinary charitable behavior is irrational sentiment. As someone who works on the psychology of prosocial behavior, I want to push back. The empirical literature does not quite say what Singer wants it to say, and the lessons it offers for movement-building are more interesting than his essay suggests.

Start with warm glow itself. The term comes from James Andreoni's economic models of giving, and it does pick out a real phenomenon: people derive utility from the act of giving, somewhat independently of the impact of the gift. But Andreoni's point was not that warm glow is a moral failing to be overcome. It was that giving is a multi-motive activity, in which impact, identity, social signaling, and direct hedonic reward all play roles. Designing institutions and norms that ignore this multiplicity will fail. And Singer's framework, in its purer forms, does ignore it. He treats giving as if it were a problem of cool optimization that has been distorted by emotional contaminants. The psychology suggests the opposite: emotional engagement is the engine that makes any sustained giving possible at all, and severing it in favor of pure spreadsheet rationalism is a recipe for burnout and disengagement.

Second, on the identifiable victim effect. Singer invokes the Batkid story to show that we are biased toward visible, identifiable beneficiaries over statistical ones. This is true and well documented — Paul Slovic's work on "psychic numbing" shows that our compassion can actually decrease as the number of victims grows. But Singer draws the wrong lesson. He concludes that effective altruists should override the identifiable-victim bias by relying on numbers. The more nuanced lesson, which the same body of research supports, is that effective altruism needs identifiable victims of its own. The reason GiveDirectly has been so successful at attracting donors is that it tells specific stories about specific recipients. The reason AMF's website works is that it shows photographs of children under nets. Effective communication of effective causes requires the same emotional infrastructure that Singer wants to discount.

Third, there is an underappreciated body of research on how moral demandingness affects behavior. When ethical norms are set very high — when we are told that anything short of "doing the most good we can" is a failure — the predictable psychological response is not greater effort but moral licensing, disengagement, or guilt-driven withdrawal. People who are told they should give 50 percent of their income and feel they cannot, often give less, not more, than people who are told a modest target like 10 percent and reach it. There is some evidence that the existential-stakes rhetoric Singer favors actually decreases sustained giving compared to softer framings. Whether or not "a fully ethical life" requires what Singer says, telling ordinary people that it does is not the most effective way to get them to give more.

Fourth, and most importantly: who actually becomes a sustained, generous giver? The longitudinal research suggests that the strongest predictors are not exposure to philosophical arguments about marginal utility. They are early experiences of giving within communities of giving, identity formation around generosity, religious or civic embeddedness, and direct relationships with beneficiaries or organizations. The communitarians and religious traditionalists, in other words, have been doing the empirical work of producing generous people for a long time, with techniques very different from Singer's. The effective altruist movement has been remarkably successful at attracting a particular demographic — analytically minded, often male, often atheist, often in tech and finance — but it has been less successful at the mass project of moving a society toward greater generosity. Understanding why requires taking the psychology of giving seriously, on its own terms, rather than dismissing most of it as warm-glow noise.

None of this is an argument against effective altruism. Evidence-based giving is better than evidence-free giving, and Singer is right to push donors toward the question of impact. But a movement that wants to grow, and to grow durably, needs to do more than rationally outclass the alternatives. It needs to build emotional, relational, and institutional architectures of giving — and those will look more like the religious communities and civic associations Singer dismisses than like the optimization seminars he champions. The psychology, on this, is fairly clear.

Effective altruists can support hard-to-evaluate work, he might say, if they have good reason to believe it does more good than the easily evaluated alternatives. In principle, this is true. In practice, the movement's center of gravity sits exactly where the evidence is cleanest — bed nets, deworming, cash transfers — and its rhetoric trains donors to feel that anything which cannot produce a comparable evidence base is somehow soft, sentimental, or unrigorous. The result is a slow tilt of the philanthropic field toward whatever can be measured in a controlled trial, regardless of whether what can be measured corresponds to what most matters.

Long-shot scientific research. Most institution-building, of any kind, anywhere. Most policy work. The smarter writers in the movement have begun to talk about "hits-based giving," about the value of supporting work with high variance and high expected value even when individual outcomes are hard to verify. The implicit message to ordinary donors is: when in doubt, trust the spreadsheet. And the spreadsheet has a worldview.

The worldview, in brief, is that the morally serious goods are those that can be counted in lives saved or DALYs averted, on relatively short time horizons, with relatively clean attribution. This worldview will systematically underweight: the value of healthy institutions, which are difficult to attribute; the value of freedom and democratic accountability, which are hard to monetize; the value of cultural and scientific inheritance, which pays out across generations; and the value of empowering the people one is supposedly helping to determine their own futures, which is messy and often goes badly before it goes well. None of these omissions is intentional. All are predictable consequences of the framework.

I want to be fair. There are donors who should not be funding journalism or advocacy because they would do so badly; there are foundations whose marginal grant really is better spent on bed nets; there is enormous waste in the parts of philanthropy that have insulated themselves from evaluation. A movement pushing back against that waste is doing something useful. The problem comes when the push becomes a default — when every philanthropic question is reframed as a question of measurable cost-effectiveness, and everything that resists this reframing is treated as suspect.

The hardest, most consequential philanthropic work I know of has been work that could not have justified itself to a GiveWell evaluator at the time the grants were made. These paths might have allowed him to influence decisions that affect millions of people, potentially doing more good than any individual donation. They would also require different skills—political judgment, coalition-building, public persuasion—than those valued by the effective altruism movement.

I do not mean to suggest that personal giving is worthless. Singer is right that we should take seriously our obligation to help others and that we should think carefully about how to do so effectively. But effective altruism, as currently practiced, risks substituting individual charity for political responsibility. A truly effective approach to reducing suffering would include both, but the balance should shift more toward collective action than Singer acknowledges.

Peter Singer's effective altruism is a moral framework built on reason and evidence, but it largely ignores the role of emotion in ethical life. The result is a vision of moral agency that is intellectually impressive but psychologically one-sided—and ultimately less compelling for it.

Singer's critique of "warm glow" giving is representative. Those who donate to many small causes, seeking the emotional satisfaction of giving, are dismissed as less rational than those who concentrate their donations on the most effective organizations. This contrast—reason versus emotion, calculation versus feeling—structures the entire essay. Emotion is the problem; reason is the solution.

But this framing is too simple. Emotions are not merely biases to be overcome; they are essential components of moral life. Moral emotions like empathy, compassion, and care enable us to perceive the needs of others and motivate us to respond. Without such emotions, we might know intellectually that people are suffering, but we would lack the motivation to help them.

The effective altruist might respond that they feel good when they do good, and that this emotional satisfaction provides motivation. This is true but incomplete. The satisfaction that effective altruists report is often a reflective satisfaction—the knowledge that one is doing the right thing—rather than direct emotional connection to specific recipients. A mother who sees her hungry child and feels compassionate compassion is moved to act by an immediate emotional response. An effective altruist who calculates that a donation will reduce suffering in an distant country may feel satisfied by the calculation, but the emotional texture of their motivation is different.

This difference matters for moral culture. If we systematically channel charitable giving toward the most effective causes, based on careful calculation rather than emotional connection, what happens to our capacity for spontaneous compassion? Singer worries that "warm glow" giving is inefficient, but the impulse to give, to respond with care to visible suffering, is the foundation of moral concern. Efficiency is important, but it is not the only value at stake.

Consider the Batkid example. Singer calculates that $7,500 could save three lives if given to malaria prevention. The implication is that Make-A-Wish is a worse choice. But many people who give to Make-A-Wish are moved by direct emotional engagement with a specific child's wish. This engagement is not merely inefficient; it reflects a genuine moral response to human vulnerability and desire. A society that cultivates such responses may produce more overall moral concern than one that trains everyone to calculate optimal charitable allocations.

The emphasis on reason also neglects the role of moral relationships. Singer suggests that effective altruists can set aside time for family and friends, implying that these relationships are separate from ethical life. But most ethical traditions treat relationships of love and friendship as intrinsic to moral flourishing. We are not isolated moral calculators but members of communities bound by ties of care and obligation.

None of this means that Singer's practical recommendations are wrong. Thinking carefully about the impact of giving is valuable, and many effective altruists are motivated by genuine care for others. But the framework would be stronger if it incorporated a more robust understanding of moral psychology—one that treats emotion as a source of moral insight rather than merely a bias to be corrected. The goal should not be to replace feeling with calculation but to cultivate both in a harmonious whole.
