Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Federalism is not just a slogan; it is the constitutional design that lets states act as laboratories on contested social questions. On drug policy, that experimentation is overdue. Vermont's pivot toward treatment for opiate addiction, Colorado and Washington's marijuana legalization, and the quieter shift in dozens of states toward drug courts and diversion all reflect different judgments about what works in different communities. The federal government's role should shrink to match.

That doesn't mean Washington should withdraw entirely. Interstate drug trafficking, cartels, large-scale manufacturing, and money laundering are precisely the problems federal law enforcement was designed to handle. The DEA and FBI have comparative advantages in cross-border investigations that no state agency can match. But routine possession cases, street-level sales, and even mid-level distribution within a state are already prosecuted ably in state courts, which handle the vast majority of drug cases anyway. Federal prosecution there mostly duplicates effort, fills federal prisons—now roughly half of whose inmates are drug offenders—and imposes mandatory minimums that state legislatures have increasingly rejected.

Attorney General Holder's recent guidance scaling back mandatory minimums and the Cole memo deferring to Colorado and Washington point in the right direction, but they are reversible by the next administration. Congress should codify the deference: leave possession and intrastate distribution to state law, and limit federal prosecution to genuinely interstate or international cases.

Critics worry about a patchwork. But a patchwork is what federalism produces, and on a question where the national consensus has visibly fractured, that is a feature, not a bug.

The states are right to treat drug abuse as a public health challenge rather than solely a criminal one, and the federal government should respect those choices—but that does not mean Washington should withdraw entirely from drug enforcement. A sweeping retreat would be as reckless as the one-size-fits-all crackdowns of the past. Similarly, Attorney General Holder’s “Smart on Crime” initiative, which directs U.S. attorneys to avoid mandatory minimums for low-level, nonviolent drug offenders, acknowledges that over-prosecution of street-level users and small-time sellers has filled federal prisons without making communities safer. These calibrated steps suggest a practical path: the federal government should generally leave local drug cases to state courts, which are better positioned to blend punishment, treatment, and social services. Vermont’s emphasis on rehabilitation over incarceration, for example, reflects local priorities that Washington should not second-guess.

Yet a complete federal withdrawal would be a dangerous overcorrection. Drug cartels do not respect state lines, and no single state has the investigative reach to disrupt the trafficking organizations that move heroin, methamphetamine, or cocaine across borders and through national distribution chains. Federal prosecutors remain essential to target these large-scale networks, and the Controlled Substances Act retains a valid role in fighting interstate commerce in the most harmful substances. Handing all drug enforcement to the states would also invite a regulatory race to the bottom, where traffickers exploit the weakest jurisdiction.

The federal government should not prosecute a student caught with a joint or a low-level addict peddling heroin to support a habit. Those cases belong in state systems, where therapeutic courts and diversion programs can offer a smarter response. But when organized crime moves drugs across state boundaries, Washington must remain the backstop. The goal is not monolithic enforcement but a sensible division of labor—one that honors federalism while recognizing that the drug trade is a national, not merely local, problem.

Let the Laboratories Run Their Experiments

The genius of American federalism is that we don't have to guess what works — we can watch. Vermont is trying to treat heroin addiction as a public health crisis. Washington and Colorado are testing whether a regulated marijuana market can replace a black one. New York and California are recalibrating sentencing. These are exactly the kinds of experiments Justice Brandeis had in mind when he praised states as "laboratories of democracy." Federal prosecution of local drug cases threatens to shut those laboratories down before we learn anything from them.

For forty years, Washington has imposed a one-size-fits-all approach: criminalize, interdict, incarcerate. The results are well known. The United States has roughly five percent of the world's population and nearly a quarter of its prisoners. Overdose deaths have climbed despite the spending. Black and Latino Americans have been arrested for drug offenses at rates wildly disproportionate to their actual use. Whatever else one says about the drug war, the federal version of it has not delivered on its promises.

States are in a better position to balance the tradeoffs. A rural Vermont sheriff knows that locking up a 22-year-old with an opioid problem is unlikely to end well; a Denver regulator knows the local dispensary owners by name; a Brooklyn district attorney sees firsthand how a marijuana conviction can wreck a young person's job prospects. None of this knowledge sits in a DEA field office answering to Main Justice.

The Cole memo, which signaled the Justice Department would not interfere with Colorado and Washington as long as certain priorities (sales to minors, diversion across state lines, cartel involvement) were respected, points the right way. It is not full devolution, but it accepts that the federal government's comparative advantage lies in cross-border trafficking and large-scale organized crime, not corner sales. That is the proper division of labor.

Critics worry about a patchwork. But we already have a patchwork in alcohol regulation, gambling, firearms, education, and criminal procedure, and the republic survives. Drugs are not so different that they require uniform national rules dictated from a single building in Washington. If Utah wants strict prohibition and Oregon wants decriminalization, both should be allowed to try, and citizens unhappy with their state's choice can vote, organize, or move.

Federal prosecutors should focus on what only they can do — international trafficking, interstate distribution networks, corruption — and leave possession, low-level distribution, and treatment policy to the states that actually have to live with the consequences. The federal government can help; it should stop trying to dictate.

Federal Power Exists for a Reason

It has become fashionable to argue that drug policy should be handed over to the states. The argument has a certain appeal, especially as voters in Colorado and Washington have grown impatient with marijuana prohibition. But before we dismantle a federal role that has existed in some form since the Harrison Act of 1914, we should remember why that role was built in the first place.

Drugs do not respect state lines. The heroin killing Vermonters does not come from a Vermont poppy field; it travels up I-91 from cities further south, and before that, across an international border. Methamphetamine in the Midwest is moved by transnational networks. Pills diverted from Florida pain clinics end up in Kentucky and West Virginia. State police and county sheriffs cannot subpoena bank records in Mexico, run wiretaps on a multistate conspiracy, or coordinate with foreign governments. The DEA and federal prosecutors can. Removing them from local cases would leave the supply side untouched while states scramble to clean up the demand side.

There is also a constitutional point worth making. The Supreme Court settled in Gonzales v. Raich (2005) that Congress's Commerce Clause power extends to local marijuana cultivation because of its aggregate effect on the interstate market. One can disagree with that ruling, but it is the law. A federal government that selectively declines to enforce duly enacted statutes — depending on which state's voters most recently passed a referendum — corrodes the rule of law. If the Controlled Substances Act is bad policy, Congress should change it. Until then, executive nonenforcement is a troubling precedent that future administrations may apply to laws progressives like.

The states-rights frame also tends to assume that state policy is uniformly more humane than federal policy. It isn't. Some states impose harsher penalties than federal law, particularly for crack and methamphetamine. Some have done virtually nothing to address the opioid crisis. A purely state-driven system would produce not just Colorado and Vermont, but also jurisdictions where addicted people get longer sentences and less treatment than they do today.

The right answer is not abdication but recalibration. The Justice Department should continue to prioritize traffickers over users, support state treatment initiatives, and use its prosecutorial discretion sensibly. But "leaving it to the states" as a slogan papers over the genuine national interests — public health, organized crime, border security, equal protection of the laws — that a federal role is meant to serve. We should be careful what we wish away.

Treat Addiction as a Health Problem, Wherever It Is Treated

The most important sentence in the prompt is the first one: states have responded to increased drug use "by emphasizing treatment and rehabilitation." That is the real story, and it is bigger than any jurisdictional debate. After decades of treating addiction primarily as a crime, governments are starting to treat it as the chronic, relapsing medical condition that physicians have long understood it to be. Whether that shift happens at the state or federal level matters less than that it happens.

Vermont's approach, articulated by Governor Shumlin in his recent State of the State address devoted entirely to heroin, is instructive. Rather than promising more arrests, he proposed expanded medication-assisted treatment, more recovery beds, and diversion of low-level offenders into care. This is consistent with what addiction medicine has been telling us for years: punishment alone produces poor outcomes, while sustained treatment — particularly buprenorphine and methadone for opioid use disorder — meaningfully reduces overdose deaths, infectious disease transmission, and criminal activity.

The relevant question, then, is which level of government does what to support this shift. The federal government has unique tools: it funds research through NIH and NIDA, it sets Medicaid policy that determines whether treatment is covered, it regulates which medications doctors can prescribe and under what conditions. The Affordable Care Act's parity requirements for behavioral health are arguably the most significant federal contribution to addiction treatment in a generation. None of that is going to happen at the state level alone.

What federal authorities should stop doing is using prosecutorial resources to lock up low-level users and distributors whose underlying problem is addiction. Mandatory minimums for street-level offenders have crowded federal prisons with people who would be better served — and at lower public cost — by treatment courts, probation, and clinical care. Attorney General Holder's recent moves to scale back charging in low-level cases are a welcome correction.

So the choice is not really "federal versus state." It is "punishment versus health." The states leading on treatment deserve federal partnership, not federal interference. The states still relying heavily on incarceration deserve federal incentives to change course, much as we used federal funds to push seat belts and drinking-age laws. And in the meantime, federal prosecutors should husband their scarce resources for traffickers and violent offenders, leaving the addicted to the clinicians and the drug courts.

That is a coordinated policy, not an abandonment of one level of government to the other. Done right, federalism and public health reinforce each other instead of competing.

The Constitutional Question We Keep Avoiding

Most of the debate over drug enforcement is about policy: does prohibition work, are sentences too long, is marijuana more or less harmful than alcohol? These are important questions, but they obscure a prior one. Under what constitutional authority does the federal government criminalize the possession of a plant grown in someone's backyard for personal use?

The historic answer was: none. When the country wanted to ban alcohol in 1919, it understood that a constitutional amendment was required. The Eighteenth Amendment did the work that mere statute could not. When Prohibition was repealed in 1933, the Twenty-First Amendment explicitly recognized state primacy over alcohol regulation. Drugs got no such amendment. The Controlled Substances Act of 1970 rests on the Commerce Clause, stretched in Gonzales v. Raich (2005) to cover homegrown marijuana on the theory that even non-commercial cultivation affects interstate markets.

That reasoning, as Justices Thomas and O'Connor pointed out in dissent, would justify almost anything. If growing six plants in your basement is "interstate commerce," the limiting principle of enumerated powers has effectively dissolved. One need not be a Tea Party constitutionalist to be uncomfortable with that. A federal government that can prosecute a Colorado dispensary operating in full compliance with Colorado law has powers the Framers would not have recognized.

Leaving drug enforcement to the states is therefore not just good policy; it is closer to the original constitutional design. States have always exercised the general police power — public health, safety, and morals — that the federal government does not possess. Murder, theft, assault, and the regulation of doctors and pharmacies are state matters. Drugs, historically and constitutionally, belong in the same category.

This does not mean the federal government has no role. Interstate trafficking is genuinely interstate; international smuggling implicates the federal foreign affairs power; controlled substances moving through federal facilities or the federal mails are properly federal concerns. These are real interstate commerce questions, not legal fictions. The DEA can and should police them.

But the everyday prosecution of growers, sellers, and users operating wholly within a single state goes beyond what the Constitution, fairly read, permits. The current détente — federal nonenforcement of federal law where state law differs — is unsatisfying because it makes federal policy turn on prosecutorial whim rather than legal principle. The cleaner answer is for Congress to repeal or substantially narrow the CSA, returning to the states the authority over intrastate drug matters that they never properly surrendered. Federalism, in this area, is not a slogan. It is the law.

A Practical Division of Labor

Asking whether the federal government should "even prosecute local drug cases" frames the issue too starkly. In practice, federal and state prosecutors already divide work along reasonably sensible lines, and the question is whether to refine that division — not whether to scrap one side of it.

Roughly 99 percent of drug arrests in America are made by state and local police. Federal cases are a small fraction of the total, and they cluster around larger quantities, organized networks, and conduct that crosses state or international lines. A typical street-corner sale ends up in state court; a multi-kilo shipment from Sinaloa ends up federal. This is not an accident. It reflects the comparative advantage of each system. Federal agencies have wiretap capacity, money-laundering expertise, and relationships with foreign counterparts. State systems have the local intelligence, the patrol officers, and the courts with the bandwidth to handle high volume.

The recent shifts in policy — the Cole memo on marijuana, Attorney General Holder's "Smart on Crime" initiative restricting charging for low-level offenders, the move away from mandatory minimums where possible — are sensible refinements of this division of labor. They acknowledge that federal resources are scarce and best aimed at conduct only federal agencies can effectively prosecute. They do not represent abdication.

What would be unwise is a categorical rule. Imagine the federal government announcing that it will never prosecute "local" drug cases. That would create perverse incentives: traffickers could structure operations to look local, corrupt local officials would face less external check, and states with weak enforcement would become safe harbors for distribution into neighboring states. The mere possibility of federal intervention, even rarely used, deters this.

Similarly, in jurisdictions where state authorities are unwilling or unable to act — because of corruption, capacity limits, or political dysfunction — the federal role provides a backstop. Civil rights enforcement in the Jim Crow South is the historical analogy. We may not need that backstop often, but we should not eliminate it.

The right approach, then, is roughly the current one, applied more consistently. Federal prosecutors should concentrate on traffickers, organized crime, public corruption, and interstate networks. They should generally defer to states on possession, low-level sales, and on medical and recreational marijuana programs that meet baseline regulatory standards. And Congress should reform the CSA — reschedule marijuana, revisit mandatory minimums, expand drug courts — so that the law on the books better matches the law in practice.

That is not a thrilling answer. It is, I think, the right one.

The Real Problem Is the War Itself

Debating which level of government should prosecute drug cases is a little like debating whether the captain or the first mate should steer a ship that is heading for the rocks. The deeper problem is the drug war, and devolution to the states will not, by itself, end it.

Consider what four decades of prohibition have produced. The United States incarcerates more people than any country on earth, with drug offenses driving much of the growth since 1980. Cartels in Mexico have killed tens of thousands and corrupted entire states. HIV and hepatitis spread because clean syringes are treated as paraphernalia. People who overdose die because their friends are afraid to call 911. Pain patients can't get the medications they need because doctors fear DEA scrutiny. None of this is incidental to prohibition; it is what prohibition produces, just as alcohol prohibition produced Al Capone and bathtub gin.

States can ease some of this. Colorado and Washington are doing something genuinely valuable by replacing a black market with a regulated one. Vermont is doing something valuable by treating addiction as illness. Portugal, which decriminalized all drug possession in 2001, suggests how much further we could reasonably go: rates of problematic use down, overdose deaths down, HIV transmission down, no surge in casual use. But states are constrained by federal law on every front. They cannot reschedule marijuana federally. They cannot give state-legal cannabis businesses access to banks, because federal banking rules treat the money as drug proceeds. They cannot authorize supervised injection facilities without federal liability. They cannot expand methadone access without bumping into federal regulations from the 1970s.

So yes, leave more enforcement to the states — but recognize that this is at best a half-measure. The federal government should not just back off; it should affirmatively dismantle the apparatus of prohibition. Reschedule or deschedule marijuana. Repeal mandatory minimums. Redirect interdiction budgets to treatment, prevention, and harm reduction. End civil asset forfeiture, which has corrupted policing at every level. Pardon nonviolent drug offenders. Treat addiction the way we treat diabetes — as a medical condition requiring ongoing care, not a moral failing requiring punishment.

The states-versus-federal debate is useful chiefly as a way of opening up space for these larger reforms. Each state that moves toward treatment or legalization makes the federal status quo a little harder to defend. Eventually, perhaps, enough cracks will open in the dam that the whole thing comes down. That, not a tidier division of prosecutorial labor, is what we should be aiming for.
