Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Saez and Zucman have done the country a service by dragging the question of how — and whether — to tax extreme wealth into the center of our political debate. Their diagnosis is largely persuasive: the federal tax system has become a flat tax that turns regressive at the very top, the corporate tax has been gutted, the estate tax is a husk, and a handful of fortunes have grown to historically unusual sizes. Madison's worry that "an immoderate, and especially an unmerited, accumulation of riches" corrupts republican government is not an antiquarian one. Where I depart from Saez and Zucman is not on the goal but on the instrument. Their wealth tax is more administratively fragile, more legally vulnerable, and more politically combustible than its closest cousins — and several of those cousins would deliver most of what they want with fewer of these defects.

Start with valuation. Saez and Zucman argue persuasively that listed equities, bonds, and most real estate can be valued without much fuss, and that the remaining roughly 30 percent of top-end wealth held in private firms can be handled through a "pay in kind" option: if the IRS overvalues Cargill, the family hands over shares, which the government auctions. This is clever, but it papers over real difficulties. Many closely-held assets are bundled with control rights, debt covenants, restrictive shareholder agreements, and family voting trusts that make a 2-percent slice non-equivalent to a 2-percent equity claim. The Cargill family will not cheerfully accept the IRS putting up tranches of voting stock to "private equity funds, foundations, or other rich families" each year — they will sue, they will restructure, and they will lobby. The mature literature on minority-discount and marketability-discount valuation exists precisely because shares in closely held firms do not trade like ADM shares. None of this is insurmountable, but it is far more contested than the essay concedes, and the IRS — whose enforcement budget has fallen roughly 20 percent in real terms over the past decade — is not currently equipped for the running gun battle that would ensue. A wealth tax stands or falls on enforcement, and enforcement requires institutional capacity that Congress has spent forty years dismantling.

Next, the constitutional question. Saez and Zucman do not address it, but they should. Article I, Section 9 requires that "direct taxes" be apportioned among the states by population. The Sixteenth Amendment carved out an exception for income taxes; whether a tax on the stock of wealth is "direct" within the original meaning is genuinely unsettled. Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust (1895) treated a tax on income from property as a direct tax — a holding the Sixteenth Amendment overturned for income only. A wealth tax will face an immediate constitutional challenge before a Supreme Court that is, on present membership, unlikely to be sympathetic. This is not an idle worry: even an eventual win on the merits would mean years of uncertainty, during which the revenue and the deterrent effect both evaporate. Any responsible advocate of a wealth tax must have a plan for what happens if the Court invalidates it, and "we'll amend the Constitution" is not a plan.

Now consider the European evidence, which the essay dismisses too briskly. A dozen OECD countries had wealth taxes in 1990; only three or four still do. The reasons varied — capital flight in small open economies, exemptions that swallowed the base, valuation disputes, administrative costs that ran high relative to receipts — but the pattern is striking and Saez and Zucman owe readers a fuller engagement with it than the parenthetical they offer. The United States is admittedly different: it taxes citizens on worldwide income, it is large, and it has deeper financial markets. These factors mitigate some of the European failure modes. But the European experience suggests that political durability, not first-year revenue projections, is the binding constraint, and durability is exactly what an exotic, litigation-prone tax administered against well-lawyered families is likely to lack.

Here is the better path, in my view. Almost everything Saez and Zucman want can be accomplished by taxing income properly. The mechanism that lets Buffett accumulate untaxed gains inside Berkshire is the realization doctrine combined with step-up basis at death. Senator Wyden and others have sketched a mark-to-market regime for tradeable assets held by very wealthy taxpayers: each year, gains on publicly traded securities are taxed as if realized, with deferral and an interest charge available for non-tradeable assets. This is, in effect, a wealth tax on the appreciation of wealth — closer to the constitutional safe harbor of the Sixteenth Amendment, more aligned with how we already think about income, and free of the recurring valuation problem for the 70 percent of top-end assets that have market prices. Pair it with the abolition of step-up basis, a serious minimum corporate tax on book income, restoration of estate tax thresholds and enforcement, and a top ordinary rate in the 50–60 percent range, and the regressive curl at the top of the U.S. tax distribution disappears. Buffett, on Saez and Zucman's own numbers, would owe taxes on roughly $3 billion of accrued gains a year rather than on the trickle he chooses to realize.

This leaves the "radical" wealth tax, which the authors concede is not really about revenue but about de-concentration — a Pigouvian levy on oligarchy. I am sympathetic to the underlying intuition that extreme wealth is itself a public bad, in the way that extreme carbon is. But the analogy is also instructive. We tax carbon because we have a defensible estimate of the social cost of a ton of CO₂; we do not yet have anything comparable for the marginal billionaire. The case for confiscating most of Bezos's fortune over time has to rest on a political-economy judgment that the policy will not, in the process, induce constitutional crisis, capital flight, or backlash that swamps its democratic benefits. That judgment is contestable.

Finally, a word about timing. As I write, financial markets are collapsing under a pandemic and the federal government is preparing to spend trillions to keep the economy alive. Whatever one thinks about wealth taxation in principle, the next year will be a brutal stress test of the proposition that the rich can be made to pay. If the recovery is financed by money creation and middle-class taxes while asset values rebound for those who hold them, the political case Saez and Zucman make will only get sharper. The question is whether we answer it with the instrument they propose, or with something stronger because it is simpler.

The argument for a federal wealth tax has found prominent champions in Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman, who make a spirited case that such a levy is not only necessary to restore tax justice but also essential to protect democracy from the corrosive effects of extreme wealth concentration. Their diagnosis of rising inequality and a tax system that has grown far less progressive over recent decades is largely accurate. Yet the prescription—a direct annual tax on household net worth, starting with a moderate 2 percent and escalating in a more radical vision to confiscatory rates—stumbles on a series of legal, practical, and economic obstacles that its advocates too quickly dismiss.

The first and most formidable barrier is the U.S. Constitution. Article I requires that direct taxes be apportioned among the states according to population, a formula that makes an individual wealth tax all but impossible to administer because tax liability would vary depending on which state a person lives in. The Sixteenth Amendment carved out an exception only for taxes on incomes, not on property. The Supreme Court has long treated taxes on the ownership of property as direct taxes requiring apportionment. Proponents sometimes suggest that a wealth tax could be structured as an income tax on the imputed return from wealth, but that workaround is legally precarious. Even a tax labeled a “wealth tax” would face an immediate legal challenge and a strong likelihood of being struck down by a conservative judiciary. Ignoring this constitutional reality is not a minor oversight; it is the central reason the United States relies on income and estate taxes rather than an annual net-worth tax. To enact a wealth tax, we would first need either a constitutional amendment or a revolutionary shift in Supreme Court doctrine—neither of which is on the horizon.

Even if the constitutional hurdle could be cleared, the practical problems of implementation would prove far thornier than the clean-sounding “create the missing market” solution suggests. Saez and Zucman point out that about 70 percent of the wealth of the top 0.1 percent consists of assets with readily observable market prices, such as publicly traded shares and real estate. But the remaining 30 percent—mainly stakes in privately held businesses—is concentrated in exactly the fortunes the tax aims to reach. Take Cargill, the giant agribusiness they cite. Updating a 1992 valuation using profit multiples of public competitors is not a trivial exercise; private firms differ from their public counterparts in capital structure, risk profile, and growth prospects, and reasonable experts often produce wildly divergent estimates. When the IRS arrives at a figure, the owners will predictably claim it is too high. The proposed remedy—that the government accept shares in kind and auction them—turns the tax collector into an unwilling and unsuitable stakeholder. The IRS would suddenly own fractional interests in private companies, with all the governance headaches that implies, from voting on board seats to handling lawsuits. Then it would have to sell those illiquid stakes into a market it has itself conjured, likely at distressed prices, depressing the very value the original tax calculation assumed. This is not a serious administrative solution; it is an invitation to chaos and litigation on a scale that makes the current tax disputes seem quaint.

Liquidity concerns, too, deserve more than a hand-wave. It is easy to scoff at billionaires who claim they lack cash, but the economic substance matters. An entrepreneur whose wealth is tied up in a fast-growing but profitless startup would face a tax bill that could force the sale of equity at an inopportune moment, diluting control and potentially harming the firm’s prospects. Allowing payment in shares still forces a divestiture, which may not be costless for the business or for other stakeholders, including employees. The property taxes that the middle class pays on real estate are different in kind: they are levied on an asset class that is immobile and typically not the primary source of productive investment. Financial wealth, by contrast, is highly mobile and its forced liquidation can have wider ripple effects.

The invocation of the mid‐twentieth‐century experience with sky-high top income tax rates also deserves scrutiny. It is true that statutory marginal rates exceeded 90 percent in the 1950s, but the effective tax rates paid by the very rich were much lower because of generous deductions, exemptions, and the simple fact that few people earned enough in taxable income to face those rates. The steep rates did not so much compress top incomes as redirect them into untaxed or lightly taxed forms—perks, retained corporate earnings, and tax-exempt bonds. Moreover, the postwar decades of shared prosperity were the product of a unique conjunction of factors—European and Japanese industrial devastation, pent-up demand, high union density, and a relatively closed global economy—that cannot be replicated by a tax code tweak. The idea that confiscatory taxes caused the broad-based growth of that era mistakes correlation for causation.

International evidence, too, cuts against the desirability of a wealth tax. Between 1990 and 2017, a dozen European countries repealed their wealth taxes after experiencing capital flight, administrative complexity, and disappointing revenue yields. France, often cited as a cautionary tale, saw its wealth tax chase away thousands of wealthy individuals and capital before being converted into a tax on real estate alone in 2018. Advocates counter that those taxes were poorly designed with low exemption thresholds and myriad loopholes, and that a U.S. version with a very high threshold could avoid those failings. But even well-designed taxes must contend with the reality of globalized capital markets: a wealth tax puts a large annual price on simply holding assets in the United States. Some ultrarich would restructure holdings or change residency, and a few would renounce citizenship altogether. Even if only a fraction do so, the loss of their entrepreneurial activity, investment, and consumption can dampen economic dynamism—a cost the wealth tax’s proponents have not fully factored into their calculus.

None of this is to deny the urgent need to address extreme inequality. It is to say that the wealth tax is a misguided instrument for that goal. Far better to double down on the progressive taxes we already have, closing the gaping holes that allow billionaires to pay trivial rates. Tax capital gains at death rather than letting heirs inherit assets with a stepped-up basis. End the carried interest loophole that lets investment managers treat their fees as capital gains. Raise the top marginal income tax rate and, crucially, tighten the rules that let wealthy individuals defer and avoid taxation of investment income. Pair those with a robust estate tax and a corporate minimum tax that prevents profit-shifting to tax havens. Such an approach avoids the constitutional landmine, respects the hard-won administrative capacity of the IRS, and keeps the government out of the business of owning private companies.

Extreme wealth concentration is indeed a threat to democracy, as Madison grasped. But the remedy must be calibrated to our legal and economic reality, not to a theoretical ideal that cannot withstand contact with the messy world of implementation. A wealth tax, however elegant on the page, is a bridge too far.

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman offer a compelling, historically grounded diagnosis of a profound structural problem: the American tax system has become terrifyingly regressive at the very top. As they rightly note, the ability of ultra-high-net-worth individuals to accumulate vast fortunes while reporting negligible taxable income is an affront to the concept of tax justice. When Warren Buffett pays a lower effective tax rate than his secretary, the system is fundamentally broken. Furthermore, Saez and Zucman are correct that extreme wealth concentration threatens democratic institutions. However, while their diagnosis is unimpeachable, their prescription—a progressive and potentially "radical" wealth tax—relies on administrative fantasies and risks severe economic collateral damage. We can solve the taxation gap at the top without resorting to a policy that requires the federal government to become a compulsory shareholder in private American enterprise.

The most glaring flaw in Saez and Zucman’s proposal lies in their cavalier approach to the valuation and liquidity of private assets. To their credit, they acknowledge that valuing privately held, illiquid companies is the traditional Achilles' heel of wealth tax proposals. Yet their proposed solution to this hurdle is staggering in its implications. To solve the problem of valuing a massive private enterprise like Cargill, they suggest that the IRS simply allow the owners to pay their tax "in kind" by handing over company shares, which the government would then auction off to the highest bidder.

This "missing market" solution is administratively dystopian. It would fundamentally transform the Internal Revenue Service into the largest liquidator of private assets in world history. Consider the mechanics: the IRS would continuously receive equity in thousands of private businesses, venture-backed startups, family farms, and real estate partnerships. The government would then be tasked with auctioning these shares. But private company shares are typically encumbered by complex shareholder agreements, restrictions on transferability, and varying voting rights. Who buys a 2 percent minority stake in a closely held family business at a fair market price when the family still controls the board? The buyers would likely be private equity firms purchasing these captive shares at steep discounts, effectively penalizing the original owners far beyond the statutory tax rate and creating a new avenue for rent-seeking by Wall Street.

Furthermore, the in-kind payment system creates perverse incentives regarding corporate control. Saez and Zucman note that under their radical 10 percent wealth tax, Jeff Bezos would be forced to pay a staggering percentage of his wealth annually. Because his wealth is tied up in Amazon stock, he would have to sell off massive portions of his ownership every year. While the authors view this de-concentration of wealth as a feature rather than a bug, they ignore the systemic corporate governance issues it creates. Forcing founders to constantly liquidate their holdings inevitably strips them of control over the companies they built, transferring voting power to institutional investors and index funds who prioritize short-term quarterly returns over long-term innovation. If entrepreneurs know that reaching a certain valuation means the government will systematically dismantle their equity and control, the incentive to scale businesses to a massive, globally competitive size within the United States will be severely diminished.

We must also look to recent history, which Saez and Zucman dismiss too hastily. Over the last three decades, the vast majority of European countries that implemented wealth taxes have repealed them. France, Germany, Sweden, and others discovered that wealth taxes raised disappointing amounts of revenue while triggering massive capital flight and incurring exorbitant administrative costs. The authors argue that the U.S. can avoid this through strict enforcement and exit taxes, but capital is remarkably fluid. While a billionaire might not renounce their U.S. citizenship immediately, a radical wealth tax would absolutely alter where new capital is deployed and where future entrepreneurs choose to incorporate and grow their businesses. In a globally integrated economy, unilaterally declaring a war of attrition on capital accumulation is a recipe for economic stagnation.

Fortunately, we do not need to accept the massive administrative burdens and economic distortions of a wealth tax to ensure the ultra-rich pay their fair share. The American tax code already contains the architectural foundation necessary to capture this wealth; it simply requires modernization.

The true engine of tax avoidance for the billionaire class is the realization principle—the rule that capital gains are only taxed when an asset is sold. Because the ultra-wealthy can borrow against their unsold stock to fund their lifestyles (a strategy known as "buy, borrow, die"), they never realize the income and thus never pay the tax. The most elegant solution to this is to implement a "mark-to-market" tax system for the ultra-wealthy. Under this system, billionaires would pay annual taxes on the paper gains of their publicly traded assets. If Jeff Bezos’s Amazon stock appreciates by $10 billion in a given year, he would owe capital gains tax on that amount, regardless of whether he sold the shares. This directly targets the wealth accumulation mechanism of the modern plutocracy without requiring the IRS to appraise private companies or seize unlisted shares. For illiquid private assets where mark-to-market is difficult, the tax code could apply a retrospective capital gains tax with a deferral interest charge upon the eventual sale, erasing the benefit of deferring the tax.

Additionally, we must eliminate the "stepped-up basis" loophole at death. Currently, when a billionaire dies, the capital gains on their unsold assets are wiped out, allowing their heirs to inherit the wealth completely tax-free. Taxing capital gains at death, combined with a robust and heavily enforced estate tax, would achieve Saez and Zucman’s goal of de-concentrating dynastic wealth over time, but at the point of transfer rather than through an invasive annual wealth appraisal.

Saez and Zucman are absolutely correct that the status quo is indefensible. The tax system must be a tool for democratic preservation, and extreme inequality is a corrosive force. When billionaires pay a lower effective tax rate than the working class, the social contract is fractured. But the authors' proposed wealth tax—and particularly the radical version they entertain—is a blunt, structurally flawed instrument. By relying on in-kind payments and government auctions of private equity, it invites administrative chaos and massive economic distortion.

We can achieve the moral and economic imperatives of the wealth tax without the wealth tax itself. By taxing capital gains as they accrue for the ultra-rich, closing the loopholes that allow generational wealth transfers to escape taxation, and adequately funding the IRS to enforce the laws already on the books, we can restore progressivity to the American tax system. Reforming the definition of taxable income to reflect modern financial reality is a far more practical, enforceable, and economically sound path to tax justice than attempting to inventory and seize the wealth of the nation year after year.

Saez and Zucman are right about the central fact: the American tax system has lost much of its ability to reach the very top. A person whose income comes as wages encounters withholding, payroll taxes, and regular reporting. A person whose economic income appears as the rising value of a company can often choose when, whether, and how much to realize. That asymmetry is not a loophole at the margins; it is a basic architectural feature of the modern tax state. It helps explain why many Americans find the tax code both unfair and mystifying. The nurse’s paycheck is transparent to the IRS; the billionaire’s appreciation is often invisible until sale, borrowing, gift, or death.

They are also right that the issue is not merely revenue. Extreme wealth is not only purchasing power. It is agenda-setting power, veto power, philanthropic power, media power, and political power. The United States need not believe that every large fortune is ill-gotten to worry that a society of hundred-billion-dollar fortunes will become less democratic over time. Campaign finance law, antitrust law, labor law, corporate governance, and public financing of elections all matter here. But taxation is inevitably part of the answer, because taxation is one of the few instruments that can operate at the scale of the fortunes themselves.

Still, the case for taxing the superrich is stronger than the case for any particular wealth tax design. Saez and Zucman sometimes write as though the choice is between an annual wealth tax and resignation to plutocracy. That is too narrow. The United States has several ways to tax wealth more effectively: taxing capital gains at death, ending stepped-up basis, strengthening the estate and gift tax, taxing unrealized gains of the very rich on a mark-to-market basis where valuation is straightforward, raising corporate taxes while limiting avoidance, and enforcing existing law with a rebuilt IRS. A wealth tax may belong in that toolkit, but it should not be treated as the only serious instrument.

The most compelling argument for a wealth tax is that income is too easily manipulated. Buffett can avoid dividends; founders can borrow against appreciated shares; private equity managers can convert labor income into capital gains; multinational firms can shift profits. But annual net worth is also manipulable. The superrich do not simply hold piles of Berkshire Hathaway stock. They hold family partnerships, trusts, carried interests, private funds, art, real estate entities, derivatives, intellectual property, and claims on future income streams. Some of these assets can be valued; others can be valued only with wide bands of uncertainty. A tax system can tolerate some uncertainty, but annual recurrence magnifies every dispute. If a valuation question must be fought every year, the administrative burden is not incidental.

Saez and Zucman’s proposed solution—allowing payment in kind—is ingenious but not a cure-all. If a family that owns a private company disputes the IRS valuation, it could hand over shares and let the government sell them. In theory this creates the missing market. In practice, the sale price of a small, illiquid, minority stake in a controlled family business may reflect lack of control, lack of information, restrictions on transfer, and strategic behavior by potential buyers. A buyer may demand a steep discount precisely because the shares do not confer governance rights. The family may then argue that the sale proves the IRS overvalued the firm. The government could try to auction a larger or more powerful stake, but then the tax begins to alter corporate control, not merely collect public revenue. Perhaps that is acceptable in some cases, but it should be acknowledged as a choice rather than a technical fix.

The European experience does not prove that wealth taxes are impossible, but it does counsel modesty. Several European countries repealed or narrowed wealth taxes because they raised less revenue than hoped, encouraged avoidance or emigration, and created administrative headaches. France’s wealth tax became politically notorious even though its revenue was limited; Sweden and Germany abandoned their versions under different pressures. The United States is larger, has citizenship-based taxation, and could design a better system with better third-party reporting. It also has a uniquely sophisticated financial sector that can help value assets—but that same sector can help transform assets into harder-to-tax forms. The lesson is not “don’t try.” It is “design for adversaries.”

A moderate wealth tax with a very high exemption threshold is therefore much more plausible than a radical one. A tax of 2 or 3 percent on fortunes above $50 million or $1 billion would still be ambitious, but it would leave room for normal returns, valuation error, and compliance costs. A 10 percent annual tax on billionaires is a different creature. Saez and Zucman embrace that difference: the point is not simply to raise money but to reduce the number and size of large fortunes. That goal is defensible, but it raises harder questions than their carbon analogy suggests.

Carbon emissions are a social harm independent of who emits them. Wealth is more complicated. Some fortunes reflect monopoly, rent extraction, inherited privilege, or political favoritism. Others reflect genuine innovation, scale, and consumer surplus, even if our tax code then allows the resulting gains to compound too lightly taxed. A tax that deliberately makes it difficult to remain a multibillionaire may be justified as a democratic safeguard, but it will also tax patient ownership, company-building, and concentrated founder stakes. If the concern is monopoly power, antitrust law may be better targeted. If the concern is dynastic inheritance, estate and gift taxation are better targeted. If the concern is political influence, campaign finance and lobbying rules are better targeted. Wealth taxation can supplement these, but it cannot substitute for them.

There is also the question of constitutional risk. Many scholars believe a federal wealth tax could survive as an indirect tax or as a tax distinguishable from the unapportioned direct taxes prohibited by the Constitution. Others disagree, pointing to the constitutional treatment of direct taxes and the history that led to the Sixteenth Amendment. A wealth tax would almost certainly face immediate litigation. That does not mean policymakers should avoid it, but it does mean that progressives should not put all their eggs in a basket the Supreme Court may throw out. Repealing stepped-up basis, taxing capital gains at death, raising estate tax rates, tightening grantor trusts, improving information reporting, and taxing publicly traded assets of billionaires on an accrual basis would face fewer constitutional obstacles.

The income tax can also be made to reach more wealth than it currently does. For publicly traded securities, annual mark-to-market taxation for the very richest households is administratively easier than a comprehensive wealth tax. If Jeff Bezos’s Amazon shares rise by $10 billion in a year, the gain can be measured. Taxing that gain annually, with loss offsets and rules for liquidity, would attack the “buy, borrow, die” strategy directly. For private assets, a retrospective tax upon sale or transfer—with an interest charge for deferral—could reduce the advantage of waiting without requiring yearly appraisals of every asset. This approach is imperfect, but it aims at the core problem: untaxed appreciation.

None of this diminishes the moral force of Saez and Zucman’s argument. The United States has allowed the estate tax to wither, the corporate tax to erode, capital gains preferences to persist, and enforcement against sophisticated taxpayers to decline. Meanwhile, payroll taxes fall heavily on work, and state and local taxes often burden lower- and middle-income families. A democratic society can reasonably ask why someone with $50 billion should be permitted to decide almost entirely for himself when his economic income becomes taxable.

The best path is a layered one. Restore the IRS’s capacity to audit complex wealth. End stepped-up basis and tax large gains at death. Strengthen the estate and gift tax, including rules on trusts and valuation discounts. Tax capital gains more like labor income. Adopt mark-to-market rules for the ultrawealthy where market prices exist, and deferral charges where they do not. Raise corporate taxation while coordinating internationally against profit shifting. Then consider a moderate wealth tax as a backstop for the largest fortunes, with strong reporting rules, an exit tax, and careful treatment of illiquid assets.

Saez and Zucman have done a great service by shifting the debate from whether extreme wealth should be taxed to how. But “how” matters. A tax system that overpromises and underdelivers could discredit the very egalitarian project they seek to advance. The goal should be radical in ambition and sober in design: not symbolic punishment of billionaires, but a durable fiscal constitution for a democracy in which private fortunes no longer tower over public power.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The proposal advanced by Saez and Zucman rests on a compelling diagnosis of America's distorted tax system. Their observation that the current regime has become regressive at the very top—where the 400 richest Americans pay lower effective rates than ordinary workers—is both troubling and well-supported by available evidence. The Buffett example, though now familiar, remains instructive: a man worth $62 billion whose effective federal income tax rate falls below one-tenth of one percent exemplifies precisely the failure that any reform must address. I share the authors' conviction that extreme wealth concentration poses genuine risks to democratic governance, and I agree that the income tax alone cannot solve the problem of billionaire tax avoidance.

Yet several considerations warrant deeper attention before embracing the radical wealth tax as the preferred remedy. First, the administrative complexities are more formidable than the authors acknowledge. While Saez and Zucman correctly note that most wealth at the top consists of easily-valued assets like publicly traded equities, the harder cases matter most. Private businesses, for which no market price exists, represent a significant portion of elite fortunes. The Cargill example illustrates this: almost three decades have passed since any shares changed hands, and any IRS valuation would rest on contested assumptions about future earnings and comparable firms. The authors propose an in-kind payment mechanism that theoretically solves this problem, but creating functioning markets for illiquid assets is easier to describe than to execute. The practical challenges of valuation, dispute resolution, and enforcement would be substantial, and a wealth tax's fairness depends entirely on whether it can be applied consistently.

Second, the incentive effects, though downplayed by the authors, deserve more serious treatment. Saez and Zucman argue that high marginal income tax rates during the mid-twentieth century did not harm economic growth, and they are correct that the historical record does not support simple claims that high marginal rates destroy incentives. But a wealth tax operates differently from an income tax. It taxes returns to existing wealth regardless of whether those returns materialize, and it does so even when the underlying assets lose value. A billionaire whose portfolio falls from $2 billion to $1.5 billion in a market crash still owes $60 million annually under the proposed 3 percent rate on fortunes above $1 billion. Such a tax could amplify procyclical pressures and penalize long-term investment horizons. The authors' own estimates suggest that a radical wealth tax would have modest revenue effects over time as fortunes erode—but that erosion represents real losses for individuals who made productive investments. The question is not whether deconcentration is desirable but whether this mechanism achieves it without unacceptable collateral damage.

Third, and perhaps most fundamentally, the political economy of tax enforcement matters enormously. The reason the estate tax has become "moribund" is not that it was theoretically unsound but that the ultra-wealthy have mounted sustained campaigns to weaken it. A wealth tax would face identical political pressures, and the complexity of compliance and enforcement would provide endless opportunities for circumvention. The authors correctly note that wealth taxes have worked in some European countries, but they also acknowledge the significant failures elsewhere—failures that stemmed largely from evasion and political reversals rather than inherent design flaws.

A more promising approach might combine a modest wealth tax with fundamental reforms to capital gains taxation, inheritance reform, and international tax coordination. The goal of requiring billionaires to pay taxes commensurate with their ability to pay is achievable; whether it requires specifically the radical mechanism Saez and Zucman propose remains genuinely uncertain.

The Case for Going Further Than Saez and Zucman

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman have done the country a service. By translating abstract concerns about plutocracy into a concrete fiscal instrument, they have moved the conversation forward in a way that decades of hand-wringing about inequality never could. Their central insight—that the income tax, however we tinker with it, structurally fails to reach the kind of unrealized appreciation that constitutes most billionaire fortunes—is correct and important. Their second insight—that quasi-confiscatory rates are not a regrettable side effect of a revenue grab but the whole point—is even more important. I want to argue, however, that their proposal is still too timid, and that the moment in which we now find ourselves makes the case for going further.

Consider the present situation. As I write, equity markets have shed nearly a third of their value in three weeks. A pandemic is shuttering economies across the world. Workers in restaurants, hotels, gig platforms, and small retail are about to discover, if they have not already, that they have no real savings to fall back on, no employer-provided sick leave, and no meaningful safety net beyond an unemployment insurance system designed for short cyclical downturns. Meanwhile, the people who own the means of production—and increasingly that means a few hundred families and a few thousand institutional investors—will absorb paper losses they can afford to absorb and, history suggests, will buy heavily into the trough. The crisis we are entering will end with wealth more concentrated, not less, unless we act.

Saez and Zucman's moderate wealth tax (2 percent over $50 million, 3 percent over $1 billion) raises about one percent of GDP. That is real money, but it is not transformative. Their radical alternative—10 percent above a billion—begins to bite, but even their own simulations show Mark Zuckerberg still worth $21 billion in a counterfactual world where the tax had been in place all along. This is what "radical" buys you: a Facebook founder who is merely twenty-one times richer than a normal lifetime of work could yield, rather than sixty-one times richer. We should be honest that this is still an enormous concentration of private power.

If we take Madison and the authors at their word—that the problem is power, not inequality of consumption—then the appropriate rate structure is one designed to make the existence of multi-decabillionaires essentially impossible over a generation. A wealth tax that hits, say, 12 percent above $1 billion and 15 percent above $10 billion, combined with restored top marginal income tax rates of 70 percent above $5 million and a genuine estate tax with no step-up in basis, would within twenty years return the wealth distribution to something closer to the 1960s. The lesson of Saez and Zucman's own historical chart is that the period of greatest American shared prosperity coincided with rates that today's commentariat would call "unthinkable." They were thinkable then. They built the interstate highways and the universities and the suburbs.

The objections to this aggressive posture come in two flavors. The first is the technical worry about valuation, liquidity, and avoidance. Saez and Zucman handle this elegantly with their pay-in-kind proposal: if you think the IRS overvalues your private company, hand over shares and let the market clear them. This is brilliant precisely because it puts the burden of proof where it belongs—on the holder, not the tax authority—and it generates real price discovery for assets that today exist in a fog of self-serving appraisals. The same logic could be extended to art, jewelry, and other valuables: declare a value, pay tax on it, and accept that the government has the right to purchase the item at your declared value. Overnight, valuation games end.

The second objection is about innovation and dynamism. Won't a 12 percent wealth tax destroy the incentive to build? Here I think we have to be empirical and unsentimental. Jeff Bezos did not build Amazon because he was promised the right to hold $130 billion in personal wealth at the age of 56. He built it because he wanted to build Amazon, and because the first hundred million, or perhaps the first billion, was already more than any human being can plausibly use. Beyond a certain point, the marginal motivational value of additional wealth is zero, while the marginal social cost—through political influence, through control over media and platforms, through the ability to wait out competitors—is enormous and rising. The "innovation tax" of confiscatory rates at the very top is a fiction; we have run the experiment, in the United States, from 1945 to 1975, and growth was faster than it has been since.

There is, however, a deeper issue Saez and Zucman dance around but never quite confront. A wealth tax taxes accumulated stock. It does not change the flows that produce extreme wealth in the first place. As long as we have an economy structured to channel the surplus from labor and from network effects upward to a small class of owners, we will keep producing billionaires faster than we tax them away. A truly serious agenda for the de-concentration of economic power would pair the radical wealth tax with: vigorous antitrust enforcement against the platform monopolies; sectoral bargaining and codetermination to push wages and conditions back up at the source; a public option in banking, in pharmaceuticals, and in broadband to discipline rentier sectors; and a serious campaign finance regime that breaks the loop between private fortune and political access.

In other words, the wealth tax should not be the centerpiece of an anti-plutocracy program; it should be the backstop. The front line is making the economy produce less extreme inequality in the first place. The wealth tax then catches whatever the front line misses and grinds it down before it can ossify into hereditary class.

I want to close on a note about political economy. Saez and Zucman observe, almost in passing, that their own thinking has evolved—that they now believe society would benefit from curbing huge fortunes even if it meant lower tax revenue in the long run. This is an important admission and it deserves emphasis. The wealth tax debate has been conducted in a register of accountancy: how much will it raise, what will the elasticities be, can we plug holes faster than billionaires can drill them. But the real question is constitutional in the small-c sense: what kind of republic do we want to be? A republic in which a single individual can spend $500 million on a presidential campaign without noticing the dent, or one in which political voice is roughly proportionate to citizenship rather than to net worth?

Madison, whom Saez and Zucman rightly quote, was unambiguous. The "silent operation of laws" should reduce "extreme wealth towards a state of mediocrity." Note the language: not toward modest comfort, not toward affluence, but toward mediocrity. The founders, for all their flaws, understood that a republic of equals cannot coexist with a class of demigods. We have spent forty years pretending otherwise, and the result is the politics we have, the inequality we have, and the public health and infrastructure capacities we are about to discover we no longer have.

A radical wealth tax is not radical enough. But it is the right place to start, and Saez and Zucman deserve credit for laying out the blueprint with such care. The remaining argument is about ambition. In a moment when the economy is buckling and millions of Americans are about to learn how thin the floor beneath them is, I do not think we can afford to be modest.

A Wealth Tax Is the Wrong Instrument

Saez and Zucman have written a sophisticated brief for a policy that, on closer inspection, will not do what they say it will, cannot be administered the way they propose, and would produce a long list of consequences they have not adequately addressed. I share their concern about the political effects of extreme wealth concentration. I do not share their faith that a federal wealth tax is the right tool for the job, and I want to lay out several reasons why a sympathetic reader should remain skeptical.

Start with the most basic problem: the Constitution. Article I, Section 9 prohibits any "direct tax" that is not apportioned among the states by population. The Sixteenth Amendment carved out an exception for taxes on "incomes, from whatever source derived," but it did not abolish the direct tax clause; it created a single, narrow exception. A federal tax assessed annually on the stock of a person's net worth is, on any natural reading, a direct tax. It is not a tax on a transaction, on a transfer, or on income; it is a tax on the mere fact of owning property. The Supreme Court in Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust (1895) struck down the income tax precisely because it taxed income from property, which the Court treated as equivalent to taxing the property itself. To save such a tax under current doctrine, one would have to convince a Court that includes five committed originalists that the framers' direct tax clause means something different from what it says. Constitutional law scholars are divided, but the prudent assumption is that a wealth tax would face years of litigation and quite possibly be struck down. Saez and Zucman do not even mention this problem. That is a striking omission in an essay framed as a policy blueprint.

Set aside the Constitution and turn to administration. The authors are confident that valuation is a soluble problem, and they propose a clever pay-in-kind mechanism for illiquid assets. The clever mechanism has been celebrated in the press; it has been studied very little. Consider what it would actually involve. The IRS, today an agency so under-resourced that it audits the working poor at higher rates than the affluent, would be tasked with annually estimating the market value of every privately held business, every closely held partnership, every piece of high-end art, every parcel of undeveloped land, every interest in a hedge fund's carried-interest position. For Cargill, perhaps, public comparables exist. But the United States has tens of thousands of substantial private firms with no clean public peers, owned through layers of trusts and LLCs that themselves require valuation. Each of these valuations will be contested. Each contested valuation will produce litigation. The authors' answer—take the shares as payment—is elegant in theory and bizarre in practice. Who will manage the federal portfolio of forced-sale stakes in family businesses? At what price will the government actually clear minority equity interests in companies whose controlling shareholders are hostile to outside ownership? The discounts will be brutal. The administrative cost will be enormous. And the most sophisticated taxpayers will simply restructure to ensure that there is nothing clean to value.

The European experience deserves more attention than Saez and Zucman give it. Twelve OECD countries had wealth taxes in 1990. Today, three do. Sweden, France, Germany, Austria, Denmark, Finland, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg all repealed theirs. The reasons differed, but they shared a common pattern: high administrative costs, modest revenue, distortions in business structure and asset holding, and capital flight that proved hard to measure but easy to feel politically. The authors note these failures and wave them away with the claim that the United States is different—larger, with worldwide taxation of citizens, with more sophisticated financial markets. There is something to this. But it is also true that the U.S. has never tried to value, annually, the entire stock of capital owned by its richest citizens. The presumption ought to be that an unprecedented administrative undertaking will encounter unprecedented problems.

There is also the question of behavioral response. Saez and Zucman's revenue estimates depend on relatively modest elasticities. But the truly rich are not typical taxpayers. They have armies of advisors, mobility, and—crucially—patience. A wealth tax with marginal rates of 6 or 8 percent on net worth above $1 billion is, in present value terms over a normal investment lifetime, the equivalent of confiscating a very large fraction of one's principal. The expected behavioral response is not "shrug and pay." It is a comprehensive restructuring of how wealth is held: foundations, dynasty trusts, expatriation (for those willing to pay the exit tax), aggressive valuation discounts, charitable vehicles that retain de facto family control, and migration of business activity to forms and jurisdictions that resist measurement. Some of these responses produce social benefits (more charitable giving); many do not. None of them produce the clean revenue stream the authors project.

Saez and Zucman are aware of avoidance and dismiss it as a matter of "political will." This is too easy. Tax avoidance is not primarily a matter of insufficient enforcement budgets. It is a matter of the structural difficulty of taxing a stock rather than a flow in a financialized economy where ownership can be sliced, layered, and synthesized indefinitely. We have decades of evidence that even the simple income tax is gamed effectively by the very rich. There is no reason to believe a more complex tax, on a more ambiguous base, will be gamed less.

I want to be clear: I agree with the diagnosis. The U.S. tax system is far less progressive at the top than it should be. Buffett's effective rate is scandalous. The decline of the corporate tax and the gutting of the estate tax are policy failures. But the right response is to reform the instruments we already have, not to invent a new one whose constitutional status is doubtful and whose administrative requirements are extraordinary.

What would such reforms look like? First, end the step-up in basis at death. This single change—closing the loophole that lets unrealized gains escape income taxation entirely when the holder dies—would do more to reach Buffett-style wealth than a wealth tax probably could. Second, mark-to-market the capital gains of the very wealthy on a sliding schedule, treating unrealized appreciation in publicly traded assets as income annually for taxpayers above some threshold. This is administratively far simpler than a wealth tax (for liquid assets, valuation is trivial) and it sits comfortably within the Sixteenth Amendment. Third, restore the corporate tax base. The 2017 cuts went too far; a 28 percent rate with a serious minimum tax on book income and a real global anti-avoidance regime would recover much of the progressivity Saez and Zucman rightly mourn. Fourth, rebuild the estate tax with a lower exemption, a higher rate, and the elimination of valuation games such as the discounting of family limited partnerships.

These reforms, taken together, would reach almost all of the income and wealth Saez and Zucman want to reach. They would do so through legal instruments that have constitutional sanction, decades of administrative experience, and far less scope for unintended consequence. They are less politically thrilling than the wealth tax, which is part of why they get less attention. But policy is not theater. The goal is to fix the actual problem in a way that survives litigation, administration, and the next election.

I close by noting one thing on which Saez and Zucman are absolutely right: extreme wealth concentration is a problem for democracy. The question is whether the cure they propose is one that will work, or whether it will become, like so many ambitious tax reforms before it, an occasion for litigation, evasion, and disappointment. I fear the latter. The Buffetts and Bezoses of the world deserve to be taxed more. We should tax them in ways that actually work.

The Mark-to-Market Alternative

There is a great deal to admire in Saez and Zucman's essay, and one large thing to dispute. The diagnosis is correct: our income tax leaks at the top because most billionaire wealth consists of unrealized capital gains, and we tax realization rather than appreciation. The historical narrative is correct: the high-rate, broad-base postwar system was more progressive than ours, and its erosion has fueled the rise in inequality at the very top. The Buffett example is devastating and clarifying. Where I part company with the authors is on the instrument. I think there is a less novel, more administrable, and constitutionally safer tool that gets us most of what a wealth tax would get, with fewer of the risks. That tool is a serious mark-to-market regime for the very wealthy.

The Buffett puzzle, as Saez and Zucman lay it out, is this: Berkshire Hathaway does not pay dividends. Buffett can sit on his unrealized gains indefinitely, selling only what he needs for personal consumption. He pays income tax on a trickle while his wealth compounds at the corporate level. When he dies, his heirs get a step-up in basis, and the lifetime of accumulated appreciation escapes income tax forever. This is a real scandal, and it is the central case for the wealth tax. But notice that the underlying problem has a different shape. Buffett's "income" in any economic sense is the change in the value of his Berkshire holdings each year. We have decided, by convention, not to tax that economic income until realization. The wealth tax responds to this by taxing the stock instead of the flow. A more direct response is to tax the flow we are currently missing: appreciation itself.

A mark-to-market regime for the top, say, 0.1 percent of households would work as follows. Each year, the change in the value of a taxpayer's marketable securities portfolio is treated as ordinary income (or perhaps as a separate category with its own rate). Declines produce losses that can be carried forward. For publicly traded assets, valuation is trivial: the IRS already knows the closing prices on December 31. For privately held businesses, there is genuine complexity, but it can be handled through deferral with an interest charge upon realization (a "retrospective" approach), so that the eventual tax matches what would have been paid had the gain been recognized annually. This combination—mark-to-market for liquid assets, deferral-with-interest for illiquid assets—has been studied for decades, in particular by tax scholars like David Miller, Daniel Halperin, and David Shakow. It is not exotic. Hedge fund managers already mark their books to market for various purposes. The information infrastructure exists.

What does this buy us, compared to a wealth tax?

First, constitutional safety. The Sixteenth Amendment authorizes taxes on "incomes, from whatever source derived." A tax on the annual change in the value of one's assets is plausibly a tax on income, since economic income is conventionally defined (Haig-Simons) as consumption plus the change in net worth. The wealth tax, by contrast, raises live questions under the direct tax clause that Saez and Zucman simply do not address. I would rather build my policy on solid constitutional ground than win a brilliant academic argument and lose at the Supreme Court.

Second, administrability. For the bulk of top-end wealth—publicly traded equities, bonds, fund interests—mark-to-market is mechanical. There is no need to value Cargill annually if Cargill's owners can pay interest on deferred gains, settling up when shares change hands. The pay-in-kind mechanism Saez and Zucman propose for the wealth tax is creative, but it puts the federal government in the business of running a private equity auction house every year. A mark-to-market regime mostly avoids this.

Third, integration with existing law. We already tax capital gains. We just tax them at the wrong moment and at the wrong rate. Mark-to-market is a modification of an existing system, not a wholly new tax. It can be enacted by ordinary statute. It can be enforced by existing IRS units. It dovetails with the elimination of stepped-up basis, which should accompany it.

Fourth, political durability. A wealth tax has, in the European experience, proved fragile—repealed in country after country as administrative burdens mounted and revenue disappointed. Income taxation, even when reformed at the top, tends to be politically stickier because it is part of an established system everyone understands.

What about the parts of Saez and Zucman's argument that a mark-to-market regime cannot fully address?

The most important is the de-concentration argument. The authors are explicit that their radical wealth tax (10 percent over $1 billion) is not primarily about revenue. It is about reducing the number of multi-billionaires, on the theory that very large fortunes corrupt democracy. Mark-to-market taxation at ordinary income rates—say, 50 percent including state and federal—would slow the accumulation of wealth at the top, but it would not actively grind down existing fortunes the way a 10 percent annual wealth tax does. A billionaire whose portfolio appreciates by 10 percent in a year would pay 5 percent of that, leaving net wealth growing at 5 percent per year. Compounding still works in their favor.

I think this is a real point but a smaller one than Saez and Zucman make it. Most existing billionaire fortunes were built through the appreciation of a single company stake—Bezos with Amazon, Zuckerberg with Facebook, Page and Brin with Google. Had these founders been subject to mark-to-market on their unrealized gains from the IPO forward, their net worth today would be a fraction of what it is. The wealth concentration we observe is, in large part, a consequence of the tax system's failure to reach exactly the appreciation that mark-to-market would reach. Fix that, and the de-concentration problem largely takes care of itself over a generation, without having to design a system that explicitly aims at confiscation.

There is also a normative issue I want to raise. The authors argue that "extreme wealth concentration, like carbon emissions, imposes a negative externality." This is a powerful analogy, but it should be applied carefully. We tax carbon because we have a reasonable measure of the externality (tons emitted) and a reasonable price for it (estimated social cost of carbon). We have no comparable measure for the political externality of a billionaire. Is the right Pigouvian rate 2 percent? 10 percent? Why not 20? Saez and Zucman's argument for the radical rate is essentially that it would produce a particular distributional outcome they prefer. That is a legitimate policy goal, but calling it Pigouvian taxation gives it a scientistic veneer it does not earn.

The honest version of their argument is closer to Madison's: a republic cannot tolerate concentrations of private power that rival public power. I agree. But the right response to a political problem is, often, a political solution—campaign finance reform, antitrust enforcement, structural separations between media and finance and politics—rather than a tax engineered to produce the desired political outcome through fiscal means.

In sum: yes to taxing the rich more, yes to closing the realization loophole, yes to ending stepped-up basis, yes to restoring the corporate tax and the estate tax. These are the steady, technocratic moves that will get us most of what Saez and Zucman want without betting the farm on an unprecedented and constitutionally fragile new instrument. The wealth tax has captured the political imagination, which is a virtue at a moment when timidity has run the country aground. But policy must survive contact with courts and accountants. Mark-to-market is the workhorse policy that does most of the same job, and it deserves more attention than it has received.

Wealth, Power, and the Limits of Fiscal Policy

Saez and Zucman build their case for an aggressive wealth tax on a fundamentally political premise: that extreme wealth concentration threatens democracy by translating economic power into political power. I want to take this premise seriously—more seriously, in fact, than they do—and ask whether their proposed remedy actually responds to the disease they have diagnosed.

The Madisonian framing with which the essay opens is striking. Madison did not worry about inequality primarily because of its consumption implications. He worried because he believed concentrated wealth would buy concentrated political influence, distort the operation of the laws, and turn the republic into something that wore republican clothing while operating on oligarchic principles. This is, I think, a correct diagnosis of contemporary America. The question is whether a wealth tax actually addresses it.

Consider what we know about how money translates into political power in the current United States. It does so through several distinct channels. There is direct campaign contribution, now largely unconstrained at the high end through Super PACs and dark-money vehicles. There is media ownership: Bezos owns the Washington Post, Bloomberg his eponymous empire, Murdoch the largest cable news operation in the country. There is foundation funding, which shapes the agendas of universities, think tanks, and advocacy groups. There is the revolving door, in which former officials are paid handsomely to ease the way for current donors. There is the soft power of social access—the dinners, the boards, the Davos invitations—which shapes what counts as a "serious" idea in elite discourse. And there is the threat of capital flight or layoffs, which gives concentrated owners a kind of structural veto over policies that might displease them.

A wealth tax addresses none of these channels directly. It reduces the stock of wealth at the top, which over time reduces the resources available for political deployment. But it does so slowly, even at the radical rates Saez and Zucman propose; their own counterfactual has Zuckerberg still worth $21 billion in 2018 had a 10 percent annual wealth tax been in place all along. Twenty-one billion dollars is more than enough to buy a great deal of political influence. It is more than the GDP of dozens of countries. The wealth tax, even in its aggressive form, leaves the underlying political pathology largely intact.

Compare this to the impact of, say, a constitutional amendment overturning Citizens United and authorizing real limits on independent political expenditure. Or a serious public financing system for federal elections, of the sort proposed by Lawrence Lessig and others. Or strict separations between media ownership and political activity. Or robust antitrust enforcement that breaks up the platform monopolies whose owners have become the new political barons. Each of these would, I think, do more to address the Madisonian concern than any plausible tax instrument.

This is not to say a wealth tax is undesirable. It is to say that defenders should be clear about what it accomplishes and what it does not. As an instrument of tax justice—ensuring that ability to pay is matched by actual tax liability—it is a reasonable proposal, though one I would still prefer to see implemented through mark-to-market income taxation and a restored estate tax. As an instrument for the protection of democracy, it is much weaker than its advocates suggest.

There is a deeper issue here that I want to surface. Saez and Zucman's approach exemplifies what might be called the fiscalization of politics. Political problems—concentration of power, corruption of public discourse, capture of regulatory agencies—are recast as tax problems. This is appealing because tax policy is technical and the authors are technicians of unusual skill. It is also appealing because it allows progressive politics to be conducted through the relatively low-stakes terrain of revenue and rate-setting rather than through the high-stakes terrain of constitutional reform and institutional design.

But politics cannot be reduced to taxation. The reason the United States has the politics it has is not primarily that the rich pay too little; it is that the rules of the political game have been written to amplify the voice of capital and to mute the voice of labor. The right response is to rewrite those rules. Tax policy can support that project; it cannot substitute for it.

There is also a strategic question. Suppose, for the sake of argument, that a wealth tax is enacted. The class of people targeted by it—numerically tiny, organizationally formidable—will respond. They will fund litigation aimed at having the tax struck down under the direct tax clause; they will fund think tank work documenting its administrative failures; they will fund electoral campaigns aimed at electing officials who will repeal it. Some of this will succeed. The history of comparable reforms in the United States—the post-2009 financial reforms, the Affordable Care Act—is one of immediate counter-mobilization, partial rollback, and decade-long trench warfare over implementation. The wealth tax would be no different, and the implementing capacity of the IRS would be precisely the part of the system most vulnerable to defunding.

The Madisonian project of preventing concentrated wealth from concentrating power requires institutions strong enough to withstand the counter-attack. A tax instrument administered by a single, politically vulnerable agency, defended by a single statute, is not such an institution. Constitutional rules, broad-based political mobilization, and structural reforms to the political system itself are sturdier.

Let me put my own view as plainly as I can. I think Saez and Zucman are correct that the postwar consensus on progressive taxation was a great achievement, that its erosion is a great loss, and that restoring high rates at the top is a worthy goal. I think the specific tax design they propose is innovative and probably workable in principle, though I have technical concerns about valuation, constitutional concerns about Article I, and behavioral concerns about avoidance. But none of these are my main concern. My main concern is that we are channeling enormous political energy into a fiscal instrument that, even in its most ambitious form, will not by itself rescue American democracy from the plutocratic drift Saez and Zucman correctly diagnose.

The wealth tax has, in the current political moment, become a kind of synecdoche for a broader anti-oligarchy program. That is understandable; it is a concrete proposal in a sea of vague aspiration. But synecdoches mislead. If we treat the wealth tax as the whole program, we will discover, after enacting it (if we enact it), that the plutocracy is still here, that the politics still favors capital, and that the public has been told the problem is solved when it has barely been touched.

Madison's "silent operation of laws" was not, in his thinking, a fiscal program. It was a broader project of institutional design. Saez and Zucman gesture at this but their proposal does not deliver on it. A serious anti-oligarchy agenda for 2020 must include the wealth tax, or something like it, as one component. It must also include constitutional reform of campaign finance, structural separation in media and finance, vigorous antitrust enforcement, labor law reform that restores worker bargaining power, and a public investment program large enough to demonstrate that the public sector can deliver outcomes the private oligarchy cannot. Without these, the wealth tax will at best moderate the symptoms of our political pathology while leaving its causes untouched.

I close where the authors began: with Madison. Madison's worry was about power. We should not let ourselves be talked into believing that the way to combat the power of concentrated wealth is to send it a slightly larger annual bill. The way to combat power is with power—the power of organized citizens, structured by institutions, defending rules that constrain what private wealth can buy. The wealth tax is a small piece of that. It is not the whole piece, and it is not even the largest piece.

The Quiet Costs of Confiscation

I want to register a more uncomfortable response to Saez and Zucman's essay than most of what has appeared in the symposium. I do not begin from libertarian premises. I share the authors' concern about inequality, accept that the tax system has become less progressive at the top, and agree that the very rich pay too little. What I find unconvincing is the leap from these reasonable propositions to the embrace of a "radical wealth tax" whose explicit purpose is "to disrupt privilege, impede rent extraction, and protect democracy" by reducing the number of multi-billionaires. The authors are admirably candid that their goal is not primarily revenue but de-concentration—what an earlier generation would have called confiscation. I want to argue that this candor reveals a set of problems they have not fully reckoned with.

First, the historical baseline. Saez and Zucman point to the high marginal rates of the 1940s through 1960s as evidence that confiscatory taxation can work. But the postwar United States was an extraordinary moment, not a normal one. The country emerged from the war with most of the industrial world destroyed, with capital controls firmly in place, with a labor movement at peak density, and with a political culture forged by depression and total war. High marginal rates coexisted with a tax base so riddled with shelters that effective rates were dramatically lower than statutory rates—a point the authors elide. Top earners in 1960 did not, in general, pay anything like 91 percent of their incremental income; they used oil depletion allowances, deferred compensation, real estate shelters, and a hundred other devices that have since been closed. The headline rates of the postwar period are not a model we can simply pull off the shelf; they were one piece of a much larger system that no longer exists and that we could not easily reconstruct even if we wanted to.

Second, the question of innovation. The authors dismiss the concern that confiscatory rates would discourage entrepreneurship by noting that growth was strong in the postwar period. But the modal billionaire today does not look like the modal high earner in 1960. He is the founder of a high-growth firm built on intangible assets, deployed at global scale, in winner-take-most markets. The wealth of Bezos or Zuckerberg or the Google founders did not exist in 1960 because the technological substrate did not exist. The relevant question is not whether postwar America grew with high rates; it is whether the next Amazon would be built in an America that imposed a 10 percent annual wealth tax above $1 billion.

I do not pretend to know the answer with certainty. But the structural logic is worrying. The founder of a fast-growing technology firm typically holds most of her wealth in the form of concentrated equity in her own company. The point of holding that equity is to align her incentives with the long-term success of the firm and to retain control over its strategic direction. A radical wealth tax would force her, year after year, to sell down her stake (or transfer it to the IRS, which would sell it for her). Within a decade of going public, she would lose voting control of her own firm. The implicit message of the policy is: you may build a great company, but you may not own it; the state will, through annual extraction, transfer ownership to a more dispersed shareholder base. This may be desirable on equity grounds; it is hard to see how it is neutral with respect to incentives.

The authors anticipate this objection but address only the consumption-incentive version of it ("Bezos has more than he can spend, so why does he care?"). They do not address the control-incentive version, which I think is the real one. The marginal motivation for entrepreneurs at the very top is not consumption; it is the ability to direct large enterprises, to shape industries, to leave a mark. Confiscatory wealth taxes do not just redistribute consumption capacity; they redistribute control. The political case for doing so is real—the authors' Madisonian argument is exactly about this—but the cost to entrepreneurial energy is also real, and it deserves more than dismissal.

Third, the question of philanthropy. The current American system, for all its flaws, channels enormous resources from the very rich into private philanthropy. Gates, Buffett, and other signatories to the Giving Pledge have committed to giving away the bulk of their fortunes. Gates's foundation funds malaria eradication, vaccine development, and educational research at scales no government program currently matches. A radical wealth tax would, by design, prevent this kind of accumulation in the first place. Saez and Zucman might say: good, those decisions should be made democratically rather than by individual donors. I have sympathy for that view. But I also notice that the public health infrastructure the United States is about to discover it desperately needs, in the midst of the unfolding coronavirus pandemic, has been kept partly afloat by private foundation funding precisely because public funding has been inadequate. A world without billionaires might also be a world without the Gates Foundation, and the second-order consequences of that exchange are not obvious.

Fourth, the political economy of the proposal itself. Saez and Zucman write as if the only obstacles to a radical wealth tax are technical (valuation, liquidity) and ideological (opposition from the rich). They do not engage seriously with the possibility that ordinary voters, when confronted with a policy whose explicit aim is to "reduce the number of multi-billionaires," will balk. Polling on wealth taxation tends to show majority support, but the polling generally describes moderate versions of the tax (Warren's 2/3 percent design, for instance) rather than the radical 10 percent version the authors prefer. The American political tradition includes a strong streak of suspicion of confiscation as such, distinct from support for taxing the rich. Conflating the two has been a long-running mistake of the left.

Fifth, the moment we are in. As I write, the coronavirus pandemic is reshaping every assumption about economic and fiscal policy. Equity markets have fallen sharply; many large fortunes have shrunk by a third in a matter of weeks. The federal government is contemplating fiscal interventions on a scale not seen since World War II. In this environment, the case for a wealth tax shifts in interesting ways. The acute need is for revenue and for redistribution to households facing immediate hardship; the longer-term question of billionaire de-concentration recedes somewhat. The authors wrote their essay in a different moment. A response written now must take account of the fact that we are about to discover what real fiscal emergencies look like, and that the policy tools developed for an era of slow-burn inequality may need to be retooled for an era of acute crisis. A mark-to-market regime, a real corporate minimum tax, and a substantial increase in top income tax rates can be deployed quickly and produce revenue when revenue is most urgently needed. A wealth tax, even if eventually enacted, will be tied up in valuation disputes and litigation for years.

None of this amounts to a defense of the status quo. The current U.S. tax system is shamefully tilted toward the very top, and the authors are right to call for a major reorientation. My disagreement is narrower. I think the wealth tax in its moderate form is defensible, the radical form considerably less so, and the framing of the entire project as a Madisonian defense of democracy oversells what the policy can actually deliver. The danger is that we will adopt a confiscatory policy whose distributional benefits are smaller than advertised, whose innovation costs are larger than admitted, whose constitutional status is uncertain, and whose political durability is fragile—and that having done so we will believe we have addressed the problem when in fact we have barely touched it.

The country needs a serious conversation about how to tax the very rich. Saez and Zucman have provided important material for that conversation. They have not, in my view, provided the conversation's conclusion. The honest position is that we need to raise more revenue from the top, that we should do so through instruments with proven constitutional and administrative footing, and that we should pair fiscal reform with the structural political reforms—campaign finance, antitrust, labor law—that actually address the concentration of power. The first difficulty is conceptual. The authors define wealth as market value minus debts, a definition that works well enough for listed stocks or bank accounts but founders on the vast majority of assets that are not continuously priced. They acknowledge this and propose a creative fix: let the government accept shares in kind and sell them to create a market. But this solution is not nearly as frictionless as they suggest. But that growth occurred in a world without global capital mobility, before the rise of international financial markets and tax havens. Today a billionaire can renounce her citizenship, move to a low-tax jurisdiction, and take much of her capital with her. To tax wealth because some of it might be spent on politics is to punish the innocent—the philanthropist, the quiet investor, the entrepreneur who reinvests profits in research—for the sins of a few.

The United States is not immune from this dynamic.

Second, what is the tax unit? Wealthy families do not organize themselves neatly as individual taxpayers. Assets may be held by spouses, children, trusts, partnerships, family offices, private foundations, and related entities. A serious wealth tax must include aggregation rules. Otherwise avoidance will be immediate: divide ownership among family members, shift assets into trusts, or create entities that obscure beneficial ownership. The law should look through formal title to ultimate control and economic benefit.

Third, how is wealth reported? The United States cannot rely on voluntary self-assessment by billionaires. It needs third-party reporting from financial institutions, brokers, private funds, trustees, insurance companies, and large private firms. The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act provides one model of aggressive information reporting, though not a perfect one. The basic principle is clear: the tax authority must receive information from parties other than the taxpayer. Without information, rates are theater.

Fourth, how are hard-to-value assets treated? Saez and Zucman are right that valuation difficulty is not unique to a wealth tax. Estate taxation, charitable deductions, divorce proceedings, financial accounting, and private investment all require valuation. Still, annual valuation raises the stakes. Publicly traded assets can be marked to market. Real estate can be assessed with appraisal methods, though assessment quality varies. The hard cases are closely held businesses, carried interests, private equity stakes, art, collectibles, intellectual property, and complex derivatives.

For these assets, the law should use a combination of methods. Recent arm’s-length transactions should create presumptive values. Comparable public companies and cash-flow models should be used where appropriate. Insurance values and loan collateral values should be reportable; taxpayers should not be able to tell a bank that an asset is worth $500 million and the IRS that it is worth $100 million. Large discounts for lack of marketability or minority control should be limited by statute, because these discounts have been a major avenue of estate tax avoidance. Penalties for substantial undervaluation should be severe.

The in-kind payment idea is intriguing but should be used carefully. Allowing taxpayers to pay with shares can solve liquidity problems and discipline valuations. If the government says a private company stake is worth $100 million and the taxpayer insists it is worth far less, payment in shares gives the taxpayer an option. But routine government auctions of private company stakes could be messy. They may raise confidentiality concerns, produce strategic purchases, or destabilize firms. A better approach might make in-kind payment available in limited cases, with the government holding shares temporarily through a blind public asset management vehicle or selling them under rules designed to protect competition and transparency. The mechanism should not become a backdoor industrial policy administered by the IRS.

Fifth, how is liquidity handled? Saez and Zucman are right that many liquidity objections are overstated. A household worth $500 million can usually borrow or sell assets. But some cases are genuine: founders of private firms, owners of illiquid real estate portfolios, or taxpayers whose wealth is tied to a volatile startup. The law could allow deferred payment with interest for a narrow class of illiquid assets, secured by a lien and capped to prevent abuse. The danger is that deferral becomes the new loophole. The rule should be strict: if you claim illiquidity, the government obtains a senior claim, information rights, and interest.

Sixth, how do we prevent exit? A wealth tax without an exit tax is an invitation to expatriation. The United States already taxes citizens on worldwide income, an unusual but powerful feature. A wealth tax should apply to citizens and long-term residents, with a mark-to-market exit tax for those who renounce citizenship or residency. It should also coordinate with estate and gift taxes to prevent pre-exit transfers. International cooperation would help, but the United States should not wait for a global tax agreement before acting. Its financial system gives it substantial leverage.

Seventh, what about constitutional risk? This cannot be waved away. A federal wealth tax would face litigation under the Constitution’s direct tax clauses. Reasonable legal scholars disagree about whether it would survive. Policymakers should therefore develop backup plans in advance. These include mark-to-market taxation of publicly traded assets for the very wealthy, taxation of unrealized gains at death, stronger estate and gift taxes, and surtaxes on capital income. A wealth tax bill should not be the only vehicle for progressive tax reform. If courts strike one instrument, others should remain.

Eighth, what is the rate structure? Here I would counsel ambition in goals but caution in initial implementation. A moderate wealth tax—say 2 percent above a high threshold and 3 or 4 percent at billionaire levels—would be a major institutional innovation. Before adopting 8 or 10 percent annual rates, the government should establish the reporting system, survive legal challenge, and observe taxpayer responses. Very high rates may be justified if the democratic goal is deconcentration, but they also intensify avoidance, valuation disputes, and political backlash. The first objective should be to make the tax real, enforceable, and durable.

This is not an argument against radicalism. Durability can be radical. Social Security began as a limited program and became a pillar of American life because it was administrable, visible, and politically embedded. A wealth tax that works at moderate rates could later be strengthened. A wealth tax that collapses under administrative or legal stress could discredit the project for a generation.

Ninth, how should revenue be used? Saez and Zucman emphasize that the radical wealth tax is not primarily about revenue. Still, visible uses matter. If citizens see wealth tax revenue financing universal child allowances, health care, public health, debt-free college, climate infrastructure, or housing, the tax will be harder to repeal. If the money disappears into general accounts while wealthy opponents publicize every valuation dispute, support may erode. Earmarking is often bad budgeting, but political linkage can be valuable: extreme private fortunes should help finance broad public security.

The coronavirus crisis now unfolding gives this discussion a sharper edge. The United States suddenly faces urgent needs: testing, hospitals, paid leave, unemployment support, aid to state and local governments, and protection for workers and small businesses. This is not the moment to pretend that public capacity is optional. For years we have tolerated a tax system that allowed enormous private accumulation while public systems remained brittle. A wealth tax would not provide immediate pandemic relief; designing and implementing it would take time. But the crisis should change our sense of what fiscal capacity is for. A society that can mobilize trillions to stabilize markets should be able to ask more from those who have benefited most from those markets.

Finally, enforcement is the heart of the matter. The IRS has been underfunded and politically battered. Audits of the wealthy are complex and time-consuming. If Congress enacts a wealth tax but does not build a modern enforcement agency, the result will be cynicism. The agency will need economists, valuation experts, forensic accountants, partnership specialists, international tax lawyers, data scientists, and litigators. It will need stable funding insulated from annual political sabotage. It will need authority to collect information before wealth disappears into layers of entities.

Saez and Zucman are right that administrative objections often disguise ideological opposition. Many people who say “it cannot be done” mean “it should not be done.” But supporters should not respond by minimizing the administrative challenge. They should embrace it. Taxing extreme wealth is a state-building project. It requires law, expertise, data, and political commitment.

My conclusion is therefore supportive but institutional. The United States should tax extreme wealth, but it should do so with a design that anticipates avoidance, litigation, valuation conflict, liquidity cases, and political attack. A wealth tax is not merely a rate schedule. It is an architecture of democratic accountability for concentrated property.

If we build that architecture carefully, the wealth tax can do what Saez and Zucman promise: restore progressivity, raise revenue, and reduce the danger that private fortunes will dominate public life. If we build it carelessly, we will hand its opponents the failure they predict. The choice is not between moral clarity and technocratic detail. We need both.

The Madison Doctrine: Why Extreme Wealth Is Incompatible with Democratic Equality

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman have rendered a valuable service by returning James Madison to the center of our tax policy debates. Their invocation of Madison's 1792 warnings about the political dangers of concentrated wealth should give every American pause. Here is the father of the Constitution himself, the man who more than any other architected our republican institutions, explicitly connecting extreme wealth inequality to the corruption of self-government. This is not radical left-wing propaganda; it is the foundational concern of the American political tradition.

The lead essay's central insight—that wealth is power, and extreme wealth is extreme power—demolishes the naive notion that economic inequality is merely a matter of private fortune. When Jeff Bezos can deploy his billions to influence elections, when the Koch brothers can fund a nationwide network of advocacy organizations, when a handful of families can shape the ideological landscape through their foundations and media investments, the pretense of political equality becomes a joke. We do not have one person, one vote in any meaningful sense when a single family can outspend millions of ordinary citizens combined.

What strikes me most forcibly about the Saez-Zucman analysis is their reframing of the question. Critics of progressive taxation typically ask: Is it fair to take more from the wealthy? But Madison asked a different question: Is it safe to allow such concentration of power? The fairness question admits of genuine disagreement; the safety question is empirical. Do democracies survive extreme wealth inequality? The historical record suggests they do not. The Roman Republic collapsed under the weight of aristocratic concentration. The French Revolution was triggered by feudal privilege. And Madison himself observed that "the same malignant aspect in republicanism may be traced in the inequality of fortunes."

I want to push the Saez-Zucman argument further along the democratic dimension. They correctly note that concentrated wealth distorts policy to favor the rich. But the problem runs deeper than policy outcomes. Extreme wealth inequality shapes what we can even imagine as politically possible. When the top 400 Americans own more than the bottom 60 percent of the population combined, when billions flow to think tanks and lobbying firms, when campaign finance requires hundreds of millions for competitive Senate races, the very horizon of political debate shifts rightward. Policies that would benefit 99 percent of Americans—single-payer healthcare, free public education, aggressive antitrust enforcement—become unmentionable not because they are unpopular but because the people who would benefit lack the resources to make their voices heard.

The authors' proposed solution—a progressive wealth tax starting at $50 million—is both more modest and more radical than it appears. It is modest in that it would affect fewer than 0.1 percent of families. It is radical in that it would begin to reverse four decades of bipartisan policy that has systematically transferred power upward. The authors are right that the estate tax is insufficient; waiting for billionaires to die is cold comfort when they are actively using their wealth to reshape society while alive.

I am less enthusiastic about the authors' "moderate" versus "radical" distinction. Yes, a 3 percent tax on billion-dollar fortunes would generate substantial revenue. But if the goal is democratic equality—if Madison was right that extreme wealth is inherently dangerous—then we should not flinch from more aggressive rates. The comparison to carbon taxation is instructive. We do not set carbon taxes at rates merely sufficient to "fill a gap" in the tax system; we set them at levels necessary to alter behavior and prevent catastrophic harm. Why should we treat the concentration of economic power any differently?

Some will object that such taxes would drive wealth flight or discourage entrepreneurship. The historical evidence, however, tells a different story. The highest peacetime top marginal income tax rates in American history—reaching 90 percent or more—coincided with the most broadly shared prosperity this country has ever known. The postwar economic boom, with its rising wages, expanding middle class, and democratized homeownership, occurred precisely when the tax system most aggressively constrained extreme wealth accumulation. The argument that high taxes on the rich destroy prosperity is refuted by the American experience itself.

There is, finally, a moral dimension that the authors touch on but could elaborate further. The superrich of today increasingly derive their fortunes not from creating value but from extracting it. Financial engineering, patent thickets, regulatory capture, monopoly pricing—these are the engines of contemporary billionaire wealth. When Mark Zuckerberg can exploit network effects and data monopolies to accumulate $60 billion while social media tears the social fabric apart, when pharma executives can price essential medicines beyond the reach of the dying, the case for confiscatory taxation strengthens not from envy but from justice. These fortunes are not merely the result of market rewards for value creation; they are often the product of political manipulation, regulatory failure, and social infrastructure that the wealthy did not create and do not pay for.

Madison understood this. He wanted to "withhold unnecessary opportunities from a few" and to use "the silent operation of laws" to prevent "an immoderate, and especially an unmerited, accumulation of riches." A progressive wealth tax is precisely such a law. It is time to stop treating extreme inequality as an unfortunate byproduct of success and to recognize it for what Madison knew it to be: a threat to the republic itself.

A Wealth Tax Sounds Fair in Theory. In Practice, It Would Be a Disaster.

The appeal of a wealth tax is obvious. We tax income, we tax sales, we tax property—so why not tax accumulated wealth? Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman make a compelling case that the ultra-rich pay too little, and their historical analysis of the postwar tax system is largely correct. But good intentions do not make for good policy, and on close examination, the wealth tax proposals advanced by Senator Warren and Senator Sanders would create more problems than they solve.

The fundamental challenge is valuation. The authors spend considerable effort arguing that most wealth is easy to value—Berkshire Hathaway shares trade publicly, after all. But they gloss over the hard cases, and it is the hard cases that determine whether a policy succeeds or fails. Consider the private company problem in its full complexity. Cargill may be unusual in that 90 percent of the company is held by 125 families and shares have not traded since 1992, but Cargill is not unique in having illiquid, privately-held assets. The same is true of countless family businesses, partnerships, and closely held corporations across America. The authors propose that the IRS value these firms using comparables from public markets. This sounds reasonable until you try to apply it.

Private companies often differ dramatically from their public counterparts. They may have different capital structures, different risk profiles, different management quality, different competitive positions. The price-to-earnings ratios of public competitors may bear little relation to what a willing buyer would actually pay for a controlling stake in a private firm. Moreover, the IRS has neither the expertise nor the resources to conduct sophisticated private valuations for thousands of wealthy taxpayers. The agency is already struggling to audit the existing tax code; asking it to value complex private businesses annually would be a recipe for arbitrary assessments, endless litigation, and unequal treatment.

And then there is the question of what assets count as wealth in the first place. Human capital—the present value of future earnings—is the largest component of most people's net worth. If we tax financial wealth but not human capital, we create perverse incentives. A young doctor with $500,000 in student loans and a high future earnings potential would owe nothing under a wealth tax, while an elderly retiree living off a $10 million nest egg of low-yielding bonds would owe hundreds of thousands annually. Is this justice?

The liquidity problem, which the authors dismiss too easily, is real for a different category of taxpayer: the founder with a large stake in a single company. Yes, the authors suggest paying taxes in stock. But this solution creates its own problems. Forcing founders to sell shares annually to pay taxes would dilute their control, potentially destabilizing companies that depend on strong founder vision. It would also force share sales into markets, potentially depressing stock prices and harming ordinary shareholders.

Consider the incentive effects more broadly. The argument that high taxes do not discourage entrepreneurship assumes that entrepreneurs are motivated by pure creation rather than ownership. But if I know that every year I am successful, the government takes a percentage of my accumulated stake, I have less reason to build companies in the first place. The wealth tax is not like a carbon tax, which taxes a negative externality; it taxes the accumulation of assets that benefit society. Why should we expect entrepreneurs to work as hard knowing that success will be systematically taxed away?

International competitiveness is another concern the authors minimize. America does not exist in a vacuum. If we impose a 3 or 6 percent annual wealth tax on billionaires, some will vote with their feet. Monaco, Singapore, and other tax havens will happily welcome them. Yes, citizens of other countries owe taxes to the United States on their worldwide income, but citizenship-based taxation is already under pressure, and a dramatic new wealth tax would accelerate the exodus of the wealthy and their lawyers who specialize in avoiding it.

More fundamentally, the authors' historical comparison to the postwar era is misleading. The high marginal tax rates of that period applied to income, not wealth. And they coincided with capital controls, a relatively closed economy, and limited international competition for mobile capital. None of these conditions hold today. We live in a world of floating exchange rates, mobile capital, and global markets where the wealthy can relocate their taxable presence with a phone call.

None of this means we should accept the status quo. The authors are right that the current system is indefensible—billionaires paying effective tax rates of 0.058 percent is an outrage. But the solution is not a wealth tax with all its practical difficulties and incentive distortions. Instead, we should reform the income tax to eliminate the loopholes that allow billionaires to avoid taxation, close the preferential rates on capital gains, and actually enforce the corporate tax code against the largest corporations. These are harder politically, but they are achievable without creating a new bureaucracy to value private companies, litigate disputes over asset values, and police the boundaries of citizenship.

Forget the Wealth Tax: The Corporate Minimum Is the Real Answer

Reading Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman's sophisticated analysis, I find myself agreeing with much of the diagnosis but questioning the prescription. Yes, the current tax system is riddled with inequities. Yes, billionaires often pay shockingly low effective tax rates. Yes, extreme inequality threatens democracy. But is a wealth tax really the right remedy?

I want to propose an alternative focus: not taxing wealth but taxing corporate excess. Specifically, I advocate for a robust corporate alternative minimum tax combined with reforms to close the gap between book income and taxable income. This approach would achieve much of what Saez and Zucman want—ensuring that the wealthy pay their fair share—without the practical difficulties of valuing illiquid assets or the political challenges of a new tax on wealth.

The corporate income tax, as the authors note, has declined dramatically from its postwar levels. But this decline reflects not just rate cuts but massive erosion of the tax base through deductions, credits, and the proliferation of shelters. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act compounded these problems by reducing the rate while failing to close any of the underlying loopholes. The result is that profitable corporations often pay far less than the statutory rate suggests.

The solution is not simply to raise the corporate rate to 50 percent as in the 1950s. The authors acknowledge that corporate ownership has changed—pension funds and foreign investors now hold substantial equity stakes, so higher corporate taxes no longer hit wealthy individuals as directly as they once did. But this observation points toward a different reform: a corporate alternative minimum tax that ensures every profitable corporation pays some minimum amount regardless of its deductions.

A corporate AMT of 15 percent on book income—the income companies report to shareholders before applying tax adjustments—would dramatically simplify enforcement. Book income is already disclosed in financial statements; no new valuation disputes would arise. And unlike a wealth tax, which requires annual appraisals of private company equity, a book-income-based AMT requires only that we compare two numbers corporations already calculate.

Such a tax would directly address the Buffett problem. Berkshire Hathaway's book income is substantial; it is the tax deductions and the lack of dividends that allow Buffett to report little taxable income. A minimum tax on book income would ensure that Berkshire pays something regardless of how cleverly its accountants structure transactions.

The political case for corporate reform is also stronger than for a wealth tax. Warren's and Sanders' wealth tax proposals are polling favorably, but they face intense opposition from the donor class. Corporate tax reform, by contrast, can be framed as addressing a simple fairness problem: why should a profitable corporation pay zero in taxes while a nurse pays 22 percent of her income? The answer is indefensible, and bipartisan majorities have supported corporate tax reform even when they oppose other progressive measures.

There is also the matter of international competitiveness. The United States already has one of the highest statutory corporate tax rates in the developed world, though the 2017 cuts brought us closer to the OECD average. A corporate AMT on book income is less distortionary than higher statutory rates because it operates within the existing framework and applies equally to all companies regardless of where they book profits.

I am not opposed to a wealth tax in principle. If we could implement one fairly and efficiently, it would be worth doing. But the practical challenges are immense, and the political obstacles are even greater. Corporate tax reform offers a more achievable path to the same goals: ensuring that the wealthy cannot escape taxation by hiding behind complex structures, and raising substantial revenue from those who have benefited most from our economic system.

The Roosevelt Analogy Doesn't Hold: What History Actually Tells Us About Taxing the Rich

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman invoke the New Deal tax system as proof that aggressive taxation of the wealthy is both historically precedented and economically successful. They point to the 90 percent top marginal rates of the 1950s and note that income inequality fell dramatically during the postwar decades. The implication is clear: we can restore broadly shared prosperity by restoring those high rates. But this historical argument is deeply misleading.

First, consider what those high rates actually meant in practice. The 90 percent bracket applied to income above extraordinary thresholds—the equivalent of $6.7 million today. Very few taxpayers ever reached it. Most wealthy individuals structured their affairs to avoid the highest brackets long before hitting them. The effective tax rates paid by the rich, while higher than today, were far below the statutory rates. The authors' own data shows that the top 1 percent's share of income fell by half between 1940 and 1970—but they present no evidence that the tax rates caused this decline rather than other factors.

What other factors? The most important was the collapse of asset values during the Depression and World War II. When you measure inequality across the entire twentieth century, the big story is not progressive taxation but the destruction of inherited wealth by economic catastrophe and military mobilization. The rich became less rich not because they paid higher taxes but because their assets were devastated. After the war, a combination of factors—strong unions, regulated finance, rising productivity, and the GI Bill—sustained middle-class growth. Tax policy may have played a role, but it was far from the central driver.

Second, the economy of the 1950s bears little resemblance to today's globalized economy. Capital was relatively immobile; international capital markets were underdeveloped; and exchange controls kept wealthy individuals from moving money abroad to escape taxation. Today, a billionaire with a Cayman Islands holding company can restructure assets overnight. The high tax rates of the postwar era were sustainable in a world of capital controls that would be politically impossible to reimpose today.

Third, the authors' claim that high taxes on the wealthy boosted economic growth is dubious. The 1950s were a period of reconstruction in Europe and Japan; American dominance was assured regardless of tax policy. Moreover, the economic literature on tax cuts and growth is contested. Some studies find positive effects, others find none, and almost none find effects large enough to dominate distributional considerations. The notion that we can engineer faster growth by cutting taxes on the rich has been tested repeatedly since 1980 and has failed to produce the promised results. But the converse—that raising taxes will boost growth—does not follow.

I am sympathetic to the goal of reducing inequality and ensuring that the wealthy pay their fair share. But we should not base our arguments on historical analogies that do not withstand scrutiny. The real lesson of the postwar era is not that we need higher marginal rates but that we need a comprehensive approach: stronger unions, better education access, antitrust enforcement, campaign finance reform, and yes, a tax system that does not let the super-rich escape their obligations.

The European Lesson: Wealth Taxes Failed There, and They Will Fail Here

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman address the European experience with wealth taxes only to dismiss it. They claim that concerns about wealth taxation failing in other countries are exaggerated and that the United States can succeed where others have struggled. This is wishful thinking. France, Germany, Sweden, and other European nations have tried wealth taxes and largely abandoned it. Their experience offers crucial lessons we should heed before repeating their mistakes.

France provides the most instructive case. The French wealth tax, or Impôt de Solidarité sur la Fortune, existed from 1988 to 2017. For nearly three decades, France attempted to tax the accumulated assets of its wealthiest citizens. The results were disastrous. Thousands of wealthy French residents left the country, taking their tax revenue with them. A 2016 report by the French Senate estimated that the wealth tax had driven away over 12,000 millionaires between 2000 and 2010, costing the government approximately 50 billion euros in lost revenue. When France finally abolished the tax, it was replaced by a higher property tax that hit the middle class more than the wealthy.

Germany repealed its wealth tax in 1997 after similar problems. Sweden's wealth tax contributed to an exodus of capital and entrepreneurs before its abolition in 2007. In each case, the pattern was the same: the wealth tax raised less revenue than projected, drove away mobile capital and the wealthy who held it, and imposed disproportionate compliance burdens on those who remained.

What went wrong? Several things. First, the wealthy proved far more mobile than policymakers anticipated. Unlike income, which must be earned where work is performed, wealth can be relocated. Bank accounts can be moved, companies can be restructured, residences can be changed. The European experience demonstrates that the superrich can and do flee higher taxes.

Second, compliance costs proved crushing. Administering a wealth tax requires annual valuations of diverse assets—private companies, art collections, real estate holdings, financial instruments. Each category presents unique valuation challenges, and each creates opportunities for disputes and litigation. The IRS is already underfunded and overstretched; asking it to conduct thousands of complex annual valuations is unrealistic.

Third, exemptions and carve-outs proliferated. European wealth taxes were riddled with exceptions for business assets, agricultural property, and other favored categories. The result was a tax that hit diversified investors but spared those whose wealth was tied up in farms, family businesses, and other politically connected interests. The authors propose a clean tax with few exemptions, but their proposal ignores the political economy of tax-making.

The United States has significant advantages over European nations in administering taxes: a large domestic market, the dollar as the world's reserve currency, and the citizen-based tax system that taxes Americans on worldwide income regardless of residence. But these advantages are offset by the extraordinary mobility of the ultra-wealthy and the sophistication of their advisors. When Warren Buffett can structure his affairs to pay 0.058 percent of his income in taxes, we should not assume that a wealth tax will be easily collected.

I agree with Saez and Zucman that the current system is inequitable. But the solution is not to import a failed European policy. Instead, we should focus on comprehensive income tax reform: eliminating the preferential rates on capital gains and dividends that allow the wealthy to shelter income, strengthening the estate tax with better enforcement and lower exemptions, and actually funding the IRS to audit high-net-worth taxpayers. These approaches work within our existing framework and do not require the administrative miracles that a wealth tax would demand.
