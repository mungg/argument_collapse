Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Nir Rosen has produced the most useful piece of reporting on Afghanistan I have read this year, in part because he refuses to let the abstractions of the Washington debate float free of Highway 601. The watermelon on the blanket, the dead Lieutenant Farid, the boy kicked in the chest, the prisoner with the wrong ringtone—these are the materials from which any honest argument about counterinsurgency in Afghanistan has to be assembled. Rosen's essay is, in effect, an ethnographic refutation of the McChrystal assessment, and on the merits of the refutation he is largely right. Where I want to push back is on the conclusions he draws, and on what he is willing to leave unsaid about the alternatives.

Start with what Rosen gets unambiguously correct. The architecture of the surge in Iraq depended on conditions that have no analog in Afghanistan: a Sunni population that had already lost a civil war and discovered that al Qaeda in Iraq was a worse landlord than the Americans; a Shia government that, however venal, possessed bureaucratic continuity from Baathist times; an urban population that could be walled off, registered, and monitored; oil revenues that could pay the Sons of Iraq and a bloated security force. None of these obtain in Helmand or Kandahar. Afghanistan is rural, illiterate, fiscally unsustainable, never centrally governed in the modern sense, and possessed of a recent memory of expelling outsiders that is closer to a founding myth than a historical episode. To borrow Rosen's framing: in Iraq the United States stepped into a void; in Afghanistan, the United States is the void, the disturbing presence around which local life has had to reorganize itself for eight years.

His point about the Afghan National Police is also indisputable, and the figure of 60 percent positive drug tests in some southern districts ought to be on the desk of every member of Congress who voted for the supplemental. McChrystal's plan to double the force to 400,000 is not a serious answer to the question of why the existing 170,000 are not effective; it is the bureaucratic instinct to scale up an input when the output disappoints. The COIN expert Rosen quotes—if you put gangbangers through eight weeks of training and return them to the same neighborhood, why would the outcome change?—has identified the central confusion in the troop-increase logic. You cannot manufacture political legitimacy by adding bodies to an illegitimate institution.

And yet. The harder question, the one Rosen mostly elides in his final pages, is: compared to what? He arrives at the edge of an alternative—address root political grievances, do "the work of decent politics"—and then leaves the policy proposition almost entirely undescribed. This is the weak point of his argument, and it matters, because the actually existing debate in the White House this autumn was not between McChrystal's COIN and Rosen's preferred world. It was between McChrystal and Vice President Biden's "counterterrorism-plus" option: a smaller footprint, drone strikes from over the horizon, training missions, and special operations raids against al Qaeda in Pakistan. Obama, on December 1, chose a hybrid that may turn out to be the worst of both: enough troops and enough rhetoric of nation-building to inherit responsibility for failure, paired with a July 2011 drawdown date that ensures no Afghan with a survival instinct will commit irrevocably to the American side. Rosen is justifiably contemptuous of McChrystal's plan, but the implicit contrast in his essay—COIN versus going home—is not the contrast the president faced.

This bears on Rosen's treatment of al Qaeda, which is the analytical soft spot in an otherwise tough-minded piece. He is right that the assumption "Afghan state collapse equals al Qaeda safe haven equals attack on the U.S. homeland" is a chain with weak links. Al Qaeda's senior leadership has been in Pakistan since 2001; Afghan Pashtuns do not, by most accounts, love their Arab guests; bases in eastern Afghanistan would be more vulnerable to American firepower than the FATA. All true. But Rosen then slides from this defensible position to something larger: that al Qaeda is essentially a reactive movement responding to "imperial excesses," that its grievances are legitimate even if its methods are not, and that a vigilant superpower has little to fear from a few hundred angry extremists. The first part is partly true and politically unspeakable; the second part underestimates what asymmetric actors can do. The 9/11 attacks, he writes, "had little impact on the American economy or way of life." This is wrong in the only way it matters: the response to those attacks has cost trillions of dollars, two wars, the moral standing built up since 1945, and—as he himself notes—the Guantanamos and Abu Ghraibs that now define America for much of the world. The lesson is not that al Qaeda is harmless; it is that the United States has demonstrated a dangerous incapacity to absorb attacks proportionately. That is an argument for restraint in response, not for complacency about threats.

Where Rosen is on firmest ground, and where I think the lead essay deserves to be read most carefully, is on the institutional critique. The American military, he writes, "lacks the curiosity to understand other cultures and the empathy to understand what motivates people." This is too sweeping—there are Westbys, and there are the FAO officers, and there are people like Kilcullen and the handful of anthropologists in the Human Terrain System who are trying. But Rosen's deeper point is structural: COIN doctrine demands cultural fluency, linguistic capability, patience measured in decades, and continuity of personnel; the U.S. Army is built around twelve-month rotations, force protection, careerist incentives, and a personnel system that treats Afghanistan deployments as boxes to be checked. Each unit, as he says, takes its knowledge home and leaves its successor to relearn. The Field Manual is a beautiful document; the institution that produced it cannot execute it. This is not a temperamental failure of particular soldiers. It is the predictable output of a Cold War-legacy military trying to perform a colonial-era mission with neither the colonial mindset nor the colonial timeframe.

Two more places where I would qualify Rosen. First, on the Afghan elections. He is right that the August vote was a fiasco and that Western validation of it was embarrassing. But his suggestion that elections themselves were the mistake—that they "empowered warlords and preserved a corrupt order"—understates how hard it is to articulate any alternative source of legitimacy for a post-Taliban government. The Loya Jirga model was tried in 2002 and 2003 and produced, ultimately, Karzai as well. The pathology is not elections; it is the gap between the formal state Westerners insist on and the patronage networks that actually distribute power in Afghan provinces. Until that gap is acknowledged, no electoral design will rescue legitimacy.

Second, on tribes. Rosen scoffs at the "obsession" with tribalism and the idea that Afghan tribes can be bought as the Anbar sheikhs were. He is right that Pashtun social structure is not Iraqi tribal structure—the qawm is more fluid, the maliks weaker, the Taliban itself partly a movement against traditional tribal authority. But the underlying instinct of the tribal-engagement crowd is not entirely wrong: in the absence of a functioning state, you have to deal with whatever local structures provide a measure of order. The mistake is to think these structures can be enrolled in a national project; they probably cannot. They can, at best, be paid not to host one's enemies. That is a counterterrorism strategy, not a counterinsurgency one—which brings us back to the Biden alternative.

So where does that leave us, on the first day of 2010? Rosen's reportage suggests, and I think the evidence supports, that the McChrystal plan as adopted will not succeed on its own terms. The Afghan security forces will not reach the size or quality required; the central government will not gain legitimacy; the population will not be protected in any sense that matters; and the July 2011 drawdown date will become, by 2012, the moment at which Afghans calculate that the Americans were never serious. The question is how much damage—human, fiscal, moral—is done in the interval. Rosen's essay is most valuable as a warning against the easy assumption that more is better, that resolve is a strategy, and that the celebrity of the commander is a proxy for the soundness of the plan. The harder, less satisfying conclusion is that the choice was never between victory and withdrawal; it was between several flavors of managed disappointment, and the administration has, as governments often do, chosen the most expensive one.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Nir Rosen's vivid dispatch from Highway 601 captures the maddening contradictions of the war in Afghanistan as it entered its ninth year. He shows us American soldiers, well-intentioned and frustrated, trying to mentor Afghan police who are corrupt, incompetent, or simply terrified; he shows us villagers caught between a predatory government and a ruthless insurgency; and he shows us the yawning gap between the clean theories of counterinsurgency and the bloody, chaotic reality on the ground. His conclusion—that the American project is doomed, that counterinsurgency is a fantasy, and that the very threat from al Qaeda has been exaggerated—will resonate with anyone wearied by a war that seems to have no end. But for all the power of his storytelling, Rosen’s argument ultimately rests on a series of deeply flawed assumptions. The strategy he derides may be enormously difficult, but it is neither impossible nor, given the alternatives, irresponsible. Indeed, the most important lesson to draw from his reporting is not that we should abandon counterinsurgency in Afghanistan, but that we must pursue it more seriously, with greater resources, patience, and honesty about what it demands.

Rosen’s central charge is that population-centric counterinsurgency—COIN, in military jargon—cannot work in Afghanistan because the conditions that supposedly enabled the “surge” in Iraq are absent. In Iraq, he argues, sectarian violence had already exhausted the country by the time additional U.S. troops arrived in 2007; the Sunni community had been defeated and was ready to turn against al Qaeda; and the urban geography allowed U.S. forces to concentrate their efforts. Afghanistan, by contrast, is overwhelmingly rural, its insurgency is undefeated and deeply rooted in the Pashtun south, and the government in Kabul is corrupt and illegitimate. The comparison is by now a familiar one among critics of the war, and it contains an important kernel of truth: Afghanistan is not Iraq. But the inference—that COIN is therefore doomed—is a non sequitur. Counterinsurgency is not a rigid template that demands a sectarian civil war as a prerequisite. Its core insight, as articulated in the Army/Marine Corps field manual that Rosen cites, is simply that the population is the center of gravity, and that protecting it and earning its support is the only route to lasting success. The specific tactics and timelines must be adapted to local conditions. That adaptation is precisely what General McChrystal’s campaign plan, and the Obama administration’s December 2009 strategy review, attempted to do.

Rosen is right to note the enormous obstacles. Afghanistan is one of the poorest, most illiterate, most rugged countries on earth. Its formal government has never exercised real authority over much of the territory, and the Taliban, after being routed in 2001, have spent years rebuilding their shadow state in the countryside. The Afghan National Army and especially the Afghan National Police are plagued by illiteracy, drug abuse, corruption, and staggering casualty rates. But these very facts cut against Rosen’s policy preference, which appears to be a rapid drawdown of U.S. forces and a return to a narrow counterterrorism mission focused on al Qaeda. If the Afghan government is as weak and predatory as he says, a precipitate American withdrawal would not lead to a stable equilibrium. It would lead to the swift collapse of the regime in Kabul, a return to full-scale civil war, and the likely restoration of the Taliban to power in large parts of the country. The Taliban of 2010 are not a monolithic movement, as Rosen himself notes, but their leadership remains closely allied with al Qaeda, and their governing philosophy remains a brutal theocracy that would once again deny Afghans basic human rights, especially women. The idea that a Taliban victory would have no serious consequences for U.S. national security is a gamble that Rosen seems willing to take, but it is not one that a responsible commander-in-chief can accept.

That brings us to the question of al Qaeda. Rosen argues that the terrorist network poses little threat to the United States, that it is already ensconced in Pakistan, and that the Taliban are not interested in global jihad. Each of these claims is contestable, and together they dangerously underestimate the enemy. It is true that al Qaeda’s core leadership is weakened and dispersed, and that the Taliban’s primary focus is on driving foreign forces out of Afghanistan. But the two groups remain intertwined through personal bonds, shared ideology, and tactical cooperation. If the Taliban retook control of southern and eastern Afghanistan, they would almost certainly invite al Qaeda back, just as they did in the 1990s. The argument that the U.S. could simply bomb any new al Qaeda camps from the air ignores the lesson of the pre-9/11 period, when limited strikes in remote regions failed to disrupt the plotting that culminated in the 2001 attacks. Moreover, the notion that al Qaeda is a spent force is a snapshot, not a prediction. The ideology of global jihad continues to inspire new recruits, including homegrown terrorists in the West, and the symbolic victory of forcing the world’s superpower out of Afghanistan would be an enormous propaganda boon for the movement. Rosen is entitled to his view that the United States overreacted to 9/11, but the American public and their elected leaders have, by a wide margin, concluded that allowing Afghanistan to once again become a sanctuary for transnational terrorists is an unacceptable risk. That judgment is not irrational.

Rosen’s narrative repeatedly emphasizes the apparent futility of small-unit operations—the “whack-a-mole” patrols that clear a village one day only to see the Taliban return at night, the Afghan police who are more burden than help, the roadside bombs that make a mockery of armored vehicles. These scenes are not fiction; they reflect a grim reality. Yet they also reflect the fact that the coalition has never deployed enough troops to hold the ground it clears. Counterinsurgency theory holds that a force ratio of roughly twenty to twenty-five counterinsurgents per thousand inhabitants is necessary to provide even minimal population security. Afghanistan’s population is around thirty million, suggesting a requirement for some 600,000 to 750,000 troops. The combination of ISAF and Afghan forces, even under McChrystal’s expansion plan, would reach only about 400,000, and many of those Afghan units are, as Rosen documents, unreliable. In other words, the chronic under-resourcing of the war—not COIN itself—explains much of the stasis Rosen decries. The question is whether the United States and its allies are willing to make the necessary investment of troops, money, and time. The answer from the Obama administration in December 2009 was a conditional yes: an additional 30,000 U.S. troops, a surge of civilian advisors, and a commitment to begin a conditions-based transition in July 2011. Rosen may think this is still insufficient, but that is an argument for doing more, not for doing nothing.

A related issue is the competence of the U.S. military. Rosen is scathing about the institution’s lack of cultural knowledge, its reliance on celebrity pundits, and its inability to learn. He quotes a government specialist who says no one in Central Command seemed to realize how strong the insurgency had become. There is some truth here: the U.S. military was slow to adapt after the initial invasion, and the Iraq war diverted attention and resources from Afghanistan for years. But Rosen’s portrait is too sweeping. The fact that the Army and Marines produced and now widely teach the Counterinsurgency Field Manual is evidence of a serious institutional effort to change. Officers like Captain Nate Westby, who patiently cajole and mentor their Afghan counterparts, who sit with elders over watermelons and promise to fix irrigation pipes, are not simply “paying lip service” to COIN. They are doing the hard, unglamorous work that the doctrine demands. Rosen may sneer at the “cult of celebrity” around generals like Petraeus and McChrystal, but the attention reflects a genuine intellectual ferment within the officer corps, a search for a better way after years of blundering. That some units are better at COIN than others is inevitable in any large organization; the solution is to spread best practices, not to declare the entire enterprise pointless.

The most painful pages of Rosen’s article concern the Afghan police and the civilians trapped by the fighting. He recounts the death of Lieutenant Farid, the near-mutiny of the police unit, the old men who fear Taliban reprisals if they cooperate with the Americans. These stories underscore the profound difficulty of building a legitimate state from the ground up in a society where trust has been shredded by decades of war. But Rosen seems to draw from them a counsel of despair. He suggests that the effort to extend the reach of the government only causes instability, that past attempts at demobilization and reintegration failed, and that the upcoming “exit strategy” will simply incentivize the government to do nothing. There is an alternative reading: the very weakness of the Afghan state is the reason it must be strengthened, and the only way to break the cycle of violence and counterviolence is to provide a credible, lasting security presence that gives people a reason to reject the Taliban. The “government in a box” McChrystal proposed is wildly ambitious, but the alternative—abandoning rural Afghanistan to the insurgents—is a strategy for defeat. And even Rosen acknowledges that the presence of some police checkpoints, however flawed, provides a modicum of order on Highway 601. The process is ugly and costly, but it is not futile.

Rosen’s title, “Something from Nothing,” is meant ironically: he argues that the United States is trying to conjure a functioning Afghan state out of thin air. But Afghanistan is not nothing. Before the Soviet invasion, before the civil war and the Taliban, Afghanistan did have a modest but functioning government, a national army, and a degree of central authority, particularly in the cities. The country has a strong sense of national identity, and surveys repeatedly show that most Afghans, even many Pashtuns in the south, do not want the Taliban back. The challenge is to convert that latent sentiment into durable political arrangements. That requires time, and it requires a commitment that no external power can fully control. Rosen’s piece, for all its vivid detail, ultimately offers no realistic alternative to the course it criticizes. A return to a narrow counterterrorism strategy—drones, special forces raids, and the occasional bombing—would, as the last eight years have shown, simply cede the countryside to the insurgents and allow the government in Kabul to grow ever more parasitic and disconnected. A full withdrawal would invite a catastrophic collapse. That leaves counterinsurgency, with all its frustrations, as the only strategy that at least strives to align American security interests with the aspirations of ordinary Afghans. It may yet fail, but Rosen’s confident verdict of impossibility is premature. The lesson of his own reporting is that the war has been under-fought and under-thought for years. The remedy is not to give up, but to finally wage it properly.

Nir Rosen’s dispatch from Helmand Province paints a visceral and deeply unsettling picture of the war in Afghanistan. By embedding us with Team Prowler and capturing the daily frustrations of American troops tasked with mentoring an underpaid, ill-equipped, and heavily infiltrated Afghan National Police force, Rosen provides a necessary antidote to the sanitized bureaucratic language often found in Washington policy papers. He correctly notes that counterinsurgency (COIN) is a grueling nightmare asking young soldiers to act as warriors and armed social workers. However, from these grim tactical realities, Rosen leaps to deeply flawed strategic conclusions. By arguing that the COIN effort is essentially pointless, that the Afghan state is an impossible illusion, and that al Qaeda poses no existential threat, he advocates a path of abandonment that would carry devastating consequences for both the region and the United States.

Rosen’s central military critique is that COIN is the wrong strategy for Afghanistan, primarily because the country lacks the urban density and sectarian exhaustion that facilitated the 2007 Surge in Iraq. He points to the corrupt Afghan security forces as evidence that we are trying to build “something from nothing.” But if COIN is not the answer, what is the alternative? From 2002 until late 2008, the United States largely pursued a minimalist, counterterrorism-focused strategy. We relied on warlords, limited our footprint, and focused on hunting high-value targets. It was precisely this light-footprint approach that allowed the Taliban to reconstitute in the rural south and east. A narrow counterterrorism mission requires actionable local intelligence, which in turn requires a secure population. Locals will not provide tips if they believe the Taliban will slaughter them at night. COIN, for all its frustrations, is simply the recognition that military force divorced from population security is a treadmill of endless violence.

Furthermore, Rosen oversimplifies the political objectives of the current strategy. He claims that General Stanley McChrystal and the Obama administration are foolishly attempting to create a highly centralized, functioning state in a country that has never had one. While Karzai’s government is deeply corrupt and the recent election was marred by massive fraud, the administration’s strategy does not hinge on turning Kabul into a centralized Western capital. Instead, the goal is to build sufficient localized governance and security to prevent the insurgency from overwhelming the country. The focus on rural districts does not make COIN impossible; it simply dictates a different application, one that empowers local shuras and tribal structures alongside provincial governments. Rosen dismisses these local engagement efforts as naive, yet historically, localized security initiatives have been the only effective bulwark against radical insurgencies in decentralized societies.

Perhaps the most alarming aspect of Rosen’s essay, however, is his casual dismissal of the strategic threat. He asserts that the Taliban are "not interested in global jihad" and that any future al Qaeda bases in Afghanistan could simply be bombed from the air. This assumes that the Taliban, having successfully outlasted a global superpower, would suddenly decide to strictly police their territory and sever ties with the very extremist networks that supported their fight. This ignores the 1990s, when the Taliban’s hospitality provided al Qaeda the exact sanctuary needed to plan the September 11 attacks. Relying on offshore bombing to disrupt entrenched terrorist networks in mountainous terrain is a fantasy that failed under the Clinton administration and would fail again today.

Rosen closes his essay by minimizing al Qaeda itself, describing it as a few hundred angry extremists who pose little danger to a "vigilant superpower," and suggesting that American "imperial excesses" are the true root of the problem. This argument conflates legitimate foreign policy debates with the immediate necessity of denying safe haven to a network actively seeking mass casualties. As the attempted bombing of a Detroit-bound airliner on Christmas Day 2009 demonstrates, the global jihadist network remains highly adaptive, relentless, and capable of striking the American homeland. While addressing the broader political grievances of the Muslim world is a worthy long-term goal, it does not absolve us of the responsibility to dismantle the physical sanctuaries where complex terror operations are born.

The Afghan war is undeniably messy, frustrating, and burdened by strategic neglect. The soldiers of Team Prowler have every right to their anger; they are bearing the brunt of a decade of under-resourced policies. But the grim tactical picture on the ground in Helmand should not blind us to the severe strategic consequences of failure. Retreating to a strategy of offshore strikes, or abandoning the country to a resurgent Taliban out of frustration with local corruption, would merely reset the clock to the late 1990s. We are not trying to create a utopian democracy out of nothing; we are fighting to prevent the return of a terrifying vacuum, one that history has already shown us we cannot afford to ignore.

Nir Rosen’s dispatch from Highway 601 is a bracing antidote to Washington abstractions. It is hard to read his account of American soldiers coaxing unpaid, ill-equipped, frightened Afghan policemen into a road-clearing mission and still believe that “population-centric counterinsurgency” is a doctrine that can be willed into existence by a commander’s assessment. Rosen is right that the central question is not whether COIN sounds humane compared with search-and-destroy, but whether the United States can actually perform it in Afghanistan, through Afghan institutions that are corrupt, predatory, and often absent. On that test, the case for escalation is dangerously weak.

The most important point in Rosen’s essay is that counterinsurgency is not a set of tactics; it is a political strategy. Protecting civilians, compensating victims, holding shuras, and training police are useful only if they connect the population to an authority it regards as legitimate. But in much of Afghanistan, the authority we are asking people to trust is the Karzai government, whose legitimacy was badly damaged even before the fraudulent 2009 election and is now weaker still. A villager who fears the Taliban at night and the police by day is not irrational if he hedges. He is making the only prudent choice available.

This is why the Iraq analogy is so misleading. The reduction in violence in Iraq after 2007 owed something to additional American troops and a change in tactics, but also to prior and independent political facts: the Sunni insurgency’s exhaustion, revulsion against al Qaeda in Iraq, the sectarian sorting of Baghdad, Muqtada al-Sadr’s cease-fire, and the willingness of Sunni fighters to accept American money and protection because they had lost the battle for the Iraqi state. Afghanistan offers no comparable moment. The Taliban have not been defeated; they are not universally hated in the areas where they operate; and many Afghans see them less as foreign terrorists than as a brutal but familiar alternative to venal officials and abusive police.

Still, Rosen’s argument risks going too far in the other direction. He is right to warn against conflating the Taliban with al Qaeda, but too quick to minimize the security problem that would follow a Taliban return to large-scale sanctuary. The Taliban’s relationship with al Qaeda may be based partly on convenience, honor, and shared enemies rather than identical ideology. Yet such ties matter. The Taliban leadership protected bin Laden before 2001 at immense cost to Afghanistan. Even if some Taliban commanders now resent Arab jihadists, it is an assumption—not a policy—that they would reliably prevent al Qaeda or similar groups from operating under their protection. The United States does have a legitimate interest in preventing Afghanistan and Pakistan’s borderlands from serving as an uncontested platform for transnational attacks.

But that interest does not require remaking Afghanistan into a centralized, Weberian state. Indeed, pursuing that maximal goal may undermine the narrower counterterrorism objective. Every additional village we promise to secure becomes another test of American credibility; every corrupt police unit we expand in the name of state-building becomes another recruiting tool for insurgents; every air strike that kills civilians converts a local grievance into a strategic defeat. A mission designed to deny sanctuary to al Qaeda has metastasized into a campaign to defeat the Taliban, reform Afghan governance, build a national army and police force of several hundred thousand, suppress narcotics, empower women, construct roads, and transform rural political culture. No serious strategy can treat all of these as prerequisites for success.

The better course is not immediate abandonment, which could produce panic among Afghans who have taken risks on the assumption that America will not simply disappear. Nor is it the open-ended COIN campaign implied by McChrystal’s assessment. The United States should narrow its aims and align its means accordingly. That means accepting that much of Afghanistan will be governed through local bargains, tribal and district-level arrangements, and imperfect power brokers rather than through ministries in Kabul. It means making reconciliation with Taliban elements a central line of effort rather than an afterthought. It means distinguishing between insurgents who want foreign forces out, local commanders who want autonomy or money, and irreconcilable jihadists tied to al Qaeda.

Such a strategy would be morally unsatisfying. It would leave many Afghans, especially women and minorities, vulnerable to ugly local compromises. But the alternative being sold in Washington is also morally compromised: asking young Americans and Afghans to die for institutions that often prey on the population they claim to serve. If we cannot protect villagers after promising to do so, our promises become a form of deception. Consider what the Soviets attempted in Afghanistan between 1979 and 1989. They came in with overwhelming conventional force and quickly secured the cities. They installed a friendly government — first Karmal, then Najibullah — whose legitimacy never extended much beyond the urban centers and the road network. They tried, with considerable resources, to build an Afghan national army, eventually fielding something like 300,000 troops on paper. They invested in development projects, particularly in education and women's literacy, well beyond anything the current intervention has attempted. They cycled through tactical doctrines, moving from large-unit sweeps to small-unit hunter-killer teams to "Afghanization" under Najibullah. They struggled with rural insurgents who could melt into Pakistan, with corruption in their client state, with police forces that often sold their weapons to the resistance.

They lost.

It is fashionable in American policy circles to insist that the comparison is unfair — that we are different because we have better technology, better intentions, better counterinsurgency doctrine, and (the polite formulation) better strategic patience. Some of this is true. The Soviets had nothing like the precision munitions or surveillance capabilities of the modern American military. Both struggled to extend that government's reach beyond Kabul and the major provincial capitals. Both faced a rural insurgency in a country where two-thirds of the population is rural. Both attempted to build national security forces by quantity rather than quality, with predictably mixed results. Both found themselves protecting infrastructure projects and convoys rather than civilians. Both saw their tactical battlefield success failing to translate into political consolidation. Both became dependent on increasingly absurd narratives of progress to justify continued commitment.

Rosen alludes to the Soviet experience in passing — noting that police corruption was a huge problem then as now — but does not push the comparison as hard as he might. Let me push it.

The Soviets at their peak deployed approximately 115,000 troops to Afghanistan. The mujahideen did not need to win battles; they needed to outlast the Soviet political will. His essay demonstrates why policy discussions in Washington so often seem disconnected from conditions on the ground, and why the gap between strategic ambition and operational reality has grown so dangerously wide.

The core of Rosen's argument is irresistible: the United States military is attempting to build a centralized state in Afghanistan when no such entity has ever existed there, using counterinsurgency methods developed for fundamentally different contexts, while relying on Afghan security forces that remain incapable of standing on their own. The mathematical impossibility of these combined objectives should be apparent to anyone willing to examine them honestly.

Rosen's account of the Afghan police reveals the hollowness at the heart of American strategy. When police commanders cannot read maps, when sixty percent of officers in some districts test positive for narcotics, when police are more feared by civilians than the Taliban themselves, the entire framework of "train and transfer" collapses. The American approach assumes that additional resources, better training, and more mentoring will produce effective security forces. Rosen's reporting suggests this assumption rests on fantasy rather than evidence.

The election fraud documented in the essay deserves particular emphasis. When turnout in the south falls below ten percent and systematic fraud renders the results meaningless, the assumption that legitimate governance can emerge from democratic processes proves false. Rosen is correct that "the failed elections were a message to Afghans that there was no hope of improvement or change." This message, more than any tactical development, will shape Afghanistan's future.

The author's analysis of COIN doctrine's institutional challenges within the American military deserves wide attention. The comparison with Iraq is particularly illuminating. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum. Applying Iraq lessons to Afghanistan commits the classic error of assuming that historical analogies trump current conditions.

If the mission cannot succeed, if the costs outweigh the benefits, and if continued presence only strengthens the forces America seeks to defeat, then the rational choice is to leave.

The remaining question is whether withdrawal can be managed responsibly. Rosen's analysis suggests that a sudden departure would create chaos, but a gradual drawdown tied to verifiable Afghan capacity might create space for political accommodation. The Taliban are not ideologically committed to global jihad; they want foreign forces removed and Islamic governance established. For American policymakers, Rosen's essay should prompt fundamental reassessment.
