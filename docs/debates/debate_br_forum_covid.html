Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have produced an indictment that deserves a serious hearing, and on several points they are simply right. The prolonged closure of public schools in blue states long after evidence accumulated that children were not major drivers of transmission was a policy failure with generational costs, borne most heavily by the poor and minority students whose interests progressives claim to champion. Government pressure on social media platforms to suppress dissenting scientific views, including the lab-leak hypothesis, was an unjustifiable assault on the epistemic norms a liberal democracy depends on. The contemptuous treatment of the Great Barrington signatories—whatever one thinks of "focused protection" as policy—revealed an ugly tendency among public health elites to convert empirical disagreement into moral excommunication. On these points, a postmortem is overdue, and Macedo and Lee perform a public service by pressing for it.

But the essay's central empirical claim—that the non-pharmaceutical interventions deployed in 2020 were essentially ineffective and that decades of pre-pandemic planning anticipated as much—is considerably weaker than the authors let on. And the methodological move they use to support it, comparing aggregate mortality across U.S. states by partisan lean, cannot bear the argumentative weight they place upon it.

Consider first what the pre-pandemic literature actually said. Macedo and Lee quote the WHO's 2019 report and the Johns Hopkins document accurately, but those documents were primarily written with influenza in mind. SARS-CoV-2 differs from pandemic influenza in epidemiologically crucial ways: a longer serial interval, substantial presymptomatic transmission, an enormous age gradient in severity, and—critically—the absence of the meaningful pre-existing population immunity that limits influenza pandemics. The 2019 WHO report's skepticism about quarantine and border closures was reasonable given the pathogen its authors had in mind. It does not follow that the same skepticism was warranted against a novel coronavirus with an estimated initial R0 around 3 and an infection fatality rate roughly an order of magnitude above seasonal flu. The authors of those documents would, I suspect, be the first to say that pandemic planning is pathogen-specific, and the appearance of a pathogen that did not behave like flu was a legitimate reason to revisit prior assumptions.

The example of East Asia complicates the lead essay's narrative further. Taiwan, South Korea, Japan, and—however coercively—mainland China demonstrated in early 2020 that aggressive case detection, isolation, and contact tracing could suppress community transmission of SARS-CoV-2 for substantial periods. Macedo and Lee mention these efforts in passing but do not grapple with what they imply: that the pre-pandemic consensus against containment was, at minimum, partially refuted by experience in the spring of 2020. The relevant question is not whether the techniques worked anywhere, but whether they could have worked in the institutional and cultural context of the United States, which is a different and more difficult question.

This brings me to the cross-state comparison that anchors the essay's empirical case. The Oxford stringency index aggregates policy on paper, not policy in practice. A state can mandate masks and have nobody wear them; a state can lift its stay-at-home order while its citizens continue, voluntarily, to stay home. By the summer of 2020, individual behavior in Democratic-leaning states (mobility data, restaurant attendance, mask use in private settings) diverged sharply from behavior in Republican-leaning states even when nominal policy was similar. If you want to estimate the causal effect of "restrictions" on mortality, you need to disentangle policy stringency from behavioral response, and the simple state-level comparison does not do that.

The comparison also struggles with timing and geography. The Northeast was hit catastrophically in March and April 2020, before anyone knew how to treat the disease, before dexamethasone, before improvements in ventilation protocols, and before the population had any acquired immunity. The Sun Belt's first major wave arrived in summer 2020, with substantially improved clinical management. Comparing cumulative per capita mortality across states therefore compares not just policies but the moment in the pandemic each state caught its largest waves, the density and age structure of the population exposed, the share of multigenerational households, the prevalence of long-term care facilities, and much else. The authors say they control for some of this, but the residual confounding is large, and several careful studies have found that excess mortality in the U.S. South in 2020-2021 was substantially higher than in the Northeast once age and timing are properly accounted for.

Macedo and Lee cite the 2022 Johns Hopkins meta-analysis and the Scottish inquiry report as showing that NPIs were ineffective. They omit that the Johns Hopkins paper has been sharply criticized on methodological grounds—its inclusion criteria excluded much of the strongest empirical work on NPIs—and that other systematic reviews, including Cochrane work on community-level interventions, have reached more equivocal conclusions. The honest summary of the literature is that NPI effectiveness varies enormously by intervention, timing, baseline transmission, and compliance, and that confident claims of "no effect" are no more warranted than confident claims of large effects. This is, of course, partly the authors' own point about uncertainty—but the rhetorical posture of the essay is that uncertainty cuts only in one direction, against the policies that were adopted. It cuts both ways.

A further difficulty: the lead essay treats counterfactuals asymmetrically. The costs of lockdowns—learning loss, unemployment, business closures, deaths of despair, the homicide surge—are attributed to policy. The deaths that did occur are treated as a baseline against which policy is judged. But what would have happened in the absence of stay-at-home orders in March 2020, when Italian hospitals were rationing ventilators and New York's ICUs were on the verge of collapse? The Imperial College projections were almost certainly too high as unmitigated baselines, but the relevant counterfactual for the spring of 2020 is not "no lockdown and everything else equal." It is "no lockdown and the resulting collapse of hospital systems, with cascading mortality from non-COVID conditions." Macedo and Lee invoke that cascading mortality (cancer screening, heart disease) as a cost of restrictions, which is fair; but they do not consider that some portion of it would have occurred, and likely on a larger scale, had hospital systems been overrun.

None of this rescues the actual conduct of the pandemic response from criticism. The right critique, in my view, is narrower and harder than the one Macedo and Lee offer. It is that the initial shock-and-awe response of March-April 2020, defensible under genuine uncertainty about hospital capacity and IFR, calcified into a year-long policy regime—particularly around schools—that should have been unwound far faster than it was as evidence accumulated. The failure was not lockdowns in the abstract but the inability of institutions to adjust, to acknowledge tradeoffs publicly, and to tolerate dissent. On these points the lead essay is powerful. Where it overreaches is in conscripting a genuinely uncertain empirical record into a confident verdict that the whole apparatus of NPIs was a mistake. The postmortem we need will be honest about how much we still do not know.

The COVID-19 pandemic was an unprecedented stress test of democratic governance, expert institutions, and public trust. Macedo and Lee offer a sweeping and deeply critical assessment, answering each of their three framing questions with a resounding “No.” Their essay performs a vital service by insisting on an honest reckoning, but in important respects it overreaches. The narrative they construct—of pre-pandemic wisdom recklessly ignored, of dissent stifled by groupthink, and of costly policies adopted with negligible benefit—is too tidy, too dismissive of the genuine dilemmas that decisionmakers faced in real time, and at times itself risks misrepresenting the available evidence. A more balanced postmortem, even five years on, must resist the temptation to replace one orthodoxy with another.

Macedo and Lee’s first charge is that institutions tasked with representing science to the public failed to communicate uncertainties and faithfully convey what was known. They lean heavily on pre‑2020 planning documents that expressed skepticism about non‑pharmaceutical interventions, arguing that these were cast aside when COVID‑19 struck. But this framing elides crucial context. Those same plans were designed for influenza pandemics, where viral characteristics—incubation period, asymptomatic transmission, and severity—differ markedly from SARS‑CoV‑2. The 2011 UK strategy, for example, assumed a virus that spread too fast to contain; COVID‑19, especially early on, did not fit that template, as the success of countries like South Korea and New Zealand in initially suppressing transmission demonstrated. Moreover, the WHO’s pre‑pandemic caution against travel restrictions and quarantines was itself rooted in a specific influenza‑focused evidence base and a reasonable fear of counterproductive panic. When faced with a novel pathogen and alarming hospital scenes in Lombardy, public health agencies updated their assessments—not dishonestly, but adaptively. To call that a betrayal of science is to confuse the messy process of evidence synthesis under urgency with a lack of truthfulness.

The lead essay also makes much of the fact that prominent experts like Fauci initially doubted the effectiveness of lockdowns. Yet it was precisely because the evidence was uncertain that modelers’ projections carried weight. The Imperial College report of March 2020, for all its later revisions, was a legitimate attempt to quantify worst‑case scenarios if no action were taken. Its core insight—that uncontrolled spread would rapidly overwhelm intensive care capacity—was tragically plausible. By mid‑2025, we know that the original fatality projections were too high, but that does not mean the models were cynical or unscientific; it means they performed their role as heuristic tools under uncertainty. Indeed, a fairer reading of the science communication record would acknowledge that officials did, in fact, often emphasize uncertainty. The phrase “flatten the curve” was a deliberate attempt to convey that the goal was not elimination but slowing spread to buy time. The CDC’s shifting mask guidance was clumsy, but the broader arc was one of evolving recommendations as understanding of airborne transmission improved. The essay’s outrage that YouTube removed a video of Ioannidis is misplaced: at that early stage, there was a legitimate public interest in preventing the amplification of claims that could lead people to disregard life‑saving precautions before any countervailing evidence had been rigorously evaluated. That does not justify heavy‑handed censorship, but it reflects the tense reality of an information environment where delay could mean thousands of deaths.

The second charge—that we failed to adequately weigh tradeoffs—is the essay’s emotional center. Macedo and Lee catalogue the immense collateral damage of school closures, economic disruption, and excess mortality from non‑COVID causes. These costs are undeniable, and the essay rightly insists that they must be accounted for. Yet their analysis repeatedly conflates the consequences of the pandemic itself with the consequences of the policy response. Emergency room visits fell not only because people were told to stay home, but because they feared infection in medical settings; cancer screening delays were a global phenomenon even in countries with less stringent lockdowns. Unemployment skyrocketed in part because consumer behavior shifted dramatically even where governments imposed few restrictions—airlines and restaurants hemorrhaged customers before any official shutdowns. Attributing all these harms to NPIs, without a careful counterfactual, overstates the case.

Central to their argument is the claim that tighter restrictions did not save lives. Their Figure 2, showing little difference in cumulative per capita mortality between Republican and Democratic states through April 2021, is meant to be damning. But that chart masks far more than it reveals. The pandemic unfolded in asynchronous waves, with early devastation in the Northeast that prompted more stringent rules precisely because the threat was more immediate. Many Democratic‑leaning states that kept restrictions longer were also denser, more urban, and had greater pre‑existing racial and economic vulnerabilities that amplified transmission risk. Without those restrictions, their mortality might well have been far higher; the absence of a crude difference across states does not demonstrate policy ineffectiveness. Tellingly, the authors acknowledge that after vaccines arrived, Republican states with lower uptake suffered significantly worse mortality, which confirms that policy choices (around vaccination) did have major effects. It is inconsistent to credit vaccines while dismissing NPIs on the basis of the same broad‑brush comparison.

The tradeoffs were also not ignored, as the essay implies. The very existence of the Great Barrington Declaration—and the furious public debate it ignited—shows that the costs of lockdowns were being actively contested, including within elite circles. The fact that Collins and others tried to discredit its authors reflects poorly on those individuals, but it does not prove that the broader public health establishment refused to discuss tradeoffs. Mainstream journals published studies on learning loss, mental health, and economic scarring throughout 2020 and 2021. Elected officials in many states explicitly weighed these considerations in deciding when to reopen. The claim that elites were insulated and therefore blind to the costs is an ad hominem speculation that doesn’t engage with the substance of the decisions they made.

Finally, on the question of postmortems, Macedo and Lee lament an absence of thorough retrospection. It is true that comprehensive, government‑led after‑action reports have been slower than many would like, but this is hardly evidence of denialism. By mid‑2025, multiple major inquiries—in the United Kingdom, Sweden, and elsewhere—had published extensive findings, and the U.S. had seen congressional hearings and a raft of academic analyses, including the very Lancet study the essay cites. The authors’ own piece is itself a lengthy, data‑driven postmortem, and it sits within a forum expressly designed to foster such debate. The real question is not whether such conversations are happening, but why they have not yet coalesced into a politically viable consensus about what to do differently next time. That failure owes less to liberal tribalism than to the fact that the pandemic’s lessons are genuinely complex and resist the sort of clean narrative that either the essay’s authors or their political opponents would prefer.

Macedo and Lee warn that avoiding hard truths will further erode public trust. They are right. But public trust also suffers when legitimate, difficult decisions made under extreme stress are retroactively dismissed as products of groupthink or class bias. The pandemic response had devastating failures, particularly in protecting the elderly in nursing homes and in mitigating educational harms. A forthright accounting must name those failures without suggesting that everything we did was wrong, that the experts were uniformly dishonest, or that the policies were adopted out of callous disregard for the vulnerable. That is not a sober postmortem; it is a separate kind of tunnel vision, one that threatens to undermine the very institutions we will need when the next novel pathogen emerges.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have done us a profound service by breaking the uneasy silence that has settled over the United States’ handling of the COVID-19 pandemic. Writing from the vantage point of mid-2025, more than five years after the world fundamentally altered its daily rhythms, it is astonishing how little institutional appetite remains for a rigorous, unsparing retrospective. The authors correctly diagnose a pervasive reluctance to audit our pandemic response, attributing it to a fear of validating populist narratives or reopening partisan wounds. But as they rightly warn, dodging this reckoning leaves us uniquely vulnerable to the next crisis, armed only with untested assumptions and a shattered foundation of public trust.

Macedo and Lee offer a sweeping indictment of the non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) deployed in 2020 and 2021, arguing that they defied pre-pandemic scientific consensus, failed to meaningfully alter mortality trajectories, and imposed catastrophic collateral costs that fell disproportionately on the working class and children. Their essay is a vital provocation. Yet, in their effort to correct the historical record and champion the skeptics who were marginalized during the crisis, they risk constructing an equally oversimplified counter-narrative. To truly learn from the tragedy of COVID-19, we must acknowledge the catastrophic failures of prolonged NPIs—most notably school closures—without dismissing the terrifying, data-poor reality of early 2020 that made initial lockdowns a rational, if blunt, triage mechanism.

The authors anchor their critique in pre-2020 pandemic preparedness plans from organizations like the WHO and Johns Hopkins, which cautioned against societal lockdowns and border closures. But it is crucial to remember that these plans were overwhelmingly modeled on influenza. SARS-CoV-2 was a vastly different pathogen. It possessed an insidious combination of traits: a longer incubation period, a high rate of asymptomatic and pre-symptomatic transmission, and a hospitalization rate that far exceeded seasonal flu.

When the virus hit Northern Italy and then New York City in early 2020, the theoretical debates of pandemic planners collided with the grim reality of overrun intensive care units, doctors forced into agonizing triage decisions, and refrigerated trucks serving as makeshift morgues. The initial pivot to lockdowns was not merely the result of elite panic or a desire for politicians to "be seen to be in control." It was an emergency brake pulled to prevent the outright collapse of regional healthcare systems. The failure to contain the virus through contact tracing—a failure Macedo and Lee cite as evidence against NPIs—was largely due to our total lack of testing infrastructure in those crucial early months. We were fighting a raging fire completely blind.

Macedo and Lee rely heavily on state-level comparisons, arguing that because highly restrictive Democratic states did not ultimately show vastly superior mortality outcomes compared to less restrictive Republican states prior to the vaccine rollout, NPIs were fundamentally ineffective. This ecological comparison, while popular in retrospective analyses, flattens a highly complex epidemiological landscape. First, state-level mandates often did not neatly correlate with human behavior. Cell phone mobility data from the spring and summer of 2020 clearly showed that Americans began altering their behavior—staying home, canceling travel, avoiding restaurants—days or even weeks before official stay-at-home orders were issued, and that they continued to do so regardless of what their governors decreed. The virus itself, and the fear it generated, drove economic and social contraction as much as state mandates did.

Furthermore, comparing the outcomes of New York and the Dakotas based on a stringency index ignores the critical variables of population density, the timing of viral seeding, and regional transit hubs. When New York locked down in March 2020, the virus was already deeply entrenched. When less dense states stayed relatively open, they were initially protected by their geography, not their policies. When the winter wave of 2020-2021 eventually swept through the entire country, pandemic fatigue had set in everywhere, rendering NPIs less effective across the board. NPIs were never a cure; they were a delay tactic to buy time for healthcare capacity, therapeutics, and ultimately, vaccines. In that narrow sense, the initial circuit-breakers likely saved tens of thousands of lives by keeping hospitals from collapsing in the first six months.

However, where Macedo and Lee’s argument strikes with undeniable and devastating accuracy is in their analysis of what happened after the initial emergency phase passed. Once the immediate threat of hospital collapse was mitigated, and once the steep age-stratification of COVID-19 risk became clear, the continuation of broad NPIs became increasingly divorced from a rational cost-benefit analysis.

Nowhere was this failure more tragic than in the prolonged closure of public schools. As the authors point out, the evidence by late spring 2020 strongly suggested that schools were not primary engines of community transmission, and that children faced astronomically low risks of severe illness from the virus. European nations, looking at the same data, prioritized reopening their schools by the fall of 2020. Yet, in the United States, school closures became a grueling, year-long—and in some places, longer—ordeal, strictly correlated with the partisan leaning of the state and the political power of teachers' unions.

We are now living with the generational fallout of this policy disaster. The learning loss documented by 2025 is staggering, with the burden falling entirely on the marginalized populations that progressives historically championed. Minority children, students in poverty, and kids with special needs were abandoned to the whims of "remote learning" while their wealthier peers transitioned to private schools or well-funded learning pods. The surge in adolescent depression, anxiety, and behavioral issues cannot be untangled from the social isolation imposed upon them. Macedo and Lee are entirely correct that this represents a profound moral failure. Policymakers exhibited a catastrophic tunnel vision, treating the reduction of COVID-19 cases as the single metric of human well-being, while ignoring the holistic health and development of a generation.

This tunnel vision was undeniably insulated by class. The authors’ identification of a "laptop class" bias is essential to understanding the pandemic response. For white-collar knowledge workers, lockdowns meant working in sweatpants, ordering groceries via delivery apps, and watching asset portfolios swell thanks to federal stimulus and low interest rates. The costs of the pandemic response were externalized onto working-class populations who had to physically pack the warehouses, deliver the food, and staff the essential retail sectors. The failure of our opinion-shaping elites to internalize these asymmetrical costs severely distorted the public debate, making it far too easy to moralize staying at home as an unalloyed virtue, rather than a privileged luxury.

This brings us to the essay’s deeply resonant points regarding institutional trust and the policing of dissent. The authors recount the vilification of those who questioned the prevailing orthodoxy, focusing particularly on the authors of the Great Barrington Declaration (GBD). It is true that the academic and media establishment’s reaction to the GBD was shockingly vitriolic. The coordinated efforts by public health officials to execute a "devastating published take down" of dissenting scientists, alongside the aggressive censorship regimes enacted by social media companies, severely damaged the credibility of the scientific establishment. When platforms algorithmically throttled debates about mask efficacy or the possibility of a lab leak—only to quietly reverse course years later—they validated the deepest suspicions of the American public.

However, recognizing the unfairness of the backlash against the GBD does not mean we must accept the GBD’s proposed strategy of "focused protection" as a viable alternative. The declaration offered an appealingly elegant theory: let the young and healthy live normally to build immunity, while building a protective wall around the elderly and vulnerable. But in the messy reality of American life, this was a logistical fantasy. Millions of vulnerable individuals live in multi-generational households. Nursing homes are not islands; they are staffed by young and middle-aged workers who live in the broader community. You cannot let a highly contagious respiratory virus rip through the general population and expect it to magically stop at the doorstep of the vulnerable. In communities where the virus spread unchecked prior to the vaccine, the vulnerable died in horrific numbers, regardless of attempts to shield them. The tragedy of the GBD debate was that it forced a false binary between endless, universal lockdowns and a Darwinian "let it rip" strategy, leaving no room for a nuanced, highly targeted middle ground.

Macedo and Lee rightly highlight the "noble lies" deployed by public health officials as a primary driver of institutional distrust. From the early assertions that masks were unnecessary (driven by a desire to preserve PPE for healthcare workers), to the shifting goalposts on herd immunity thresholds, to the categorical dismissal of the lab leak theory as a racist conspiracy, public health communicators repeatedly chose paternalism over transparency. They treated the public as fragile children who needed to be managed, rather than democratic citizens who deserved the unvarnished truth, complete with all its terrifying uncertainties. In doing so, they squandered the most vital currency in a public health crisis: credibility.

The authors frame their essay as a plea for a comprehensive post-mortem, and on this, they deserve our full support. A functional democracy cannot suffer a trauma that killed over a million of its citizens, spent five trillion dollars, fundamentally altered its social fabric, and then simply shrug and move on. We desperately need a bipartisan, independent COVID-19 Commission—akin to the 9/11 Commission—granted broad investigatory powers and staffed by experts spanning public health, economics, sociology, and education.

This commission must be willing to state uncomfortable truths. It must be willing to say that the initial lockdowns were a necessary, desperate measure to prevent catastrophe, but that their continuation past the summer of 2020 yielded diminishing returns at a devastating societal cost. It must state unequivocally that the prolonged closure of schools in blue states was one of the worst public policy errors of the 21st century. It must acknowledge that state interventions contributed to inflation and exacerbated inequality, while also noting that doing nothing would have sparked its own economic and social collapse. And it must hold the scientific and media establishments accountable for substituting moral panic for empirical rigor, thereby driving a wedge of distrust that will make managing the next pandemic exponentially harder.

Macedo and Lee’s essay is a necessary corrective to the establishment’s amnesia. While their epidemiological conclusions regarding the total inefficacy of NPIs may lean too heavily on retrospective state-level data that strips away the chaotic, terrifying context of early 2020, their sociological and ethical critiques are dead center. We allowed fear to calcify into tribalism. We allowed the mitigation of a single pathogen to blind us to the holistic needs of human flourishing. And we allowed the most vulnerable among us—our children and our working poor—to bear the heaviest burdens of our sweeping interventions.

We cannot change the decisions made in the fog of war in 2020. But in the clear light of 2025, to refuse to examine those decisions, to refuse to weigh the immense costs against the purported benefits, is not just intellectually dishonest; it is a dereliction of our duty to the future. If we do not ruthlessly audit our failures now, we will be doomed to repeat them when the next emergency inevitably arrives.

Macedo and Lee are right about the need for a serious reckoning. The United States has never really held one. We have had memoirs, partisan score-settling, agency reports, congressional hearings that often seemed designed to confirm priors, and a great deal of private regret. But we have not had a broad public accounting that could command confidence across ideological lines. That failure matters. A democracy that cannot examine an emergency after the fact will enter the next emergency with less trust, less patience, and fewer shared facts.

But a useful reckoning must be careful about the opposite danger too: replacing one overconfident story with another. The dominant story in many elite institutions in 2020 and 2021 was that “the science” clearly supported sweeping restrictions, that dissent was mostly reckless or malign, and that the central policy problem was public noncompliance. Macedo and Lee rightly challenge that story. Yet their own account sometimes moves too quickly toward a different simplification: that because many non-pharmaceutical interventions were weakly evidenced, highly costly, and often poorly correlated with state-level mortality outcomes, the broad turn toward restrictions was essentially a mistake from the start. That conclusion is too strong.

The hardest fact about March 2020 is that officials were making decisions under conditions of radical uncertainty, with hospitals in northern Italy collapsing, New York City approaching catastrophe, little testing capacity, no vaccine, no effective treatment, limited knowledge of transmission, and plausible estimates of infection fatality that were frighteningly high for older adults. Pre-pandemic plans were relevant, but they were not scripture. Many were written with influenza in mind. SARS-CoV-2 had important differences: substantial presymptomatic and asymptomatic transmission, overdispersion, a long enough incubation period to make some forms of isolation potentially useful, and a sharply age-stratified risk profile. The fact that earlier plans warned against border closures, quarantines, or prolonged school closures does not settle what should have been done when a novel coronavirus was already spreading silently through major cities.

Nor should we forget what “flatten the curve” meant at the time. The original case for temporary restrictions was not that governments could hermetically seal society until the virus disappeared. It was that an uncontrolled surge could overwhelm hospitals, causing preventable deaths from COVID and everything else. In that setting, a short period of drastic contact reduction was defensible, even if the evidence base was imperfect. Public officials often must act before randomized trials are available. Waiting for certainty can also be a decision—sometimes a disastrous one.

That said, the strongest part of Macedo and Lee’s essay concerns what happened after the first emergency phase. Temporary measures hardened into moral tests. Officials and commentators too often blurred the distinction between “reasonable as a stopgap” and “proven as a long-term strategy.” School closures, in particular, became indefensible in many places long before they ended. By summer 2020 it was already clear that children faced far lower risk of severe disease than adults, that schools were not the dominant engines of spread many had feared, and that remote learning was failing many students—especially poor children, children with disabilities, English-language learners, and children whose parents could not supervise online classes while working. Keeping public schools closed while bars, restaurants, casinos, and private schools reopened was not a triumph of science. It was a political and institutional failure.

The same is true of the failure to acknowledge tradeoffs. Public health officials are not wrong to focus on health, but health is not only avoidance of one infectious disease. Missed cancer screenings, untreated addiction, mental illness, isolation among the elderly, lost learning, domestic violence, unemployment, and the developmental needs of children are also health concerns. The slogan “lives versus the economy” was always misleading. Livelihoods, education, social connection, and public order are among the conditions that make life livable and healthy. Macedo and Lee are right that the laptop class could too easily experience restrictions as inconvenience rather than devastation. A policy that protects professionals by shifting risk and cost onto service workers, children, migrants, the poor, and small business owners should not be described as egalitarian merely because it is justified in universal language.

They are also right that candor failed. The early masking reversal—first discouraging public mask use partly to preserve supply, then later presenting mask-wearing as an obvious moral obligation—damaged credibility. Officials could have said: “Medical masks are scarce and health workers need them first; cloth face coverings may help somewhat, especially indoors, but the evidence is limited.” Instead, too many messages were massaged for behavioral effect. Similar habits appeared in discussions of outdoor transmission, surface cleaning, school reopening, vaccine durability, herd immunity, and the lab-leak hypothesis. Even when officials had defensible reasons for concern, they too often spoke as if uncertainty were the enemy of compliance. In the long run, that posture undermined compliance itself.

The suppression or stigmatization of dissent was another mistake. Some COVID claims really were false and dangerous: miracle cures, denial that the virus existed, wild anti-vaccine conspiracies. Platforms were not wrong to worry about misinformation. But when disagreement about school closures, cloth masks, natural immunity, vaccine mandates, or possible origins of the virus was treated as beyond the pale, institutions confused scientific authority with message discipline. Science advances through argument, not through the designation of a single authorized view. In an emergency, public debate can be messy and even harmful at the margins; but the alternative—private debate among experts and simplified commands for everyone else—is incompatible with democratic trust.

Still, the evidence about restrictions is more complicated than the lead essay allows. State-level comparisons are suggestive but limited. Policies were endogenous: governors imposed restrictions when danger seemed high, and relaxed them when danger seemed lower. Voluntary behavior changed before and apart from mandates. Early seeding, population density, household structure, weather, nursing-home vulnerability, occupational patterns, prior immunity, hospital quality, and reporting differences all shaped mortality. A state with strict rules and high deaths may have had high deaths because it was hit early and hard, not because rules failed. A state with loose rules and similar deaths may have benefited from later seeding, lower density, more outdoor life, or voluntary caution. Conversely, some restrictions may have reduced infections without producing obvious mortality differences because the benefits were offset by other factors. “No clear correlation” is not the same as “no effect.”

It is more plausible to distinguish among interventions. Temporary cancellation of large indoor gatherings during explosive spread likely reduced transmission. Improving ventilation, moving activities outdoors, paid sick leave, testing in high-risk settings, and high-quality masks for vulnerable people and health workers were sensible. Closing beaches and playgrounds, spraying surfaces, imposing arbitrary curfews, banning outdoor recreation, and treating all institutions as equally dangerous were not. Border restrictions after widespread domestic transmission were often symbolic. Contact tracing was useful when prevalence was low, but nearly futile when cases were everywhere. Broad stay-at-home orders may have bought time in spring 2020, but they were blunt tools with rapidly diminishing returns. School closures for older students during severe waves may have been arguable; prolonged closure of elementary schools was a grave error.

The Great Barrington Declaration deserves a similarly mixed assessment. Its authors were not cranks, and they were right to emphasize age stratification, collateral damage, and the impossibility of suppressing the virus indefinitely. The campaign to brand them “fringe” was shameful. But “focused protection” was easier to invoke than to implement. The United States did not know how to protect nursing homes well even while imposing broad restrictions; it is not obvious that it could have shielded millions of vulnerable elderly and immunocompromised people while allowing widespread transmission among everyone else. Many older Americans live with younger relatives. Many vulnerable people work in public-facing jobs. Infection among the young does not remain neatly confined to the young. The declaration identified real failures, but it did not solve the operational problem at the heart of the pandemic.

Any accounting should also include the successes. Operation Warp Speed and the rapid development, testing, manufacture, and distribution of vaccines were extraordinary achievements. They saved many lives. Clinicians learned quickly: dexamethasone, improved respiratory management, anticoagulation practices, and better hospital protocols reduced mortality. Wastewater surveillance, genomic sequencing, and rapid tests improved, though too slowly. Many Americans made sacrifices for neighbors they would never meet. The story is not only one of institutional arrogance and failure.

The central lesson is not that emergency public health powers are always illegitimate, nor that experts should retreat from public life. It is that expertise must be disciplined by humility, transparency, and democratic accountability. Officials should state what they know, what they suspect, what they do not know, and what costs they are imposing. Emergency measures should have expiration dates and evidentiary thresholds for renewal. Schools should be treated as essential infrastructure, not as the first institutions to close and the last to reopen. Data systems should be built before crises, so we are not forced to govern by anecdote and dashboard panic. Public health should focus less on commanding society and more on enabling safer choices: ventilation, sick leave, targeted protection, rapid testing, clear risk communication, and surge capacity in hospitals.

Macedo and Lee’s call for a postmortem is therefore necessary. But the best postmortem will resist vindication. It will not say that the public health establishment was simply right and its critics reckless. It will not say that restrictions were useless and critics were prophets. It will say that fear, uncertainty, partisanship, institutional self-protection, and moral fervor distorted judgment across the spectrum. The next crisis will bring its own fog. We will not meet it well if we remember COVID only as a morality play. We need something harder: an honest democratic memory.

The lead essay by Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee raises important questions about the COVID-19 response, but in doing so, it commits a fundamental error that should concern anyone who values honest policy analysis: it judges historical actors by standards they could not have met while simultaneously claiming the high ground of epistemic humility. The authors want us to believe that the evidence clearly pointed away from society-wide non-pharmaceutical interventions, but the pre-pandemic literature they cite—which rated evidence as "very low" for NPIs—does not establish what they claim. They were confronting a novel pathogen about which almost nothing was known with certainty: its transmission dynamics, its case fatality rate, whether children were significant vectors, and how hospitals would fare under surge conditions. The Imperial College models that so troubled policymakers projected potential death tolls in the hundreds of thousands to millions. Was it reasonable for officials to treat these projections as certain? Of course not. Was it reasonable to treat them as alarming signals requiring response? Almost certainly yes. The authors criticize officials for "tunnel vision," but what alternative did they propose in real time? The Great Barrington Declaration's "focused protection" approach assumed that vulnerable populations could be isolated while the young and healthy developed immunity. This required a degree of coordination and household isolation that had never been achieved in any society facing an infectious disease threat.

The authors present data showing that states with more restrictive policies did not consistently outperform less restrictive states in mortality outcomes. This is a legitimate finding that warrants careful analysis. But the analysis the authors perform is not careful. States did not exist in isolation from one another; movement between states continued, and the country as a whole was a connected system. The relevant counterfactual is not "strict state versus lax state" but rather "country with interventions versus country without interventions." The authors acknowledge this difficulty, note that "disentangling the effects of the response from those of the pandemic itself is difficult," and then proceed to make confident claims about that very disentanglement.

The authors are particularly critical of the suppression of dissent, citing the treatment of the Great Barrington Declaration authors and the removal of skeptical content from social media platforms. Here they identify a genuine problem. The email from NIH Director Francis Collins urging a "quick and devastating published take down" of the declaration was inappropriate, and the broader pattern of treating policy disagreements as misinformation rather than legitimate scientific discourse deserves scrutiny. But the authors overreach when they suggest that this suppression was decisive in shaping policy. The scientific consensus that emerged around NPIs was not manufactured by censorship; it reflected the emerging evidence as scientists and public health officials understood it at the time.

What the authors do not adequately address is the epistemic position of those implementing policies. They write as though officials had access to the retrospective analyses they now wish had been conducted, as though the harms of school closures were knowable in March 2020, as though the costs of NPIs could have been weighed against benefits with the precision of a ledger sheet. This is not how public health decision-making works, particularly in the fog of a novel pandemic. Officials had to make consequential decisions under radical uncertainty, with the information available to them. The proper question is not whether they made the optimal choice in retrospect but whether they made reasonable choices given what they knew and could reasonably anticipate.

There is a deeper problem with the essay's framing. By treating the pandemic response as a story of institutional failure—characterized by dishonesty, groupthink, and class bias—the authors risk throwing out the baby with the bathwater. Public health institutions, for all their imperfections, achieved something remarkable: the development of multiple effective vaccines in under a year, the massive expansion of testing infrastructure, and the treatment of millions of severely ill patients. The authors barely mention vaccines, and when they do, they note that only 1 percent of federal spending went to vaccine development. But this misses the point. The mRNA platform that enabled rapid vaccine development had been in development for decades; the federal investment that made Operation Warp Speed possible was a bipartisan success story that deserves more attention than the authors give it.

The authors conclude by calling for accountability and honest evaluation. I share this call. But honest evaluation requires acknowledging complexity rather than retroactively assigning blame based on outcomes that were far from certain at the time decisions were made. It requires distinguishing between what officials should have known and what they actually could have known. It requires engaging with the strongest version of the positions we disagree with rather than the weakest. The COVID-19 pandemic was a tragedy on a scale that most people alive today had never experienced. It demanded responses that no playbook could fully anticipate. The appropriate response to that tragedy is not to find villains but to learn lessons—and learning requires the kind of intellectual honesty that treats even our own critiques as provisional, subject to revision as new evidence and perspectives emerge.

The Censorship Was the Story

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have written a careful, restrained essay. It is so careful, in fact, that it understates what is arguably the most disturbing legacy of the pandemic response: the systematic suppression of dissenting expert opinion by an alliance of federal officials, social media platforms, and the prestige press. The authors mention this in passing—the YouTube takedown of Ioannidis, Meta's 27 million removed posts, Francis Collins's "quick and devastating takedown" email, the Stanford Faculty Senate's censure of Scott Atlas—but they treat these as illustrations of a broader epistemic failure. They are more than that. They are the central scandal, because they explain why the broader epistemic failure was so durable and so total.

Consider what the lead essay establishes. Before March 2020, the dominant view among pandemic planners—WHO, Johns Hopkins, the UK Department of Health, D.A. Henderson and his coauthors, the Institute of Medicine—was that population-wide NPIs were of uncertain effectiveness, that border closures and contact tracing were not recommended, that quarantine of the healthy was perhaps the least likely measure to work, and that the social costs of prolonged restrictions would be devastating. This was not a fringe position. It was the considered judgment of the world's leading public health institutions, rendered in writing, repeatedly, over more than a decade. Anthony Fauci himself voiced it in late January 2020.

Now ask: between January and March 2020, what new evidence emerged that justified abandoning this consensus? Essentially none. The Imperial College model was a model, not evidence. China's claimed success in Wuhan was reported by an authoritarian regime with strong incentives to project competence and was, in any case, not externally validated. What changed was not the evidence but the political and emotional climate. The previously mainstream view became, almost overnight, "misinformation."

This is where the censorship apparatus matters. If the Great Barrington Declaration had been published in 2018 as a planning document, its core claims—age-stratified risk, focused protection of the vulnerable, skepticism of population-wide restrictions—would have been unremarkable. Published in October 2020, the same claims were treated as a public health emergency in themselves, with the NIH director personally directing a "devastating takedown" and platforms suppressing discussion. The Fifth Circuit's findings in Missouri v. Biden (later Murthy v. Missouri at the Supreme Court) established that federal officials coerced platforms with "intimidating messages and threats of adverse consequences." That is not a footnote. It is a constitutional event.

Macedo and Lee are right to note the chilling effect on debate, but they don't quite name what was lost. What was lost was the ordinary scientific process. Science advances through public, contested, often messy disagreement. Steven Goodman's comment—that public debate among scientists can "confuse people and undermine consistent messaging"—captures the alternative epistemology that displaced ordinary science during the pandemic: a managerial model in which a designated authority (the WHO, the CDC, Fauci) decides what is true and the role of dissenters is to be silent or be silenced. Under this model, the proper response to Jay Bhattacharya, Sunetra Gupta, and Martin Kulldorff was not to engage their arguments but to delegitimize them. That is precisely what happened. And it worked, in the sense that for roughly two years it foreclosed the public discussion that might have changed policy.

The authors are too gentle about the press in this story. The New York Times, the Washington Post, the Atlantic, NPR—these outlets did not merely fail to interrogate official claims. They actively amplified the delegitimization. The Atlantic called Georgia's reopening "an experiment in human sacrifice"; the Times ran the doomsaying models on the front page without serious counter-modeling; the Post quoted Collins calling distinguished epidemiologists "fringe" without noting that their position was, in fact, the pre-pandemic mainstream. When the lab leak hypothesis—endorsed in private by senior virologists from the earliest weeks, as we now know from released emails—was labeled "misinformation," major papers parroted that label rather than reporting the underlying scientific disagreement. Zeynep Tufekci's "we were badly misled" column this March was a striking admission, but it should have appeared in 2021, and the misleading was not passive.

Why does any of this matter five years later, when the emergency is over? Three reasons.

First, the infrastructure of suppression remains. The pressure channels between federal agencies and platforms have been formalized, not dismantled. The legal questions about whether such pressure constitutes state action under the First Amendment remain unresolved. The next emergency—whether a pandemic, a climate event, a geopolitical conflict—will find these mechanisms ready to use, and there is little institutional learning to suggest they will be used with more restraint.

Second, the credentialing institutions that misfired have largely escaped accountability. Francis Collins retired with honors. The Stanford Faculty Senate has not, to my knowledge, withdrawn its censure of Atlas, even though Atlas's substantive positions on masks and school closures look considerably better in retrospect than the positions of those who censured him. Universities have not investigated the suppression of their own faculty. Journals have not retracted clearly mistaken commentary. Without accountability there is no learning.

Third—and this is where Macedo and Lee are entirely correct—the populist suspicion of expertise that Donald Trump exploits in 2025 is partly the predictable harvest of these failures. When public health authorities tell the public that schools must close based on "the science" and the science turns out to have been a contested model dressed in consensus clothing; when the same authorities denounce a lab leak as conspiracy theory and then quietly concede its plausibility; when platforms remove posts at government direction and the government denies doing so until courts force disclosure—the rational response of citizens is to discount future expert pronouncements. Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s confirmation as Secretary of Health and Human Services, Jay Bhattacharya's elevation to lead the NIH, the broader collapse of vaccine confidence: these are not bolts from the blue. They are responses, however overshooting, to an institutional betrayal.

Here I want to push past the lead essay's framing in one more respect. Macedo and Lee describe the failure as "tunnel vision" and "class bias" among elites. These descriptions are accurate but insufficiently structural. What we witnessed was the emergence of a coalition—federal health bureaucracies, large platforms, prestige media, university administrations, and aligned NGOs—that exhibited a remarkable capacity for coordinated message discipline. This was not a conspiracy; nobody had to be in a room together. It was something more troubling: a set of institutions that, having internalized similar political commitments and similar professional norms, converged on a single permissible position and treated departures from it as moral as well as factual errors. Call this what you will—the cathedral, the credentialed class, the laptop class. The pandemic revealed its existence and its power.

A serious reckoning with COVID-19 therefore cannot be only a retrospective on NPI effectiveness, though that work is essential and Macedo and Lee summarize the relevant findings well. It must include institutional reforms: legal limits on agency pressure of platforms, transparency requirements for "trust and safety" communications between government and industry, structural protections for academic dissenters, and—perhaps most importantly—a recovery within journalism of the habit of treating official sources as sources rather than as authorities.

I would add one further reform. We need a formal, independent national commission on the pandemic response, modeled on the 9/11 Commission. The United Kingdom has its COVID Inquiry; Sweden conducted one; Scotland commissioned the report Macedo and Lee cite. The United States, the country that spent more and disrupted more lives than any peer democracy, has nothing remotely comparable. The Senate HELP minority report and a handful of House select subcommittee reports do not substitute for a comprehensive, bipartisan, public-facing inquiry with subpoena power. That such a commission has not been established—five years on, under three different congressional configurations—is itself evidence of the elite consensus to move on. We should not move on.

One worry about my framing: doesn't emphasizing censorship and institutional malfeasance play into exactly the populist anti-expert narrative that, as Macedo and Lee note, the authors of this critique should be wary of strengthening? I do not think so. The strongest defense of expertise is honesty about its failures. Expertise that cannot be criticized is not expertise but authority, and authority claiming the prestige of expertise is precisely what corrodes public trust. The way back to a healthy relationship between scientific institutions and democratic publics runs through, not around, the painful work of acknowledging what went wrong.

Macedo and Lee have begun that work. Their essay deserves the broad public debate they call for. But the debate should not stop at whether the NPIs were effective—the empirical question. It should extend to how a society organized around free inquiry came to suppress inquiry on its most consequential policy question in living memory, and what institutional changes can prevent a recurrence. That is the postmortem we have not yet had.

The Easy Wisdom of Hindsight

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have produced an essay that reads with the calm confidence of people who were not, in February and March 2020, responsible for any decision whatsoever. Five years on, with bodies counted, mortality curves plotted, and the worst behind us, they offer "dispiriting answers" about institutional performance during the greatest public health emergency in a century. Their answers deserve scrutiny, because under the veneer of measured retrospection they assemble a tendentious narrative that mistakes the artifacts of a real crisis for the symptoms of elite folly.

Begin with their central empirical claim: that non-pharmaceutical interventions did not work. They support this primarily with a state-level correlation analysis showing that Republican and Democratic states had similar per capita mortality through April 2021, despite different policy stringency. They cite a 2023 Lancet study and a Scottish inquiry report that finds insufficient evidence for various measures. Therefore, they conclude, "imposing stricter policies appears not to have saved lives."

This is bad inference dressed up as analysis. State-level comparisons of stringency to mortality are among the weakest possible designs for evaluating NPI effectiveness. They confound dozens of variables the authors cannot adequately control for: population density, household structure, age distribution beyond crude percent-over-65, public transit usage, occupational composition, baseline health conditions, climate, seasonality, timing of viral introduction, and—crucially—the behavior of individuals independent of mandates. People in Democratic states reduced mobility before mandates and continued reducing it after mandates ended; people in some Republican states behaved cautiously despite the absence of orders. The "treatment" the authors are trying to measure is not state policy but the combination of state policy, voluntary behavior, and viral exposure timing. To attribute the residual to "policy ineffectiveness" is to misunderstand what the variation can tell us.

More importantly, the comparison the authors actually want to make—what would mortality have been with no NPIs at all?—cannot be made by comparing states that all had substantial NPIs at the beginning of the pandemic. By their own account, 43 governors issued shelter-in-place orders within three weeks of March 19, 2020. The relevant question is not whether Florida and California had similar mortality by April 2021. It is what mortality would have been if no jurisdiction had implemented anything in the spring of 2020—when the virus was spreading exponentially, hospitals in New York and Lombardy were collapsing, and a still-naive population had no immunity and no vaccine. We have no good counterfactual for that scenario. Macedo and Lee write as if we do.

Their treatment of pre-pandemic planning documents is similarly selective. Yes, the WHO 2019 report rated evidence for many NPIs as "low quality." That is what evidence ratings look like for any rare-event intervention: you cannot run randomized trials on lockdowns because pandemics are infrequent and consent is impossible. "Low quality evidence" is not "evidence of ineffectiveness"; it is "we have not had occasion to test this rigorously." The Johns Hopkins and UK documents the authors cite were also written for ordinary influenza pandemics with case fatality rates an order of magnitude lower than COVID-19's appeared to be in February 2020. The novelty of SARS-CoV-2—a virus with substantial asymptomatic transmission, no existing immunity, no vaccine, and uncertain but apparently high mortality—was precisely why prior planning documents were obviously inadequate. The authors treat the planning documents as a kind of scripture that policymakers betrayed, when in fact one of the basic lessons of crisis response is that plans don't survive contact with reality.

Now consider their rehabilitation of the Great Barrington Declaration. The Declaration's signature recommendation was "focused protection": allow lower-risk people to live normally and "build up immunity through natural infection," while protecting the vulnerable. This was not, as Macedo and Lee imply, a sober application of pre-pandemic planning wisdom. It was a specific and questionable policy proposal, and the criticisms of it were substantive, not merely tribal.

How, precisely, would you "protect the vulnerable" in a society where the virus is circulating freely? The elderly do not live in hermetically sealed compounds. They live with families, in mixed-age neighborhoods, in nursing homes staffed by lower-wage workers who go home to multigenerational households. The infectious disease epidemiologists who criticized GBD made this point repeatedly: there is no realistic mechanism by which a virus circulating uncontrolled in the under-65 population stays out of the over-65 population. You cannot quarantine vulnerability. The "focused protection" framing dissolved on contact with empirical reality—as Sweden's mortality, far higher than its Nordic neighbors', demonstrated.

Furthermore, the GBD framing assumed durable natural immunity from infection. By late 2020, this was already questionable; by 2021, with the Delta and Omicron variants, it was clearly wrong. Reinfection became common, immune-evading variants emerged, and "build up immunity through infection" became a recipe for repeated cycles of infection without the predicted endgame. The critics of GBD were not engaging in moral panic. They were correctly identifying that the proposal rested on biological assumptions that would not hold.

Macedo and Lee also do not grapple seriously with what "doing nothing" looked like in spring 2020. The authors invoke the planning documents to suggest that policymakers should have known NPIs would not work. But governors in March 2020 were watching ICU wards fill, refrigerated trucks pull up to hospitals, and exponential case curves point toward systemic collapse. The choice they faced was not "implement carefully calibrated focused protection" versus "shut everything down." It was "act drastically now, with imperfect information" versus "let hospitals collapse and see what happens." Anyone who has seriously studied crisis decision-making understands the asymmetric pressures involved. You can be criticized for overreacting; you will be politically destroyed if you underreact and hospitals fail. To call this "tunnel vision" is to substitute aesthetic critique for political realism.

On the censorship question, the authors offer their strongest material, but I want to register a complication. Platforms in 2020 were not engaged in a coherent suppression of dissident science. They were drowning in actual disinformation—ivermectin grifters, bleach drinkers, anti-vaccine conspiracists, "plandemic" videos, fraudulent miracle cures. Distinguishing legitimate dissent from harmful misinformation at scale, in real time, with millions of posts a day, is genuinely hard. Did platforms make errors? Of course. Did they sometimes remove content that turned out to be defensible? Yes—the lab leak case is the clearest example. But the framing of "censorship of dissenting experts" elides the much larger universe of content that was, in fact, dangerous nonsense. The mistake was not that platforms moderated; it was that they outsourced their judgment to authorities (the WHO, the CDC) whose own confidence often outran the underlying evidence. That is a real lesson, but it is a lesson about epistemic humility under platform pressure, not about a censorship regime.

Finally, the class-bias argument. Yes, knowledge workers fared better than service workers during lockdowns. Yes, school closures disproportionately harmed disadvantaged children. These are important truths, and Macedo and Lee are right to insist on them. But the implication that policy should have therefore been less restrictive ignores something equally important: it was the lower-wage, often minority workforce that staffed nursing homes, meatpacking plants, warehouses, hospitals, and grocery stores. When the virus circulated unchecked, those workers and their families bore much of the burden. Reduced community transmission—whatever its source—protected them too. The class story is real but cuts both ways, and the authors only acknowledge one direction.

None of this is to defend every pandemic policy. School closures in California and Oregon went on far too long, and the role of teachers' unions in extending them deserves the criticism it has belatedly received. CDC communication was often muddled and sometimes dishonest, particularly on masks in spring 2020. The lab leak question was handled badly. The fiscal response, while necessary in scale, was poorly targeted and contributed to inflation. A serious postmortem should examine all of this.

But Macedo and Lee's essay is not really a postmortem. It is an indictment, structured to reach a predetermined verdict. They cite skeptics as if those skeptics' positions were obviously correct in 2020; they cite mainstream positions as if those positions were obviously wrong; they treat post-hoc data analyses as definitive while waving away the methodological problems. The result is a narrative that flatters the readers of 2025 by suggesting that, had they only been in charge in 2020, the obvious right answer would have been implemented.

This is the easy wisdom of hindsight. A real postmortem would be harder, less certain, and considerably less satisfying.

What the State-Level Data Can and Cannot Tell Us

Macedo and Lee have done public discourse a service by insisting that the COVID-19 response deserves a proper reckoning. On the broad point—that we have not adequately interrogated our pandemic decisions, that institutions have been too quick to move on, that polarization stunted what should have been an ordinary process of expert dissent and revision—they are correct, and I am sympathetic to most of their normative claims. My objection is narrower but important: their central empirical argument relies on a methodology that cannot bear the weight they place on it.

Their Figure 2 is the load-bearing piece of evidence. It shows that, grouped by 2020 presidential vote, U.S. states had broadly similar cumulative per capita COVID-19 mortality through April 2021 despite very different policy stringency. From this they conclude that "more restrictive policies" did not "succeed in reducing death and serious illness." They reinforce the claim by citing a 2023 Lancet study that finds restrictions associated with lower infections but not lower mortality, and a Scottish review concluding insufficient evidence for most NPIs.

There are at least four problems with treating this evidence as decisive.

First, ecological inference. Comparing state-level stringency to state-level outcomes is a classic ecological design, and it is well known that ecological associations can misrepresent individual-level relationships. A state's stringency score is an aggregate of many distinct policies—school closures, business restrictions, gathering limits, travel rules, mask mandates—that may have very different effects. The Oxford Stringency Index sums these into a single number for tractability, but that number is not the "dose" of any particular intervention. Two states with identical scores can have radically different policy bundles. Two states with very different scores can have similar de facto behavioral environments, because formal policy and actual behavior diverge in complex ways. Macedo and Lee acknowledge none of this.

Second, confounding by initial conditions. The states that locked down hardest in spring 2020 were disproportionately the states hit first and hardest: New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Connecticut, California. These were also states with higher urban density, more international travel exposure, older populations in some cases, and substantial nursing-home infrastructure. Comparing their cumulative mortality to that of Wyoming or Mississippi without adjusting for these radically different starting conditions—the authors say they control for percent over 65, obesity, urbanization, and insurance, which is a partial set—does not tell us what we want to know. The lockdowns and the mortality were both responses to the same underlying epidemic intensity. Disentangling cause requires more than per capita comparisons.

Third, behavioral substitution. Lockdowns happened in a context where people were already changing behavior. Google mobility data from spring 2020 shows that mobility in Florida, Georgia, and other less restrictive states fell nearly as much as in heavily restricted states in March and early April, before reopening began. The marginal effect of a formal stay-at-home order on top of voluntary behavior change is much smaller than the effect of all the behavior change combined. State-level policy comparisons therefore systematically underestimate what NPIs as a category accomplished, because the comparison set is "more aggressive NPIs vs. less aggressive NPIs," not "NPIs vs. no behavioral change at all."

Fourth, temporal heterogeneity. Macedo and Lee themselves note that mortality diverged after the vaccine rollout, with Republican states experiencing 30 percent higher mortality by January 2023. This is important. It suggests that when the policy variable was vaccination rather than NPIs, large mortality differences did appear. The authors interpret this as supporting vaccines while indicting NPIs. But it is equally plausible that NPI variation in 2020 mattered less than vaccine variation in 2021–22 because (a) the NPI variation across states was smaller than it appeared once voluntary behavior is netted out, (b) the NPIs were implemented imperfectly and inconsistently, or (c) the dominant determinants of 2020 mortality were features like age structure and nursing-home protocols that NPI policy did not directly target. None of these would license the conclusion that NPIs "did not work" as a category.

The Lancet study they cite is more nuanced than their summary suggests. Stricter restrictions were associated with lower infection rates—a finding the authors mention but quickly elide. Lower infection rates, all else equal, should translate into lower mortality, particularly among the vulnerable. The reason the study did not find a clear mortality association is likely the same set of identification problems I have just described, not that NPIs fail to reduce mortality. Absence of evidence in observational designs is not evidence of absence.

What about the pre-pandemic planning documents? Here I think Macedo and Lee are on stronger ground in identifying a tension—pre-COVID consensus did not endorse society-wide lockdowns—but on weaker ground in treating that consensus as the gold standard against which 2020 policy should be judged. Those documents were written for influenza, not for a novel coronavirus with substantial asymptomatic transmission, R0 around 2.5–3, and apparent CFR (in February 2020) in the low single digits. Many of the planning assumptions were specific to flu and did not translate. The IFR of COVID-19 in the unvaccinated, particularly among the elderly, turned out to be in the range of 0.5–1 percent overall and dramatically higher in older cohorts—numbers that would have justified more aggressive intervention than influenza planning anticipated.

This does not vindicate every policy decision. Long school closures in many U.S. jurisdictions were clearly excessive in retrospect. By fall 2020, the evidence that K–8 schools were not major drivers of community transmission was substantial, and schools elsewhere in the OECD reopened. The U.S. failure here was not principally a failure of pandemic science; it was a failure of governance, in which teachers' unions had veto power and federal CDC guidance was excessively cautious for too long. Macedo and Lee are right to highlight this, and right to note its disproportionate burden on disadvantaged children.

Similarly, the suppression of debate about lab leak origins, the inconsistent messaging on masks in spring 2020, and the dismissive treatment of the Great Barrington signatories were genuine failures—failures of process and norms more than of specific policy choices. A scientific community confident in its position does not need to censor dissenters. The aggressive moralization of dissent that the authors describe was both intellectually unworthy and practically counterproductive. On these points the lead essay is largely correct.

But the leap from "process failures occurred" and "specific policies (long school closures) were wrong" to "NPIs as a class did not save lives" is where the essay overreaches. The first two claims are well-supported. The third is asserted on the basis of evidence that cannot establish it. And the difference matters, because what we tell ourselves about NPI effectiveness will shape the next pandemic response. If the conclusion is "NPIs don't work, don't bother," we will face the next novel pathogen having disarmed ourselves of tools that, properly targeted and time-limited, may indeed save lives. If the conclusion is "specific NPIs were misapplied, sustained too long, and imposed inequitable costs that were not adequately weighed," we will be better prepared.

A serious postmortem—the kind Macedo and Lee correctly call for—should foreground methodological humility. It should distinguish between policies (where evidence is mixed and intervention-specific), processes (where failures are clear), and norms (where suppression of debate damaged the scientific enterprise). It should resist the temptation, equally present on left and right, to extract a single clean lesson from a complex event. The pandemic was not a controlled experiment, and we will not get clean answers by pretending it was.

Macedo and Lee have asked the right question. The answer requires more care than they have given it.

The Pandemic Was a Polarization Event, Not an Elite Failure

The most interesting fact in Macedo and Lee's essay is one they note but, I think, do not fully metabolize. Through mid-March 2020, COVID-19 policy in the United States was bipartisan. Forty-three governors issued shelter-in-place orders within three weeks of March 19. Republican and Democratic states alike closed schools, restricted gatherings, and shut nonessential businesses. The initial response was a national consensus, supported by Trump's own (inconsistent) statements and implemented with public approval that crossed partisan lines.

The divergence Macedo and Lee document—Democratic states maintaining stringent measures more than twice as long as Republican states, school closures stretching into 2021 in blue jurisdictions, the partisan sorting of attitudes toward masks and later vaccines—did not begin in February 2020. It began in late spring and accelerated through the summer. By the time the Great Barrington Declaration appeared in October, COVID-19 was no longer a public health emergency in the public mind. It was a political identity marker, structured around the upcoming election and the Trump–anti-Trump axis that dominated everything else.

This is the analytical frame I think the lead essay underweights. Macedo and Lee describe the pandemic response as a failure of expert judgment, distorted by elite class interests and a moralized intolerance of dissent. There is truth in this. But "elite failure" misses what was actually happening, which is that the pandemic response became a domain in a much broader, prior pattern of affective polarization. The substantive policy disagreements that emerged—about lockdowns, masks, schools, vaccines, lab leak—did not produce the polarization. The polarization absorbed them.

Consider the timing. Mask wearing did not become a partisan signal until Trump made it one in spring 2020, refusing to wear masks publicly and mocking opponents who did. Once that signal was set, blue Americans wore masks as identity expression and red Americans refused them as identity expression, more or less independent of evolving evidence about mask efficacy. The Great Barrington Declaration entered this environment, and the response to it—both the dismissal by Collins and Fauci and the embrace by anti-lockdown activists—was structured by the same identity logic. The declaration's authors became partisan symbols whether they wanted to be or not. Bhattacharya's eventual elevation to direct the NIH under Trump in 2025 completes the arc: the dissenters were not merely vindicated; they were absorbed into the opposing political coalition.

What does this mean for Macedo and Lee's diagnosis? Mainly that the prescription—a more honest, balanced expert discourse that acknowledges uncertainty and weighs tradeoffs—was, by mid-2020, no longer available. The expert institutions the authors criticize were not operating in a neutral public sphere where careful argument might have shifted opinion. They were operating in a political environment in which any acknowledgment of uncertainty about NPIs would have been weaponized by the Trump administration to justify abandoning the response entirely. Francis Collins's "devastating takedown" email looks ugly out of context; in context, it reflects the (probably correct) judgment that any opening for skepticism would be exploited not for reasoned policy adjustment but for total abandonment of mitigation.

This is not a justification of the suppression of dissent. It is an explanation, and the distinction matters because the prescriptive implications differ. If the problem were narrowly elite failure, the solution would be better elites: more humble, more honest, more attuned to tradeoffs. If the problem is structural—polarized publics making nuanced expert communication impossible, partisan media transforming every empirical question into an identity claim, social platforms accelerating both—then the solution requires structural change in the information environment, not just better personnel at the CDC.

Macedo and Lee gesture at this when they discuss "moral and epistemic tribalism." But they then revert to treating the failure as one of elite judgment: scientists, journalists, and academics should have been more honest, more balanced, more willing to engage dissent. I agree that they should have. I am skeptical that doing so would have changed outcomes much. The cognitive scientists who study polarization—Dan Kahan's work on cultural cognition, Lilliana Mason's on social identity, the abundant work on motivated reasoning—suggest that in a high-salience political environment, more information and more nuance from experts tend to amplify polarization, not reduce it, because partisans selectively absorb evidence consistent with their priors. The climate change case, which Macedo and Lee briefly mention, is precisely such a case: decades of careful, increasingly confident expert communication have not produced consensus because the issue is not, for most citizens, a knowledge question but an identity question.

The pandemic ran on the same track, only faster. Within months, attitudes about masks, lockdowns, and schools had crystallized into stable partisan positions that proved largely impervious to subsequent evidence. The state-level data Macedo and Lee present—Democratic states maintaining high stringency through summer 2021 even as Republican states reopened with no obvious mortality penalty—are not, in my reading, mainly a story about cautious experts insulated from costs. They are a story about partisan governance: governors responding to the preferences of their political coalitions, with Democratic voters demanding caution and Republican voters demanding normalcy. The "experts" the authors target were operating within, and partly responsive to, this partisan structure.

This reframing has implications for what a postmortem should look like.

First, it implies that institutional reform of public health agencies, while needed, will not solve the underlying problem. The CDC could become more transparent, more humble, more willing to acknowledge uncertainty—and still find its messaging absorbed into partisan identity battles in the next emergency. Reform is necessary but insufficient.

Second, it implies that the focus of the postmortem should include the political economy of polarized media and platforms, not just expert institutions. The role of cable news, partisan podcasters, and social media algorithms in transforming COVID policy into identity politics deserves as much attention as the role of the WHO and CDC. Macedo and Lee largely confine themselves to the latter.

Third, it suggests that the most important pandemic preparedness investment for the next crisis is not better NPI evidence (though we should fund that), but mechanisms for de-escalating partisan capture of public health communication. This is genuinely hard. It might involve depoliticizing the structure of expert advisory bodies, pre-committing to evidence-review protocols before crises strike, or building cross-partisan public health communication infrastructure. I do not have a clean recipe. I am confident that "more honest experts" is not, by itself, the answer.

Fourth, and finally, the polarization frame should make us cautious about the postmortem itself. The lead essay is appearing in a political moment in which a particular reading of COVID—the lockdowns were a catastrophic mistake, experts cannot be trusted, the dissenters were heroes—is ascendant in the new administration and aligned media. Macedo and Lee write carefully and do not endorse the more extreme versions of this narrative. But the public reception of their essay will be shaped by the polarized environment, with their measured critique enlisted on one side and dismissed by the other. We risk replacing one polarized misreading of COVID with another, with no greater institutional learning at the end.

The deeper challenge Macedo and Lee identify—how can liberal democracy maintain expert institutions that are both authoritative and accountable in a polarized environment?—is not specific to pandemics. It is the central institutional question of our political moment. Climate, AI, elections, foreign policy, monetary policy: each domain faces some version of the same problem, in which credentialed expertise must operate amid public distrust without becoming either unaccountable technocracy or pure partisan instrument. The pandemic was a particularly intense expression of this challenge, not an aberration from it.

So I agree with the authors that we need a reckoning. I just think the reckoning has to look harder at ourselves—at the polarized publics that, in 2020, neither expert humility nor expert confidence could have made into a deliberative democracy. The pandemic did not reveal a failure of expertise. It revealed the limits of expertise in a polity that has lost the capacity to deliberate. Recovering that capacity, not improving the experts, is the project ahead.

The Reckoning We Need

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have written a courageous and necessary essay. Their argument—that the U.S. pandemic response failed on the core questions of scientific honesty, trade-off deliberation, and institutional self-examination—should become the starting point for a long-overdue accountability process. I share their dispiriting answer: No, we did not perform well, we did not weigh tradeoffs adequately, and we have resolutely refused to conduct an honest postmortem. But where I differ is in my assessment of the consequences: the cost of this failure is not merely a matter of wasted resources or avoidable death, though those are staggering. The deepest wound is to democratic trust itself. By pretending that our actions were firmly grounded in settled science when they were not, by silencing dissent and moralizing disagreement, our institutions betrayed the public in ways that will echo for decades.

The first failure—truthful scientific communication—is by now undeniable, yet still rarely acknowledged with the seriousness it deserves. As Macedo and Lee document, the pre-pandemic consensus was clear: the evidence base for sweeping non-pharmaceutical interventions was extraordinarily thin, and experts warned against their blanket application precisely because the secondary harms would be severe. The World Health Organization’s 2019 guidance rated nearly all such measures as having “very low” quality evidence and explicitly advised against border closures and quarantine. The U.S. Institute of Medicine, in 2007, had already cautioned that in a crisis there would be immense pressure to implement unproven interventions, and that this pressure should be resisted in favor of contextual assessment. Yet within weeks of the first U.S. cases, those warnings were not merely ignored; they were memory-holed. Public health authorities and journalists spoke as if lockdowns, school closures, and mask mandates were the scientific equivalent of gravity—imperatives about which no reasonable person could disagree.

Consider the early mask saga. In February and March 2020, figures like Dr. Anthony Fauci and Surgeon General Jerome Adams told the public that masks were not effective and should not be worn. Fauci later admitted that the advice was at least partly strategic: a concern that healthcare workers would face shortages. Whatever the justification, the public was misled. Then in a sharp reversal, masks became compulsory, with authorities insisting that even cloth masks—whose efficacy was barely studied—would significantly curb transmission. The whiplash wasn’t just confusing; it was corrosive. Millions of Americans correctly perceived that they were being managed, not informed. The eventual admission that some of this communication was a “noble lie” cemented a lasting suspicion that public health guidance is less a matter of transparent science than of paternalistic expedience.

That erosion of trust was amplified enormously by the organized suppression of dissenting perspectives. The episode surrounding the Great Barrington Declaration is the paradigmatic example. Its authors were not cranks; they were respected infectious-disease epidemiologists and biostatisticians at leading universities. Their proposal for “focused protection” was, as Macedo and Lee show, a direct echo of pre-pandemic planning documents that warned lockdowns would disproportionately harm the working class and the young. Yet the response from the scientific establishment was not a refutation by evidence but an anathematization. NIH Director Francis Collins called for a “quick and devastating published take down” of what he labeled “fringe” epidemiologists. The American Public Health Association and other organizations pronounced the declaration “NOT based in science” without grappling with its evidence or reasoning. On social media, platforms working in concert with the government removed content that questioned official dogmas, including doubts about lockdown efficacy or the lab-leak theory of the virus’s origin—the latter of which is now increasingly acknowledged as plausible, if not likely.

The second failure—weighing tradeoffs—flows directly from the first. Because the official line was that NPIs were unquestionably necessary, any mention of their enormous costs was treated as morally suspect. Yet the costs, as the lead essay chillingly enumerates, were nothing short of catastrophic. More than 50 million children were ejected from schools, with the most vulnerable—poor, Black, and Hispanic students—suffering learning losses that experts say will take “many years to recoup.” A generation of the youngest children show delays in basic social and motor skills. The surge in homicides in 2020 was the largest year-over-year increase in U.S. history, concentrated in neighborhoods where schools closed and young men lost work. Excess deaths from delayed healthcare, drug overdoses, and other non-COVID causes rivaled the official pandemic toll in some demographics. And the United States added trillions to the national debt, with a significant share lost to fraud.

If these sacrifices had clearly saved lives, we could at least weigh them in a moral calculus. But as Macedo and Lee demonstrate with their state-level analysis, there is no robust correlation between the stringency of restrictions and COVID-19 mortality—at least not until vaccines arrived, after which Republican states with lower vaccination rates fared worse. The policy divergence between Democratic and Republican states from April 2020 through mid-2021, while enormous, left almost no imprint on cumulative per capita deaths. This finding, replicated by other major studies including a 2023 Lancet article, should have been front-page news and prompted an urgent re-evaluation of policy. Instead, it was largely ignored. Even now, many commentators attribute the entire tragedy to the virus itself, as if the policies were a neutral, inevitable response. But school closures were not a viral phenomenon; they were human choices. The resulting damage belongs to us.

The third failure—the dearth of honest postmortems—is the most damning because it is the most easily avoided. The lead essay’s central plea is for a comprehensive retrospective, and it is remarkable that more than five years after the outbreak, nothing of the sort has been undertaken with the rigor or independence required. Congressional hearings have been partisan theater. The academic public health community, with honorable exceptions, has largely circled the wagons, framing any critique as ammunition for the populist right. The press has produced occasional mea culpas about “things could have been handled better,” but these rarely engage the central epistemological and moral questions. We have not commissioned a national inquiry akin to the 9/11 Commission or the Warren Commission, with subpoena power and a mandate to examine not just what went wrong but why institutions that were supposed to be guardians of empirical reality so signally failed.

This is not simply an academic failure. It is a political emergency. The loss of trust in science, medicine, and government is now one of the defining features of our public life. Donald Trump’s return to power—if indeed it occurs, and as of mid-2025 the prospect looms large—is fueled in part by a widespread perception that elite institutions do not level with the public. When those institutions behave exactly as their critics claim, by covering up uncertainty and punishing dissent, they validate the very forces they purport to oppose. The path to restoring credibility does not run through doubling down on past justifications. It runs through a full, painful accounting that says: we were not honest with you; we did not weigh the costs fairly; and we are going to find out what must change so that we never repeat these mistakes.

Macedo and Lee are right that the risks of a frank conversation are real. There will be bad-faith interpretations; there will be opportunistic attacks. But the alternative—continued evasion—guarantees that the next time a novel pathogen emerges, the public will greet official pronouncements with cynicism or outright defiance. Pandemics are a stress test for democracy, and we failed not because we lacked the tools but because we lacked the courage to use them truthfully. The reckoning is overdue.

Defending the Firewall: Why Our Institutions Did What They Had To

Macedo and Lee’s essay is a serious and sobering contribution to the debate over COVID-19 policy. It correctly identifies many of the lasting tensions that the pandemic created, and it does well to demand a deeper public conversation. Yet its central thesis—that U.S. institutions failed to represent science honestly, failed to weigh tradeoffs, and have evaded a reckoning—is not simply mistaken; it is a dangerous oversimplification that, if accepted as the dominant narrative, will make us less prepared for the next crisis. The authors commit two cardinal errors: they treat the pre-pandemic planning consensus as if it were a set of definitive, actionable prohibitions rather than a cautious baseline, and they evaluate policy outcomes with the benefit of a hindsight that was utterly unavailable to decision-makers in the terrifying spring of 2020.

First, on scientific honesty. The lead essay assembles a compelling list of pre-2020 documents that warned against non-pharmaceutical interventions and questioned their evidence base. But a careful reading of those same texts reveals a more nuanced picture: they did not say “never use NPIs”; they said the evidence was weak and that deployment must be context-sensitive and accompanied by assessment of harms. That is precisely what happened—at least in the initial weeks, when the context was an explosive, poorly understood respiratory virus with a case fatality rate that early reports pegged at between 2 and 5 percent, far deadlier than seasonal flu. In China, and then in northern Italy, health systems collapsed. Morgues overflowed. The WHO’s own director-general, whom the authors quote criticizing lockdowns earlier, by March 2020 was urging countries to “test, trace, isolate, and treat” and praising China’s lockdown as having “suppressed” the outbreak. The sudden pivot was not born of a conspiracy to mislead but of a harrowing encounter with new data points—data that no amount of pre-pandemic modeling could have substituted for.

The Imperial College model that drove U.S. and UK policy on March 16–17, 2020, has been criticized for its dire projections. But at the time, it was the best epidemiologists could offer. The model predicted that without intervention, 2.2 million Americans could die. To wait for randomized controlled trials—which are ethically and practically impossible to conduct in the middle of a pandemic surge—would have been a gamble with millions of lives. Public health operates on the precautionary principle: when the stakes are high and the evidence is mixed, the sane choice is to act to prevent worst-case outcomes, not to do nothing. That is not dishonesty; it is responsible stewardship.

Moreover, the lead essay’s argument rests heavily on the claim that because state-level variation in NPIs did not produce commensurate variation in mortality through April 2021, NPIs “did not work.” This is a textbook ecological fallacy. States did not implement restrictions in a vacuum; their neighbors’ policies, the national media environment, and voluntary behavioral changes all smoothed out effects. Republican states that kept schools open still saw decreased mobility and mask-wearing in many localities, thanks to private sector mandates and individual caution. Furthermore, more stringent states like New York and New Jersey were hit early, before lockdowns could fully take effect; the virus then spread later to less stringent southern states, meaning the comparison is temporally confounded. When researchers construct more sophisticated models that account for timing, population density, mobility, and weather, a different picture emerges: closures of schools and businesses did reduce transmission, even if those effects were modest and difficult to disentangle from voluntary behavior. The 2023 Lancet study the authors cite found that mask mandates were not associated with lower mortality, but it did find that restrictions on gatherings and stay-at-home orders were associated with lower infection rates. Cherry-picking the null findings while ignoring the broader literature does not constitute a refutation.

The authors’ treatment of the Great Barrington Declaration reveals a deeper problem. They present the declaration as a sober, evidence-based alternative that was unjustly vilified. But the declaration was deeply flawed, not because its authors were immoral but because its core prescription—focused protection—was operationally hollow. How exactly do you “protect” the elderly and medically vulnerable in a society where multigenerational households are common, where nursing homes depend on low-wage staff who cannot afford to isolate, and where a quarter of Americans lack access to paid sick leave? The “focused protection” idea assumed a degree of social compartmentalization that simply does not exist. Worse, its call to allow the young and healthy to become infected presumed that natural immunity would be benign and durable, and that long COVID and post-viral complications would be rare. We now know, from abundant research by 2025, that even mild infections can cause persistent cardiovascular, neurological, and autoimmune damage; that immunity wanes; and that allowing mass infection would have meant millions of Americans with chronic illness. The denunciation of the declaration by public health leaders was harsh, but it was grounded in a recognition that the plan would have caused massive, avoidable suffering, especially among the working class the declaration purported to protect.

On censorship, the essay paints a portrait of an Orwellian collusion between government and Big Tech. But the reality was far messier. In the early months, when a novel pathogen was killing hundreds of Americans daily, social media platforms were awash in genuinely dangerous content: videos promoting bleach as a cure, posts falsely claiming that face masks cause carbon dioxide poisoning, and coordinated campaigns to discourage vaccination. Platforms, facing intense public pressure and the specter of regulatory action, turned to authoritative sources like the CDC and WHO for guidance on what to remove. That process was imperfect—the suppression of lab-leak discussions, for example, was an overcorrection that harmed scientific openness. But to equate the removal of harmful misinformation with a coordinated attack on dissent is to ignore the genuine public health crisis those platforms confronted. A federal circuit court in 2023 agreed that some government communications crossed lines into “intimidation,” and that warrants remediation. But it does not demonstrate that the entire edifice of content moderation was a conspiracy against truth-tellers.

What of the tradeoffs? Macedo and Lee are right that the harms of school closures, job loss, and delayed medical care were severe and fell disproportionately on the disadvantaged. But their analysis assumes that these harms were largely a product of lockdowns, rather than of the pandemic itself. Is it really plausible that without any restrictions, unemployment would have remained low? The service sector cratered not just because of government mandates but because people were terrified of dying; air travel, restaurant visits, and tourism collapsed in countries with minimal restrictions as well. School closures, too, were not simply a matter of governor’s orders: many parents, especially in low-income communities of color, were deeply reluctant to send children back into classrooms without adequate mitigations, and teacher unions in many blue states refused to work in person without extensive safety protocols. The policy debate is far more entangled with public sentiment and labor conditions than the lead essay acknowledges.

Finally, the call for a comprehensive postmortem is laudable. But the kind of postmortem we get matters. If it becomes an exercise in scapegoating public health professionals for acting with imperfect knowledge, it will only accelerate the brain drain from government service and further politicize the next response. The better model is the aviation safety investigation: a meticulous, forward-looking analysis that asks how systems can be improved, not who should be punished. That approach would require acknowledging that the vaccine rollout, especially Operation Warp Speed, was an extraordinary success; that the U.S. did manage to prevent the total collapse of its hospital system; and that many of the worst disparities were rooted in long-standing structural inequalities, not in bad-faith decisions by experts.

Macedo and Lee fear that a frank assessment will be weaponized by Trump and his allies. Their solution is to get ahead of that weaponization by offering an even more devastating critique. But this strategy risks burning down the village to save it. The institutions of science and public health, for all their flaws, remain our best defense against the chaos of a post-truth politics. A reckoning is necessary, but it must be one that strengthens those institutions by making them more transparent and responsive, not one that hands their enemies the cudgel with which to demolish them. The lead essay, for all its nuance, tilts too far toward the latter.

The Enemy Was Uncertainty, Not Science

Five years on, the pandemic feels like a dream that the world has collectively agreed to forget. Macedo and Lee are right to jolt us out of that lethargy, but the argument they advance—that U.S. institutions systematically failed to tell the truth, to weigh tradeoffs, and to look inward—is at once too sweeping and, in a peculiar way, not sweeping enough. It is too sweeping because it conflates the genuine epistemological predicament of the early pandemic with institutional bad faith. It is not sweeping enough because it never quite confronts the profound challenge that radical uncertainty poses to democratic governance. The real failure of the COVID-19 response was not that “science” was misrepresented but that our public institutions proved structurally incapable of navigating the fog of a truly novel threat. Both the lockdown enthusiasts and their libertarian critics shared the same fallacy: the conviction that, if only the right data had been applied, the road not taken would have been clearly superior.

Begin with the question of scientific communication. The lead essay documents, with meticulous sourcing, that the pre-pandemic consensus warned against broad society-wide NPIs. The WHO’s 2019 report, the Johns Hopkins preparedness blueprint, the UK’s 2011 influenza strategy—all stressed that the evidence for lockdowns, school closures, and border restrictions was weak, that unintended consequences were severe, and that such measures should not be undertaken lightly. The essay then argues that when COVID-19 struck, these warnings were ignored, and the public was told a triumphalist story of “following the science” that papered over deep uncertainties. That much is undeniable. But is it evidence of a cover-up, or of a genuine shift in expert judgment? The documents in question were not holy writ; they were context-dependent heuristics written for an unspecified future pandemic. When the future arrived with a pathogen that had a high reproduction number, a significant infection fatality rate, and an apparently frightening capacity to overrun intensive care units, decision-makers—not just in the United States but across every continent—reassessed. They looked to on-the-ground reports from Wuhan and Bergamo, not just to models, and concluded that the old playbook was insufficient. This was not, as the essay sometimes implies, a paternalistic conspiracy; it was adaptive, albeit hasty, reasoning under fire.

The real sin, if there is one, was not the decision to intervene but the failure to build learning into the intervention from the start. Macedo and Lee note that policy “only weakly adjusted to changing conditions” and that partisan identity soon locked in divergent approaches regardless of epidemiological indicators. That is a critique of political polarization and bureaucratic inertia, not of the scientific advisory process per se. Yet the essay repeatedly attributes this rigidity to a moralized public health establishment that crushed dissent. There is truth there—the demonization of the Great Barrington Declaration was a shameful episode, and the suppression of legitimate debate about lab origins and cloth mask efficacy should alarm anyone who values open inquiry. But the causal arrow points both ways. The right-wing media ecosystem’s embrace of hydroxychloroquine, ivermectin, and anti-vaccine conspiracies created an environment where any admission of uncertainty by officials was instantly weaponized. Public health communicators who might have preferred to level with the public faced a classic “noble lie” dilemma: speak with nuance and risk emboldening the forces of denial, or project confidence and risk losing credibility later. No amount of institutional virtue can resolve a Catch-22 of its own making, but that Catch-22 was itself a product of political tribalism, not the other way around.

The essay’s empirical anchor is the state-level comparison of stringency and mortality. The authors show that from March 2020 through mid-2021, Republican and Democratic states implemented sharply different policies, yet their cumulative COVID-19 mortality was nearly identical. This is an important and under-discussed finding, and it deserves serious engagement. But as any epidemiologist will quickly note, the absence of a correlation in observational state-level data does not prove the absence of a causal effect. States are interconnected. Florida’s relative openness may have contributed to New York’s later outbreaks, and vice versa, through travel. Voluntary behavior—mask-wearing, social distancing—was often more widespread in places where mandates were absent but citizens were fearful. And the most vulnerable populations, like nursing home residents, were subject to federal regulations and common supply-chain problems that did not vary by state policy. To interpret the null finding as proof that “NPIs did not work” is to ignore the complex ecology of a pandemic. The same data could equally be read to suggest that early, aggressive action by blue states in March and April saved lives that were then lost to later, less restrictive policies in red states, creating a wash in totals. The truth is we simply do not know. The lead essay, while accusing others of false certainty, veers dangerously close to asserting a certainty of its own.

This points to a deeper philosophical issue: what does it mean for a policy to “work” in a pandemic? If the goal was to permanently suppress the virus until a vaccine arrived, then almost all countries failed, because the virus eventually infected most of the planet. If the goal was to “flatten the curve” and prevent hospital collapse, then the United States arguably succeeded, albeit at staggering collateral cost. The lead essay is entirely correct that many prominent figures—in the Safra Center roadmap, for instance—explicitly invoked a wartime mobilization metaphor that implied total victory over the virus, and that this framing was a catastrophic mistake. But the error was one of ambition and rhetoric, not necessarily of policy substance. School closures, for example, were indeed imposed with far too little attention to the evidence that children were low risk and poor transmitters. But part of that oversight stemmed from a genuinely uncertain science: in the spring of 2020, we did not know with confidence whether children were major drivers of spread, and the cautious course (closing schools) seemed, to many, the safer bet. That it proved to be a disastrously wrong bet for millions of children is a truth we must face, but facing it requires us to accept that in environments of deep uncertainty, plausible bets can still lose catastrophically. That is not a pathology; it is the human condition.

A better postmortem would thus focus not on rooting out the villains who misled the public but on redesigning the machinery by which evidence is translated into policy. The lead essay laments that we have not had a comprehensive reckoning, and it is right. But the kind of reckoning it envisions—a truth commission that establishes the factual record of what was known when—is only half the project. We also need an institutional architecture that can make policy more nimbly, with built-in mechanisms for randomized evaluation, transparent updating, and political accountability for tradeoffs. When the next novel pathogen emerges, we will once again be in the dark. The question is not whether we will make mistakes but whether we will have created systems that correct mistakes quickly, rather than entrenching them for months or years.

Macedo and Lee have performed an invaluable service by insisting that we stop looking away. But their essay, for all its careful scholarship, occasionally slides from indictment to overcorrection. To say that the public health establishment failed is true; to imply that a better establishment would have had clear, ex ante answers to the question “lock down or not?” is to succumb to the very illusion of certainty that they rightly criticize. The real lesson of COVID-19 is that liberal democracy has not yet learned how to govern under catastrophic uncertainty without either falling into technocratic hubris or retreating to partisan bunkers. That is a problem bigger than any one agency or profession. It is a challenge for the entire political order.

The Cost of Neglecting the Vulnerable

Macedo and Lee have written a sweeping indictment of the U.S. pandemic response, and their core charge—that expert institutions succumbed to tunnel vision, moralized dissent, and refused to weigh tradeoffs—contains substantial truth. But as I read their essay, a quiet discomfort grows. Their argument, for all its empirical sophistication, ultimately rests on a frame that is more congenial to the right than they might wish to admit: that the big mistake was government overreach, that restrictions were ineffective, and that the main victims were those harmed by the lockdowns, not those ravaged by the virus itself. A very different story can be told, and it is one that centers race, class, and structural inequality rather than the epistemological sins of public health officials. From that vantage, the real failure was not that we did too much, but that we did not do nearly enough for those on the receiving end of a social order that was lethal long before COVID-19 arrived.

Let’s start with the premise that NPIs did not save lives. The lead essay’s Figure 2—showing virtually identical mortality across red and blue states through April 2021—is striking, but the interpretation is trickier than it seems. The virus did not respect state lines. Many of the deadliest early outbreaks happened in densely packed, lower-income communities in New York City, Newark, and other urban centers that were locked down hard but already had high seeding before restrictions took effect. Meanwhile, rural, predominantly white communities in states with loose policies often had less population density and fewer international travelers, which may have delayed their surges but not prevented them. The counterfactual we need—what would have happened if no state had locked down at all—cannot be read from these graphs. We do know, however, that in the absence of any restrictions, the virus disproportionately sickened and killed essential workers: grocery clerks, bus drivers, meatpacking plant employees, home health aides. These were not the “laptop class” that the lead essay correctly identifies as insulated from hardship; they were the very working-class people the essay says were most harmed by policy. But they were harmed by a virus that flourished where workplaces remained open and protections were absent. The decision to shutter restaurants and offices while keeping logistics hubs and factories running was a tragic compromise, but it was driven by economic necessity, not by a scientific consensus. And it was that compromise—the designation of some workers as sacrificial—that produced the stark racial and class disparities in infection and death we saw throughout 2020. The lead essay notes that Black Americans experienced the largest rise in all-cause mortality, but it attributes much of this to policy side effects. In truth, many of those excess deaths were directly from COVID-19, and they occurred among people who could not afford to stay home and whose jobs were deemed “essential” without offering adequate safety nets.

This brings me to the Great Barrington Declaration. The lead essay treats the declaration’s authors as unjustly persecuted truth-tellers, and I agree that the personal vitriol they faced was unacceptable. But the substance of their proposal deserves far more scrutiny than the authors give it. “Focused protection” is a seductive phrase, but in the United States it was a mirage. How do you protect an elderly parent who lives in a multigenerational household in a one-bedroom apartment in Queens? How do you protect a dialysis patient who relies on public transportation and a low-wage job to survive? The declaration’s premise was that society could be neatly divided into the “vulnerable” and the “resilient,” and that the latter could resume normal life while the former were shielded. But vulnerability is not a discrete category; it is a gradient produced by poverty, by racism, by the very structures of American life. The only way to make focused protection work would have been to provide guaranteed income, safe housing, and comprehensive healthcare to every high-risk person and their families for the duration. That would have required precisely the kind of massive government intervention that the declaration’s supporters—many affiliated with free-market think tanks—have spent decades opposing. The proposal was thus either naïve or disingenuous. In practice, letting the virus rip would have meant that the same working-class, minority communities that the lead essay champions as lockdown’s losers would have faced a tidal wave of sickness and death with even less support, because the public health apparatus would have effectively stood down.

Which is not to say that school closures were harmless. They were devastating. But the lead essay’s framing—that closures were an unforced error by elites—misses the agency of educators, parents, and communities themselves. In many urban districts serving large Black and Latino populations, families were deeply reluctant to send children back into aging buildings with poor ventilation, no testing, and crowded classrooms. The lead essay's core argument is that non-pharmaceutical interventions lacked evidentiary support and therefore should not have been deployed at the scale they were. This argument mistakes the nature of emergency decision-making. Policymakers do not wait for certainty before acting; they cannot. The pre-pandemic literature questioning NPI effectiveness was real, but it did not demonstrate that NPIs would be useless against COVID-19 specifically—it showed that evidence was limited, a different and important distinction.

Consider what alternative frameworks might have guided decision-making. The "focused protection" approach advocated by the Great Barrington Declaration sounds reasonable in retrospect, but it required assumptions about voluntary behavior by vulnerable populations that had no historical precedent. The steep age gradient of COVID-19 mortality was not fully understood until months into the pandemic. Protecting nursing homes while allowing community spread was the stated intent of many policies; the catastrophic outbreaks in eldercare facilities showed how difficult this would prove in practice.

The essay correctly notes that cross-state policy variation did not produce divergent mortality outcomes by early 2021. But this analysis is incomplete. The vaccines arrived in late 2020, confounding any comparison of "lockdown versus no lockdown" outcomes. We cannot observe counterfactual history; we can only model it, and models offered widely varying projections.

The critique of institutional communication deserves more nuanced treatment. The essay faults public health officials for not communicating uncertainty—but research on risk communication shows that expressing uncertainty to frightened publics can undermine trust and compliance at precisely the moment when cooperation matters most. This is not a justification for deception, but it complicates the easy judgment that officials should have simply been more candid. Effective communication during crisis requires careful calibration that the essay does not acknowledge.

Class bias affected who bore the costs of both the pandemic and the policies designed to address it. The essay is right that elite insularity distorted policy judgments, but this critique applies across the political spectrum.

The most illuminating question in Macedo and Lee's essay is not whether lockdowns "worked" but why we implemented policies so divorced from our own pre-pandemic planning. Before COVID-19, the official consensus held that society-wide lockdowns were unworkable, counterproductive, and inadequately supported by evidence. Yet within weeks in 2020, nearly every Western democracy adopted exactly these measures. How did this happen, and what does it reveal about institutional failure?

The essay traces this reversal with care, documenting how the WHO itself rated most NPIs as ineffective or inadvisable just months before recommending them against COVID-19. The Imperial College models predicting catastrophic mortality catalyzed action, but those models proved wildly pessimistic. Why did institutions that had spent years preparing for pandemics so thoroughly abandon their own guidance?

Part of the answer lies in epistemic capture. The public health community had invested in "containment" strategies that emphasized aggressive early intervention. When COVID-19 arrived, this investment created pressure to act decisively, even beyond what the evidence supported. The models offered numerical precision that obscured their underlying assumptions. A prediction of 510,000 UK deaths commands attention in ways that hedged uncertainty cannot.

But the deeper failure was communication. The essay documents how public health institutions shifted from acknowledging uncertainty to asserting confidence, often without new evidence to justify the shift. Early statements questioning lockdowns gave way to pronouncements that suppression "could" work, then to claims that it "would" work, then to the implicit assumption that anything short of suppression was morally unthinkable.

This communicative drift caused lasting damage to institutional credibility. When officials who had expressed doubt subsequently defended policies they had initially questioned, the public had legitimate grounds for suspicion. The lesson is not that officials should never change their minds—science advances through revision—but that the reasons for revision must be transparently communicated. Credibility depends on honesty about what we know and what we do not.

The treatment of dissenting experts is documented well in the essay. The email exchange between Francis Collins and Anthony Fauci discussing how to achieve a "quick and devastating published take down" of the Great Barrington Declaration represents a failure of scientific culture. Genuine scientific disagreement should be adjudicated through evidence and argument, not through coordinated professional condemnation. The signatories of the declaration may have been wrong—their focused protection strategy faced genuine implementation challenges—but they deserved engagement rather than dismissal.

The essay's analysis of cross-state policy variation and outcomes is its weakest component. The claim that "imposing stricter policies appears not to have saved lives" overstates what the evidence can support. Cross-sectional comparisons of state policies are confounded by numerous factors: demographics, density, timing of waves, informal behavior, compliance. The Lancet study the essay cites found that restrictions were associated with lower infection rates, even if mortality effects proved harder to isolate. These are genuinely difficult empirical questions that deserve more careful treatment.

The essay's strongest contribution is its analysis of class bias in pandemic policy. The "laptop class" that designed and debated policy was insulated from its costs in ways that distorted judgment. School closures persisted longest in jurisdictions where professionals could supervise remote learning; the children most harmed were those whose parents lacked this flexibility. Essential workers bore infection risks while decision-makers worked from home. The distributional consequences of pandemic policy were both predictable and predicted, yet received inadequate attention in real-time.

What should we learn from this episode? First, that pandemic planning documents should be treated as living guidance rather than definitive protocol—their value lies in alerting decision-makers to tradeoffs and uncertainties, not in prescribing specific responses to novel threats. Second, that institutional credibility requires honesty about what is known and unknown; confidence must be earned through transparency, not assumed through authority. Third, that the costs of policy must be continuously monitored and reported, not merely assumed to be justified by whatever benefits accrue.

The essay calls for a "frank and open evaluation." I agree. But frankness requires acknowledging that the evidence is more contested than the essay suggests, while remaining open to conclusions that might cut against our prior commitments. We will not build better institutions by substituting one confident narrative for another.

Macedo and Lee's essay raises questions that needed asking. But I want to push back on its framing in ways that reveal a deeper problem: the essay critiques pandemic policy from the perspective of those who believe they knew better, without adequately examining how we came to believe we knew better—or who gets to claim that status at all.

The essay indicts public health institutions for failing to acknowledge uncertainty. But uncertainty cuts both ways. The pre-pandemic literature questioning NPI effectiveness did not demonstrate that lockdowns would fail; it merely indicated that evidence was limited. The authors treat this distinction as unimportant, but it is central. Policymakers in crisis must act despite uncertainty; they cannot wait for certainty that may never arrive. The question is whether they acted reasonably given available information, not whether their actions can be vindicated by later research.

Consider the Great Barrington Declaration, which the essay presents sympathetically as a heterodox scientific view that was unfairly suppressed. The authors were credentialed experts, yes. But credentialed expertise does not exempt claims from scrutiny, and the focused protection strategy they advocated faced serious implementation challenges. Protecting vulnerable populations while allowing uncontrolled community spread among others assumed that vulnerable people could be isolated—which required compliance, logistics, and enforcement capacities that had no demonstrated feasibility. Nursing homes, despite having months of advance warning, became COVID epicenters. Why would we expect focused protection of community-dwelling elderly to succeed where isolation of institutionalized elderly had failed?

The essay criticizes the "swift and dismissively contemptuous" response to the declaration. But criticism of policy proposals is not suppression. The American Public Health Association's response argued that the declaration's claims were not supported by evidence; this is not an ad hominem attack but an epistemological claim that should be evaluated on its merits. The authors of the declaration were permitted to publish, to speak, to appear in media. They eventually received positions of influence—one became NIH director. The claim that they were "silenced" strains credulity.

The essay's treatment of social media moderation is similarly distorted. Content removal represents a genuine tension for liberal democracy, and the Oversight Board's oversight of Meta's policies is a model for institutional accountability. But the essay presents the removal of demonstrably false claims as straightforward censorship, without adequately engaging with the question of when platforms might legitimately limit speech that causes demonstrable harm. The "lab leak" hypothesis was not suppressed because it was politically inconvenient—it was suppressed because the leading scientific consensus at the time considered it unlikely. This consensus has since shifted, which shows how science works, not how it fails.

The class critique in the essay is compelling but incomplete. The authors argue that "laptop class" elites were insulated from pandemic harms. This is true as far as it goes. But the same class dynamics operated in the critique itself. Who gets to publish retrospective critiques of pandemic policy? Who gets to claim that the costs were overstated or the benefits understated? The essay assumes that its own analysis represents honest accounting, but alternative analyses that might reach different conclusions also claim the mantle of honesty. The essay does not interrogate its own assumptions about who possesses the epistemic authority to evaluate what happened.

What would genuine accountability look like? It would require examining not only policy decisions but the frameworks through which we evaluate those decisions. The pre-pandemic literature questioning NPI effectiveness reflected expert consensus—but expert consensus had also endorsed the models that predicted catastrophic mortality and endorsed the containment strategies that made lockdowns seem necessary. We should be humble about our capacity to know, ex ante, what policies were correct. The essay's confident assertions about NPI ineffectiveness deserve the same skepticism it applies to official claims about NPI effectiveness.

The essay calls for accountability but does not model the epistemic humility that accountability requires. Genuine accountability means acknowledging uncertainty in both directions: uncertainty about harms caused by policies and uncertainty about benefits prevented. It means resisting the temptation to treat contested empirical claims as settled. It means recognizing that the people who made decisions in March 2020 faced genuinely impossible choices under conditions of radical uncertainty—and that those of us writing five years later have advantages they did not possess.

We need serious postmortem. But postmortem requires more than confident assertions about what should have been done. It requires careful attention to how decisions were actually made, what information decision-makers had, and what alternatives were genuinely available. The lead essay sometimes falls short of this standard. We should demand better from all parties to this debate.
