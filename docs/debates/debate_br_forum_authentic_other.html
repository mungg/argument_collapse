Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Perloff's diagnosis of the Yasusada affair is sharp where it concerns the editors and slack where it concerns the poems. She is right that something embarrassing has been exposed: that Bradford Morrow, Arthur Vogelsang, Jon Silkin, and Ron Silliman did not bother to consult a single Japanologist before placing a "Hiroshima survivor" in their pages, and that the appetite for an avant-garde hibakusha who had also conveniently read Spicer and Barthes tells us more about American literary cravings circa 1996 than about Japanese poetry. Her broader point — that the multiculturalist demand for "positionality" has become a backdoor Orientalism, recycling the same gentle, ritualized Japan that Pound's *Cathay* gave us eighty years ago — strikes me as exactly correct, and worth saying loudly.

But I want to push back on the equanimity of her closing pages, where Johnson becomes a kind of useful provocateur, an Ossian for our moment, who has "performed an invaluable service." The McPherson analogy flatters the project. Ossian invented a bardic past; Yasusada invents a particular dead wife and a particular dead daughter incinerated on August 6, 1945. The asymmetry matters. To borrow the iconography of the hibakusha — Chieko in the blast, Akiko dying four years later of radiation sickness — as raw material for a meditation on Foucault, the New Sentence, and the limits of single-author composition is not the same kind of literary mischief as inventing a Gaelic epic. The Wesleyan reader's "queasiness," which Perloff treats as squeamish moralism, seems to me a defensible response, not a failure of literary sophistication.

This matters because Perloff's own analysis of the poems quietly concedes the point. She shows beautifully how "Telescope with Urn" and "Mad Daughter and Big-Bang" *work* on the American reader: they trigger "residual guilt," they let us "empathize with the tragedy" while making "no demand on us," they offer a Hiroshima stripped of the political complications that haunt Shinzaburo Horie in Kristof's *Times* piece. If that is what the poems do, then the persona is not incidental decoration around an interesting prosodic experiment. The persona is the engine. Strip away "Yasusada" and the surreal turnip-pulling in the vegetable patch is a competent late-Spicerian exercise; attach the biographical note, and it becomes unforgettable. Johnson has not exposed our hunger for the authentic so much as fed it while pretending to critique it.

Perloff is also too quick to accept Johnson's planted clues — the anachronistic Celan, the impossible *Empire of Signs*, the Howard McCord giveaway — as evidence of sophisticated intent rather than carelessness retrospectively reframed. Hoaxers always claim, once caught, that the clues were there all along. The fact that editors missed scubadivers in a 1925 haiku is damning for the editors; it does not automatically convert the hoax into Pound's *Propertius*, which never asked anyone to mail royalty checks to a fictional widow.

Where Perloff is unanswerable is on the contemporary Japanese poets — Tanikawa, Shiraishi, Ito — whom *APR* has not been translating. The scandal beneath the scandal is that American journals can find space for an invented Hiroshima ghost but not for the living, secular, ribald, postmodern poets writing in Tokyo right now. That is the Orientalism worth naming, and Perloff names it. One only wishes she pressed Johnson as hard as she presses his marks.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Marjorie Perloff’s rigorous dissection of the Yasusada affair brilliantly uncovers the poetic mechanics and cultural desires that made the hoax so seductive. Yet in her eagerness to expose the “new Orientalism,” Perloff herself reinscribes a troubling divide between authentic Japanese poetry and its Western simulation. Her analysis, for all its acuity, ultimately reveals less about the hoax’s failings than about the persistent critical hunger for a genuine, unmediated Other—a hunger that the Yasusada texts deliberately frustrate, and that Perloff’s own argument echoes.

Perloff argues that the editors and poets who admired Yasusada were taken in by a “perfect recipe for a new Orientalism”: an archaic, elliptical lyricism blended with eroticized imagery and the aura of atomic victimhood. This reading, however, flattens the responses of readers like Ron Silliman, who praised the poems’ “elevation of tone” and their capacity to keep him awake for nights. Silliman’s enthusiasm, as Perloff herself notes, was expressed on a poetics listserv where the formal properties of language writing were constantly debated. To dismiss that response as mere thirst for exotic suffering overlooks the possibility that Silliman and others were engaged by the work’s structural audacity—its dislocation of the sentence, its parodic performance of renga linkage, its brazen mixing of registers. The “scubadivers” and “investors” Perloff cites as clues to the hoax are precisely the kinds of torque that Language poetry prizes. The hoax succeeded not simply because it pandered to Western stereotypes, but because it spoke fluently in the poetic idiom of its moment, an idiom that has long questioned the very notion of authentic voice.

Perloff’s counter-examples of “real” contemporary Japanese poetry—the ribald free verse of Shiraishi Kazuko, the domestic surrealism of Ito Hiromi—are meant to prove that vibrant, non-victimized Japanese writing is ignored by American editors. Yet the selection she offers, drawn largely from Leith Morton’s 1993 anthology, is itself a curated, English-language frame that inevitably shapes what counts as “Japanese.” That Morton’s volume includes six women poets out of sixteen, and emphasizes colloquial, sexually frank work, reveals its own positioning within late-twentieth-century gender and genre politics. There is no unmediated access to Japanese poetry; any act of translation and anthologizing constructs a version of Japan. Perloff’s claim that contemporary Japanese poets “will not allow themselves to be patronized” attributes a heroic resistance to a body of work that, in translation, is just as susceptible to reader projections as any Yasusada poem. The authentic Other remains elusive here, slipping between the lines of her own citations.

This points to a deeper tension in the essay. Perloff invokes Foucault’s author-function to explain the academy’s interest in positionality, yet she repeatedly appeals to an older, auratic standard of authenticity. She cites the “Benjaminian aura” of the real thing, and implies that the Yasusada archive is morally deficient because it does not make the same ethical demands as Toge Sankichi’s direct witness. But the hoax’s very fabric—its impossible influences, chronological absurdities, and slapstick footnotes—refuses the contract of authentic testimony. The fictional Yasusada, with his too-convenient biography and his anachronistic reading of Barthes, is less a hoax than a critical allegory. By making the victim-position so flagrantly manufactured, Johnson (if he is the author) may be indicting the literary marketplace that rewards trauma only when it wears the right exotic costume. The “severed head” of the mad daughter, grotesquely turned into a turnip, reads less as kitsch than as a savage parody of the redemption narrative Western readers expect from hibakusha poetry.

Perloff herself acknowledges that the hoax’s clues were “surely not unintentional,” but she stops short of fully crediting the critical work the Yasusada texts perform. She finds the “New Sentence” genealogy dubious because it appropriates a Japanese form without acknowledging Language poetry’s own cultural specificity. However, the fakery of the renga essay in *Aerial*—with its suspiciously fluent quotation of Earl Miner and its claim that Spicer influenced Yasusada in the 1930s—is so over-the-top that it reads as a reflexive joke on the American avant-garde’s habit of claiming Eastern precedents to bolster its innovations. The editors may have been complicit in the game, not just dupes. Perloff’s insistence that they should have consulted “bona fide Japanese poets” assumes that the work’s value hinges on its ethnographic accuracy, rather than on its intervention in American poetry politics.

The real scandal of the Yasusada affair is not that a white poet pretended to be a Japanese survivor, but that the literary community’s protocols of authentication are so selectively applied. As Perloff reminds us, Rexroth’s *Marichiko* was an admitted fabrication, yet it remains in print and is widely taught. The outcry over Yasusada, including calls of “criminal act,” reveals a new anxiety about cultural appropriation at a time when postcolonial theory has made that anxiety palpable. But the hoax also exposes the limits of that anxiety: it is easier to denounce a fraudulent position than to examine the structures that make the position of the victim so valuable. Perloff’s own conclusion, that we need to “look searchingly and critically at what is always already there,” is precisely the activity the Yasusada “notebooks” provoke. They force us to notice how much we project onto the image of a Hiroshima poet, and how our standards of authenticity are themselves constructed by a tradition that is far from innocent. The hoax, in other words, is not the problem; it is a diagnostic tool. By mistaking the symptom for the disease, Perloff ends up longing for a genuine encounter with the Other that her own logic shows to be impossible.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Marjorie Perloff’s dissection of the Araki Yasusada hoax is a masterful exposure of the American literary avant-garde's theoretical vanities. She correctly identifies that the breathless reception of Yasusada’s poetry by editors and poets—who certainly should have known better—was driven by a hunger for an "authentic other" that conveniently mirrored their own aesthetic and ideological prejudices. By exposing how the American poetry establishment ignored glaring anachronisms in their eagerness to champion a marginalized Hiroshima survivor, Perloff holds up a necessary mirror to the inadvertent Orientalism of contemporary multiculturalism. However, in her admiration for the hoax's diagnostic brilliance, Perloff is too quick to dismiss the profound ethical transgression of appropriating the specific, catastrophic trauma of August 6, 1945.

Perloff is entirely convincing in her demonstration of why poets like Ron Silliman and editors at American Poetry Review fell for the ruse. Yasusada was the perfect, custom-built phantom for the post-structuralist 1990s. As she notes, he satisfied the Foucauldian demand for the dissolution of the authorial ego, mapped seamlessly onto the "New Sentence," and obligingly cited Jack Spicer and Roland Barthes. The editors did not discover a Japanese master; they discovered a reflection of their own graduate seminars. Furthermore, Perloff rightly castigates the publishing world’s indifference to actual, living Japanese poets—like Tanikawa Shuntaro or Ito Hiromi—whose urbane, cosmopolitan verses refuse to flatter the Western desire for Zen-inflected suffering. Our institutions too often seek victims to validate our theoretical paradigms, rather than seeking equals who might challenge them.

Where Perloff’s argument falters, however, is in her breezy exoneration of the hoax’s author. She calls the condemnation of the manuscript as a "criminal act" absurd, likening Yasusada to James Macpherson’s Ossian or Ezra Pound’s Sextus Propertius. But this comparison collapses under the weight of history. Macpherson fabricated a Celtic bard from the mists of a mythic past; Pound translated and ventriloquized an ancient Roman whose empire had long since crumbled. Kent Johnson—or whoever is ultimately pulling the strings—appropriated the very real, flesh-and-blood tragedy of a nuclear holocaust that occurred barely half a century ago. To invent a daughter who dies of radiation sickness, to write poems about her ashes in a crane-decorated urn, is not merely a "time-honored device in literature." It is a colonization of another people's historical trauma for the sake of an avant-garde parlor trick. The horror of Hiroshima is not a mythic playground, and the proximity of the event demands a moral gravity that the hoax format inherently violates.

Perloff points to the deliberate anachronisms in the text—the references to scuba divers, crematoriums, and impossible publication dates—as evidence of the author's brilliant, postmodern intentionality. She views these "clinamens" as proof that the author wanted to problematize representation and leave a trail of breadcrumbs for the astute reader. But viewed through an ethical lens, these clues look less like postmodern brilliance and more like moral cowardice. They serve as an alibi. By inserting these winks to the reader, the author attempts to inoculate himself against the charge of pure exploitation. It allows him to dabble in the gravitas of atomic devastation while retaining a smirk, reserving the right to say, "I was only joking; look at the scuba divers." This irony does not temper the guilt, as Perloff suggests; rather, it trivializes the immense suffering it mimics.

Perloff is right that the Yasusada affair forces us to look critically at the way the American literary market fetishizes trauma and exoticizes the non-Western world. The hoax succeeded because it gave guilt-ridden American intellectuals exactly the forgiving, aesthetically pleasing victim they desired—one who spoke their language of fragmentation and theory. But recognizing the vanity and gullibility of the poetry establishment does not require us to celebrate the cynicism of the hoaxer. It is possible to agree with Perloff that the editors' unquestioning acceptance of Yasusada reveals a deeply flawed, Orientalist multiculturalism, while still maintaining that the impersonation of a Hiroshima survivor crosses a line of basic human decency.

As we approach the millennium, the demand for authentic voices from the margins will surely continue, and the academic obsession with "positionality" shows no signs of waning. But the solution to our uncritical reception of the Other is not to applaud those who expertly forge their signatures in the ashes of the dead. It is, as Perloff herself concludes, to do the harder work of listening to the living, unpalatable and un-exotic as they may sometimes be.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Marjorie Perloff is right to insist that the Yasusada affair is more interesting than a simple scandal of gullible editors and one mischievous poet. The manuscripts were not merely “believed”; they were desired. They arrived bearing precisely the credentials most likely to disarm skeptical reading in the present climate: avant-garde difficulty, documentary apparatus, cross-cultural authority, and the aura of historical suffering. The invented poet was not just Japanese, not just a Hiroshima survivor, not just an undiscovered modernist, not just a precursor of Language poetry, not just a Buddhist-inflected anti-authorial figure. He was all of these at once. That combination should have made him suspect. Instead, it made him irresistible.

But Perloff’s essay, for all its brilliance, is too quick to assimilate the Yasusada materials to a venerable history of literary imposture. Ossian, Propertius, Marichiko, heteronyms, masks, invented translators: literature has always thrived on such devices. Yet there remains a difference between inventing a poetic speaker and forging the testimony of a historical wound. The issue is not that Hiroshima is too sacred for artifice. No event is beyond the reach of art, parody, indirection, or even grotesque comedy. The issue is that the Yasusada apparatus asks readers to grant moral and evidentiary authority to a nonexistent victim. It does not merely say, “Imagine such a person.” It says, “Here are his notebooks; here are his translators; here is his family history; here is the ash of the dead child.” That is not simply pseudonymity. It is counterfeit witness.

Perloff is surely correct that the texts themselves contain many clues: the impossible dates for Hiroshima University, the premature Barthes and Celan, the absurd Spicer chronology, the odd Japanese names, the suspiciously convenient Howard McCord. These are not minor slips. They are flashing signals. Yet to say that the author “wanted his readers to find something perplexing” does not absolve the deception. A forged banknote may contain a private joke in the serial number; it is still designed to circulate as currency. The Yasusada poems circulated in journals not as fiction, not as conceptual art, not as an openly unstable archive, but as translated work by a dead Japanese poet. Their reception depended on that claim. Had the same poems arrived under the name Kent Johnson, many editors might have found them clever, even moving, but they would not have carried the same charge.

This is why the question of quality cannot be separated from the question of attribution. Some defenders of the project seem tempted to say: if the poems moved us before exposure, why should they move us less after? The answer is that poems are not autonomous verbal machines, however much certain avant-garde theories may wish them to be. Context is not an ornament added after the fact; it is part of how meaning is made. “Telescope with Urn” is a different poem if it is read as the elliptical mourning of a Hiroshima father, as opposed to an American poet’s construction of such mourning. “Mad Daughter and Big-Bang” is a different poem if its surreal horror is grounded in remembered loss, rather than in an imaginative appropriation of someone else’s catastrophe. The language has not changed, but the ethical relation has.

At the same time, the outrage of editors who published the work should not be allowed to become self-exculpating theater. Perloff’s most persuasive pages concern the conditions that made the hoax successful. American literary culture has often preferred its Japans usable: Zen Japan, haiku Japan, courtly Japan, victim Japan, erotic Japan, minimalist Japan. Contemporary Japan—urban, consumerist, sexually candid, technologically saturated, formally various, politically uneasy—has been less welcome because it does not flatter American expectations. A living Japanese poet writing about noodles, telephones, bodies, boredom, jokes, and metropolitan estrangement is harder to consume as spiritual otherness than a dead Hiroshima survivor arranging flowers beside an urn.

That neglect is not accidental. It reflects a larger habit in American multicultural reception: we often want the other to arrive already marked by suffering, distance, and moral legibility. The foreign poet is most easily honored when he or she can be made to testify against some recognizable evil: war, dictatorship, colonialism, patriarchy, censorship. Such testimony matters, of course. But when suffering becomes the passport to translation, whole literatures are reduced to wounds. Perloff is right that the real scandal is not only that Yasusada was invented, but that actual contemporary Japanese poets have been so little read in the same venues that embraced him.

Still, I am uneasy with Perloff’s suggestion that Johnson has performed “an invaluable service.” Perhaps he has, in the limited sense that a successful fraud exposes a market’s vanity. But one may expose vanity by exploiting it, and exploitation is not thereby transfigured into critique. If American editors crave authentic others, the cure is not to feed them a synthetic hibakusha. The cure is to translate more carefully, edit more responsibly, consult more widely, and read beyond the categories that confirm our innocence. A hoax can reveal a disease, but it is not medicine.

Nor should we confuse skepticism about authenticity with indifference to truth. The lesson of Foucault’s “author function” is not that authors do not matter, nor that every attribution is merely a game. It is that names organize reading, authority, circulation, and value. Precisely. That is why the name “Araki Yasusada” mattered. It organized the poems as testimony, as translation, as recovered archive, as Hiroshima memory. To invent that name, attach it to fabricated bereavement, and submit it to journals as fact is to manipulate the author function while relying on its power.

The best outcome of the Yasusada affair would not be a retreat into naïve authenticity, as if every poem must be certified by passport, bloodline, and trauma. Nor should it be a cynical celebration of imposture. The better response is a more demanding cosmopolitanism. We should be willing to read across cultures without requiring that foreign writers perform our fantasies of depth, purity, victimhood, or sacred difference. We should also be willing to read invented personae as invented personae. Masks are legitimate. Forged graves are another matter.

Yasusada may endure as a troubling literary artifact: sometimes beautiful, sometimes kitschy, often cunning, and inseparable from the deception that gave it force. But if the affair sends readers back to Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko, Shiraishi Kazuko, Tanikawa Shuntaro, Ito Hiromi, and other Japanese writers not yet sufficiently present in American magazines, then some good may come of it. The authentic other was never hidden in Johnson’s notebooks. The authentic others were already writing. We failed to look.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Perloff's essay is a masterful unmasking of the assumptions underlying the Yasusada hoax, but in her commendable desire to explain why editors and critics were "taken in," she may underestimate the poems themselves. Whatever one's view of Johnson's ethics, the poems published under the Yasusada name represent a genuine achievement—and recognizing this is essential to understanding what the hoax actually accomplished.

Consider "Telescope with Urn," which so impressed Ron Silliman. The poem's fragmentary syntax, its juxtaposition of scientific language ("at such and such velocity") with Zen imagery, its unsettling compression of the cosmic and the intimate—this is not merely a clever pastiche but a genuinely compelling lyric. When Perloff notes that the poem recalls "the ritual and stylization of Noh and Bunraku," she is right, but she understates the case: the poem achieves something difficult and rare, a voice that feels both archaic and urgent, both other and recognizable. The same might be said of the "Mad Daughter and Big-Bang" piece, with its surrealist evocation of Hiroshima's aftermath. Whether or not these poems were written by a fictional Hiroshima survivor, they are poems that demand to be read on their own terms.

This is not to excuse the hoax's deceptions—chronological impossibilities, invented correspondences, the exploitation of readerly desire for "authentic" voices. But there is a distinction worth making: between the ethics of publication and the quality of the work published. Perloff herself gestures toward this distinction when she writes that "Kent Johnson has done a brilliant job in inventing a world at once ritualized and yet startlingly modern." If the invention is brilliant, the resulting poems merit attention regardless of their origins.

The broader context here is the debate over cultural appropriation in contemporary poetry, a debate that was already well underway by 1997. Perloff is right that the Yasusada affair exposed a double standard: editors eager to publish work by members of "oppressed" groups proved careless about verification when that work confirmed their expectations. Yet one might push her analysis further. The readiness to believe in Yasusada was not simply a failure of critical vigilance; it was also a function of the poems' genuine power to move. Readers responded to what they found in the poems themselves—their grief, their strange beauty, their oblique engagement with catastrophe. That this response was mediated by stereotypes about Japanese poetic tradition does not make it less authentic.

What is perhaps most striking about the hoax is how little it ultimately damaged the poems' reception. Once exposed, Yasusada became a scandal, but the work continued to circulate, and critics could engage it more honestly. In this light, Johnson's invention looks less like a deception and more like a provocation—a way of getting readers to encounter certain kinds of poetry under conditions that made genuine response possible. This is, admittedly, a charitable reading, and one that may not survive scrutiny of Johnson's own statements about his project. But it captures something important: the poems remain, and they reward reading.

The harder question is what we owe to actual Japanese poets, whose work Perloff discusses extensively in her essay. Here she is unanswerable: contemporary Japanese poets like Tanikawa Shuntaro and Ito Hiromi are producing work of remarkable quality and diversity, yet they remain virtually unknown in the United States. The Yasusada hoax did nothing to change this situation; if anything, it may have reinforced the assumption that "Japanese poetry" means something other than what living Japanese poets actually write. In the end, this may be the hoax's most troubling legacy—not the deception itself, but the missed opportunity it represents. Instead of asking how we came to believe in a fictional Hiroshima survivor, we might ask why we neglect the real poets who are writing in Japan today, and what we might learn from them.

**The Hoax Was the Point: Defending Kent Johnson**

Marjorie Perloff has written a generous and incisive essay, but she stops short of drawing the conclusion her own evidence demands. She acknowledges that the Yasusada texts are riddled with deliberate anachronisms—scubadivers among the chrysanthemums, a 1967 letter praising a book Barthes had not yet written, a Hiroshima University that did not exist until 1949, the appearance of Paul Celan as a prewar influence on a Japanese coterie. She concedes Kent Johnson "purposely set the stage for suspicion." And yet she lingers over the question of moral propriety, of whether the editors were "taken in," as though that were still the most interesting frame.

It isn't. The Yasusada project is best understood not as a hoax but as a conceptual work, and the indignation it has produced is part of its content. Arthur Vogelsang's denunciation of Johnson's submission as a "criminal act" is itself a found poem—a perfect document of how thoroughly the literary economy now traffics in identity as the precondition of value. The Wesleyan reader who admired the poems but felt "queasy" once authenticity was in doubt enacted the precise contradiction Yasusada was constructed to expose. If the poems were good when believed authored by a hibakusha and embarrassing when believed authored by an Illinois community-college instructor, then what was being evaluated all along was not the poems.

This is, I think, what makes Yasusada more interesting than the standard pseudonym cases Perloff lists. Macpherson's Ossian and Rexroth's Marichiko traded on exoticism, but they did not embed contradictions that any attentive reader could catch. Yasusada is closer to Borges's Pierre Menard or to Pessoa's heteronyms: a fiction designed to make its readers conscious of the apparatus of attribution. The scubadivers are not a slip. They are a test. The Howard McCord joke—planting Johnson's own college mentor as the proprietor of City Lights and then "correcting" the error in a footnote—is the wink of a writer who would prefer not to be believed for long.

Perloff's deeper argument is that the editors who published Yasusada did so under the influence of a Foucauldian common sense in which the cultural position of the speaker outweighs the formal qualities of the speech. There is something to this. The peculiar selectivity of *American Poetry Review*—publishing testimonial Hiroshima poems and medieval court ladies but not Tanikawa Shuntaro or Shiraishi Kazuko—does suggest that contemporary Japan is somehow illegible to American editors precisely because it is not "other" enough. The Japan that sells is the Japan of dignified suffering and Zen restraint, a Japan that lets American readers feel both guilty and ennobled at once.

But I would put the indictment less on the editors and more on the structure of the market they operate within. Editors do not have time to vet every submission against a network of Japanese scholars, and they should not be required to. What the Yasusada affair reveals is that authenticity, once converted into a currency, becomes counterfeitable. The more publications stake their prestige on having discovered the "real" voice of a marginalized witness, the more vulnerable they become to a writer clever enough to manufacture the markers of that voice. The hoax is not an aberration in such an economy; it is the economy's logical product. If editors did not want to be hoaxed, they would not have constructed a system in which the editorial reward function so heavily privileges biography.

I would push back, however, on Perloff's claim that the Yasusada poems make "no demand" on the American reader. The demand they make is just not the one she expects. Read as the work of an actual Hiroshima survivor, "Mad Daughter and Big-Bang" is straightforwardly affecting—too straightforwardly, perhaps, in the way Perloff describes. Read as what it actually is, the poem becomes vertiginous. Who is permitted to imagine a severed daughter's head? Who is permitted to write, in the first person, about the death of a child in the atomic blast? If only hibakusha may treat this material, then American literary culture has effectively declared certain subjects off-limits to imagination, a position that would have astonished every poet from Homer to Celan himself. (Celan, we should remember, wrote "Todesfuge" partly out of testimony he gathered, not only from what he had directly witnessed.)

The deeper question Yasusada poses is whether sympathy and indignation can survive the discovery that their object was constructed. Most of the editors, on the evidence, decided they could not. They wanted their payments back. This is the response of consumers who feel defrauded, not of readers who feel implicated. A more interesting response would be to ask: why did these poems work on me when I thought they were one thing, and why do they fail for me now that I know they are another? That question has nothing to do with criminality and everything to do with the strange contract between reader and signature.

Perloff ends by hoping that Yasusada will send readers back to "the actual Hiroshima memoirs" and to "Japanese postwar poetry in its specific articulations." I hope so too. But I also hope Yasusada will be remembered for what it is on its own terms: a self-deconstructing artifact that, by exposing the machinery of authentication, may have done more to interrogate American Orientalism than a hundred earnest anthologies of dignified hibakusha tanka could ever do. The scandal is not that Kent Johnson wrote these poems. The scandal is what his writing them has revealed about how poems are read.

**Stop Calling It Brilliant**

Marjorie Perloff's essay strains throughout to maintain a tone of high-toned analytic disinterest, but underneath the Foucault and the Pound parallels and the dutiful citations of Tanikawa Shuntaro, what it amounts to is an extended apology for a particularly nasty piece of literary tourism. I do not think Kent Johnson has "performed an invaluable service." I think he has done something shabby, and the fact that he has done it cleverly does not redeem it.

Let us be clear about what Johnson actually wrote. He wrote, in the first person, a poem in which the speaker stumbles upon the severed head of his own daughter, killed by the bomb dropped on Hiroshima. He wrote letters from a hospital ward of a man dying of radiation-related cancer. He wrote the man's grief for his wife and infant daughter, both incinerated on August 6, 1945. And he affixed to these inventions a biography of an actual category of human being—the *hibakusha*—and submitted them to journals as the discovered manuscripts of such a man.

Perloff treats this as a clever bit of poststructuralist play, a "perfect simulacrum," an "invaluable" provocation against essentialism. I find the description grotesque. There is a difference between Macpherson inventing an ancient Gaelic bard and an American poet inventing a recently dead survivor of a still-living atrocity. There is a difference between Pound's *Homage to Sextus Propertius*—which Perloff repeatedly invokes as a parallel—and Johnson's Yasusada. Pound did not pretend to be Propertius. He signed his own name. He layered his frigidaires into Latin elegy openly, as Ezra Pound, and dared the reader to follow. That is irony. Johnson's procedure is not irony. It is impersonation.

Perloff's defense, when she offers one, is that Johnson seeded his text with clues—the scubadivers, the impossible chronology of Barthes, the misnamed Hiroshima University—so that any sufficiently attentive reader would catch on. This is the standard alibi of every clever hoaxer: I left signals; you should have noticed. But the signals were not noticed. They were not noticed by *American Poetry Review*, by *Conjunctions*, by *Grand Street*, by *Stand*, by the anonymous Wesleyan reader, by Ron Silliman staying up nights with the poems. If the clues were so unmistakable that the work was always already declaring itself as fiction, then the editors would have read it as fiction. They didn't. They read it as testimony. Which means either Johnson's clues were too faint to function as he claims they were intended, or Johnson was happy to have them go unnoticed for as long as the credit kept accruing. Either way the alibi fails.

The deeper offense, though, is not against the editors. It is against the witnesses. There exists a real literature of Hiroshima—Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko, the tanka poets in Nakano's anthology whose lines Perloff somewhat condescendingly dismisses as "more notable for their subject matter than for their poetic quality." These writers paid for the right to say what they say. They paid in radiation sickness, in the loss of children, in decades of survivor's burden. To produce a more "sophisticated" Hiroshima poetry—more elliptical, more Spicer-inflected, more pleasingly fragmented for the contemporary American avant-garde—and to do so by puppeting a fictional survivor is to treat their actual suffering as raw material for a literary career move. It does not matter how skilled the puppetry is. Skill is not the question.

Perloff almost glimpses this when she observes that Yasusada's poems make "no demand" on the American reader, that they offer a manageable, aestheticized Hiroshima that lets us "concentrate on the effectiveness of Johnson's fiction" rather than wrestle with responsibility. But having registered the point, she fails to follow it. The reason Yasusada makes no demand is precisely that he isn't real. Real survivors make demands. Their language is sometimes flat, sometimes polemical, sometimes embarrassing in its directness ("Help me help me / Water / Water / Somewhere / Someone"). It refuses the elegance the literary marketplace would prefer. Johnson's Yasusada gives the marketplace the elegance it wants—Spicer with a death count attached. That is not a critique of Orientalism. That is Orientalism's most refined product.

I will grant Perloff this: the affair does expose something about the American poetry economy that deserves exposure. The journals' uneven appetite for contemporary Japan—voracious for hibakusha testimony, indifferent to Shiraishi or Tanikawa—is genuinely damning, and her catalogue of *APR*'s translation choices is the most useful part of her essay. The editors are not blameless. They have constructed an economy of curated otherness in which Yasusada was a predictable arrival.

But the diagnosis that they are complicit does not absolve the perpetrator. A confidence man who exploits a community's weakness for sentimental narratives is not engaged in social criticism, even if his fraud incidentally reveals the weakness. He is engaged in fraud. The lesson is not that we should celebrate his ingenuity. The lesson is that he ought not to have done it, and that the editors and readers who fell for it ought to read more carefully, and that we should now spend our attention on the actual living Japanese poets whose absence from our magazines made Johnson's fabrication thinkable in the first place.

Perloff would agree with that last sentence, I think. I wish her essay had gotten there sooner, and without quite so much admiration for the man who made the detour necessary.

**A Japanese Reader's Note**

I write as someone who has spent his working life inside contemporary Japanese poetry, and I want to offer a perspective somewhat different from the framing Marjorie Perloff gives the Yasusada case. Perloff's essay is admirable in many respects—she has read more contemporary Japanese poets than nearly any of the American editors involved in this affair, and her insistence that Tanikawa Shuntaro, Shiraishi Kazuko, and Ito Hiromi exist and ought to be translated is one I share. But I want to register what the Yasusada hoax looks like from the side of the culture being impersonated, because that perspective is largely missing from the American debate.

First, the elementary errors. Perloff has catalogued some of them—"Araki" used as a given name, "Nomura" used as a first name, "Motokiyo" misspelled as "Motokiyu," "Ogyu" as "Ojiu," a Hiroshima University that did not yet exist, a Paul Celan who was a child in the years he was supposedly being read by the *Soun* group. To this list I would add other things that would strike any Japanese reader as wrong immediately. The casual deployment of "fucking" as intensifier in a putative 1930s renga is not merely anachronistic in English translation; there is no register in Japanese of that era that corresponds to it. The notion that prewar avant-garde renga groups would have engaged in the kind of programmatic theorizing Yasusada's "translators" attribute to them—the talk of "flat linking," "mosaic linking," "linking through paragram"—reflects a contemporary American Language-poetry sensibility imposed retroactively onto a tradition that did not work that way.

Above all, the Japanese poetic field of the 1920s and 30s did not look like Yasusada's. It was dominated by debates between modernism and proletarian poetry, between Anzai Fuyue and Kitagawa Fuyuhiko and the *Shi to shiron* circle on one side and the Marxists on the other, with figures like Hagiwara Sakutaro and Miyazawa Kenji occupying their distinctive ground. A serious avant-garde Hiroshima poet of the period would have a relationship to those debates. Yasusada has no relationship to them. He floats free of his ostensible context, available only to influences—Spicer, Barthes, Celan—that locate him precisely where the contemporary American reader would like him to be.

This is the deepest problem, and it is the one Perloff sees most clearly. Johnson did not invent a Japanese poet. He invented an American fantasy of a Japanese poet, and then attached to that fantasy enough biographical pathos to deter close scrutiny. John Solt, quoted in *Lingua Franca*, was not wrong to call this "Japanized crap." His diagnosis was perhaps too brisk, but it pointed at something real: the construction begins not from a knowledge of Japan but from a set of American expectations about what Japan should sound like.

I want, however, to complicate one strand of Perloff's argument. She suggests that contemporary Japanese poets have been reluctant to write about Hiroshima and Nagasaki because the subject forces an unbearable confrontation with the question of Japanese subjectivity—are we descendants of the bombed or of the bombers? My friend Nagahata Akitoshi, whom Perloff quotes, puts this elegantly. But the situation is more layered than even his careful formulation suggests. There has in fact been a continuous, if often quieter, engagement with the *genbaku* (atomic bomb) experience in Japanese poetry across the postwar period, including in work by poets too young to have witnessed it. What has changed is the rhetorical mode. Direct testimony, in the manner of Toge Sankichi, gave way to oblique, often allegorical treatments in figures like Ishigaki Rin or Ibaragi Noriko. The American demand for hibakusha testimony in its rawest form has been met increasingly by translations of an older generation, while the younger work has been less exportable because it is less recognizably "about" the bomb in the way American editors wish.

This is the cultural transaction Yasusada exploits. He provides what the older testimonial tradition provides—the survivor's voice—while also providing what the younger experimental tradition provides—the ellipsis, the surreal image, the elegant restraint. It is a chimera composed of features the American market wants but cannot easily find combined in a single real Japanese poet, because no real Japanese poet has the biography Johnson constructed for Yasusada. That is the diagnostic value of the fabrication, and Perloff is right to insist on it.

Where I differ from her is in the moral weighing. Perloff is too quick, I think, to dismiss the editorial reaction as panicked overreaction. Calling the submission a "criminal act" may be overheated, but the queasiness the Wesleyan reader felt was not foolish. It registered something important: that to impersonate, in the first person, a victim of mass atrocity is not the same kind of literary gesture as to write under a pseudonym. The pseudonym hides the author's identity. The impersonation appropriates someone else's. The line between them is not absolute, but it is real, and it is worth defending.

That said, I do not want this affair to become a referendum on Kent Johnson alone. The more serious project—the one Perloff begins and that I hope others will continue—is to ask why American poetry journals find a fictional Hiroshima survivor easier to publish than a living, working Tokyo or Osaka poet. That is the question with which Japanese readers of this debate will mainly be left. The answer will not be flattering to American editorial culture, and it will not be remedied by withdrawing Yasusada's check.

**The Authenticity Trap**

What strikes me most forcefully in Marjorie Perloff's account of the Yasusada affair is not the cleverness of Kent Johnson nor the gullibility of the editors but the strangeness of the standard against which both are being measured. The complaint against Johnson is, fundamentally, that he produced moving poems while occupying the wrong position—that the poems' value depended on a biographical fact that turned out not to obtain, and that without the fact the poems lose whatever they had. The complaint against the editors is that they failed to verify the fact before publishing. Both complaints rest on the same premise: that the position of the author is constitutive of the work's worth.

This premise is now so naturalized in literary culture that even its critics tend to argue within it rather than against it. Perloff comes closer than most to questioning it. She notes the irony that a literary culture officially committed to the Foucauldian critique of the author has produced an unprecedented hunger for biographical authentication, and she suggests, rightly, that the two phenomena are connected: that the dismissal of "excellence" as essentialist creates a vacuum into which positionality rushes as the new criterion of merit. But she does not, I think, quite take the measure of how peculiar the resulting situation is.

Consider what the editors of *American Poetry Review* are effectively saying when they demand the return of Johnson's payment. They are saying: the poems we published, which we found good enough to publish under the impression they were written by a hibakusha, are not in fact good enough to publish under the impression they were written by an Illinois professor. The poems have not changed. Their language, imagery, line breaks, ironies, surreal turns—all of these are identical to what they were when the journal accepted them. What has changed is a fact about the author. And that fact, apparently, is determinative of whether the work merits publication.

If this is the criterion, then the work was never being evaluated as poetry. It was being evaluated as testimony, with poetic features as a kind of aesthetic surcharge. That is an honorable thing to publish, but it is not what the editors of *APR* or *Conjunctions* or *Grand Street* present themselves as publishing. They present themselves as publishing literature. The Yasusada affair has shown, perhaps inadvertently, that for at least some kinds of submission they are not in fact doing so. They are publishing biographical credentials with poems attached.

I do not raise this to scold the editors. The pressure on them is intense, and the system rewards the curation of identity in part because no one has solved the prior problem of how a non-curatorial editorial practice should work in a literary culture that has rejected the older universalisms. But I do think the discomfort the affair has generated is, at root, a discomfort with seeing the criterion of authenticity laid bare.

There is another aspect of the case I want to dwell on. Perloff argues that Johnson's invented Japan is an Orientalist construction—a Japan of ritual, restraint, sexual frankness in a Buddhist key, melancholy elegance, the entire Barthes-via-Pound apparatus—and that this is what made it sellable to American editors. I think this is broadly correct, but I want to add: the Orientalism in question is not principally Johnson's. It is the editors'. Johnson produced what would sell. Pierre Menard would have produced something else if a different market obtained. The fact that the Yasusada package satisfied the particular Orientalist appetites of the periodicals he submitted to is not primarily an indictment of him but of them.

This is awkward, because it means the moral asymmetry the literary world has tried to construct—Johnson as transgressor, editors as victims—runs in the opposite direction. Johnson is at worst a clever opportunist. The editors are the ones who have spent years constructing the appetite that made him an opportunity. The frequent translation of Bengali women, Polish dissidents, Nicaraguan revolutionaries, but not contemporary Tokyo—the equation of "Japanese" with the medieval and the Zen—the choice to publish hibakusha tanka but not Tanikawa's wry, witty, urbane lyrics—these editorial patterns are what Yasusada answered. They are the original condition of which he is the symptom.

What would a healthier literary culture do? It would, I think, evaluate poems substantially without reference to whether their authors had the experiences ostensibly described in them. This is not the same as saying biography is irrelevant. Biography contextualizes; biography modulates how a work is read. But the work's value would not collapse on the discovery that the speaker is not the author. Such a culture would recognize that the lyric "I" has always been a fiction, even when the fiction happens to coincide with biographical truth. It would publish Yasusada under Johnson's name if the poems are good, and reject them if they are not, and the question of who suffered what in 1945 would be a separate question—an urgent one, but a separate one.

We are not in that culture, and there are reasons for that, some of them honorable—the desire to amplify voices that have historically been silenced is a real and important desire. But the Yasusada affair shows the cost of letting that desire become the dominant editorial principle. The cost is that we become unable to read. Perloff's essay is at its best when it gestures toward this difficulty. I wish only that it pressed harder on the implication: that the way out of the Yasusada trap is not better authentication procedures but a renewed willingness to read poems as poems, knowing they are signed by people whose lives may or may not match the lives the poems imagine.

**Pound, Propertius, and the Limits of an Analogy**

Marjorie Perloff returns several times in her essay to Ezra Pound, and the analogy bears scrutiny. She compares Yasusada's anachronisms—scubadivers, crematoria, capitalist investors among the chrysanthemums—to Pound's frigidaire in *Homage to Sextus Propertius*. She compares the layered linguistic registers of the Yasusada renga to Pound's *Electra*, where Greek tragedy meets the American vernacular. She concludes that Johnson, like Pound, is performing a kind of deliberate temporal overlay, "problematizing representation and tone." The analogy is suggestive, but I think it ultimately obscures more than it illuminates, and the differences between Pound and Johnson are worth spelling out.

Pound, in *Homage to Sextus Propertius*, signed his own name. He titled the work "Homage," not "Translations of." When the classicists attacked him for the frigidaire and for what they called his mistranslations of the Latin, Pound responded with the famous letter to the editor stating that he was not translating Propertius but performing an act of dialogue with him across two thousand years. The work's effect depended on the reader's awareness that two voices were present: the Latin elegist and the modernist American, layered into a palimpsest. Take away the awareness of Pound, and the *Homage* loses its constitutive irony. The frigidaire is funny because Pound has put it there.

Yasusada works differently. The Yasusada presentation does not invite the reader to perceive two voices. It invites the reader to perceive one voice—an obscure Hiroshima poet, recently deceased, whose notebooks have at last reached the West. The anachronisms function, in Perloff's reading, as clues that this single voice is in fact a fiction, but the clues are designed to be discovered only by readers careful enough to verify them, and—as the publication history makes plain—most readers, including very sophisticated readers, do not discover them. The work's effect on its actual audience is dependent on the audience not noticing what Perloff says they were "meant" to notice. This is a very different aesthetic situation from Pound's.

Perloff acknowledges the difference but folds it into the structural argument: "Pound was already famous when he wrote his Latin 'translation' and so he could afford to be Ezra Pound, whereas the unknown Kent Johnson, writing in what is an increasingly glutted and cutthroat poetry market, had no such alternative." This is curiously sympathetic to Johnson—as though impersonating a Hiroshima survivor were a regrettable but understandable career adaptation by an unknown poet competing for limited journal space. I want to push back. Many unknown poets in the current market do not impersonate Hiroshima survivors. Most of them, in fact, simply submit work under their own names and accept that this is harder. The Macpherson route is not the only route available to the obscure. It is a particular route, with particular ethical baggage.

The deeper trouble with the Pound analogy is that it imports Pound's authority into a work that has not earned it. Pound's *Homage* and his *Cathay* are situated within a body of openly signed work in which the poet's procedures are visible and accountable. We know how Pound used Fenollosa; we can compare *Cathay* against the Fenollosa notebooks Hugh Kenner reproduces; we can argue with Pound's choices and locate them within his larger project. Yasusada is not situated. The "translators" are themselves pseudonyms. The supposed source notebooks do not exist. We have no body of work against which to triangulate Johnson's choices, because we do not even know with certainty that Johnson is the author—Perloff has to use the qualifier "or whoever the 'real' Yasusada turns out to be." The reader has nothing to grasp except the surface texts and a swirl of attribution claims, each one less verifiable than the last.

I do not say this to insist that work must be signed by traceable persons to count as literature. Pessoa's heteronyms are signed by Pessoa; "Pierre Menard" is signed by Borges. The question is what kind of literary contract the work asks the reader to enter into. Pound, Pessoa, Borges—each of these writers asks the reader to perceive the apparatus of attribution as part of the work. Yasusada asks the reader, at least at first, not to perceive it. That asymmetry is what makes the case ethically distinctive, and the Pound analogy elides it.

There is one further consideration. *Homage to Sextus Propertius* makes free with a Roman elegist twenty centuries gone. The temporal distance affords an enormous license. No living person reads Pound's frigidaire and feels that their suffering has been appropriated, because Propertius's suffering, whatever it was, has been absorbed into a literary tradition for which Pound was a legitimate inheritor. The Hiroshima dead are not so distant. The survivors are still alive. The political and historical questions surrounding nuclear warfare are not yet settled. To overlay anachronistic registers onto a Hiroshima narrative is not analogous to overlaying them onto Propertius, because the historical material is not analogous. To pretend otherwise—to invoke Pound's example as legitimating Yasusada—is to flatten an important difference.

Perloff is by no means a naive partisan of Johnson, and her essay raises questions about the American poetry establishment that need to be raised. But I think the Pound analogy works in Johnson's favor more than it ought to, conferring a modernist legitimacy on what is really a postmodernist provocation of a quite different and quite more troubling kind. We can defend Pound's frigidaire without thereby defending Yasusada's scubadivers. The two cases are not the same, and we lose something important when we treat them as if they were.

**What This Hoax Tells Us About Language Poetry**

There is an undercurrent in Marjorie Perloff's essay that deserves to be brought to the surface. Among those who responded most enthusiastically to the Yasusada poems were Ron Silliman, Bradford Morrow, Rod Smith, and the editors of *Aerial*—figures associated, broadly, with the Language poetry movement and its allied currents. Yasusada's "translators" published a tape-essay in *Aerial* on "Renga and the New Sentence" in which they explicitly aligned the supposed Hiroshima poet with Silliman's theoretical apparatus, claiming that Japanese haikai renga had anticipated by four centuries the procedures Silliman codified in 1980. Silliman, in turn, told the Buffalo Poetics List that the Yasusada poems took his breath away. The mutual flattery here is conspicuous, and it points at something the Yasusada episode reveals about Language poetry that the movement's defenders would prefer not to confront.

Language poetry has, from its earliest manifestos, insisted on the constructedness of the lyric subject, the political importance of disrupting the bourgeois "I," the necessity of breaking the syllogistic, expressive habits of conventional verse. The new sentence, in Silliman's account, is non-syllogistic; it resists the integrated subjectivity of the speaker; it foregrounds the materiality of the writing itself. Authorship, in the movement's theoretical statements, is supposed to be displaced, decentered, made visible as a construction rather than naturalized as a voice.

If any movement should have been resistant to the Yasusada package, it ought to have been this one. The Yasusada poems are presented as discovered notebooks—the most romantic of authorial fictions, evoking nothing so much as the Victorian sensation novel's discovered manuscript or the Romantic lyric's solitary effusion. They come surrounded by the apparatus of authenticating biography: the dying wife, the radiation-stricken daughter, the bereaved father's English lessons, the correspondence with poetic confidants. Every feature of the presentation foregrounds precisely the integrated, suffering, expressive subject that Language theory was supposed to have dismantled. And yet the Language milieu was where the poems found their warmest welcome.

This is not an incidental embarrassment. It tells us something structural about how the movement actually operates, as distinct from how it represents itself. The theoretical commitment to the death of the author has always coexisted, in practice, with an extraordinarily strong attention to who the author is, where they come from, what their cultural position is, who they correspond with on the listserv. The "new sentence" is, in Silliman's hands, a non-syllogistic device—but the question of whose new sentence, written from what biographical location, has never been bracketed in Language-poetry reception. The community of readers attends to provenance even as the theory denies provenance's relevance.

Yasusada exploited this gap with surgical precision. The poems' surface—elliptical, fragmentary, dissonant, full of jarring registers—reads as a sophisticated cousin of Language poetry. The biography—Hiroshima survivor, lifelong devotee of avant-garde renga, late convert to Spicer and Barthes—gives the work the cultural position the movement values: experimental but not metropolitan, victimized but not sentimental, theoretically literate but exotically situated. The tape-essay aligning him with the new sentence completed the package by inviting Silliman and his colleagues to recognize themselves in the work. The flattery was the lure.

I do not believe Silliman was naïve in any simple sense. He is one of the most careful readers in contemporary American poetry, and his enthusiasm for the Yasusada poems was an enthusiasm for real poetic qualities he correctly identified. But the speed with which the entire Language network embraced the work—without anyone apparently checking the basic facts, without anyone consulting a Japanese scholar, without anyone noticing that Hiroshima University did not exist in 1925 or that *Empire of Signs* postdates Yasusada's supposed reading of it by years—suggests that the movement's official skepticism toward authorship is less robust than its members claim.

Perloff puts the broader point well: contemporary poetry culture pays "lip service" to the Foucauldian critique of the author while in practice exhibiting an unprecedented hunger for the Benjaminian aura. What I would add is that this is not a contradiction the avant-garde has escaped. It is a contradiction the avant-garde particularly enacts. The Language poets have built a movement on the deconstruction of the lyric subject and a community on the constant exchange of correspondence about who is doing what, who has been published where, who is teaching at which institution. The Yasusada hoax succeeded in part because it offered a perfect synthesis of these two appetites: a deconstructed subject *and* a biographically intriguing community member, all in one package.

There is a salutary lesson here. Theoretical positions are not the same as reading habits, and a movement can profess one while practicing the other for decades without noticing the discrepancy. The Yasusada affair, in addition to whatever else it accomplishes, has held up a mirror to the Language community and shown it acting in ways its theory should have prevented. I do not think this discredits the theoretical work, which has produced some of the most interesting American poetry of the past quarter century. But it does suggest that the work of dismantling the author is harder than the manifestos pretend, and that the deconstruction has to begin at home—with our own reading practices, our own susceptibilities to provenance, our own willingness to be flattered by an apparently kindred voice from abroad.

If Yasusada has done nothing else, he has demonstrated that the new sentence is, after all, perfectly compatible with old-fashioned authorial mystique. That is an uncomfortable lesson, and it is one I doubt the figures involved will publicly absorb. But it is, I think, the most consequential thing the affair has shown us about the state of American poetry in 1997.

**The Editor's View**

I have edited a small literary magazine for some years, and I want to respond to Marjorie Perloff's essay from the perspective of those of us who sit on the receiving end of submissions like the Yasusada manuscript. Perloff's analysis of the literary-cultural pressures that made the hoax legible is acute, but I think she underestimates the practical situation editors actually face, and her account leans too heavily on the implication that we ought to have caught what we did not catch.

Consider what an editor does. The submission arrives. It is presented as the work of a previously unknown Japanese poet, deceased, whose notebooks have only recently been opened. The translators have plausible names. The work is striking. There may be a few oddities—a reference here, a chronology there—but no one piece of evidence shouts "fraud," and the cumulative weight of oddities is something one only sees in retrospect, after the case has broken. The submission arrives alongside dozens of others that month; one reads it; one is moved; one accepts it.

What would the diligence Perloff implies have looked like? Should I have written to a Japanese scholar to verify the founding date of Hiroshima University? Should I have checked whether Yasusada's reading of *Empire of Signs* in 1967 was chronologically possible against the publication date of the French original? Should I have consulted with a specialist in postwar Japanese poetry to ask whether the *Soun* group existed and whether Paul Celan was discussed in its meetings? In principle, yes. In practice, magazines do not have the resources, and the implicit norm in literary publishing has always been to trust the translator's note unless something obvious is wrong.

This is not a defense of the editors who published Yasusada—of whom I am not one—so much as a description of the conditions under which they were operating. A serious vetting infrastructure does not exist for poetry translations. There is no analogue to peer review. There is no analogue to the documentary verification that scholarly publishing demands. There is the editor, the manuscript, and the editor's literary judgment, supplemented occasionally by what one can glean from a translator's note. In that environment, an elaborate fabrication accompanied by a coherent biographical apparatus and persuasive translator's framing is going to succeed against most editors, most of the time, because the system is not built to detect it.

Perloff says that the editors "have put the blame on everyone but themselves." This is unfair to several of those involved. Rod Smith of *Aerial*, by Perloff's own account, told her in conversation that he suspected the manuscript was a hoax all along and published it because he found it charming. *American Poetry Review*, by Perloff's own account, published a recension and apology in their September/October 1996 issue. Editors have done what editors can do once a fabrication is revealed: acknowledge it, correct the record, refuse further publication. To demand more than that is to misunderstand what the editorial role is.

What the editors are accused of, when one strips away the rhetoric, is having been moved by poems that turned out not to be what they appeared to be. The implicit charge is that they ought to have suspected fraud at the time of acceptance. But suspicion is corrosive, and an editorial culture that treats every interesting submission as presumptively fraudulent until verified would produce nothing. We rely on a certain baseline of good faith from submitters because the alternative is paralysis. When that good faith is violated, the violation is on the violator, not on the editor whose default assumption was trust.

That said, I do think the Yasusada affair should change editorial practice in one specific way. Perloff is right that the periodicals involved have shown a curious selectivity in their treatment of Japanese poetry—a willingness to publish hibakusha testimony and medieval Zen, an unwillingness to publish contemporary Tokyo. This is an editorial pattern we can correct. We can commission translators to bring us Tanikawa Shuntaro and Shiraishi Kazuko. We can ask why our pages have not featured them. We can examine the implicit cultural map our publication choices have been drawing and consider whether it reflects the actual literary terrain of contemporary Japan or only the parts of that terrain that satisfy our preconceptions.

This is the editorial reform the Yasusada affair calls for, and it is the one Perloff's essay most usefully points toward. It is not a reform of vetting procedures or of fraud detection; those are second-order issues. It is a reform of editorial appetite. If our magazines published more of the actual range of contemporary Japanese poetry, the cultural slot Yasusada filled would not exist, and the temptation to fill it with a fabrication would diminish.

I will close with a small observation. Perloff suggests that the Yasusada package satisfied the editors' "current disciplinary demand" for an avant-gardist who is also a victim, a Hiroshima survivor who is also a renga master, etcetera. I think this is correct as far as it goes, but I would add that the same package satisfies a very ordinary human appetite—the appetite for the moving and the strange. It is not only theory-laden academics who want to read a poem about a man finding his dead daughter's head in a vegetable patch and feel something. The desire to be moved, and to be moved by what feels like real testimony, is older than Foucault and will outlast him. Editors are not exempt from it. We are, on the contrary, particularly susceptible to it, because our work consists in finding things that move us and passing them on. The Yasusada affair is, in part, a reminder that this susceptibility, which is our professional virtue, is also the vulnerability through which we can be deceived.

**A Modest Defense of the Editors**

Marjorie Perloff's essay reaches a verdict on the editors who published Yasusada—not quite a guilty verdict, but something close. They are described as having operated under "the Foucauldian notion of cultural construction"; as no longer trusting "the individual talent to rise above mass culture"; as practitioners of a "New Multiculturalism" that, despite itself, has become "Orientalism's inadvertent promoter." Their failure to consult "bona fide Japanese poets, scholars, or translators" is treated as evidence of a deeper intellectual complacency. I want to enter a modest defense.

The premise of Perloff's argument is that the Yasusada poems' anachronisms—scubadivers in a 1925 haiku, *Empire of Signs* in a 1967 letter, Paul Celan as an interwar Japanese influence—were obvious enough that careful readers should have caught them. She marshals these clues in numbered lists and treats them as glaring. But this is hindsight criticism. Once one knows a manuscript is a hoax, the clues organize themselves into a pattern. Before one knows, they are scattered oddities in a thicket of other oddities, no more salient than the many genuinely strange features of any unfamiliar foreign literature.

Consider: is "scubadiver" obviously anachronistic in a translated 1925 haiku? Only if one happens to know the history of scuba technology. The word in Japanese for an underwater diver predates scuba; one could imagine a translator using "scubadiver" loosely. Is the 1967 letter's reference to *Empire of Signs* obviously impossible?
