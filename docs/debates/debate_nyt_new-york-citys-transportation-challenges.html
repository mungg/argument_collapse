New York City’s Transportation Challenges

The next mayor of New York City faces some tough challenges, including in setting transportation priorities. Should he or she expand bikeshare programs, or worry more about making the streets safe for pedestrians? And what can be done to improve commute times for New York City residents beyond Manhattan and for the bridge-and-tunnel crowd?

This is the second in a series of discussions on what awaits the next chief executive of New York.

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The framing of this debate—bikeshare versus pedestrian safety—presents a false choice. Citi Bike, launched just months ago, has already logged millions of trips with no fatalities, and the protected bike lanes that accompany its expansion are themselves pedestrian safety infrastructure. Wider medians, shorter crossing distances, and slower turning speeds protect everyone on foot. The next mayor should treat cycling investment and Vision Zero-style street redesign as a single agenda, not competing priorities.

The harder problem is the outer-borough commute. A worker in eastern Queens or southeast Brooklyn can spend ninety minutes each way on a bus-to-subway slog, while a commuter from Westchester rides express rail into Grand Central in forty. Three concrete moves deserve priority. First, accelerate Select Bus Service with genuine physical bus lanes, signal priority, and camera enforcement—Fordham Road and First/Second Avenue have shown this works cheaply and quickly. Second, push the MTA and the next governor to allow CityTicket-style fares on Metro-North and LIRR stops within city limits, unlocking underused commuter rail capacity for places like Co-op City, Rosedale, and eastern Queens. Third, finish the Second Avenue Subway's first phase on schedule and plan Phase 2 seriously.

For the bridge-and-tunnel crowd, the politically painful but honest answer is congestion pricing, in some form resembling the Ravitch or Sam Schwartz plans. It would shorten car commutes, fund transit for those without alternatives, and rationalize a tolling system that currently punishes free-bridge users in the outer boroughs. The next mayor should spend political capital making that case in Albany.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The next mayor must recognize that bikeshare expansion and pedestrian safety are not opposing goals but complementary halves of a safer, more efficient street network. The Citi Bike launch in May 2013 demonstrated enormous latent demand, with over 40,000 annual members in just months and few serious crashes. That success shouldn’t obscure the pedestrian safety crisis: in 2012, 148 pedestrians were killed in traffic, a number largely unchanged for years. Expanding protected bike lanes and calming traffic with curb extensions and slower speed limits directly protects walkers while making cycling feel safer, especially for the less confident riders a larger bikeshare system would attract.

Improving commutes for residents beyond Manhattan and the “bridge-and-tunnel crowd” demands investments that shorten the door-to-door trip, not just subway ride times. The mayor should aggressively scale up Select Bus Service across outer-borough corridors, where dedicated lanes and pre-board payment have cut travel times by up to 20 percent on routes like Fordham Road. More ambitiously, the city should partner with the MTA to restore and expand commuter rail capacity on the Long Island Rail Road and Metro-North, and implement CityTicket pricing all day, not just weekends, to make those trains a viable alternative for neighborhoods far from the subway. Ferries are not a panacea, but a thoughtfully planned East River route expansion, with full MetroCard integration, could relieve pressure on chronically crowded lines like the 7 train. These steps would signal that the new administration takes seriously a city where more than half of workers commute from outside Manhattan.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

The debate over whether the next mayor should prioritize the expansion of the new bikeshare program or focus on pedestrian safety rests on a false choice. Making streets safer for pedestrians and improving bicycle infrastructure go hand in hand. The “complete streets” approach championed during the Bloomberg administration must be expanded, not rolled back. Dedicated bike lanes and pedestrian plazas naturally calm traffic, reducing the speeding that is the primary threat to pedestrians, while organizing the historically chaotic flow of cars, cyclists, and foot traffic. Citi Bike is a triumph, but to truly serve the city, it must expand alongside physical infrastructure that protects all vulnerable road users.

For the millions of New Yorkers living beyond the current reach of bikeshare and outside Manhattan’s dense subway grid, commute times are the defining transportation crisis. Because large-scale subway expansions are prohibitively expensive and agonizingly slow to build, the next mayor must look to the surface. Expanding Select Bus Service into a true, comprehensive Bus Rapid Transit network is the most cost-effective, immediate way to connect outer-borough transit deserts. By implementing physically separated bus lanes, off-board fare collection, and transit signal priority, the city can drastically reduce commute times in Queens, Brooklyn, the Bronx, and Staten Island without waiting decades for new rail lines.

Finally, we cannot improve commute times for the bridge-and-tunnel crowd—or adequately fund the MTA—without rethinking how we price our road space. The next administration should seriously pursue toll reform, such as the widely discussed "fair pricing" plans. By tolling the currently free East River bridges while lowering tolls on outer-borough crossings that lack viable transit alternatives, the city could dramatically reduce gridlock in the central business district. This would simultaneously speed up travel for those who must drive and generate essential, dedicated revenue to modernize our aging transit infrastructure.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

The next mayor should reject the false choice between bikes and pedestrians. Citi Bike is a modest, useful addition to the transportation network, especially for short trips below 59th Street and in Brooklyn, but it cannot be allowed to become the symbol of transportation policy. The larger test is whether streets are designed for people moving at human speed. That means protected bike lanes where they fit, shorter crossing distances, leading pedestrian intervals, stricter speed enforcement and calmer arterials such as Queens Boulevard, Atlantic Avenue and the Grand Concourse. A city where cyclists are separated from fast traffic is also usually a city where pedestrians are safer.

The bigger inequity is geographic. Manhattan already has the densest transit web; many outer-borough residents face two-fare, two-hour commutes. The city should push the MTA and Albany for more Select Bus Service, real bus lanes that are enforced, all-door boarding, signal priority and better crosstown routes that do not force every trip through Manhattan. These improvements are cheaper and faster than new subways, though projects like the Second Avenue subway and East Side Access still matter.

For drivers and the bridge-and-tunnel crowd, pretending that roads can be widened out of congestion is fantasy. Pricing scarce road space—through congestion pricing or more rational bridge tolls—should be back on the table, with revenue dedicated to transit. Freight delivery hours, curb management and parking policy also deserve attention. The goal should not be to favor one mode, but to move more New Yorkers safely and reliably.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The next mayor will inherit a transportation system that, while remarkable in many respects, has accumulated years of deferred attention to pressing needs beyond Manhattan's core. The question of bikeshare versus pedestrian safety presents a false choice. The Citi Bike program, launched in late May, has shown early promise, but its expansion must go hand in hand with infrastructure that protects all users of the street—cyclists, pedestrians, and drivers alike. The beauty of a robust bikeshare network is its potential to reduce short-hop transit reliance, easing crowding on buses and subways while giving New Yorkers a healthier, more flexible commuting option. But this expansion only makes sense if paired with protected lanes and redesigned intersections that bring down the pedestrian injury rates that have made our streets too dangerous for seniors and children alike.

The harder challenge lies in the outer boroughs, where transit options remain thin and commute times remain stubbornly long. The MTA's capital needs are enormous, and the city must advocate forcefully for state investment in subway reliability and expansion. But the city itself has tools it has underused: better bus service through dedicated lanes, expanded ferry options along the waterfront in Queens and Brooklyn, and zoning incentives that would encourage development around existing transit nodes. The "bridge-and-tunnel" framing suggests this is merely a suburb problem, but hundreds of thousands of New Yorkers living in the Bronx, eastern Queens, and southern Brooklyn face commutes that would be unthinkable to most Manhattan residents.

The next mayor should resist the temptation to treat transportation as a zero-sum game between modes. The goal must be a unified, multimodal system that serves working-class New Yorkers wherever they live, and that makes the city's streets places for people first.

**Don't Retreat from the Bike Lane Revolution**

The next mayor's biggest temptation will be to treat Janette Sadik-Khan's transportation legacy as a political liability instead of what it actually is: the most cost-effective urban policy success of the Bloomberg era. Citi Bike launched in May and already logged its millionth ride before July 4th. Protected bike lanes on Ninth Avenue have cut injuries to all street users — pedestrians included — by roughly half, according to DOT's own studies. Tearing this up to placate a few loud community boards and tabloid columnists would be policy malpractice.

The framing of the question — bikeshare *or* pedestrian safety — sets up a false choice. They are the same project. Every trip that shifts from a car to a bike is a trip that doesn't threaten a pedestrian in a crosswalk. The streets where DOT has installed bike lanes, plazas, and pedestrian refuges (Times Square, Prospect Park West, Columbus Avenue) are measurably safer for people walking. The slowest, most chaotic streets remain the ones we left untouched, with four lanes of speeding traffic and no design intervention at all.

The real fight for the next mayor is geographic. Citi Bike currently stops at 59th Street and barely reaches Brooklyn beyond Bed-Stuy. Phase two needs to push into Harlem, Astoria, Sunset Park, and the South Bronx — exactly the neighborhoods where households are least likely to own cars and most likely to benefit from a cheap last-mile option to the subway. Bike infrastructure is not a yuppie amenity; in low-income immigrant neighborhoods, the bike is already the workhorse vehicle of delivery workers who currently ride on sidewalks because we have given them nowhere safe to go.

Bill de Blasio, Christine Quinn, Bill Thompson, Joe Lhota — whoever wins should commit to three things on day one: keep Polly Trottenberg or someone like her at DOT, double the bike network by 2017, and adopt a "Vision Zero" traffic-fatality target like Stockholm's. The cost is trivial compared to a single subway extension. The political courage required is the real scarce resource. New York finally has a 21st-century street network taking shape. The next mayor's job is to finish it, not to apologize for it.

**Stop Designing a City for Manhattan Below 96th Street**

Reading the debate over Citi Bike, you would think New York City consisted of the West Village, Williamsburg, and a few op-ed pages. The actual transportation crisis of this city is being lived by the home health aide in Canarsie who leaves the house at 5 a.m. for a 7 a.m. shift in Riverdale, by the restaurant worker in Corona transferring twice to reach a Midtown kitchen, by the Staten Islander whose express bus crawls across the Verrazano. Bikeshare is fine. It is also irrelevant to these people.

The next mayor inherits a transit geography that has barely changed since Fiorello La Guardia. The subway is a hub-and-spoke system that assumes everyone wants to go to Midtown. But the fastest-growing job centers — Long Island City, the Brooklyn Navy Yard, Hunts Point, JFK-area logistics — generate huge cross-borough commutes that the MTA simply doesn't serve. The result is a two-tier city: a 35-minute commute if you work in Manhattan, an 75-minute commute if you don't.

What can a mayor actually do, given that the MTA is a state agency? Three things, immediately. First, build out true Bus Rapid Transit — physically separated lanes, off-board fare collection, signal priority — on the corridors Albany will never give us a subway for: Woodhaven Boulevard, Utica Avenue, Nostrand, Hylan. Select Bus Service is a half-measure; we need the full Bogotá treatment. Second, push the MTA and LIRR to integrate fares so that a Rosedale resident isn't paying $10 each way to reach a job at JFK. Third, finally use the underused LIRR Atlantic Branch and the Bay Ridge freight line for in-city passenger service — the Regional Plan Association has been begging for this for thirty years.

The bridge-and-tunnel question is really a congestion pricing question. Sam Schwartz's plan — toll the East River bridges, lower tolls on the outer crossings — would relieve Manhattan gridlock, fund transit, and treat outer-borough drivers more fairly than today's perverse system. Bloomberg couldn't get it through Albany; the next mayor must try again.

Bikes are a nice amenity. Buses and tolls are the policy.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Put Pedestrians First, Because Pedestrians Are New Yorkers**

Every year, roughly 150 pedestrians are killed on the streets of New York City and thousands more are injured. That is more than the number of people murdered with firearms in most years. We treat one number as a national emergency and the other as weather. The next mayor needs to stop accepting traffic deaths as the cost of doing business in a big city.

Walking is the dominant mode of transportation in New York. Far more trips happen on foot than by bike, taxi, or private car. Yet our streets are still engineered, signed, and policed as if their primary function were moving motor vehicles through as quickly as possible. Crosswalks that give pedestrians seven seconds to cross six lanes. Turn signals that put left-turning cars into the same phase as people stepping off the curb. NYPD precincts that close fatal-crash investigations in hours, almost always concluding "no criminality suspected."

The good news is that the tools to fix this are well understood and inexpensive. Lower the citywide default speed limit from 30 to 25 mph; physics does the rest, because survival rates in pedestrian-vehicle crashes fall off a cliff above 30. Install leading pedestrian intervals at every signalized intersection — a three-second head start before cars get the green. Daylight intersections by banning parking within 20 feet of the corner, so drivers and pedestrians can see each other. Build neckdowns and raised crosswalks on the corridors where children walk to school. Stockholm, London, and now San Francisco have shown that a "Vision Zero" approach — designing streets on the assumption that humans make mistakes but should not die for them — actually works.

This agenda is not in conflict with bikeshare; it is the same agenda. But it does require a mayor willing to confront the NYPD's culture of indifference to traffic violence and to back a DOT commissioner against the inevitable backlash from drivers who feel inconvenienced by a four-second delay. The measure of the next mayor's transportation record should not be how many bike lanes were striped or how fast the FDR moves. It should be how many New Yorkers came home alive.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**The Honest Answer: The Mayor Has Less Power Than You Think**

Before we hand the next mayor a wish list, it is worth being candid about what the office can and cannot do. The subways and commuter rails are run by a state authority whose board the governor controls. The Port Authority crossings are a bi-state fiefdom. Congestion pricing died in Albany, not at City Hall. Even the bus routes that the city desperately needs upgraded are MTA property. The mayor of New York is, in transportation terms, mostly the mayor of the curb.

That said, the curb is not nothing. It is where bike lanes, bus lanes, loading zones, parking, plazas, and crosswalks live, and it is the lever Bloomberg used to genuinely change the city. The question for his successor is which fights are worth picking with the limited authority the office actually has.

Three priorities suggest themselves. First, finish and defend the bike network. Citi Bike is a fait accompli; the next mayor's job is to extend it to neighborhoods that need cheap mobility most, not to relitigate Prospect Park West. Second, get serious about buses, which are the only mode that the city can meaningfully improve without Albany's permission, since DOT controls the streets the buses run on. Dedicated lanes with camera enforcement, all-door boarding, and signal priority on a dozen key corridors would do more for outer-borough commute times than any imaginable subway expansion in a four-year term.

Third — and this is the unglamorous one — the next mayor needs to be a relentless lobbyist in Albany for the things only the state can deliver: a fair tolling plan along the lines of Sam Schwartz's proposal, a funded MTA capital program that doesn't bleed riders with fare hikes, and the Second Avenue Subway's phases beyond 96th Street. The mayor's bully pulpit on these issues matters more than any individual policy lever at City Hall.

What I would not want is a mayor who treats transportation as a culture war — either by pandering to motorists' resentment of bike lanes or by treating every windshield-perspective tabloid column as a mandate. The Bloomberg-Sadik-Khan years showed that incremental, evidence-driven street redesign works. Keep doing that. Pick the Albany fights worth picking. And measure success in minutes saved and lives saved, not headlines.
