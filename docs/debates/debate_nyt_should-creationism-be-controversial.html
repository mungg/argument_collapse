Belief in biblical creation is, in one sense, utterly unremarkable. Gallup has polled Americans on origins for three decades, and the share who say God created humans in their present form within the last 10,000 years has held stubbornly around 45 percent. A view held by nearly half the country cannot be called fringe. In that demographic sense, Virginia Heffernan's confession is no more surprising than a neighbor saying grace before dinner.

What made her essay controversial was not its content but its framing. Heffernan didn't argue that Genesis is literally true; she said she preferred its "narrative" to what she portrayed as the dreary, unreliable storytelling of science. That move — choosing an origin story on aesthetic grounds, and dismissing evolutionary biology as just another yarn — is what drew the outcry, and rightly so. There is a real difference between a person of faith who accepts evolutionary biology and reads Genesis as theology (the position of most mainline Christians, the Catholic Church since Pius XII, and plenty of working scientists), and someone who treats a 150-year-old, massively corroborated body of evidence as a competing fairy tale. The first is not controversial. The second is, because it confuses categories Pinker was trying to keep straight.

Why are people drawn to Genesis? Because origin narratives do work that data cannot. They tell you who your people are, why suffering exists, what you owe your parents and children, where the moral weight of the cosmos lies. Common descent from a population of African hominins, however true, does not console anyone at a funeral. Scripture's pull is not ignorance; it is the human need for stories that locate us morally. Conversely, people are drawn to the scientific account because it is shockingly powerful: it predicts antibiotic resistance, dates fossils to within a percent, and gets the genetic distances among species to line up with the morphological tree drawn before DNA was discovered. That is not "a story"; it is a converging body of independent measurements.

So the answer is yes and no. It should not be controversial to find meaning in Genesis, any more than to find it in the Bhagavad Gita or in Lucretius. It should be controversial — and remain so — to claim that preferring such a narrative excuses you from taking the evidence about the natural world seriously. Heffernan got grief because she blurred that line, and the line is worth defending.

To call creationism “controversial” is to acknowledge a fracture in our public conversation, not merely a disagreement among private citizens. A personal belief in a literal six-day creation, a young earth, or the special creation of kinds is, in a pluralistic society, unremarkable. What elevates creationism from private conviction to public controversy is its insistence on being treated as a competing scientific explanation, one that should be taught alongside or in place of evolutionary biology in public-school classrooms. That move transforms a theological claim into an empirical one, and it is there that the controversy ignites.

The reason is straightforward. Science is not a catalog of facts but a method—a self-correcting process of testing hypotheses against observable evidence, peer review, and provisional consensus. By that standard, modern evolutionary theory is one of the most robustly supported frameworks in human knowledge, drawing on genetics, paleontology, developmental biology, and geology in a coherent explanatory web. Creationism, by contrast, begins from a revealed text and interprets data to fit that text. Its central claims—a global flood, a 6,000-year-old earth, the absence of common descent—are contradicted by mountains of evidence from multiple independent disciplines. When a Virginia Heffernan writes of being drawn to the poetic power of Genesis, she is making an aesthetic and spiritual confession; she is not offering a testable model of biogeography. The outrage that followed her essay stemmed not from her private faith but from a sense that a prominent writer was blurring that crucial boundary at a moment when science literacy already feels fragile.

Yet the controversy also persists because the scientific story, for all its explanatory power, does not satisfy every human longing. Origin narratives like Genesis give us intentionality, moral order, and a cosmos shaped by a purposeful mind. They speak to the “why” of existence in a register that a description of mutation and natural selection cannot. For many, that existential resonance is more immediate than the patient, impersonal mechanisms of science. The draw is not ignorance; it is the hunger for meaning.

The real question, then, is not whether creationism should be controversial in a person’s inner life—we all navigate the world with a mix of reason, intuition, and inherited story. It is whether it should be controversial as a matter of public knowledge and educational policy. As long as its proponents insist on inserting a non-falsifiable, scripture-grounded narrative into the science curriculum, the answer will remain yes. That tension is not an insult to faith; it is the natural friction between two different ways of knowing, each making claims on the same public square.

It is easy to see why people are drawn to the Genesis account. Humans are profoundly narrative creatures, wired to seek purpose, agency, and meaning. Genesis offers a cosmos built specifically for us, infused with moral weight and divine intention. It is a beautiful, centering story. By contrast, evolutionary biology describes a blind, unguided process of genetic mutation and natural selection. While magnificent in its staggering complexity, the scientific account can feel existentially cold. It does not tell us we are the pinnacle of creation; it tells us we are primates who survived through adaptation.

Conversely, those drawn to the scientific story—and those who defend it fiercely, like Steven Pinker—are motivated by a commitment to empirical reality over comforting myth. The scientific method provides a reliable, testable framework for understanding the physical universe. Evolutionary theory does not command the consensus of the global scientific community because it tells a soothing story, but because it is corroborated by an overwhelming convergence of fossil, genetic, and geological evidence. It offers a different kind of wonder—the awe of discovering how the natural world actually operates, independent of our wishes.

However, when creationism is publicly elevated as a valid alternative to evolutionary science, it crosses a dangerous line. Treating the two as interchangeable consumer choices threatens science education and basic public literacy. In 2013, as we face complex, global challenges that demand rigorous scientific comprehension—such as climate change and antibiotic resistance—we cannot afford the luxury of epistemological relativism. If we accept that personal preference can overrule empirical evidence on the origins of life, we erode the very foundations of how we make informed decisions in a modern democracy.

Biblical creation should be controversial only when it is presented as an account of nature on the same footing as science. As a matter of worship, identity or poetry, Genesis is not a scandal. Millions of intelligent people read it devotionally, allegorically or liturgically. Creationism, in its young-earth or anti-evolution forms, does not generate comparable predictions or survive the same tests. It offers a moral universe: human beings are made with purpose, the world is not an accident, and suffering and disorder have an intelligible place in a larger drama. It seems to me the question is posed in a misleading way. "Should creationism be controversial?" lumps together at least three different things, and the answer depends entirely on which one you mean.

The first is the metaphysical claim that the universe and life have a creator. This is not, and cannot be, scientifically controversial, because it isn't a scientific claim. Science investigates regularities in nature; it has nothing to say about whether those regularities are themselves the product of some deeper intention. A working geneticist can be a devout Catholic, like Francis Collins, or a militant atheist, like Jerry Coyne, and their lab notebooks will look identical. On this terrain, belief in a creator is a matter of personal conviction and should not be controversial at all.

The second is the literalist claim that the Earth is about 6,000 years old, that all species were created in their present form, and that a global flood explains the fossil record. This is a scientific claim, and it is overwhelmingly, unambiguously false. Stratigraphy, radiometric dating, plate tectonics, comparative genomics, and the fossil record converge on conclusions that are simply incompatible with young-earth creationism. To call this "controversial" in a scientific sense is to misuse the word. Among working scientists in relevant fields, it isn't controversial; it's wrong.

The third is the political claim that public school biology classes should teach creationism, or its rebranded cousin intelligent design, alongside evolution. This is genuinely controversial — not because the science is in dispute, but because Americans disagree about what public education is for, who controls curricula, and how to balance majority religious sentiment with epistemic standards. Kitzmiller v. Dover settled the constitutional question in 2005, but the cultural fight persists.

Heffernan's essay collapsed all three categories. She wrote as though preferring Genesis as a story was equivalent to disputing the age of the Earth, and her critics, in turn, responded as if her literary preference were a policy proposal. Both sides were arguing past each other.

Why are people drawn to Genesis? Because human beings are narrative creatures who want to know what they are for, not just where they came from. Why are others drawn to the scientific account? Because they find the actual history of nature, in all its strangeness, more astonishing than any myth. These are different appetites, and they need not be in conflict — until someone tries to make a literal cosmology out of a story that was never meant to be one, or a creed out of a method that was never meant to be that either.

Behind the squabble over Virginia Heffernan's essay lies a much older and more interesting question: what kind of thing is an origin story for? Until we sort that out, the debate just goes in circles, with one side accusing the other of bad science and the other accusing the first of bad faith.

For most of human history, origin narratives were not in competition with anything we would recognize as science. Genesis, the Enuma Elish, Hesiod's Theogony — these are not failed hypotheses about geology. They are accounts of why there is order rather than chaos, why human beings are responsible to one another, why suffering and labor exist. They answer questions that the question "how old is the Earth?" does not even pose. The trouble began when modern science offered an empirical account of origins that competed with the literal surface of these stories, and some religious communities decided to defend the literal surface as if their whole tradition stood or fell on the geological column.

That decision was, in my view, a theological mistake, not just a scientific one. Augustine warned against it in the fourth century — he cautioned Christians not to embarrass themselves by clinging to literal cosmologies that any educated pagan could refute. The modern fundamentalist conviction that Genesis must be read as a science textbook is a relatively recent development, traceable largely to early-twentieth-century American Protestantism. Most Christians worldwide, including Catholics and mainline Protestants, accept evolution without theological crisis.

So when Heffernan claims to be a "creationist" because she finds Genesis more meaningful than scientific accounts, she is making a category error, but a particularly modern one. She thinks she has to choose between the two stories because she has accepted the fundamentalist framing that the choice is binary. The new atheists, ironically, accept the same framing — they just choose the other side. Both camps treat Genesis as a defective scientific document, when its original audience would have found that reading bewildering.

The grown-up position is that science tells us how, and origin narratives — religious, literary, philosophical — help us figure out what to do about it. Neither displaces the other. The age of the Earth is 4.54 billion years; this is a settled empirical fact. Whether human existence has a purpose, and what that purpose might be, is not the kind of question radiometric dating can answer. People drawn to Genesis are usually asking the second question. The unfortunate thing is when they think they're answering the first.

The framing of this debate — controversy versus consensus — obscures what is actually at stake. The real fault line isn't between "people who like science" and "people who like the Bible." It's between two competing pictures of what counts as legitimate knowledge in a pluralistic society.

On one picture, knowledge is whatever survives the gauntlet of public, replicable, falsifiable inquiry. On this view, Genesis as cosmology has been falsified the way phlogiston has been falsified, and continued belief in it is a kind of epistemic obstinacy. On the other picture, knowledge includes the lived wisdom of traditions — moral, communal, contemplative — that cannot be reduced to laboratory tests but that have, over millennia, taught human beings how to live. On this view, dismissing Genesis because it fails as geology is like dismissing King Lear because it fails as a meteorological report.

Both pictures have a point, and the loudest combatants in this argument usually flatten one into the other. The new atheists tend to assume that if a claim isn't empirically grounded, it isn't worth taking seriously. Many creationists, conversely, treat their tradition as if it had empirical jurisdiction over questions it was never designed to address. Heffernan's essay was a confused gesture in the direction of the second position; her critics' response was a less-confused but still incomplete defense of the first.

Where I come down: on questions where the empirical method has clearly delivered the goods — the age of the Earth, the mechanism of species change, the descent of humans from earlier primates — Genesis simply has no standing. To insist otherwise is to claim a kind of authority for one's tradition that other traditions don't get to claim, and that the tradition itself, properly understood, never sought. But on questions of meaning, value, and purpose — what we owe each other, how to face death, what makes a life worth living — science offers tools but not answers, and treating Genesis (or the Tao Te Ching, or the Bhagavad Gita) as a serious interlocutor is not anti-intellectual. It's appropriate humility about the limits of any single method.

So is creationism controversial? Young-earth literalism, as a claim about natural history, shouldn't be — it lost the argument long ago. But the broader human attraction to creation narratives is not controversial at all; it's universal. The interesting question isn't why people still read Genesis. It's why some of us imagine that reading it well requires pretending the fossils aren't there.

Secular intellectuals have not yet done this work well. Until they do, creationism will remain attractive — and controversy will remain beside the point.
