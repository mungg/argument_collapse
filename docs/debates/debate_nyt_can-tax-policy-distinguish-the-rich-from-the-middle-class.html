Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The 529 debacle revealed less a failure of tax policy than a failure of political vocabulary. "Middle class" in American politics is aspirational, not statistical: households earning $250,000 routinely describe themselves that way, and politicians of both parties indulge the fiction. Any tax reform that hopes to address inequality has to begin by acknowledging that the income distribution is continuous, and that drawing a sharp line between "rich" and "middle" will always feel arbitrary to the people just above it.

That said, the tools to differentiate exist and already work reasonably well — we simply use them inconsistently. Three mechanisms matter. First, marginal rate brackets: the top bracket at $400,000-plus already isolates roughly the top 1 percent, and additional brackets above $1 million and $10 million would sharpen the distinction further. Second, the treatment of capital income: the gap between the 23.8 percent rate on long-term capital gains and dividends and the 39.6 percent rate on wages is the single biggest reason Warren Buffett pays a lower rate than his secretary. Equalizing those rates, or taxing accrued gains at death as Obama's budget proposes, targets the wealthy almost surgically, because the top 1 percent receives the overwhelming share of capital income. Third, phase-outs and refundability: deductions and exclusions (mortgage interest, employer health insurance, 529s, retirement accounts) flow disproportionately upward because they are worth more in higher brackets and require disposable income to use. Converting deductions to capped credits, as Martin Feldstein and others have proposed, would redirect those subsidies.

The 529 episode shows the political constraint: tax expenditures, once established, generate constituencies that punch above their demographic weight. A household earning $180,000 in Westchester does not feel rich, and its vote counts. The honest answer, then, is that tax policy can distinguish the rich from the middle class — through rate structure, capital income reform, and credit design — but only when politicians are willing to defend the line they draw.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The swift political demise of President Obama’s proposal to curb the tax advantages of 529 college savings plans captured a central paradox of American tax policy. On paper, the benefits of these accounts flow overwhelmingly to the top 5 percent of households, those earning more than $200,000 a year. Yet the outcry that killed the idea framed it as an assault on the middle class itself, demonstrating just how difficult it is for the tax code to draw a bright line between the merely comfortable and the truly wealthy. The question is not merely technical—can tax policy distinguish the rich from the middle class?—but political: will we ever let it?

From a design standpoint, the tools are readily available. A steeply progressive income-tax rate structure is the most direct instrument. Rates that kick in at high thresholds, such as the 39.6 percent bracket on taxable income above roughly $415,000 for single filers in 2015, explicitly target top earners. To make that distinction bite, the code can phase out deductions and personal exemptions for high-income taxpayers, as the Pease limitation already does, or means-test benefits directly. The Earned Income Tax Credit, for instance, phases out entirely before a family reaches middle-class income, while the child tax credit begins to shrink above $110,000 for married couples. Such provisions show that the federal government is perfectly capable of reserving help for those with modest incomes and extracting more from those at the summit.

The deeper trouble, however, lies not in scarcity of policy mechanisms but in the slippage between income categories and the social language of class. A threshold of $200,000 may sound rich in Mississippi but feels decidedly middle class to a family with two earners, a mortgage, and day-care costs in New York or San Francisco. Many tax expenditures that economists view as regressive—the mortgage interest deduction, the exclusion for employer-provided health insurance, the lower rate on capital gains and dividends—are defended as pillars of middle-class security because they benefit a broad swath of voters who think of themselves as anything but wealthy. When President Obama proposed capping the value of itemized deductions at 28 percent for the top brackets, it was framed not as a levy on the rich but as a threat to charitable giving and home values that would cascade downward. The 529 reversal was only the latest proof that any tax benefit, however skewed at the top, can be successfully rebranded as an aspirational middle-class entitlement the moment it is threatened.

This points to a fundamental mismatch. Tax policy can easily differentiate by the neutral metric of dollars earned, but it cannot so cleanly sort by the politically charged and self-identified notion of “middle class.” The very ambiguity of the term is its political strength. A majority of Americans consider themselves middle class, making any measure that explicitly draws a line between them and the “rich” vulnerable to the charge that it will penalize hard work, discourage saving, or eventually creep down the income ladder. This is why the most durable tax increases on the wealthy over the past generation—such as the creation of the top bracket in 1993 or the Affordable Care Act’s surtax on investment income—were coupled either with very high thresholds or with reassurances that the vast majority of filers would be untouched. Even then, they passed by razor-thin margins and under intense partisan fire.

Ultimately, the federal tax code can and does separate high earners from the rest through rates, phase-outs, and targeted credits. But the question of whether policy can distinguish the rich from the middle class is the wrong one. The real issue is whether the public will accept any distinction that moves beyond the demonstrably super-rich—the top 1 percent—and encroaches upon the professional and entrepreneurial families who see themselves not as privileged but as the core of the American middle. The 529 fracas suggests the answer, for now, is no.

The recent uproar over 529 college savings accounts perfectly illustrates this phenomenon. Households earning over $200,000 a year are statistically in the top 5 percent of the income distribution, yet they vehemently view themselves as middle-class parents simply trying to save for their children's education.

If lawmakers genuinely want to address inequality without triggering massive upper-middle-class backlash, they must recognize that high-earning professionals still identify as working people. A successfully progressive tax system should draw its most important line not just by how much money citizens make, but by how they actually make it.

Federal tax policy can distinguish the rich from the middle class, but only if it stops pretending that one bright line can do all the work. “Middle class” is partly an income category, partly a wealth category, and partly a political identity. A family earning $210,000 in Manhattan with children and student loans is not in the same position as a billionaire, but it is also not typical: median household income is roughly a quarter of that. Tax law should acknowledge both facts.

The best approach is not to ask whether a household feels rich, but whether it has unusual capacity to pay or is receiving subsidies that overwhelmingly flow upward. On that test, many tax preferences fail. The mortgage-interest deduction, preferential rates on capital gains, estate-tax exemptions and college-savings breaks often deliver the largest dollar benefits to households with the most assets. A 529 account is useful to some upper-middle-class savers, but families struggling to pay rent or tuition cannot benefit much from tax-free investment gains because they have little money to invest. Calling every affluent beneficiary “middle class” makes the term meaningless.

Still, policy must be careful. Income in a single year can mislead: a small-business owner may have a temporary spike, a retiree may realize capital gains, and regional costs vary. That argues for graduated phaseouts rather than cliffs, multi-year averaging where appropriate, and separate treatment of wages, inherited wealth and capital income. It also argues for designing benefits as refundable credits or direct aid when the goal is to help low- and middle-income families, rather than deductions and exclusions that are worth most to those in high brackets.

A reasonable tax code would use several markers: adjusted income, accumulated wealth, type of income, family size and the nature of the tax benefit. It would not put a schoolteacher and a hedge-fund partner in the same category merely because both claim a deduction, nor would it treat a $200,000 household as needy simply because it is not super-rich.

The real obstacle is not technical but political. Every tax preference has beneficiaries who describe themselves as middle class. If Congress accepts that claim at face value, inequality policy becomes impossible. If it measures ability to pay honestly, the distinction is difficult but entirely workable.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

One of the most persistent puzzles in tax design is whether the code can reliably draw a line between the rich and the middle class without creating perverse incentives, administrative headaches, or misleading labels. The definition of “rich” is inherently elastic: it can be anchored to a fixed dollar amount, to a percentile of the income distribution, or to a broader notion of wealth or consumption. Because income, wealth, consumption, and family circumstances all move together, any single metric is inevitably a simplification. The question is whether that simplification serves policy goals or undermines them.

The existing federal income tax already differentiates between high earners and the middle class through progressive rates. The top marginal rate kicks in above roughly $400,000 for single filers in 2015, while the 10‑ and 15‑percent brackets apply to the bulk of middle‑income taxpayers. Phase‑out provisions, such as those that reduce the benefit of the Earned Income Tax Credit or the child tax credit at higher incomes, add another layer of targeting. The 529 plan example illustrates both the promise and the pitfalls: by offering a tax‑advantaged savings vehicle limited to families that can afford to set aside $14,000 per year, the policy effectively subsidizes a group that, by most measures, is already well‑off.

Some analysts argue that income is an inadequate proxy for economic well‑being and that a wealth tax or a consumption tax would more accurately separate the rich from the middle class. A wealth tax would capture assets that generate income but are not reported on annual returns, while a consumption tax would tax people based on what they spend rather than what they earn. Both approaches face formidable design problems: valuation of assets is costly, and a broad consumption tax would hit low‑income households disproportionately unless paired with a generous exemption. Moreover, targeting benefits to groups defined by wealth or consumption raises privacy concerns and invites strategic reshuffling of assets.

The political appeal of a “middle‑class” label is strong: it signals fairness and broadens the coalition for a tax change. Yet that appeal can distort design. When a benefit is framed as helping the middle class, it may attract households that barely qualify, and when it is stripped from high earners, the backlash can be swift, as the 529 rollback showed. An honest approach would define targeted provisions by specific income ranges or measures of economic need and would communicate those criteria transparently. This would reduce demagoguery and allow the tax code to allocate resources where they reduce inequality.

The Line Is Real, and We Already Draw It

The flap over 529 accounts obscures a basic truth: federal tax policy already distinguishes between the rich and the middle class, and does so with reasonable precision. The Earned Income Tax Credit phases out around the low-to-middle ranges. The Child Tax Credit phases out beginning at $110,000 for couples. The American Opportunity Credit phases out at $180,000. The Pease limitation on itemized deductions and the PEP phase-out of personal exemptions kick in around $300,000. The top marginal rate of 39.6 percent applies above roughly $465,000 for joint filers. The 3.8 percent net investment income tax targets households above $250,000.

The mechanism is the phase-out, and it works. By using adjusted gross income thresholds with gradual reductions, Congress can deliver benefits to families earning $60,000 without writing checks to families earning $600,000. The 529 episode was not a failure of design but a failure of politics: the President proposed taxing accumulated gains in vehicles that disproportionately serve the affluent, and affluent families — who vote, donate, and write op-eds — complained loudly enough that the proposal collapsed within a week.

The honest question is not whether tax policy can distinguish rich from middle class, but where we want the lines drawn and what we want to do at each level. Households between $100,000 and $250,000 are an awkward category — comfortable in Tulsa, squeezed in San Francisco — and they are large enough and politically active enough that any administration touching their tax preferences pays a price. Calling them "middle class" is rhetorically convenient but statistically generous: a household at $200,000 sits above 95 percent of American families.

So the technical answer is straightforward. AGI thresholds, phase-outs, and differential treatment of wage versus capital income give policymakers ample tools. What's hard isn't the engineering. It's persuading the upper-middle class that they aren't the middle.

"Middle Class" Is a Feeling, Not a Bracket

Ask a family earning $300,000 whether they are rich, and almost none will say yes. Ask a family earning $40,000 whether they are middle class, and almost all will say yes. That is the political problem the Obama administration just ran into with 529 accounts, and it is the problem any administration runs into whenever it tries to nudge tax burdens upward on the professional class.

The reason is that "middle class" in American usage is a cultural identity, not an income range. It means: I work for my living, I worry about my bills, I haven't inherited my position, my kids' college isn't a sure thing. A two-earner household of a public school principal and a hospital administrator in suburban New Jersey can clear $200,000 and feel every bit of that description. So can a self-employed couple in Denver with two kids in braces and a mortgage. They are, by the numbers, in the top 5 percent. By their own lights, they are squarely in the middle.

This is why income-threshold politics keeps misfiring. The 529 program was a textbook case of an upper-middle-class subsidy — Government Accountability Office studies have shown most of the benefit flows to households well above the median. But the families using it don't experience the benefit as a windfall; they experience it as relief from the punishing cost of tuition, which has risen far faster than their wages.

Tax policy can certainly draw lines — at $250,000, at $400,000, at $1 million. The question is whether those lines correspond to anything the public recognizes as the rich. Until policymakers stop using "rich" to mean "professional class with two incomes and a mortgage" and start reserving it for the genuinely wealthy — the dividend-and-capital-gains class, households in the top 1 percent and especially the top 0.1 percent — they will keep getting clobbered when they try to redistribute.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Stop Drawing Lines and Broaden the Base

The premise of the question is itself the problem. The American tax code is a thicket of carve-outs, credits, deductions, and phase-outs precisely because we have spent forty years trying to use it to differentiate among groups of taxpayers — rich, middle, poor, parents, students, homeowners, savers, retirees. The 529 episode is a perfect illustration of where this leads: a preference is created, a constituency forms around it, and any future attempt to repeal it is denounced as a tax hike on the middle class, regardless of who actually benefits.

A better approach would stop trying to micro-target. Broaden the base, lower the rates, eliminate most preferences, and use a small number of clearly progressive marginal brackets plus a refundable credit at the bottom to handle distributional concerns. The Tax Reform Act of 1986 — under a Republican president and a Democratic House — moved in this direction and was widely admired. Since then, we have undone most of its work by piling preferences back into the code.

If the President wants to address inequality, he has cleaner tools than tinkering with 529s. Raise the top marginal rate. Tax capital gains and dividends as ordinary income above some threshold. Close the carried-interest loophole. End the step-up in basis at death, which lets enormous fortunes pass untaxed across generations. Each of these touches the genuinely wealthy without affecting the dentist in Cincinnati saving for her daughter's tuition.

The reason these proposals are politically harder than killing 529s is instructive. The genuinely wealthy are well-represented in Washington; the upper-middle class is loud. The path of least resistance has been to nibble at upper-middle-class preferences while leaving the real fortunes alone. That is not progressive reform. It is tax policy theater, and the public is right to be skeptical.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Geography Is the Missing Variable

Two hundred thousand dollars buys very different lives in different parts of the country, and any honest discussion of where the middle class ends has to grapple with this. In rural Mississippi or eastern Tennessee, a $200,000 household income places a family in something like local aristocracy. In Manhattan, San Francisco, Boston, or the Washington suburbs, it buys a modest apartment, a long commute, and a constant low-grade anxiety about private school tuition. The federal tax code mostly ignores this.

It shouldn't. The standard deduction and bracket thresholds are uniform nationally, and while the state and local tax deduction provides some implicit adjustment for high-tax (and usually high-cost) areas, it does so in a regressive way — it's worth most to those who itemize and face the highest marginal rates. A more honest approach would index income thresholds for major federal preferences and brackets to regional cost of living, the way Social Security uses a national average wage but the way many federal pay scales already include locality adjustments.

The political resistance to this is obvious: it would look like a giveaway to blue-state professionals. But the underlying principle — that purchasing power, not nominal income, is the right measure of who is rich — is sound. The 529 debate hinged on it. Families using 529s most heavily are concentrated in high-cost metros where college savings of $200,000 or more per child are not a luxury but a necessity, given sticker prices at private universities now exceeding $65,000 a year.

Can tax policy distinguish rich from middle class? Only crudely, as long as it treats a dollar in Birmingham and a dollar in Brooklyn as equivalent. They aren't. Until federal policy acknowledges this, the political category of "middle class" will keep expanding upward to include households who, on paper, look wealthy but, in their daily lives, do not feel that way — and reformers will keep losing the argument.

Income Misses the Point; Tax Wealth Instead

The 529 dust-up reveals something important: when we talk about distinguishing the rich from the middle class through the tax code, we are almost always talking about income. But income is a poor proxy for wealth, and wealth is what most people mean when they say "rich."

A surgeon earning $400,000 with student loans, a recent mortgage, and no inherited assets is, in any meaningful sense, in the same economic situation as her merely affluent neighbors — comfortable, secure, but dependent on continuing to work. A trust-fund heir with $20 million in index funds earning $400,000 in qualified dividends pays a lower effective federal tax rate than the surgeon and never has to work a day. The income tax sees them as roughly equivalent. They are not.

This is why arguments over where to set income brackets keep feeling unsatisfying. The truly rich — the top tenth of one percent, who hold a disproportionate share of national wealth — derive most of their economic power not from wages but from accumulated capital. Income tax tweaks barely touch them. Even raising the top marginal rate to 50 percent would leave the Walton or Koch fortunes essentially undisturbed, because those fortunes generate gains that are realized strategically, deferred indefinitely, and stepped up at death.

If we want tax policy to distinguish the rich from everyone else, the cleanest tools are: taxing capital gains at ordinary rates, ending step-up in basis, strengthening the estate tax, and considering some form of progressive wealth tax. Thomas Piketty's recent work has put these questions squarely on the table. They are politically difficult, but they target the right people.

Meanwhile, fighting over 529 accounts — a preference worth a few thousand dollars to dentists and engineers — is a distraction. It picks a fight with the upper-middle class while leaving the actually rich entirely undisturbed. That isn't a strategy for addressing inequality. It's a strategy for losing midterms.

Tax policy can distinguish the rich from the middle class if it stops obsessing over how much people earn and starts looking at how they earn it. The central divide in modern American capitalism is between those who work for a living and those who live off their assets. If the goal is to truly separate the rich from the middle class, the federal income tax is the wrong tool entirely. Its complexity invites loopholes, and its focus on annual income misses accumulated wealth entirely. A far more effective approach is to shift toward a progressive consumption tax, a move that would make the distinction automatic and politically durable. Under a system like the Bradford X-tax or a carbon tax with a lump-sum rebate, households are taxed on what they spend, not what they earn, but the rate rises steeply with consumption levels. A household spending $300,000 would pay a much higher marginal rate on spending above a certain threshold, while a multi-millionaire’s yacht purchase would face top rates. No one needs to define “middle class” by an income number; the distinction falls out of actual behavior. The wealthy consume more and save more, and a consumption tax hits the former while leaving the latter untaxed—channeling investment into the economy. The 529 controversy showed the trap of income-based definitions: parents saving for college were treated as fat cats because their income looked high on paper. A consumption tax would never penalize saving for education or retirement; it would only ask what you take out of the common pool to spend today. That is a system where the rich pay more not because a bureaucrat labeled them, but because their spending patterns reveal them. It is time to rethink the entire platform.
