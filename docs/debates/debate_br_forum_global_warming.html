Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Kerry Emanuel has done an admirable job of laying out, in plain language, the consensus that has emerged from a generation of climate research. His ending—that even under generous readings of the uncertainties we ought to "make sacrifices as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises"—is exactly right. But the insurance framing, while rhetorically useful, understates the peculiarity of the climate problem and obscures both the moral stakes and the practical options now in front of us. I want to push on three points: the structure of the risk, the politics of "winners and losers," and the question Emanuel's title poses but does not really answer.

Start with risk. Conventional insurance covers low-probability, bounded losses against which one is willing to pay a small premium. Climate change is not like that. It has at least three features that ordinary cost-benefit analysis handles badly. First, the damages are largely irreversible on policy-relevant timescales: a melted Greenland ice sheet does not refreeze on a human horizon, and species and coastal cities, once lost, are lost. Second, the system has the threshold behavior Emanuel describes—the paleoclimate record shows abrupt jumps rather than smooth responses to forcing, and we cannot yet say where those thresholds lie. Third, the probability distribution of the consequences appears to have a fat upper tail. The "best estimate" of 3 to 7°F by 2100 is the middle of a distribution whose right side includes scenarios that would be civilizational rather than merely costly. When the tail is fat and the worst outcomes are catastrophic and irreversible, expected-value reasoning systematically misleads. The right analogy is not fire insurance on a house; it is the precaution we take against, say, the proliferation of nuclear weapons—an investment whose justification does not depend on a confident central estimate at all.

This is why I think Sir Nicholas Stern's review for the U.K. Treasury, released this past October, is the most important policy document of 2006, even if its choice of a near-zero pure rate of time preference has provoked howls from some economists. Stern estimates that unmitigated warming could cost on the order of 5 to 20 percent of global GDP per year in perpetuity, while stabilizing concentrations at around 500–550 ppm CO₂-equivalent would cost on the order of 1 percent. One can quibble with the discounting and the damage functions; what is harder to deny is the basic asymmetry. Even William Nordhaus, who uses a much higher discount rate and arrives at a slower optimal ramp, agrees that some level of carbon pricing now is warranted. The economists are arguing about how steeply to climb the hill, not whether the hill exists.

Second, the "winners and losers" frame. Emanuel raises and largely dismisses it, but it deserves a harder treatment, because the distributional structure of climate damages is morally damning in a way the aggregate numbers conceal. The countries that have emitted the cumulative bulk of fossil CO₂—the United States, Western Europe, and increasingly Japan—lie largely in temperate latitudes where some warming is genuinely ambiguous in its effects. The countries that will bear the worst of the consequences—Bangladesh and the Nile and Ganges deltas, sub-Saharan Africa, low-lying Pacific and Caribbean island states, the Andean countries dependent on glacial meltwater—have emitted almost nothing per capita and have the least adaptive capacity. Emanuel's own example of the 1970 Bhola cyclone in Bangladesh, which killed perhaps 300,000 people, is a reminder of what tropical-cyclone intensification means in a country whose entire territory is essentially a delta. To frame this as "winners and losers," as if it were a reshuffling, is to elide what is actually a transfer of harm from the wealthy who caused it to the poor who did not. The moral arithmetic is closer to that of trespass than of bad weather.

Third, and most importantly: can we stop it? Emanuel poses the question but, having spent his essay on the science, leaves it hanging. The encouraging fact is that the answer, technically, is yes—or close enough to yes that the residual uncertainty is about politics, not engineering. Robert Socolow and Stephen Pacala published a now-famous analysis in Science in 2004 in which they decomposed the emissions challenge into seven "stabilization wedges," each representing a billion tons of carbon per year avoided by 2054. Their point was that every one of these wedges can be filled with technologies that already exist at commercial or near-commercial scale: efficiency in vehicles and buildings, a doubling of nuclear capacity, large expansion of wind and solar, carbon capture and storage at coal plants, reduced deforestation, and so on. There is no missing miracle. There is a missing price on carbon.

That price is now beginning to emerge, unevenly and imperfectly. The European Union Emissions Trading System has completed its first trading phase—plagued by over-allocation, but operational, and now being tightened for Phase II. Several Northeastern U.S. states have organized the Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative for the power sector. California's AB 32, signed in September, commits the state to roughly 1990 emissions by 2020 with binding mechanisms. The U.S. federal government remains the great laggard, but the November midterm elections have shifted control of Congress and brought to power committee chairs—Boxer, Dingell, Waxman—who have signaled intent to take up the issue. The 2008 presidential field, in both parties, includes candidates who have endorsed mandatory caps. The political ground is moving faster than even cautious observers would have predicted three years ago.

What is missing from Emanuel's essay, then, is a sense that the policy question is not paralyzed by the residual scientific uncertainty he so honestly describes. The uncertainty cuts in the direction of more action, not less, because the bad surprises are worse than the good surprises are good; the technological menu is real and growing; and the cost of a serious mitigation program looks, on every credible accounting, smaller than the cost of waiting. The argument we should now be having is not whether to act but how to share the burden between rich and poor countries, how to price carbon without crushing the energy-poor, how to handle the coal incumbents, how to fund adaptation in the places that will be hit hardest regardless of what we do over the next two decades.

Emanuel's quiet, careful catalog of what is known and what is uncertain is the right starting point for that conversation. But it is a starting point. The IPCC report due in February will sharpen the science further; it will not resolve the political question, because the political question is no longer waiting on the science. It is waiting on us.

The case Kerry Emanuel lays out is compelling in its measured clarity: a global-warming consensus grounded not in alarmism but in the disciplined uncertainties of a working scientist. He rightly distinguishes what virtually all climate researchers agree on from what remains debated, and he refuses to dismiss the possibility of net benefits even as he catalogs dangers that should keep any prudent person awake at night. Yet for all his care, the framing of his essay implicitly asks readers to consider the problem as if the central question were one of physical science: can the climate system be stabilized? In fact, the question “Can We Stop Global Warming?” is, and has always been, a question about human systems—about economics, politics, and moral imagination. The science tells us that the thermostat is broken; it tells us nothing about whether we have the collective capacity to fix it.

If the physical facts were all that mattered, the answer would already be clear. Emanuel’s summary of undisputed findings from the IPCC process is devastating in its restraint. Atmospheric carbon dioxide stands at levels unseen for at least 650,000 years. The globe has warmed about 1.2°F over the past century, with most of the recent warming attributable to human greenhouse-gas emissions. Sea levels are rising, Arctic ice is shrinking, and the aggressive 2005 hurricane season—the most active in 150 years of records—fits the pattern of intensifying tropical cyclones that his own research has tied to warming oceans. Floods, droughts, and the potential for meter-scale sea-level rise should the ice sheets destabilize are not speculative fiction; they are projections grounded in physics and in the ice-core record of a climate that does not always change gently.

What the science cannot tell us, however, is what we are willing to endure. Emanuel nods toward this when he acknowledges that some regions may benefit, at least in the short term, from longer growing seasons and reduced heating demand. But he quickly underscores the mismatch between those who benefit and those who suffer. The tropical-cyclone data he cites make this vivid: Hurricane Mitch killed more than 10,000 people in Central America; a single storm in 1970 claimed perhaps 300,000 in Bangladesh. These are not vague future scenarios—they are realities in the climates that already exist, and they will grow more acute as the thermodynamic engine of the tropics revs higher. The question is not whether there will be winners and losers, but whether the losers will be permitted to drown, starve, or be driven from their homes while the winners debate the precise discount rate to apply to future damages.

This is where the conversation must shift from millibars and radiative forcings to the machinery of political economy. By January 2007, the world has had a decade of experience with the Kyoto Protocol, and it has been an education in the limits of international environmental governance. The protocol’s architecture—binding emissions targets for industrialized countries, no obligations for developing nations, and the exclusion of the United States, the world’s largest emitter—has produced only modest reductions among the Annex I parties that ratified it, even as global emissions have continued their steep ascent. The Clean Development Mechanism and emissions trading have built a skeleton of carbon markets, but the price signals remain far too weak to drive the kind of transformation Emanuel rightly implies is necessary.

The obstacle has never been a shortage of technological options. As of 2007, wind and solar power are scaling up rapidly from small bases, and engineers have demonstrated that deep efficiency gains in buildings, vehicles, and industry can be had at negative net cost. Carbon capture and storage from coal plants exists at the demonstration scale, and nuclear fission, whatever its other liabilities, remains a baseload source of zero-carbon electricity. The challenge is not inventing the low-carbon economy but paying for it—and, more importantly, deciding who pays. The fossil-fuel infrastructure represents trillions of dollars of sunk capital, and any serious effort to keep the bulk of known reserves in the ground pits climate stability against the business models of the most profitable corporations in history and the sovereign interests of petrostates. That is a political fight, not a scientific one.

Emanuel himself gestures toward a way of thinking about this when he invokes the language of insurance. “Even if we believed that the projected climate changes would be mostly beneficial,” he writes, “we might be inclined to make sacrifices as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises.” The analogy is powerful and should be pressed further. The uncertainties he catalogues—the unknown behavior of ice sheets, the possibility of sudden climate jumps, the poorly understood feedback loops—are not symmetrical. The upper tail of the probability distribution contains outcomes that would be catastrophic on a scale that dwarfs any plausible net benefit from modest warming. A sea-level rise of twenty-two feet from the loss of Greenland alone would redraw every coastline on Earth, displacing hundreds of millions of people over decades and centuries. Even far less dramatic increases would render many low-lying urban areas, from Shanghai to the Netherlands, vulnerable to storm surges that are today once-in-a-century events. Insurance against such outcomes is not an expensive luxury; it is the kind of hedge that individuals and nations routinely buy for far lower stakes.

The moral case is reinforced by the distribution of risk. The industrial activities that have raised CO₂ concentrations from 280 to 380 parts per million have been overwhelmingly concentrated in the wealthy nations of the North. Yet the heaviest burdens—from drought in sub-Saharan Africa to inundation in the Ganges Delta—fall on populations that have contributed negligibly to the problem and that possess the fewest resources to adapt. This is not an issue on which one can blithely calculate global net present value, because the very act of aggregation buries the losses of the poor under the gains of the rich. If one thinks that a Bangladeshi family’s suffering counts for as much as an American’s winter heating bill, the cost-benefit calculus tilts decisively toward ambitious mitigation.

And that brings us back to the question posed by the forum: can we stop global warming? In a physical sense, probably not completely. The thermal inertia of the oceans and the long atmospheric lifetime of carbon dioxide mean that some further warming is already committed, even if emissions were to cease tomorrow. The more realistic goal—and the one that rational self-interest and elementary justice demand—is to stabilize concentrations at a level that avoids the worst of Emanuel’s surprises, and to do so before the climate system takes the matter out of our hands. That means peaking global emissions within the next couple of decades and then reducing them sharply, a trajectory that requires a rapid shift in the way the world produces and consumes energy.

Is that possible? Technically, yes. Politically, the jury is out. The required policies are no mystery: a gradually tightening cap on carbon, whether implemented through a tax or a tradable-permit system, to put a price on the externality that is currently socialized; massive public investment in energy research and deployment; regulatory standards that lock in efficiency gains; and a framework for transferring clean technology and adaptation finance to the developing world so that poverty alleviation and climate protection are pursued in tandem. None of these ideas is new. The 2006 Stern Review on the economics of climate change, commissioned by the British government, made a detailed case that the costs of strong and early action are manageable—on the order of one percent of global GDP per year—while the costs of inaction could reach five to twenty percent or more. The obstacle has never been a lack of blueprints; it has been a lack of political will in the capitals that matter most, above all Washington.

The history of the environmental movement offers a sliver of hope. When confronted with the problem of acid rain, the United States and its trading partners demonstrated that a cap-and-trade system could slash sulfur dioxide emissions at a fraction of the predicted cost, by harnessing market incentives rather than fighting them. The Montreal Protocol phaseout of ozone-depleting substances proved that even a more technically challenging global commons problem could be tackled when the science was clear and the alternatives were within reach. In both cases, industry initially warned of economic ruin and then discovered it could innovate its way to compliance more cheaply than anyone had forecast. The same dynamic is plausible for carbon—but only if the mandate is firm and the penalties for non-compliance are credible.

So the answer is not in the climatology texts; it is in the hands of legislators, executives, and the publics who elect them. Emanuel has done his part by laying out what is known and what is not, and by refusing to hide behind false precision. The rest of us—citizens, opinion-makers, and policymakers—must now decide whether to treat his insurance analogy as a rhetorical flourish or as a serious principle for action. We can stop the worst of global warming, but we cannot stop it without a new political alignment that treats climate stability as a core obligation of the state. Whether that alignment emerges in time is the one uncertainty no computer model can resolve.

Kerry Emanuel’s sober assessment of the state of climate science offers a much-needed antidote to the polarized, sensationalist debate that too often dominates our public discourse. By meticulously separating what is known from what is merely probable, and by acknowledging both the theoretical benefits and the devastating costs of global warming, Emanuel provides a credible foundation for public policy. The most crucial takeaway from his essay, however, is not the specific projection of a 3 to 7°F temperature rise or the terrifying physics of hurricane intensification. Rather, it is his concluding framing of climate action as an “insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises.”

As we enter 2007, awaiting the imminent release of the IPCC’s Fourth Assessment Report, this insurance paradigm is exactly how we must approach the climate crisis. For too long, skeptics have used scientific uncertainty as a shield against action. If we cannot perfectly predict cloud feedbacks, ice-sheet dynamics, or the exact timeline of sea-level rise, the argument goes, we should wait until the science is settled before imposing costs on the global economy.

Emanuel brilliantly turns this logic on its head. In the realm of risk management, uncertainty regarding catastrophic outcomes does not justify inaction; it demands robust precaution. As Emanuel notes, the climate system is rife with non-linear dynamics. Paleoclimatology, particularly the ice-core record, reveals a climate that does not always shift smoothly but can suddenly jump from one state to another. We are currently forcing the climate system at a rate unprecedented in human history, flying blind into a future of potentially massive positive feedbacks. We buy fire insurance for our homes not because we are certain they will burn down, but because the cost of losing the home is unacceptably high. When the "home" is the stable Holocene climate that has allowed human civilization to flourish, the necessity of a premium becomes undeniable.

What Emanuel’s essay leaves open—and what policymakers must urgently address—is how to calculate and pay this premium. Fortunately, the economic complement to Emanuel’s physical science has recently arrived in the form of the Stern Review on the Economics of Climate Change, published just a few months ago by the British government. Sir Nicholas Stern’s exhaustive analysis quantifies the very risks Emanuel describes. When we account for the agricultural devastation caused by the "twin perils" of flood and drought, the destruction of coastal infrastructure from sea-level rise, and the staggering economic toll of intensified tropical cyclones like Hurricane Katrina, the costs of unchecked climate change are astronomical. The Stern Review estimates that failing to act could cost the equivalent of 5 to 20 percent of global gross domestic product each year, now and forever.

In contrast, the cost of the insurance policy—transitioning the global economy to low-carbon energy sources—is estimated at just 1 percent of global GDP per year. Economically speaking, it is one of the most favorable cost-benefit analyses ever presented to humanity. Furthermore, as Emanuel points out, smaller natural climate swings in the past have debilitated or destroyed entire civilizations in Mesopotamia and the Americas. Our modern, heavily populated, and deeply interconnected global economy, with its highly optimized supply chains and fragile agricultural belts, may be even more vulnerable to sudden shifts in the hydrological cycle. Paying a 1 percent premium to prevent profound global destabilization is not environmental radicalism; it is fiscal prudence.

The challenge, of course, lies in the political execution of this transition. To halt the exponential rise in atmospheric water vapor and the attendant extreme weather Emanuel describes, we must radically reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The current international framework, the Kyoto Protocol, is fundamentally inadequate for this task. By exempting rapidly developing nations, Kyoto ignores the demographic and economic realities of the 21st century. China is currently on the verge of surpassing the United States as the world's largest emitter of carbon dioxide, fueled by a massive expansion of coal-fired power plants. India's energy demands are similarly surging. Any insurance policy that does not cover the entire globe will ultimately fail to prevent the worst consequences of warming.

Therefore, the United States must abandon its posture of defensive isolationism regarding climate policy. We must lead the forging of a post-Kyoto consensus that includes binding commitments from both the industrialized and developing worlds. To achieve this without stifling the economic growth necessary to lift billions out of poverty, we cannot rely solely on command-and-control regulations. We must harness the power of the market.

Implementing a global price on carbon—whether through a revenue-neutral carbon tax or a carefully structured cap-and-trade system—is the most efficient way to pay our insurance premium. A predictable price on carbon emissions would internalize the costs of the floods, droughts, and intensified hurricanes that Emanuel outlines. More importantly, it would send a massive price signal through the global economy, incentivizing the private sector to aggressively develop and deploy clean technologies. We need a renaissance in wind and solar power, next-generation nuclear energy, and carbon capture and sequestration. Furthermore, increasing energy efficiency will not only reduce carbon emissions but will also alleviate our dangerous geopolitical reliance on unstable fossil-fuel-producing regions.

Kerry Emanuel has done us a great service by laying out the physical realities of global warming with unflinching clarity. He has made it clear that while there may be some localized, short-term benefits to a warming world, the net threat to global stability, coastal geography, and human life is profound. The era of debating the fundamental physics of the greenhouse effect is over. As we look ahead to the rest of 2007 and beyond, the debate must shift entirely to the mechanics of our response. We possess the technological potential and the economic rationale to stabilize the climate. All that remains to be seen is whether we have the political maturity to sign the insurance policy before the disaster strikes.

Kerry Emanuel’s most important point is not that every place will be harmed equally by global warming, or that every scientific uncertainty has been eliminated. It is that climate change is a problem of risk management under deep uncertainty. That framing should move the public discussion beyond the sterile question of whether climate change is “proved” in some courtroom sense. We routinely spend money to insure houses against fires that are unlikely, maintain armies against threats that may not materialize, and regulate medicines and bridges before catastrophe supplies perfect evidence. The atmosphere deserves the same prudence.

The common rejoinder—that warming of a few degrees sounds mild—confuses averages with consequences. A small shift in average temperature can mean large shifts in extremes, water availability, crop reliability, disease ranges, and coastal exposure. Emanuel is right to emphasize that the danger lies not only in gradual warming but in the distribution of effects: more heat waves, stronger downpours, longer droughts, rising seas, stressed ecosystems, and possibly abrupt changes we do not yet understand. The question is not whether humanity can survive. Humanity survived ice ages, plagues, and wars. The question is how much avoidable suffering, displacement, ecological loss, and geopolitical stress we are willing to impose, especially on people least responsible for the emissions.

That ethical point deserves more emphasis. The “winners and losers” language can sound balanced, but the ledger is not morally neutral. If Canada or Russia gains a longer growing season while Bangladesh loses land to the sea, or if wealthy Americans buy air conditioners while poor farmers endure failed monsoons, we cannot simply add gains and losses as if they fell on the same households. Climate change is regressive across both geography and time. The industrial countries have placed most of the long-lived carbon dioxide in the air; the greatest vulnerability lies in poorer countries with less capital for irrigation, sea walls, public health, and disaster relief. Future generations, who have no vote in current energy policy, inherit the consequences of decisions made for our convenience.

Still, recognizing the danger is not the same as proving that “we can stop global warming,” if by “stop” we mean halt it quickly. We cannot. The climate system has inertia, oceans store heat, and carbon dioxide remains in the atmosphere for a long time. Some additional warming is already built into the system. The relevant goal is to prevent the most dangerous warming, slow the rate of change, and leave ourselves options. That requires reducing the flow of greenhouse gases toward the atmosphere and eventually moving the world economy toward very low net emissions. It also requires adaptation, because mitigation alone will not protect those already exposed to drought, flood, storm surge, and heat.

The encouraging news is that the first decades of reductions do not require waiting for miraculous inventions. Work by Robert Socolow and Stephen Pacala has popularized the idea of “stabilization wedges”: large but comprehensible blocks of action that, combined, could hold emissions on a safer path. Greater efficiency in buildings, vehicles, appliances, and industry is often the cheapest wedge. Better insulation, efficient lighting, combined heat and power, improved motors, and tighter fuel-economy standards can reduce energy demand without reducing welfare. There are also supply-side wedges: wind power, solar power, nuclear power, carbon capture and storage for coal, reduced deforestation, improved agricultural practices, and substitution of natural gas for coal where appropriate. None is sufficient by itself; all have costs and complications. But the claim that we must choose between modern civilization and climate protection is false.

The harder problem is political design. Energy systems are built around long-lived infrastructure, powerful incumbents, and prices that do not reflect environmental damage. If emitting carbon is free, we should not be surprised that firms and consumers emit too much. A carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system would put a price on the risk we are creating and allow millions of decentralized decisions to find the cheapest reductions. Economists may disagree about the exact price, but the direction is clear: carbon should no longer be treated as an unpriced sewer. Revenues from a carbon tax could be returned through lower payroll taxes, dividends, or assistance to low-income households, reducing the burden on families while preserving the incentive to conserve and innovate.

The United States has a special responsibility. It is among the largest emitters, has contributed heavily to the accumulated stock of greenhouse gases, and possesses extraordinary technological and financial capacity. Its refusal to ratify the Kyoto Protocol has damaged international trust, even though Kyoto itself is only a modest first step and excludes binding commitments for major developing countries. A workable global agreement must eventually include China, India, Brazil, and other growing economies, but it is unrealistic and unjust to demand that poor countries accept permanent energy poverty while rich countries continue wasteful habits. The bargain should be: industrial countries cut first and fastest, help finance clean development, and support technology transfer; developing countries accept increasingly serious commitments as their incomes and emissions rise.

One reason for urgency is that delay raises costs. Power plants, highways, suburbs, and factories built today shape emissions for decades. If the world builds a new generation of conventional coal plants without capturing carbon, the emissions path becomes far harder to bend. Conversely, clear policy signals now can influence investment before infrastructure is locked in. Business leaders often say they need certainty. A predictable, gradually rising carbon price, accompanied by public support for basic energy research and demonstration projects, would provide that certainty better than sporadic subsidies and speeches.

Adaptation must not be treated as surrender. Even an aggressive mitigation program will not eliminate climate-related risks. We need better floodplain management, stronger building codes in hurricane-prone regions, improved water storage and pricing, heat emergency plans, protection of wetlands, and more resilient agriculture. Katrina showed that disaster is never purely natural: vulnerability is shaped by poverty, planning, insurance, infrastructure, and government competence. Climate policy therefore overlaps with ordinary good governance. The same levee, hospital, evacuation system, or public-health network that protects against today’s extremes will be needed even more in a warmer climate.

Emanuel’s essay is strongest when it resists false certainty. Climate science contains uncertainties, but they cut both ways. Some feedbacks may be benign; others may amplify warming or produce abrupt changes. The possibility that the climate system is less sensitive than expected is not a reason for complacency when the possibility that it is more sensitive carries such grave consequences. In ordinary life, uncertainty about whether a loaded gun will fire is a reason to handle it more carefully, not less.

Can we stop global warming? Not entirely, and not immediately. But we can stop treating the atmosphere as free waste space. We can stop building policy around the hope that all uncertainties will favor us. We can stop pretending that cheap fossil energy is cheap when its costs are shifted to coasts, farms, forests, and future generations. The task is immense, but not mysterious: price carbon, invest in clean technology, improve efficiency, protect the vulnerable, and build an international bargain worthy of the scale of the problem. The real uncertainty is not whether action is possible. It is whether we will act before necessity removes the easier choices.

Kerry Emanuel's essay offers a measured, scientifically grounded assessment of what we know about climate change, and it is precisely this measured quality that deserves scrutiny. While the essay catalogs evidence with admirable clarity, its tone—of cautious balance and qualified optimism—risks obscuring the fundamental asymmetry at the heart of the climate problem. The "winners and losers" framing, the emphasis on uncertainties, and the implicit suggestion that we might reasonably "wait and see" all reflect a perspective that, however scientifically defensible, fails to reckon adequately with what Emanuel's own evidence portends.

Consider how the essay handles the question of warming's consequences. We are told that the temperature change expected over the next century is comparable to moving from Boston to Philadelphia, and that this might bring some benefits—less heating fuel, new agricultural lands, reduced cold-related mortality. This comparison is misleading in ways that matter. Moving from Boston to Philadelphia is an individual choice, reversible, and undertaken with full knowledge of the destination. What we face collectively is not a move but a forced migration of the entire climate system, one over which no individual has agency and whose destination remains uncertain. More importantly, the Boston-to-Philadelphia analogy elides the fact that climate is not experienced in isolation; it is the context within which agriculture operates, within which water resources are distributed, within which ecosystems function. Change the context and you do not simply relocate existing patterns—you disrupt the intricate adaptations that human societies have built over centuries.

Emanuel is right to note that some regions might benefit from warming, and honest scientific analysis must acknowledge this. But the essay's framing—weighing benefits against costs as though they were roughly equivalent—obscures a crucial point: the populations most likely to experience the benefits are not the same as those most likely to suffer the harms. The regions projected to gain agricultural productivity are predominantly in high northern latitudes—Canada, Russia, Scandinavia—while the regions most at risk of drought, heat stress, and agricultural collapse are in the tropics and subtropics, where billions of poor people already live. When Emanuel asks whether "the world really suffer in the net," he is implicitly assuming that costs and benefits can be aggregated and averaged across humanity. But the distribution of impacts is not neutral. It falls disproportionately on those least equipped to adapt—the same populations that contributed least to the problem. This is not merely a technical oversight; it is a moral failure in how the question is framed.

The essay's treatment of uncertainty is similarly double-edged. Uncertainty about climate sensitivity, about ice sheet dynamics, about regional precipitation patterns—these are real, and Emanuel rightly refuses to paper over them. But there is a tendency, even among careful scientists, to treat uncertainty as a reason for caution in only one direction. We are told to be humble about our knowledge of negative feedbacks that might spare us, while the possibility of positive feedbacks that might make matters worse is acknowledged but not dwelled upon. The ice core record, with its evidence of abrupt and surprising climate transitions, is mentioned almost in passing, yet it contains a profound warning: the climate system is not a smooth, predictable machine but one capable of rapid, discontinuous change. The very uncertainty that Emanuel invokes should, if anything, make us more concerned, not less. When the stakes are civilization-scale, and when we know that the system has the capacity for surprising and potentially irreversible shifts, the rational response to uncertainty is not to wait for better information but to reduce the probability of the worst outcomes.

This point bears directly on the essay's treatment of hurricanes. Emanuel's own research has demonstrated a connection between sea surface temperatures and hurricane intensity, and the 2005 season—Katrina, Rita, Wilma—provides a devastating illustration of what increased hurricane activity means in practice. The essay mentions that Katrina may cost $200 billion and has claimed at least 1,200 lives, but it is worth dwelling on what these numbers mean. They represent not merely economic losses but human suffering, displaced communities, shattered lives. They represent the limit of what even wealthy nations can absorb. And they represent, as Emanuel acknowledges, a trend that is likely to continue. If the North Atlantic has already seen a 60 percent increase in tropical cyclone power output since the 1970s, and if this increase is linked to warming, then we are not looking at a future possibility but an ongoing transformation. The question is not whether we will face more Katrinas but how many and how bad.

The essay's final section turns to policy, citing the IPCC as a model of scientific consensus-building and noting that the next assessment report is due in February 2007. The IPCC process is valuable, but it is worth remembering what it cannot do. By design, the IPCC reports represent what the scientific community can agree upon, which means they tend toward the conservative end of the spectrum of possibilities. The observations that Emanuel cites—accelerating ice loss in Greenland, declining Arctic sea ice, record temperatures—suggest that the real world may already be tracking the upper end of the projections that the IPCC was making just a few years ago. If so, the consensus estimates are not conservative; they may be optimistic. This does not invalidate the IPCC process, but it does suggest that policymakers should not treat its projections as worst-case scenarios requiring no urgency.

Emanuel ends on a note of cautious hedging, suggesting that we might consider making sacrifices "as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises." This framing—insurance against a potentially harmful future—is telling. It treats climate action as an optional add-on, a precaution that might or might not be necessary, rather than as a response to a demonstrated and ongoing problem. But the evidence the essay itself presents suggests that the future is not merely potentially harmful but already unfolding. The record warmth of 2005 was not a model projection but a measured reality. The thinning of ice sheets is not a theoretical concern but an observed fact. The intensification of hurricanes is not a speculation but a statistical trend.

What would it mean to take this evidence seriously? It would mean recognizing that the question "can we stop global warming?" is not primarily a scientific question but a political and moral one. The science tells us what is happening and what will happen if emissions continue. The policy question is whether we have the collective will to change course. And the moral question—and this is one that Emanuel's measured analysis tends to avoid—is whether we are prepared to accept the distribution of harms that current trajectories would impose on the world's most vulnerable populations.

The essay concludes that we are "by no means certain about what kind of changes are in store." This is true but incomplete. We are uncertain about the details, but we are not uncertain about the direction. The climate is warming, human activities are the primary cause, and the consequences include rising seas, intensifying storms, and disrupted precipitation patterns. These are not speculative risks to be weighed against the costs of action; they are ongoing and accelerating changes that will impose enormous costs regardless of what we do now. The only question is whether we will pay the smaller cost of reducing emissions or the larger cost of enduring the consequences. The evidence before us in January 2007 is sufficient to answer that question. What remains is the courage to act on it.

The Wrong Insurance Policy

Kerry Emanuel ends his essay on a note that has become a kind of consensus refrain among scientifically literate commentators: even if we are unsure about the magnitude of climate damages, we ought to "make sacrifices as an insurance policy" against bad surprises. It is a reasonable-sounding sentiment, and it is the place where I want to push back hardest. Not because I doubt the basic atmospheric physics Emanuel describes—I do not—but because the insurance metaphor, applied carelessly, leads to bad policy. Insurance is something you buy when the expected payout, properly discounted, justifies the premium. We rarely insure against every conceivable contingency, and when we do, we have made ourselves poorer for no good reason.

Begin with Emanuel's own numbers. He projects warming of three to seven degrees Fahrenheit over the next century, an amount he himself compares to the climate difference between Boston and Philadelphia. He acknowledges, with admirable honesty, that some effects will be beneficial—lower heating costs, longer growing seasons at high latitudes, CO2 fertilization of crops—even as others will be harmful. He flags hurricanes (his specialty), accelerated hydrological cycling, and the possibility of ice sheet instability. What he does not do, and what climate scientists in general have been remarkably reluctant to do, is put the harms and the benefits onto a common scale so we can ask whether the proposed cure is worth its cost.

When economists have attempted that exercise—William Nordhaus most prominently, but also Richard Tol, Robert Mendelsohn, and others—the results are sobering in the opposite direction from what most readers expect. The net economic damages of a doubling of atmospheric CO2 over a century, expressed as a fraction of global GDP at the end of that century, are typically estimated at one to three percent. That is not a trivial number; it is, in absolute terms, very large. But the global economy in 2100 will, on any plausible growth path, be several times richer than today. A world that is, say, four times wealthier minus two percent is still about 3.9 times wealthier. To prevent that two-percent shortfall, we are being asked to consider transformations of the global energy system that, in the most aggressive proposals (Stern Review, released just months ago, comes to mind), would cost one to two percent of GDP indefinitely, starting now—when the world is poorer and the marginal utility of consumption higher.

This is not climate denial. It is a request that we apply to climate policy the same kind of cost-benefit reasoning we apply to highway safety, drug approvals, and pollution regulations of every other kind. The Stern Review reached its alarming conclusions only by adopting a near-zero pure rate of time preference, effectively treating the welfare of someone living in 2200 as equally weighty as someone living today. Nordhaus has shown that with conventional discount rates—the kind we actually use to evaluate, say, transportation infrastructure—the optimal carbon tax rises gradually over decades rather than spiking now. The "climate ramp" he advocates does not abandon mitigation; it sequences it sensibly.

Emanuel hints at, but does not engage with, the deepest problem with aggressive near-term mitigation: it is regressive across both time and space. Across time, because we are being asked to make people who are, on average, much poorer than their grandchildren will be, sacrifice consumption so that those richer descendants will be marginally richer still. Across space, because the marginal ton of carbon will, for the foreseeable future, be emitted in China, India, and other developing economies where energy is the precondition of escape from poverty. Tell the household in Bihar that just got its first lightbulb that the coal plant which powers it must close. The hurricane risk that worries Emanuel most—Bangladesh in 1970, Mitch in 1998—falls precisely on populations whose vulnerability is principally a function of poverty. Wealthy Floridians ride out Category 5 storms; impoverished Hondurans do not. The fastest way to reduce the human toll of hurricanes is to make poor countries less poor, which requires energy, which for the next several decades will mostly mean fossil fuels.

What about the catastrophic scenarios—the ice sheet collapse, the sudden climate jump that Emanuel rightly says we do not understand? Here I think the honest answer is that conventional cost-benefit analysis breaks down, and we have to think differently. But "differently" does not mean "spend whatever it takes." It means investing seriously in the things that hedge against the tail. Three priorities suggest themselves.

First, research and development. The most underfunded climate policy in the world today is basic energy R&D. Federal spending on energy research, in real terms, is a fraction of what it was after the oil shocks of the 1970s. The reason nuclear power, the only proven scalable low-carbon baseload technology, costs what it does is not physics; it is regulation and the absence of standardized designs. The reason solar photovoltaics cost what they do is that we have not yet pushed the learning curve far enough. A few tens of billions of dollars per year in public R&D, globally coordinated, would do more to bend the long-term emissions trajectory than any number of Kyoto-style emissions caps that wealthy countries will ratify and then quietly miss.

Second, adaptation. Sea walls, improved building codes, better hurricane forecasting (Emanuel's own field has contributed enormously here), drought-tolerant crops, and—above all—the economic growth that allows societies to redeploy resources in response to changing conditions. The Dutch have lived below sea level for centuries; Bangladesh can learn to do the same, if we let it become rich enough. Adaptation has the considerable advantage that its benefits accrue to those who pay for it, sidestepping the international free-rider problem that has bedeviled mitigation efforts since Rio.

Third, research into geoengineering. This is taboo, and I understand why. The prospect of deliberately injecting sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere, or fertilizing the oceans with iron, raises governance nightmares. But if we are genuinely worried about a sudden climate jump—the scenario Emanuel says he is "worried" about—we should want to know whether emergency interventions are possible, what their side effects would be, and how quickly they could be deployed. To rule out the research because we find the prospect distasteful is to insist on insurance while refusing to read the policy.

Emanuel's essay closes a circle that has become familiar: science is settled enough to warrant action, action means mitigation, mitigation means emissions caps, emissions caps mean Kyoto or its successor. Each step in this chain is contestable, and the science section, however solid, does not by itself justify any of them. I take the IPCC findings seriously. I take Emanuel's hurricane work seriously. What I do not take seriously is the claim that this body of evidence translates into an obvious policy prescription. A world that grows rapidly, invests heavily in energy innovation, prices carbon modestly and rising, and adapts pragmatically to the changes that come anyway will, I suspect, end the twenty-first century better off than a world that flagellates itself into stagnation now in pursuit of stabilization targets it cannot meet and may not need.

That is also, incidentally, the only kind of climate policy that has any chance of being adopted. Voters in democracies will not accept large reductions in living standards on the basis of model projections about 2100, and they should not be asked to. The countries that emit most of the future's carbon are not democracies and will not be coerced. Any climate policy that is not compatible with continued economic growth, in the developed world and—especially—in the developing world, is not a policy. It is a moral posture. We can afford a number of those, but we should not confuse them with the work of actually reducing human suffering, which is what an honest accounting of the climate problem ought finally to be about.

The Understated Catastrophe

Kerry Emanuel has done a great service by writing about climate science with the calm authority of someone who actually does the work. His essay is sober, fair-minded, and—I want to argue—too gentle by half. By the standards of public discourse, where commentators still treat the entire problem as somehow open to question, Emanuel sounds like an alarmist. By the standards of what the science actually says, and especially what it has been saying with increasing urgency over the past five years, his essay reads to me as a careful but understated brief. Three places where the understatement matters: the framing of winners and losers, the treatment of ice sheets, and the silence about what happens after 2100.

Take the winners-and-losers framing first. Emanuel writes that "there will be winners and losers, but will the world really suffer in the net?" He answers, eventually, that yes, the world will probably suffer in the net, but he gives the rhetorical question more breathing room than it deserves. The framing is misleading in two ways.

First, it treats the climate system as a redistributor of agricultural productivity and heating bills, when in fact the harms and benefits are radically asymmetric in character. A longer growing season in Manitoba does not feed the population of Bangladesh when their rice paddies salt up from rising seas. A reduction in winter heating costs in Boston does not compensate Bangladeshis for the cyclone that drowned 300,000 of their grandparents in 1970 and whose intensified successors Emanuel's own research predicts. The "winners" are mostly populations that are already wealthy, well-adapted, and politically powerful; the "losers" are overwhelmingly populations that are poor, tropical, and voiceless. Aggregating them in a global net erases the moral structure of the problem.

Second, the framing implicitly imagines a new equilibrium—a slightly warmer world to which we will adjust, much as we adjusted to the medieval warm period or the little ice age. But we are not heading toward an equilibrium. We are heading toward a transient, and the rate of change matters as much as the destination. Ecosystems can tolerate the climates of two degrees ago and two degrees from now; what they cannot easily tolerate is the journey between, especially when habitat is fragmented and migration corridors are blocked by farms and cities. The same is true of human societies. The question is not whether humans can survive a Philadelphia-like climate in what used to be Boston; we obviously can. The question is whether the institutions, infrastructure, and agriculture built for one climate can be re-engineered for another in fifty or a hundred years without enormous disruption. The historical record Emanuel himself cites—civilizations destroyed by smaller climate swings than what we are now committing to—suggests the answer is sometimes no.

Now the ice sheets, where I think Emanuel is most carefully understated. He notes that we do not understand the physics of ice under pressure well, that meltwater is reaching the bed of the Greenland ice sheet and may be lubricating its flow, and that "were the entire Greenland ice cap to melt, sea level would increase by around 22 feet." All true, all relevant. What he does not emphasize is how recently and how dramatically glaciologists' opinions have shifted. As recently as 2001, the IPCC treated the major ice sheets as essentially stable on century timescales, contributing little to twenty-first century sea level rise. The observations of the past five years—accelerating discharge from Jakobshavn, Helheim, and Kangerdlugssuaq glaciers, the apparent surface melt-driven speedup, the unexpected mass loss from West Antarctica—have undermined that view. The pace of revision is not what one would expect from a mature science converging on a stable estimate. It is what one would expect from a field discovering, late, that its models were missing essential physics, with the missing physics pointing in the direction of faster ice loss than predicted.

This is important because the difference between sixteen inches of sea level rise by 2100 (Emanuel's upper-end IPCC number) and, say, four or five feet—which is what some glaciologists are now whispering is possible if the West Antarctic ice sheet starts to come apart—is the difference between an expensive coastal engineering problem and the loss of major world cities. Miami, much of New Orleans, large parts of the Nile and Ganges-Brahmaputra deltas, the Maldives entirely, lower Manhattan, much of the Netherlands' coastal defenses overtopped. The probability of the high end is not high, but it is also not negligible, and it is precisely the kind of risk against which a rational society insures.

Then there is the silence about post-2100. The IPCC, by convention, projects to 2100, and Emanuel follows suit. But 2100 is not the end of history. The CO2 we put in the atmosphere this century will mostly still be there in 2200 and substantially still be there in 2500. If we are on a trajectory toward 700 or 800 ppm by 2100 (a perfectly plausible business-as-usual outcome given current growth in Chinese and Indian coal use), we will not stop at 800; we will keep going. The equilibrium warming associated with such concentrations, once the oceans finish absorbing heat over the following centuries, is in the range of 5 to 10°C above pre-industrial. That is not a Boston-to-Philadelphia shift. That is a Boston-to-Atlanta shift, with the corresponding shifts everywhere else, plus the elimination over millennia of essentially all the world's terrestrial ice. The transient response by 2100 understates the commitment we are making.

I do not raise these points to score rhetorical points against Emanuel, who has been a model of scientific responsibility in a debate disfigured by bad actors. I raise them because the public conversation continues to treat climate change as a problem on roughly the same scale as, say, air pollution or ozone depletion—serious, manageable, requiring reasonable policy adjustments. The honest assessment is that the upper portion of the projection range, which we cannot rule out, describes a planetary transformation without precedent in the history of civilization. We have very few tools for thinking about events of that magnitude. Cost-benefit analysis, as conventionally practiced, breaks down because the discount rates that make sense for thirty-year decisions produce absurdities when applied across centuries. Insurance metaphors break down because there is no underwriter for a transformed planet.

What follows? A few things. First, that the locus of policy debate should shift from "is the problem real?" (settled) and "is some action warranted?" (yes, obviously) to "what stabilization target are we actually trying to hit, and what does the energy system have to look like in 2050 for us to hit it?" The implied transformations are enormous: a global power sector that is essentially decarbonized by mid-century, an industrial sector following close behind, transportation electrified or running on synthetic fuels. We are not on that trajectory and nothing currently proposed gets us there.

Second, that adaptation, while necessary, is not a substitute for mitigation; it is a complement. The argument that we should adapt rather than mitigate makes sense only if the warming we adapt to is bounded. If we mitigate enough to bound the warming at two or three degrees, adaptation may be feasible. If we do not, adaptation eventually becomes flight. There is no Dutch engineering solution to a six-meter sea level rise sustained over centuries.

Third, that scientists like Emanuel need to keep talking, including in venues like this one, because the alternative is to cede the public conversation to the polished obfuscations of the contrarian think tanks. The signal achievement of the last several years has been the gradual conversion of the major scientific institutions—the National Academies, the Royal Society, the American Geophysical Union, even (belatedly) the American Association of Petroleum Geologists—into more or less unified voices saying that the science is in. That signal needs to keep getting through.

Emanuel has done his part with grace. My quarrel is only that the picture, accurately drawn, is darker than even his careful prose conveys.

After the Science, the Politics

Kerry Emanuel's essay is a clear summary of where the climate science stands at the beginning of 2007. I have nothing to add to his account of the physics and not much to subtract from it either; the bullet points in the second half of his piece could serve as a primer for any educated citizen who has not been paying attention. But here is the trouble: educated citizens who have not been paying attention are not the audience that matters anymore. The science is well enough established that the obstacles to action are now almost entirely political, institutional, and economic. And on those questions, Emanuel—like most working scientists who venture into the public arena—has little to say. I want to spend my space saying something about them.

The political situation at the start of 2007 is more promising than it has been in fifteen years, and more frustrating than it should be. Promising, because the Democratic recapture of Congress in November means that the Senate, in particular, is now controlled by people who take the science seriously. Barbara Boxer replaces James Inhofe as chair of the Environment and Public Works Committee; the difference between a chair who calls global warming "the greatest hoax ever perpetrated on the American people" and one who has cosponsored serious mitigation legislation is hard to overstate. Several Republican senators—McCain prominent among them—have signaled willingness to work on a cap-and-trade scheme. State-level activity has been impressive: California's AB 32, passed last year, commits the state to reducing emissions to 1990 levels by 2020 and is the most ambitious sub-national mitigation policy in the world. The Regional Greenhouse Gas Initiative in the Northeast is moving toward a regional cap-and-trade system for power-sector emissions. The Supreme Court is currently considering Massachusetts v. EPA, which could force the agency to regulate greenhouse gases under the Clean Air Act.

Frustrating, because the federal executive branch remains hostile, the international framework is in tatters, and the policy mechanisms most likely to be adopted are not necessarily the ones most likely to work.

Let me take these in turn. The Bush administration has spent six years denying, then minimizing, then quietly accepting the basic science while opposing every binding mitigation framework on the grounds that it would harm the American economy and exempt the major developing emitters. This is not a position from which retreat is easy; there is no graceful path from "the science is uncertain" to "we need an aggressive carbon policy" that does not require admitting prior error, which this administration does not do. We will get no federal action of consequence before January 2009 at the earliest.

The international framework is more troubling. Kyoto's first commitment period runs through 2012, and it is increasingly clear that most signatories will not meet their targets. The countries that have made the most progress—Britain, Germany—did so largely for reasons unrelated to climate policy (the dash to gas, reunification). Spain, Italy, Canada are far over target with no realistic plan to come down. Meanwhile, China is now adding roughly two large coal-fired power plants per week and will surpass the United States as the world's largest CO2 emitter, probably this year or next. India is on a similar though delayed trajectory. The Kyoto framework, which exempts developing countries from binding caps, cannot address these emissions even in principle, and any framework that tries to impose caps on China and India will not be ratified by them. We need a new architecture, and the negotiations for a post-Kyoto agreement have barely begun.

The mechanism question is the one I most want climate-concerned readers to think about. The political momentum in the United States, to the extent there is any, is behind cap-and-trade systems modeled on the successful SO2 program of the 1990 Clean Air Act amendments. Cap-and-trade has the political virtue of looking like a regulation rather than a tax, which matters in a political culture allergic to anything that can be labeled a tax. It has the additional virtue, from the standpoint of incumbent industries, that the allowances can be given away rather than auctioned, providing an enormous transfer of wealth from consumers to polluters. This is, perversely, a feature, not a bug, of cap-and-trade's political viability: it buys off the most powerful opponents of action.

But cap-and-trade has serious weaknesses. It produces an uncertain price, which is a problem for investors deciding whether to build a nuclear plant or a coal plant. The European Emissions Trading System, in its first phase, allocated too many permits and watched the carbon price collapse, demonstrating the kind of regulatory failure to which the mechanism is prone. It invites endless gaming, offsets fraud, and lobbying over allocation. It scales awkwardly across borders.

A straight carbon tax is, by almost every measure that academic economists care about, a superior instrument. It produces a predictable price that businesses can plan around. It raises revenue that can be used to cut other distorting taxes, producing a "double dividend." It is administratively simple, especially if imposed upstream on a few hundred fossil fuel producers and importers. It cannot be gamed by manipulation of allocation rules because there are none. The principal objection is political: it has the word "tax" in it. This is a serious objection in American politics but it is not a reason that an honest essayist should pretend to find compelling. A carbon tax, set at a modest initial level (say, $20 per ton of CO2, equivalent to about 18 cents per gallon of gasoline) and rising on a pre-announced schedule, is the policy that would do the most good with the least economic disruption. We should be advocating for it openly rather than chasing the politically convenient but economically inferior alternative.

What about the developing world? Here I think we need a kind of intellectual honesty that the international negotiations have so far not produced. China and India are not going to accept binding emissions caps that constrain their growth, and we are not in a strong position to demand that they do, given that essentially all of the carbon currently in the atmosphere was put there by us. The path forward is some combination of: (a) developed-country mitigation deep enough to demonstrate that we are serious; (b) substantial transfers of clean energy technology to the developing world, possibly through a reformed Clean Development Mechanism; (c) eventual developing-country commitments tied to per-capita rather than absolute emissions; and (d) a credible commitment device—possibly a border carbon adjustment—that prevents the worst free-riding. None of this is easy. All of it will require a generation of patient diplomacy. We have not yet begun.

Finally, technology. Emanuel does not say much about it, but the climate problem is fundamentally an energy technology problem. We need to decarbonize the global power sector by mid-century, which means some combination of: large expansions of nuclear (probably with new reactor designs—the AP1000, perhaps eventually fast reactors or thorium); aggressive deployment of wind, which is now competitive in good sites; continued cost reductions in solar PV, which is not yet competitive but is approaching it on a learning curve that has been quite steady; carbon capture and sequestration, which is unproven at scale and faces severe storage challenges but may be necessary if we are to keep using coal at all; and substantial efficiency improvements throughout the economy, which are the cheapest abatement available but which face notorious market failures in their adoption. The R&D budgets to make all of this happen are currently grossly inadequate. The energy R&D budget of the U.S. government, in inflation-adjusted dollars, is less than half what it was in 1980. This is the single most fixable failure in current climate policy.

Emanuel ends his essay with the insurance metaphor. I want to end mine with a slightly different one: triage. We are not going to do everything that the science says we should do, because we never do. The question is what we should do first, and the answer, in roughly this order, is: price carbon (preferably with a tax), fund energy R&D at war-effort levels, and begin the long diplomatic process of bringing China and India into a workable global framework. Everything else is commentary.

What We Owe, and to Whom

I want to set aside, briefly, the question of what climate science tells us and consider a question that climate science cannot answer but which it makes urgent: what do we, the living, owe to people who are not yet born?

Kerry Emanuel's essay closes with the suggestion that we might want to "make sacrifices as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises." That formulation is sensible and modest, and I do not want to quarrel with it. But it papers over a deep ethical problem that lies at the heart of every climate policy debate, and that the technical discussions of discount rates, abatement curves, and stabilization targets cannot resolve without taking a position on it. The problem is this: nearly all the harms and benefits of climate policy fall on people who do not yet exist. They cannot vote, lobby, sue, or bargain. They cannot threaten us, persuade us, or compensate us. They have no leverage of any kind. Our policy toward them is, in the strictest sense, charity—or, if we choose, indifference. What grounds our obligation to them, and how stringent is it?

The economist's standard answer, encoded in the discount rates that drive every integrated assessment model, is that future welfare should be discounted at some positive rate, often three to five percent per year. The justification is partly empirical—people behave as though they discount the future—and partly normative—future generations will, on plausible growth assumptions, be much richer than we are, so a dollar's worth of consumption matters less to them. The mathematical consequence is dramatic. At a five-percent discount rate, a catastrophe costing one trillion dollars two hundred years from now has a present value of about sixty million dollars. Catastrophes far enough away, or far enough in the future, simply do not register.

This procedure has been challenged from two directions, and the challenges are worth taking seriously.

The first challenge, associated most prominently with the philosopher Derek Parfit and recently revived by Nicholas Stern in his review for the British government, is that pure time preference—discounting the future simply because it is the future—is ethically indefensible. We do not think that the suffering of people far from us in space matters less than the suffering of people near us; why should the suffering of people far from us in time matter less? The intuition that it does matter less is, on this view, a parochial bias that careful reflection should overcome. Stern, accordingly, adopts a pure time preference of 0.1 percent per year—essentially zero, with a small adjustment for the probability of human extinction—and from this assumption follows much of his report's alarming conclusions about the costs of inaction.

The second challenge concerns the empirical claim that future generations will be much richer. They probably will be, in conventional GDP terms, if the global economy continues to grow at twentieth-century rates. But conventional GDP is a poor measure of welfare when the underlying physical and biological substrate of human life is being altered. A world that is twice as rich in marketed goods but that has lost the Greenland ice sheet, the Amazon rainforest, and most of its coral reefs is not unambiguously a better world. The growth-based justification for discounting assumes a kind of substitutability between natural capital and produced capital that may not hold in the limit. If there are some environmental goods whose loss cannot be compensated by any quantity of additional television sets, then the appropriate discount rate for the consumption-substitutable portion of welfare is not the appropriate rate for the irreplaceable portion.

I find both challenges persuasive, though not in their strongest forms. Pure time preference at five percent seems too high to me; pure time preference at zero seems too low, mostly because zero implies obligations to indefinitely distant generations that quickly swamp our obligations to anyone now living. (At a zero discount rate, almost any current sacrifice is justified if it produces any expected benefit, however small, for any sufficiently large set of future people. This is the climate version of Pascal's mugging.) Somewhere in between lies a defensible position, but I do not pretend to know exactly where.

What I do think, however, is that the choice of discount rate—and more broadly the choice of how to value future welfare against present welfare—is a moral choice, not a technical one, and it should be made democratically and transparently rather than buried in the parameter assumptions of integrated assessment models. The policy debate has so far been conducted as though the rate were a matter for experts to determine. It is not. It is a matter on which everyone has a stake and an opinion, and one which a healthy democracy should debate openly.

Set alongside the question of intergenerational equity is the question of contemporaneous equity. Emanuel mentions, almost in passing, that hurricanes have killed 300,000 people in Bangladesh and 10,000 in Central America. He does not say, because he does not need to, that the number of people killed by hurricanes in Boston in living memory is approximately zero. The harms of climate change, and the harms of any climate response, are distributed wildly unequally across humans alive today. The countries that emitted the carbon are not, in general, the countries that will bear the worst consequences. The carbon emitted by Americans heating our homes contributes, marginally but reliably, to the storm surges that drown Bangladeshi farmers. This is not a metaphor; it is the basic physics of the atmosphere. The relationship between American consumption and Bangladeshi mortality is causal, traceable, and—I would argue—morally weighty in a way that requires response.

What response? The international negotiations, such as they are, have circled this question without answering it. The notion of "common but differentiated responsibilities," enshrined in the UN Framework Convention, gestures at the problem. The proposal that developed countries take on emissions caps first, with developing countries joining later, follows from it. So does the idea of technology transfer and adaptation funding. So, I think, does a more radical idea that has not entered the mainstream debate: that the developed countries have, simply, a debt to pay to the populations that have been and will be harmed by emissions to which they did not consent and from which they did not benefit. This debt is not metaphorical. It is the kind of debt that, in any other context, we would expect a tort system to recognize.

I do not have a worked-out theory of how this debt should be paid—through reparations, through unconditional adaptation funding, through accepting climate refugees, through deep technology transfers, or through some combination of these. I am sure that the answer is not "we will think about it after we have figured out a domestic cap-and-trade system." The climate problem is global in origin and global in incidence; any response that does not address the gross inequalities it implicates is morally incomplete.

Two final thoughts. First, on uncertainty. Emanuel emphasizes how much we do not know about the climate system, and his honesty is admirable. The standard economic response to uncertainty is to take expected values and proceed. But there is an older tradition in moral philosophy—going back at least to Pascal, and revived in recent decades under the heading of the precautionary principle—that holds that catastrophic and irreversible outcomes deserve special weight, beyond what expected-value reasoning would give them. The asymmetry is intuitive: if I overestimate climate sensitivity and we spend too much on mitigation, my descendants are slightly poorer than they might have been. If I underestimate it and we spend too little, my descendants may inherit a transformed planet. The errors are not symmetric, and our policy should not treat them as though they were.

Second, on the question of what kind of beings we are. Climate change is the first problem in human history that requires the species, as a whole, to constrain its behavior in light of consequences that will materialize over centuries. Flexible policies that can tighten or loosen as evidence accumulates are preferable to sweeping commitments that become either obsolete or politically unbearable.

The more useful questions are: How much warming can we tolerate? How can we adapt to the changes that are coming? And how can we internalize the costs of carbon emissions in a way that harnesses rather than suppresses human ingenuity? These are the questions that policy should address, and they point toward carbon pricing and technological investment rather than the panicked overreaction that seems to grip so many commentators on this issue.

The history of environmental concern is littered with predictions of catastrophe that did not materialize. Paul Ehrlich's population bomb, the coming ice age of the 1970s, the ozone hole as an existential threat—each generated headlines and policy responses that now seem overblown. I am not suggesting climate change is a hoax or that we should ignore the evidence. But I am suggesting that a measured response based on cost-benefit analysis serves us better than alarm. We have time to adapt, and we have the tools to do so. Let us use them wisely.

## The Complacency of Incrementalism

Kerry Emanuel has written a careful and technically rigorous essay, and I do not dispute the science he presents. But I am struck by how thoroughly his framing domesticates what should be a crisis. The lead essay asks "Can We Stop Global Warming?" and then proceeds to list a series of consequences—sea level rise, hurricane intensification, drought, flood—that would have been considered catastrophic if predicted for any previous generation. Yet the author seems almost blase about them. "Winners and losers," he says. "Will the world really suffer in the net?" This is not the response I would expect from someone who had just documented the thinning of polar ice, the increase in hurricane power, and the risk of civilizational collapse.

Let us be clear about what the science actually says, stripped of false equivalence and academic hedging. The Greenland ice sheet is not merely thinning at the margins; it is being lubricated by meltwater that accelerates its slide into the sea. The Antarctic ice sheet is losing mass overall. The 2005 hurricane season did not merely set records; it produced the most intense Atlantic storm ever measured. The global mean temperature is now higher than at any point in at least the past millennium. These are not projections or forecasts; they are observations of processes already underway.

The lead essay reassures us that the magnitude of projected change is "similar to the temperature change one could experience by moving from Boston to Philadelphia." This analogy is insidious. Moving to Philadelphia does not melt ice sheets, intensify hurricanes, or cause the collapse of agricultural systems. The analogy obscures the fact that we are not talking about a simple temperature shift but about a forcing of the entire climate system—the patterns of rainfall, storm tracks, and ocean circulation that support billions of people. A world five degrees warmer than today would be fundamentally different from today's world, not merely relocated.

Emanuel notes that "the earth and indeed human beings survived" past ice ages and interglacial periods. This is true but misleading. Human beings did not survive ice ages in anything like our current numbers or with our current technology. Homo sapiens existed as small bands of hunter-gatherers for most of our species' history, before the development of agriculture allowed population to expand. The civilizations that flourished and failed in response to climate shifts—the Mayans, the Mesopotamians, the Anasazi—did not have nuclear weapons, internet communications, or global supply chains. They did not have four billion people to feed. The question is not whether humanity could survive climate change; it is whether our civilization as we know it could survive it.

The IPCC projections that Emanuel cites are, if anything, optimistic. They assume relatively smooth climate change without triggering the feedbacks that ice cores reveal have occurred in the past. They do not adequately account for the possibility of rapid sea level rise from ice sheet instability. They underestimate the non-linearities in the climate system that have produced sudden jumps in the geological record. The "consensus" estimates, as the history of climate science shows repeatedly, have consistently underestimated the pace of change.

Consider what happened in 2005. The warmest year in the instrumental record produced the most destructive hurricane season in 150 years. Katrina alone caused over a thousand deaths and two hundred billion dollars in damage. If this is what two degrees of warming looks like, what will four or six degrees look like? The IPCC's own scenarios project that hurricane intensities will continue to increase. The economic and human costs of these storms will grow exponentially as coastal development continues and ocean temperatures rise.

I am also concerned about the distributional effects of climate change, which Emanuel's essay barely mentions. The winners and losers he mentions are not equally distributed. The countries and peoples who have contributed least to carbon emissions—the poorest nations, the most vulnerable populations—will suffer the most. The Sahel will become even more arid; Bangladesh will face greater flooding; Pacific island nations may disappear entirely. Wealthy countries like the United States have resources to adapt; poor countries do not. This is not merely a scientific question; it is a moral one.

The lead essay suggests that we might make sacrifices "as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises." This is an understatement of historic proportions. We are not buying insurance against an uncertain future; we are watching a catastrophe unfold in slow motion and debating whether to take precautions. The insurance metaphor implies that we could continue with business as usual while paying a modest premium. The reality is that the premium is rising with every year of inaction, and the coverage is shrinking.

What would appropriate action look like? At minimum, it would include mandatory caps on carbon emissions, with declining caps over time. It would include aggressive investment in renewable energy, energy efficiency, and carbon capture technologies. It would include adaptation assistance for vulnerable nations. And it would include a fundamental rethinking of how we organize our economy—one that does not treat the atmosphere as a free dump for carbon waste.

Emanuel is right that the IPCC represents a valuable scientific consensus-building process. But consensus-building is not the same as action. The IPCC has been producing increasingly alarming reports for two decades, and emissions have continued to rise. What we need now is not better science but the political will to act on the science we already have. The question "Can We Stop Global Warming?" is not primarily a scientific question; it is a question about power, interests, and values. And on that front, the news is not good.

## Beyond the Science: What Policy Makers Should Learn

Kerry Emanuel has provided a clear and accurate summary of climate science as it stood in early 2007. The facts he presents are not in dispute: CO2 concentrations are rising, temperatures are increasing, ice is melting, sea level is rising, and extreme weather events are likely to become more common. What is less clear from his essay is what follows from these facts—what actions policy makers should take and how they should think about the choices before them. That is the gap I want to address.

The first thing to recognize is that the climate change problem is fundamentally an economics problem. The atmosphere is a common resource, and using it to absorb carbon is an externality—a cost imposed on others that is not reflected in the market price of carbon-intensive goods. Left to their own devices, individuals and firms will emit too much carbon because they bear only a fraction of the costs. The solution is to put a price on carbon that reflects the full social cost of emissions. This can be done through a carbon tax or through a cap-and-trade system. Both approaches have advocates and critics, but both internalize the externality in a way that market forces can then respond to.

A carbon price is not sufficient by itself, but it is necessary. Without a price signal, there is no incentive for businesses to invest in lower-carbon technologies, for consumers to choose efficiency, or for entrepreneurs to develop new solutions. We have seen this played out in the electricity sector: when natural gas prices fell in the 1990s, utilities switched from coal because it was economically rational to do so. Market forces responded to price signals. The same would happen with carbon if the price were right.

The challenge, of course, is political. A carbon tax is regressive—it hits the poor harder than the rich because lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on energy and transportation. This political economy problem has blocked action for decades. The solution is to return the revenue from a carbon tax as a per-capita dividend, ensuring that low-income households come out ahead. This approach—popularized by economists like James Hansen and Greg Mankiw—would reduce emissions while protecting vulnerable households. It would also build political support for carbon pricing by making it feel like a dividend rather than a tax.

Beyond carbon pricing, government has a role to play in funding basic research and development. The private sector underinvests in energy research because the benefits of successful innovation are widely shared and cannot be fully captured by the innovator. We would not expect the market to develop adequate levels of research on nuclear fusion, advanced solar cells, or carbon capture technologies without public support. The Department of Energy's research programs, the national laboratories, and university research all play essential roles in developing the technologies that will power our future economy.

I am sympathetic to those who argue that international agreements are essential—that climate change is a global problem requiring global solutions. But international negotiations have been slow and will remain so as long as major emitters like the United States and China have incentives to free-ride on others' efforts. The better approach is to lead by example: adopt carbon pricing at home, demonstrate that it is compatible with economic growth, and negotiate agreements from a position of strength rather than hypocrisy. The United States cannot credibly demand sacrifices from developing nations while refusing to take action itself.

Emanuel's essay mentions that media coverage of climate change has been obsessed with sensational debate. I would extend this critique to much of the policy debate as well. We have spent too much time arguing about whether climate change is real (it is) and whether it is caused by humans (it is) and not enough time developing the mechanisms that will actually reduce emissions. The science is largely settled; the policy is not. This is where scholars and commentators should focus their efforts.

One area that deserves more attention is the potential for technological breakthroughs. The same forces that produced the exponential improvement in computing power—the combination of venture capital, public markets, and government research—could work in energy technology. The venture capital industry has already begun to invest in clean energy startups. Regulatory frameworks that allow these investments to flourish will be crucial. We need to reform permitting for transmission lines and nuclear plants, streamline approval processes for new technologies, and create markets for clean energy that do not distort the price signals.

Finally, we should not forget adaptation. Even if we stopped emitting carbon today—which we will not—the atmosphere will continue to warm for decades due to the long lifetime of CO2 already emitted. We need to invest in resilience: seawalls and flood control infrastructure, drought-resistant crops and irrigation systems, early warning systems for extreme weather. Adaptation does not excuse inaction on mitigation, but it is a necessary complement to it.

The science tells us what is happening. Now we need the policies to address it. A carbon price, public investment in research, regulatory reform, and adaptation funding—this is an ambitious but achievable agenda. What we lack is not knowledge but political will.

## Questioning the Frame: Can We, or Should We?

The title of Kerry Emanuel's essay poses a question: "Can We Stop Global Warming?" But before we can answer whether we can stop it, we must ask whether the question itself is coherent. What exactly would "stopping" global warming mean? And is this the right goal to be pursuing?

One interpretation of "stopping" would be returning the climate to some pre-industrial baseline—bringing CO2 concentrations back down to 280 parts per million, restoring ice sheets, halting sea level rise. This is clearly impossible in any practical sense. The carbon we have already emitted will remain in the atmosphere for centuries; the warming already locked in by past emissions will play out over decades. We cannot un-burn the fossil fuels that powered the industrial revolution.

A more modest interpretation would be stabilizing climate at some level warmer than today but below the worst-case projections. Even this is enormously ambitious. Global carbon emissions have been growing at roughly 3 percent annually over the past decade. A stabilization scenario would require not merely reducing emissions but changing the trajectory entirely—turning a rising curve into a declining one. To what level? By when? These questions have no easy answers.

Perhaps the more useful question is whether we should stop global warming at all. Emanuel's essay presents the case for concern, but it also notes that there will be winners as well as losers—that some regions will benefit from warmer temperatures and that some adaptation is possible. If warming is modest—say, two or three degrees—might the net effects actually be positive for many people, at least in the near term? This is not a comfortable question to ask, but it is one that policy makers must grapple with.

Consider the geography of climate impacts. High northern latitudes—Siberia, Canada, Scandinavia, Alaska—will warm most dramatically and may see significant agricultural benefits. These are also regions with small populations and abundant resources. The tropical and subtropical regions that will warm least in absolute terms but most relative to their current climate are among the most densely populated on Earth. The asymmetry of impacts is not random; it reflects the fact that wealthy countries are generally located at higher latitudes and have more resources to adapt, while poor countries are generally located at lower latitudes and have fewer resources. This has implications for how we think about both mitigation and adaptation.

I do not raise these points to argue that climate change is nothing to worry about. The risks are real, and the ice cores, hurricane records, and sea level measurements Emanuel cites are genuinely alarming. But we do ourselves a disservice by framing the debate in terms that obscure rather than illuminate the choices before us. "Stopping" climate change is not a policy goal; it is a fantasy. Preventing dangerous climate change is a policy goal, and it requires us to think carefully about what "dangerous" means and for whom.

One thing that strikes me about the climate debate is how confident everyone seems to be about very uncertain things. The IPCC provides probability ranges for temperature rise, but these ranges reflect model uncertainties, not true probabilistic knowledge. We do not know how the climate will respond to doubled or tripled CO2; we have theories and models that give us estimates, but these have been revised repeatedly and will be revised again. Emanuel himself acknowledges that "our understanding of the physics of ice under pressure is poor." If we cannot model ice sheets accurately, how can we project sea level rise a century hence?

This uncertainty should temper our confidence in both predictions and prescriptions. A warming of four degrees might be manageable; it might trigger feedbacks that make it eight degrees. A carbon tax at $50 per ton might be enough to stabilize emissions; it might be far too small. We need policy that is robust to uncertainty—policy that performs reasonably well across a range of possible futures rather than assuming we know exactly which future will occur.

Risk management is the appropriate framework here. We should invest in reducing emissions because the downside risk of unmitigated climate change is severe, and we should invest in adaptation because some warming is now inevitable. We should also invest in research to reduce the fundamental uncertainties about how the climate system works. And we should be humble about our ability to predict the future and honest about what we do not know.

Emanuel concludes that we might be inclined to make sacrifices "as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises." I agree with this framing, but I would add a caveat: insurance is only valuable if the premium is proportionate to the risk. We should be wary of proposals that impose enormous costs today on the basis of speculative scenarios about the future. The appropriate level of investment in mitigation is a function of both the expected damages and the probability that they will occur. High-end scenarios should receive attention, but they should not crowd out consideration of more moderate outcomes.

The climate debate will not be resolved by better science alone. We need to think more clearly about what we are trying to achieve and why. Stopping global warming is not a realistic goal; preventing dangerous climate change is. But "dangerous" is a normative concept, and its definition involves choices about values, risk tolerance, and distributional justice that science alone cannot adjudicate. These are the real questions before us, and they deserve more serious treatment than the binary framing of "can we stop it?" suggests.
