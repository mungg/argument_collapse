What Standards Must Be Met for the U.S. to Kill an American Citizen?

A Justice Department white paper, obtained by NBC News, states that it is lawful to kill a United States citizen if “an informed, high-level” government official decides that the target is a ranking Al Qaeda figure who poses “an imminent threat of violent attack against the United States.”

What standards must be met for the government to assassinate an American citizen? And does this deprive U.S. citizens of their constitutionally protected due process rights?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The leaked white paper's framework is alarming not because targeted killing of Americans must always be illegal, but because the standards it articulates collapse under the slightest pressure. Consider the three pillars: an "informed, high-level official," an "imminent" threat, and infeasibility of capture. Each term has been emptied of its ordinary meaning.

"Imminent," in the paper, does not require evidence of a specific plot or near-term attack. A senior operative who is "continually planning" suffices. This inverts the word's plain sense and the international-law tradition from which it borrows. "Infeasibility of capture" similarly does heavy lifting without definition, and "high-level official" replaces what the Fifth Amendment plainly contemplates: a neutral arbiter.

Due process is not synonymous with judicial process, as the Hamdi plurality reminded us, but Hamdi still required some adversarial mechanism before the government deprives a citizen of liberty—let alone life. The administration's position that the executive may unilaterally determine all relevant facts, in secret, and act on them without subsequent review, is not a balancing of process; it is its elimination.

A defensible standard would require: (1) clear evidence, reviewable by a court before or promptly after the fact, that the target is an operational leader of a group with which we are in armed conflict; (2) a genuine, particularized threat; (3) demonstrated infeasibility of capture; and (4) a FISA-like tribunal to test these claims. Anwar al-Awlaki may well have met such a standard. The point is that no one outside the executive was ever permitted to ask.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The leaked Justice Department white paper lays bare a disturbingly low bar for the targeted killing of American citizens. Under this framework, an “informed, high-level” official—likely a cabinet secretary or someone of similar rank—can secretly designate a U.S. citizen for death based on a finding that the person is a senior Al Qaeda operative posing an “imminent threat” of violent attack. This standard, entirely internal to the executive branch, substitutes bureaucratic instinct for the bedrock constitutional guarantee of due process.

The most glaring deficiency is the elastic definition of “imminent.” In traditional self-defense or law enforcement contexts, imminence means an attack is just about to happen—a ticking time bomb scenario. But in the counterterrorism realm, the government has stretched the term to encompass those who are merely suspected of ongoing plotting, perhaps months or years from fruition, based on intelligence that the target can never see or challenge. This guts the concept of any real constraint. If a threat need not be immediate, then the executive can kill an American citizen based on fluid and often untested threat assessments, effectively making the official both prosecutor and jury.

That deprives a citizen of life without the due process the Fifth Amendment commands. The Constitution requires, at minimum, that before the government takes a life, there be some neutral determination—whether by a court or another independent body—that the individual truly poses such a grave, unavoidable danger. No such mechanism exists here. The white paper’s process is a closed loop: the same branch that gathers the intelligence, interprets it, and orders the strike. The citizen is denied notice, counsel, or any chance to contest the evidence. Even in wartime, American citizenship does not evaporate; the Supreme Court has long held that citizens are entitled to process, and drone strikes far from conventional battlefields only magnify the risk of erroneous execution. Without meaningful, reviewable standards overseen outside the executive, the government assumes the power to be judge, jury, and executioner over its own people, a practice alien to our constitutional order.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

The Constitution does not make citizenship a battlefield immunity card. An American who joins an enemy force and takes an operational role in attacks against the United States may be a lawful military target, just as a citizen fighting in a conventional enemy army would be. But citizenship does mean the executive cannot be the sole author, interpreter and judge of the rules for killing him.

At minimum, several standards should be required. First, the government must show that the citizen is not merely a propagandist, sympathizer or former associate, but a senior operational member of Al Qaeda or an associated force covered by the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force. Second, the threat must be truly imminent — not “someday dangerous,” but tied to ongoing planning or command responsibility for attacks where waiting would materially increase the danger. The white paper’s apparent dilution of imminence is the most troubling feature of the policy.

Third, capture must be infeasible, not merely inconvenient or politically awkward. The government should have to explain why arrest, extradition, cooperation with local authorities or other less lethal means cannot reasonably work. Fourth, the operation must comply with the laws of war: necessity, distinction, proportionality and a reasonable effort to avoid civilian casualties.

The hard question is process. A full criminal trial is not always possible before a strike in Yemen or Pakistan. But due process is not satisfied by an internal executive branch memo. Before targeting a citizen, the administration should have to present classified evidence to an independent court or a specially designated federal judge, much as sensitive surveillance applications are reviewed. In the rare case where urgency makes prior review impossible, prompt after-the-fact review and notification to Congress should be mandatory.

The president needs power to defend the country, but a power to kill citizens based on secret legal standards and undisclosed evidence is too dangerous to leave unchecked. Due process may be adapted to war, but it cannot be erased by it.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The white paper's "imminent threat" standard raises serious constitutional concerns. While the government has legitimate authority to use military force against enemies of the United States, applying this authority to American citizens requires far more rigorous safeguards than the white paper provides.

The Fifth Amendment's guarantee that no person shall be "deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law" is not merely procedural—it demands meaningful judicial oversight before the executive takes a life. The white paper's language is dangerously vague: who determines what constitutes an "informed, high-level" official? What evidence suffices to establish an "imminent" threat? The document provides no clear answers.

Mathematical formulas cannot capture the complexity of individual guilt or innocence, and the law must account for human fallibility. Executive discretion without judicial check creates conditions for arbitrary killing. If the standard requires only executive determination of threat, it effectively places the power of life and death beyond constitutional constraint—a result incompatible with our fundamental commitments.

I would propose a stricter framework. First, the government must present evidence of imminent threat to an independent federal judge before any strike. Second, capture must be attempted whenever operationally feasible. Third, meaningful congressional oversight must occur, with classified briefings subject to judicial review in secret proceedings when national security requires.

The al-Awlaki case illustrates the problem. Regardless of his alleged terrorist affiliations, no American should be killed based solely on executive determination without judicial review. Due process does not require impossible procedural formalities during wartime, but it does require that a neutral decision-maker evaluate evidence before the ultimate sanction.

The administration's position conflates military necessity with constitutional exemption. That equation must be rejected.

The leaked white paper is a constitutional travesty dressed in the language of legal sophistication. The Fifth Amendment is not ambiguous: no person shall be deprived of life "without due process of law." The Framers who wrote those words had just fought a revolution against a king who claimed the prerogative to designate enemies and dispatch them. The notion that an "informed, high-level official" — meaning, in practice, some functionary in the executive branch answerable to the president — can constitute the sum total of process due before an American is incinerated by a drone strike inverts the entire structure of our system.

Consider what the white paper actually claims. It redefines "imminence" to mean something that need not be imminent in any ordinary sense. It redefines "infeasibility of capture" so loosely that the executive itself decides when capture is too inconvenient. And it provides no judicial review whatsoever — neither before the killing nor after. The targeted citizen has no opportunity to contest the evidence against him, because he is not told he is a target until a Hellfire missile arrives.

Defenders will point to Anwar al-Awlaki and say: surely you don't want this man walking free. But that response begs the question. We have processes for trying treason and material support for terrorism precisely because the government's certainty that someone is dangerous is not, by itself, due process. J. Edgar Hoover was certain about Martin Luther King Jr. The Palmer Raids were conducted by men certain about anarchists. The history of American national security is littered with the wreckage of executive certainty unchecked by courts.

At minimum, a lawful targeted-killing program would require: a judicial finding, perhaps through a FISA-like court, that the target meets specific statutory criteria; a genuine, evidence-based showing of imminence; demonstrated infeasibility of capture; and post-strike review by an independent body with subpoena power. The current framework — secret memos justifying secret killings under secret standards — is not law. It is fiat. And fiat power over life and death is the very definition of tyranny the Constitution was written to prevent.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

We should be honest about what the white paper actually says and what it does not. It does not authorize the killing of Americans for political dissent, for protest, or for membership in disfavored groups. It addresses a narrow category: senior operational leaders of Al Qaeda or associated forces who have joined an enemy with which Congress has authorized armed conflict. In that narrow case, the analysis is not novel — it is the straightforward application of the law of armed conflict to a citizen who has taken up arms against his country.

This principle is older than the republic's quarrels with terrorism. American citizens who fought for the Confederacy were killed on battlefields without trial. American citizens who joined the Wehrmacht were shelled in their bunkers without judicial process. In Ex parte Quirin, the Supreme Court upheld the military trial and execution of a U.S. citizen who served as a Nazi saboteur. Citizenship has never conferred immunity from the consequences of joining an enemy force in wartime.

The harder question is procedural: who decides, and on what evidence, that a particular individual has crossed that line? Here the white paper's framework is defensible in outline but thin in detail. An "informed, high-level official" is not nothing — it implies accountability within a chain of command up to the President — but it is also not enough. A reasonable middle path would establish: (1) congressional oversight by the intelligence committees with full access to targeting criteria and case files; (2) inspector general review after each strike against an American citizen; (3) a presumption of capture over kill when operationally feasible, with documented justification when it is not; and (4) some form of after-the-fact judicial accountability, perhaps through wrongful-death suits in which the government must defend its determination.

What due process does not require, in the context of active armed conflict, is a pre-strike adversarial hearing. Battlefield decisions cannot be subjected to advance judicial approval without paralyzing the war effort Congress authorized. The Constitution is not a suicide pact, but neither is it a blank check. The task is calibration, not absolutism in either direction.

Most of the commentary on the white paper focuses on the wrong question. We argue about whether the executive's procedures are sufficient, as if procedure were the heart of the matter. The deeper problem is the legal architecture in which these procedures sit: a perpetual, undefined, geographically unbounded "war" against an enemy whose membership is determined by the executive itself.

The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force, passed in the immediate aftermath of September 11, named no specific enemy beyond those responsible for the attacks and those who harbored them. More than eleven years on, the executive has stretched that authority to cover affiliates, associates, and successors that did not exist in 2001 and have no operational connection to the original conspirators. Anwar al-Awlaki's al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula is not the organization that attacked the World Trade Center. Yet the same authorization is invoked to kill him.

Once you accept that we are in a global, permanent armed conflict against an enemy of executive definition, the targeted-killing memos follow with grim logic. Of course the executive can kill enemy combatants. Of course citizenship doesn't immunize battlefield enemies. The legal infrastructure forces these conclusions. So the meaningful debate is not about white-paper procedures but about whether Congress will reclaim its constitutional responsibility to define the conflict.

Congress should repeal and replace the 2001 AUMF with a narrower, time-limited authorization specifying which groups it covers and which it does not. It should require executive reporting on targeted-killing criteria. It should mandate that the addition of new organizations to the target list requires fresh congressional authorization. And it should sunset the framework after a defined period, forcing periodic reaffirmation.

Without that structural fix, debating the precise definition of "imminence" in a Justice Department memo is like rearranging furniture in a burning house. The fundamental issue is who decides when the country is at war, with whom, and for how long. The Constitution gives that power to Congress. Congress has surrendered it. Until that abdication is reversed, every refinement of executive procedure is window dressing on an unaccountable power.

Imagine the following scenario. An American citizen has traveled to Yemen and assumed operational responsibility within a group that has attempted, repeatedly, to bring down U.S. airliners. He is in a region where the local government cannot or will not arrest him. He continues to plan attacks. The intelligence is corroborated, redundant, and credible. What, exactly, should the United States do?

The civil libertarian answer — indict him, demand extradition, try him in absentia, or simply wait — is a fantasy in this scenario. There is no functioning extradition partner. He will not turn himself in. A ground operation to capture him would risk American lives and might fail. Meanwhile, the next plot proceeds.

I am genuinely sympathetic to the constitutional concerns raised by the white paper. The Fifth Amendment does say what it says. But due process is not, and has never been, a single fixed procedure. The Supreme Court has held repeatedly — in Mathews v. Eldridge and elsewhere — that the process due depends on the interests at stake, the risk of error, and the burdens of additional procedure. In wartime, against an enemy combatant abroad, the calculus tilts toward executive discretion in a way that would be unacceptable in domestic criminal law.

What the white paper gets right is the recognition that some procedural framework is required even in this context. What it gets wrong is the secrecy. There is no good reason the framework itself — the criteria, the standards of evidence, the chain of approval — should be classified. Publishing the framework would not compromise operations; it would compromise only the executive's ability to act without scrutiny.

So my answer is: yes, the government can kill American citizens who have joined Al Qaeda and pose genuine threats, provided no capture option exists. But the rules under which it does so must be public, debated, and ratified by Congress. The current situation, where Americans learn the framework only through leaks, is corrosive to democratic legitimacy. Acknowledge the program. Defend the program. And let the public decide whether the rules are right.

There is something deeply uncomfortable about treating American citizenship as the relevant moral variable in this debate. Why does the death of Anwar al-Awlaki provoke a constitutional crisis while the deaths of hundreds of Yemeni, Pakistani, and Somali nationals killed by the same drone program receive a fraction of the attention? If targeted killing is wrong, it is wrong because it kills human beings without adequate process, not because it occasionally kills the kind of human being who carries a U.S. passport.

That framing matters, because the citizenship-focused debate generates perverse legal arguments. Defenders of the program point out, correctly, that Americans who join enemy forces have historically been killable in combat. From this they conclude that the program is constitutionally sound. But this gets the analysis backwards. The proper question is not "can we kill Americans the way we kill foreigners?" It is "are we killing anyone, citizen or not, on the basis of standards we would accept if applied to ourselves?"

By that standard, the program fails badly. Signature strikes target individuals whose identities are unknown, based on behavioral patterns that fit a profile. The "imminent threat" standard, as redefined in the white paper, requires no actual imminence. The geographic scope of the conflict has no clear limit. The number of civilian casualties is contested precisely because the government counts military-age males in strike zones as combatants unless proven otherwise.

A serious framework for targeted killing — for citizens and non-citizens alike — would require: independent verification of intelligence; a meaningful, time-bound definition of imminence; published rules of engagement; transparent casualty accounting; and consequences for errors. None of this currently exists in robust form.

If we limit the conversation to American citizens, we will end up with a two-tiered system in which the procedural protections demanded for U.S. nationals serve as a fig leaf over the largely unconstrained killing of everyone else. The Constitution speaks of persons, not citizens, in the Fifth Amendment for a reason. The standards that should govern this program are universal, or they are not really standards at all.

The Justice Department's white paper is best understood not as a legal analysis but as an executive branch self-authorization. Strip away the citations and what remains is the claim that the executive, applying criteria it has itself defined, may kill an American citizen whom it has itself determined to be dangerous, with no meaningful check from any other branch. The trinity of "high-level official," "imminent threat," and "infeasibility of capture" sounds like law, but each term is defined by the same actor who applies it.

This is not how the constitutional system is supposed to work. The genius of separation of powers is that the branch that exercises a power is not the branch that defines it. Congress writes the law; the executive carries it out; courts say what the law means. On targeted killing, all three functions have collapsed into one. The executive writes the rules, applies them in secret, and refuses to acknowledge the results in any forum where they could be challenged. When the ACLU has tried to litigate these questions on behalf of targeted Americans' families, the government has invoked state secrets and standing doctrine to shut the courthouse door.

The remedy is not to abolish targeted killing — there are circumstances where it may be the least bad option — but to restore the separation of powers. Congress should legislate specific criteria, not leave the executive to invent its own. A specialized court, modeled loosely on the FISA court but with adversarial counsel, should review proposed strikes against American citizens before they happen, except in true emergency cases where review must follow. Strikes should be reported to Congress within days, with full classified documentation. And families of those killed should have a cause of action to challenge the determination after the fact, with damages available if the government cannot meet its burden.

None of this is exotic. It is the application of ordinary American legal architecture — legislative rules, judicial review, available remedies — to a domain currently governed by executive fiat. The question is whether we still believe in that architecture, or whether terrorism has frightened us out of it.
