Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Agnes Callard has written a bracing essay, and its most important service is to embarrass a certain kind of philosophical hygiene — the move by which both Stoics and sentimentalists discreetly invent purified surrogates ("transition anger," "righteous indignation") to do anger's moral work without anger's stains. She is right that this is suspicious, and right that grudges and revenge cannot be waved away as mere psychological tics. Still, I think her two central arguments — for Grudges and for Revenge — depend on slippages that, once noticed, deflate the more vertiginous conclusion that we cannot be good in a bad world.

Start with the Argument for Grudges. Callard reasons: the reason you had to be angry on Tuesday was that I wronged you, that fact is unchangeable, therefore you have that same reason to be angry forever. But "you wronged me" is not, strictly, what anger responds to. Anger responds to standing in a certain live relation to a wrongdoer: someone who has done me harm and has not yet acknowledged it, repaired it, or recommitted to the norms between us. When I apologize, pay restitution, and promise reform — and you believe me — the historical fact of the theft remains, but the relational fact (an unrepaired breach between us) does not. Compare grief: the death of someone I love is permanent, yet grief that did not attenuate over years would not count as a more faithful response to the fact; it would count as pathological mourning. Reasons for emotions track not bare events but the present significance of those events. Callard writes that "anger... is a way of grasping the fact that it is broken." Exactly — and once it is, in the relevant sense, no longer broken, the grasping ceases to be apt. Her argument equivocates between the historical and the relational reading of "what you were angry about."

The Argument for Revenge has a similar shape. Callard says holding someone accountable means turning their wrong into "a principle and rule for our interactions" — and she glosses this as "making my bad your good." But these are two different things. To turn a wrong into a principle is to insist: you may not do this; if you do, there are consequences; the norm you violated still binds us. That can be done through protest, demand for repair, withdrawal of trust, sanction, even punishment — none of which requires me to reorganize my own welfare around your harm. Vengeance does require that reorganization: the avenger's good consists in the wrongdoer's suffering. Holding accountable does not. A judge who sentences a thief is not better off because the thief is worse off; she is doing something else entirely, namely upholding a norm. Callard's vivid phenomenology — the colonized fantasy life, the mental effigy, the "uncanny intimacy" — describes vengeful obsession beautifully, but it does not show that this is what moral accountability *is*. It shows what happens when accountability is privatized and emotionalized rather than handed off to institutions, friends, or time.

Her own anecdote actually cuts against her view more than she lets on. Her grandmother, full of warranted historical anger, treats a German girl who had wronged no one as if she were continuous with the perpetrators. Callard is honest enough to call this not innocent on either side. But notice what the example actually illustrates: the grudge here does not faithfully track moral reality; it overgeneralizes, transferring guilt across persons. If grudges were the rational extension of accurate moral perception, we would expect them to discriminate. Instead they smear. That is evidence that grudges are not the lucid emotional inference Callard's argument makes them out to be, but a familiar cognitive distortion — one whose pull we should acknowledge without dignifying as valid reasoning.

This leaves the deepest layer of her essay — the Nietzsche-Girard-Foucault thought that morality and violence are mutually implicated — with real force but a smaller scope than she wants. Yes: any practical regime of holding-accountable will involve coercion, exclusion, sometimes suffering inflicted in the name of the good. There is no antiseptic morality. But "the institutions of accountability carry traces of the violence they regulate" is a much weaker claim than "the only rational response to injustice is grudge and revenge, so victims of oppression are morally damaged by their own moral responses." The first is a sober observation about non-ideal ethics. The second sounds like an insight only because Callard has built it by collapsing accountability into vengeance and apt protest into eternal anger.

We can do morality without doing it perfectly. That is not purification; it is the ordinary, difficult work of being responsive to wrong without becoming its mirror.

Agnes Callard’s provocative lead essay draws a great deal of its unsettling power from a single, deceptively simple move: she treats the reasons for anger as if they were frozen in time, immune to the very forms of moral repair that our practices of accountability make possible. Once I steal from you, she argues, the fact of the theft is forever fixed in the past, a permanent gap between what I did and what I ought to have done. Nothing I do later—apology, restitution, sincere promise—can erase that fact. So, she concludes, your reasons for being angry remain intact; you have a reason to be angry forever. The Argument for Grudges thus appears to fall out of a few unremarkable premises. But this line of reasoning conflates having a reason with having a sufficient or undefeated reason, and in doing so it drains anger of its essentially communicative nature—a nature that Callard’s own sensibility might otherwise lead her to appreciate.

What the Argument for Grudges misses is that the reasons for anger are not exhaustively backward-looking. Yes, anger is a way of grasping that something is broken, but it also makes a demand: that the wrongdoer recognize the wrong, disavow it, and take responsibility. This is the grain of truth running from Adam Smith through Strawson: the reactive attitudes like anger treat their targets as members of a moral community, capable of answering for what they’ve done. When I apologize with full sincerity, make restitution, and credibly commit to change, I am not trying to make the past disappear. I am answering your demand. In doing so, I give you a new kind of reason: a forward-looking reason to let the anger go, not because the past was not terrible, but because the moral relationship has been repaired insofar as it can be. The fact that the theft was wrong does not change, but its significance for our ongoing relationship shifts. My amends alter the normative landscape between us, and your anger, if it is to remain rational, must be responsive to that shift.

Callard anticipates this reply and implicitly dismisses it by insisting that the original reasons remain. But this is like saying I have a reason to grieve a lost loved one forever because the loss is permanent. That is true in a thin sense: I will always have some reason to feel the pain of the absence. But it doesn’t follow that interminable, unmodified grief is rationally required, or even rationally fitting. The passage of time, the integration of the loss into a meaningful life, and the other values I pursue all give me reasons to modulate my grief. Similarly, your anger, if it is part of a healthy moral psychology, is not a static reaction but a dynamic attitude that must answer to the full range of practical reasons, including the reason to move forward with someone who has done the hard work of making amends. The Argument for Grudges works only if we accept an implausibly atomistic picture of reasons, one that ignores how reasons for reactive attitudes are essentially relational and temporally extended.

The deeper problem with Callard’s approach is that it subtly relocates the location of moral corruption. Her analysis makes anger’s rational endpoint a kind of self-perpetuating fixation, and then she argues that such fixation is both rationally grounded and morally corrupting. But if anger genuinely contains an internal orientation toward repair—if what it fundamentally seeks is not blood but acknowledgment—then the “dark side” of grudge-bearing and disproportionate revenge is not anger’s logical conclusion but a pathological deviation. This is not to retreat into a philosopher’s fiction of purified indignation. It is to recognize that emotions are not inferential machines whose outputs are fully determined by a single unchanging premise. They are shaped by habituation, by normative ideals, and by a cultivated responsiveness to the complex moral reality of other people. The fact that many people in fact cling to vengeful grudges no more shows that such clinging is rationally required by anger than the prevalence of crippling anxiety shows that anxiety rationally demands paralysis.

Callard’s Argument for Revenge is similarly contestable. She portrays revenge as a way of holding the wrongdoer responsible by converting the one-off opposition of interests into a rule: I make your bad my good. But this is a deeply partial picture of what holding responsible involves. A far more central and widely practiced form of accountability is what we might call the moral demand for recognition. You hold me responsible not by inflicting symmetrical harm but by insisting that I confront what I have done, by withdrawing trust until I earn it back, by demanding that I internalize the wrongness of my action. These forms of holding responsible are perfectly responsive to the backward-looking fact of the wrongdoing without requiring that you, the victim, transform your own good into my suffering. Revenge, as Callard describes it, is one possible expression of the angry impulse, but to present it as the central mechanism of moral accountability stacks the deck in favor of the conclusion that morality is inescapably corrupt.

The result is that Callard’s crisis—the claim that victims are inevitably morally damaged, whether by anger or by acquiescence—rests on a false dilemma. She assumes that any anger worth the name carries a permanent, quasi-rational orientation toward perpetuating harm, and that any attempt to steer clear of this orientation is either an idle fantasy or a form of acquiescence. But there is a vast middle ground that has been the subject of careful philosophical attention at least since the virtue-ethical tradition took up the idea of tempering the passions. We can be sensitive to injustice, motivated to protest and resist, and yet cultivate habits of anger that do not demand limitless payback. The fact that this is psychologically difficult and often imperfectly achieved does not make it fictional. It makes it an aspiration, one that the very moral communities Callard draws on—the Jewish tradition with its emphasis on justice and remembrance, the anti-racist and feminist movements that transform anger into organized political action—have long tried to realize.

None of this denies the profound insight Callard brings: our moral lives are marked by tragedy, by the impossibility of a perfectly clean response to being wronged. Her grandmother’s anger was not pristine, and her own childhood game was not innocent. But the lesson to draw from this is not that being wronged makes us worse people in a way that is equivalent to the corruption of the wrongdoer. It is that moral life forces us to navigate ineliminable tensions—between backward-looking protest and forward-looking repair, between the demand for justice and the pull of mercy—without the comfort of a pure, unambivalent stance. That is not corruption; it is the condition of finite, relational creatures who must live with one another after harm. Framing it as corruption risks treating the very difficulty of the moral life as a kind of contamination, rather than as the terrain on which moral maturity is forged. And that, in the end, is a more hopeful conclusion than Callard’s, not because it denies the darkness she sees, but because it refuses to mistake difficulty for defeat.

Agnes Callard presents a deeply provocative and ultimately despairing vision of human morality. By arguing that anger cannot be divorced from endless grudges and boundless vengeance, she arrives at the bleak conclusion that responding to injustice inevitably corrupts us. In Callard’s view, because the historical fact of an injustice cannot be erased, the rational justification for anger remains forever. Therefore, she claims, victims of oppression are morally compromised by the very act of holding their oppressors accountable. While her argument is elegant in its relentless logic, it relies on a fundamentally static view of human relationships and a misunderstanding of what moral anger actually tracks. We can, in fact, be good in a bad world, provided we understand anger not as a permanent ledger of historical facts, but as a dynamic, relational demand for moral equality.

The hinge of Callard’s thesis is what she calls the “Argument for Grudges.” She asks us to imagine a theft on Monday, followed by complete restitution, apology, and behavioral change on Wednesday. Callard posits that if it was rational to be angry on Tuesday, it remains rational to be angry on Thursday, because the apology does nothing to alter the fact that the theft occurred. “Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever,” she writes.

This argument makes a category error regarding the object of our moral emotions. As Peter Strawson famously delineated, our reactive attitudes—like resentment and indignation—are responses to the *quality of will* expressed by another person, not merely to the material harms they cause or the historical timeline of their actions. When someone steals from me, the reason for my anger is not merely the absence of my property. My anger is a response to the communicative content of their action: the thief’s behavior asserts that my rights do not matter, that my boundaries are permeable, and that I am of lesser moral status.

When the offender returns the property, compensates me, and sincerely apologizes, they do not change the past, but they absolutely change the present moral reality. A sincere apology is a retraction of the original insult. It is a communicative act in which the offender disavows their previous quality of will and publicly aligns their values with my own, acknowledging my equal moral standing. Therefore, the reason for my anger *has* been addressed. The historical fact of the theft remains, but the ongoing moral threat—the disrespect and the assertion of unequal status—has been neutralized. It is entirely rational to let go of anger at this point, not because we have developed amnesia, but because the relational breach that the anger was protesting has been repaired. By treating anger as a response to a static historical event rather than a dynamic relational stance, Callard ignores the rational mechanism of forgiveness.

Callard’s “Argument for Revenge” suffers from a similar reductionism. She argues that revenge is simply how we hold one another accountable: because the offender made our bad their good, we must make their bad our good. She claims this adversarial embrace is the logical endpoint of moral responsiveness. But this conflates holding someone accountable with the infliction of suffering for its own sake.

True moral accountability is not a zero-sum transfer of pain; it is a demand for recognition. When we hold someone accountable, we force them to reckon with the reality of our personhood and the consequences of their actions. If punishment is involved, its morally rational aim—distinct from sadism or bloodlust—is to bring the offender to an understanding of the harm they caused, ideally leading to their reintegration into the moral community. Callard describes the angry person forcing their own thinking "down [the offender's] throat," but she misidentifies the goal. The goal of righteous indignation is not to eternally enslave the offender in a flipped hierarchy, but to establish a mutual understanding of justice that was absent during the offense.

The most troubling consequence of Callard’s framework is her conclusion regarding victims of systemic injustice. She argues that because anger inherently contains the seeds of vengeance and grudges, long-term oppression inflicts long-term moral damage on the oppressed. In her words, the oppressors make the oppressed "morally worse people."

This is a profound misreading of the function of anger for marginalized groups. Far from being a corrupting force, anger in the face of systemic degradation is often a vital assertion of one’s own humanity. In her seminal 1981 essay "The Uses of Anger," the poet and essayist Audre Lorde articulated how the anger of Black women in a racist society is not a moral failing, but an essential tool for survival and clarity. Lorde distinguished clearly between hatred, which seeks the destruction of the other, and anger, which demands change and the rectification of a distorted relationship.

To label the defensive, boundary-asserting anger of the oppressed as “moral corruption” shifts a heavy philosophical burden onto the vulnerable. It pathologizes the very emotional mechanism by which marginalized people resist internalizing the degradation forced upon them. When Callard’s grandmother—a survivor of unimaginable trauma—reacted with anger to a German girl, she was not necessarily executing a perfectly rational moral deduction about collective guilt. She was reacting from a place of profound trauma. We can hold deep compassion for the psychological scars of oppression without conceding that eternal vengeance is the logically sound conclusion of moral injury. Trauma may make letting go of anger incredibly difficult, but that is a psychological reality, not a proof of Callard’s philosophical rule.

Callard challenges us to step inside our own moral theories and face the darkness she believes is baked into them. But the darkness she sees is a shadow cast by an incomplete theory. If we strip away the possibility of genuine relational repair, if we deny the moral power of apologies, and if we view accountability only as a mirroring of cruelty, then of course we are left with nothing but bloodlust and cynicism. But human morality is vastly richer than this. Anger is the alarm bell that alerts us to a fire; it is entirely rational to turn the alarm off once the fire has been extinguished. We are not doomed to be corrupted by our injuries. Through the demanding, rational work of accountability, restitution, and forgiveness, we can indeed be good in a bad world.

Agnes Callard is right that anger’s defenders too often sanitize it. Anyone who has been angry knows that “indignation” is not a tidy moral perception with the fangs removed. It fantasizes, repeats itself, recruits allies, refuses sleep. It can make the offender more present to the victim than beloved friends are. In that sense Callard performs a useful service: she refuses the comforting picture in which anger simply detects injustice and then politely hands the case over to practical reason.

But her central argument depends on an equivocation about what it means to have a reason. The fact that you stole from me on Monday remains true forever. The fact that you ought not to have stolen remains true forever. It does not follow that I have the same reason to be angry forever. Reasons are not museum labels attached permanently to facts; they are considerations whose force depends on context, relation, and aim. If I am thirsty, the fact that there is water in the glass is a reason to drink. After I drink, the same fact is no longer a reason to drink. If you insult me, the insult gives me reason to protest, demand recognition, protect myself, revise my trust, or seek redress. Once you have apologized, compensated me, and shown genuine repentance, the original fact remains, but its practical and emotional significance has changed.

Callard anticipates this by saying anger is “not a desire to fix something but a way of grasping the fact that it is broken.” Yet this makes anger strangely contemplative, almost aesthetic: a permanent gaze at the crack in reality. Much anger is not like that. It is addressed to someone. It asks: Do you see what you did? Are you still the person who did it? Am I still vulnerable to you in the same way? What do you take me to be? Apology and restitution do not alter the past, but they can alter the present meaning of the past because they alter the offender’s stance toward it. A sincere apology says, “I now stand with you in condemning what I did.” That matters to anger precisely because anger is not only about the bad event; it is about the will revealed in the event.

This is why the ordinary distinction between resentment and forgiveness is not as philosophically flimsy as Callard suggests. Forgiveness is not amnesia. Nor is it the judgment that the wrong no longer occurred. It is a change in the moral relationship between victim and offender, made possible—when it is possible at all—by repentance, repair, or sometimes by the victim’s independent decision not to let the offender occupy the center of her inner life. There are wrongs for which forgiveness would be premature, sentimental, or even morally ugly. But that does not show that anger always retains its original rational force. It shows that the conditions under which anger should recede are demanding.

The argument for revenge is even less persuasive. Callard writes that revenge holds the wrongdoer accountable by making his maxim into the rule of interaction: since he made my bad his good, I now make his bad my good. But this is not accountability; it is imitation under the description of pedagogy. To hold someone responsible is to address him as answerable to a standard that both of us, at least in principle, can recognize. Revenge replaces that shared standard with a private reversal of advantage. It says not “what you did was wrong,” but “your wrong licenses me to wrong you.”

That distinction is not a philosopher’s nicety. It is the difference between punishment and vendetta, between judgment and feud. Of course actual legal punishment is often brutal, racist, excessive, and self-deceived; Foucault’s suspicion of modern discipline has ample targets. But the failures of institutions do not erase the conceptual difference between a practice that aspires to public justification, proportionality, evidence, and limits, and a practice whose internal logic is “because you hurt me, I may hurt you.” Indeed, the very fact that we criticize courts, prisons, and police for cruelty shows that we possess standards not reducible to revenge. We say punishment has become vengeance when it loses those standards.

Callard’s strongest point is that anger can feel like the only available alternative to acquiescence. Here the anti-anger traditions can sound inhuman. To tell the humiliated, the assaulted, the colonized, or the bereaved simply to relinquish anger is often to demand that they perform moral elegance for the comfort of others. Anger may be the first sign that one has not accepted degradation as normal. It can break the spell of submission. It can gather the scattered perceptions of injury into a public claim: this happened; it was wrong; it must stop.

Still, it does not follow that the anger which awakens self-respect must be granted legislative authority over the soul. The history of nonviolent movements is relevant here, not because they were anger-free, but because they treated anger as material to be disciplined. Gandhi, King, and many lesser-known organizers did not ask oppressed people to pretend they were not enraged. They asked them to convert rage into courage, endurance, strategy, and public witness. That conversion was not a philosopher’s fiction. It was difficult, unstable, and never pure; but it was real enough to move bodies into streets, jails, boycotts, and negotiations without making the enemy’s suffering the measure of success.

Callard’s conclusion that oppression makes the oppressed “morally worse people” is therefore too quick and too harsh. Oppression wounds moral agency; it burdens victims with fear, suspicion, bitterness, and impossible choices. It may deform habits of attention. It may tempt the injured toward cruelty. But temptation, trauma, and moral damage are not the same as moral corruption. To be wronged is to be placed under pressure; it is not to be stained. The survivor who cannot stop hating has suffered a further injury, but we should hesitate before redescribing that injury as vice.

Her story about her grandmother makes this clear in spite of itself. A Holocaust survivor who cannot bear to see her granddaughter marching beside a German child may be unjust to the child. But the moral fact that presses on us is not that the grandmother has become “worse.” It is that atrocity has extended its reach into perception, play, and kinship. The wrongdoers have made innocence unavailable in certain moments; they have not thereby acquired the power to make their victims morally equivalent to themselves.

The better lesson is tragic but not despairing. We cannot be perfectly good in a bad world, if perfection means never feeling hatred, never misjudging under pressure, never wanting the wrongdoer to taste fear. But we can respond better and worse. We can remember without nursing, punish without cruelty, protest without dehumanizing, and forgive without denying. Anger is neither pure moral knowledge nor mere madness. It is a dangerous capacity by which finite creatures register that something sacred has been violated. The task is not to purify it completely; Callard is right that this fantasy should be abandoned. The task is to prevent anger from becoming sovereign.

Callard's essay is genuinely unsettling in the way good philosophy should be. She presents two arguments that seem internally coherent—the Argument for Grudges and the Argument for Revenge—and draws from them a conclusion so stark it risks paralyzing moral action entirely. Yet I want to push back on whether those arguments, even granted, force us toward the conclusion she reaches. There may be room to accept her diagnostic insights without accepting that moral corruption is our inescapable fate.

The Argument for Grudges rests on the premise that once wrong has been done, the fact of that wrong remains forever. Apologies, restitution, and promises do not alter the historical record: I stole, I ought not to have stolen, and no subsequent behavior can undo that divergence between what happened and what should have happened. Callard concludes from this that one always has reason to be angry. But here I suspect a conflation between two distinct things: the propositional content of anger (a wrong was committed) and the emotional state of anger itself. I can fully believe "I was wronged" while remaining free of the psychological burden of anger. The argument, as stated, proves too much: by its logic, I would have to remain perpetually angry about every historical injustice, which seems not merely irrational but incoherent. A perpetual emotion about a fixed past event suggests we have ceased to think clearly about what emotions are for.

The Argument for Revenge is more compelling but, I think, incomplete. Callard offers a sophisticated account of revenge as holding someone accountable by making their evil one's good—a mechanism of moral responsibility distinct from mere restitutive repair. This is illuminating as a description of how revenge functions psychologically. But she claims it is how we hold one another morally responsible, as if no alternative existed. Legal punishment, social sanction, restorative justice practices, even mere disapproval—all of these involve holding wrongdoers accountable without requiring the adversarial intimacy she describes. One might grant her analysis of revenge's logic while denying that it captures the only available mechanism of moral accountability. Her argument shows revenge is intelligible, not that it is necessary.

This matters because Callard's most challenging claim is that victims of injustice are "not innocent"—that the morally correct response to wrongdoing (holding grudges, seeking revenge) is itself a form of moral corruption. The oppressed face an impossible choice: acquiesce in evil by suppressing their anger, or become morally compromised by embracing that anger's vengeful implications. She writes that long-term oppression amounts to long-term moral damage to the oppressed group.

This conclusion troubles me, and not only because it seems to recapitulate exactly what oppressors want to believe about their victims. There is a category error lurking here: between the moral status of actions and the moral status of persons. Callard's argument demonstrates that certain responses to injustice are impure, that they involve psychological mechanisms with dark implications. But "impure" in the sense she means does not straightforwardly translate to "morally compromised" in any ordinary sense. We regularly navigate situations where every available option carries costs, without concluding that the people who choose among those options are therefore morally corrupted. A doctor who must choose which of two patients receives a scarce life-saving treatment is not morally corrupted by that choice; they are making the best of a tragic situation. Similarly, victims of injustice who must choose between acquiescence and vengeful anger are navigating an impossible situation—not thereby establishing their moral impurity.

The political implications of her view are significant and troubling. If Callard is right, then systems of oppression damage their victims not just materially but morally. This strikes me as dangerously close to a logic that has historically been used to justify further oppression: if the oppressed are themselves morally compromised, their claim to justice weakens. I do not think this is Callard's intent—she explicitly says oppressors also damage their souls—but the structure of her argument lends itself to deployment against victims rather than in solidarity with them.

Her use of personal narrative—the grandmother, the German girl, the marching game—raises its own questions. She argues that her childhood play was "not innocent" because her family's Holocaust trauma made anti-anti-Semitism the inescapable theme of her childhood. But "not innocent" in this context seems to stretch a long way. Her childhood game was not morally equivalent to collaboration with Nazism, nor did it make her complicit in the crimes of the Third Reich. The connection is retrospective and inferential rather than immediate. Callard may be demonstrating how completely the dark side of morality has colonized her imagination, but she has not shown that this colonization makes her morally impure in the sense her argument requires.

Callard ends by criticizing Nietzsche, Foucault, and Girard for their "timidity"—for holding anthropological distance from their own insights. The devastating conclusion they failed to draw, she suggests, is that humans cannot respond rightly to being wrongly treated. We cannot be good in a bad world.

Perhaps. But there is another possibility: that the impossibility she identifies is not a fixed constraint but a challenge to be navigated. Human moral life has always involved making peace with imperfect options, with dirty hands, with corruption that cannot be fully purged. This is not because we lack the imagination to purify our practices, but because purity itself may be a category that does not fit the moral life. If anger necessarily involves some vengeful residue, that may be a feature of our moral psychology to be worked around rather than a corruption to be mourned. The question is not whether we can be good despite this, but whether we can build social practices, institutions, and relationships that contain anger's worst impulses while preserving its capacity to register injustice.

Callard's essay shows us the depth of the problem. I am not convinced it shows us that we are stuck with the solution she implies.

**Transition Anger Is Not a Philosopher's Fiction**

Agnes Callard's provocative essay accuses both Stoics and sentimentalists of bad faith: in trying to carve off the "moral side" of anger from its "dark side," she says, we have invented philosopher's fictions like "indignation" and "transition anger" that name nothing real. The real thing — what actually surges in us when we are wronged — is, on her view, ineliminably tied to grudge-bearing and to the desire to make another's evil our good. Purifying anger, she concludes, is impossible, and the project of doing so distracts us from the genuine moral crisis: we cannot respond rightly to being treated wrongly.

This is a powerful argument, and I want to take its central provocation seriously. But I think Callard mistakes a feature of one phenomenology of anger for the structure of anger as such, and in doing so she underestimates the resources Nussbaum's "transition anger" actually has.

Consider Callard's Argument for Grudges. The reasons that originally justified anger — that I stole from you, that I ought not to have — are not erased by my apology and restitution. They remain true forever. Therefore, she concludes, you have reason to be just as angry on Thursday as on Tuesday. Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever.

But notice the equivocation here between "reason to be angry" and "reason to be angry to the same intensity, in the same form, indefinitely." It is true that the past wrong remains a wrong forever. What is not obvious is that the appropriate affective response to "you wronged me two years ago, you apologized sincerely, you made restitution, and you have lived as a friend to me since" is the same affective response as to "you just wronged me." The fact picked out as the object of anger changes over time even if the underlying historical event does not. The fact is no longer "this person is in unrepented violation of moral relations with me" but "this person did, on one occasion, violate moral relations with me, and has since done everything possible to repair them." These are different facts, and they warrant different responses.

Callard might reply that the past wrong itself, as such, remains a reason. But this proves too much. By the same logic, gratitude should also be eternal and undiminished: you saved my life in 1995, that fact is unchanging, therefore I owe you the same intensity of gratitude forever. We do not actually think this. Gratitude can be deep and lasting without being constantly felt at the same pitch. Reasons for affective states are not like reasons for beliefs; they are temporally textured.

The Argument for Revenge fares no better. Callard offers a strikingly elegant gloss: revenge takes the principle implicit in your wronging me — "your bad is my good" — and turns it back on you as a rule, "educating" you into moral accountability. The image is compelling. But it is also a description of one particular cultural-psychological enactment of holding-responsible, not of holding-responsible as such. The Greek tragedies Callard's tradition reaches for are full of this logic; so is the honor culture of the Mediterranean and the Pashtun and the antebellum South. But there are also cultures, and individuals within every culture, where holding-responsible takes other forms: refusing to do business with a cheat, telling the truth about what happened, naming and shaming, withdrawing trust, refusing to pretend. None of these requires the intimate, colonizing reciprocity Callard describes.

What Callard has done, I think, is take Strawson's reactive attitudes and read into them the maximal version: not just resentment but the brooding, fantasy-life-colonizing resentment of the wronged who cannot move on. Anyone who has been wronged knows that state. But anyone who has been wronged also knows other states: the state of feeling the wrong cleanly, naming it, and then turning back to one's own life; the state of refusing to forgive while also refusing to be consumed; the state of letting time soften an injury without erasing its truth. These are not philosopher's fictions; they are ordinary human achievements. They are what Nussbaum is pointing at with "transition anger" and what therapeutic, religious, and political traditions across the world cultivate.

Callard's response will be that these "achievements" are achievements only because we have suppressed the rational response, which is endless vengeance. But this confuses a phenomenological claim with a normative one. The mere fact that endless vengeance is psychologically possible, and that some humans default to it, does not show it is what reason requires. Reason often requires us to weigh many considerations, including the moral status of the wrongdoer-after-repentance, the costs of grudge-keeping to one's own flourishing, the duty not to colonize one's psychic life with another's evil, and the recognition that human beings are, in fact, capable of moral repair.

There is also something curious about Callard's portrait of the wrongdoer in the original example. By Wednesday, the thief has not merely returned the goods but compensated, apologized sincerely, offered gifts, and promised reform — and the victim believes the apology is sincere. Callard insists the historical fact "you stole" is unchanged. True. But she elides the changed fact: this person, who stole, has become a person-who-stole-and-fully-repented. To be angry with them on Thursday as on Tuesday is to refuse to update one's representation of who they now are. This is not rational tenacity; it is a failure to track the moral world as it currently is.

I do not want to deny the dark phenomenology Callard describes. Grudges colonize. Revenge has its own seductive logic. The wronged often are, as she says of her grandmother, "full of anger" — and this anger is not innocent. Callard's point that long-term oppression morally damages the oppressed is important and underappreciated. It deserves the prominence she gives it.

But the conclusion she draws — that this damage reflects the rational structure of anger, that the alternative to corruption is acquiescence, that purification is a fiction — does not follow. Between the eternal vengeance of the unhealed grudge and the supine acquiescence of the doormat lies a vast territory of human moral practice: protest, repair, mourning, forgiveness (sometimes), accountability-without-cruelty, and yes, transition anger. To call all of this a "philosopher's fiction" is to insult the actual lives of people who have done the difficult work of being wronged without being deformed by it.

Callard ends by saying we can't be good in a bad world. Perhaps. But the bad world she describes is, in part, a function of the maximalist picture of anger she has chosen to defend. Loosen that picture, attend more carefully to the actual phenomenology of moral repair, and the world looks less hopeless. It does not look innocent — Callard is right that innocence is not on offer — but it looks like a world in which transition anger names something real: the residue of injury, felt and acknowledged, that does not have to harden into bloodlust to remain morally serious.

The deepest mistake in Callard's argument is, I think, the assumption that the alternative to vengeance must be erasure. Either you keep the wrong alive by hating the wrongdoer, or you betray the wrong by letting it go. This binary is false. Memory is not the same as resentment. Knowing what happened is not the same as nursing one's wrath to keep it warm. There is a way to carry an injury that neither denies it nor lets it govern one. Calling that way a fiction is what allows the argument for limitless violence to look plausible. It is not plausible. It is one option, and not the one reason recommends.

**The Extreme Stoic Has More Friends Than Callard Thinks**

Near the end of her essay, Callard reaches for a figure she calls the Extreme Stoic, who "places no value on anything the world can remove from himself" and so can be entirely anger-free. She names Socrates, says she knows no modern thinker who represents this position, and treats it as a kind of asymptotic limit that the rest of us cannot reach. "Most of us are neither willing nor able to achieve the kind of detachment that this immunity from wrath requires."

I want to push back on this framing in two ways. First, the Extreme Stoic is not as extreme as Callard makes him out to be, nor as friendless. Second, what the contemplative traditions actually recommend is not the elimination of moral response but a transformation in what we take ourselves to be defending when we respond. Once we see this, Callard's claim that we cannot purify our response to injustice looks less like a deep discovery about morality and more like a parochial conclusion drawn from a single tradition's vocabulary.

Begin with the Buddhists. Callard nods to Śāntideva, but she treats Buddhist anti-anger as a maximalist outlier. In fact, the Buddhist analysis of anger is detailed, sophisticated, and has been refined over two millennia of meditative and ethical practice. The Buddhist does not deny that wrongdoing happens or that it should be opposed. The bodhisattva is enjoined to act vigorously against suffering and injustice. What the Buddhist denies is that the affective state called *dveṣa* (hatred, aversion) is required for or even helpful to such action. On the contrary: the Buddhist holds that hate is a kind of cognitive distortion, one that fuses an accurate perception ("this is harmful and should stop") with an inaccurate one ("the being causing this harm is a fixed, separate self maximally identified with this harmful action and deserving of suffering in return").

Disentangle the accurate from the inaccurate, the Buddhist says, and you get something that looks a lot like what Nussbaum calls transition anger: clear-eyed recognition of the wrong, fierce motivation to address it, but without the colonizing grudge-life Callard describes. Callard says this purified response is a "philosopher's fiction." But it is not a fiction; it is the explicit, documented result of long contemplative training, attested by countless practitioners and refined into specific techniques (loving-kindness, equanimity, analytic meditation on the causes of harmful action).

Callard's response would presumably be: yes, but those people have given up the moral seriousness of anger. They no longer hold wrongdoers responsible in the colonizing, intimate way that anger requires. Exactly so. The Buddhist's reply is: that intimate colonization is not in fact required for holding-responsible, and the fact that it feels required to those raised in our particular moral grammar reflects a feature of our culture, not of morality. Holding-responsible can be done from a position of clarity rather than from a position of grievance. Indeed the Buddhist would say it is done *better* from clarity, because the brooding fantasy-life of the grudge-holder distorts perception and motivates disproportionate response.

Now consider what Callard says about Socrates. She notes that he died anger-free. She does not mention that he also held that no one does wrong willingly, that injustice harms its perpetrator more than its victim, and that the appropriate response to being wronged is therefore something like pity rather than anger. This is not a quirky aside; it is the cornerstone of the Socratic ethical position. And it has had enormous downstream influence — through the Stoics, through Augustine, through much of Christian ethics, and through modern figures like Tolstoy, Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr., all of whom are quite recognizably modern thinkers and quite recognizably not in Callard's roster.

King is an especially interesting case because his nonviolent practice is sometimes glossed as if it required suppressing anger. It did not. King wrote movingly about the role of righteous indignation in the civil rights movement, but he also wrote with equal care about *agape* — a form of love that is not affective sentiment but a disciplined will toward the good of the other, including the oppressor. King's claim was precisely that one could hold white supremacists fully accountable for their crimes while refusing the colonizing grudge-life of vengeance. He thought this was not only possible but morally necessary, because vengeance would corrupt the movement and reproduce the very logic of oppression it sought to overcome. King is, in Callard's terms, a near-Extreme Stoic, and he is anything but acquiescent.

So we have a tradition stretching from Socrates through the Stoics, through Buddhism, through Christian agape, through modern nonviolence — a tradition that says: yes, respond to wrong, fiercely if necessary; no, you do not have to do so by making the wrongdoer's bad your good. This is not one philosopher's fiction. It is a vast and well-attested human possibility.

Why, then, does Callard treat it as marginal? I think because she is taking the phenomenology of anger in our particular culture as evidence about the structure of moral response as such. In our culture, anger does often have the structure she describes: it does colonize, it does fantasize vengeance, it does cling. But that is a cultural fact, not a transcendental one. Cultures that have trained their members differently produce different phenomenologies.

Here Callard might object that her Arguments for Grudges and Revenge are arguments about rationality, not about culture. The wrong remains a wrong; therefore the reason to be angry remains; therefore the anger is rational. But this argument proves too much. By the same logic, every reason-giving fact in the world would generate undiminished affective response forever. The fact that my friend died ten years ago is unchanged; the fact that I have a body is unchanged; the fact that suffering exists is unchanged. We do not think it is irrational to feel grief differently at ten years than at one week, or to feel embodied joy without constant amazement. Reasons for affective states are temporally and contextually structured. To say "once you have reason X, you have it forever" is to confuse the persistence of a propositional content with the appropriateness of a constant affective intensity.

The Buddhist and the Stoic make a subtler claim: the original anger-response was itself based on a partly mistaken construal of the situation. When you trained yourself out of that construal, you trained yourself out of the anger. Not by repression but by understanding. The wrong remains; what changes is your relation to it, because you now see it more accurately — as the product of causes and conditions, as an event that does not require you to construct a vengeful counter-self in response.

Callard concludes that "we can't be good in a bad world." This is a striking line, and it may be true on some construal. But the construal that makes it true is one in which goodness is defined by maintaining a particular affective response to wrong, and that response is described as ineliminably vengeful. Change either part of that definition — and the contemplative traditions give us serious resources for doing so — and the conclusion dissolves.

I do not want to suggest the path is easy. Of course it is not. Callard is right that most of us, most of the time, get angry in the colonizing way she describes. But "most of us, most of the time" is not the same as "rationality requires." The Extreme Stoic is not a fantasy figure; he is a recognizable human achievement, partial and imperfect but real, and the traditions that cultivate him have given us more guidance than Callard's essay acknowledges. Before concluding that we cannot be good in a bad world, we should look harder at the people who, by ordinary lights, manage something pretty close to it.

**Anger, Politics, and the Anger of the Oppressed**

Agnes Callard's essay arrives at a striking conclusion: long-term oppression morally damages the oppressed, because the anger of the wronged is itself a form of moral corruption. Either the oppressed acquiesce, which is bad, or they get angry, which is also bad — anger being, on her account, ineliminably linked to grudge-bearing and vengeance. The essay closes with a personal story about her grandmother, a Holocaust survivor whose rage toward a German child was not, Callard says, innocent.

I want to take this conclusion seriously and to disagree with it sharply. I think Callard's framing — for all its philosophical ingenuity — leads her into a politically dangerous place, and that the dangers come from the abstraction at which her argument operates.

Start with the question her essay foregrounds but does not really answer: whose anger are we talking about? The whole tradition Callard surveys — Seneca, Nussbaum, Strawson, Flanagan — treats anger as a generic phenomenon. Someone steals from you; you get angry; we ask whether your anger is rational. But the phenomenology of being wronged once by a peer is very different from the phenomenology of being wronged systematically by an entire social order. Audre Lorde's "The Uses of Anger," Mari Matsuda's work on hate speech, the writings of James Baldwin on Black rage — these are all attempts to think about anger in conditions where the wrongdoer is not a discrete individual who can apologize on Wednesday but a structural feature of the world that cannot be addressed by any single act of restitution.

In such conditions, Callard's Argument for Grudges takes on a different complexion. The "reason to be angry forever" is not a logical artifact about unchanging past facts; it is the entirely live reason that the wrong is still happening. The Black American who is angry about police violence is not nursing a grudge about something resolved on Wednesday. She is responding to a present and ongoing injury. To frame her anger as "moral corruption" — even with the qualification that the alternative is worse — is to misdescribe her situation.

Callard's grandmother provides a different but related case. The Holocaust was not, in any meaningful sense, over for Callard's grandmother. Her son's cerebral palsy, her surviving relatives' nightmares, the destroyed community, the rebuilt synagogue blown up again — these were ongoing presences in her life. Her refusal to allow her granddaughter to march with a German girl was not, I would suggest, best described as colonized fantasy life or as nursing wrath to keep it warm. It was a reasoned (if perhaps mistaken) judgment about the meaning of certain gestures in a world that had not in fact moved past the conditions that produced the Holocaust.

I do not want to deny that political anger can deform those who hold it. It can; the literature on the psychic costs of long-term oppression is extensive. Fanon's writings on colonization, Du Bois on double consciousness, more recent work on racial trauma — all of this is part of the moral landscape Callard is gesturing at. But the response to that literature is not to call the anger of the oppressed "moral corruption" and leave it there. The response is to insist on changing the conditions that produce the anger.

Callard knows this; she would say her point is that, given the conditions, no perfectly clean response is available. Fine. But the framing matters. To name the anger of victims as "corruption" — even reluctantly — places the moral burden in exactly the wrong location. The corruption belongs to the oppressor, who created the situation in which only corrupted responses were available. To redistribute the corruption equally — "oppressors corrupt themselves, but the oppressed are also corrupted, just to a lesser degree" — is to obscure the asymmetry that makes the situation morally legible in the first place.

Here I think Callard's debt to Nietzsche shows. The Genealogy of Morals contains, alongside its real insights, a fundamentally reactionary suggestion: that the morality of the oppressed is constituted by ressentiment, that the slaves invented Christianity to take revenge on the strong, that pity and equality are weapons. This framing has had a long and ugly afterlife. When Callard writes that "the oppressors have made the oppressed morally worse people," she is in Nietzsche's vicinity, and she should be careful about the company she keeps. The claim that suffering ennobles is false; so is the claim that suffering corrupts. Suffering does various things to various people, and the moral assessment of those who suffer should not be loaded onto philosophical schemata about the structure of anger.

Now turn to the politics of Callard's claim about vengeance. She argues that revenge is rational because it "turns the principle of my action into a rule for your conduct toward me." This is a philosophical reconstruction of what cycles of retaliation look like from the inside. It captures something real. But it also abstracts away from the practical question that every political community has to answer: how do you stop the cycle?

Callard's argument suggests that, qua angry person, you have no reason to stop the cycle. Other considerations — prudence, the well-being of others, exhaustion — may give you reasons, but anger itself does not. This is, I think, true in a narrow sense and dangerously misleading in a broader sense. Anger is not the only voice in the wronged person's deliberation; nor is it isolated from the political and ethical resources available to her. Truth and reconciliation commissions, restorative justice practices, the long Christian tradition of forgiveness, the Jewish concept of *teshuvah*, the Islamic *sulh* — these are not external constraints imposed on anger from outside but resources that the wronged themselves draw upon to shape and transform their anger. To treat anger as a freestanding logical structure that "rationally" demands eternal revenge is to abstract it from the cultural and ethical contexts in which it actually lives.

What makes Callard's essay politically dangerous, in my view, is not its diagnosis — much of which is acute — but its conclusion that purification is a fiction. If purification is a fiction, then practical movements like nonviolent resistance, restorative justice, and political forgiveness are exposed as either dishonest (concealing their inner vengefulness) or self-defeating (suppressing the anger that gives them moral force). I think this is just empirically wrong. The civil rights movement was neither dishonest nor self-defeating. The South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission, for all its limitations, was neither dishonest nor self-defeating. These are real human practices that channeled anger into something other than vengeance, without thereby acquiescing in injustice.

I will grant Callard her starting observation: the project of carving anger neatly into "good" and "bad" sides is harder than the philosophical literature makes it look. There is no clean line. The wronged are not innocent. Innocence is not on offer. But the conclusion she draws — that anger is moral corruption all the way down — is too strong, and it lands in a place that disserves the very victims it claims to defend.

The political task is not to declare innocence impossible and shrug; it is to build the practices, institutions, and traditions that allow anger to be morally serious without being eternally vengeful. Those practices exist. They are imperfect. They are not philosophers' fictions. And they are what we need more of, not less.

**A Logical Flaw in the Argument for Grudges**

Callard's essay turns on two arguments she calls the Argument for Grudges and the Argument for Revenge. If these arguments are valid, then we face the crisis she describes: anger is rational all the way to its bloody conclusion, and there is no clean response to wrongdoing. If the arguments are invalid, the crisis dissolves and the more familiar story — that we can distinguish appropriate moral response from disproportionate vengeance — can be reinstated, perhaps in modified form.

I want to argue that the Argument for Grudges contains a fatal equivocation, and that once we see it, the Argument for Revenge loses much of its force as well.

Here is the Argument for Grudges, as I understand it:

(1) On Tuesday, you have reason to be angry at me because I stole from you on Monday. (2) Your reason for being angry is that I stole. (3) On Thursday, after I have apologized and made restitution, it remains true that I stole. (4) Therefore, on Thursday, you still have the same reason to be angry. (5) Therefore, your continued anger on Thursday is rational.

The equivocation is in premise (2). What exactly is the reason for the anger? Callard's argument requires that the reason be the bare proposition "you stole from me on Monday." But this is not actually what anger tracks. Anger tracks something more like "you are now in a state of wronging me" or "the moral relation between us is currently disrupted in a way you have caused."

Consider a parallel case. On Tuesday I am hungry, and the reason is that I have not eaten. On Wednesday I eat. On Thursday the historical fact "I did not eat on Tuesday" remains true forever. Do I have, on Thursday, the same reason to be hungry as on Tuesday? Obviously not. The reason for hunger is not the historical fact of past non-eating; it is the present state of being unfed. Past non-eating is evidentially relevant to present unfedness, but the reason for the affective state is the present state of the world, not the historical record.

Similarly, the reason for anger is not the bare historical fact that you were wronged once; it is something about the present moral situation. And the present moral situation can change. When the wrongdoer sincerely apologizes, makes restitution, and reforms, the moral situation changes — not by erasing the historical fact, which cannot be erased, but by introducing new historical facts that bear on the present. The moral relation between us is no longer in the disrupted state it was in on Tuesday. The wrong has been (as far as humanly possible) repaired.

Callard would presumably reply: but the wrong itself cannot be undone. Yes — but the reason for anger was never the metaphysical demand that the wrong be undone (an impossible demand, as she notes). The reason for anger was the disruption of moral relations. The wrong as a discrete past act is not what anger responds to; anger responds to a disrupted present, which the past act caused. When the present is repaired, the reason for anger has been addressed, even though the historical event remains historical.

This is not a sophistical maneuver. It tracks how affective responses actually work. Consider gratitude, which Callard's argument should treat symmetrically. You saved my life on Monday. On Thursday it remains true that you saved my life. Do I have, on Thursday, the same reason to feel gratitude as I did on Monday? In some sense yes — the fact persists — but the appropriate affective expression of that gratitude is not the same on Thursday as on Monday. I do not throw my arms around you in tearful relief every time I see you for the rest of my life. The reason persists, but its temporal expression is shaped by the changing situation.

Or consider fear. I am afraid on Tuesday because there is a bear in the room. On Wednesday the bear leaves. On Thursday it remains true that there was a bear in the room on Tuesday. Am I rationally required to be just as afraid on Thursday as on Tuesday? Of course not. The fear was responsive to the present threat, not the historical fact of the threat.

Callard might say: anger is different, because anger is moral. But this difference, while real, does not save her argument. Moral facts also are temporally textured. The fact that you wronged me yesterday and the fact that you, having repented and made amends, are now my ally — these are different moral facts, and they call for different responses. To insist on the first while ignoring the second is not rational tenacity; it is selective attention.

There is a deeper diagnosis available here, I think. Callard's argument moves from "the past is unchangeable" to "the present is unchangeable in its moral character." But this slide is illicit. The past is indeed unchangeable; the moral character of the present is not. Repair, apology, and restitution change the moral character of the present even though they do not change the past. To treat anger as tracking only the past is to misunderstand what anger is for.

Now turn to the Argument for Revenge. Callard says that anger holds the wrongdoer accountable by "turning the principle of his action into a rule" — making his bad my good. This is a beautiful piece of philosophical phenomenology, and it captures something real about the dark intimacy of vengeful anger. But it does not establish that this is what holding-responsible *is*; only that this is one psychological form holding-responsible can take.

There are other forms. I can hold you responsible by demanding repair, by telling the truth about what you did, by withdrawing certain forms of trust, by insisting that the community recognize your action for what it was, by refusing to do business with you, by working to ensure you cannot do it again. None of these requires the colonized fantasy life Callard describes. They are forms of holding-responsible that operate through public truth and structural consequence rather than through private vengeance.

Callard would say: but unless I *feel* the colonizing anger, these public actions are merely strategic; they lack the moral charge that anger gives. I am not sure this is true. I can act from a clear-eyed sense that what you did was wrong, that justice requires consequence, and that I will participate in securing that consequence, without my fantasy life being colonized by your image. People do this all the time. Prosecutors do it; judges do it; truth and reconciliation commissioners do it; sometimes victims do it. The moral charge comes from the clarity about the wrong, not from the affective intensity of the response.

Once we drop the equivocation in the Argument for Grudges and recognize that the Argument for Revenge describes one psychological form of holding-responsible rather than its essence, Callard's crisis dissolves. We are left with the more familiar problem: anger is sometimes appropriate, often dangerous, frequently disproportionate, and always worth scrutinizing. This is not a crisis; it is the ordinary terrain of moral life. Nussbaum's transition anger and Strawson's resentment are not philosopher's fictions but names for the responses we are actually trying to cultivate. The fact that we often fail to cultivate them does not show they are fictions; it shows that ethical life is hard.

Callard has done us a service by pressing on the assumption that pure moral response is easily available. It is not. But pressing on an assumption is not the same as refuting it, and the refutation she offers, on inspection, does not go through. The crisis at the heart of anger, I think, is not the one Callard describes. It is the more ordinary crisis of trying, imperfectly and often unsuccessfully, to respond to wrong without being deformed by it. That work is real and hard, and the philosophical tradition Callard dismisses has been engaged in it for a long time.

**Mourning, Melancholia, and the Work of Anger**

Reading Callard's essay alongside Freud's "Mourning and Melancholia" (1917) is illuminating. Freud's classic essay distinguishes two responses to loss. In mourning, the bereaved gradually detaches affective investment from the lost object, painfully but successfully, and emerges able to love and work again. In melancholia, this detachment fails; the lost object is incorporated into the ego, where it becomes a site of unending ambivalent attack. The melancholic does not get over the loss because the loss has become part of who he is.

Callard's essay can be read as making a Freudian argument about anger, though she does not put it this way. Her claim, in effect, is that anger at wrong always tends toward melancholia rather than mourning — because the wrong, unlike a death, cannot be "processed" through grieving but only by either the (impossible) undoing of the wrong or by an incorporated, ongoing relation to the wrongdoer through grudge and revenge.

I want to use the mourning/melancholia distinction to make a different argument: that anger, like grief, admits of both melancholic and mournful forms, and that Callard has identified anger with its melancholic form alone.

What would it mean to "mourn" a wrong? It would mean to feel the wrong fully, to acknowledge its reality, to allow oneself the full force of the injury, and then, gradually and painfully, to detach one's affective investment from the project of making the wrongdoer pay. Not to forget; not to forgive in the sense of pretending the wrong did not happen; but to release the wrong from the present tense, to let it become past, to allow oneself to live again in a present that contains the wrong as a remembered fact but not as a colonizing presence.

This is not what Callard says is impossible. She says that *the reason for anger* persists, and therefore *anger itself* must rationally persist. But the mourning analogy suggests another path. The reason for grief also persists. My friend who died ten years ago is still dead today and will be dead tomorrow. The reason for grief is in this sense eternal. And yet we do not think it is irrational, or even particularly difficult, to feel grief differently at ten years than at one week. The work of mourning is precisely the work of bringing one's affective state into a new relation with an unchanging reason.

Callard might say grief and anger are different because anger has a target — there is someone to hold responsible, whereas death (typically) has no agent. This is true, but it does not undermine the analogy. The target of anger is not infinite or eternal. The target is a person, a particular person who did a particular thing, and who is now (let us suppose) a person who did that thing and has fully repented. The work of mourning anger is the work of bringing one's affective relation to that person into accord with the new reality.

Note that the mourning of anger does not require the wrongdoer to apologize. One can mourn anger toward a wrongdoer who has not apologized, who has died unrepentant, who has even continued to commit the same wrongs. The mourning consists in releasing one's own psychic life from the project of vengeance, not in pretending the wrong is okay. This is what survivors of profound injury sometimes describe achieving — not forgiveness exactly, but a release from the colonized inner life of resentment, even while maintaining clear-eyed knowledge of the wrong.

What Callard describes, by contrast, is melancholic anger. Her account of the "uncanny intimacy" of vengeance — "no one could be closer to you than me," "I've colonized your fantasy life" — is a precise description of melancholic incorporation. The wrongdoer has become part of the self, an internalized object of ambivalent attack. The melancholic cannot mourn because the loss has become identity.

Callard treats this state as the rational form of anger. But Freud, and the long tradition of psychoanalytic thought that follows him, treats it as a pathology — a failure to complete the work of mourning. It is not that the melancholic is irrational, exactly; he has reasons. It is that the reasons are entangled with a refusal to update, a refusal to let the loss become past. Mourning is the alternative, and mourning is hard, and mourning is what therapy, religion, and various contemplative traditions are partly designed to support.

I want to suggest that Nussbaum's "transition anger" is best understood not as a state we are supposed to achieve by some impossible purification, but as the endpoint of the work of mourning a wrong. Initially, anger is hot, raw, and proto-vengeful. With time, support, and inner work, the anger can be transformed — not eliminated, but transformed — into something more like what Nussbaum describes: a clear protest at the wrong, a refusal to acquiesce, but without the colonized fantasy life. This is not a philosopher's fiction; it is what survivors describe, what therapists work toward, what religious traditions offer practices for.

Callard's argument implicitly assumes that what anger is at time T1 is what it must be at time T2, absent some irrational or distracting intervention. But this is to confuse the persistence of a reason with the impossibility of psychic work. Mourning is psychic work, and it does change our relation to unchanged reasons. There is no reason to think anger is uniquely immune to this kind of work.

Now, Callard's most haunting passage is about her grandmother. The Holocaust survivor whose son had cerebral palsy, who lost everyone, who refused to let her granddaughter play with a German child. Was her grandmother's anger melancholic? Yes, I think it probably was. Did she have a kind of right to her melancholia? Also yes. The injuries inflicted on Callard's grandmother were of a magnitude that no plausible work of mourning could fully process. But the fact that mourning is sometimes impossible — sometimes the wound is too great — does not show that mourning anger is always impossible, or that the work has no value when it can be done.

Indeed, one of the most important questions raised by the Holocaust literature is exactly this: how do survivors live? Some live in melancholic anger, some in a kind of numbed acceptance, some in active political engagement, some in something like a hard-won serenity. The variation is not a function of philosophical mistake; it is a function of psychic capacity, support, time, and grace. The serenity, when it is achieved, is not a fiction. It is a real human accomplishment, and it does not require the abandonment of moral seriousness about the wrong.

So I would resist Callard's claim that the alternatives are acquiescence or moral corruption. There is a third option: the work of mourning the wrong. This work is not magical. It does not make the wrong okay. It does not require forgiveness in the sense of restoration of relationship (though it may permit it). What it does is release the survivor from the melancholic incorporation of the wrongdoer, allowing her to live in a present that includes the wrong as past rather than as colonizing presence.

This is, I think, what the contemplative traditions have been trying to teach for a very long time, and what therapy has been working at for a century. To call it a philosopher's fiction is to dismiss too much actual human accomplishment. Callard's essay is right that we should not pretend the work is easy or always available. It is wrong that the work is unreal.

Mourning is not innocence. Callard is right that innocence is not on offer. But mourning is not corruption either. It is something else: the laborious human achievement of carrying a wound without being defined by it. That is what we should be cultivating, naming, and supporting — not declaring a fiction.

**Forgiveness, Grace, and the Limits of Philosophical Analysis**

Agnes Callard's essay is a remarkable piece of philosophical analysis, but it is also, in a way she does not quite acknowledge, a deeply secular essay. The traditions she canvasses — Stoic, Aristotelian, Strawsonian, sentimentalist, even her dark trio of Nietzsche-Foucault-Girard — are all attempting to think about anger and moral response within an essentially human horizon. The possibility she does not seriously consider, and which would dissolve much of her crisis, is the religious one: that the resources for responding to wrong without corruption are not generated by human moral reasoning alone but received from a source outside it.

I want to make this case from a Christian perspective, though analogous arguments could be made from Jewish, Islamic, or Buddhist resources. My claim is not that one needs to share Christian theology to take the point. My claim is that Christian thought, taken seriously, identifies precisely the problem Callard identifies and proposes a solution that is invisible to her framework — and that even those who reject the theology can learn from the analysis.

The Christian tradition has thought about anger and forgiveness for two thousand years. Augustine, Aquinas, Luther, Bonhoeffer, Wolterstorff, Volf — there is a vast literature here. And one of the things this literature recognizes, with greater clarity than the philosophical tradition Callard surveys, is that the human victim cannot, by her own moral resources, generate a fully adequate response to grave wrong. The wrong remains wrong; the victim's anger has its own logic; and no act of will can simply cancel that logic. Callard is right about this. Where she goes wrong is in concluding that, since human moral resources are inadequate, no adequate response is available.

The Christian claim is that forgiveness is possible because it is not, in the end, the victim's achievement. It is a gift, received. The victim does not, by an act of will, abolish her own anger; she opens herself to a grace that does what she cannot. The structure here is important. The Christian does not say: "If you reason correctly, you will see that you have no reason to be angry." That would be Stoicism, and it would fail for the reasons Callard gives. The Christian says: "Your reasons for anger are real and unchanging. And yet, by grace, the anger can be released."

This is not a magic trick. It is the recognition that human moral life cannot be made fully coherent by human moral resources alone — that there is a remainder, a surd, which only something beyond the human can address. The cross, in Christian theology, is precisely the symbol of this. The wrong was real; the wrong was not canceled or undone; the wrong was borne. And in being borne, transformed.

Callard would presumably reply: this is theology, not philosophy. And of course it is. But philosophy that refuses to acknowledge the resources religion has developed for problems philosophy cannot solve will continue to produce essays like Callard's, which arrive at impasses the religious tradition has known about for millennia and has, in its own way, addressed.

Let me put the point less polemically. Callard's essay arrives at the conclusion that "we can't be good in a bad world." This is, in fact, a deeply Christian conclusion. The Christian doctrine of original sin says exactly this: human beings cannot, by their own resources, be fully righteous in a fallen world. Where the Christian and Callard part ways is that the Christian says: but grace can do what we cannot. Callard, having no concept of grace, is left at the impasse.

What does this look like practically? Consider the witnesses. Desmond Tutu, leading the Truth and Reconciliation Commission in South Africa, did not claim that the victims of apartheid had no reason to be angry. He did not claim that their anger was morally corrupting. He claimed something more interesting: that forgiveness was possible, that it was different from forgetting, that it required acknowledgment of the wrong, and that it could be a gift the victim chose to offer — or, when she could not, a possibility she could remain open to.

The witnesses of the Amish community at Nickel Mines, who forgave the man who murdered their schoolchildren, did not claim that the murder was not a wrong, or that they had no reason to be angry. They drew on a tradition of practiced forgiveness that allowed them to release their anger in a way that astonished the watching world. Callard might call this a philosopher's fiction. The Amish would say it is what their tradition has taught them how to do.

I do not want to romanticize. The Amish forgiveness was controversial within the community and outside it; not everyone could enact it; the costs were real. Forgiveness in the Christian tradition is hard, and the tradition recognizes its hardness. It is not a feel-good cliché. But it is a real possibility, attested by real practitioners over a real history, and to call it a fiction because it does not fit a particular philosophical framework is unphilosophical in the bad sense.

There is a moment in Callard's essay where she gestures toward this territory but then turns away. She speaks of the Extreme Stoic and identifies Socrates as the paradigm. But she does not mention Christ, who is the more obvious paradigm in Western thought of a figure who responded to grave wrong without anger — "Father, forgive them, for they know not what they do." Callard's silence here is interesting. It suggests that her framework can only see the response to wrong-without-anger as a kind of detachment-from-the-world (Stoic) rather than as an active form of love (Christian agape).

These are very different ideas. Stoic detachment achieves anger-freedom by reducing one's investment in the world. Christian agape achieves something closer to anger-transformation by intensifying one's investment in the wrongdoer's good, even at cost. The two should not be confused. Callard's Extreme Stoic is a man who places no value on what the world can remove. Christ is a man who places infinite value on the souls of those who are crucifying him. These are not the same posture.

I am not arguing that everyone should become Christian, or even religious. I am arguing that Callard's diagnosis of an impasse in moral philosophy is parochial in its sources. There are traditions of practical wisdom about exactly her problem, and they have produced exactly the kind of response — clear-eyed moral seriousness about wrong, combined with release from the colonizing anger she describes — that she calls a fiction. The fact that her sources do not point her there is a feature of her sources, not of the problem.

The Christian point, finally, is this: human moral life is indeed inadequate by its own resources to the problem of grave wrong. Callard is right about this. But the response is not despair, and it is not the cynical Nietzschean acceptance that we cannot be good in a bad world. The response is to look beyond the resources of human moral reasoning to the resources of practice, tradition, community, and (for the religious) grace. These resources do not make innocence possible — Callard is right that innocence is not on offer. But they make something else possible: a hard-won wholeness, a forgiveness that is not forgetting, a moral seriousness that is not vengeance.

That this is invisible to certain styles of philosophical analysis is a problem for philosophy, not for the practices.

**What the Psychology Actually Shows**

Agnes Callard's essay is a piece of armchair philosophy that makes empirical claims. It claims, in essence, that the phenomenology of anger has a particular structure: anger does not naturally fade in response to apology and restitution; the felt link between the other's evil and one's own good is constitutive; revenge fantasies are rational extensions of moral perception. These are not just conceptual claims. They are claims about how human minds actually work, and they can be checked against the substantial empirical literature on anger, forgiveness, and moral emotions.

I want to summarize what that literature tells us, because I think Callard's picture, while powerful as philosophy, does not match what the psychology actually shows.

First, on the relationship between apology and anger. There is now a robust experimental literature on apology and forgiveness, going back to early work by Robert Enright in the 1980s and developed extensively by Everett Worthington, Michael McCullough, and many others. The basic finding is that sincere apology, accompanied by restitution and demonstrated change, reliably reduces anger in the wronged party. Not always; not completely; not for all injuries; but reliably, and across cultures. This is not a fact that requires explanation in terms of irrational fizzling-out. It is exactly what you would expect if anger were tracking the present state of moral relations (rather than the bare past fact of injury) and updating when that state changed.

Callard's argument requires that any reduction in anger in response to apology be either irrational or merely a function of nonrational fizzling. This is empirically implausible. The reduction is not random; it is calibrated to features of the apology — its sincerity, its specificity, its acceptance of responsibility, its accompanying change in behavior. Anger updates on these features in fine-grained ways. This calibration is exactly what we would expect if the cognitive content of anger included something like "the wrongdoer remains in unrepented violation of moral relations." Apology and restitution change this content; anger updates accordingly.

Second, on the relationship between anger and revenge. The empirical literature on revenge — work by Kevin Carlsmith, Mario Gollwitzer, and others — shows that taking revenge does not, contrary to folk wisdom, reliably make people feel better. In fact, it often makes them feel worse, and prolongs rumination on the original wrong. Forgiveness, by contrast, is robustly associated with better psychological outcomes for the forgiver, including reduced rumination, lower cortisol, lower rates of depression. This is not a moral claim; it is a finding about human psychology.

Now, Callard might say: my argument is not about what makes people feel good. It is about what is rational. Revenge is rational regardless of its hedonic consequences. Fine. But notice that the psychological data tell against her descriptive picture of revenge as the natural completion of anger's logic. If revenge were the natural completion of anger, we would expect it to satisfy the angry person. It does not. What this suggests is that anger is more complicated than Callard's argument allows; that the relationship between anger and its supposed object (the making-bad-of-the-wrongdoer) is not as simple as she claims.

Third, on the cross-cultural variability of anger. The anthropological and cross-cultural psychological literature shows that the phenomenology Callard describes — the colonized fantasy life, the intimate hatred, the eternal grudge — is not a human universal. Cultures vary substantially in how anger is expressed, sustained, and dissolved. The work of Anna Wierzbicka on emotion vocabularies, of Catherine Lutz on the Ifaluk, of Paul Ekman and his critics on basic emotions, all suggests that what we call "anger" in English is not a single cross-cultural phenomenon but a family of related but distinct phenomena. Some cultures cultivate practices that reduce or transform anger; others amplify it. Callard's picture is recognizably ours — recognizably Mediterranean-honor-culture, recognizably modern individualist — but it is not the picture of human anger as such.

Fourth, on the trainability of emotional response. The contemplative neuroscience literature of the last twenty years — work by Richard Davidson, Tania Singer, and others — has shown that long-term meditation practice produces measurable changes in neural responses to negative emotional stimuli. Trained meditators show reduced amygdala activation in response to provocation, increased prefrontal regulation, and (relevant for our purposes) reduced behavioral expression of anger without reduced perception of injustice. This is exactly the combination that Callard says is a philosopher's fiction: clear moral perception of wrong, decoupled from the colonizing affective response.

I want to be careful here. None of this empirical literature settles the philosophical question. Callard could maintain that what the meditators have achieved is not "real" anger-without-vengeance but something else, perhaps moral indifference dressed up as virtue. She could maintain that the cross-cultural variability is variability in how anger is *expressed*, not in its underlying structure. She could maintain that the apology literature shows only that we are skilled at suppressing anger we still rationally have. These moves are available.

But the moves come at a cost. Callard's argument was supposed to be that purified anger is a fiction, that the actual phenomenon of anger has the structure she describes. If she has to discount the empirical evidence of trained, apologized-to, or culturally distinct individuals as not really having the relevant phenomenon, she is increasingly fitting the theory to a particular subset of cases — angry Western individualists in conditions where apology has not occurred — and presenting it as universal.

There is a related point about her Argument for Grudges that the empirical literature illuminates. Callard says the reason for anger is the past fact of wrong, which never changes. But the psychological literature on anger consistently finds that anger has present-tense intentional content: the wronged person represents the wrongdoer as currently culpable, currently threatening, currently in violation. When this present-tense content updates — through apology, through restitution, through demonstrated change — the anger updates with it. The persistence of the past fact does not, by itself, sustain the anger; the persistence of the present moral situation does. Callard's argument requires the past-fact construal, but the empirical evidence supports the present-situation construal.

What does this all mean for the philosophical argument? I think it means that Callard's essay, while elegant, is doing armchair phenomenology that has not been checked against the lab. The picture of anger she paints is recognizable; it captures something real about how anger sometimes goes. But it overstates the necessity of the structure it describes. Anger can be calibrated, updated, transformed, and (in some cases) released, without anyone needing to deny that the original wrong was a wrong, and without anyone falling into acquiescence.

This matters because Callard's conclusion has practical import. If we believe her — if we believe that the alternative to acquiescence is corruption, that purified moral response is a fiction, that the wronged are necessarily damaged — we will give up on practices like therapy, restorative justice, and contemplative training that aim at exactly the transformed response she calls fictional. We will become more pessimistic about the possibility of moral repair after grave wrong. We may even, perversely, license forms of vengeance on the grounds that they are the rational completion of justified anger.

The empirical literature does not support this pessimism. Human beings do, in fact, sometimes manage the difficult work of being wronged without being deformed by it. They do it imperfectly, partially, and with support. But they do it. And the practices that support this work are not fictions; they are real human achievements, with real (if modest) empirical track records.

Callard's essay deserves admiration as a piece of philosophical provocation. As a description of how anger actually works, it is partial in ways that matter. The phenomenology she captures is real but not exhaustive, and the conclusions she draws about the impossibility of purification go beyond what the actual data support. There is more room for moral repair than her essay allows, and the disciplines that pursue it are not chasing ghosts.

**Tragedy, and Why Callard Is Almost Right**

I am going to defend most of Agnes Callard's argument. I think she has put her finger on something deep that the analytic philosophy of moral emotions has indeed been hiding from, and I think the discomfort her essay produces is exactly the right kind of discomfort. Where I want to push is at the very end, at her closing line: "We can't be good in a bad world." I think this is too quick — not because the bad world allows for goodness, but because Callard, for all her radicalism, has not gone radical enough about what tragedy means.

First, let me say what I think Callard gets right, because I think she gets quite a lot right.

She is right that the analytical neat-and-tidy distinction between "indignation" (good anger) and "vengeance" (bad anger) is a kind of philosophical hand-waving. Anyone who has been seriously wronged knows that the affective response does not divide that cleanly. There is no place in actual psychological experience where the morally serious recognition of wrong ends and the desire to make the wrongdoer suffer begins. They are entangled.

She is right that this entanglement is not contingent. The Argument for Grudges captures something real: the reasons that justify anger do not evaporate when the wrongdoer apologizes. Apology and restitution change something — they do not change nothing — but they do not change the fundamental fact of the wrong, which remains, and which remains a reason. The standard story that has us "moving on" treats the persistence of the wrong as if it were psychological inertia rather than rational tracking.

She is right that the Argument for Revenge captures something the philosophical tradition has been embarrassed by. The deep human practice of vengeance — older than any of our ethical theories, present in every culture — is not a malfunction of moral cognition. It is moral cognition, in one of its native forms. The intimate, colonizing structure she describes is real and is recognizably what holding-responsible feels like from the inside, at least in our culture.

She is right that Nietzsche, Foucault, and Girard, for all their differences, are tracking something the analytic tradition has missed: that the morality we have is not separable from its dark side, that the practices of moral life are saturated with the very violence they oppose. And she is right to fault those thinkers for keeping themselves at an anthropological distance from the implications of their own theories.

Where I want to push is at the move from these observations to the conclusion. Callard's conclusion is despairing: we cannot be good in a bad world. I want to suggest a different reading, which I will call tragic rather than despairing.

The tragic reading goes like this. Callard has correctly identified that human moral life contains an irreducible remainder of corruption. Every response to wrong is itself, in some measure, wrong. The wronged person who gets angry participates in the dark side of morality; the wronged person who acquiesces participates in injustice; there is no clean exit. So far Callard is right.

But the despairing reading concludes from this that goodness is impossible, that we are all morally compromised, that the language of innocence is empty. The tragic reading concludes something different: that goodness is not the same as innocence, and that the genuine moral achievement of human beings — when it occurs — consists precisely in the willingness to be morally compromised in the service of something larger than one's own moral cleanliness.

Consider Greek tragedy, which is the form of art most concerned with exactly Callard's problem. Antigone buries her brother and is destroyed. Orestes avenges his father and is hounded by the Furies until the Areopagus institutes a precarious peace. Achilles' rage destroys him and almost destroys the Greeks. The tragic insight is not that the protagonists should have chosen differently — Antigone could not have not-buried her brother; Orestes could not have not-avenged Agamemnon — but that the moral landscape they inhabit makes their actions both right and wrong, and that this doubleness is the texture of human life.

Tragic heroes are not innocent. But they are also not corrupt in the sense of being failed moral agents. They are agents whose engagement with the world has cost them their innocence — and the engagement is what makes them admirable. Antigone is great because she buries her brother knowing what it will cost her. Achilles is great because he chooses the short and glorious life. The tragic vision does not condemn them for their loss of innocence; it honors them for the engagement that required the loss.

I want to suggest that this is the right frame for thinking about Callard's anger problem. The wronged person who gets angry is participating in the dark side of morality, yes. But she is also doing what moral seriousness requires: she is refusing to acquiesce, she is holding the wrongdoer to account, she is asserting that what happened was a wrong. The "corruption" Callard names is not a defect in her moral character; it is the price of moral engagement in a world where engagement requires it.

Callard is so close to this view that I am sometimes confused why she does not state it. She says "we can't be good in a bad world." But within the tragic frame, the better statement would be: in a bad world, goodness has a different shape than it would have in a good world. In a good world, goodness might mean serene innocence. In a bad world, goodness means engaged compromise — the willingness to enter into morality's dark side because the alternative (acquiescence) is worse.

This is not despair. It is a mature recognition of what moral seriousness costs in a world that is not what it should be. The cost is real and should not be sentimentalized. But paying the cost is not failing at goodness; it is what goodness looks like under conditions of injustice.

Compare here Bernard Williams's writings on moral luck and on tragic conflict. Williams argued, against much of analytic moral philosophy, that there are situations in which any choice involves moral remainder, and that the moral seriousness of the agent is shown precisely in her recognition of the remainder, not in her capacity to act with clean hands. The agent who acts and then feels the moral remainder — what Williams called "agent-regret" — is a fuller moral agent than the one who has talked herself out of feeling anything because she has decided she did "the right thing."

Callard's anger is Williams's agent-regret with the volume turned up. The wronged person feels the corruption of her own anger, and the feeling is itself a sign of moral seriousness. She is not the kind of person who can be wronged and shrug; nor is she the kind who can be wronged and avenge without cost. She is the kind who is wronged and feels the wound and feels the corruption of the wound's expression, and lives in the painful awareness of both.

This is what most of human moral life actually looks like, I think. Not innocence, not corruption-pure-and-simple, but engaged compromise lived with awareness. Callard's essay illuminates this beautifully but ends in a wrong key — the despairing key of "we can't be good," when the right key is the tragic key of "goodness in our world has this shape."

What practical difference does the tragic reading make? Quite a lot, I think. The despairing reading leads to a kind of nihilism: since we cannot be good, why try? Or it leads to a defeated relativism: every response is equally compromised, so all responses are equivalent. The tragic reading leads somewhere different: it leads to attention. We attend to the cost of our responses, we feel the agent-regret, we try to minimize the corruption where we can, we honor those who have borne the cost without sentimentalizing them, and we work — slowly, partially — to build a world in which the cost is lower.

Callard's grandmother is not, in the tragic reading, a corrupted woman. She is a moral agent who lived through more than any moral agent should be asked to live through, and whose response — including its rigid refusal of the German child — was the response of a moral agent doing the best she could in a world that had made her best very hard. This is not innocence. But it is also not corruption. It is tragedy, and tragedy is what most of us live in, most of the time.

Callard has done the philosophical world a service by pressing past the tidy distinctions and naming what the analytic tradition was hiding. But the conclusion she draws is too dark, because it measures human goodness against an impossible standard of innocence. Drop that standard, replace it with the tragic standard of engaged compromise lived with awareness, and her crisis becomes the ordinary condition of being a moral creature in a fallen world. Hard, yes. Devastating, no. We can be good in a bad world. Goodness just looks different here than it does in heaven.

**The Question Is Wrong: A Pragmatist Reply**

Agnes Callard sets up her essay as a debate between two camps — Stoics who want to eliminate anger and sentimentalists who want to retain its "moral side" — and then argues that the supposed disagreement masks a shared error: both camps think we can purify anger of its dark side, and both are wrong. The real question, she says, has been hidden: is it rational to bear grudges and seek revenge? Her answer is yes, with the unsettling consequence that moral response is itself a form of moral corruption.

I want to argue that Callard's reframing, while clever, retains the basic shape of the question that has gotten the debate stuck. Both the original Stoic-vs.-sentimentalist debate and Callard's reframing share an assumption that John Dewey would have flagged: the assumption that we can answer the question "what is the rational form of anger?" by introspection on a static feature of moral psychology, abstracted from the practical situations in which anger arises and the practices through which it is shaped.

A pragmatist approach would refuse the question in this form. Anger is not a thing with a fixed rational structure to be discovered by analysis. It is a practical response embedded in social practices, and its rationality is a function of how it functions in those practices, not of timeless conceptual relations between past wrongs and present feelings.

Consider Callard's Argument for Grudges. The argument moves: (1) the past wrong remains a reason for anger; (2) reasons do not evaporate; (3) therefore, the rational anger persists forever. The argument has the form of a syllogism about timeless reasons. But a pragmatist asks: what is anger *for*? What work does it do in human life? And how do its persistence and dissolution figure in that work?

Anger, on the pragmatist view, is a response to disrupted social relations that has multiple functions. It signals to the wrongdoer that a violation has occurred. It mobilizes the wronged party to seek redress. It communicates to bystanders that a norm has been violated. It marks the seriousness of the violation in the wronged party's own self-understanding. And so on.

Now ask: given these functions, what kind of temporal structure should anger have? Should it persist forever, regardless of the wrongdoer's response? Clearly not, because the function of signaling and mobilizing would be defeated if anger could not update on the wrongdoer's behavior. If the apology is sincere and the restitution is real, the social relation has been (largely) repaired, and the signaling-mobilizing function of anger has been (largely) discharged. Anger that persists at full intensity past the point of repair is not better-calibrated anger; it is anger that has lost its grip on the practical situation.

This is not to say that the wrong is "undone" — Callard is right that it is not — but to say that anger's job is not to undo wrongs (an impossible task) but to participate in the practical management of social relations under conditions where wrongs occur. Its appropriate temporal structure is shaped by that practical role.

The pragmatist diagnosis of Callard's argument is therefore: she has taken anger out of the practical context in which its rationality can be assessed and treated it as a logical relation between propositional contents. Once you do that, you get her conclusion: the wrong persists, therefore the reason persists, therefore the anger persists. But this is to mistake the kind of thing anger is. Anger is not a logical relation; it is a practical response, and practical responses are answerable to the practical situations in which they occur.

The same diagnosis applies to the Argument for Revenge. Callard says revenge is rational because it is how we hold responsible — it makes the wrongdoer's principle into a rule binding on him. This is an elegant philosophical reconstruction. But it abstracts entirely from the practical question of whether revenge actually accomplishes the holding-responsible it is supposed to. A pragmatist asks: under what conditions does revenge actually function to hold the wrongdoer responsible? When does it function to escalate cycles of violence that defeat the practice of holding-responsible altogether?

The empirical record on this is clear. Cycles of revenge often defeat the very practice they claim to serve. The wrongdoer, far from being "educated" by being on the receiving end of his own principle, typically re-experiences himself as a wronged party and replies in kind. The cycle escalates. The community is destabilized. The original wrong is multiplied. This is why every functioning legal system in human history has moved away from private vengeance toward public adjudication — not because of some philosophical mistake about anger's logic, but because private vengeance is *practically bad at the job it is supposed to do*.

Callard might reply that this is a separate question from whether revenge is rational. Revenge can be rational as a form of holding-responsible even if, all things considered, it should not be enacted. Other considerations override. But this reply hides the practical bite of the issue. If revenge is structurally bad at holding-responsible, then the "rationality" Callard ascribes to it is a thin and ghostly thing — a logical possibility detached from any actual practical role.

The pragmatist alternative is to ask: what practices do actually work to hold wrongdoers responsible without producing the cycles Callard describes? And the answer is: lots of them, with varying degrees of success. Public adjudication. Truth and reconciliation. Restorative justice. The various practices of communal accountability documented in the anthropological literature. None of these is perfect; all of them have costs; but they are real human practices, refined over generations, and they show that the link Callard draws between holding-responsible and colonizing-anger is a contingent feature of one set of practices rather than a logical necessity.

This pragmatist reframing has implications for Callard's closing claim. She says we cannot be good in a bad world. The pragmatist reply is: the question "can we be good in a bad world?" is poorly posed. There is no view from outside our practices from which we could assess our goodness as a totality. There are only particular practices, particular situations, particular agents, and the question of whether the practices and agents are working well by the standards internal to the practices themselves.

By those standards, lots of things work. Apology can repair social relations. Restitution can address material wrong. Punishment, well-calibrated, can hold wrongdoers responsible without escalating cycles. Forgiveness, when it is possible, can release the wronged from the colonizing anger Callard describes. None of these is perfect; all of them are practices we continue to refine. But they work, in the ordinary pragmatist sense of working: they do the practical jobs they are supposed to do, well enough often enough to keep human communities going.

Callard's essay reads, from a pragmatist perspective, like a metaphysician's lament: things are not perfect by the standards I have set, therefore things are tragic. The pragmatist replies: drop the standards, or rather, replace them with the more modest standards of practical efficacy, and the tragedy looks more like the ordinary difficulty of living together. Hard, yes. Irresolvably tragic, no.

There is one place where I want to agree with Callard against the more triumphal versions of pragmatism. She is right that our practices are imperfect, that the dark side of moral response is real and persistent, and that the philosophical literature has been complacent in pretending we have neat conceptual separations between good and bad forms of anger. The pragmatist should not be smug about how well our practices work. They work badly, often. They fail spectacularly. The wronged often are deformed by their anger; the wrongdoers often escape accountability; the cycles of vengeance Callard describes are real human disasters.

But the response is not to declare goodness impossible. It is to keep refining the practices. To build better institutions of accountability. To support better resources for those who have been wronged. To cultivate the contemplative and communal traditions that help anger find better forms. This is slow, partial, sometimes discouraging work. It is also the only work there is, and it has produced real human goods.

Callard's grandmother lived through the worst that humans can do to humans. That her anger was not innocent is not a deep philosophical truth about the structure of moral response; it is the ordinary fact that she was a human being in a particular situation, doing the best she could with the resources she had. The fact that her best was imperfect does not show that goodness is impossible. It shows that we should build a world in which fewer grandmothers are asked to bear what she bore. That is the practical task. The metaphysics of anger can wait.

In Coherent Darkness: A Defense of Callard’s Tragic Morality

Agnes Callard has given us a disturbing but ultimately persuasive account: morality itself, when activated by injustice, generates an inescapable logic of grudge and revenge. Her conclusion—that victims of wrongdoing are morally corrupted by their entirely rational anger—cuts against comfortable illusions. I want to extend her argument, not by adding new reasons, but by showing how thoroughly this paradox shapes even our most noble struggles for justice. If we accept Callard’s premise, then the moral life is a permanent negotiation with our own corruption, and any movement for social change must reckon with the fact that it not only resists evil but absorbs it.

Callard’s core insight is that the reasons for anger are eternal. The fact that a theft occurred on Monday is not altered by Wednesday’s restitution; the gap between what was done and what ought to have been done remains permanently inscribed in the world. Apology and compensation do not fill that gap; they merely add new events alongside it. Therefore, the reason to be angry is forever, and the desire to make the wrongdoer suffer follows naturally from holding them accountable. This argument, she admits, is not a full-dress proof, but its intuitive force is startling. It brings into view the rational structure behind what we usually dismiss as mere pathology.

Nowhere is this more apparent than in the context of historical injustice. Consider the legacy of slavery in the United States. The ancestors of enslaved people were wronged in ways that no apology, no restitution, no historical commemoration can erase. The fact that chattel slavery ought never to have happened is as true now as it was in 1860. If Callard is right, contemporary anger about that history is not a disorder; it is an impeccably rational response to an unchanged moral fact. And the corresponding demand for reparations, for symbolic acknowledgments, for the overturning of monuments—these can be seen as forms of revenge in the precise sense Callard gives: making the evil of the past into a present rule for the conduct of descendants, forcing the nation to taste the moral wrongness it committed. Even the most peaceable activist who demands that America “live up to its ideals” is, in a sublimated way, imposing the principle of slavery’s badness upon the current body politic, turning the wrongdoer’s logic against itself. This is not to condemn activism; on Callard’s accounting, it is the only fitting moral response. But it is to acknowledge that such activism carries within it the seeds of potential cruelty, the willingness to inflict suffering on others in the name of righteousness.

Callard’s narrative of her grandmother and the German girl illustrates this tension with painful clarity. Her grandmother’s anger was a direct, rational response to the Holocaust; she could not treat a German child as innocent because, in the moral universe shaped by that crime, innocence was not available. Yet the grandmother’s refusal to let her granddaughter play across the fence also marked a moment where the victim, in holding the grudge, imposed a separation that—however justified—replicated a kind of emotional border wall. The granddaughter was damaged by the very anger that honored the dead. Callard’s point is that there was no third path: neither pure forgiveness nor pure rage was possible, only a muddled corruption. In such stories, we see that morality’s demand to remember is indistinguishable from a command to resent.

If this is our condition, what can we do? Callard says we must sometimes get “somewhat angry,” accepting a dose of moral corruption as the price of resistance. This is a realistic and sobering prescription. I would add that recognizing this paradox might actually temper our anger in one crucial respect: it deprives us of the illusion that we can be righteous without cost. Much contemporary moral discourse, especially on social media, operates under the assumption that indignation is a pure and cleansing fire. Callard’s analysis shows this to be a fantasy, and a dangerous one, because it prevents people from seeing the vengeful impulses that inevitably accompany their moral stances. By admitting that we are corrupted by our own sense of justice, we might become more careful about how far we let that corruption go. We might, paradoxically, be better able to set limits on revenge precisely because we understand that morality itself cannot supply them.

Callard has, I believe, made a durable case. Her argument does not solve the problem of anger, but it clarifies why the problem has no solution. If anger is a moral sense, then morality is inherently tragic. To be wronged is to be drawn into a shadow system where the good of the victim and the suffering of the oppressor become entangled. We cannot escape this entanglement, only navigate it with open eyes. And if that means we are never as innocent as we wish, well, that too is a truth worth holding onto.

Beyond the Payback Wish: A Stoic Rebuttal

Agnes Callard’s essay is a powerful challenge, but it is built on a quiet mistake: she assumes that the only alternative to eternal grudges and vengeance is acquiescence in evil. This dichotomy drives her to the grim conclusion that victims are inevitably morally corrupted. The Stoic tradition, which she mentions only to marginalize, actually offers a coherent third way—one that resists injustice without embracing the emotional logic of anger. Callard’s arguments fail because they do not properly understand what it means to judge a wrong without being enflamed by the “payback wish.”

Callard’s first argument, the Argument for Grudges, rests on the claim that the fact of a past wrong never changes. “Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever,” she writes. Apologies and restitution, she says, do nothing to cancel the moral fact that the theft occurred and ought not to have occurred. But this treats reasons for emotion as static entities stored in a museum of morals. The Stoic—and indeed the Buddhist—replies that a reason to be angry is not simply the bare fact of a wrong; it is that fact combined with a judgment about how to respond in the present. On Tuesday, when the theft was fresh and its consequences ongoing, there was a reason to be stirred up and to demand repair. On Wednesday, after sincere apology and compensation, the situation has changed. The past wrong remains a historical fact, but its relevance to my current emotional state is now different. The apology does not erase the theft, but it removes the social meaning of ongoing protest. The wrongdoer has aligned herself with the moral order, and to continue treating her as an enemy is to misjudge the present. The Stoic sage does not forget the wrong, but she evaluates it as something that no longer requires passionate objection. Her emotional state is transformed into a calm recognition that this person once erred and has since made amends. This is not acquiescence, because the sage would still act to prevent future thefts, but she does so without the inner turmoil of anger.

Callard anticipates this move somewhat when she notes that Stoics like Nussbaum try to hive off “transition anger” from full-blooded anger. She dismisses this as a philosopher’s fiction. But the fact that a purified response is difficult does not make it fictional. It is a real psychological achievement, cultivated through disciplines of attention and reframing. The Stoics did not claim that such detachment comes naturally; they developed extensive practices to reach it, convinced that the untutored passions are dangerous. For Callard to simply assert that the “item” referred to by “indignation” does not exist is to beg the question against a tradition that has produced countless testimonies of emotional transformation, from Epictetus, who maintained serenity even under torture, to modern practitioners of mindfulness who report a marked decrease in reactive anger.

The Argument for Revenge is similarly vulnerable. Callard says revenge is the mechanism of holding one another morally responsible; we “educate” the wrongdoer by imposing the principle of their action on them. But this confuses moral accountability with retribution. Moral accountability is fundamentally about requiring the wrongdoer to recognize the moral truth and to willingly reform. Revenge, by contrast, bypasses recognition and simply inflicts suffering. It may indeed force the wrongdoer to taste their own logic, but it does so through a hostile act that further erodes the moral community. The Stoic alternative—already present in Strawson’s account of resentment as a communication—is to express the protest in a way that aims at the other’s understanding, not at their pain. Genuine moral education requires maintaining the other’s capacity to see their wrong as wrong, which is undermined when we adopt their methods. Moreover, Callard’s description of revenge as rational is a dangerous rationalization. The fact that something is “intelligible” does not make it rational in any full sense. A policy of revenge perpetuates hostility indefinitely, as Girard saw, and leads to the very escalations that call for containment. Moral reason, far from endorsing such escalation, demands that we find ways to break the cycle.

The deepest flaw in Callard’s argument is her assumption that being treated wrongly inevitably taints the victim. She says, “oppressors have made the oppressed morally worse people.” This is a counsel of despair. It denies the possibility of moral integrity in the face of evil. Yet history furnishes us with examples—Nelson Mandela, for one—who emerged from decades of brutal oppression without becoming corrupt. Mandela’s lack of vengefulness was not acquiescence; it was a moral strength that helped build a new nation. Callard might say that Mandela was still morally compromised in some subtle way, but that would be to define corruption so broadly that every human being is corrupt, which renders the term useless. The Stoic insight is that our moral worth resides not in what happens to us but in how we choose to respond. The victim who refrains from revenge does not thereby endorse the crime; she affirms her own character and refuses to be shaped by the wrongdoer’s values.

In the end, Callard has shown only that impure anger is common and that the fantasy of complete purity may be unattainable for most people. But the Stoic ideal remains a regulative one: we can strive to diminish the vengeful elements of our anger, and that striving is itself a moral good. The alternative is to accept corruption as inevitable and to set about managing it—a project that, on Callard’s own terms, has no stopping point. That is not a middle path; it is a surrender to the very logic it purports to critique.

The Law's Answer to Infinite Vengeance

Agnes Callard argues that the logic of anger is one of eternal grudge and rational revenge, and that this poses a deep, irresolvable crisis for individual morality. But her analysis, while penetrating at the personal level, overlooks the most important human invention for managing the dark side of anger: the political and legal order. Once we shift our attention from the solitary moral agent to the collective enterprise of justice, Callard’s tragic conclusion loses much of its force. The question is not whether we must choose between corruption and acquiescence; it is whether we can build institutions that channel anger into accountable, finite, and reasonably fair forms of punishment, thereby breaking the cycle she describes.

Callard’s Argument for Revenge holds that revenge is how we hold one another morally responsible. She paints a picture of two individuals locked in a dyad, where the victim must become the mirror of the wrongdoer, making her evil the rule of their interaction. This is indeed a plausible description of what happens in the absence of any third-party authority. In a state of nature, as Hobbes saw, the right to punish is every person’s own, and retaliatory violence spirals endlessly, because each act of vengeance is interpreted by the other side as a new injury requiring its own response. Girard, whom Callard cites, describes this as the sacrificial crisis that ancient societies attempted to solve through rituals of scapegoating and prohibition.

But the modern state solves it differently. By establishing a legal system, we transfer the right to punish from individuals to an impartial arbiter. When someone steals from me, I call the police; I do not set out to steal from them in turn. The criminal trial is an institutionalized form of what Callard calls “making the bad my good”: the state imposes a penalty that is intended to be proportional to the offense and that communicates public condemnation. Crucially, however, this penalty has a built-in stopping point. Once the fine is paid or the sentence served, the matter is legally settled. The law does not pretend to undo the original wrong; it acknowledges that a permanent moral lacuna remains. But it provides a social mechanism for acknowledging that wrong publicly and then moving on. In this way, law externalizes and limits the vengeful impulse, substituting a finite punishment for an infinite grudge.

Callard might object that this is merely a pragmatic compromise, not a genuine resolution of the underlying moral reasons. The victim, after all, may still feel that no prison term can make up for what was done, and on her own premises, that feeling is perfectly rational. But this is where the political perspective reveals a different order of rationality. Within a legal framework, the question is not “Has the moral universe been set right?” but “What rules of interaction permit a society of moral agents to coexist despite inevitable wrongdoing?” The rationality of ending a grudge is not grounded in the metaphysics of past wrongs but in the social goods of peace, cooperation, and the possibility of future moral relationships. Apology and restitution are not irrelevant gestures; they are moves within a game whose goal is to restore civic trust. When an offender sincerely apologizes and makes amends, she signals her recommitment to the norms she violated. The victim who insists on remaining as angry as before, refusing to accept those signals, is no longer acting within the cooperative framework that makes morality a shared enterprise. From the viewpoint of the political community, such a refusal is irrational, because it undermines the very system that protects everyone, including the victim, from the chaos of private vengeance.

This is not to say that legal systems always work justly, or that they adequately address systemic oppression. Callard’s own family history is a testament to the failure of institutions to protect Jews from the Nazis. In cases of deep injustice where the state is itself the oppressor, the political solution breaks down, and victims may indeed be thrown back upon their own moral resources. But even then, the history of successful resistance movements suggests that they aim to establish a new and better legal order, not to persist in a logic of eternal vengeance. The South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission, imperfect as it was, attempted to create a finite, public process for acknowledging past wrongs and allowing victims and perpetrators to live together in a reformed state. The goal was not to erase the reasons for anger but to create conditions under which the continuation of grudge-bearing would itself be unreasonable by society’s lights.

Moreover, Callard’s claim that the oppressed are made “morally worse” by oppression is a dangerous one if it is used to fuel endless retaliation. It can easily become a justification for never laying down arms, because any cessation seems like a surrender to evil. But a political morality recognizes that the oppressed have a stake not only in protest but in eventual reconciliation—not with the oppressor in their oppressing role, but with a community that has renounced that role. The transformation of the wrongdoer is a genuine change, and it is one that the political order is uniquely equipped to achieve. A legal sentence, unlike private revenge, carries a public meaning: it says that this person has paid their debt to society. The victim may still carry personal pain, but the social warrant for anger has been satisfied.

None of this dismantles Callard’s philosophical argument at the level of pure moral reasoning. She may still maintain that, sub specie aeternitatis, every wrong leaves an infinite trace that rational anger would track forever. But if that is the case, then her conclusion is merely that humans cannot be saints who perfectly love their enemies. That much we already knew. The more pressing question is how to arrange our collective life so that the inevitable imperfections of our moral psychology do not lead to permanent strife. On that question, the political response is robust: we channel anger into law, and law into justice, which, though rough, is enough to let most people sleep at night without a grudge.

The Rationality That Time Erases

Callard’s case for the rationality of eternal grudges is seductive because it treats emotional reasons like mathematical truths: once true, always true. But emotion psychology suggests a very different picture of how reasons for anger work. The human mind is not a logic machine; it is an adaptive system tuned to present dangers and social repair. What appears as a “reason to be angry forever” may actually be a cognitive glitch—a failure of emotional recalibration—that most people overcome, and for good practical reasons. Callard’s normatively charged claim that such recalibration is irrational rests on an overly intellectualized model of emotion.

Consider the Argument for Grudges. “What I did will always diverge from what I ought to have done,” Callard writes. “Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever.” But this inference assumes that the only reasons that matter are historical facts, divorced from the ongoing relationship between the parties. In real life, anger is not just a “grasping” of a past brokenness; it is also a motivational state that functions to motivate certain behaviors: demanding apology, signaling threat, enforcing norms. Once the wrongdoer apologizes and compensates, those functions are no longer called for. The anger system is designed—by evolution if not by philosophical design—to dial down when social equilibrium is restored. A person who remains as furious after full amends as before is not demonstrating an especially rational grasp of eternal moral truth; she is displaying a dysregulation of her emotional system, akin to a smoke alarm that blares long after the fire is extinguished.

Empirical research supports this. Studies on forgiveness and reconciliation show that sincere apology and restitution do alter victims’ emotional responses. They do not erase the memory of the wrong, but they change the perceived wrongness of the current relationship. The wrongdoer is no longer seen as a continuing threat or as someone who endorses the original violation. The moral fact “that happened and was wrong” remains a true proposition, but the emotional system, if functioning properly, updates its evaluation of the situation. The reason for anger is not just the abstract wrong; it is the ongoing presence of an unrepentant offender. When that offender repents, the reason dissolves, even if the abstract wrongness does not. Callard’s argument confuses a reason for a retrospective judgment with a reason for a current emotion.

The Argument for Revenge is similarly undercut. Callard claims that revenge is the mechanism of moral accountability, a way of turning the wrongdoer’s principle against her. But psychological studies of revenge find that people who take revenge often do not feel better; they may ruminate more about the offender, and the act can prolong the emotional distress rather than resolve it. This suggests that revenge is a poor tool for achieving what it purports to achieve—namely, a resolution of the moral tension. If a response systematically fails to bring about its own end, we might question whether it is truly rational. Callard might reply that rationality is not about well-being but about fittingness. Yet if revenge is the natural expression of holding someone accountable, why doesn’t it typically produce a sense of closure? The better explanation is that revenge is a misfire of the impulse to restore one’s status, and that moral accountability is better served by forward-looking measures like demanding apology, reforming the offender, or seeking institutional sanctions.

Callard tries to preempt the charge of pathology by calling her arguments “simple and intuitive.” But intuitive arguments can be psychologically naïve. The tendency to bear grudges and seek revenge is indeed common, but commonness does not equal rationality. Many common human biases, like the sunk-cost fallacy, can be described in “simple and intuitive” terms and yet are nonetheless considered irrational by most standards. The fact that we can construct a syllogism to make a grudge seem coherent does not make it a good guide for life.

Finally, Callard’s personal story about her grandmother reveals the high cost of this alleged rationality. Her grandmother’s anger, however historically justified, led her to forbid a harmless childhood friendship, imposing a generational burden that Callard herself seems to lament. If that anger was rational, then rationality can be deeply destructive. At some point, we must ask whether a form of reasoning that consistently produces misery and social fracture deserves the honorific label “rational.” A more psychologically informed view would say that emotions are rational when they appropriately track the current state of our relationships and promote our long-term flourishing. By that measure, the grandmother’s grudge, however understandable, failed. And Callard’s attempt to vindicate it philosophically risks enshrining trauma rather than healing it.

The Grace That Breaks the Cycle

Agnes Callard paints a world where morality is a closed circuit of wrongdoing and vengeful response, offering no exit except a muddled corruption. Her argument is rigorous and, within the secular moral framework she adopts, almost airtight. But the Christian tradition has long recognized precisely this dark logic and insisted that it can only be broken from outside the human moral agent: by the grace of forgiveness. To the Christian, Callard’s crisis is real but not final, because the story of salvation is one in which God absorbs the infinite debt of sin and offers a way for humans to forgive without being corrupted. Her argument, therefore, is a powerful negative apologetic: it shows why a world without divine grace is indeed trapped in the economy of eternal grudge.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges depends on the permanence of the moral gap created by sin. “What I did will always diverge from what I ought to have done,” she writes, and so the reason to be angry persists forever. Christian theology agrees that no finite restitution can undo the ontological reality of a wrong. The Apostle Paul writes, “the wages of sin is death”—a debt that can never be repaid by merely human effort. This is why the Christian account of atonement centers on the crucifixion: Christ, the innocent one, takes upon himself the punishment that would otherwise be visited upon the guilty. On the cross, the moral gap is not erased but transferred and exhausted. In theological terms, God’s justice is satisfied so that mercy may flow. For the human victim who participates in this new life, the anger that was once anchored in an eternal reason finds a new anchor: the belief that the ultimate reckoning has been placed in God’s hands. “Vengeance is mine; I will repay, says the Lord” (Romans 12:19). This is not a suppression of anger but a redirection of it toward the one who can justly bear it.

Callard considers only two options: clinging to anger or acquiescing in evil. But forgiveness, in the Christian sense, is a third and distinct act. It does not deny that the wrong was wrong; it does not paper over the injustice. Instead, it makes a promise to the offender: I will not hold this against you, I will not seek revenge, and I will work toward reconciliation insofar as it depends on me. This promise is made possible precisely because the forgiver trusts that God will deal with the wrong perfectly, either through the repentance of the offender or through final judgment. The victim does not become complicit in evil but rather participates in a divine economy that does not require her to be the agent of retribution. This is not a philosopher’s fiction; it is a way of life attested by countless Christians who have forgiven enemies—from Corrie ten Boom forgiving the Nazi guard who tormented her to the families of the Emanuel Nine forgiving the white supremacist who murdered their loved ones. Such acts often strike secular observers as incomprehensible, even morally suspect. But if Callard is right that the alternative is permanent moral corruption, then the capacity to forgive is anything but suspect—it is liberation.

The Argument for Revenge as the mechanism of moral accountability is also transformed by the Christian lens. Callard says that revenge “educates” by turning the wrongdoer’s principle upon themselves. But this education is coercive and, as she admits, imposes a heavy psychological cost on the avenger. The Christian proposal is that the ultimate education happens through the disclosure of sin in the light of God’s love. The cross itself is the paradigm: it reveals human sin in its horror—the execution of the innocent—and simultaneously offers forgiveness. The goal is to bring the wrongdoer to genuine repentance, not simply to impose suffering. And repentance is a real change: the person who repents is no longer the same moral agent who committed the crime. In the Christian understanding, through repentance and faith, the sinner is “a new creation; the old has passed away.” This is not a mere verbal apology; it is a metaphysical transformation that Callard’s static model of moral identity cannot accommodate.

Callard’s story of her grandmother and the German girl is heartbreaking, but it illustrates the limits of a purely secular morality. Her grandmother, shaped by the Holocaust, could not see the German child as separate from the enemy. Her anger was rational on Callard’s terms, yet it perpetuated a barrier that kept even the innocent locked out. The Christian response would not be to dismiss the grandmother’s anger but to point to a God who can bear that anger and who commands love of enemies—not a sentimental love that forgets the evil, but a costly love that refuses to be defined by it. The grandmother might still have struggled, and fail, to forgive. But the grace of God would mean that her struggle was not without hope, and that her moral standing did not depend on perfectly balancing anger and acceptance.

Callard’s essay, in the end, is a profound testimony to the inescapable tragedy of moral life under the conditions of sin. But if there is a God who forgives, then the cycle can be broken not by our own efforts, but by the infusion of a grace that makes possible a genuine, non-corrupting moral response. That does not make anger disappear, but it places it within a larger story that ends not in eternal grudge but in restoration.

The Relational Heart of Anger

Agnes Callard’s analysis of anger, for all its brilliance, suffers from a deep individualism. She treats moral life as a confrontation between two isolated selves—the wrongdoer and the victim—locked in a zero-sum struggle over value. Feminist philosophers have long argued that such a picture misses the fundamentally relational character of human existence. Once we see ourselves as embedded in networks of care and interdependence, anger reveals itself not as an inherently corrupting force but as a signal for relational repair. Callard’s tragic conclusions arise not from the nature of anger but from the impoverished moral ontology she assumes.

Consider the Argument for Grudges. Callard insists that apology and restitution do not alter the fact of the wrong and therefore cannot erase the reason for anger. But this treats moral facts as inert, free-floating propositions detached from the ongoing relationship between persons. In a relational model, an apology is not a mere attempt to change the past but a transformative act that reshapes the meaning of the past within the present relationship. When I apologize sincerely and you accept that apology, we are not undoing what happened; we are renegotiating the terms of our connection going forward. Your anger was, in large part, a reaction to a rupture in our bond, a betrayal of trust. My apology constitutes a recommitment to that bond, and your acceptance signals that the rupture is no longer a defining feature of our interaction. To hold on to the same intensity of anger after such an exchange is not to honor an eternal moral truth but to refuse the possibility of renewed relationship.

Callard might respond that the moral fact remains, and that is enough for eternal anger. But anger is not a mathematical theorem; it is a human emotion that arises within a context of expectation, trust, and shared life. A feminist perspective emphasizes that our moral identities are constituted in and through our relationships. Wrongdoing damages those relationships, but repair can restore them—not perfectly, but sufficiently. If anger is the appropriate response to the damage, then the appropriate dissolution of anger is the appropriate response to repair. To insist otherwise is to freeze the relationship at the moment of injury, foreclosing the very future that morality is meant to protect.

The Argument for Revenge is even more troubling from a care ethics standpoint. Callard portrays revenge as the mechanism of moral accountability, turning the wrongdoer’s evil logic against her. But this adversarial model of accountability ignores the alternative of holding someone accountable with the aim of bringing them back into right relationship. Accountability, in this view, is not about inflicting proportional suffering but about communicating the wrong in a way that invites understanding, repentance, and eventually reconciliation. This may involve protest, the setting of boundaries, and even temporary separation. But its end is restorative, not retaliatory. Callard’s “uncanny intimacy” of anger is indeed a sign of connection, but she reads it as necessarily hostile. In reality, that intimacy can be the very ground on which transformation occurs. When we care about someone, their wrongdoing wounds us deeply, but our anger can also be an expression of that care—a demand that they be better, for the sake of the relationship. The vengeful impulse is not the only or primary expression of anger; it is often a secondary reaction when the offender refuses to listen.

Callard’s own story of her grandmother and the German girl inadvertently supports this relational reading. She says her grandmother’s anger was a rational response to the Holocaust, but the outcome was a severed connection—not just with the perpetrators but with an innocent child who had no part in the atrocity. The grandmother’s anger, however historically grounded, failed to distinguish between the source of harm and the possibility of new relationship. In relational terms, that anger became destructive precisely because it was allowed to override the complex web of human connection in which even enemies’ grandchildren are, in some sense, neighbors. Callard herself, as a child, instinctively reached across the fence, recognizing a relational impulse that her grandmother’s logic suppressed. The tragedy of the situation is not that anger is essentially corrupting but that trauma can so narrow one’s relational vision that it becomes impossible to see the other as anything but a threat.

If anger is not inherently vengeful, then the dichotomy between corruption and acquiescence collapses. One can protest a wrong without desiring the infinite suffering of the wrongdoer. One can demand change without clinging eternally to the wrong. This is not a philosophical fiction; it is a daily practice for those who live in close relationships where forgiveness, boundaries, and accountability coexist. It is not easy, but it is real. Teaching children to say “I’m sorry” and “I forgive you” is not training them in a fantasy but in the skills of relational repair. The fact that such repair sometimes fails, that some wrongs are too deep for reconciliation, does not mean the project is hollow.

Ultimately, Callard has shown the dark side of a morality built on the model of autonomous individuals in combat. But that is not the only morality on offer. A care-based framework sees anger as a danger signal demanding a restoration of just relationship, and it equips us with practices—apology, forgiveness, and above all, the willingness to stay in connection—that can transmute anger into something that neither corrupts nor acquiesces. The world is not divided into saints and the corrupt; it is filled with people constantly reweaving the threads of connection, one apology at a time.

The Temporality of Moral Reasons

Agnes Callard’s argument for the rationality of eternal anger rests on a claim about the timelessness of moral facts. “What I did will always diverge from what I ought to have done,” so the reason to be angry is forever. This is a powerful move, but it relies on an unexamined meta-ethical assumption: that moral reasons are atemporal, floating free of the changing circumstances of moral life. If we deny that assumption—if we adopt a contextualist or temporalist view of moral reasons—then Callard’s argument collapses and we can see why apologies and time genuinely change the rational status of anger.

The core issue is whether the “ought” of “I ought not to have stolen” is a simple descriptive fact about the past, akin to “Caesar crossed the Rubicon,” or a fact that is deeply connected to present duties and relationships. Callard treats it as the former: a permanent, unchanging truth that stands as a constant grounds for anger. But suppose we think of moral obligations as essentially tied to our actual circumstances, including temporal ones. When I wrong you, I incur a specific set of obligations: to apologize, to compensate, to reform. Once I have done those things in good faith, the moral landscape is not the same. The past wrong remains part of history, but its moral significance for our ongoing interaction is transformed. The “ought” that was violated is no longer a live demand because the conditions that made it an active obligation—my status as an unrepentant wrongdoer—have been removed.

This is not to say the past wrong becomes acceptable. It is to say that the appropriate moral response to a fully addressed wrong is different from the response to an ongoing, unaddressed wrong. To see this, consider a parallel: suppose I borrow a book from you and fail to return it, thus wronging you. Later, I return it and apologize. The fact that I once kept it past due is true, but does that fact give you a continuing reason to be angry, even after I have returned it? Most of us would say no, because the wrong was precisely that I deprived you of the book and broke trust. Returning it and apologizing fulfills the demands that arose from the wrong. The moral reason for anger was not about abstract historical truth; it was about the state of our interaction. Once that state is set right, the reason dissolves. Callard’s example of theft and compensation is no different; it only seems different because she frames it as an eternal gap.

Callard anticipates this when she says apology and compensation are not “forms of redress” that alter the past fact. But this merely begs the question against the contextualist. The contextualist does not claim the past fact is altered; she claims that the past fact, in itself, is not the whole of the reason for anger. The reason for anger was always the fact that a wrong was done *and has not been addressed*. The phrase “has not been addressed” contains an implicit temporal index. Once it is addressed, the complete reason for anger no longer obtains. The persistence of the bare historical fact is beside the point.

The same analysis applies to revenge. Callard argues that revenge is rational because it is how we hold the wrongdoer accountable by turning their principle against them. But this again assumes that the wrongdoer’s moral stance is frozen in time. A sincere apology and amendment demonstrate a change in that stance. The person who pays restitution and promises not to steal again is no longer operating on the principle that it is acceptable to take your goods. To keep treating her as if she holds that principle is to ignore the updated moral reality. Revenge, then, is rational only against the unrepentant. Against the repentant, it becomes cruelty, not accountability.

Of course, one might doubt the sincerity of apologies, especially for deep historical wrongs. But that is an epistemological, not a metaphysical, problem. If the apology is genuine, the moral situation has altered, and with it the grounds for anger. Callard’s framework leaves no room for this because it is, at bottom, committed to a version of moral realism that locates moral truth in facts unaffected by human responses. But a more plausible meta-ethics sees moral obligations as arising from and being extinguished by social processes. That is why forgiveness is not a denial of the past but a recognition that the moral debt has been paid.

If this is correct, then Callard’s tragic conclusion—that victims are inevitably corrupted by their otherwise rational anger—is a product of a flawed picture of moral reasons. We are not stuck with eternal grudges because the reasons for anger are not eternal. Time and sincere repair do not just soothe emotions; they rationally change the moral score. The grandmother in Callard’s story was not irrational for still feeling pain, but she may have been wrong to treat that pain as a continuing moral warrant for wrath against a child decades removed from the crime. A more temporally sensitive morality would allow her to honor the past without being enslaved to it.

Anger says: adjust your behavior, compensate me, show respect, or face consequences.

A person known to “hold a grudge” may be less likely to be cheated.

It is not accidental that human beings across cultures have developed practices of punishment, feud, honor, compensation, shaming, and exclusion. The question is not whether the past fact remains true, but what emotional posture best manages present and future interaction in light of that fact.

If you are already bearing the weight of injustice, and now you are told that your anger about that injustice is itself a form of moral corruption, what are you left with? Not peace. Not dignity. Not even the clean consolation of victimhood. Just a moral condition that Callard herself refuses to name clearly — somewhere between compromised and condemned.

I am not suggesting Callard is wrong. I am suggesting that the implications of her view land differently on different bodies. To tell that woman — or anyone in a comparable position — that her anger is rationally eternal but also morally corrupting is to offer her a philosophical diagnosis without a prescription. And that is, in practice, a kind of cruelty, even if it is also, as Callard insists, the truth.

The essay acknowledges at the end that most of us are "neither willing nor able" to achieve Extreme Stoic detachment. But this concession feels like an afterthought. If Callard is right that the realistic project must be distinguished from the fantasy of purification, then the real philosophical work is not done by the arguments she offers — it is done by the practical wisdom we develop for living inside the tension she identifies. What does it mean to "permit ourselves" anger in the specific situations we actually face? She gestures toward this question but does not develop it.

What I want from Callard — and what I suspect she deliberately withholds — is an account of what to do next. Not a guide to Stoic tranquility, which she rightly dismisses as fantasy. But some honest acknowledgment that living inside the logic she describes is not merely an intellectual exercise. For millions of people on April 22, 2020 — inside prisons, inside abusive households, inside neighborhoods blighted by structural racism — the question of how to relate to anger is not academic. It is the question of how to survive.

Callard's core move — the Argument for Grudges and the Argument for Revenge — deserves to be taken seriously, and I will do so. But I want to argue that her case, while compelling in isolation, rests on a premisedistinction that her own essay eventually undermines.

The Argument for Grudges holds that once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever, because apologies and restitution cannot alter the fact that wrongdoing occurred. Callard is right about this. If I steal from you and then return what I took, the return does not un-steal. The event happened, and it happened wrongly, and no subsequent action can change that temporal fact. So your reason to be angry — that I did something wrong — persists in some logical sense. QED.

But here is the problem: Callard's argument conflates the persistence of a logical fact with the rationality of a persistent emotional response. Just because the proposition "You wronged me" remains true forever does not mean that the psychological state of being angry at that fact is rationally sustained forever. Rationality, even in its most permissive formulations, is not purely logical. It is also concerned with the functioning of the agent — with what allows a person to act, decide, flourish, and maintain meaningful relationships.

Consider an analogy. Suppose I make a promise to you on January 1. The fact that I made the promise is true forever; it is an immutable historical fact. But if I have since honored the promise in every possible way — kept it meticulously, gone above and beyond its terms, apologized for any incidental inconvenience — the rational response to the question "Did you make a promise?" is not "Yes, and therefore you are eternally bound in the way you were on January 1." The force of the promise has been discharged. The fact remains; its normative grip weakens.

Something similar applies to anger. Callard is right that apologies do not un-happen the wrong. But the moral demand generated by a wrong is not exhausted by its initial occurrence. Restitution, genuine apology, and demonstrated behavioral change speak not only to the original wrong but to the moral orientation of the wrongdoer. And orientation matters. A person who genuinely repents and reforms is not the same moral subject as the person who wronged you. Treating them identically — remaining just as angry, holding the grudge without concession — is not, as Callard implies, the height of rational moral responsiveness. It is a failure to track morally relevant changes.

I want to be precise about what I am conceding. Callard is right that there is something bogus about the common rhetorical move in which an apology is treated as a full moral reset — the "I said sorry, so move on" maneuver. That is morally evasive. But the opposite move — the insistence that no amount of amends can alter the original fact of wrongdoing — is equally evasive in the other direction. It refuses to recognize moral change in the wrongdoer, which is itself a failure of moral perception.

The Argument for Revenge fairs somewhat better in Callard's hands, but I think it still overreaches. Her claim is that revenge holds the wrongdoer accountable by refusing to let them "live it down" — by making the one-off opposition between your interests and mine into a permanent principle of interaction. This is a genuinely insightful point. There is something to the idea that allowing someone to forget their wrongdoing means the accountability relationship simply dissolves, and that this dissolution is morally significant.

But accountability and vengeance are not the same thing, and Callard's essay tends to slide between them. Holding someone accountable can take forms that are not vengeful — sustained scrutiny, conditional relationships, public accounting — and these forms need not involve "making your evil my good." The intimate, colonizing anger Callard describes, in which the wrongdoer "infiltrates the patterns of your thought," is not the only way to maintain moral accountability. It may not even be the best way. It is, as Callard acknowledges, psychologically costly and relationally destructive. A more measured accountability — one that recognizes the wrong, tracks the wrongdoer's moral progress, and adjusts accordingly — is both less corrupt and more rational.

None of this rescues the Stoic fantasy of purified anger. Callard is right that we cannot extract the moral sensing from the vengeful tendency. But the inability to purify does not entail that the two are identical in every context. It means we need a more nuanced ethics of anger than either the Stoics or the sentimentalists have given us — one that holds the tension Callard identifies without dissolving into either Stoic denial or vengeful permanence.

What strikes me most about Callard's essay is not her arguments but her method. She has taken two opposing philosophical traditions, identified the shared assumption that structures both, and then shown that this shared assumption is false. I want to approach the question phenomenologically — that is, from the perspective of what it is like to be angry, rather than what anger logically entails. Callard herself offers a phenomenological aside when she describes the "uncanny intimacy" of anger: the way the wrongdoer colonizes your mental life, infiltrates your thoughts, preoccupying you even as you despise them. This is vivid and accurate. But it functions in the essay as evidence for a logical point — that anger is rationally connected to permanent accountability — rather than as a phenomenon worth examining in its own right.

Consider the phenomenology of anger more fully. When you are angry, the world takes on a particular quality. Things appear differently: a comment that would normally pass unnoticed becomes charged with significance; a person who might have been a stranger becomes a presence that fills the room. Anger is not just an attitude toward a proposition — it is a transformation of experience. Martin Heidegger, in Being and Time, described mood as the way "Dasein is disclosed to itself," and anger is one of the most disclosive moods there is. It reveals the world as structured by obligations, as populated by agents capable of fulfilling or betraying those obligations, as charged with stakes that transcend the merely prudential.

This phenomenological point has implications Callard does not draw out. If anger transforms experience in this way, then the question of whether to permit anger is not merely a question of whether a given response is rationally justified. It is a question of what kind of world you want to inhabit — what structure of significance you want your experience to have. Someone who opts for perpetual anger inhabits a world in which moral failures are always present, always legible, always calling out for response. Someone who opts for the suppression of anger inhabits a world that may be more livable but that is also, in a specific sense, morally impoverished — a world in which wrongdoing does not register with full force.

Callard would say that this is just another version of the impossible choice between moral compromise through anger and moral compromise through acquiescence. And she would be right. But the phenomenological approach adds something her logical analysis lacks: a sense of what is actually at stake for the angry person, experientially. It is not just that anger is logically linked to grudges. It is that anger makes the world morally real in a way that no other state quite replicates. The person who has never been genuinely angry at genuine injustice has not fully encountered the moral reality of the situation they are in.

This, I think, is why the sentimentalist tradition Callard discusses has such intuitive pull. When Peter Strawson argues that emotions are the fundamental mechanism of moral accountability, he is not just making an epistemological point. He is describing something we recognize in our own experience: that the moral world is real to us in proportion to the emotional investment we have in it. Anger is not the only morally significant emotion, but it is among the most powerful, and its power is not merely logical. It is experiential. It is the way moral reality feels from the inside.

What does this add to Callard's argument? Not a refutation — her logical points stand on their own terms. But a qualification. The moral corruption of anger is not just a logical entailment; it is an experienced condition, with its own texture and its own rewards. People choose anger not only because it is rationally connected to accountability but because it is, in a way that is difficult to articulate, the morally alive state. The question of how much anger to permit is thus not just a question of how much moral corruption to accept. It is also a question of how much moral reality you want in your life.

Callard's essay is strongest where it engages the philosophical tradition, and I want to engage it there — specifically, to argue that she misreads what the sentimentalist tradition actually requires.

The sentimentalists — Shaftesbury, Hutcheson, Hume, Smith — held that moral judgment is grounded in feeling. This is often summarized as the view that emotions "lie at the bottom of our practices of holding one another morally responsible," to use Callard's phrase. But this summary obscures something crucial: the sentimentalists were not moral relativists, and they were not psychological enthusiasts. They were, in their own ways, as concerned with the rationality of moral feeling as any Stoic.

David Hume, for instance, drew a sharp distinction between the direct passions (desire, aversion, hope, fear) and the calm or "secondary" passions — those that, through reflection and habit, come to function with a regularity that approximates reason. For Hume, the goal was not unmediated emotional response but the cultivation of a trained moral sense — one that feels the right things in the right ways about the right objects. This is not so far from the Stoic ideal of trained emotional response; it differs primarily in its account of what training looks like and what it aims at.

Adam Smith, in The Theory of Moral Sentiments, is even more explicit. Smith argues that the man of perfect propriety — the person whose moral sentiments are fully developed — has a sense of resentment that is calibrated by sympathy and reflection. He does not fly into every passion that arises; he feels anger in response to genuine injustice, calibrated by a sense of what a neutral spectator would approve. This is a sentimentalist account that builds Stoic-style training into the emotional response itself.

The point is not that the sentimentalists would endorse Callard's arguments for grudges and revenge. But they would not accept her framing of them as the logical conclusion of the sentimentalist position. For the sentimentalists, the moral emotions are not just any emotions that happen to arise; they are the product of a process of cultivation, reflection, and sympathetic calibration. The sentimentalist tradition is thus not committed to every form of anger that logically follows from the premise that emotions are morally essential. It is committed to a trained anger, an anger that has been subjected to the disciplines of moral imagination.

This matters because Callard's essay uses the sentimentalist tradition as a foil — the position she is correcting and extending. But she selectively quotes from it, drawing on Strawson's insight about emotions as mechanisms of moral accountability without engaging the subtler strands of the tradition that complicate her project. Hume would have agreed that emotions are the foundation of moral judgment; he would not have agreed that therefore any anger that logically follows from a wrong is justified. Smith would have agreed that moral accountability requires emotional engagement; he would not have agreed that the permanently angry person is the morally ideal person.

I am not suggesting Callard should have written a different essay. I am noting that the philosophical tradition is richer and more contested than her binary framing suggests. The debate between Stoic and sentimentalist is not a simple opposition between erasure and endorsement. There are positions within the sentimentalist tradition that take the cultivation of moral emotion seriously enough to distinguish between anger that is genuinely moral and anger that has become something else — a grudge, a fixation, a substitute for the harder work of moral judgment. Callard is right that the Stoic purification project is quixotic. But the sentimentalist tradition itself contains resources for thinking about trained moral response that she does not fully exploit.

The Buddhist tradition — which Callard discusses only through the lens of Flanagan's appropriation of it — offers a different model. Śāntideva, whom she mentions, does indeed counsel the elimination of anger's seeds. But the Buddhist analysis of anger is not simply Stoic. It does not say that anger is irrational and should be replaced by reason. It says something more subtle: that anger arises in response to a misperception of causality. You are angry because you believe that a person or event is the sole and sufficient cause of your suffering, and that this cause deserves to be punished. The Buddhist correction is not Stoic detachment; it is a re-perception of conditions. When you see the chains of causation that produced the harmful act — the trauma, the ignorance, the structural forces — your anger does not dissolve into acceptance. It dissolves into something more like grief, which is also a moral response but one that does not require an enemy.

This is not the same as the Stoic move Callard critiques. The Stoic says: This event does not concern your inner self, so you should not be angry. The Buddhist says: This event concerns many selves, none of which is the simple author of the harm, so your anger is pointed at the wrong target. The first is a retreat from engagement; the second is a reorientation of it.

I find this reorientation practically useful in a way that Callard's stark dichotomy — between corruption through anger and corruption through acquiescence — is not. The pandemic context makes this concrete. I am angry about the failures of government response, the disparities in who bears the burden of illness and economic loss, the failures of public health infrastructure that were entirely foreseeable. Callard would say my anger is rational, that it is tied to grudges and vengeance, and that it constitutes a form of moral corruption. This may all be true. The grief model is not acquiescence. It is not the false serenity of someone who has decided not to be angry. It is a transformation of the emotional object of moral engagement — from the person who wronged you to the conditions that produced the wrong. This is a genuinely different moral phenomenology, and it is one that the philosophical debate Callard describes has not adequately considered. The Stoics want to eliminate anger; the sentimentalists want to endorse it; the Buddhists want to re-perceive it. That third option is closer to what most of us actually need.

I want to focus on a single claim in Callard's essay, because I believe it is the hinge on which the entire argument turns: "Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever."

This is the Argument for Grudges, and it is stated with confident brevity. But confidence is not clarity, and I want to press on what it actually means. "Have a reason to be angry" is doing a lot of work in this formulation, and I am not sure it can bear the weight Callard puts on it.

On one reading, to "have a reason to be angry" means that a certain proposition is true: that you wronged me, that the wrong was serious, that you were responsible. If this is what it means, then Callard is right that the fact persists — it will always be true that you wronged me. But the question is whether the proposition's persisting truth is a persistent reason for anger. A reason, in the normative sense, is not just a fact. It is a fact in light of which a certain response is appropriate. And appropriateness is sensitive to context in ways that raw factuality is not.

To use an analogy: "He made a promise" is a fact that is true forever. But it is not always a reason to hold someone to a promise. If the promise was made under duress, if the circumstances have changed so radically that keeping it would produce disproportionate harm, if the person to whom the promise was made has explicitly released the promiser from the obligation — in all these cases, the fact persists but the reason dissolves. The fact that you wronged me is the reason I got angry. Whether it remains a reason to stay angry depends on whether the other normative facts have changed: whether you have repented, whether the relationship has been reconstituted, whether holding the grudge serves any purpose that the original anger was meant to serve.

This is not the sentimentalist's move of "letting go" through an act of will. It is a recognition that the normative situation has shifted. When I wrong you and then genuinely make amends, the moral situation is not identical to the one in which I wronged you and did nothing. The wrong occurred, yes. But a relationship of accountability has been established, and that relationship has a structure — it has terms, expectations, and trajectory. The person who remains permanently angry is not tracking a static fact; they are refusing to acknowledge the morally relevant developments that have occurred since the fact was established.

I am aware that Callard anticipates this objection. She says that restitution and apology "do nothing to cancel or alter the fact that I stole." True. But the question is not whether they cancel the fact; it is whether they alter the reason. And in most cases, they do. A thief who returns what was stolen and demonstrates genuine remorse has changed the moral landscape in ways that are not merely psychological but normatively significant. The reason to be angry has not disappeared, but it has been joined by other reasons — reasons of proportion, reasons of relation, reasons of forward-looking justice — that pull in the other direction.

Callard wants to tell a story in which the only morally relevant fact is the original wrong and its logical consequences. But morality is not a logical system; it is a practice, and practices are sensitive to change. The Argument for Grudges mistakes the foundation of a practice for the whole of it.

If the victims of structural and historical injustice cannot permit themselves anger without moral compromise, and they cannot acquiesce without moral compromise, then what form of moral response remains available to them?
