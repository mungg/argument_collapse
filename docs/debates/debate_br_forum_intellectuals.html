Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Robin D. G. Kelley's essay is a powerful indictment of the present and a needed corrective to a sanitized memory of Chomsky's 1967 intervention. The genealogy he draws — from Macdonald and Chomsky through Gramsci, Du Bois, Drake, Cayton, Claudia Jones, William Patterson, Césaire — is not a detour but the main road. The Black antifascist tradition recognized decades before our current commentariat that fascism is not an importation from Europe but a domestic possibility nourished by colonial violence, lynch law, and the suppression of labor. Kelley is right that any account of "the responsibility of intellectuals" written today that does not begin from this archive will be provincial. And he is right that the conditions for dissident speech inside the U.S. university have sharply deteriorated, that the post–October 7 wave of firings, suspensions, and police actions amounts to the harshest repression of academic freedom since the McCarthy years, and that university administrators who capitulated first to donors and then to the Trump administration cannot now plead surprise at the consequences.

Where I want to push back is on the essay's central rhetorical move: the claim that Chomsky's injunction to "speak the truth and expose lies" has been rendered "incomplete" or its power "radically diminished" because we are in a "post-truth" age. I think this concedes too much to the right and, more importantly, misreads the very tradition Kelley wants to recover.

Consider what the organic intellectuals he celebrates actually did. *Black Metropolis* is a 800-page work of empirical sociology. *We Charge Genocide* is, as Kelley notes, a meticulous documentation of "hundreds of incidents of anti-Black violence" framed as a legal petition. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* is an evidentiary demolition of the Dunning School. Patterson and Robeson did not march into the UN with a mood; they marched in with footnotes. C. L. R. James wrote *The Black Jacobins* against an archive Europeans had built to deny that Haitians had made a revolution. These figures were partisans, yes — they had "chosen a side" — but their partisanship was inseparable from a ferocious commitment to documentary truth. They knew that the lies of empire were not merely rhetorical preferences but specific, falsifiable claims, and that exposing them mattered both to the morale of the oppressed and to the conscience of potential allies. To say truth-telling is now "incomplete" risks suggesting that the partisans Kelley admires were doing something other than truth-telling. They weren't. They were doing it harder, and in worse conditions, than most of us are willing to.

The "post-truth" diagnosis is also analytically suspect. The right's narratives — that immigrants depress wages, that the 2020 election was stolen, that anti-Zionism is antisemitism — do not succeed because audiences have become indifferent to evidence. They succeed because the institutional architecture that once translated evidence into shared belief has fractured: local newspapers, broadcast monopolies, faculties insulated from donor caprice, professional norms in mainstream journalism. Audiences have been pre-sorted into epistemic communities by platform incentives and partisan media, and the right has invested far more than the left in building counter-institutions to circulate its claims. The conclusion is not that we should abandon Chomsky's injunction but that truth-telling now requires institutional reconstruction in addition to individual courage. Kelley gestures at this when he praises the Institute on Inequality and Democracy, the Hampton Institute, the Tricontinental, and unionization drives. These are exactly the right targets. But they are infrastructure for truth-telling, not alternatives to it.

A second concern is with the Gramscian framework on its own terms. Kelley admits, candidly, that MAGA has its own organic intellectuals: Yarvin, Anton, Rufo, Bannon, the Project 2025 contributors. They too are "embedded" in a class fraction, articulating its interests and its imagined future. They too refuse the pose of detachment. They too see themselves as choosing sides and fighting. If "organic intellectual" describes anyone who consciously works on behalf of a social bloc, the category cannot, by itself, distinguish liberatory from reactionary intellectual practice. Something else has to do that work — and what does it, I would argue, is precisely the commitment to truthful description of the world that Kelley risks treating as quaint. Yarvin's work is bad not because he is partisan but because his historical claims about democracy, race, and political order are largely false. Anton's "nation of settlers" argument is bad not because it advances a class project but because it misrepresents American demographic and political history. The criterion of evaluation is, in the last instance, the criterion Chomsky named.

A third worry is more practical. The exhortation to "choose sides" is unobjectionable in the abstract; in concrete movement contexts it can underwrite its own intolerance. The Palestine solidarity movement that Kelley rightly defends from doxxing campaigns and university repression has, in places, also enforced its own loyalty tests, with consequences for Jewish, Israeli, and Arab scholars who articulate positions deemed insufficiently aligned. Atalia Omer, whom Kelley names as a casualty of Harvard's capitulation, is a useful example of what the "choose sides" framing can flatten: she is a scholar of peacebuilding whose work resists exactly the demand that one's commitments collapse into the slogans of any single bloc. A genuinely Gramscian sensibility — Gramsci was, after all, the great theorist of the *war of position* — should be more patient with that complexity than the current discourse often allows. Solidarity does not require unanimity, and movements that punish internal heterodoxy tend to reproduce, in miniature, the institutional pathologies they oppose.

None of this contests Kelley's core claim that intellectuals today must be more than commentators; that the university is contested terrain rather than a sanctuary; that academic freedom is meaningless if it does not extend to those whose speech the powerful most want suppressed; or that the Palestinian case is the live test of whether American academic institutions have any moral seriousness left. On all of this he is correct, and his catalog of fired and harassed colleagues should be read as a roll of honor and a warning. Tiffany Willoughby-Herard's question — "What job do I have if the students don't have a future?" — is the right question, and Kelley is right to end there.

But the answer to that question, I'd argue, still includes the older Chomskyan duty alongside the newer Gramscian one. The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide is to organize, yes — to build counter-institutions, to unionize, to plug into movements, to refuse the comforts of detached commentary. And it is still to tell the truth: about Gaza, about the longue durée of American racial violence, about the lies that justify deportation, about the structural conditions that produce billionaire-class authoritarianism. The Black radicals Kelley honors did both at once and would not have recognized the distinction. Our task is not to choose between truth and partisanship but to rebuild the institutions in which truth, told from a position, can again move people to act.

Robin D. G. Kelley’s sweeping and urgent essay rightly insists that Chomsky’s half-century-old formulation of the intellectual’s responsibility—to speak truth and expose lies—is no longer sufficient, if it ever was. In an era of unabashed fascist mobilization, scholasticide in Gaza, and a revanchist right that has built its own dense ecosystem of organic intellectuals, Kelley argues that we must move from truth-telling to taking sides, embedding ourselves in movements, and building counter-institutions. The historical recoveries he performs, particularly of the Black antifascist left of the 1930s and 1940s, are indispensable. They demonstrate that the most lucid diagnoses of fascism’s entwining with racism and colonialism came not from tenured radicals writing in the *New York Review of Books*, but from organic intellectuals of the subaltern classes who understood, as Langston Hughes put it, that fascism was simply a new name for an old terror.

Yet there is a tension in Kelley’s essay, one that is not so much a flaw as a productive difficulty the left must sit with. The difficulty is this: if the power of truth-telling against fascism has been “radically diminished,” as Kelley contends, then what exactly are we doing when we produce essays like his, or responses like this one? If the right is not bound by liberal values, if it manufactures its own “evidence” and operates through emotional mobilization rather than reason-giving, then the act of marshaling historical evidence, making analytical distinctions, and appealing to the conscience of a reading public can appear quixotic. And yet Kelley’s essay is itself a formidable act of truth-telling. It assembles a suppressed archive, names the dead and the deported, documents the machinery of repression from the Canary Mission to Project Esther, and draws a bright line from the antifascism of St. Clair Drake to the campus encampments of 2024. The essay’s very existence complicates its own thesis about truth’s diminished power, and this is a complication worth exploring.

Perhaps the issue is not that truth-telling is impotent, but that we have misunderstood what truth-telling does and for whom. The liberal model, which Chomsky largely inherited even as he radicalized it, imagines truth as a kind of solvent: expose the lie, and the edifice of power will corrode. The fantasy is that the state’s legitimacy depends on its credibility, that there is a public sphere in which reasons can be adjudicated, and that the intellectual’s primary audience is the undecided citizen who can be swayed by facts. But as Kelley’s own evidence shows, the American state and its university administrators have long since abandoned any serious commitment to being swayed by factual exposure. Matthew Miller’s admission about Israeli war crimes—that his podium statements were never expressions of personal opinion but the conclusions of the United States government—is a frank acknowledgment that the state’s representative speech is a weapon, not a bid for rational consensus. The intellectuals aligned with power, whether in 1967 or 2025, are not confused or misinformed; they are, as Kelley says, ideologues who have chosen their side.

What, then, does truth-telling accomplish if it does not persuade the powerful? Kelley offers an answer without quite naming it: truth-telling is an act of movement-building, not persuasion. The value of We Charge Genocide! in 1951 was not that it moved the United Nations to act—it did not—but that it named a reality that Black communities already knew, provided a juridical and moral language for that knowledge, and connected domestic terror to the global architecture of genocide prevention in a way that reshaped the political imagination of the anticolonial left. The document was suppressed, its authors harassed and blacklisted, but it became a touchstone. It told the truth not to the state but to the insurgent class, and in doing so it helped constitute that class as a political subject.

This reframing helps resolve another tension in Kelley’s essay: his simultaneous celebration of the university as “contested terrain” and his catalog of the university’s utter capitulation to donors, the security state, and the Trump administration. If the university is irredeemably compromised, why fight for it? The answer is that the university, like the broader public sphere, is not one thing. There is the university of trustees, endowments, and federal grants, and there is the university of study groups, teach-ins, and encampments. The latter is not a diminished version of the former; it is a different institution altogether, one that is built and rebuilt in moments of collective action. When Kelley points to UCLA’s Institute on Inequality and Democracy as a model, he is not pointing to a reformed administrative unit but to a space that has been carved out through struggle, sustained by militant scholarship, and tethered to movements beyond the campus. The truth produced in such spaces is not addressed to the dean or the provost, though it may be weaponized against them. It is addressed to the people with whom one is in struggle.

This points to the deepest challenge Kelley’s essay poses. If the responsibility of intellectuals is to choose a side and fight, then the intellectual’s primary obligation is not to produce accurate statements about the world but to be *with* others in ways that make collective action possible. Kelley’s closing image—Tiffany Willoughby-Herard, zip-tied and dragging against police, shouting that she has no job if her students have no future—is the essay’s real argument. It is not an argument about the content of her speech but about the location of her body. She has answered the question of responsibility not by saying something true about genocide or fascism, though she has said that too, but by standing between the police and her students. That act of standing with is what transforms truth-telling from a liberal gesture into an antifascist practice.

And here we might gently press on Kelley’s treatment of Chomsky. It is true that Chomsky’s 1967 essay focused on the lies of the state and the complicity of the professoriate, and that it had little to say about the kind of organic intellectual work Kelley recovers. Chomsky has always been more comfortable with the role of the dissident individual than with the discipline of collective organizing. But it is worth remembering that Chomsky himself has spent decades not just speaking truth but repeatedly showing up: at protests, in solidarity movements, in jail cells, in the unglamorous work of answering endless emails from activists. The distinction between the truth-teller and the fighter is not always as sharp in practice as it is in polemic.

For this present moment, Kelley is right that the rubric of “telling truth to power” is exhausted if it means addressing power in a language power has already learned to absorb and neutralize. What remains urgent is telling truth to one another—not in the spirit of self-confirming righteousness, but in the spirit of clarity, strategic analysis, and historical memory. Fascism thrives on the destruction of shared reality, but the counter to that destruction is not simply to insist on facts. It is to rebuild the social relationships within which facts can be collectively interpreted, debated, and acted upon. That is the work Willoughby-Herard was doing on that concrete, and it is the work Kelley’s essay invites us to take up. The essay does not, in the end, abandon truth-telling. It reorients it, toward the only audience that can make truth matter: those who are prepared to fight for a future worth having.

In his piercing and timely essay, Robin D. G. Kelley offers a necessary corrective to Noam Chomsky’s celebrated 1967 manifesto, “The Responsibility of Intellectuals.” Writing from the vantage point of September 2025—a moment defined by the consolidation of a second Trump administration, the aggressive implementation of the Heritage Foundation’s right-wing agenda, and the relentless, catastrophic violence in Gaza—Kelley rightly identifies the limits of Chomsky’s liberal framework. For Chomsky, the highest calling of the intellectual was to “speak the truth and to expose lies.” This injunction rested on an unspoken premise: that exposing the machinations of the state to a purportedly democratic public would shock the conscience of the nation and compel a change in policy.

Kelley shatters this premise. He points out that in an era of post-truth politics, where the American right has openly embraced fascism and unapologetically constructed its own alternative epistemologies, the mere exposure of a lie is politically impotent. When power no longer requires the illusion of moral coherence, truth-telling alone cannot stop it. To remedy this, Kelley reaches for Antonio Gramsci’s concept of the “organic intellectual” and the rich history of the Black radical tradition, arguing that intellectuals must abandon the myth of objective detachment, cast their lot with the insurgent class, and physically enter the struggle. For Kelley, the responsibility of the intellectual today is not merely to speak truth to power, but to help build the power necessary to defeat fascism.

Kelley’s diagnosis of our political reality is flawless, and his call for solidarity is morally urgent. However, in his eagerness to bury Chomsky’s framework and dismantle the "myth of the liberal university," Kelley risks discarding the specific tactical advantages that intellectual labor and academic institutions provide to any resistance movement. While truth-telling may no longer shock the state into changing course, it remains the essential scaffolding upon which any durable counter-hegemonic movement must be built. Furthermore, by framing the university primarily as a compromised terrain to be moved beyond, Kelley inadvertently risks ceding the very institutional fortresses that the fascist right is currently fighting so ruthlessly to capture. The responsibility of the intellectual in 2025 is not to abandon truth-telling or the institutions of inquiry for the picket line, but to weaponize truth and rigorously defend the university as vital fronts in the antifascist war.

First, we must critically examine Kelley’s assertion that "whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished." It is true that pointing out the hypocrisy of the state does not stop the bombs from falling or ICE from conducting mass deportations. But to declare truth-telling diminished is to misunderstand how the right wing operates. If truth were truly powerless, the MAGA political bloc would not expend such immense political capital and institutional energy to suppress it.

The coordinated campaign to ban books, dismantle diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, rewrite history curricula, and fire dissenting professors is not the behavior of a regime that views truth as irrelevant. The Heritage Foundation’s Project Esther and the broader Project 2025 agenda are explicitly designed to criminalize and silence critics because the right understands that historical and material truth—when rigorously documented and widely disseminated—remains a potent threat to their ideological hegemony. When intellectuals stop insisting on the factual record, they surrender the epistemological battlefield to the right’s organic intellectuals—the Stephen Millers, Christopher Rufos, and Michael Antons whom Kelley correctly identifies as the architects of this new fascist bloc.

Kelley grounds his argument in the heroic examples of Black radical intellectuals from the 1930s and 1940s, such as St. Clair Drake, Horace Cayton, William L. Patterson, and Claudia Jones. He rightly notes that these figures did not pretend to be detached; they were explicit antifascists embedded in social movements. Yet, it is crucial to recognize *how* these organic intellectuals fought fascism. They did not abandon the rigorous pursuit of truth; they weaponized it.

When Patterson, W. E. B. Du Bois, and the Civil Rights Congress submitted *We Charge Genocide!* to the United Nations in 1951, they were engaged in a monumental act of truth-telling. The document was a meticulously researched, evidentiary indictment of American state-sanctioned racial violence. Its power derived precisely from its unassailable documentation of facts. Similarly, Drake and Cayton’s *Black Metropolis* was a landmark sociological study. These intellectuals did not view rigorous academic inquiry and movement building as mutually exclusive; they understood that the movement required the unique labor that only intellectuals could provide—the labor of research, synthesis, historical contextualization, and systemic analysis.

If we accept Kelley’s implication that Chomsky’s injunction to "speak the truth" is obsolete, we risk cultivating an anti-intellectualism on the left that flattens the division of labor required in any successful mass movement. Gramsci’s concept of the organic intellectual is instructive here. Gramsci did not simply want intellectuals to join the workers on the barricades. He understood that the bourgeoisie maintained dominance not just through force, but through cultural hegemony—controlling the common sense of society through schools, media, and the church. The task of the working-class organic intellectual was to build a counter-hegemony. This requires deep, often quiet, and sustained intellectual labor. It requires writing the books, compiling the data, and teaching the classes that will shape the consciousness of the next generation. If the intellectual's only valid function is physical confrontation or organizing, then the movement loses its strategists, its historians, and its theorists.

This brings us to the question of the university itself. Kelley paints a bleak but accurate picture of higher education in 2025. It is thoroughly corporatized, heavily reliant on finance capital, and entirely subservient to conservative state legislatures, hostile donors, and the Trump administration's aggressive funding mandates. In the wake of the October 7 attacks and the ensuing student encampments, administrators have capitulated to right-wing pressure, resulting in the mass firing, doxxing, and arrest of scholars advocating for Palestinian liberation. Consequently, Kelley suggests that the myth of the liberal university is shattered and that our efforts must be directed toward "moving beyond the ivory tower" and creating a "new university."

But we must be incredibly cautious about abandoning the defense of the existing university, however compromised it may be. The fascist right is currently engaged in a highly organized, well-funded campaign to conquer American higher education. They are not abandoning the university; they are capturing it. From the hostile takeover of the New College of Florida, which served as a blueprint, to the systematic purging of left-leaning faculty and the installation of right-wing ideologues in university presidencies across the country, MAGA organic intellectuals recognize that universities are engines of cultural reproduction and institutional power.

If progressive, antifascist intellectuals view the university merely as "contested terrain" where they occasionally stage disruptions before inevitably being fired—or worse, if they view academic freedom and tenure as outdated liberal myths not worth defending—they will effectively hand the keys of these vital institutions to the fascists. Defending the traditional tenets of the liberal university—academic freedom, tenure, and the right to free inquiry—is ironically a radical necessity in 2025. These mechanisms were designed, however imperfectly, to protect dissenting voices from the exact kind of state and corporate repression we are witnessing today. To dismiss these protections because they are currently failing is akin to abandoning a fortress because its walls are being breached. We do not need to abandon the fortress; we need to fight to repair and hold it.

We must look no further than the catastrophic situation in Gaza, which Kelley profoundly highlights, to understand the value of academic institutions. The concept of "scholasticide," coined by Karma Nabulsi, describes the intentional destruction of Palestinian intellectual life and academic infrastructure by the Israeli military. The annihilation of every college and university in Gaza is not merely collateral damage; it is a targeted campaign to erase the history, culture, and future of the Palestinian people. Why does the Israeli state target universities with such ferocity? Because institutions of learning are the lifeblood of national identity and resistance. Palestinian scholars and students have consistently fought for their universities, not just as staging grounds for political protest, but as sacred centers of learning, research, and truth-telling.

By recognizing the profound tragedy of scholasticide in Gaza, American intellectuals must also recognize the inherent value of their own academic institutions. If universities are critical enough to the survival of a people that an occupying power will systematically bomb them into rubble, then they are critical enough for us to fiercely defend from administrative rot and fascist takeover at home. We cannot effectively stand in solidarity with Palestinian scholars whose institutions have been physically destroyed if we voluntarily surrender our own institutions to the ideological destruction wrought by the Trump administration and its allies.

Kelley concludes his essay with a moving tribute to his colleague, Professor Tiffany Willoughby-Herard, who was arrested by riot police while trying to protect her students at a Palestine solidarity encampment at UC Irvine. Her words, "What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?" are haunting and profound. Her willingness to put her body on the line is an undeniable act of moral courage.

But we must also ask the inverse question: How can we secure a future for our students if we abandon our jobs, our research, and our institutions? The physical bravery of standing between students and riot police is deeply commendable, but it cannot be the sole metric of intellectual responsibility. A movement against fascism needs martyrs and frontline activists, but it also desperately needs tenured radicals who can leverage the resources of the university to fund insurgent research. It needs sociologists who can document the mechanisms of mass deportation; it needs historians who can expose the long, ugly roots of Christian nationalism; it needs legal scholars who can dismantle the jurisprudence of the radical right; and it needs educators who can teach students how to parse the difference between fascist propaganda and material reality.

Chomsky’s 1967 framework was indeed a product of its time—a time when there was still a functioning, albeit deeply flawed, liberal consensus that could be shamed by its own contradictions. Kelley is absolutely correct that this consensus is dead, murdered by a right-wing movement that views hypocrisy as a virtue and cruelty as a political strategy. But the death of the liberal consensus does not render truth-telling obsolete; it renders it more vital, more dangerous, and more necessary than ever before.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide is a dual mandate. We must, as Kelley demands, cast our lot with the oppressed, organize our workplaces, and integrate our intellectual labor with the material struggles of our communities. But we must do so without losing sight of our specific function within that struggle. We are not just activists who happen to hold degrees; we are the custodians of history, the analysts of the present, and the architects of the theoretical frameworks necessary for the future.

We must not cede the concept of truth to the post-truth fascists, nor should we cede the institution of the university to the right-wing operatives seeking to dismantle it. We must speak the truth, not because it will magically change the minds of those in power, but because it arms the powerless. We must fight for our institutions, not because they are pure, but because they are necessary weapons in a war we cannot afford to lose. Defeating the fascism of 2025 will require all the organizing power, all the solidarity, and all the physical courage we can muster, but it will also require the cold, hard, unrelenting force of the truth.

Robin D. G. Kelley is right to insist that “speaking truth to power” is no longer an adequate description of intellectual responsibility. It was never enough by itself, and in the present crisis its insufficiency is glaring. The facts are not hidden in the way they once were. We have watched Gaza destroyed in real time. We have heard Israeli officials use eliminationist language, seen starvation used as a weapon, seen universities, hospitals, archives, libraries, and schools reduced to rubble. International courts have found the charge of genocide plausible and have ordered provisional measures; human rights groups, UN officials, doctors, journalists, and Palestinian witnesses have provided overwhelming evidence of mass killing, forced displacement, and deliberate deprivation. The problem is not that nobody knows. The problem is that knowledge has not compelled power to stop.

That fact should shake any residual faith in the liberal model of the intellectual as a solitary truth-teller whose task is to correct error in the public sphere. The fantasy was always too flattering. It imagined that lies rule because truth has not yet been spoken clearly enough, that reasonable people will change course once the evidence is assembled, that institutions fail because they misunderstand their own principles. But many institutions understand perfectly well what they are doing. Governments know when they are arming atrocities. University presidents know when they are sacrificing students and faculty to donors, trustees, congressional committees, and federal threats. Editors know when “balance” becomes euphemism. Experts know when their expertise is being used to launder cruelty. In these cases the intellectual’s task is not merely disclosure. It is interruption.

Still, I would put the matter slightly differently than Kelley does. Truth-telling has not become obsolete; it has become inseparable from organization. Fascism does not fear facts in the abstract. It fears facts attached to institutions, movements, solidarities, unions, legal strategies, strikes, boycotts, classrooms, archives, and disciplined political power. A report that documents state violence can be ignored. The same report, taken up by tenants’ unions, student workers, faith communities, nurses, dockworkers, civil liberties lawyers, and elected insurgents, can become a weapon. Truth is not weak because it is truth. It is weak when it is socially disarmed.

That distinction matters because the call to “choose a side,” while necessary, can be dangerous if severed from obligations to accuracy, complexity, and self-criticism. Fascists also choose sides. Empires choose sides. Universities choose sides while pretending to be neutral. The question is not whether intellectuals have commitments; everyone does. The question is what disciplines those commitments. An emancipatory intellectual politics cannot be satisfied with loyalty, even loyalty to the oppressed, if loyalty means saying only what one’s camp wants to hear. The responsibility of intellectuals is not to become propagandists for the good side. It is to help build movements capable of acting truthfully.

That means refusing two temptations at once. The first is liberal innocence: the claim that universities, courts, newspapers, and professional associations are neutral homes of reason temporarily corrupted by outside pressure. Kelley rightly demolishes this myth. The modern university has always been entangled with empire, capital, policing, and racial hierarchy, even as it has also housed dissent. The second temptation, however, is anti-institutional romance: the idea that because institutions are compromised, the only honest work happens outside them. That, too, is false. Institutions are not pure, but they are terrain. A library budget, a union contract, a faculty senate rule, a graduate worker strike, a tenure case, a legal clinic, an admissions policy, a divestment campaign—these are not distractions from struggle. They are forms of struggle.

The campus repression of Palestine solidarity since October 2023 has made this plain. Administrators did not simply “fail” to defend academic freedom. Many actively redefined it as a privilege contingent on political obedience. They treated encampments against mass death as threats to order while treating donor intimidation, doxxing campaigns, police raids, and congressional show trials as normal governance. They invoked student safety while inviting armed police onto campuses. They denounced antisemitism, which is real and must be fought, while often ignoring anti-Palestinian racism, anti-Arab racism, Islamophobia, and the physical danger faced by students marked as supporters of Palestine. In doing so, they taught a brutal lesson: the university’s speech principles are often strongest when least needed and weakest when most tested.

But the answer cannot be to abandon the language of academic freedom, civil liberties, or antisemitism to bad-faith actors. If the right weaponizes antisemitism to criminalize criticism of Israel, then intellectuals must fight that weaponization while also fighting antisemitism itself. If university leaders invoke “safety” to suppress dissent, then dissenters must offer a better account of safety: one that includes Jewish students threatened by antisemitic harassment, Palestinian students grieving murdered relatives, Muslim and Arab students surveilled and smeared, Black and immigrant students familiar with police occupation, and all students whose future is imperiled by militarism and climate collapse. A politics that cannot hold these together will be too brittle for the moment.

Kelley’s recovery of Black antifascist thought is therefore indispensable. Black radicals understood fascism not as an inexplicable European aberration but as a political form nourished by colonialism, racial capitalism, and counterinsurgency. It is disciplined solidarity: telling the truth, joining with others to make truth consequential, defending the vulnerable, refusing complicity, and building institutions capable of sustaining freedom. It requires moral clarity without moral vanity, courage without recklessness toward others, partisanship without contempt for evidence, and hope without guarantees.

Fascism thrives by isolating people—by making each worker, student, migrant, dissident, and scholar feel exposed and replaceable. Genocide thrives by narrowing the circle of grievable life. Intellectual responsibility begins by breaking both spells. No one’s safety can be purchased through another people’s disposability. No institution’s reputation is worth more than the lives it helps endanger. And no truth worthy of the name can remain content with being spoken. It must be organized into the world.

The history of leftist movements is littered with intellectuals who chose sides enthusiastically and were subsequently proven catastrophically wrong—about the Soviet Union, about the Chinese Cultural Revolution, about the virtues of various liberation movements that turned out to be neither liberating nor movements in any meaningful sense.

This is where Chomsky's original emphasis on truth-telling remains essential, not merely as a complement to political commitment but as its necessary condition. The intellectual who surrenders the capacity for independent judgment—who accepts that truth is determined by factional loyalty—has not become an organic intellectual but has instead become an ideologue, a propagandist, a servant of power in ideological clothing. The damage done by such intellectuals is often greater than that done by those who simply serve the state, precisely because their self-righteousness blinds them to their own complicity.

The current moment demands this kind of intellectual humility more than ever. We face genuine fascism in the Trump administration and its allies. We face genuine genocide, or something close enough to warrant the word, in Gaza. We face a global turn toward authoritarianism that threatens not only human rights but the possibility of reasoned discourse itself. But responding to these threats requires that we maintain our capacity for critical analysis, including analysis of the movements we support. It is not enough to stand with students, to join encampments, to denounce repression. We must also ask hard questions: about the strategies being employed, about the coalitions being built, about the longer-term goals that animate current activism. Otherwise, we risk becoming the organic intellectuals of a project that fails, that harms those it claims to help, that repeats the errors of previous movements while congratulating itself on its unprecedented radicalism.

The answer to fascism is not organic intellectualism but democratic intellectualism: the insistence that all claims to truth are subject to scrutiny, that no movement or faction has a monopoly on correct analysis, that intellectuals must maintain their independence not as a privilege but as a necessity for the kind of collective learning that genuine struggle requires. This does not mean abstaining from politics or failing to take stands. It means taking stands while maintaining the capacity to revise them, to acknowledge error, to learn from those who disagree. It means refusing the comfort of factional loyalty in favor of the harder work of genuine inquiry.

The lead essay concludes with a gesture toward solidarity: "we need to always stand in solidarity and fight for others as if our lives depended on it." This is moving but insufficient. Solidarity is not enough; what is required is solidarity combined with truth-telling, political commitment combined with intellectual humility, the willingness to choose sides combined with the recognition that we might choose wrong. This is the responsibility that the age of fascism actually demands.

The lead essay is strongest when it recovers forgotten histories—the work of Black antifascist intellectuals in the 1930s and 1940s, the circulation of radical ideas between colonized and metropole, the connections between colonial violence and domestic fascism. This historiographical contribution is its most enduring contribution, the kind of work that only an academic of Kelley's caliber can perform. But I want to push back against the essay's framing of the contemporary situation, which I think misunderstands both the nature of the current crisis and the resources available for responding to it.

The essay repeatedly uses the language of fascism to describe the Trump administration, its allies, and the broader shift toward authoritarianism. This is not wrong, exactly, but it risks obscuring more than it reveals. Fascism, as a historical phenomenon, was characterized by specific features: mass mobilization, charismatic leadership, expansionist nationalism, paramilitary violence, the fusion of state and party, the total subordination of civil society to ideological imperatives. What we are seeing in the United States is something different: the capture of an existing state apparatus by a movement that combines illiberal nationalism with oligarchic rule, attacks on marginalized groups with the dismantling of democratic institutions, and the use of state violence with the cultivation of a loyal base. It is alarming enough without needing to be classified as fascism.

The dangers of the fascist label are practical as well as analytical. If we define the current situation as fascism, then the question becomes: what is the appropriate antifascist response? The historical answer is united fronts, popular fronts, the building of broad coalitions against the immediate threat. But this is precisely what is difficult in the current context, where the left is fragmented along multiple axes—regarding Israel/Palestine, identity politics, electoral strategy, and the meaning of socialism itself. The antifascist coalitions of the 1930s were possible because there existed significant left-wing movements with mass bases, including communist and socialist parties that could mobilize millions. Today's left is far weaker, more fragmented, and more inclined to treat tactical disagreements as moral disqualifications.

The essay seems to recognize this fragmentation without fully grappling with its implications. It calls for solidarity with "so many movements," from tenant organizing to disability justice to the fight for a livable future. But these movements are not automatically allies; they have different histories, different constituencies, different strategic orientations, and different theories of change. Building solidarity among them requires not just good intentions but political work—the patient construction of common frameworks, shared analysis, collective capacity for action. This is the work that the essay gestures toward but does not fully address.

I also want to question the essay's implicit understanding of intellectual labor. The organic intellectual framework assumes that ideas come from the analysis of experienced intellectuals and are then transmitted to movements. But the most significant intellectual developments of our era have often emerged from movements themselves, from the grassroots theorizing that happens when people collectively confront their conditions. The movement for Black Lives did not simply apply academic theories about racial justice; it produced new concepts, new forms of organizing, new analyses that reshaped intellectual discourse. The same is true of #MeToo, of climate justice activism, of disability rights organizing. Intellectuals can contribute to this process, but they are not its primary authors.

This suggests a different model of intellectual responsibility: not the production of correct analysis for consumption by movements, but the support of movement-generated knowledge. This includes making academic resources available to organizers, providing legitimacy for ideas that emerge from below, helping to document and preserve the history of struggles. It also means being willing to learn from activists, to acknowledge that lived experience produces genuine knowledge that formal education cannot replicate. The academic who thinks she is bringing enlightenment to the masses is probably wrong about the direction of intellectual influence.

Finally, I want to note the essay's almost complete silence on a crucial dimension of the current crisis: the environmental catastrophe that makes all other crises more dangerous and less tractable. Climate change is not mentioned once in an essay that purports to address "the age of fascism and genocide." This omission is not trivial. The coming ecological disruptions will produce mass migration, resource wars, authoritarian responses to social stress, and the breakdown of existing institutions. Any analysis of fascism that ignores climate is like analyzing cancer while ignoring cells. The intellectual responsibility we bear includes the responsibility to engage with ecological crisis, to connect it with the political phenomena we are analyzing, to contribute to the construction of movements capable of addressing both.

None of this diminishes the importance of the historical recovery that Kelley performs. The tradition of Black antifascist intellectuals is a resource for the present, not merely a matter of historical interest. But using that tradition effectively requires clarity about our present situation, honest assessment of the left's weaknesses, and the construction of new forms of intellectual practice adequate to the challenges we face. The age of fascism is also the age of ecological collapse, digital transformation, and resurgent nationalism. Intellectuals who would serve the cause of liberation must develop frameworks adequate to all of these, not just those that fit neatly within existing left frameworks.
