Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Adom Getachew is right to want to rescue something from the wreckage that the word "populism" has become in our political vocabulary. Read enough op-eds and you would think populism were a kind of weather event — a storm that occasionally lashes liberal democracies from outside, against which the only response is shelter. Jan-Werner Müller's influential definition, with its emphasis on the "anti-pluralist" claim to exclusive representation of the real people, has done much to entrench this view, as has the journalistic habit of using "populist" as a near-synonym for "authoritarian nationalist." The result, as Getachew and Michael Denning point out, is a kind of category error: democratic politics necessarily involves the constitution of a people, and the suggestion that one can do politics without ever invoking that "we" is itself a partisan position, usually one that benefits whoever already enjoys disproportionate institutional power. Reclaiming the concept is therefore worth attempting.

But I want to register two worries about the project as Getachew frames it, both of which I think the symposium and the broader left will need to face squarely in the months ahead. The first concerns the analytic burden the word "populism" is being asked to carry. The second concerns whether the structural conditions for a left populism in the United States actually exist, or whether the model on offer — the Chicago Teachers' Union, the Working Families Party, the Sanders insurgency — describes something both more promising and more limited than "populism" suggests.

Start with the conceptual problem. To say that every politician must address and constitute a people is true but thin. It does not yet tell us what distinguishes populism from, say, ordinary democratic mobilization, or from socialism, or from civic republicanism. Ernesto Laclau, the theorist most responsible for left populism's intellectual rehabilitation, located populism in a specific discursive operation: the construction of a chain of equivalences among unmet popular demands and their unification against a frontier — an oligarchy, an elite, a regime. By this measure populism is not merely the appeal to a people; it is a particular way of constructing antagonism. That is a real and useful concept. But it also means populism is essentially formal: it tells us how to mobilize, not what for. Hugo Chávez and Donald Trump both fit Laclau's schema. So did Father Coughlin and Huey Long; so did the Tea Party and Occupy. If the term picks out a strategy rather than a content, then "reclaiming" populism amounts mostly to insisting that left projects, too, may legitimately use this strategy. That is a defensive move against a smear, not yet a positive program.

The trouble is that the smear has bite for reasons that go beyond elite squeamishness. The original American Populists, whose iconography the contemporary left periodically reaches for, are a genuinely instructive case. In the 1880s and 1890s the Farmers' Alliances, the Knights of Labor, and at moments the Colored Farmers' Alliance built a remarkable interracial agrarian movement that came closer to threatening the bipartisan settlement than anything since Reconstruction. The North Carolina Fusion government of 1894–98, in which Populists and Republicans (including many Black voters) governed together, briefly gave the lie to the idea that Southern politics had to be the politics of white supremacy. And then it was destroyed, by the Wilmington coup of 1898 and the disenfranchisement campaigns that followed. Tom Watson, the Georgia Populist who in the early 1890s had appealed openly for Black-white agrarian solidarity, ended his life as one of the most rabid race-baiters and antisemites in American public life. The Populist moment shows that interracial mass politics from below is possible, and that it has a historic tendency, under pressure, to collapse along racial lines. Any serious left "reclamation" has to start there, not from a Denning-style insistence that populism is just a name for democratic politics as such. It is striking that Getachew's introduction gestures at white supremacy as a "challenge," but the deeper point is that white supremacy has been the specific solvent of every previous interracial popular alliance in U.S. history. The mechanism by which that solvent works — the rapid abandonment of working-class whites by elites who suddenly remember their racial solidarity in moments of fusionist threat — is not a residual problem to be managed. It is the central problem.

This brings me to the structural worry. The populist projects Getachew describes — the CTU-WFP-DSA alignment in Chicago, the Sanders campaign nationally, Corbyn's Labour, Morales's MAS — vary enormously in their political-economic contexts, but they share a feature that distinguishes them from their nineteenth- and twentieth-century antecedents. They are not, except in pockets, embedded in dense organizations of working-class life. The Populists had the Alliances and their cooperatives, with hundreds of thousands of dues-paying members and an extraordinary network of lecturers. Mid-century social democracy had mass unions, working-class press, party clubs, fraternal orders. MAS in Bolivia, perhaps uniquely among recent left projects, did build on something like that base — the cocaleros, the neighborhood juntas of El Alto, the indigenous federations — which is part of why it had such durability before November's coup. Sanders 2020 was, by contrast, a campaign organization that tried to function as a movement. The "political revolution" it called for was supposed to bring new constituencies into being through the act of voting. When older Black voters in South Carolina and across the South declined the invitation, the campaign discovered, painfully, that there was no organized social infrastructure it could fall back on to recruit them on a different timetable. Sanders's loss is being narrated as a failure of message — too socialist, not socialist enough, too focused on class, insufficiently attentive to race — but a more sobering reading is that the campaign was attempting to do, in six months of primaries, what the Farmers' Alliances took fifteen years to build, and at a moment when the social materials for that kind of construction are far thinner.

The CTU's trajectory is, in this respect, an exception that proves the rule. It is precisely because the union spent a decade and a half rebuilding rank-and-file power, fusing community organizations to its bargaining campaigns, training a generation of organizers through the Caucus of Rank-and-File Educators, and then leveraging that base into electoral interventions, that Chicago looks the way it does today. The election of Brandon Johnson, the Socialist Caucus on City Council, Jeanette Taylor's path from hunger striker to alderwoman — these are real achievements, and they suggest a model. But the model is, properly speaking, less "populist" than syndicalist-democratic: it builds power from particular workplaces and neighborhoods outward, rather than constructing a discursive "people" against an "elite" and hoping that the social referent of the term will appear when called. The difference matters. The CTU's victories are difficult to scale not because they lack the right rhetoric but because most American workers do not belong to anything like the CTU, and the legal and political conditions for building such institutions have grown harder, not easier, over the past forty years.

This is where I find Getachew's framing of the COVID moment most useful and most worth pushing on. She rightly cites Nikhil Singh: the pandemic will not produce the transformation we want but will set the terms for it. The terms it is setting, however, do not obviously favor populist mobilization of any stripe. Crises in their acute phase tend to produce rally-round-the-flag effects that benefit incumbents — note the recent polling rebound for governors of both parties, and indeed for Trump in March before his April collapse. They license dramatic expansions of executive and surveillance power that are easier to install than to dismantle. They atomize the very forms of assembly — picket lines, mass meetings, door-knocking, congregational worship — through which populist movements have historically constituted themselves. And they hand to incumbents a quantum of fiscal and monetary intervention that, until February, the bipartisan consensus held to be impossible. The CARES Act's $2.2 trillion is, among other things, an enormous demonstration that "how will you pay for it" was always a political question and never an economic one. But the demonstration is being given by Mitch McConnell and Steven Mnuchin, not by Bernie Sanders, and the political beneficiary of having proven Modern Monetary Theory correct in practice may well be the right rather than the left. Getachew acknowledges this risk; I would press it harder. The historical record on crises and left advance is at best mixed: the Depression produced the New Deal in the U.S. but fascism in much of Europe; the 1970s stagflation crisis produced neoliberalism, not its opposite. Which way COVID cuts will depend on prior organization, and prior organization is what the left most conspicuously lacks.

So where does this leave the project of "reclaiming populism"? I want to suggest a friendly amendment rather than a refusal. The most important work the symposium can do is to disentangle three things that "populism" tends to fuse: the rhetorical act of constituting a people, the strategic construction of antagonism against an oligarchy, and the patient organizational work of building the institutions through which a multiracial working class can act for itself. The first is necessary but cheap. The second is powerful but combustible, and historically as available to the right as to the left. The third is the precondition for the other two to amount to anything, and it is the one for which our current vocabulary — "populism," "movement," "campaign" — is least suited. The Chicago example matters because it shows that this third kind of work is still possible. The defeats of Morales, Corbyn, and Sanders matter because they remind us how far short of what is needed even the most ambitious electoral expressions of left populism have so far fallen. Reclaiming populism is worth doing; rebuilding the institutions that could make populism mean something is the larger task.

The appeal of populism is undeniable. In an age of profound democratic dissatisfaction, the language of “the people” versus “the elites” cuts through the noise of technocratic governance and speaks to a widely felt sense of powerlessness. Adom Getachew is right to remind us that every political force must, in some fashion, constitute a people, and that dismissing populism out of hand risks ceding the terrain of democratic mobilization to the right. Yet the push to “reclaim” populism for a left-led, multiracial working-class alignment requires more than a semantic reappropriation. It demands a clear-eyed reckoning with the term’s deep structural tensions—tensions that the recent electoral defeats and the ongoing pandemic have thrown into stark relief. The core problem is not that populism has been unfairly maligned, but that its central category, “the people,” is a vessel that resists being filled with truly pluralist, anti-racist content. The practical task is less about reclaiming a label than about building a politics of solidarity that can survive the gravitational pull of exclusionary nationalism and racial hierarchy, and the failures of the Sanders, Corbyn, and Morales projects are instructive in precisely this regard.

Getachew notes Michael Denning’s axiom that “every politician is a populist,” but this formal observation obscures more than it illuminates. The democratic necessity of constructing a political subject does not make all such constructions equivalent. Populism, as it has operated historically and conceptually, does not simply name any appeal to the people; it characteristically posits a unified and morally pure “people” set against a corrupt “elite,” and in doing so, it flattens internal difference. In the United States, that act of flattening has been inextricable from the project of white supremacy. The “people” of Jacksonian democracy, of the Populist Party itself in the late nineteenth century, and of the mid-twentieth-century backlash against civil rights was always already racialized as white, with its grievances directed not only at economic elites but also at racialized outsiders. Left populists today intend to build a multiracial “people,” but they inherit a political grammar in which the singular voice of the people is most easily articulated through the erasure of minority claims. The question is not whether a multiracial populism is thinkable, but whether the populist form itself—with its tendency toward a majoritarian, homogenizing rhetoric—is the most fertile soil for such a project.

The experience of the Bernie Sanders campaign, which animated so much of the October conference’s hope, makes this difficulty concrete. The campaign advanced a boldly class-focused agenda, and its rhetoric of taking on the billionaire class and the “establishment” was textbook left populism. Sanders also took significant steps to foreground racial justice, hiring a diverse senior staff, releasing comprehensive racial equity plans, and repeatedly denouncing systemic racism. Yet the movement’s electoral coalition hit a hard ceiling defined by race. Despite overwhelming support among young voters of all backgrounds, Sanders was unable to win over older Black voters in the South or enough working-class white voters in the Rust Belt who had previously flirted with right-wing populism. In the pivotal South Carolina primary, Joe Biden’s support among African Americans was decisive, and this pattern held across the Super Tuesday states that followed. The campaign’s class-first frame, for all its sincerity, could not overcome the durable political logic of racial bloc voting, nor could it fully reconcile the tension between a universalist economic message and the particular, lived experience of anti-Black racism that shapes political loyalties. This was not a failure of messaging but a structural feature of a populism that, by invoking a singular “people,” struggles to adequately name and confront the way race constitutively divides the working class itself.

Getachew’s essay wisely points to the Chicago Teachers Union strike as an alternative model, one grounded in local organizing and multiracial coalition-building rather than a charismatic national leader. This is a crucial insight. The CTU’s success grew from years of patient base-building in communities of color, linking demands for school funding to broader fights against austerity and structural racism. The hunger strike led by Jeanette Taylor to save a southside high school was not merely an act of protest but a reconstitution of the political subject around a specific, shared stake: the neighborhood school as a public good. That kind of organizing constructs “the people” not as an abstract, pre-existing entity but as a concrete, plural coalition forged through struggle over tangible demands. It is, in important respects, anti-populist in its method: it eschews the rhetorical shortcut of the unitary people in favor of the messy, slow work of building trust across difference. The challenge is scale. The CTU’s victories are real, but they remain local and depend on the remnants of a labor movement that has been decimated by decades of deindustrialization, right-to-work laws, and the fissuring of the workplace. A national politics cannot simply replicate the CTU model without a massive revival of union density and community-based organizations that can mediate between local grievances and a national vision.

And that vision collides with the hard constraints of the national form. Getachew is right to insist that the American defeat must be reconsidered in a global frame, alongside the ouster of Evo Morales in Bolivia and the collapse of Labour under Jeremy Corbyn in the United Kingdom. Each case exposes a different facet of the populist dilemma. Morales’s Movimiento al Socialismo was the most successful left populist experiment of the twenty-first century, deeply rooted in Indigenous social movements and delivering extraordinary reductions in poverty and inequality. Yet it ultimately fell to a right-wing coup backed by sections of the military and conservative elites, a stark reminder that the institutional power of the state and transnational capital is not easily overcome by electoral mandates alone. The Bolivian experience suggests that populism, even when it genuinely reorders the political field, can be profoundly vulnerable to extra-electoral sabotage. A purely national strategy of winning power, in a world of globalized capital and imperial hierarchies, confronts structural limits that no amount of popular mobilization can easily surmount.

Corbyn’s defeat in the United Kingdom highlighted a different peril: the ease with which left populism can be fractured along lines of national identity and cultural belonging. Brexit tore apart the Labour coalition, pitting older, working-class Leave voters in deindustrialized towns against younger, cosmopolitan, pro-Remain supporters. Corbyn’s attempt to triangulate and focus on economic class grievances satisfied neither camp. Worse, the campaign was engulfed by accusations of antisemitism within the Labour Party, a controversy that exposed how the “people” of left populism can reproduce exclusionary logics even when it explicitly claims to be anti-racist. The Jewish community, a tiny minority, became the symbolic target of a conspiratorial anti-elite discourse that crossed the line from anti-capitalism into anti-Jewish tropes. This was not an accident of personal failings but a warning about the susceptibility of a political style that frames the world as a binary struggle between the virtuous people and a corrupt cabal. Once that door is opened, the definition of the cabal can drift in dangerous directions, and the left’s own house can become polluted.

The current pandemic throws these vulnerabilities into sharp relief. The racial disparities in COVID-19 deaths—in Chicago and Milwaukee, as Getachew notes, African Americans dying at proportions vastly exceeding their share of the population—are not just evidence of healthcare inequality; they are the direct product of a racial capitalism that literally determines who lives and who dies. Any political project that seeks to build a majority around economic justice must treat this fact not as an ancillary concern but as the very substance of its work. A populism of “the people” that promises universal programs—Medicare for All, a federal jobs guarantee, a just transition—will fail unless it also names and attacks the mechanisms that produce premature Black and Brown death. The danger is a color-blind universalism that alienates the communities most in need of state action while failing to convince white voters that their own fate is tied to dismantling racial hierarchy. The Trump administration’s response, marked by xenophobic border restrictions and the scapegoating of China, shows how easily crises can fuel right-wing nationalism. A left response that merely demands a bigger, fairer state, without articulating an explicitly anti-racist, internationalist framework, will struggle to break the grip of this reactionary common sense.

What then is to be done? The Working Families Party’s aim to build a “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” is essential, but it may be better served by abandoning the language of populism rather than reclaiming it. The term comes with too much baggage, both analytical and historical. Instead, we might speak of democratic majoritarianism, of class politics, or simply of multiracial organizing. The substance of the project matters more than the label. That substance must include several elements. First, it requires a permanent, deep investment in base-building organizations that are rooted in communities of color and that connect workplace struggles to housing, schools, and policing. The model is the CTU and community unions, not the presidential campaign rally. Second, it demands a political narrative that treats racial justice as foundational to economic justice, not as a separate plank or a demographic outreach strategy. This means foregrounding solutions like reparations, community control of land and housing, and the defunding and demilitarization of police alongside universal social programs. Third, it must rebuild the organizational capacity for solidarity across borders, pushing back against the nation-state container that populism tends to reinforce. Internationalism cannot be an afterthought; it must be built into campaigns against climate change, tax-dodging corporations, and pandemic profiteering from the start.

The defeats of Sanders, Corbyn, and Morales are not the end of the left. They are a painful education in the limits of a certain style of politics. The COVID-19 crisis has laid bare the brutality of the existing order and created a demand for radical alternatives. Universal health care, once dismissed as pie in the sky, now appears as obvious common sense. The trillions spent on corporate bailouts have destroyed the myth of scarcity. But the window of opportunity will not be seized by a left that relies on the old populist scripts. The task is to build a new political grammar that can hold together a genuinely plural “we”—one that names the specificity of anti-Black and anti-Indigenous violence, that refuses to scapegoat migrants or foreign nations, and that builds power in neighborhoods and workplaces before asking for votes. This is not populism reclaimed but populism transcended. The name for such a politics remains to be invented, but its outlines are already visible in the strikes, the mutual aid networks, and the demands for a just recovery that are erupting beneath the surface of the crisis. Our failure to find a shorthand for it is less pressing than the work of bringing it into being.

Adom Getachew rightly identifies a crucial misstep in contemporary liberal discourse: the tendency to treat populism as an inherent pathology, a dangerous deviation from democratic norms that inevitably leads to authoritarianism and exclusion. By ceding the concept of populism to the right, the liberal center has effectively abandoned the most potent mechanism of democratic politics—the ability to define and mobilize “the people.” As Getachew points out, citing Michael Denning, every political force must constitute a people. Politics without populism is reduced to technocratic management, a sterile administration of the status quo that offers no compelling narrative to those who are suffering under it. In the terrifying and unprecedented context of April 2020, with a global pandemic ravaging our communities and the global economy in freefall, the necessity of reclaiming populism for the left has never been more urgent.

The immediate backdrop to this discussion is one of profound political defeat for the institutional left. As Getachew notes, the collapse of Bernie Sanders’s presidential campaign, preceded by the defeat of Jeremy Corbyn in the UK and the coup against Evo Morales in Bolivia, has forced a painful reckoning. The Sanders campaign represented the most viable attempt in modern American history to construct a left-populist project—a “multiracial working-class alignment” that sought to draw a sharp line of antagonism between the 99 percent and the billionaire class. Its failure to secure the Democratic nomination, culminating in the elevation of Joe Biden, suggests that the mechanics of building this alignment are far more complex than a straightforward economic appeal.

To reclaim populism successfully, the left must confront the specific reasons why right-wing populism currently holds the upper hand. Right-wing populism thrives because it provides a simple, albeit deeply toxic, narrative of exclusion. It defines “the people” through the familiar boundaries of the nation-state and the deeply entrenched hierarchies of white supremacy. When Donald Trump scapegoats immigrants or uses the pandemic to justify closing borders, he is tapping into a pre-existing ideological architecture. Right-wing populism protects the economic elite by directing the righteous anger of the masses downward, toward the marginalized, or outward, toward foreign adversaries.

Left populism, by contrast, must direct that anger upward, toward the structural forces of racial capitalism. But constructing “the people” along these lines is inherently difficult in a society fractured by racial disparities. Getachew highlights the horrifying reality of the COVID-19 pandemic in cities like Chicago and Milwaukee, where African Americans account for 70 to 80 percent of deaths despite making up a fraction of the population. This racialized death toll is not a tragic accident; it is the predictable outcome of decades of redlining, environmental racism, chronic disinvestment in public health, and the disproportionate concentration of Black and Brown workers in low-wage, “essential” jobs that offer no protective gear or hazard pay.

In this context, a left populism that relies solely on colorblind economic universalism will inevitably fail. The Sanders campaign struggled to win over older Black voters in the South not because those voters are inherently opposed to progressive economic policies, but because a populist project must build deep, localized trust to overcome the profound skepticism bred by centuries of structural racism. Left populism cannot simply assume the existence of a unified working class; it must actively forge that unity by acknowledging and fighting the specific, racialized ways that capitalism exploits different communities.

This is where Getachew’s invocation of the Chicago Teachers Union (CTU) is so instructive. The CTU’s strikes in 2012 and 2019 are not merely examples of traditional labor disputes; they are the blueprint for a successful left-populist politics. By engaging in “bargaining for the common good,” the CTU expanded the scope of their demands beyond wages and benefits for their own members. They demanded smaller class sizes, nurses, social workers, and sanctuary protections for undocumented students. In doing so, they bridged the gap between the producer (the teacher) and the consumer (the student and the parent). They effectively drew a populist frontier: on one side was the multiracial working class of Chicago, fighting for the survival of their communities; on the other side was a neoliberal political establishment intent on austerity, school closures, and privatization.

This model of local, rooted solidarity is the foundation upon which any national left-populist project must be built. The pandemic has laid bare the fundamental truth of the CTU’s approach: the well-being of the worker is inextricably linked to the well-being of the community. Today, we are seeing the seeds of this broader struggle in the wildcat strikes and walkouts at Amazon warehouses, Instacart, and meatpacking plants across the country. Workers are risking their livelihoods to demand basic safety protections, exposing the hypocrisy of a system that brands them “essential” while treating them as entirely disposable. A revitalized left populism must capture the energy of these workplace struggles and articulate them as part of a broader fight for a society that values human life over corporate profit.

However, as Getachew astutely observes, the challenge of reclaiming populism is not limited to the domestic sphere. The urgent need to link political realignment at home with a new internationalism presents perhaps the greatest theoretical and practical hurdle for left populism. Historically, populism is a nationalist phenomenon. The very act of defining “the people” usually requires a border. How, then, do we construct a left populism that does not fall into the trap of economic nationalism or xenophobia?

The global nature of the COVID-19 pandemic underscores the absolute necessity of solving this puzzle. The virus does not respect borders, yet the international response has devolved into a grim competition for scarce resources. The United States and other wealthy nations are engaged in a bidding war for ventilators and personal protective equipment, while the Trump administration maintains brutal economic sanctions on countries like Iran and Venezuela, intentionally crippling their ability to respond to the public health crisis.

Frighteningly, the liberal establishment has shown little interest in offering a genuine internationalist alternative. Getachew rightfully condemns the recent campaign ad released by Joe Biden, which attacked Trump from the right on China. By relying on anti-Chinese rhetoric, the presumptive Democratic nominee is validating the very nationalist framing that fuels right-wing populism. This xenophobic posturing not only endangers Asian Americans, who are facing a surge of hate crimes, but it also forecloses the possibility of global cooperation just when we need it most.

A left-populist internationalism must reject this paradigm entirely. It must define “the elite” not as a foreign nation-state, but as the transnational capitalist class—the pharmaceutical executives who fiercely guard intellectual property rights while the world begs for a vaccine; the fossil fuel executives who continue to pollute the planet; the architects of a global supply chain that exploits cheap labor in the Global South to deliver cheap goods to the North. The “people” in this populist equation is the global working class, united by a shared vulnerability to the predations of neoliberalism and the ravages of a warming, disease-stricken planet. Building this international solidarity requires concrete political demands: the immediate lifting of all economic sanctions, the suspension of intellectual property rules for medical supplies, and massive debt relief for the Global South.

Furthermore, the domestic response to the pandemic has permanently altered the terrain of economic debate, creating a unique opening for left-populist policies. For decades, the primary weapon deployed against the left’s vision for a robust welfare state was the disingenuous question: “How will you pay for it?” Whether the demand was Medicare for All, a Green New Deal, or universal higher education, the gatekeepers of neoliberal austerity insisted that the state simply could not afford it.

In the span of a few weeks in March and April 2020, that myth has been pulverized. Congress passed the CARES Act, injecting trillions of dollars into the economy virtually overnight. The Federal Reserve has promised unlimited quantitative easing to prop up the financial markets. The sudden, immense mobilization of state resources proves that the constraints on public spending were never economic; they were entirely political.

This is a dangerous and highly volatile moment. As Getachew notes, quoting Nikhil Singh, the pandemic will not automatically create the social transformation we seek; it only sets the terms. The right is highly adaptable. There is a very real danger of a right-wing drift toward "welfare chauvinism"—a system where a muscular state provides economic relief and social protections exclusively to a favored, racially defined segment of the population, while maintaining brutal exclusion at the borders and in the carceral system. We are already seeing glimpses of this in the way the CARES Act excluded undocumented taxpayers from receiving stimulus checks, effectively weaponizing state aid.

To combat this, the left must aggressively champion a populism of radical inclusion. If universal health care is an obvious reply to a public health crisis, then we must fight for a system that covers every single person within our borders, regardless of immigration status or employment. If mass incarceration is a public health catastrophe, as the exploding infection rates in jails and prisons demonstrate, then decarceration must become a central pillar of our public health strategy.

Ultimately, reclaiming populism is about having the courage to abandon the politics of polite consensus and embrace the necessity of political conflict. The liberal desire for a return to "normalcy" under a Biden administration is a profound misreading of the historical moment. Normalcy was the gig economy; normalcy was half a million people bankrupt every year from medical debt; normalcy was the systematic defunding of public schools and the hyper-militarization of police. Normalcy is what left us entirely defenseless in the face of this pandemic.

We cannot afford to go back. The task ahead is daunting, especially in the wake of recent electoral defeats and in the midst of staggering grief and economic anxiety. But the elements for a multiracial, working-class majority are present. The Trouble with Reclaiming a Concept

Adom Getachew opens her essay by quoting Michael Denning's pleasantly disarming line: "Every politician is a populist." It is the kind of formulation that has the ring of common sense and the function of an evasion. Yes, every politician courts voters; yes, every democratic appeal invokes some figure of "the people." But to slide from this banality to the conclusion that populism is a defensible — even necessary — political form for the left is to perform a sleight of hand. The critics of populism have not been worrying about the trivial fact that candidates campaign in voting districts. They have been worrying about something much more specific: a political style that claims an exclusive moral monopoly on representing "the real people," that treats opposition as illegitimate by definition, and that erodes the institutional checks that allow political losers to remain political actors. These are not pedantic concerns invented by anxious centrists. They are descriptions of what populism, in its actually existing twenty-first-century forms, has tended to do.

I want to make a case that seems unfashionable on the contemporary American left: the project of "reclaiming populism" is more likely to corrupt the left than to revitalize it. The case rests on three claims. First, populism as a concept does meaningful analytic work only when it names something more specific than democratic mass politics — and what it names, when properly specified, is incompatible with the multiracial, pluralist future Getachew and her co-thinkers want to build. Second, the historical record of left populism, including some of the cases Getachew gestures toward, is not as encouraging as the rhetoric suggests. Third, the actual political weaknesses of the left in 2020 — the collapse of unions, the fragmentation of working-class identity, the geographic sorting of the electorate — are not problems populism is well-suited to solve. They demand patient institutional work, not rhetorical reanimation.

Begin with the concept. Jan-Werner Müller's now-familiar definition of populism is not a smear but a tool: populism is an anti-pluralist politics that claims to represent the singular, authentic people against a corrupt elite, and that treats any electoral or institutional loss as evidence of betrayal by that elite rather than as a legitimate outcome. This is what unites Erdoğan, Orbán, Modi, Trump, and Bolsonaro across very different ideological terrains. Notice what this definition does not include: redistributive economics, mobilization of the poor, criticism of oligarchy, anti-imperialism, or any number of substantive left commitments. One can be a passionate egalitarian without being a populist. Eugene Debs was a socialist who built mass support without claiming that he and he alone embodied the will of "the real America." Many of the most successful European social democracies of the twentieth century were built not by populists but by patient coalition-builders who took pluralism — including the pluralism of the working class itself — as a starting condition.

The temptation to "reclaim" populism rests on the assumption that the term's anti-pluralist core can be drained off and only its mobilizational energy retained. But concepts are sticky. To call your politics populist is to invite the friend/enemy logic, the moralized division between authentic people and corrupt elite, that has tended to overwhelm whatever substantive program populists begin with. Hugo Chávez began with a real redistributive agenda; the regime he bequeathed to Maduro is a cautionary tale about what happens when you build institutions around the premise that opposition is illegitimate. One can argue this was a deviation, an accident of personality, the work of external sabotage — but the structural vulnerability was there from the start, and it was a vulnerability populism as a form invites.

Getachew points to the recent setbacks of three left projects — Morales in Bolivia, Corbyn in the UK, Sanders in the United States — and asks us to think about them in a global frame. Fair enough. But the comparative frame is not flattering to the populist hypothesis. Morales, whose ouster Getachew rightly calls a coup, had also pushed through a constitutional reinterpretation allowing himself a fourth term in violation of the result of a referendum he himself called. Whatever one thinks of the coup that followed — and I think it was indefensible — the prior erosion of constitutional self-limitation was real, and it was the kind of erosion populist logic specifically encourages. Corbyn's Labour was defeated in part because it could not assemble a pluralist coalition: it lost working-class Leave voters in the north and midlands while failing to consolidate Remain voters elsewhere, and the populist insistence on Corbyn as a singular embodiment of "the many against the few" made it difficult to compromise on the very issue — Brexit — that was tearing his coalition apart. Sanders, similarly, ran a campaign that often relied on the rhetorical structure of populist appeal — a corrupt establishment, an authentic movement, a singular leader — and discovered in primary after primary that large parts of the Democratic electorate, including substantial blocs of older Black voters, did not recognize themselves in that "people." The failure was not just tactical. It was a failure of pluralism within the left's own constituency.

The dangers of populism are not abstract. To respond to this politics with a populism of our own — even one that aspires to be multiracial and progressive — is to accept the terms of his battle and to relinquish the only ground from which he can be defeated: the patient, pluralist, institutional ground of democratic constitutionalism. That ground is not glamorous. It is not insurgent. It is not "movement." But it is, I think, where the left's future actually lies.

Adom Getachew's framing essay is generous, careful, and politically serious. What the left needs is not a populism but a class politics adequate to the twenty-first century — and these are not the same thing.

Let me say first what I take to be the strongest case for the populist framing, because I want to do it justice before quarreling with it. The strongest case, which Ernesto Laclau spent much of his career elaborating, runs roughly like this: politics is irreducibly a matter of constituting collective subjects, and "the people" is the name of one such constitution. Because society does not have a pre-given unity — because the working class, for instance, is fractured by race, sex, sector, region, citizenship status — political subjects must be built through what Laclau called the articulation of disparate demands into a chain of equivalence. Populism, on this account, is not a deviation from democratic politics but its constitutive form: the moment in which scattered grievances are bound together into a "we" that can act. To refuse populism is to refuse the work of constituting collective subjects at all, which means ceding the field to whichever forces are willing to do that work — which today means the right.

I think this argument is partly right and partly misleading. It is right that collective subjects must be built and that they do not pre-exist political action. But it is misleading in its suggestion that the only or best way to build such subjects is through the populist form — the binary of people-versus-elite, the moralized opposition, the mobilization around affect rather than interest. The history of the left contains many other modes of subject-formation: the patient organizing of industrial unions in the 1930s, which built solidarity across ethnic and craft lines through institutional rather than charismatic means; the civil rights movement's church-based, locally rooted, theologically dense mass mobilization; the various twentieth-century socialist parties of Europe, which created entire counter-publics — newspapers, choirs, sports clubs, mutual aid societies — through which class identity was lived rather than performed. These were not populist projects in any meaningful sense. They built peoples without calling themselves populist, and the peoples they built were arguably more durable than the peoples populism builds.

Why is this distinction worth insisting on? Because populism, even at its theoretical best, tends to organize political identity around the figure of the leader and the moment of the rally — around what Laclau himself called the "empty signifier" that gives unity to disparate demands. This emphasis has consequences. It produces movements that are remarkably mobilized but institutionally thin, that can fill a stadium but cannot run a school board, that depend on the affective charisma of a particular figure and decline precipitously when that figure exits the stage. Look at what is happening to the Sanders movement now that his campaign has ended. Some of it will persist through pre-existing institutions — DSA, the unions that supported him, the progressive PACs — but a great deal of the energy will simply dissipate, because it was attached to him personally and to the campaign as a vehicle. Compare this to what Getachew rightly celebrates in Chicago: the slow accretion of power by the CTU, the patient running of candidates, the welding of strike action to community organization to electoral politics. That is not populism. That is something older and better. It is organizing.

The COVID-19 moment, which Getachew identifies as transformative, sharpens this point. What has the pandemic exposed? It has exposed, first, the brutal political economy of essential work: the fact that the people who keep our societies running — grocery clerks, delivery drivers, warehouse workers, nurses, home health aides, meatpackers, transit workers, custodians — are paid least, protected least, and respected least. It has exposed, second, the racial and gendered structure of that disposability: the disproportionate Black death rates in Chicago, Milwaukee, Detroit, and New Orleans; the over-representation of women, and especially women of color, in the most exposed care work. It has exposed, third, the catastrophic consequences of the long neoliberal hollowing-out of public capacity: the lack of testing, the inability to distribute protective gear, the impotence of public health departments that have been defunded for decades. And it has exposed, fourth, the global character of these crises: that no country can be safe while others are unsafe, that the production chains that link the world also transmit pathogens, that sanctions which deny medical supplies to Iran or Venezuela are not merely cruel but epidemiologically suicidal for everyone.

What political form is adequate to these exposures? I would submit that it is not populism. It is socialism, properly understood — a politics oriented toward the democratic ownership and control of social wealth, the decommodification of essential goods, and the building of international working-class solidarity. The populist frame, with its emphasis on people-versus-elite, tends to obscure the fact that the political economy of disposability is not a matter of bad elites who can be replaced by good ones; it is a matter of structural arrangements — wage labor, capital mobility, the international division of labor — that must be transformed at the root. A populism that treats the problem as one of corrupt elites rather than of capitalism as such ends up either tilting at windmills or, worse, finding scapegoats in immigrants, foreigners, and racialized minorities who can be coded as part of the elite's clientele.

This is not a paranoid worry. It is exactly what is happening on the right, and it is the structural risk the left runs whenever it adopts the populist form. Trump's response to the pandemic has been to call it the "Chinese virus," to halt funding to the WHO, to escalate sanctions on Iran, to expel asylum seekers under public health pretexts. Bolsonaro's response has been to deny the crisis entirely and to attack governors who try to manage it. Modi's response has been to use lockdown to discipline Muslim communities. In each case, the populist form has served as a vehicle for ethnonationalist response, and in each case the form is not incidental to the content. The people-versus-elite binary is intrinsically prone to ethnicization, because once you posit a singular authentic people, the question of who counts and who doesn't becomes definitive.

Some on the left will respond that this is precisely why we need a left populism: to occupy the populist form before the right monopolizes it. I have two answers. First, the empirical record of left populism is mixed at best. Where it has succeeded — in mid-century Latin America, for instance — it has often done so by accepting compromises with national bourgeoisies and by building patronage networks whose durability has been questionable. Where it has more recently been attempted — Syriza, Podemos, Sanders, Corbyn — it has either lost or, when it won, been forced into retreat by capital flight and institutional resistance. Second, and more importantly, the populist form is not the only mobilizational form available, and the alternatives are arguably stronger. Mass-membership parties with internal democracy, militant unions with strike capacity, community organizations with deep local roots, and tenant federations with the ability to coordinate rent strikes are all forms that mobilize without depending on the leader-affect-stadium dynamic that populism relies on.

What would a non-populist left politics look like in the United States in 2020? I think it would look something like this. It would build, first, from the strike. The wave of teacher strikes that began in West Virginia in 2018 and continued through Los Angeles, Oakland, Chicago, and elsewhere has been the most generative source of left energy in years, and it has been generative precisely because it has been institutional: rooted in unions, durable in its organizational form, capable of producing leaders who go on to other offices and other struggles. It would build, second, from the workplaces that COVID has revealed as essential: the Amazon warehouses, the Instacart shops, the meatpacking plants, the hospitals where nurses are being asked to reuse single-use protective equipment. These are sites of class struggle in the most classical sense, and they are also sites where the multiracial, multinational character of the contemporary working class is undeniable. It would build, third, from the tenant struggle that COVID is making unavoidable: with 26 million unemployment claims in five weeks and rent due on the first of every month, the conditions for a mass tenant movement are emerging whether the left organizes them or not. It would build, fourth, from a renewed internationalism: an end to sanctions, debt relief for the Global South, support for global vaccine and medicine access, opposition to the militarization of the pandemic response.

None of this is populist. All of it is socialist, in the sense that it is oriented toward the democratization of economic life and the decommodification of essentials. The fact that the language of socialism is now sayable in American politics for the first time in nearly a century — that "socialism" polls better than "capitalism" among Americans under thirty — is the actual opening that the left should be exploiting. To reach for "populism" instead is to reach for a weaker concept that the right is better positioned to wield.

I want to close by registering my agreement with Getachew on what I take to be her deepest point: that the failures of the last six months — Bolivia, Britain, the United States — must be analyzed together rather than separately, and that the comparative frame discloses problems that the national frame conceals. What it discloses, I would say, is that the international left in 2020 confronts a capitalist class that is more mobile, more financialized, and more politically organized than at any point in postwar history, and a state apparatus in most countries that has been gutted of the capacities needed to discipline that class. No rhetorical reanimation, populist or otherwise, will substitute for the slow work of rebuilding the institutional capacity — both inside and outside the state — to confront capital on something like equal terms. That work is unglamorous, but it is the work. Calling it populism does not make it any easier; it only makes it less honest about what it actually is.

The Color of "the People": Race and the Limits of Populist Reclamation

Adom Getachew's framing of this forum is honest about something many recent celebrations of populism have not been: that any project to "reclaim" populism in the United States must confront the persistence of white supremacy as a structuring force in American political life. This honesty is welcome. But I want to argue that the persistence of white supremacy is not merely an external problem that a reclaimed populism must overcome; it is woven into the historical fabric of American populism itself in ways that should give us serious pause about whether reclamation is the right metaphor at all. At the same time, I want to argue that the response to this difficulty is not to abandon mass politics in the name of an enlightened pluralism, but to take seriously a different genealogy — one that runs through Black radical, abolitionist, and anticolonial thought — that has offered its own theories of "the people" without falling into the racial traps of producerist populism. The question is not whether to build a mass democratic politics; the question is which traditions we are building from.

Begin with the historical record. The People's Party of the 1890s is the canonical American reference point for populism, and the canonical story about it has long been ambivalent. On one side: the Southern Farmers' Alliance, the early speeches of Tom Watson, the brief moment when Black and white tenant farmers could imagine a common political project against the merchants, railroads, and bankers who were extracting their labor and land. On the other side: the rapid collapse of that interracial moment under the pressure of the Democratic Party's white supremacist counterattack, the subsequent embrace by many former Populists of disenfranchisement and lynching as instruments of "reform," and the trajectory of Watson himself, who ended his life as a senator notorious for anti-Black, anti-Catholic, and antisemitic agitation. C. Vann Woodward's classic biography of Watson presented this as tragedy: a man and a movement betrayed by circumstance. More recent historians — Charles Postel sympathetically, Lawrence Goodwyn movingly, but also critics like Edward Ayers and Steven Hahn — have offered more textured accounts in which the racial limits of the movement were not external impositions but internal features. The Populist appeal to the "producing classes" against "parasites" was always racially structured, even when its formal language was inclusive, because the categories of producer and parasite mapped onto racially coded distinctions in the agrarian South. White yeomen could imagine themselves as producers because Black sharecroppers and tenants were always available as either invisible labor or threatening competition.

This pattern is not specific to the 1890s. Michael Kazin's work on the populist persuasion in American politics traces a recurring rhythm in which mass mobilizations against concentrated economic power tend, in the American context, to articulate themselves through racial idioms of producerism and to fracture along racial lines whenever they confront the choice between cross-racial coalition and white solidarity. The labor movement of the late nineteenth century, the Townsend movement of the 1930s, the followings of Huey Long and Father Coughlin, the Wallace campaigns of 1968 and 1972 — each in different ways illustrates how easily American populism slips its multiracial moorings. This is not because Americans are uniquely racist (they are not), but because the political economy of the United States has, since its founding, been structured by racial extraction in ways that make racially neutral mass politics extraordinarily difficult to sustain. Producerism in a settler colonial slave society is never just producerism.

Getachew is too sophisticated a thinker not to know this, and her essay nods at the problem. But the rhetorical project of "reclaiming populism" tends, in practice, to underweight this difficulty. Reclamation implies that there is a usable kernel of populist tradition that can be extracted from its racial entanglements and reactivated in a new form. I want to suggest that the metaphor of reclamation is misleading, and that a more honest framing would be construction — the construction of a new mass politics that draws selectively on multiple traditions, including some that have historically been excluded from the category of "populist" altogether.

Consider, for instance, the political thought of W. E. B. Du Bois. Black Reconstruction in America (1935) is in many ways the most ambitious theory of multiracial democracy ever produced on American soil, and it offers a vision of mass political agency — the "general strike" of enslaved people that helped bring down the Confederacy, the brief flowering of interracial democratic governance in the Reconstruction South — that does not rely on the populist binary of people-versus-elite. Instead, Du Bois offers a theory of political agency grounded in the practical activity of the formerly enslaved themselves, building schools, churches, militias, and political organizations under conditions of extreme constraint. The "people" in Du Bois is not an empty signifier waiting to be filled by a leader; it is a concrete network of institutions through which Black political agency was exercised and then violently destroyed. The lesson Du Bois draws from this is not that mass politics is dangerous, but that mass politics requires institutional infrastructure and confrontation with the racial state.

Or consider the tradition that runs from Ida B. Wells through Ella Baker to Fannie Lou Hamer — a tradition of Black women's organizing that has explicitly resisted the charismatic, leader-centered model that populism tends to favor. Ella Baker's famous insistence that "strong people don't need strong leaders" was not a piece of rhetorical modesty; it was a substantive theory of how durable mass politics is built. SNCC in its early years, before it was reorganized along more hierarchical lines, was an attempt to enact this theory: decisions made locally, leadership distributed, the work of organizing prioritized over the work of mobilization. This is a different theory of mass democratic politics than the one populism offers, and it has, I would argue, a much better track record of producing durable change. The civil rights movement at its height was the most successful mass political project in American history. It was not a populist movement. Calling it populist would obscure what made it work.

There is a third tradition worth invoking here, which is the tradition of Black internationalism — Du Bois again, but also George Padmore, C. L. R. James, Claudia Jones, Walter Rodney, and many others. This tradition has consistently insisted that the question of who counts as "the people" cannot be answered within national frames alone, because the structures that produce racialized disposability operate at a global scale. Getachew's own scholarly work, especially in Worldmaking After Empire, has reconstructed this tradition with extraordinary care and shown how the anticolonial generation grappled with the question of how to build democratic political subjects across imperial divides. It is striking, then, that the populism literature in the United States rarely engages with this body of thought, which has the most to teach us about the construction of multiracial political solidarity under conditions of extreme inequality. If anything is to be "reclaimed" for a left politics adequate to 2020, it should be this tradition — and "populism" is a misleading name for it.

The COVID-19 moment makes the racial stakes especially clear. Getachew notes the staggering disparities: Black residents of Chicago and Milwaukee dying at rates two to three times their share of the population; the over-representation of people of color in the essential workforce that is now being asked to risk infection without protection; the collapse of immigration detention facilities into petri dishes; the doubling-down on deportations and border closures even as a pandemic respects no border. These are not separate "racial issues" to be managed alongside a populist economic program. They are constitutive of the political economy that COVID has revealed. A politics that treats them as add-ons will not work — not analytically, because it will misdiagnose the problem, and not practically, because it will fail to mobilize the workers of color who make up a growing share of the actually existing American working class.

What does this mean for the project of building a multiracial democratic majority? It means, I think, that the left should be more comfortable using the language of the traditions that have actually built multiracial coalitions and less attached to the language of a tradition that has consistently struggled to do so. The language of solidarity — old-fashioned, perhaps, but specific in its implications — is more useful than the language of populism. The language of abolition, which has been generatively reinvented in the contemporary movement against mass incarceration, is more specific and more honest than the language of people-versus-elite. The language of the commons, which has been developed by both feminist and ecological theorists, is more capacious than the language of "the people." The language of social reproduction, which centers the work — disproportionately done by women of color — that sustains daily life, is more analytically powerful than the producerism that has historically organized populist appeals.

I want to be clear about what I am not arguing. I am not arguing that mass democratic politics is impossible or undesirable in the United States, nor that the project of building a multiracial working-class majority is misguided. On the contrary: I think that project is the only serious political horizon available to the American left. What I am arguing is that the populist tradition is not the best resource for that project, and that reclamation is not the right metaphor for the work required. The work required is constructive, not restorative. It involves drawing on multiple traditions — Black radical, feminist, ecological, internationalist, and yes, some elements of labor and agrarian populism too — to build something genuinely new. The temptation to give that new construction the old name of populism is understandable. The term has a certain insurgent ring; it signals a willingness to break with the technocratic liberalism that has failed; it gestures toward mass mobilization. But the term carries baggage that cannot easily be unpacked, and the baggage is precisely the baggage of racial exclusion that the new construction must overcome.

The Chicago Teachers' Union story that Getachew tells so movingly is, in fact, a story about this kind of construction. The CTU did not "reclaim populism." It built a new kind of unionism — explicitly anti-racist, deeply community-rooted, attentive to the gendered character of teaching as care work, willing to confront the political economy of urban disinvestment as itself a racial project. It allied with Black community organizations, with parent groups, with immigrant rights organizations, with housing activists. When Jeanette Taylor went on hunger strike to save Dyett High School, she was not performing populism; she was performing a specific theory of Black community defense that has its own genealogy in the South Side of Chicago, running back through the welfare rights organizing of the 1960s and 1970s and before that. The political form that emerged in Chicago is something new, and giving it the old name of populism does it a disservice. It deserves its own name. Until we find that name, we should be cautious about the names we attach to it. "Populism" is, I think, not the right one.

The View from Outside: Populism, Empire, and the Global Conjuncture

Adom Getachew is right to insist that the recent setbacks of the left — Morales in Bolivia, Corbyn in Britain, Sanders in the United States — must be read together, in a global frame, rather than as discrete national stories. Her own scholarship on anticolonial worldmaking has done as much as any recent work to remind English-language readers that the great political questions of the twentieth century were never simply national, and that the architects of the postcolonial world understood the construction of democratic peoples to be inseparable from the construction of a more just international order. I want to take up Getachew's invitation to think comparatively and push it further than her brief essay can. The argument I want to make is that the global lens does not just clarify the recent defeats of the left; it also clarifies the limits of the "reclaiming populism" framing itself, by revealing that populism as a category has done very different political work in different parts of the world, and that the contemporary American debate has not adequately reckoned with this variation.

Consider, first, the Latin American case, which is where contemporary scholarly debates about populism began and which provides the deepest theoretical archive for thinking about left-populist politics. The "pink tide" governments of the 2000s — Chávez and Maduro in Venezuela, Lula and Rousseff in Brazil, Kirchner and Fernández in Argentina, Morales in Bolivia, Correa in Ecuador, Vázquez and Mujica in Uruguay — are routinely lumped together as instances of left populism, but they were in fact quite different projects with quite different fates. Bolivia under Morales was perhaps the most genuinely transformative of these projects, because it was rooted in indigenous social movements that long predated MAS as an electoral vehicle and that maintained an autonomous political life alongside it. The 2019 ouster of Morales was, as Getachew rightly says, a coup — but the conditions for that coup were created in part by the erosion of MAS's social-movement base over the course of its time in office, as the party-state apparatus increasingly substituted itself for the movements that had originally made it. This is the recurring pathology of left populism in Latin America: a movement comes to power on the strength of autonomous popular organization, and then proceeds to absorb or marginalize that organization in the name of the leader and the state. By the time the right counter-mobilizes, the movement's defenses have been thinned by years of incorporation.

Venezuela is the most dramatic example. Chávez came to power in 1999 with the support of an extraordinarily mobilized urban poor, and he initiated genuine redistribution that pulled millions out of poverty. But the institutions he built — the Bolivarian Circles, the communal councils, the various participatory mechanisms — were always in tension with the personalist character of his rule, and that tension became fatal under Maduro, who inherited the personalism without the charisma or the oil revenues. Today Venezuela is in catastrophic crisis, and while a great deal of that crisis is the result of external sabotage (sanctions, opposition intransigence, U.S. interference), the internal vulnerabilities are also real and structural. The lesson is not that left projects in Latin America cannot succeed; it is that the populist form has specific liabilities — over-centralization, leader-dependence, the tendency to substitute state for movement — that have repeatedly undone left projects even when they began with genuine democratic energy.

The right-populist wave in Latin America that has followed the pink tide tells another part of the story. Bolsonaro's victory in Brazil, Macri's in Argentina (before Fernández's recent return), Duque's in Colombia, Piñera's continued presence in Chile: these are not simply the result of Latin American electorates rejecting the left. They are the result of right-wing political projects that have learned populist techniques — the moralized binary, the charismatic leader, the appeal to authentic national identity against foreign and elite contamination — and deployed them with considerable skill. The recent Latin American experience does not vindicate the project of reclaiming populism for the left. It complicates it.

The Indian case complicates it further. Narendra Modi's BJP has been since 2014 one of the world's most successful right-populist projects, and it has been so on the basis of a Hindu-nationalist redefinition of "the people" that explicitly excludes Muslims, who are recast as foreign elements requiring discipline. The Citizenship Amendment Act of December 2019, the National Register of Citizens, the abrogation of Article 370 in Kashmir, and the brutal handling of the Delhi pogroms in February 2020 are all instantiations of a populism in power. The Indian left's response — to the limited extent that an organized Indian left exists at the national scale — has not been to reclaim populism for itself but to defend a more capacious, secular, pluralist conception of the Indian nation that has its roots in the anticolonial movement and in the constitutional vision of Ambedkar and Nehru. This is significant: in India, the most serious opposition to right populism has come not from a counter-populism but from a defense of constitutional pluralism understood as a substantive political project. The shaheen bagh protests, which sustained a remarkable women-led occupation against the CAA for months, were not populist in form. They were pluralist, deliberative, and explicitly anti-majoritarian. They are now being dispersed by lockdown and police repression, but the political vocabulary they articulated is worth studying.

The European case adds another dimension. The Corbyn defeat in Britain has been read in many ways, and I do not want to litigate it in detail. But I want to note that Corbyn's project was always less populist in form than its critics suggested. It was, more accurately, a traditional social-democratic project — public ownership of utilities, redistribution, increased welfare spending — that happened to be led by a figure the British press could caricature as a populist outsider. The defeat in December 2019 was driven less by the failure of populism than by the unresolvable contradiction over Brexit, which fractured the coalition that Corbyn had assembled in 2017. The Labour seats that fell were almost entirely Leave-voting constituencies, and they fell because Labour could not satisfy them on Brexit without losing its Remain-voting urban and youth base. The lesson here is not about populism per se; it is about the specific difficulty of holding cross-cleavage coalitions together when an issue cuts across class lines, as Brexit did.

Compare this to the more genuinely populist projects elsewhere in Europe. Syriza in Greece came to power in 2015 on an explicitly left-populist program, was crushed by the Troika within months, and ended its term implementing the very austerity it had been elected to oppose. Podemos in Spain articulated an explicitly Laclauian left-populist theory and has spent the years since adjusting itself downward into a junior coalition partner in a center-left government. La France Insoumise has stalled at around twenty percent of the vote and has struggled to translate its mobilizational energy into durable institutional power. The Five Star Movement in Italy, which began as a left-adjacent populist project, has ended up in coalition first with the far right and then with the center-left, demonstrating exactly the kind of ideological promiscuity that the populist form invites. None of this is encouraging for the proposition that populism is the form left projects should adopt.

The American case must be understood in this comparative context. The Sanders campaign was not, I would argue, a populist project in the strictest sense, though it sometimes deployed populist rhetorical tropes. It was, more accurately, a social-democratic project that was trying to build a mass base for policies — Medicare for All, free public college, a Green New Deal — that have majority support in opinion polls but have struggled to find political vehicles. Its failure in the Democratic primary cannot be explained by any single factor: the consolidation of moderate support after South Carolina, the long-standing affection of older Black voters for Biden, the COVID-19 disruption of the campaign's final phase, the structural disadvantages Sanders faced with party elites and donors. But one comparative observation is worth making: in countries where social democracy has succeeded, it has done so on the basis of institutional infrastructure — unions, parties, mutual aid societies, cooperatives — that the United States simply does not possess in comparable form. The American labor movement organizes around eleven percent of the workforce; in Sweden it organizes around seventy percent. No campaign, populist or otherwise, can substitute for that institutional gap. Sanders's project, in retrospect, was attempting to build social democracy without the institutional preconditions that social democracy historically required.

This is where the international frame is most clarifying. The crisis of social democracy in the global North and the crisis of left populism in the global South are not separate phenomena. They are linked manifestations of the same underlying transformation: the rise, over the last forty years, of a global capitalist order in which capital is mobile, labor is fragmented, the state is hollowed out, and the institutional forms that the twentieth-century left built — industrial unions, mass parties, redistributive welfare states — have been systematically dismantled or weakened. No national project, no matter how well led, can fully address this transformation on its own. What is needed, as Getachew rightly insists, is a renewed internationalism — not just rhetorical solidarity but concrete coordination across borders to constrain capital, regulate finance, share medical and technological capacity, and confront climate change as the global emergency it is.

The COVID-19 pandemic has clarified this need with brutal force. The competition for ventilators and protective gear, the hoarding of vaccine research, the wielding of sanctions to deny medicine to Iran and Venezuela, the immigration restrictions imposed under public health pretexts, the absence of any meaningful global coordination — all of these are manifestations of the same nationalist enclosure of what should be transnational solidarities. The countries that have done best epidemiologically have done so by drawing on regional and global expertise (Taiwan, South Korea, Vietnam, New Zealand), and the countries that have done worst have done so by closing themselves off rhetorically while remaining structurally exposed to the global circuits they depend on (the United States above all). The Trump administration's response — pulling funding from the WHO, blaming China, doubling down on sanctions, expelling asylum seekers — is the populist response to a global crisis, and it is failing on every dimension simultaneously.

What would an alternative look like? An internationalist left politics adequate to 2020 would, I think, have several features. It would treat the WHO and other multilateral institutions not as inadequate to current needs (which they are) but as worth defending and strengthening rather than abandoning. It would push for immediate suspension of sanctions that impede pandemic response and for debt relief for countries in the global South that cannot otherwise mount fiscal responses to the crisis. It would advocate compulsory licensing of vaccines and treatments to ensure global access at affordable prices. It would treat migration not as a crisis to be managed but as a constitutive feature of the world we live in, requiring rights-based frameworks rather than enforcement-based ones. It would understand climate, pandemic, and migration as a single complex of global commons problems requiring coordinated rather than nationalist responses.

This is not populism. It is something older and, I think, more useful: it is internationalism. The tradition that runs from the First International through the anticolonial movements to the contemporary global justice movement has consistently understood that the construction of democratic peoples within nations and the construction of solidarity across nations are not separate projects but a single project. The American left, in its current preoccupation with reclaiming populism, has perhaps lost touch with this tradition. The global frame Getachew rightly insists on is most powerful when it reminds us that the resources for a left politics adequate to our moment are international, and that the parochialism of contemporary American political debate — even when that debate is conducted in the name of "the people" — is itself part of what needs to be overcome.

The Strike, Not the Stadium: Essential Workers and the Politics COVID Has Made

Adom Getachew's introduction touches on the Chicago Teachers' Union strike, almost in passing, before moving to the larger theoretical questions of populist reclamation and global comparison. I want to invert her emphasis. The CTU story, and the wider story of the 2018–2020 wave of teacher strikes, is not a sidebar to the question of how the left should reposition itself. It is the most important political development on the American left of the last decade, and it points to a politics — neither populist in any meaningful sense nor reducible to standard liberal pluralism — that the COVID-19 moment is poised to amplify. The argument I want to make is simple: the political form the left should be cultivating in 2020 is the strike, broadly understood, and the political subject it should be cultivating is the essential worker. Neither of these maps cleanly onto the populist framework Getachew's forum sets out to recover.

Begin with what the strikes of 2018 and 2019 actually accomplished. The wave began in West Virginia in February 2018, when teachers walked out across all fifty-five counties in a state with no collective bargaining rights and won pay raises and benefits through statewide political pressure. It spread to Oklahoma, Kentucky, Arizona, Colorado — red states with weak union laws, where teachers built mass actions through Facebook groups and informal networks as much as through union infrastructure. It then moved to blue cities: Los Angeles in January 2019, Oakland in February, Chicago and St. Paul in October. In each case, the strikes accomplished material gains — pay, class size limits, hiring of nurses and counselors — but they also accomplished something less tangible. They demonstrated, to participants and observers, that mass collective action could still work in the United States in the twenty-first century. They re-introduced the strike, as both a tactic and an idea, into American political imagination in a way that it has not been since the 1970s.

This is more important than it sounds. For four decades, American political culture has been organized around the assumption that the strike is dead — that the institutional and legal frameworks erected in the wake of the air traffic controllers' defeat in 1981 had succeeded in pricing collective action out of the realm of the possible. The teachers' strikes punctured that assumption. They showed that in particular sectors, under particular conditions, with particular tactical innovations, the strike could still produce concessions. And they did so in a sector — public education — that is itself one of the most politically significant in the country, both because of its size (over three million teachers nationally) and because of its connections to nearly every other constituency: parents, students, taxpayers, community organizations.

The COVID-19 moment has the potential to extend this development dramatically. As Getachew notes, over 48 million workers are doing essential work without basic protections; the unemployment rate has spiked toward 18 percent; the pandemic has revealed that the people who keep the country running are systematically underpaid, unprotected, and disrespected. What it has also revealed is that these workers have leverage. The grocery store cannot function without clerks. The Amazon warehouse cannot function without pickers. The hospital cannot function without nurses, aides, and janitors. The meatpacking plant cannot function without line workers. The delivery economy cannot function without drivers. And in a moment when the cost of an outbreak in any one of these workplaces is enormously high — not just to workers but to managers, owners, and the public — workers have a degree of strategic position that they have not had in decades.

We are already seeing this leverage being exercised. The Instacart shoppers struck on March 30 demanding hazard pay and protective equipment. Amazon warehouse workers in Staten Island walked out the same day. Whole Foods workers staged a "sick-out" on March 31. Workers at General Electric demanded the company convert manufacturing capacity to ventilator production. Sanitation workers struck in Pittsburgh. Nurses at multiple hospitals have engaged in protest actions against inadequate protective equipment. Meatpacking workers at Smithfield, Tyson, and JBS have demanded plant closures. These are not, on the whole, traditional unionized actions; they are emerging from the gaps in the existing labor regime, often through informal networks, social media coordination, and ad-hoc workplace committees. But they are strikes in the substantive sense, and they are establishing a pattern that the left should be working hard to extend.

What is the political form here? It is not populism in any usable sense. The Instacart strike was not articulated in terms of "the people" against "the elite." It was articulated in terms of specific demands — hazard pay, gloves, masks, paid sick leave for workers who become ill — against a specific employer, on the basis of a specific workplace relationship. The Amazon workers in Staten Island were not invoking the moral monopoly of authentic America against corrupt cosmopolitans; they were saying, accurately, that they did not have the protective equipment they needed to do their jobs safely, and that they would withhold their labor until they got it. This is the language of class struggle in its most classical sense, and it is also the language of practical politics that wins concessions.

There is, of course, a question about how to scale this. A strike at a single warehouse can win concessions for the workers in that warehouse; what about the millions of workers in similar conditions who lack any organizational infrastructure? Here is where the institutional question becomes central, and where I want to register a partial disagreement with the more optimistic readings of the current moment. Strikes are easier to call than to win, and easier to win than to consolidate. The teacher strike wave produced real gains, but it has not yet produced the durable institutional power that would translate those gains into long-term political transformation. The CTU is exceptional in this regard precisely because it has invested for over a decade in building exactly that durable institutional power — a militant union, deep community ties, an electoral apparatus that has now elected councillors and county commissioners. Most of the strikes of 2018 and 2019 did not have anything like the CTU's infrastructure behind them, and many of the gains they won have been chipped away in the years since.

The COVID-19 strikes face an even harder version of this problem. They are happening, for the most part, in sectors with weak or nonexistent union representation, in workplaces where workers can be fired easily and quickly, against employers (Amazon, Walmart) with enormous resources and well-developed anti-union expertise. The early Amazon strike was followed, predictably, by the firing of its organizer. The Whole Foods sick-out was followed by surveillance and intimidation. Without serious institutional backing, these actions can light up the news for a day or two and then be extinguished. The political task for the left, then, is not to celebrate the strikes as expressions of "people power" but to build the institutional infrastructure — unions, legal defense funds, mutual aid networks, political vehicles — that can sustain them. This is unglamorous, multi-decade work, and it is the work.

Here is where I want to connect back to Getachew's framing. The "reclaiming populism" project, as articulated in the introduction and the WFP literature it draws from, is meant to support exactly this kind of institutional construction. The Working Families Party is, in many ways, a model of the patient electoral and organizational work that the moment requires. The CTU's transformation from a sclerotic AFT local into one of the most politically powerful unions in the country is another model. The Sanders campaign, whatever its failures, has left behind a generation of organizers with experience, contact lists, and political imagination that did not exist a decade ago. All of this is good and important work, and Getachew is right to celebrate it.

But I want to insist that this work is not best understood as populism, and that calling it populism obscures more than it clarifies. It is, more accurately, a kind of new labor politics — a politics that takes the workplace, the strike, and the union as central political sites while extending the boundaries of each of these beyond their twentieth-century forms. The CTU is not just a union of teachers; it is a union that has redefined teaching as care work, education as a public good, schools as community institutions, and bargaining as a vehicle for community-wide demands ("bargaining for the common good"). The first problem with the populist frame is its constitutive ambiguity about who “the people” are. Getachew, following Michael Denning, notes that “every politician is a populist” in the sense that all democratic politics must appeal to some conception of the people. This is true as a matter of formal description, but it elides the qualitative distinction between a politics that treats the people as a diverse collection of citizens with competing interests and one that invokes a singular, unified people whose will is claimed to be self-evident and indivisible. The liberal democratic tradition, for all its flaws, has generally sought to accommodate pluralism through institutional mechanisms: separation of powers, federalism, judicial review, free press, and the protection of individual and minority rights. These mechanisms are designed precisely to prevent any single faction from claiming to embody the authentic voice of the people. Populism, by contrast, tends to regard such institutions as obstacles to the expression of popular sovereignty. The populist leader claims a direct, unmediated connection to the people, bypassing intermediaries like parties, courts, and the media. This is not a bug of populism; it is its defining feature. To think that a left populism can adopt that same structure without replicating its pathologies seems to me wishful thinking.

The historical record bears this out. The first great left-populist experiment in the Americas was the Mexican Revolution, whose Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) built its rule on a rhetoric of the “revolutionary people” while constructing a one-party state that crushed dissent, co-opted unions and peasant organizations, and systematically foreclosed genuine pluralism. In Argentina, Juan Perón’s populism did deliver real material gains for the working class, but it did so through a corporatist structure that concentrated power in the executive, persecuted opponents, and cultivated a cult of personality around the leader—a template that would later be used by right-wing populists as well. In Venezuela, Hugo Chávez’s Bolivarian Revolution, for all its genuine social achievements, gradually eroded the independence of the judiciary, the media, and the opposition, leaving a political system so hollowed out that his successor could preside over economic catastrophe and massive repression while still claiming the mantle of “the people.” These episodes are not simply deviations from an otherwise pure left-populist ideal; they are the working-out of a political logic that concentrates authority in the executive, delegitimizes dissent, and treats institutional constraints as anti-democratic.

Getachew might reply that the populism she and the Working Families Party are reclaiming is different: a pluralist, multiracial, democratic populism that eschews the strongman style. The very structure of a political appeal to a unified “people” against a “corrupt elite” tends to demand a focal point of identification—a leader who embodies the popular will. Contrast this with the approach of a more institutionally minded left, one that focuses on building durable organizations—unions, community groups, cooperative enterprises, independent media—that do not rise or fall with a single electoral cycle. That kind of left does not need to claim to speak for the whole people; it advances the interests of working people through the slow, cumulative work of organizing and legislating.

A second danger of the populist frame is its tendency to oversimplify conflicts into a moral binary of the virtuous people and the corrupt elite. Real democratic politics is far more complicated. The working class is not a monolithic bloc; it is riven by race, gender, region, religion, and sector. Employers and political elites are not a unified cabal; there are real conflicts between, say, finance capital and industrial capital, or between anti-immigrant nationalists and cosmopolitan globalizers. A politics that frames every issue as a struggle between the people and the elite is poorly equipped to navigate these complexities. This is a politics that takes seriously the Madisonian insight that factions are inevitable and that the only way to prevent tyranny is to multiply them, creating a system in which many different interests must negotiate and bargain. Such a politics does not speak of “the people” but of working families, organized labor, civil rights organizations, environmentalists, and faith communities, each with their own perspectives and priorities. It does not promise to overthrow the establishment but to reform it, step by step, through legislative action, regulatory enforcement, and judicial appointments. It is, admittedly, less exciting than the romantic vision of a people rising as one. We would do better to offer a different paradigm entirely: not the politics of the angry People, but the politics of patient, pluralistic, institutional reconstruction. That means defending and improving the democratic institutions we have—Congress, the courts, the administrative state—rather than deriding them as corrupt barriers to the popular will. That is not a weakness to be overcome by the right kind of leader or the right kind of rhetoric. Let me be blunt: the history of populism in the United States is a history of the white “people.” The People’s Party of the 1890s, often romanticized as the righteous predecessor of today’s left, was an overwhelmingly white movement rooted in agrarian grievances. While some Populist leaders in the South attempted to forge interracial alliances of poor farmers—a genuinely courageous effort—the movement as a whole was ultimately captured by the Democratic Party’s white supremacist faction, and many former Populists became the architects of Jim Crow. The language of “the people versus the interests” turned out to be perfectly compatible with the violent exclusion of Black people from the polity. This was not an accident. The “producer” identity that Populists celebrated was already racialized: the small farmer who worked the land was imagined as white, while Black agricultural workers—largely sharecroppers and tenant farmers—were cast as a dependent class, not fully part of the producing people. The Populist critique of “monopoly” and “speculation” occasionally took on anti-Semitic tones, targeting Jewish financiers as alien parasites. In other words, the rhetorical structure of populism—the appeal to a virtuous, hard-working people against a corrupt, rootless elite—has always been available for racial and ethnic scapegoating.

The progressive populism that Getachew and the Working Families Party champion is, of course, consciously anti-racist. The CTU, with its multiracial leadership and its demands that directly benefit Black and brown students, is a model of what is possible. But even here, I want to sound a note of caution. The “multiracial working class alignment” can function as a euphemism that avoids naming the specific experience of Black people in America. The working class is multiracial, yes, but it is not evenly racialized. Black workers face a distinct set of conditions: higher unemployment, lower wages, disproportionate incarceration, police violence, predatory housing policies, and a legacy of dispossession that no amount of class solidarity can simply erase. When left populists speak of “working people,” they often slide too quickly over these differences, imagining that a rising tide of social democracy will lift all boats. But as the historian Cedric Johnson has argued, the New Deal itself—often treated as the high-water mark of progressive populism—excluded the vast majority of Black workers from its most important programs, creating a welfare state that was, in Ira Katznelson’s phrase, “white by design.” Agricultural and domestic workers, overwhelmingly Black, were deliberately left out of Social Security and labor protections. The populist coalition that made the New Deal possible was built on a compromise with Southern segregationists, and it delivered material benefits to white workers that actually widened the racial wealth gap in many respects.

Today’s left populists are acutely aware of this history and would never countenance such explicit exclusions. But the structural problem persists: the racial hierarchy of American capitalism is not an overlay on an otherwise neutral class system; it is constitutive of that system. The contemporary “multiracial” working class is produced through a labor market that remains segmented by race, with Black workers concentrated in the lowest-paid, most precarious sectors—home health care, food service, warehousing—while white workers are disproportionately represented in higher-paid, unionized sectors like construction and manufacturing. The COVID-19 crisis has made this segmentation lethally visible: in Chicago and Milwaukee, as Getachew notes, Black residents account for the vast majority of deaths, not because of some genetic predisposition but because of pre-existing conditions caused by decades of environmental racism, housing segregation, and unequal access to health care, combined with overrepresentation in “essential” work that cannot be done from home. Any populism that does not foreground this specific racialized death-dealing as the central issue is engaging in a dangerous abstraction. The call for Medicare for All, for instance, is vital, but it will not, by itself, cure a Black community that has been systematically poisoned by lead pipes, confined to food deserts, and denied adequate hospital facilities. A populism that speaks of “universal” programs without addressing the particularity of anti-Black racism will reproduce racial inequality even as it reduces overall poverty.

The populist framework, with its focus on the “people” as the subject of politics, tends to obscure the fact that in a settler-colonial, slaveholding nation, the people were constituted precisely through the exclusion and domination of Black and Indigenous peoples. The very notion of a democratic people is haunted by this original sin. To reclaim populism without a radical re-founding that reckons with this history is to paper over a chasm that will eventually swallow any progressive coalition.

What would an abolitionist politics look like in the context of the pandemic? It would start not with the universal citizen but with the most vulnerable: the incarcerated, the undocumented, the unhoused, the immunocompromised. It would demand the immediate release of people from prisons, jails, and detention centers, where COVID-19 spreads like wildfire. It would demand a moratorium on evictions and foreclosures, and the cancelation of all rents, mortgages, and debts for those who cannot pay. It would demand reparations for Black communities devastated by the virus—not just a check but a full-scale redistribution of land, housing, and resources controlled by the community itself. And it would demand a transformation of the care economy, shifting control from for-profit nursing homes and hospitals to worker- and community-owned health cooperatives rooted in historically redlined neighborhoods. These are not demands that can be easily fit into a populist narrative about “the people.” They are particular, targeted, and unapologetically racial. They name the enemy not as “the elite” in the abstract but as the carceral state, the medical-industrial complex, and the ongoing system of racial extraction.

Getachew might respond that the Working Families Party’s multiracial alignment is precisely about building the coalitions necessary to make such demands politically viable. And she would be right that no single racial group can achieve these transformations alone. But the history of multiracial coalitions in America suggests that they are most durable and effective not when they are built around an abstract class identity but when each component group is allowed to articulate its specific grievances and when the coalition is bound by a shared commitment to dismantling racial hierarchy. This requires a political language that is far more attuned to difference than populism typically allows. It requires a practice of coalition that is not about merging into a single “people” but about working across irreducible differences, with the understanding that justice for Black people will sometimes require measures—reparations, for example—that are not universal, and that white members of the coalition must support those measures not because they are part of “the working class” but because they are committed to racial justice as a moral and political principle.

The international dimension is equally important. Populism is nation-bound; it imagines a people within a territory. But Blackness is a diasporic condition. The premature deaths of Black people in Chicago are linked to the deaths of Black people in Brazil, the highest toll of the pandemic in any democracy. Both are legacies of the transatlantic slave trade, plantation capitalism, and ongoing imperialism. An internationalist, abolitionist politics would link the struggles against police violence in Minneapolis to the struggles against extractivism in Latin America and the fight for reparations in the Caribbean. It would see the Trump administration’s sanctions on more than twenty-five countries as a form of collective punishment that amounts to slow-motion genocide, most acutely affecting Black and brown populations abroad. And it would demand not just an end to those sanctions but a positive program of reparative internationalism: the transfer of wealth, technology, and medical capacity from the Global North to the Global South, overseen by democratic institutions accountable to the people whose lives are at stake.

This is, I admit, a more radical vision than the one Getachew lays out. But I do not think the left has the luxury of moderation. The pandemic has exposed the rot at the core of American democracy, a rot that is fundamentally racial. The populist frame, in its search for a unifying “we,” risks pushing that rot to the margins again, hoping that a universal program will take care of the problem without ever having to name it directly. But we cannot heal what we will not name. The name is white supremacy. Until the left builds a politics that starts from that name and fights it as the central axis of oppression—not a secondary contradiction alongside class—we will continue to lose, no matter how often we reclaim the word “populism.”

Essential workers are disproportionately women, immigrants, and people of color because care and service labor have been historically devalued. Protective equipment is manufactured through international supply chains. The national economy is not a container; it is a node in a web of labor. Of course, politics still happens through states. Hospitals need funding from governments; relief checks are issued by treasuries; labor law is national; elections determine executive power. Cities can form networks around public health, climate, and migrant rights. Parties can coordinate platforms on debt, taxation, climate, and trade. Public banks and development institutions can be redesigned. International law can be used not only to protect investors but to protect labor, health, and climate. None of this is easy. If recovery is linked to care, decarbonization, public health, and global solidarity, it can become the first step toward a different order.

What then of populism? I am ambivalent. The word carries democratic energy because it insists that ordinary people, not oligarchs, should rule. It can puncture the false neutrality of technocracy and expose the class power hidden in “responsible” policy. It challenges the idea that jobs must be protected by militarized borders or hostility to foreign workers. It insists that public health includes prisons, detention centers, reservations, colonies, and sanctioned countries. It treats anti-racism and anti-imperialism as central to economic democracy. It asks American workers to see themselves not as victims of the world, but as potential participants in a global majority against oligarchy and abandonment.

It has produced nationalist leaders who can close borders but not organize testing. If this is realism, we need better dreams.

If it names a global majority against the forces that sacrifice life to profit and power, it may yet become part of a democratic internationalism adequate to the pandemic age.
