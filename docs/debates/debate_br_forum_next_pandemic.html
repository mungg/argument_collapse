Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Sheila Jasanoff's call for "technologies of humility" is a welcome corrective to the technocratic hubris that has marked so much pandemic planning. Her central diagnosis—that the United States and United Kingdom topped the Global Health Security Index and then logged some of the world's worst death tolls—is damning evidence that preparedness frameworks were measuring the wrong things. But I want to press on the argument from two directions. First, humility as Jasanoff describes it risks becoming a virtue too capacious to operationalize. Second, the COVID-19 record suggests that the deeper failure was not epistemic but institutional: a hollowing-out of public capacity that no amount of reflective inquiry could substitute for.

Begin with the GHSI puzzle. Jasanoff is right that the index failed to anticipate the dismal U.S. and U.K. performance, and right that it ignored variables like polarization, federalism, racial inequality, and trust. But notice what this concedes: the relevant unknowns were not really unknown. Political scientists have written for decades about American polarization; sociologists have documented Black communities' justified suspicion of medical institutions since at least the public reckoning with Tuskegee; ethnographers have tracked vaccine hesitancy since the Wakefield episode. The data existed. What was missing was not humility but the willingness of public health planners to treat social science as constitutive of their object rather than as a soft afterthought. That is a different problem from the one Jasanoff names. It is less about cultivating a mindset that respects unknown unknowns and more about which expert communities get a seat at the planning table. Calling for humility risks letting the architects of the playbook off the hook for what they could have known had they bothered to ask.

This connects to my second concern. Compare the United States to Germany, which Jasanoff notes outperformed its GHSI ranking. Germany did not succeed because its planners were epistemically humbler. It succeeded because it possessed a denser public health infrastructure at the Land level, more universal health coverage, higher baseline institutional trust, and a less paralyzing partisan divide over basic public health measures. Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, and New Zealand also outperformed wealthier nations, and the common thread is less philosophical disposition than institutional thickness: functioning local public health agencies, intact welfare systems that could support people through quarantine, leaders who treated the virus as a non-partisan threat. Jay Kaufman's remark, which Jasanoff quotes, gets closer to the truth than the humility framing does. The United States did not lack reflective practitioners; Anthony Fauci is many things, but unreflective is not among them. What the United States lacked was a state capable of executing a coherent response across fifty jurisdictions whose governors had political incentives to defect.

Jasanoff's most powerful move is her reversal of time's arrow: humility "pulls possible futures back to show us how to act in the present." Yet this is also where the argument is most slippery. To pull possible futures back, one must imagine them, which is itself a predictive exercise—just with attention to distributive consequences and marginal viewpoints. In that sense, the technologies of humility are not really an alternative to prediction; they are prediction done with a broader cast of characters and a longer list of harms. That is valuable, but it does not escape the basic asymmetry Jasanoff identifies between acting and holding back. It only relocates it: someone still has to decide which possible futures to take seriously and which to set aside as paranoia. The ethnographers who flagged vaccine hesitancy were correct; the activists who claimed in March 2020 that lockdowns would devastate the working poor were also correct; and any number of other Cassandras were wrong about catastrophes that never came. Humility as a procedural commitment does not, by itself, tell us how to weight these voices.

Where I think Jasanoff is most clearly right is in her critique of the militaristic framing that pervades Fineberg's editorial and much of U.S. preparedness culture. "Unified command," "crush the curve," "mobilize the public"—the language assumes a polity that consents in advance to be directed. That polity did not exist on February 1, 2020, and pretending it did meant the resulting plans were rendered useless the moment they encountered partisan resistance. Here humility really does have purchase: a planner alert to the fragility of public consent would have invested heavily in building it during peacetime, would have piloted communications strategies for communities with low institutional trust, would have war-gamed scenarios in which the federal government and key states refused to cooperate. That is not soft-edged ethnographic listening; it is hard-headed political realism, and it is precisely what was absent.

So I would refine Jasanoff's prescription. The pandemic did not reveal a deficit of humility in some abstract epistemic sense. It revealed that our preparedness apparatus was built by people who treated politics, distribution, and consent as exogenous variables when they were in fact the dependent ones. The remedy is to bring social and political analysis inside the planning process as co-equal with epidemiology—and, more demandingly, to rebuild the public institutions without which even the most humble plan has nothing to act through.

Jasanoff’s diagnosis of pandemic failure as a crisis of hubris is compelling, yet it risks understating how much the catastrophe stemmed not from an excess of certainty but from a deliberate rejection of the very institutions and knowledge that humility is meant to strengthen. The COVID-19 disaster in the United States was not simply the product of overconfident models or a militaristic preparedness mindset; it was the result of a political movement that actively dismantled public trust, scorned expertise, and elevated individual liberty as an absolute good over collective well-being. In such a context, advocating for technologies of humility without confronting the forces that feed willful ignorance may be an insufficient remedy.

Jasanoff rightly notes that the Global Health Security Index, the Obama-era playbook, and Harvey Fineberg’s call to arms all presumed a degree of social cohesion and respect for centralized guidance that did not exist. But this was not a blind spot unique to technocrats—it was a refusal by many to accept that public health requires subordinating personal preference to a common good. The governors of Florida and Texas blocking local mask mandates, the persistent undercurrent of vaccine disinformation amplified by media and political figures, and the sheer venom directed at public health officials like Anthony Fauci were not symptoms of insufficient citizen participation or failure to ask “who might get hurt.” They were the fruits of a decades-long campaign to delegitimize government and science, often funded by the very industries whose products had been subject to precautionary regulation. Humility as an epistemic practice will not easily disarm a movement that thrives on epistemic chaos.

Moreover, Jasanoff’s preferred technologies of humility—greater citizen engagement, incorporating memory and lived experience, asking whose perceptions were ignored—risk being paralyzed by the same polarization they are meant to overcome. The demand to see from the margins is essential, but in a deeply fractured society, those margins often speak with contradictory voices. Which publics should be brought to the table to deliberate on vaccine mandates or school closures? For every community that has suffered historical medical abuse and now distrusts vaccines, there is another that demands full in-person schooling for their children and views remote learning as a surrender. A deliberative process that genuinely weighs these disparate harms is not merely a matter of humility; it requires a framework for adjudicating competing claims and, ultimately, the political will to make choices that will inevitably harm some groups. Jasanoff’s call for a middle way between passivity and aggressive action is appealing, but it runs the danger of becoming yet another procedural ideal that assumes good-faith engagement from all sides.

This is not to dismiss the value of precaution. The very failures Jasanoff catalogues—the uneven mortality along lines of race and class, the collapse of childcare, the surprise at vaccine refusal—might have been mitigated had pandemic planners embedded social vulnerability into their risk assessments. The Obama playbook, for all its limitations, did flag public concern and humanitarian impacts as relevant variables, but it was discarded by an administration that saw such frameworks as bureaucratic obstacles to its own political narrative. Preparedness documents can be improved, but without a commitment to use them, they become museum pieces. The problem, then, is not so much a binary between prediction and humility as it is a political economy that undervalues public goods and treats even an infectious disease as a battlefield for culture war. True humility would require acknowledging that, in a democracy, public health sovereignty cannot function without broad legitimacy—and that legitimacy is not engineered by a checklist but built through sustained investment in institutions, education, and social safety nets that make collective sacrifice possible.

Looking back from December 2021, as the United States approaches 800,000 deaths and the Omicron variant reminds us how little we control, Jasanoff’s plea to resist the temptation of heroic mastery feels urgent. Yet the humility she advocates cannot be confined to expert deliberations or citizen juries. It must also be a political project that defends the very possibility of shared truth and common action. Without that, even the most exquisitely designed technologies of humility will be shouting into the wind, while the prepared simply dig in their heels and insist they owe nothing to anyone.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Sheila Jasanoff’s critique of prepandemic public policy exposes a profound and fatal flaw in modern global governance: the assumption that a biological crisis can be managed purely through logistical and technocratic control, devoid of political and social context. She is entirely correct that instruments like the Global Health Security Index (GHSI) and the Obama administration’s playbook operated under the illusion of a compliant, unified polity. They measured laboratory capacity and stockpile volumes, but entirely missed the crucial metrics of social trust, political polarization, and historical marginalization. However, in diagnosing this failure, Jasanoff draws a stark dichotomy between the “heroic” posture of preparedness and the “unheroic but more ethical” stance of humility and precaution. While her call for “technologies of humility” is vital, positioning humility as an alternative to preparedness risks leaving us philosophically enriched but operationally paralyzed. The solution is not to abandon preparedness, but to drastically expand its definition.

Jasanoff argues that preparedness is a “can-do posture, bravely denying the possibility of defeat,” contrasting it with humility, which “admits that defeat is possible.” But this framing mischaracterizes the true failure of our pandemic response. The planners behind the GHSI did not fail because they were preparing; they failed because they were preparing for the wrong disaster. They prepared for a pathogen invading a sterile laboratory, rather than a virus infiltrating a deeply fractured, unequal society. The failure of Harvey Fineberg’s call to “crush the curve” by June 2020 was not a failure of the concept of a unified command, but a failure to recognize that the United States lacked the social cohesion required to execute it. In this sense, the problem was not hubris in the face of nature, but profound ignorance of our own civic fragility.

Furthermore, we must be careful not to discard the very real triumphs of prediction and preparedness in our rush to embrace precaution. The rapid development of mRNA vaccines—undeniably the most significant victory of the last nineteen months—was a masterclass in predictive science and robust, risk-taking preparedness, exemplified by the financial architecture of Operation Warp Speed. If policymakers had applied a rigid precautionary principle—which demands we hold back in the absence of full scientific certainty—the deployment of novel vaccine platforms might have been delayed by years. In this instance, a “heroic” posture saved countless lives. The tragedy of late 2021 is not that we developed these vaccines too aggressively, but that our scientific preparedness vastly outpaced our sociological preparedness. We engineered a medical miracle but failed to anticipate the deep-seated vaccine hesitancy, fueled by historic institutional distrust and partisan division, that currently prevents us from defeating the Delta variant and bracing for the newly identified Omicron threat.

This is precisely where Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” must be integrated, not as an alternative to preparedness, but as its foundational layer. Humility asks us to look at the margins, to remember past injustices, and to anticipate human behavior. If we had applied this lens in 2019, an honest assessment of American preparedness would have recognized that the nation’s healthcare disparities—which guarantee that communities of color and low-wage workers will suffer disproportionately—are a catastrophic vulnerability. A truly comprehensive GHSI would not just measure hospital beds; it would measure the public’s willingness to wear masks. It would assess the vulnerability of supply chains when citizens inevitably panic-buy, and it would calculate the profound economic and educational impacts on single mothers when schools are shuttered.

To bridge the gap between Jasanoff’s critique and the realities of public administration, we need a paradigm of "socio-political preparedness." This approach acknowledges Jasanoff’s insight that “securing public health and public safety requires human beings to give up aspects of their liberty,” but it actively plans for the resistance that such sacrifices will inevitably provoke. It does not simply wring its hands over the unpredictability of human nature; it maps the contours of our social fractures just as epidemiologists map the genome of a novel virus.

As we look toward the inevitable next crisis, we cannot rely on precaution alone to save us. A society that only practices precaution may find itself too hesitant to act when rapid mobilization is required. Instead, we must forge a more mature, clear-eyed version of preparedness. We must remain ready for "any old thing," as Baden-Powell suggested, but we must finally accept that the most dangerous and unpredictable variable in any crisis is not the pathogen itself, but the deeply flawed, deeply divided human beings it infects.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Sheila Jasanoff is right that COVID-19 exposed the poverty of a purely technocratic imagination. The countries that looked best on paper did not necessarily protect their people best in practice, and the gap cannot be explained by virology alone. A pandemic is not simply a pathogen moving through bodies; it is a pathogen moving through workplaces, prisons, nursing homes, schools, households, media ecosystems, racial hierarchies, and political institutions. Any preparedness plan that treats those settings as background conditions rather than central mechanisms of transmission is bound to fail.

But I worry that contrasting preparedness with humility risks giving preparedness too little credit and humility too much work to do. The problem was not that governments prepared; it was that they prepared for the wrong things, in the wrong institutions, with too little attention to who would actually have to comply, sacrifice, trust, and bear the consequences. A more humble public policy after pandemic should not retreat from preparedness. It should remake preparedness so that uncertainty, inequality, and democratic legitimacy are built into it from the beginning.

The evidence is already visible in the pandemic record. South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore were not successful early on because they disdained preparedness. They were successful because prior experience with SARS and MERS had left them with practical systems: testing capacity, contact tracing, border protocols, public communication routines, and legal authority that could be mobilized quickly. New Zealand’s elimination strategy likewise required decisive planning, border controls, income supports, and public trust. These cases do not prove that command-and-control models are sufficient; indeed, each raised serious questions about privacy, exclusion, and the burdens of isolation. But they do show that humility without capacity is not enough. One cannot deliberate one’s way into masks, tests, ventilation, paid leave, or vaccines after the emergency has already arrived.

The United States failed partly because it lacked humility, but also because it lacked a functioning welfare state. Asking infected people to isolate is a public health measure only if they can miss work without losing wages or housing. Closing schools is an epidemiological intervention only if children have food, internet access, safe homes, and adults available to care for them. Advising vaccination is easier when people have primary care providers, transportation, time off, and confidence that medical institutions see them as more than data points. The moral question “who might get hurt?” must therefore be attached to budgets, laws, and administrative routines. Otherwise humility becomes an admirable sensibility floating above the machinery that determines life chances.

This is especially important because many of the pandemic’s “surprises” were not unknown unknowns. The vulnerability of nursing homes was not mysterious. The dependence of hospitals on just-in-time supply chains was not unknowable. The higher risk borne by Black, Latino, Indigenous, poor, incarcerated, and front-line workers followed from durable facts about American society. The fragility of public trust was visible in declining confidence in institutions, partisan media fragmentation, and years of anti-vaccine activism. To call these failures failures of prediction may be too generous. In many cases, the knowledge existed but had little political force because the people most endangered possessed too little power.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” are valuable precisely where they insist on widening the circle of relevant knowledge. Public health agencies should not consult communities only after policies meet resistance. They should involve workers, disability advocates, parents, teachers, nurses, religious leaders, immigrant organizations, and residents of highly polluted neighborhoods before crisis plans are finalized. Risk communication should not be an exercise in translating expert conclusions for lay consumption; it should be a two-way process that discovers what people fear, what they need, what they have reason to distrust, and what tradeoffs they regard as legitimate.

Still, participation cannot substitute for responsibility. Governments sometimes invoke uncertainty to delay action, diffuse blame, or pretend that every value conflict requires endless consultation. In March 2020, humility about limited evidence on masks should not have meant leaving the public confused while supplies were reserved through evasive messaging. Humility about school closures should not mean ignoring children’s losses, but neither should it mean pretending schools can operate safely without ventilation, testing, masks, vaccination, and support for families. Democratic humility must be compatible with decisions under pressure. It should make decisions more accountable, not less decisive.

The pandemic also suggests that we need a broader definition of infrastructure. Preparedness is usually imagined as stockpiles, surveillance systems, emergency operations centers, and expert dashboards. Those matter. But so do paid sick leave, universal health coverage, unemployment insurance, eviction prevention, broadband, public transit, occupational safety enforcement, and trusted local health departments. These are not merely social policies with incidental health benefits; they are pandemic controls. A society that forces millions to choose between infection and income has built viral spread into its political economy.

The task after COVID-19, then, is not to choose between heroic preparedness and chastened humility. It is to institutionalize humble preparedness: plans that assume they will be wrong in some respects; metrics that include inequality, trust, and administrative capacity; emergency powers bounded by transparency and review; supply chains designed for resilience rather than maximum efficiency; and public health agencies funded in ordinary times, not merely praised in emergencies. The next crisis will also exceed our models. That is not an argument against planning. It is an argument for plans designed by people who know that models fail, societies fracture, and harms fall unevenly unless policy is built to prevent them from doing so.

Jasanoff's diagnosis of pandemic preparedness is largely persuasive, but her proposed remedy—technologies of humility—risks becoming another technocratic framework that fails the very test she sets for others. Her argument that preparedness failed because it ignored politics, social dynamics, and the limits of knowledge is compelling when applied to the GHSI and Fineberg's wartime mobilization rhetoric. Yet the concept of humility she advances, while rhetorically attractive, remains frustratingly underspecified as a guide to action. If we have learned anything from the pandemic, it is that appeals to moral sensibility, however well-intentioned, require institutional scaffolding to translate into effective governance.

The lead essay rightly indicts the hubris embedded in preparedness frameworks that treated pandemics as logistics problems rather than political phenomena. The GHSI's assumption that all nations would prioritize collective action over domestic politics proved disastrously naive, and Fineberg's vision of unified command bore no resemblance to the fragmented federalism that actually characterized the U.S. response. Jasanoff is correct that these frameworks failed to ask the questions that mattered most: whose perceptions were systematically ignored, and what protections existed for those most vulnerable to policy failure?

Yet the technologies of humility, as presented, offer more aspiration than operational guidance. Jasanoff identifies several institutional mechanisms she believes would help: greater citizen participation, incorporating memory and experience, attending to concerns for justice. These are admirable goals, but the essay provides little insight into how such mechanisms would function under the pressures of an actual emergency. During a pandemic, decision-makers face collapsing information environments, competing claims on scarce resources, and political incentives that reward action over deliberation. Asking officials to adopt "the perspectives of those who are acted upon" is sensible in the abstract, but it does not resolve the fundamental challenge of who decides which perspectives matter when time and capacity are constrained.

This gap matters because the alternative to technocratic overconfidence is not simply more humility but better institutions for democratic deliberation under crisis conditions. The pandemic revealed that meaningful public engagement with science was possible at scales that seemed unimaginable in 2019—the proliferation of community-led testing sites, mutual aid networks, and grassroots contact tracing efforts demonstrated that citizens could participate meaningfully in pandemic response when given the tools and information to do so. Yet Jasanoff's framework offers no analysis of how such participation could be institutionalized in advance of the next crisis, rather than improvised in desperation.

The essay's most penetrating insight concerns the politics of public health sovereignty. Jasanoff observes that securing public health requires people to sacrifice aspects of liberty for the common good, and that this political project cannot be reduced to technical expertise. Her critique of preparedness frameworks for assuming away this reality is sharp. Yet having established that public health is irreducibly political, her argument seems to retreat into a technocratic mode of its own, offering "humility" as a substitute for the harder work of building political coalitions capable of sustaining difficult interventions across polarized societies. The Japanese government's mask distribution, which Jasanoff mentions approvingly, succeeded not because of humility but because Japanese political culture supported cohesive collective action—a precondition that cannot be conjured into existence by invoking Lear on the heath.

This is not to deny the value of Jasanoff's critique. The failure to anticipate vaccine hesitancy, the disregard for racial disparities in infection and mortality, the assumption that the public would defer to unified expert guidance—all represent genuine epistemic failures that better integration of diverse perspectives might have mitigated. But humility is a disposition, not a policy. What the next pandemic will require is not merely a change in mindset among policymakers but structural reforms that embed democratic participation into public health systems before crises arrive. The technologies of humility, to become more than a slogan, must specify who participates, how their input shapes decisions, and what accountability mechanisms ensure that marginalized perspectives actually influence outcomes. Until humility acquires such institutional teeth, it risks remaining a comfortable abstraction that absolves intellectuals of the harder work of political reconstruction.

Humility Without Power Is Just Another Sermon

Sheila Jasanoff has given us an elegant diagnosis of what went wrong with the world's pandemic preparedness, and a graceful prescription. Trade prediction's hubris for humility. Replace blueprints with technologies that listen, that map vulnerabilities, that pull "possible futures back to show us how to act in the present." Who could disagree? And there, precisely, is the trouble. As an ethic, humility is unobjectionable. As a politics, it is dangerously underspecified. The reason the United States lost more than 740,000 lives by October 2021 is not principally that its planners failed to think modestly about uncertainty. It is that the country is a class society organized to extract wealth from poor and racialized bodies, and that no spreadsheet of "indicators" — however humble — would have changed that without changing the underlying distribution of power.

Consider what Jasanoff lists, almost in passing, as the populations COVID-19 disproportionately killed: the poor, the elderly, Black and Hispanic Americans, residents of environmentally compromised neighborhoods, single mothers shouldering collapsed schools. She is right that none of this was unforeseeable. But the structural reasons it was foreseeable cannot be patched by better epistemic posture. They are baked into how Americans work, where they live, what air they breathe, whether they can afford to stay home, and whether the institutions that ration health care regard them as worth saving. Meatpacking workers at Smithfield and Tyson did not die at vastly elevated rates because the Global Health Security Index neglected "humility." They died because they were forced back to the line under federal protection while their employers received liability shields. A technology of humility might have mapped that vulnerability in advance; a politics of solidarity would have prevented it.

The same applies to vaccine hesitancy, which Jasanoff cites as a paradigm case of what humility could have illuminated. Ethnographers have indeed been writing for years about the Tuskegee echo, about the way medical institutions earned distrust the hard way among Black Americans, and about the libertarian and evangelical currents that turn immunization into a cultural battlefield. But the ethnography was there. The "map of power and powerlessness" was drawn. What was missing was not knowledge; it was the political will to act on it — to fund community health workers, pay people to get vaccinated, send trusted local messengers into neighborhoods, and decouple vaccine access from employer-based health systems. Calling for more humility risks suggesting that the failure was conceptual when in fact it was distributional.

I do not think Jasanoff would disagree with any of this in private. Her essay gestures repeatedly at justice, at marginalization, at "the perspectives of those who are acted upon." But the framework she offers — "technologies of humility" — keeps drifting back to epistemic virtues. It asks: how should planners think? How should expertise reckon with its blind spots? How should the framers of plans incorporate memory and experience? These are good questions for a graduate seminar at the Kennedy School. They are not the questions that decide whether a nursing aide in Queens lives or dies. Those are answered by labor law, housing policy, the structure of Medicaid, the bargaining power of unions, the political economy of the long-term care industry, and the willingness of the federal government to use coercive power against capital.

There is also a problem of agency. Whose humility, exactly, is Jasanoff calling for? The CDC's? The White House's? Mitch McConnell's? The Florida governor's, who actively sabotaged school mask mandates? The very architecture of American federalism that she correctly identifies as crippling the response is not going to be reformed by the cultivation of a more humble disposition among public health planners. A planner can be exquisitely sensitive to the limits of her own knowledge and still be powerless when a state legislature votes to strip her department of authority. Humility, in such cases, is not a policy lever; it is a mood.

Jay Kaufman, whom Jasanoff quotes, gets closer to the bone: "The U.S. Achilles's heel is not lack of humility; it is a toxic social ideology rooted in a blind obsession with individual freedom." I would add: and an even more toxic political economy that treats public goods as inefficiencies, social insurance as welfare, and labor as expendable. The pandemic did not catch the United States short on epistemology. It caught it short on solidarity, redistribution, and state capacity — the three things forty years of bipartisan neoliberalism have systematically corroded. By 2020, the country had a public health workforce a third smaller than in 2008. It had a hospital system in which a fifth of beds were owned by private equity. It had a labor force in which 30 million people had no paid sick leave. No "humble" planning document was going to compensate for those facts.

What would a more materialist alternative to Jasanoff's program look like? It would start by naming the actual obstacles. It would observe that "preparedness" failed not because experts were arrogant but because the political coalitions necessary to enact preparedness were systematically disorganized — by Citizens United, by the gutting of public unions, by the capture of regulatory agencies, by the destruction of local journalism that might have held leaders accountable. It would observe that the countries which fared best — Vietnam, South Korea, Taiwan, New Zealand, Cuba — did not necessarily have more "humble" planners; they had more capable states with greater legitimacy and, in some cases, stronger collectivist traditions. State capacity, it turns out, matters at least as much as epistemic virtue.

This is not a rejection of Jasanoff's project. The technologies of humility she sketches — citizen participation, attention to historical memory, structured engagement with marginalized perspectives — are valuable and overdue. But they need to be coupled to a frank account of who benefits from the current configuration of expertise and who pays. Without that coupling, "humility" risks becoming the latest in a long line of soft, ameliorative concepts — "resilience," "stakeholder engagement," "co-production" — that promise to humanize technocracy without disturbing its foundations. Each of those concepts, in turn, was absorbed into the institutional cultures it was meant to challenge and emerged thirty years later as boilerplate language in the executive summaries of consulting firms.

I worry that "technologies of humility" will travel the same route. Already one can imagine the slide deck: "Pillar 3: Humility. Stakeholder mapping. Vulnerability listening sessions. To prevent that, humility needs to be operationalized not only as a stance toward knowledge but as a transfer of decision-making power — over budgets, over priorities, over who lives near the freeway and who works without sick pay. The next pandemic will come. The microbes are patient. When it arrives, the question will not be whether American planners had read enough Foucault or convened enough citizen panels. It will be whether the country built, in the meantime, the kind of society in which collective sacrifice is reciprocated by collective protection. That is not a problem humility can solve. In Defense of Preparedness

Sheila Jasanoff's case against preparedness is gracefully made, but I think it gets the diagnosis wrong, and the remedy still more so. The United States did not lose three-quarters of a million people because its planners were too confident, too quantitative, or too Promethean. It lost them because, when the moment came, it discarded the playbook, ignored the experts, defunded the institutions, and elected a president who suggested injecting bleach. The problem was not that we prepared too aggressively. It was that we prepared on paper and then refused to execute. Substituting "humility" for preparedness as our governing ideal will not fix what went wrong; it will guarantee that the next time goes worse.

Let us begin with the comparison Jasanoff draws but does not quite confront. She notes that the United States and the United Kingdom topped the Global Health Security Index and then performed terribly, while less-ranked countries did better. She presents this as evidence that the GHSI's framework was misconceived — too checklist-driven, too blind to politics, too confident in the orderly cooperation of citizens and governments. But this is not the only inference available. An equally plausible reading is that the GHSI accurately measured capacity, and that the United States threw its capacity away. Capacity is necessary but not sufficient. A nation can have the world's best ventilator stockpile and still squander it; that does not prove that stockpiles are useless. South Korea, ranked ninth in the GHSI, executed brilliantly in early 2020 by using exactly the kind of tools the Index measures: rapid testing infrastructure, contact tracing capacity, transparent risk communication. Taiwan, Singapore, and Germany also drew on serious preparedness investments. The Index's predictive failure for the U.S. tells us something about American political dysfunction, not about the inadequacy of preparedness as a paradigm.

Jasanoff's critique of "the Fineberg plan" makes the same move. She points out that June 6, 2020, came and went without crushing the curve, and concludes that Fineberg's wartime mobilization rhetoric "presupposed [a] United States that never was." But Fineberg's six steps — unified command, testing, isolation, supply chains, public mobilization, equity — were not crazy. They were essentially what New Zealand and Australia executed, with results that should embarrass us. The fact that the United States could not muster a unified command is a fact about American political institutions in 2020, not a fact about the limits of preparedness thinking. To blame the manual because the country refused to read it is a curious form of analysis.

The deeper problem with Jasanoff's "technologies of humility" is that, as a positive program, they remain vague almost to the point of vanishing. We are told that humility means asking "who might get hurt, what happened when we tried before, whose perceptions were systematically ignored, and what protections are in place if we again guess wrong." These are excellent questions. They are also questions that good preparedness planning has been asking for decades. The Obama playbook explicitly flagged humanitarian impacts, political stability, and public concern. Pandemic exercises like Crimson Contagion in 2019 surfaced exactly the kind of federalism and supply-chain problems that humility advocates say were ignored. The After Action reports for H1N1 and Ebola took seriously the problem of distrust in marginalized communities. The intellectual content of "humility" is largely already inside the preparedness paradigm, even if it was honored more in the breach.

Where humility goes beyond preparedness is in its suspicion of action under uncertainty — and this is precisely where I think it courts danger. Cass Sunstein's old critique of the precautionary principle was not the caricature Jasanoff implies. His point was that "precaution" tends to be invoked asymmetrically: against new technologies, never against the harms of inaction. Imagine if a humility-driven FDA had subjected the mRNA vaccines to the kind of slow, consultative, citizen-participatory review process that Jasanoff's framework seems to recommend. We would still be debating Pfizer's emergency use authorization while another million people died. The vaccines were the single greatest policy success of the pandemic. They were the product not of humility but of audacious, predictive, capability-feeds-ambition science — Operation Warp Speed, massive parallel manufacturing bets, a willingness to compress timelines by acting in advance of full knowledge. That is preparedness at its best.

Jasanoff is right that the same was not true at every stage. The CDC's testing fiasco in February and March 2020, the on-again-off-again mask guidance, the FDA's slowness to authorize at-home rapid tests — these were real failures. But none of them was caused by overconfidence in prediction. They were caused by institutional caution, by an excess of bureaucratic conservatism, by a regulatory culture too humble to act without textbook-quality evidence. When the FDA dragged its feet on rapid tests because their sensitivity was lower than PCR, that was humility — humility about a technology's imperfections — that cost thousands of lives. When public health authorities refused to recommend masks in March 2020 because randomized trial data was thin, that too was humility. Sometimes the failure of expert institutions is precisely that they are too tentative, too hedged, too unwilling to make confident probabilistic bets under uncertainty.

There is also a sleight of hand in Jasanoff's invocation of "the unknown unknowns." Of course we should worry about surprises. But how does one prepare for what one cannot imagine? The honest answer is: by building robust, redundant capacity that can be repurposed against threats whose shape is not yet known. That is what preparedness, properly understood, is for. A well-staffed CDC, a domestic manufacturing base for PPE, a strategic national stockpile that is actually stocked, an interoperable public health data infrastructure, a trained reserve of contact tracers — these are not blueprints for fighting the last war. They are general-purpose institutional capabilities. They can be deployed against influenza, coronavirus, or something we have not yet named. The right response to unknown unknowns is not to plan less but to build more flexibly.

Finally, a word about the politics of humility. Jasanoff casts humility as the ethical alternative to the militaristic "can-do" posture of preparedness. But humility has its own moral dangers. It can shade into fatalism, into a willingness to accept harms as the price of acknowledging our limits. It can serve as alibi for inaction — exactly the charge Sunstein levels against precaution. And it can encourage a kind of expert defeatism that the public, faced with real and frightening threats, will not tolerate. People want their leaders to know what to do. The genius of the Coast Guard's "always ready" is not that it denies the possibility of defeat; it is that it commits the institution to act anyway. A democratic public can accept that its experts sometimes fail. It will not long tolerate experts who treat failure as a quasi-virtue.

The lesson of COVID-19 is not that we prepared too much. It is that we prepared abstractly, then governed terribly. The remedy is more preparedness, executed seriously, by states that fund the institutions they will need. Humility is a fine personal virtue. As a public-policy paradigm, it is no substitute for capacity, commitment, and the courage to act.

Sheila Jasanoff writes from the heights of science and technology studies, and the view from there is bracing. Her essay is one of the most thoughtful diagnoses of pandemic policy I have read. But I think her chosen target — the epistemic culture of "preparedness" — is the wrong one. The United States did not fail to control COVID-19 because its planners were insufficiently humble about uncertainty. It failed because, in January through April of 2020, when control was still possible, the country was led by an administration that actively denied the threat, suppressed information, fired or sidelined experts, and politicized basic protective measures. Replace that political fact with any one of half a dozen alternative histories — a Hillary Clinton administration, a Mitt Romney administration, even a normal-functioning Republican administration of the George W. Bush vintage — and we are talking about a very different pandemic. The frameworks would have been the same. The outcomes would not.

I do not say this to absolve the preparedness paradigm of its real shortcomings. Jasanoff is correct that the Global Health Security Index gave too little weight to political variables, that the Obama playbook assumed a level of inter-branch cooperation that no longer exists, and that "Ten Weeks to Crush the Curve" addressed a country that does not really resemble the one we live in. These are fair criticisms of expert documents that were written, in part, as if Donald Trump did not exist. But the criticism that the documents were apolitical is not the same as the criticism that preparedness as such is the wrong paradigm. The former calls for better preparedness; the latter calls for a different intellectual posture altogether. I think the former is correct and the latter is not.

Consider what actually happened in the first eight weeks of the pandemic. In January 2020, the National Security Council's pandemic preparedness directorate had been dissolved by John Bolton in 2018, removing the very coordination structure the playbook assumed. The president was briefed repeatedly on the threat from intelligence agencies and chose to dismiss it publicly, telling Bob Woodward that he wanted to "play it down." The CDC, the world's most sophisticated public health agency on paper, produced a defective test kit and then, for institutional and regulatory reasons, refused for weeks to authorize alternative tests from academic or commercial labs. The White House Coronavirus Task Force was reorganized around Jared Kushner, whose private-sector "shadow task force" undermined coherent federal procurement of PPE and ventilators. Governors were told to fend for themselves. Then, having lost the chance at suppression, the administration pivoted to a strategy of denial, theater, and culture war that turned masks into partisan symbols and undermined the eventual vaccine rollout among its own voters.

None of this was an epistemic failure. The relevant knowledge was sitting in the documents Jasanoff dissects. Anthony Fauci knew it. Robert Redfield knew it. Career officials at HHS and FEMA and the State Department knew it. What was missing was not humility; it was a functioning political executive willing to act on what was known. To attribute the catastrophe primarily to overconfident prediction is to give the political actors who actually drove the disaster a kind of intellectual alibi. They are recast as casualties of a flawed paradigm rather than as agents of choice.

The comparative evidence is instructive. Germany, which Jasanoff notes was ranked 14th in the GHSI yet outperformed the U.S. and U.K., did not deploy any radically different epistemic framework. It used essentially the same playbook of testing, tracing, isolation, and graduated restrictions that the WHO and the GHSI recommended. What Germany had was something the United States in 2020 did not: a competent head of government who took the threat seriously, deferred publicly to scientific advisors, and maintained at least the minimal political consensus required to sustain protective measures across the federal states. That is a political fact, not an epistemic one. South Korea, Taiwan, Vietnam, Australia, and New Zealand similarly succeeded not by inventing a new science of humility but by combining standard preparedness with competent political execution and, crucially, public trust earned by transparent communication.

The places that failed — the U.S., the U.K. under Boris Johnson, Brazil under Bolsonaro, India under Modi during the Delta wave — share a different profile. They were governed by populist nationalists who treated expertise as adversary and the pandemic as a public-relations problem. The pattern is striking enough that it ought to be the focus of any serious post-mortem. Yet "technologies of humility" do not really address it. What is the humility-based remedy for a head of state who refuses to listen? More citizen participation? Better stakeholder mapping? These prescriptions will not bite where the bite is most needed.

Jasanoff might respond that humility, as she defines it, is precisely about asking who is left out, who is hurt, whose voices are unheard — and that an inclusive process might generate the political legitimacy to make preparedness work. I am sympathetic to that ambition. But the historical record on participatory technology assessment is mixed at best. Deliberative exercises do good when they are coupled to actual decision authority and when the surrounding political culture has not already pre-sorted citizens into mutually hostile epistemic camps. In a country where 40 percent of the population now consumes news selected to confirm that the pandemic is a hoax engineered by Anthony Fauci and Bill Gates, an additional layer of participatory humility is not going to produce consensus. It may produce more polished documentation of the dissensus.

There is one place where I think humility, in Jasanoff's sense, would have helped: communication. The CDC's and WHO's early statements on masks, on asymptomatic transmission, and on surface contamination were issued with too much confidence and revised in ways that damaged credibility. A more candid acknowledgment, from the start, that "we are operating with incomplete data and our recommendations may change as we learn more" would have served public trust better than the false certainties followed by embarrassed reversals. Here the indictment of expert overconfidence has real bite. Public health authorities should learn to talk in confidence intervals, to flag what they do not know, to update without shame. That is humility worth cultivating.

But this is a refinement within preparedness, not a replacement of it. The architecture of pandemic response — surveillance, testing, tracing, treatment, vaccines, communication — does not change. What changes is the tone with which experts engage the public and the seriousness with which planners build political coalitions before the crisis hits. Those are valuable adjustments. They do not amount to a new paradigm, and elevating them to one risks distracting us from the unglamorous, profoundly political work of repairing the institutions and the democratic culture without which no paradigm — humble or heroic — can succeed.

The next pandemic will test, again, whether the United States can govern itself. That is the question Jasanoff's essay too gently sidesteps. Humility is a virtue. So is courage. So is competence. The country needs all three, and it needs them embodied in functioning political institutions. No reform of the expert imagination, however welcome, can substitute for that.

Whose Humility? A View from Outside the West

There is something faintly ironic about an essay urging humility that arrives, like so much of our pandemic commentary, framed almost entirely by the American experience. Sheila Jasanoff's analysis is acute, and her ethical instincts are admirable. But its frame of reference is consistently the United States and, to a lesser extent, the United Kingdom and Western Europe. The countries that actually performed well during the first eighteen months of COVID-19 — Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, Singapore, Thailand, New Zealand, Australia, Mongolia, Cuba — appear in her essay either glancingly or not at all. This is not a quibble. It bears directly on what we should conclude about preparedness, prediction, and humility, because the successful cases do not really fit Jasanoff's story.

What did the successful responders share? Not, on the whole, a more humble epistemic culture. Taiwan and South Korea, in particular, are technocratic societies whose pandemic responses leaned heavily on the most ambitious tools of prediction and control — digital contact tracing, large-scale testing programs, real-time data dashboards, integrated databases linking health insurance records to immigration data. These are, in Jasanoff's vocabulary, Promethean technologies. They worked. Taiwan's success was not built on doubt but on a kind of disciplined confidence: a central command structure under Audrey Tang and the CDC, an aggressive willingness to act on early signals from Wuhan, and a public that largely complied with restrictions. If anything, what these states demonstrated was that competent execution of relatively standard preparedness frameworks, combined with high social trust and serious state capacity, produces dramatically better outcomes than the same frameworks applied to a polarized, low-trust polity like the United States.

This matters because Alexandre White, whom Jasanoff briefly quotes, points to a deeper issue: the failure of Western pandemic policy was "rooted in the West's self-styled superiority to the rest of the world, especially when it comes to disease." The West congratulated itself on its preparedness indices while ignoring how much there was to learn from countries that had been through SARS, MERS, and avian influenza in living memory. East Asian polities did not need to manufacture humility through philosophical reflection; they had been humbled, repeatedly, by recent outbreaks, and they had built institutional muscle memory accordingly. Taiwanese officials had run pandemic exercises since 2004. Korean public health law had been revised after MERS in 2015 to permit rapid contact tracing and mandatory isolation. These are not the fruits of "humility as a stance"; they are the fruits of recent painful experience metabolized into administrative capacity.

It may be a more capable, more experienced state. The countries that did best were not the ones that thought more cautiously about what they did not know. Their epistemic posture is closer to "we have done this before; we know what to do; do it now" than to the philosophical reflectiveness Jasanoff recommends. That is not arrogance; it is hard-won institutional learning.

Moreover, several of the successful responders relied on degrees of state intrusion that would sit uneasily with Jasanoff's humility framework. I want to be fair to Jasanoff. Her deepest concern is not really about state capacity or political execution. The history of public health is, after all, also a history of coercion against the poor, against immigrants, against colonized peoples. The 1918 quarantines in San Francisco fell hardest on Chinatown. Cholera responses in the colonial 19th century were instruments of empire. But there is a tension here that the essay does not resolve. The countries that did best during COVID-19 generally did not arrive at their effective policies by foregrounding the voices of the marginalized. They arrived through executive authority exercised by relatively insulated technocracies, often with limited public deliberation, often with practices that the marginalized themselves did not endorse. The cultural homogeneity of some of these polities, and the centralized authority of others, allowed actions that no participatory process in a polarized society could have produced. The American failure, by contrast, was not principally a failure to listen to marginalized communities. It was a failure to maintain even the minimum political consensus required for the state to act coherently at all. In such a setting, more participation might deepen, rather than resolve, the impasse.

Second, the insistence that vulnerability is socially produced and predictable, and that any responsible plan must ask in advance who will bear the brunt of failure. Third, the call for genuine engagement with the populations whose distrust of expertise has its own historical reasons. These are important correctives, and they belong in any future preparedness framework.

The successful pandemic responders combined ambitious prediction, robust preparedness, decisive execution, and — only secondarily — sensitivity to political and social texture. The failed responders had the first two on paper and lacked the third entirely. The lesson is not to abandon Prometheus for a more reflective deity. It is to staff the institutions, train the personnel, fund the surveillance systems, and build the public trust that allow Promethean tools to be used responsibly. Humility is a useful supplement. It is not, on the evidence of the past two years, the missing pillar.

Public policy after the pandemic should certainly include more reflection. But it should include even more capacity. The latter, not the former, is what separated the countries that buried tens of thousands from the countries that did not.

The Humility of Leaving People Alone

Sheila Jasanoff’s meditation on pandemic policy is an honest effort to grapple with the spectacular failure of expert prediction. Her diagnosis—that the preparedness paradigm was fatally afflicted by hubris—deserves to be taken seriously. But her remedy, a suite of “technologies of humility” that would bring citizen participation, historical memory, and moral deliberation more deeply into the machinery of the state, misunderstands the very nature of the humility that a free society should cultivate. True political humility does not consist of more sophisticated mechanisms for collective decision-making. It consists of recognizing that there are hard limits on what government can know and should do, and that the most precautionary act of all is often to refrain from imposing one-size-fits-all edicts on millions of individuals who face locally distinct risks and hold widely varying values.

The pandemic did not expose a deficit of state humility; it exposed the enormous dangers of state overreach dressed in the language of science. In the United States, the most consequential early failures were not the absence of citizen advisory panels. They were the catastrophic decisions by federal and state officials to shut down economic and social life, often without clear exit criteria, and to issue continually shifting guidance about masks, surfaces, and outdoor transmission that eroded public trust. These measures were taken in the name of preparedness, but they were also the product of a particular kind of arrogance: the assumption that epidemiologists and public health mandarins could weigh the totality of costs and benefits for an entire population, substituting their own judgment for the diverse, dispersed knowledge held by individuals and communities.

Jasanoff’s call for “technologies of humility” seeks to correct this arrogance by incorporating more perspectives into the expert apparatus. But this merely broadens the circle of those who presume to plan for others. A citizen consultation process can lend a veneer of legitimacy to decisions that ought to be left to individuals. When a school board, after holding a series of listening sessions, decides to mandate masks for all children regardless of the local case rate or the particular vulnerabilities of families, it has not acted humbly; it has simply added a procedural step before imposing a uniform rule. True humility would mean allowing parents, teachers, and local communities to make their own risk assessments, with the best available information provided transparently, and without the threat of state sanction if they decline the official consensus.

This is not a libertarian caricature. It flows directly from the precautionary principle that Jasanoff herself invokes. Jasanoff's invocation of Lear is moving, but Lear's insight came after catastrophe, not before. We cannot manufacture the perspective of the marginalized through institutional design. We can, however, build systems that are robust to failure, that assume imperfection, and that can adapt when things go wrong. This is not humility in her sense, but it is a kind of epistemic modesty: not claiming to know how things will turn out, building in feedback mechanisms, and being willing to change course. That is a more modest ambition than technologies of humility, but it is also more achievable—and perhaps that is what we should expect from public policy.

Sheila Jasanoff's essay is a sophisticated account of pandemic policy failures, but it errs in attributing those failures to insufficient humility when they more accurately reflect the predictable consequences of expansive public health authority. Her framework assumes that the problem was too much confidence in the wrong direction; I would argue the problem was too much confidence in the right direction—confidence that experts knew best how to manage individual behavior for collective benefit.

Jasanoff critiques preparedness as a "heroic, can-do posture" that "bravely denies the possibility of defeat." But this framing conflates two different things: preparedness as a general disposition and preparedness as a justification for specific interventions. The latter is what generated the controversies of 2020 and 2021. Mask mandates, business closures, travel restrictions, vaccine passports—these were not failures of having a playbook. They were consequences of treating a respiratory virus as a problem that could be managed through the coordinated exercise of state power. That approach failed not because policymakers lacked humility but because the public, or significant portions of it, rejected the premise that such power was legitimate.

Jasanoff quotes Jay S. Kaufman saying that "the U.S. Achilles's heel is not lack of humility; it is a toxic social ideology rooted in a blind obsession with individual freedom." I would go further: that "obsession" is not a pathology but a feature of a constitutional order designed to limit centralized authority. The founders of the republic did not trust unified command. They separated powers, protected federalism, and enumerated individual rights precisely because they feared the consequences of concentrating decision-making in expert hands. The pandemic revealed not a failure of humility but a conflict between two visions of legitimate governance: one that prioritizes collective outcomes and accepts the costs of centralized direction, and one that prioritizes individual liberty and accepts the costs of decentralized response.

The GHSI's top rankings for the United States and United Kingdom, which Jasanoff rightly notes proved tragically inaccurate, were not simply wrong about national capabilities. They were measuring a particular kind of capacity—the ability to implement coordinated responses—that may be inherently limited in societies where such coordination is contested. Countries that scored lower but performed better did so not because they were humbler but because they had different political structures, different relationships between state and citizen, and different cultural expectations about compliance with public health guidance.

Jasanoff argues that humility "permits steps to be taken here and now in order to forestall worst-case scenarios later" through "technologies of humility" that incorporate diverse perspectives. But the history of such attempts suggests they tend to legitimate decisions made on other grounds. The Schmidt Futures Forum she describes featured distinguished experts reflecting on their own failures—a process of introspection that changed nothing about the power structures that produced those failures. Genuine humility, in her sense, would require those with authority to share it with those who lack it. But the essay never explains how this is supposed to happen within existing institutional frameworks.

The mask wars, the vaccine resistance, the school board conflicts—these were not failures of humility. They were expressions of a pluralistic society in which different communities held different values, trusted different sources of authority, and made different calculations about acceptable risk. Jasanoff sees this as a problem to be solved by better policy. I see it as a condition to be navigated, with imperfect tools, through the slow work of democratic persuasion rather than the swift exercise of expert judgment. The pandemic taught us that "following the science" was always a political choice, not a neutral one—because science could not resolve questions about what risks were acceptable, whose welfare should be prioritized, and how to balance present harms against future ones.

Humility might have softened the edges of pandemic politics, but it would not have resolved its fundamental tensions. Those tensions are built into the structure of liberal democracy, and they will persist regardless of how much we introspect about our epistemic limitations. The better lesson of the pandemic may be this: prepare for the next crisis by building resilient systems that can function even when unified command fails—by strengthening local capacity, diversifying supply chains, and maintaining redundancy in critical infrastructure. These are not humble aspirations, but they are realistic ones.

Sheila Jasanoff's call for humility resonates differently depending on where you sit. For American readers, her essay offers a critique of elite overreach from someone who is herself a Harvard professor and member of the policy establishment. For much of the world, however, the pandemic revealed a different hierarchy of failures: not primarily a crisis of overconfident preparedness but a crisis of global inequality in which wealthy nations hoarded vaccines, blocked patent waivers, and treated the virus as solved at home while it devastated countries with no access to doses.

Jasanoff's analysis is strongest when examining domestic policy but thins considerably when the scope widens. She notes that the GHSI placed the United States and United Kingdom at the top and small, war-torn states at the bottom. But this ranking was not simply wrong about preparedness; it reflected a worldview that equates health security with the capacity for coordinated state action—a metric designed by American institutions that implicitly assumed their own model as the standard. Countries that scored poorly often lacked not humility but resources, colonial legacies that disrupted indigenous governance, or populations too young and mobile to implement shelter-in-place orders. Their failures were not epistemic but material, and no amount of humility in Washington would have changed the fact that they had no vaccines.

The essay mentions that "the virus that should have been conquered" continued to mutate and spread, but it does not adequately address why. The rise of variants was not simply a consequence of insufficient humility in wealthy nations. It was a predictable consequence of vast inequities in vaccination. As long as large populations remain unvaccinated, the virus has opportunities to evolve. The Omicron variant, which emerged in southern Africa after months of low vaccination coverage, made this dynamic tragically visible. Jasanoff's technologies of humility would have asked who was left out of planning—but the answer was not that their perspectives were ignored. It was that they could not afford to participate in a system designed to enrich pharmaceutical companies in donor nations.

The patent waiver debate that roiled international bodies in 2021 illustrated the limits of humility as a policy principle. India and South Africa proposed suspending intellectual property protections for COVID vaccines to enable generic production in lower-income countries. The proposal was framed in terms of global solidarity and shared sacrifice—in the language of humility. But wealthy nations, representing populations that had already been vaccinated, blocked the waiver for months, citing the need to protect innovation incentives. This was not a failure of humility. It was a failure of interest alignment in a global system that distributes vulnerability based on wealth. No amount of institutional redesign in Washington would have produced different outcomes unless the underlying incentive structure changed.

Jasanoff is right that preparedness frameworks assumed a world that did not exist—a world of unified national responses, compliant populations, and sufficient state capacity. But the world that does exist is not simply a matter of attitudes or institutions. It is structured by material inequalities that shape who can afford to wait, who can work from home, who has access to healthcare, and who lives in multigenerational households where isolation is impossible. Humility in the face of uncertainty is a privilege of the secure. For those facing immediate survival pressures, the luxury of epistemic modesty is irrelevant.

The essay quotes Alexandre White saying that pandemic failings "are rooted in the West's self-styled superiority to the rest of the world, especially when it comes to disease." This is closer to the mark than Jasanoff's framing suggests. The failures were not primarily failures of humility but failures of solidarity—of treating global health as a market opportunity rather than a common good, of building vaccine nationalism rather than vaccine equity, of assuming that the solutions developed in Boston and Berlin would work in Botswana and Bangladesh. Genuine humility would require not just better institutional mechanisms but a fundamental reorientation of global health governance away from charity and toward justice. That is a political project, not an epistemological one, and Jasanoff's essay, for all its sophistication, underestimates the distance between acknowledging uncertainty and changing the structures that produce it.
