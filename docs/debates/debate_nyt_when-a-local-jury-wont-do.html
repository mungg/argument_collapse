When a Local Jury Won’t Do

An appeals court has upheld a federal judge’s denial of Dzhokhar Tsarnaev’s plea to move his trial in the Boston Marathon bombing outside of Massachusetts.

Tsarnaev’s lawyers said he could not get a fair trial in the city that was traumatized by the deadly bombing. But how should courts decide when a trial in a highly publicized case needs to be moved beyond where the crime occurred?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The First Circuit's refusal to compel a change of venue in Tsarnaev's case rests on a sensible premise: pretrial publicity, even saturating publicity, does not automatically poison a jury pool. The Supreme Court reaffirmed this in Skilling v. United States (2010), where Jeffrey Skilling's trial stayed in Houston despite Enron's devastation of that city. The Court emphasized that prejudice must be presumed only in extreme cases — Rideau-style situations where a televised confession or comparable spectacle leaves no realistic chance of impartiality — and otherwise must be shown through voir dire.

That framework is largely right, but it underweights a distinct factor present in Boston: communal trauma. Skilling lost people's pensions; the Marathon bombing killed and maimed neighbors, shut down a metropolitan area, and turned an annual civic ritual into a crime scene. "Boston Strong" was not just slogan but identity. When a crime is experienced collectively rather than merely reported, jurors may carry a personal stake that careful questioning cannot fully surface.

Courts should therefore apply a sliding scale. Where publicity is the main concern, voir dire is usually adequate; modern jurors regularly compartmentalize what they've read. But where the crime itself wounded the community hosting the trial — mass-casualty attacks, disasters tied to local landmarks, killings of local officials — the presumption should tilt toward transfer, with the government bearing the burden of showing why an unaffected venue cannot serve justice equally well.

This approach respects the constitutional preference for trial in the district of the offense while honoring the deeper Sixth Amendment guarantee. A defendant's right to an impartial jury should not depend on how stoically a traumatized city insists it can be fair.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The real question isn’t whether Dzhokhar Tsarnaev can get a fair trial in Boston, but what “community” means when an entire city becomes the victim. The appeals court’s decision to keep the trial in Massachusetts reflects an uncomfortable truth: our legal venue rules were built for a world where a crime’s impact rarely saturated the conscience of millions at once. Under the standard articulated by the Supreme Court in *Skilling v. United States*, a change of venue is required only when pretrial publicity creates a “presumption of prejudice” so pervasive that it’s impossible to seat an impartial jury through careful questioning. On its face, the saturation coverage of the marathon bombings, the lockdown, and the manhunt might seem to meet that bar. But the *Skilling* Court also signaled that a community’s size and its diversity of experiences matter, and that voir dire—not relocation—is the primary safeguard.

What makes the Tsarnaev case genuinely novel isn’t just media attention; it’s the nature of the trauma. The bombing wasn’t simply witnessed on television. It shut down streets people walked, killed a child from a neighborhood school, and turned the finish line of a civic celebration into a war zone. You can screen jurors for exposure to news reports, but you cannot screen them for the shared memory of being told to shelter in place while their streets were scoured by armored vehicles. That is not prejudice in the traditional sense of having formed an opinion about guilt; it is a collective wound that could color how any local juror weighs evidence or views the defendant’s humanity.

And yet, granting a change of venue on those grounds carries its own democratic cost. The Constitution guarantees a trial before a jury drawn from the district where the crime occurred, a nod to community self-governance. If we define an impacted city as inherently biased, we strip its residents of the right to pass judgment on the crime that most directly shattered their civic peace. The harder but more honest course is what the judge will now attempt: an exhaustive, transparent voir dire process that probes not just what potential jurors saw on the news but how the bombing altered their sense of safety and fairness. The test shouldn’t be whether Bostonians are emotionally scarred—they are—but whether that scar tissue still allows enough of them to deliberate on evidence alone. That, not a simple relocation, is how courts should decide.

The Sixth Amendment guarantees a criminal defendant both an impartial jury and a trial in the district where the crime occurred. In an era of ubiquitous media, fleeing publicity is impossible; a high-profile defendant will face headlines anywhere in the country. But publicity is not the same as trauma. When deciding whether to move a highly publicized trial, courts must distinguish between a community that has merely watched a tragedy unfold and one that has directly survived it.

In the case of Dzhokhar Tsarnaev, the defense’s argument for moving the trial is not simply that Bostonians consumed massive amounts of news, but that the entire metropolitan area was effectively victimized. Millions of residents were subjected to an unprecedented "shelter in place" lockdown while armed authorities swept neighborhoods. Furthermore, the pervasive "Boston Strong" civic response, while unifying and resilient for the city, inherently compromises the detached objectivity required of a jury pool. In this context, nearly every prospective local juror is a secondary victim.

Courts traditionally rely on rigorous voir dire—extensive questioning of prospective jurors—to weed out biased individuals, a standard the Supreme Court emphasized in the *Skilling* case involving Enron. However, the better comparative precedent is the 1995 Oklahoma City bombing. Timothy McVeigh’s trial was rightfully relocated to Denver because the magnitude of the trauma inflicted on the local community was too deep to trust the standard jury selection process.

Judges deciding on venue changes should look beyond mere media saturation and evaluate the psychological footprint of the crime. When an attack targets the civic identity of a city and physically threatens its entire population, the presumption of prejudice is often insurmountable. In such rare cases, basic fairness demands relocation.

Courts should not move a trial merely because a crime was notorious. If that were the rule, terrorism cases, political assassinations and mass shootings would almost always be tried far from the community most directly harmed. The Constitution promises an impartial jury, not an uninformed one.

But the Boston Marathon bombing presents the hard case in which ordinary confidence in voir dire may be misplaced. The question is not whether potential jurors have heard of the case; of course they have. It is whether the local atmosphere is so saturated with grief, civic solidarity and personal connection that jurors cannot realistically separate evidence from identity. In Massachusetts, “Boston Strong” was not just a slogan. It was a public ritual of mourning and defiance. Many residents had been at the Marathon, knew someone who was, sheltered during the manhunt, or absorbed months of local coverage about victims, police officers and the city’s recovery.

In a case like Tsarnaev’s, the strongest argument for transfer is not that Bostonians are uniquely vengeful. It is that the community’s trauma is uniquely intimate. A federal court elsewhere could still apply the law, hear the evidence and deliver justice with greater public confidence.

The Sixth Amendment guarantees trial "by an impartial jury of the State and district wherein the crime shall have been committed," and this language is not accidental. Local juries embody the community's stake in seeing justice done — and seeing it done in public, in the very place wounded by the offense. Judges should decide venue questions by looking at the actual results of jury selection, not at pretrial assumptions about community feeling. If qualified jurors can be empaneled, the trial belongs at home. Boston has earned the right to render this verdict.

We should be honest about what venue change really accomplishes in the modern era: very little. The Boston Marathon bombing was a national, indeed international, news story. There is no federal district in America whose residents are unaware of it. Moving the trial to Manhattan or Washington or San Francisco would produce a jury pool that consumed essentially the same news coverage as a Boston pool, minus the direct personal experience of the lockdown.

Is that direct personal experience legally significant? Sometimes. A juror who was running the marathon, or who lost a relative, would be struck for cause anywhere. But the marginal Boston juror — someone who watched the events on TV, like Americans everywhere — is no more biased than a marginal juror in another city who also watched on TV. Voir dire is the right tool to sort out the genuinely conflicted from the merely informed.

The deeper problem is that our jurisprudence on venue is rooted in a pre-broadcast world, when local newspapers actually shaped a juror's information environment in ways that distant newspapers did not. That world is gone. Today, the relevant question is less geographic than psychological: which prospective jurors have formed firm opinions that they cannot set aside? That inquiry can be done in Boston as well as anywhere else.

So my standard would be a procedural one. Trial courts should conduct extended, individualized voir dire in high-profile cases; allow generous use of cause challenges; and consider venue change only if the actual results of jury selection demonstrate that an impartial panel cannot be seated. Pretrial speculation about community sentiment, fueled by defense surveys and prosecutorial counter-surveys, is a poor substitute for seeing whether a jury can in fact be assembled. In Tsarnaev's case, Judge O'Toole's decision to test the waters through voir dire before ruling on venue is exactly the right approach.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

There is something faintly absurd about the venue change debate as it is usually conducted. Both sides pretend that geography is a proxy for impartiality, when in fact it is a proxy for a tangled bundle of things: media exposure, personal connection, community identity, and emotional involvement. Treating these as a single variable, and asking whether to move a trial across a state line as if that solves them all, has always been crude.

Consider some alternatives that get less attention than they deserve. Federal courts could expand the use of imported jurors — bringing in a panel from another district to hear the case in the original venue, as has occasionally been done. This preserves the symbolic and practical advantages of trying the case where the crime occurred (victim and witness convenience, community witnessing of justice) while addressing the worry about local bias. Courts could also use extended sequestration, gag orders that actually have teeth, and aggressive limits on prejudicial pretrial publicity by counsel.

We could also be more honest about the limits of voir dire. The research on juror self-assessment is not flattering. Jurors who insist they can set aside what they have read are often wrong about themselves. But this problem doesn't disappear by changing venue — it just becomes less salient. The right response is to invest more in the quality of jury selection: longer questioning, written questionnaires, individual sequestered voir dire, and a willingness to grant cause challenges generously.

For Tsarnaev specifically, I would have favored either an imported jury or a venue change to Western Massachusetts. But the more interesting reform conversation is whether we should stop framing this as a binary venue question at all. The toolkit for managing prejudice in high-profile trials is broader than judges typically use. Saturation publicity is now the rule, not the exception, and our procedures need to catch up.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The First Circuit's decision is defensible, and the criticisms of it reflect a misunderstanding of what a fair trial requires. A fair trial does not require ignorance; it requires impartiality. These are different things. Jurors in serious cases routinely know something about the events they are asked to judge. What the law demands is that they decide the case on the evidence and the judge's instructions, not on what they have heard outside the courtroom.

Tsarnaev's lawyers point to surveys showing that Bostonians overwhelmingly believe he is guilty. But Tsarnaev is guilty, in the ordinary-language sense — he was photographed planting one of the bombs, he is on video, and his own defense has signaled that the central fight will be over sentencing, not over whether he did it. A jury pool that thinks the defendant in an open-and-shut case probably did the thing is not for that reason a biased jury pool. The constitutional concern arises when juror prejudgment cannot be overcome by evidence; here, the evidence is the source of the prejudgment.

The right test for venue change has several components. First, has the publicity been substantively prejudicial — i.e., has it disseminated inadmissible information (confessions, prior records, lurid speculation) that jurors cannot unhear? Second, is the community so personally involved that jurors are effectively victims themselves? Third, can voir dire realistically sort qualified from unqualified jurors given the size of the venire? And fourth — too often ignored — would any other venue actually be better, or just differently bad?

In Tsarnaev's case, the answers point toward keeping the trial in Boston. The publicity has been heavy but largely factual. The community is large enough to yield qualified jurors. And no plausible alternative venue is meaningfully less exposed. The First Circuit got this one right, and the better course is to trust the process: rigorous voir dire, careful instructions, and faith that ordinary citizens can do their constitutional job.

In the digital age, the change of venue for high-profile crimes is becoming a legal relic. The internet, national cable news, and social media have dissolved the boundaries that once made a different physical location mean a different informational environment. When everyone with a smartphone can follow the manhunt, watch the same cellphone footage of a pressure-cooker bomb, and read the same criminal complaint on Twitter, there is no truly untainted jury pool anywhere in the country. In a nationally notorious case, where the crime is known from coast to coast, the best—and perhaps only—defense of impartiality lies in the rigor of the selection process, not in a fruitless search for some virgin territory of ignorance. The adversarial process works best when courts preserve flexibility rather than making categorical rulings based on general conditions. The Appeals Court navigated this balance thoughtfully.

We should interrogate the assumption that moving trials protects defendants. The history of venue changes reveals troubling patterns: they have been used strategically to find compliant juries, to exhaust resources, and to distance defendants from sympathetic communities. The change of venue doctrine must serve justice, not merely strategic advantage.

Tsarnaev faces overwhelming evidence: physical evidence, video documentation, witness testimony, and his own writings. No venue change can meaningfully alter the trial's outcome when the evidence is so extensive. What his lawyers likely seek is not a genuinely fair jury but a less informed one—a community where the bombing's impact has faded enough to permit procedural arguments to overshadow factual ones.

This points to a deeper tension in our justice system. We want trials to occur in communities affected by crime because accountability requires presence and participation. Yet we also want defendants to face neutral fact-finders. These goals occasionally conflict, and courts must choose. The Appeals Court chose community participation over speculative gains from transfer.

Boston's citizens deserve the opportunity to see justice done in their community. They did not ask for this attack, but they must live with its consequences. Denying them participation in the trial compounds the injury. The Appeals Court recognized this, and its decision deserves support.

The Tsarnaev case exposes a growing problem in American jurisprudence: the tension between local accountability and fair trial rights as trials become increasingly national events. When crimes are broadcast instantly and analyzed continuously, the traditional rationale for local venue—that juries represent community standards—loses coherence.

A Massachusetts juror brings the same televised footage into deliberations as a California juror. The community context that venue rules assumed no longer exists in high-profile cases. Perhaps the law should acknowledge this reality by developing new frameworks for how defendants can receive fair trials when the crime's "community" is effectively the entire nation.

The Appeals Court applied traditional doctrine to unprecedented circumstances. This is not criticism—the court had no alternative. But we should recognize that existing change of venue standards evolved when media coverage was local and bounded. The Boston Marathon bombing generated international attention within hours. Every potential juror in America knew the basic facts.

Future courts will need to address whether traditional venue analysis survives the social media age. Until then, we rely on voir dire and judicial discretion to identify fair juries. Given the alternatives—unlimited venue changes that would cripple the justice system or trials without meaningful impartiality safeguards—this reliance seems unavoidable, even if imperfect.
