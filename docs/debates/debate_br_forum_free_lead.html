Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Governor Roemer's diagnosis lands the central blow squarely: the daily work of a member of Congress has been reorganized around the call sheet, and that reorganization warps everything downstream of it — tax code, regulatory writing, even the schedule of committee hearings. He is right that the puzzle of the Research and Experimentation tax credit, perpetually temporary across three decades, is best explained not as economic uncertainty but as a fundraising convenience. Sunsets are a feature, not a bug, because they generate a recurring need to be lobbied. That is a useful, concrete piece of evidence, and it deserves more weight than the macro-rhetoric about Wall Street that occupies the middle of the essay.

Where I want to push back, or at least complicate, is on two fronts: the theory of corruption Roemer offers, and the remedy he sketches.

On the theory, Roemer says explicitly that he doubts a single member was bribed in the deregulation of derivatives — that "the system is corrupt." This is the Lessig-style structural account, and it is more honest than the cartoon version of campaign-finance critique. But it sits uneasily with the rest of his essay, which keeps reverting to the cartoon. The financial crisis was not simply a story of bought politicians. It was also a story of genuine intellectual capture among economists and regulators (Greenspan's "found a flaw" testimony in 2008 is the canonical admission), of ratings-agency conflicts that are not campaign-finance problems, of a global savings glut that pushed yields down and risk-taking up, and of regulators like the OCC and OTS competing for business with the institutions they oversaw. If we passed Roemer's voucher reform tomorrow, none of those pathologies would vanish. Money in politics is necessary to his story but not sufficient, and presenting it as the master variable — "the most important problem," ahead of the debt, foreign oil, manufacturing decline — risks substituting one monocausal politics for another.

On the remedy, the four principles are admirably disciplined, and the democracy-voucher idea (Ackerman and Ayres's "Patriot dollars" being the obvious antecedent) is the most promising small-donor design on the table. But Roemer underestimates how Citizens United, decided just eighteen months ago, reshapes the terrain he wants to fight on. His system addresses candidate fundraising. It does not touch the independent-expenditure shadow campaign that grew 130 percent in 2010 — a figure he himself cites. If a senator knows that a $10 million independent buy can materialize against her in the closing weeks of a race, the candidate-side voucher pool is a partial palliative at best. We are likely heading into a 2012 cycle in which Super PACs, not candidate committees, dominate the message environment. Reform that ignores this is reform fighting the last war.

There is also a tension Roemer does not resolve between his first principle (silence no one) and his diagnosis (independent expenditures are "the ugliest and least responsible political speech of our culture"). You cannot consistently celebrate the kernel of Citizens United while wanting to suppress its primary effect. The honest position is either to defend a constitutional amendment narrowing Buckley v. Valeo's expenditure-equals-speech holding, or to concede that vouchers are a supplement, not a cure. Roemer wants to have both.

Finally, a word about the messenger. Roemer's candidacy — a Republican former Democratic governor running on a $100-cap pledge — is admirable, and the pledge gives him standing. But the pundits dismissing his chances are not just being cynical. The structural problem he describes is precisely the one that makes his candidacy unviable: a primary system in which earned media follows poll numbers, poll numbers follow name recognition, and name recognition follows paid media. That is a vicious circle a $100 cap cannot break without the kind of small-donor infrastructure that, in mid-2011, only Obama has come close to building.

None of this is a reason to dismiss the essay. Roemer is asking the right question — what would a Congress free to lead look like? — and he is asking it from a place of genuine experience. He deserves a hearing. He also deserves a sharper answer than the one he has so far given himself.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Governor Roemer’s diagnosis of a Congress “obsessed with raising campaign funds” and a tax code rigged for “the richest and most powerful” rings true for anyone watching Washington. His autobiographical tale of the Research and Experimentation tax credit—a good policy held hostage to the permanent re-election industry—is a powerful indictment. And he is right that the 2008 financial disaster exposed a regulatory system captured by the very banks it was supposed to oversee. The central question is whether the cure he prescribes—a $100 contribution limit, a ban on PACs, and a voucher-funded alternative—can actually break that dependency. I am not convinced it can, and in important ways, it risks leaving the field even more open to the very forces he decries.

First, Roemer’s core story about the R&E tax credit is not quite the smoking gun he thinks it is. Temporary tax provisions, known as “extenders,” are a perennial feature of the code not simply because they generate campaign cash, though that happens, but because budget rules require Congress to offset the cost of making them permanent. The real obstacle has often been a coalition of deficit hawks and competing priorities, not just a thirst for contributions. The fact that Congress repeatedly extends the credit suggests broad bipartisan support, not a hostage situation. Indeed, making it permanent would require lawmakers to find tens of billions in revenue elsewhere or add to the deficit—a difficult vote for any member, regardless of who funds them. The system’s dysfunction is more complex than Roemer’s morality play.

Second, Roemer’s fix focuses almost entirely on contributions to candidates, yet the post-Citizens United landscape has made independent expenditures the dominant force. By July 2011, we have just seen the 2010 midterms awash in $300 million of outside spending, much of it from undisclosed donors through newly minted super PACs and dark-money nonprofits. A candidate who limits herself to $100 contributions—while admirable in principle—would be unilaterally disarmed against a tidal wave of negative ads funded by a single billionaire or a corporate trade association. Roemer acknowledges Citizens United but claims we can craft a system within the Court’s boundaries. The problem is that those boundaries now treat money as speech and corporations as entitled speakers; any scheme that effectively neuters candidates’ ability to respond to outside attacks will simply shift influence even further into the shadows, where no disclosure or limit applies. The recent experience of his own presidential campaign is instructive: by adhering to his principles, he has been excluded from debates and largely ignored, while well-funded super PACs define the terms of the race.

Third, the voucher proposal, while creative, runs headlong into the very constitutional constraints Roemer says we must respect. The Supreme Court in Davis v. FEC struck down the “millionaire’s amendment” that allowed candidates to raise more money when facing a self-funder, precisely because it burdened the wealthy candidate’s speech to level the playing field. A system that ties vouchers or public funds to a $100 contribution cap would almost certainly be challenged as coercive, because it conditions a government benefit on forgoing one’s right to spend. And if vouchers are simply added on top of unlimited private contributions—as Roemer seems to suggest with his tax-return allocation idea—then they become just another small stream of money alongside the torrent of outside spending, doing little to reduce Congress’s dependence on wealth.

Roemer says we must “expand the number of givers,” not silence anyone. That is exactly the logic of the Fair Elections Now Act, which he dismisses for its “large government-determined subsidies.” But his own voucher idea is, at bottom, a public subsidy too—only dressed up as a voluntary tax designation. This hardly escapes the charge that citizens would be “forced to support political speech” they disagree with, unless the voucher is strictly opt-in, which would dramatically limit its reach. The 99 percent who have never given a dime are not suddenly going to become small donors in numbers sufficient to rival the Koch brothers or George Soros, especially when they see that candidates can barely stay afloat against the outside onslaught.

The deeper flaw in Roemer’s analysis is that it reduces all of government’s failure to a single variable. Yes, money corrupts. But even a Congress miraculously freed from fundraising would still be populated by members from gerrymandered districts, driven by the demands of primary voters at the ideological poles, and skewered by a media ecosystem that rewards conflict. The R&E credit story is not only about campaign contributions; it is also about a legislative process that can’t make hard trade-offs. The financial deregulation Roemer laments was passed with broad bipartisan majorities not just because of bank lobbying, but because many economists, journalists, and voters genuinely believed that unleashing Wall Street was the path to prosperity. Ideology and groupthink can be as powerful a corruptor as cash.

None of this is to minimize the cancer of money in politics. Roemer’s call to make Congress “dependent on the people alone” is the right aspiration. But it cannot be achieved by a nostalgic return to the pre-Citizens United world of small contributions and no PACs, because that world was fragile even before the Court dismantled it. If we want a Congress free to lead, we need structural reforms that meet the moment: aggressive disclosure to force outside spenders into the light, a constitutional amendment to undo the damage of Citizens United, and yes, robust public financing that actually gives voters a reason to believe their hundred dollars matters. Roemer has started an essential conversation, but his solution has too much faith in the purity of small money and too little armor against the billion-dollar battlefield already upon us.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Governor Roemer’s diagnosis of our political dysfunction is refreshingly blunt and undeniably accurate. By tracing the straight line from campaign fundraising to the 2008 financial collapse, he lays bare the systemic corruption that dictates policy in Washington. Congress does not govern; it collects. The toothless nature of the Dodd-Frank reforms—which left the "too big to fail" banks larger than ever—serves as the ultimate testament to the financial sector's return on its campaign investments.

However, while Roemer’s diagnosis is flawless, his prescribed cure—a voluntary $100 contribution limit coupled with taxpayer-funded democracy vouchers—underestimates the structural hurdles of our current electoral landscape. We have already seen the limitations of the Internet-driven, small-donor revolution that Roemer praises. In 2008, Barack Obama shattered fundraising records largely by tapping into a vast network of small, online contributors. Millions of Americans had "skin in the game." Yet, upon taking office, the administration turned to Wall Street veterans to manage the economic recovery, favoring the very institutions that caused the crisis. A broad base of small donors does not automatically immunize a politician from the institutional gravity of the financial sector.

Furthermore, Roemer’s proposal to operate strictly within the boundaries set by *Citizens United* is a dangerous concession. He suggests that we can cure the system by expanding the pool of givers, thereby drowning out special-interest money. But how does a candidate subsisting on $100 vouchers compete with the limitless coffers of independent expenditures? The 2010 midterms offered a grim preview of our new reality: outside groups, operating free from coordination limits, unleashed unprecedented floods of negative advertising. The newly minted "Super PACs" can accept unlimited checks from billionaires and corporations. A voucher system might raise the financial floor for grassroots candidates, but *Citizens United* has entirely removed the ceiling for the wealthiest 1 percent. In this environment, asking a candidate to voluntarily cap their direct contributions at $100 is akin to sending them into a modern war zone armed only with a pocketknife.

Even if a candidate is pure, the threat of an independent, multimillion-dollar smear campaign is often enough to keep them in line. The implicit threat of corporate spending can dictate a legislative agenda just as effectively as a direct campaign contribution. If we accept the Supreme Court’s premise that spending money is equivalent to protected speech, then trying to out-fundraise corporate America through micro-donations is mathematically doomed. To truly free leadership to lead, we must pursue more aggressive structural reforms. At a minimum, Congress must pass robust disclosure requirements—such as the DISCLOSE Act, which died in the Senate last year—so that voters know exactly which corporate entities are funding the attack ads. Additionally, we should explore corporate governance reforms that require shareholder approval before a company’s treasury can be used for political expenditures. Ultimately, if the Court refuses to distinguish between democratic participation and corporate electioneering, the only permanent remedy may be a constitutional amendment.

Governor Roemer is entirely correct that money in politics is the foundational issue of our time. It is the bottleneck through which all other crises—from our ballooning national debt to our reliance on foreign oil—must pass. His willingness to eschew PAC money and rely solely on small donors is a noble, necessary demonstration of principled leadership. But we must be clear-eyed about the sheer scale of the opposition. Fixing the system requires more than adding new, small voices to the choir; it requires turning down the megaphone that the Supreme Court has handed to the highest bidders. Until we find a way to rein in independent expenditures, the boomtown of Washington will continue to thrive at the expense of Main Street.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Buddy Roemer is right to insist that corruption in Washington is not mainly a matter of envelopes passed under tables. The more serious corruption is dependency: legislators learn, often without needing to be told, whose calls must be returned, whose amendments must be taken seriously, whose “technical” objections can kill a bill. That is why the research tax credit example is powerful. A temporary provision creates a recurring tollbooth. The toll may be perfectly legal, but the public has reason to suspect that policy is being arranged to maximize fundraising opportunities rather than public benefit.

He is also right that the financial crisis exposed this dependency with unusual clarity. The years before 2008 were not merely years of mistaken economic theory. They were years in which the financial sector developed the political capacity to make its preferred theories look inevitable. Derivatives regulation was blocked; leverage was tolerated; regulators competed to be accommodating; and warnings from figures such as Brooksley Born and Jim Leach were treated as eccentric. Campaign contributions did not have to buy a specific vote to matter. They helped create an intellectual and political climate in which deference to Wall Street became the normal posture of both parties.

Still, Roemer’s argument is strongest when he describes money as a disabling condition, and weakest when he treats it as the master explanation for nearly every failure of governance. Money matters enormously, but it does not act alone. Congress is also distorted by partisan sorting, safe districts, the filibuster, the permanent campaign, cable-news incentives, and a primary electorate that is often more ideological than the general public. The current debt-ceiling confrontation, unfolding as we write, is not easily reducible to PAC checks. It is also a story about party strategy, anti-tax pledges, fear of primary challenges, and a constitutional system that makes obstruction easy.

This distinction matters because if we misdiagnose the disease, we may overpromise what campaign-finance reform can cure. A Congress funded by small donors alone might be less beholden to lobbyists, but not necessarily more deliberative. Small donors can be passionate, polarized, and unrepresentative too. Internet fundraising has democratized participation, but it has also rewarded outrage and nationalized local races. A member who depends on thousands of $25 contributions generated by ideological e-mails may be freer from Goldman Sachs and still not free to compromise.

Roemer’s own $100 limit is admirable as a personal ethic. It is less convincing as a general model. A presidential candidate with charisma, novelty, or celebrity may be able to build a national small-donor base. A little-known House challenger in an expensive media market may not. Incumbents already enjoy name recognition, official staff, franking privileges, and networks of local influence. If contribution limits are too low without some compensating public mechanism, reform can unintentionally protect the very officeholders it is meant to discipline.

That is why the best answer is not simply “less money,” but different dependencies. Congress should be dependent on a broader public. Small-donor matching systems, such as the one used in New York City, point in the right direction: a modest contribution from an ordinary citizen can be amplified, giving candidates an incentive to seek many voters rather than a few patrons. Properly designed, such systems do not silence anyone. They add speech. They do not require bureaucrats to choose winners; voters allocate the funds by choosing whom to support. Roemer’s voucher idea is similarly attractive, though I would not reject all public financing merely because tax dollars are involved. We already publicly finance courts, ballots, election administration, and the salaries of officials whose views many citizens oppose. Democracy is a public good.

Disclosure is also indispensable, especially after Citizens United. If corporations, unions, trade associations, and wealthy individuals may spend unlimited sums independently, voters should know promptly who is speaking. Disclosure should be real-time, searchable, and difficult to evade through shell organizations. Shareholders and union members should have meaningful notice of political spending done in their name. Contractors seeking federal business should not be able to hide political expenditures relevant to the officials who oversee them.

Finally, campaign finance reform must be joined to lobbying reform. The revolving door between Congress, regulators, and the industries they oversee deepens the same dependency that campaign money creates. Longer cooling-off periods, clearer rules for bundled contributions, better funding for regulatory agencies, and greater transparency in legislative drafting would all help. So would simplifying the tax code, not because simplicity is always superior, but because complexity creates hiding places for favors.

Roemer’s central moral claim remains compelling: leaders cannot lead if their first obligation is to finance the next campaign. But freedom to lead will not come from one pledge or one statute. It will require building institutions that reward candidates for listening widely, governing seriously, and explaining tradeoffs honestly. Money is not the only barrier to that politics. It is, however, one barrier we have chosen to tolerate for too long.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Governor Roemer offers a forceful diagnosis of what ails Congress, and his diagnosis is largely correct: the dependency on campaign finance has distorted legislative priorities, stalled good policy, and created a legislature incapable of addressing the nation's most pressing challenges. Where I would push back, however, is on his prescription. His proposed $100 individual contribution limit and ban on PAC contributions, while appealing in theory, encounter practical and philosophical obstacles that his essay underestimates.

Consider his four principles of reform. He insists the system must not silence anyone, must not force citizens to fund speech they reject, must let the people determine campaign resources, and must encourage small individual contributions. These principles are genuinely important, and Roemer is right that past reform efforts have often sacrificed one principle to advance another. The McCain-Feingold approach, for instance, tried to restrict corporate speech but ended up creating confusing loopholes that benefit those with the resources to navigate complexity. A system that genuinely balances all four principles would be preferable.

But the democracy voucher scheme Roemer floats deserves scrutiny. His version would allocate small funds from individual tax returns to citizens, who could then direct those funds to candidates agreeing to the $100 limit. This has genuine appeal—it expands the universe of givers rather than restricting giving, and it maintains individual choice. Yet it still faces significant challenges. Tax return allocation mechanisms add administrative complexity to an already Byzantine tax system. More fundamentally, the evidence from existing public financing programs, such as New York City's matching funds system and Arizona's clean elections program, suggests that voucher-like approaches can work but often struggle with two problems: insufficient participation rates and vulnerability to evasion through soft money channels that reform advocates underestimate. By 2011, we had already seen how disclosure requirements and contribution limits in existing systems could be circumvented through creative structuring by well-resourced donors. A voucher system would need robust enforcement mechanisms that Roemer does not fully address.

Roemer's own example of Representative Jim Leach is instructive but cuts in unexpected directions. Leach's independence stemmed from his refusal of PAC contributions, and Roemer holds this up as a model. But Leach's story also reveals a limitation: his was a voice crying in the wilderness. If the goal is a Congress that can consistently make sound decisions independent of special interests, relying on individual moral commitments by members leaves the system fundamentally unchanged. We need institutional structures that make independence the default rather than the exception. When only a handful of members take the PAC pledge, those who do are marginalized rather than empowered. The problem is structural, and structural problems require structural solutions.

There is also a tension in Roemer's treatment of the financial crisis. He correctly identifies Wall Street's lobbying power and the failure of regulatory reform, but his narrative focuses heavily on the Clinton administration's role in Glass-Steagall's repeal. This risks oversimplification. The deregulatory impulse was bipartisan and long-standing, building on decades of gradual erosion of regulatory boundaries. The Commodity Futures Modernization Act of 2000, which exempted over-the-counter derivatives from regulation, passed with broad support including from many who later criticized the results. Similarly, the Federal Reserve's emergency lending facilities that Roemer criticizes operated under authorities established long before 2008. Fixating on individual political actors misses how institutional incentives across multiple administrations created the conditions for crisis.

Roemer is surely right that money in politics constrains governance. But effective reform must be achievable within existing constitutional constraints while actually changing the incentive structure that breeds dependency. The $100 limit may be personally consistent with his principles, but as a national standard it would likely produce a system dominated by those with the resources to bundle small donations or find alternative channels for influence. Democracy vouchers offer more promise, but only if accompanied by enforcement mechanisms robust enough to prevent the creative circumvention we have observed in existing reform efforts. The diagnosis is sound; the cure needs more work.

**The Diagnosis Is Right, the Prescription Is Too Small**

Governor Roemer deserves credit for naming the disease so plainly: a Congress whose every waking calculation runs through the donor class is not a Congress capable of governing. His account of the Research and Experimentation tax credit—permanent in effect but temporary by design, precisely so it can be auctioned again and again—captures what political scientists have started calling the "extortion" model of fundraising. Members do not need to be bribed; they need only to keep the spigot open. On this, Roemer is exactly right, and his willingness to say it from inside the system, as a former Congressman, governor, and bank president, lends the indictment a weight that activists cannot supply.

But his cure is far too modest for the disease he diagnoses. A self-imposed $100 cap and a PAC-free pledge are fine personal ethics. They are not a system. A presidential campaign that raises money exclusively in $100 increments is a feel-good thought experiment; it does not bind the next candidate, and it does nothing about the actual river of money flowing through Super PACs and 501(c)(4)s in the wake of *Citizens United* and *SpeechNow*. Roemer himself acknowledges the 130 percent surge in independent expenditures in 2010. His four principles, taken seriously, leave that surge untouched.

Consider the "democracy voucher" idea he gestures toward—small public allocations that citizens direct to candidates who agree to accept only small donations. Bruce Ackerman and Ian Ayres proposed something like this nearly a decade ago in *Voting With Dollars*. Lawrence Lessig has been refining variants of it since. It is a serious idea, and I am for it. But Roemer treats it almost as an afterthought, sandwiched between general principles, when in fact it is the only structural element of his program that could conceivably scale. He should be running on the voucher, not on his personal donation limit.

Second, Roemer's insistence on working "within the boundaries given to us by the Court" is prudent but ultimately inadequate. *Citizens United* and *Buckley v. Valeo* together construct a constitutional architecture in which money is speech, corporations are speakers, and any aggregate limit on political spending is suspect. Within that architecture, the most reformers can do is expand the pool of small donors and hope to dilute the big ones. That is worth doing. But to pretend it will be sufficient is to misread the Court's trajectory. A constitutional amendment clarifying that Congress may regulate election spending is not a fringe position; it is the logical endpoint of the diagnosis Roemer has offered.

Third, Roemer's framing of the financial crisis as a story of corrupted regulation is powerful but incomplete. Yes, Wall Street bought deregulation. Yes, Glass-Steagall's repeal mattered. But the deeper problem is regulatory capture in the technical sense Stigler described forty years ago: agencies staffed by the regulated, revolving doors that turn faster than legislative cycles, and an epistemic monoculture in which Treasury, the Fed, and Goldman Sachs share the same alumni directory. Capping donations will not unwind that. We need post-employment restrictions with real teeth, a strengthened Office of Financial Research independent of the banks it studies, and—Roemer hints at this but does not push—a return to size and activity limits on systemically important institutions. The Dodd-Frank bill he rightly criticizes failed not only because Wall Street bought concessions but because Congress lacks the expert capacity to write the rules its lobbyists won't.

Fourth, a friendly worry about Roemer's tone. He writes as though the public, freed from the corrupting influence of money, would naturally converge on sensible governance. I doubt it. The Tea Party rallies and the union halls are both full of small donors, and they do not agree on what "good governance" looks like. Money is one corruption; tribal polarization is another, and the two interact in ways that small-donor democracy may exacerbate rather than soothe. The most prolific small donors in both parties tend to be the most ideologically extreme. A purer democracy of $100 checks could be a more polarized one. Reformers should think hard about this before promising that getting the money out will let reason back in.

None of this is to dismiss Roemer. A presidential candidate willing to make money in politics the issue—not a side issue, not a clause in a platform, but the issue—is genuinely valuable, especially given how thoroughly both parties have learned to monetize their offices. He is unlikely to win his party's nomination. He may not even make a debate stage. But the test of his campaign should not be electoral. The test is whether he forces his rivals, and the press, to take this question seriously. On that score, the speech is a start.

The diagnosis is right. The prescription needs more medicine.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Money Is a Symptom, Not the Disease**

Governor Roemer's speech belongs to a venerable American genre: the insider who, having served honorably, returns from Washington to tell us the system is rigged. From the Progressives through Common Cause through John McCain and now Buddy Roemer, the refrain is the same. The republic is sound; only the corrupting influence of money stands between us and good government. Strip the money out and statesmanship will flower.

I want to push back on this story, not because money in politics is harmless—it isn't—but because it has become a kind of all-purpose explanation that obscures more than it reveals.

Consider Roemer's centerpiece example: the Research and Experimentation tax credit. He treats its periodic reauthorization as proof of corruption. But there are other plausible reasons Congress prefers temporary tax provisions, including budget scoring rules that make permanent extensions look prohibitively expensive over ten-year windows and a legitimate desire to revisit whether incentives are still working. The same logic applies to the Bush tax cuts, which expire because of reconciliation rules, not because Republicans wanted donors to call them every two years. Reasonable people can disagree about whether the R&E credit should be permanent, but the fact that it isn't proves nothing about bribery, soft or hard.

Or take the financial crisis, which Roemer presents as the morality play of our time: Wall Street bought deregulation, Glass-Steagall fell, the house of cards collapsed. This is partly true and partly a comforting fable. The repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999 did not cause the 2008 crisis in any direct sense. Lehman Brothers and Bear Stearns were pure investment banks; the Glass-Steagall wall would not have protected them. Countrywide and Washington Mutual were thrifts. AIG was an insurer. The actual mechanism of crisis—subprime origination, securitization, ratings failures, and the shadow banking system's reliance on repo funding—operates largely outside the Glass-Steagall framework. Blaming campaign contributions for the crisis allows us to skip past the harder questions: Why did regulators believe their own models? Why did homeowners, borrowers, originators, ratings agencies, and investors all participate in a bubble that, in retrospect, looks obvious? "They were bought" is a more satisfying story than "we were all wrong," but it is not necessarily a more accurate one.

The deeper problem with Roemer's frame is that it cannot explain political outcomes that money cannot buy. The financial industry spent enormous sums opposing the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. It got the CFPB anyway. Big Pharma is a perennial top spender. The Medicare Part D program is generous to it, but the Affordable Care Act extracted substantial concessions too. The plaintiffs' bar has been generous to Democrats for decades and has watched tort reform advance at the state level repeatedly. If money were the master variable Roemer claims, these outcomes would be hard to explain.

What actually drives Congressional behavior is, to a much greater extent than reformers admit, ideology and constituency. The most polarized Congress in a century is not polarized because donors disagree—donors mostly want stability. It is polarized because the parties themselves have sorted ideologically, because the median Republican primary voter and the median Democratic primary voter live in different epistemic worlds, and because gerrymandering has insulated members from general-election accountability while exposing them to primary challenges from the flanks. None of this gets fixed by a $100 donation cap.

Proportional representation in some form could give third parties a path to power and break the stranglehold of the two major parties. Public financing of elections, if it were generous enough to make candidates genuinely independent, would be more effective than Roemer's small-dollar approach, though it would face serious constitutional obstacles.

None of these reforms is easy. But Roemer sells his readers short by suggesting that the solution to our political dysfunction is simply to get more people to give smaller amounts of money to campaigns. Money in politics is a symptom of deeper structural problems in our democracy, not their cause. Until we confront those deeper problems—the primary system, the two-party duopoly, the constitutional obstacles to effective campaign finance reform—we will continue to grovel.

Governor Roemer has written a characteristic polemic: confident in its diagnosis, vague in its prescription, and consistently wrong about the causes of the very problems it identifies. While I share Roemer's concern about the quality of American governance, I believe he has identified the wrong villain, misread our recent financial history, and proposed remedies that would do little to address the underlying dysfunction while doing significant harm to democratic deliberation.

Let us begin with the tax code. Roemer tells us that the Research and Experimentation tax credit remains temporary because Congress uses the tax code as a fundraising tool. This is a convenient story, but it does not survive scrutiny. Congress makes many tax provisions temporary, including those that benefit middle-class families, not because of campaign finance corruption but because temporary tax provisions are simply easier to pass politically. They can be marketed as "tax relief" rather than permanent structural changes. This is a flaw in our political rhetoric, not evidence of corruption. Meanwhile, the R&E credit has been renewed continuously for three decades. It has never actually expired. Calling it "temporary" is a political fiction, not a genuine problem.

The financial crisis narrative is similarly distorted. Roemer blames the repeal of Glass-Steagall for creating "too-big-to-fail" banks, but this misreads causation. The Bank of Credit and Commerce International collapsed in 1991, long before Glass-Steagall's repeal. The Long-Term Capital Management crisis of 1998, which required a Federal Reserve-organized bailout, occurred when Glass-Steagall was still in force. The "too-big-to-fail" problem is older than the Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act and cannot be blamed on that legislation alone. What Glass-Steagall's repeal did was allow commercial banks to engage in investment banking activities; what created the crisis was the interaction of that change with light regulation of mortgage-backed securities, the implicit guarantee of Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, and the Federal Reserve's low interest rate policies in the early 2000s.

Roemer acknowledges that "it was a slew of insanely risky financial innovations" that delivered mortgages Americans couldn't afford. He is right. But then he immediately pivots to blaming campaign contributions. The connection is asserted, not demonstrated. Many countries with far stricter campaign finance laws experienced housing bubbles and financial crises. Iceland, Ireland, Spain—all had more regulated financial sectors and stricter limits on political contributions than the United States. The crisis was not primarily a product of regulatory failure caused by campaign contributions; it was a product of genuine intellectual errors about financial risk that infected regulators, academics, and market participants across the developed world.

This matters because Roemer's diagnosis implies a cure: remove the influence of money, and good policy will follow. But policy failure is not primarily a product of corruption; it is a product of complexity, uncertainty, and disagreement about values. The R&E credit is complicated and imperfect; its proper scope is genuinely contested. Financial regulation involves trade-offs between stability, efficiency, and innovation that reasonable people resolve differently. We do not need a Congress free of influence; we need a Congress that makes wise decisions under conditions of uncertainty, which is a harder problem than Roemer acknowledges.

The remedies Roemer proposes raise their own difficulties. His "democracy voucher" system would give every taxpayer a small sum to contribute to candidates of their choice. But this creates compelled political subsidies—taxpayers would fund political speech they may find objectionable. Roemer waves this away by noting that the funds come from "the voters contributed personally," but this is sophistry. The money comes from the general treasury, and the government determines the allocation. His fourth principle—that "any system must encourage individuals to give at least small amounts of their own money"—is simply wrong as a matter of democratic theory. There is nothing virtuous about forcing citizens to contribute to political campaigns. The history of compelled political contributions is not a proud one.

Roemer has confused two distinct problems: the quality of democratic deliberation and the influence of money in politics. These problems are related, but they are not the same, and solving one does not necessarily solve the other. A Congress free of financial influence could still make terrible decisions if its members were poorly informed, ideologically rigid, or simply confused about the consequences of their choices. Money is not the root of all political evil. Sometimes it is merely money.

Governor Roemer has identified a genuine crisis in American governance, but his essay exemplifies a tendency he criticizes in others: the search for a single, simple explanation for complex problems. In Roemer's telling, money in politics is the answer to every question. Health care reform? Blocked by money. Financial regulation? Subverted by money. The debt ceiling? Money. Climate change? Money. The litany becomes a catechism, and like all catechisms, it blinds as much as it illuminates.

The deeper problem is that Roemer conflates different phenomena under the heading of "money in politics" and assumes they share a common cause. The R&E tax credit being made temporary is one kind of problem. The too-big-to-fail banking system is another. The failure of Dodd-Frank to break up large banks is a third. Each has a distinct history, distinct actors, and distinct solutions. Roemer's monocausal explanation flattens this diversity into a single narrative in which campaign contributions are always and everywhere the decisive factor.

Consider the Dodd-Frank Act, which Roemer dismisses in a sentence as having failed to address too-big-to-fail. This understates what the legislation actually accomplished and why it fell short. Dodd-Frank created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, which has actually done significant work in protecting consumers from predatory financial practices. It established orderly liquidation authority for large financial institutions, creating a legal mechanism for winding down failing banks without the ad hoc bailouts that characterized the 2008 crisis. It imposed new capital requirements and stress tests that have made the banking system more resilient. These measures are imperfect and incomplete, but they represent genuine reform.

The reason Dodd-Frank did not break up the large banks is not simply that Wall Street lobbied against it, though it did. It is that breaking up large banks is genuinely difficult and its benefits are uncertain. The largest banks provide services that depend on their scale: global payment systems, complex derivatives, international lending. Breaking them up might reduce systemic risk, or it might simply create many smaller institutions that fail in a cascade. The economic analysis was genuinely contested, and Congress responded to that contestation, not merely to campaign contributions. Blaming the failure on money misses the genuinely hard policy questions involved.

Roemer is on stronger ground when discussing the filibuster, though he does not mention it by name. He is correct that Congress cannot function effectively when a minority can block all legislation, and he is correct that this dysfunction is related to the incentives created by our campaign finance system. But the relationship is more complicated than he suggests. Incumbents benefit from the filibuster because it allows them to prevent action on issues that might hurt them politically. The current campaign finance system benefits incumbents because they have established fundraising networks that challengers cannot easily replicate. These facts are related—they both reflect the structural advantages of incumbency—but they are not the same problem, and they do not have the same solution.

What would actually restore Congressional capacity? First, structural reforms to reduce the advantages of incumbency: nonpartisan redistricting, easier ballot access for independent candidates, limits on the advantages of franking privileges and official resources. Second, genuine campaign finance reform that does not merely substitute one form of money for another but reduces the overall importance of money in political competition. This might include term limits, which Roemer does not mention, or structural changes to the primary system. Third, and most importantly, a change in political culture that rewards bipartisan problem-solving rather than partisan warfare. This last item cannot be legislated, but it can be encouraged by structural changes that make bipartisanship less costly for individual members.

Roemer ends his essay by noting that money interests laugh when they hear talk of reform. He is probably right. But they laugh because they understand something Roemer does not: the system works for them not because of any single policy choice but because the entire structure of American politics rewards the concentration of power and wealth. Changing that structure will require more than small-dollar campaign finance reform. It will require rethinking the fundamental design of our political system, from primary elections to filibuster rules to the relationship between money and speech. Roemer has pointed at a real problem. He has not, however, identified its solution.

Governor Roemer tells a compelling story about the corruption of American democracy by special-interest money, and much of what he says is true. But his essay suffers from a limitation that afflicts much contemporary political commentary: it focuses entirely on Congress while ignoring the broader constitutional architecture that makes Congressional dysfunction so damaging. If Congress cannot lead, perhaps the solution is not to reform Congress but to find alternatives to Congressional leadership. In the American system, those alternatives exist, and they have been gaining strength throughout our history.

The framers designed a system of separated powers precisely to prevent any single institution from dominating American governance. When Congress fails to act, as it frequently does, the executive branch has tools to address national problems. The EPA can regulate pollutants without Congressional authorization. The Federal Reserve can conduct monetary policy. The Department of Justice can pursue enforcement actions. These are not ideal substitutes for comprehensive Congressional action, but they are not nothing. The question is not whether Congress can lead—it frequently cannot—but whether American governance can proceed through other channels when Congress fails.

Consider the financial crisis that Roemer discusses. Much of the response to that crisis came not from Congress but from the Federal Reserve and the Treasury Department. The $700 billion Troubled Asset Relief Program required Congressional authorization, but the Federal Reserve's emergency lending programs did not. The Fed created trillions of dollars in new money through mechanisms that were never explicitly authorized by Congress and that remain controversial to this day. This was executive branch action, not Congressional leadership. The Dodd-Frank Act, which Roemer criticizes, was the most significant Congressional response, but it was shaped primarily by the executive branch's agenda and constrained by the Fed's and Treasury's preference for gradual reform.

Roemer laments that Congress cannot "talk honestly and openly and seriously about the complex issues of our time." He is right. But does this matter as much as he thinks? Executive branch officials do not face the same electoral incentives that distort Congressional deliberation. The Fed Chair does not need to raise campaign funds. Treasury officials serve at the pleasure of the President and can be removed if their policies are too controversial. This creates its own pathologies—executive branch officials can be too responsive to political pressure from the White House—but it also creates space for technocratic deliberation that Congressional politics forecloses.

The deeper question is whether our constitutional system can function effectively in the twenty-first century. The framers designed a system for a nation of three million people without mass media, without a professional bureaucracy, and without the complex financial system that now characterizes the global economy. The system has adapted remarkably well to these changes, but the adaptation has been uneven. Congress, with its district-based elections, its committee structure, and its internal rules designed for an earlier era, has struggled most.

The solution may not be to reform campaign finance but to accept that Congress is no longer the primary locus of American governance and to structure our institutions accordingly. This means strengthening executive branch capacity, independence, and accountability. It means creating more technocratic agencies modeled on the Federal Reserve that can act quickly and coherently. It means accepting that many decisions are too complex for the legislative process and delegating them to expert agencies. It means, in short, rethinking the balance between democracy and expertise in American governance.

Roemer wants a Congress that can lead. I am not sure such a Congress is possible in our current system, and I am not sure it is desirable. A Congress that could act decisively would also be a Congress that could act recklessly, without the delays and compromises that slow legislation and allow for deliberation. The Tea Party movement, which Roemer mentions approvingly, demonstrates both the appeal of populist Congressional assertiveness and its dangers. The solution to Congressional dysfunction is not necessarily a more powerful Congress; it may be a less powerful one, acting only when consensus is truly broad and delegating the rest to institutions better suited to complex decision-making.

This is not a comfortable conclusion. The framers believed in Congressional supremacy, and many Americans still do. But good governance requires adapting institutions to circumstances, not clinging to forms that no longer serve their original purpose. Money in politics has corrupted Congress. Perhaps the answer is not to uncorrupt it but to build other institutions that can do what Congress no longer can.
