When a Generation Becomes Less Tolerant of Free Speech

Free speech and the willingness to discuss uncomfortable ideas is under threat on college campuses where “trigger warnings” and “safe spaces” are becoming increasingly common. Even President Obama has raised concerns about university students being “coddled and protected from different points of view.” And recently, a provocative essay in a Wesleyan University student newspaper nearly led to the demise of the 150-year-old publication.

What does this say about this generation of young people and what they will become as future leaders of this country?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The premise of the question deserves a little scrutiny before we draw conclusions about an entire generation. Yes, there have been notable flashpoints recently — the Wesleyan Argus controversy over a column critical of Black Lives Matter, the disinvitations of speakers like Condoleezza Rice at Rutgers and Ayaan Hirsi Ali at Brandeis, the spread of trigger warnings, the campaign at Mizzou. Greg Lukianoff and Jonathan Haidt argued in The Atlantic two months ago that "vindictive protectiveness" is reshaping campus discourse. But Pew's polling this year also showed that 40 percent of millennials think government should be able to prevent speech offensive to minorities — a higher share than older cohorts, but still a minority. We are talking about a tendency, not a transformation.

Even granting the trend, the framing of "intolerance" obscures what is actually happening. Today's students did not invent the impulse to police speech. The campus speech codes of the 1980s and 1990s, the Hays Code, McCarthyism, and the long tradition of community pressure against unpopular ideas all predate them. What is distinctive now is who is doing the pressuring — students themselves, often from the left — and the therapeutic vocabulary they use. The shift from "this speech is dangerous to the republic" to "this speech is harmful to me" reflects a real change in moral grammar, one rooted in the broader expansion of concepts like trauma and microaggression.

That deserves criticism, and Obama was right to offer it. A university that cannot tolerate Christina Hoff Sommers or a sophomoric op-ed is failing at its core function. Students who graduate believing argument is a form of violence will struggle in workplaces, courtrooms, and legislatures where it isn't.

But the leap from "some loud undergraduates" to "future leaders of this country" is too fast. Most twenty-year-olds in any era hold opinions they will revise. The more honest worry is whether faculty and administrators — the adults — have the nerve to defend unfettered inquiry when students demand otherwise. The generation will be shaped by that answer.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The uproar at Wesleyan and the broader campus conversation about trigger warnings and safe spaces has been cast as a generational retreat from the First Amendment. But to frame it simply as “intolerance” misses a deeper, more familiar story. Today’s students are not rejecting free speech; they are contesting its boundaries and demanding that it be paired with a responsibility to avoid foreseeable harm—a tension that has defined every generation’s free-speech fights.

The current flashpoints echo earlier battles. In the 1990s, campus speech codes were the alleged threat to liberty, yet that generation went on to lead a vibrant democracy. Before that, civil rights activists were accused of being too sensitive when they challenged racist rhetoric as harmful. What looks like coddling is often a moral awakening to the injuries that language can inflict on marginalized people. When a student requests a trigger warning before a graphic depiction of sexual assault, she is not silencing the professor; she is asking for the cognitive space to engage without being ambushed by trauma. That is a strategy for inclusion, not censorship.

Of course, some student activism has veered into illiberal territory. Attempts to defund the Wesleyan student newspaper for publishing a provocative essay crossed a line from protest to prior restraint. Yet it is a mistake to extrapolate from the angriest voices on a few campuses to an entire generation. Most young people are navigating a difficult balancing act: they online, where unmoderated hate speech is rampant, while also learning in diverse classrooms that their words have consequences. They are experimenting with norms that make rigorous debate possible for those who historically had the most to fear from “uncomfortable ideas.” As future leaders, they will not abandon free expression; they will—like every generation before them—renegotiate its limits based on the knowledge that speech can both liberate and wound. That negotiation, however messy, is a sign of democratic vitality, not its collapse.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

The current panic over a "coddled" generation of college students often misses the underlying motivation behind the push for safe spaces and trigger warnings. When Wesleyan University students petitioned to defund their campus newspaper over a provocative op-ed, critics understandably saw a generational abandonment of the First Amendment. However, to understand what this means for our future leaders, we must recognize that these students are not acting out of malice, but out of a deeply felt—if sometimes clumsy—commitment to inclusivity.

Today’s undergraduates are part of the most diverse generation in American history. For many of them, the drive to police offensive speech stems from an acute awareness of historical marginalization. Trigger warnings and safe spaces initially emerged to protect students who had experienced genuine trauma, serving as tools of empathy in environments that have not always been welcoming to minorities, women, or LGBT individuals. In this light, the impulse to shield peers from deeply offensive rhetoric suggests that our future leaders will be highly attuned to equity and psychological well-being.

Yet, the danger lies in conflating emotional comfort with intellectual safety. When the desire to protect marginalized voices morphs into the censorship of unpopular ideas, it undermines the foundational purpose of a university. The Wesleyan incident is troubling not because students protested an article they found offensive—protest is a hallmark of free expression—but because they sought to dismantle the platform that published it. If students graduate believing that institutional power should be used to silence uncomfortable viewpoints, they will be ill-equipped to navigate the messy realities of democratic governance.

As these young people transition into leadership roles, their profound empathy will undoubtedly be an asset to the country. But if they cannot separate ideological disagreement from personal harm, their effectiveness will be severely limited. A functioning pluralistic society requires the resilience to confront hostile ideas with better arguments, rather than shutting down the forum entirely. Our task is not to dismiss this generation as overly fragile, but to guide them in reconciling their pursuit of justice with the indispensable friction of free and open debate.

It is too easy to turn a few campus controversies into a sweeping indictment of an entire generation. Today’s students are not uniquely fragile, nor are older Americans consistently brave defenders of dissent. Every era has its orthodoxies. What is distinctive now is that students have grown up amid social media, economic insecurity and a sharper public vocabulary for racism, sexual violence and mental illness. They are often less willing than their predecessors to treat speech as an abstract exercise detached from power and harm.

That instinct has real moral force. Universities have too often told marginalized students to endure humiliation in the name of “debate” while doing little to ensure that they were full participants in campus life. A request for a trigger warning is not always censorship; it can be a modest notice that allows a veteran or assault survivor to prepare for difficult material. A “safe space” can mean a necessary refuge in an institution where some students still feel isolated.

But the same impulse becomes dangerous when it tries to punish, defund or silence views rather than answer them. The Wesleyan newspaper episode is troubling precisely because student journalism exists to test arguments in public. A bad essay should be met with better essays, reporting, protest and criticism—not with efforts that make editors fear publishing controversial opinions. If students learn that the proper response to offensive speech is institutional suppression, they will carry a thin-skinned politics into workplaces, media, government and civic life.

The answer is not to sneer at students, but to teach liberal habits more deliberately. Colleges should protect protest and protect speakers; support vulnerable students and insist that discomfort is part of education. Future leaders will need empathy, but also resilience. A democratic culture depends on both.

The debate about free speech on college campuses raises legitimate concerns but risks oversimplifying complex generational and cultural shifts. While trigger warnings and safe spaces have become more common, characterizing an entire generation as intolerant of free speech misreads both the phenomenon and the young people it purports to describe.

Trigger warnings, at their most basic, represent an attempt by individuals to control their exposure to potentially distressing content—an exercise of autonomy rather than an attack on speech itself. Most students encountering trigger warnings are not demanding that the triggering content be banned, but simply requesting advance notice so they can make informed choices about their engagement. This is not fundamentally different from content warnings that have long appeared in educational settings.

Safe spaces, similarly, often reflect students creating voluntary communities with shared norms rather than demanding institutional censorship. The line between choosing one's associations and demanding conformity is blurry. That young people seek spaces of relative comfort among peers is hardly novel—what might be new is the explicit articulation of that desire in these terms.

The more troubling incidents—efforts to disinvite speakers, calls for professor discipline over controversial ideas—do deserve scrutiny. But these tend to be the exceptions that generate headlines rather than the rule. Such behaviors reflect a particular political moment where intensity on both left and right sometimes overwhelms norms of civil discourse. This is not exclusively a youth problem.

What does seem true is that this generation approaches questions of speech, harm, and community differently than its predecessors. Having come of age in a world of social media and heightened awareness of systemic inequality, many young people genuinely believe that some speech causes concrete harm and that communities have legitimate interests in setting norms. Whether one agrees with that view or not, it emerges from a coherent moral framework rather than simple intolerance.

The Wesleyan incident, rather than epitomizing suppression, actually demonstrated that student journalists could rally in defense of editorial independence. That response received far less attention than the initial controversy, but it suggests institutional cultures remain capable of protecting expressive freedom even under pressure.

If this generation has different attitudes toward speech boundaries, perhaps the appropriate response is sustained engagement rather than alarm. These young people will indeed become future leaders, and they will learn that democratic society requires negotiating disagreement. The skills they develop navigating these tensions—argumentation, empathy, the willingness to revise positions—may serve them well. College has always been a place where discomfort with ideas can be transformed into deeper engagement with them.

The hand-wringing over campus "coddling" has become a genre unto itself, and like most genres it deserves some skepticism. Yes, the Wesleyan Argus episode was ugly, and yes, some demands for "safe spaces" shade into demands for ideological conformity. But before we extrapolate from a handful of incidents to a generational indictment, we ought to ask whether the data actually support the panic.

The General Social Survey, which has tracked attitudes toward controversial speakers for decades, consistently shows that younger Americans are more tolerant of letting communists, atheists, militarists and homosexuals speak in public than their parents and grandparents were at the same age. Millennials are not, by historical standards, a censorious cohort. They are, however, the first cohort to argue out their disagreements on Twitter, where every undergraduate's worst tweet is preserved and amplified for a national audience hungry for proof of decline.

What has actually changed is not tolerance but salience. Universities have admitted more women, more students of color, more first-generation students, more openly LGBT students than ever before. These students arrive with grievances their predecessors did not have the standing to voice, and they voice them. Sometimes clumsily, sometimes illiberally, occasionally with real menace toward dissenting professors. But the alternative to their voicing those grievances was not a golden age of Socratic dialogue; it was their absence from campus altogether.

President Obama's comments were measured and correct: students should not be protected from ideas that make them uncomfortable. But the same president has been pilloried by the same students for deportations, drone strikes and Wall Street coziness. They are hardly a generation that shrinks from confrontation with power. What they shrink from, sometimes, is confrontation with each other — and that is a problem internal to youth, not unique to this one.

The honest worry is narrower than the cultural-decline narrative suggests. It is that a small set of elite campuses has developed local norms — disinvitations, formal speech codes, administrative bloat eager to adjudicate hurt feelings — that train future lawyers and journalists in habits of mind ill-suited to a pluralistic democracy. That is worth criticizing. But criticizing it requires distinguishing the genuine illiberalism of certain administrative regimes from the ordinary, healthy noise of young people learning to argue. Conflating the two flatters our nostalgia and tells us very little about what these students will actually become.

There is a real problem on campus, and pretending otherwise is its own kind of denial. When students at Wesleyan move to defund their newspaper because a single op-ed criticized Black Lives Matter, when Brandeis rescinds an honorary degree to Ayaan Hirsi Ali, when faculty at Yale are shouted down for suggesting that adults can choose their own Halloween costumes — these are not isolated absurdities. They are symptoms of an emerging campus culture that treats disagreement as harm and harm as something authorities must prevent.

The trigger warning, in its origin, was a reasonable accommodation for trauma survivors. But on many campuses it has metastasized into a presumption: that students have a right to be insulated from any material that unsettles them, and that professors who fail to provide such insulation are committing a kind of assault. "Safe space," likewise, began as a refuge and has become, in too many instances, a veto.

What is most striking is not that young people hold strong views — they always have — but that they increasingly frame disagreement as a threat to their wellbeing. This is a category mistake with serious consequences. A democracy depends on citizens who can hear arguments they find odious without claiming injury. A liberal education depends on professors who can assign Ovid, Twain and Nabokov without being reported to a Title IX office. A free press depends on student journalists who are not driven from their newsrooms for printing the wrong opinion.

The generational question the prompt asks is the right one. These students will, in twenty years, be running newsrooms, courtrooms, HR departments and government agencies. If they carry with them the conviction that uncomfortable speech is violence and that institutional power should be deployed to suppress it, the consequences will not stay on campus. We are already seeing previews in the corporate world, where employees are fired for ill-judged tweets and executives apologize preemptively for offenses no one has yet taken.

None of this means today's students are villains. Most are decent, anxious young people responding to genuine incentives — universities that market themselves as therapeutic communities, social media that punishes nuance, administrators eager to manage risk. But responding to bad incentives produces bad habits, and bad habits, once institutionalized, are hard to dislodge. We should worry, and we should say so plainly.

Every generation of elders has discovered, to its horror, that the young are doing democracy wrong. In the 1950s, students were too conformist. In the 1960s, too radical. In the 1980s, too greedy. Now, apparently, too sensitive. The diagnosis changes; the diagnostician's certainty does not.

I am not persuaded that "this generation" is uniquely intolerant of free speech. I am persuaded that the technological and institutional environment in which they argue has changed dramatically, and that we are mistaking the new acoustics of disagreement for a new pathology of mind.

Consider Wesleyan. A student wrote a critical op-ed about Black Lives Matter. Other students objected. A petition circulated. The newspaper's funding was debated. Eventually, after much public scrutiny, the paper survived in modified form. In an earlier era, the same dispute would have unfolded over a few weeks in letters to the editor and dining-hall arguments and would have left no national trace. Now it becomes a case study in The New York Times. The dispute is not bigger; the audience is.

Consider trigger warnings. Most professors who use them treat them as a courtesy, like noting that a film contains graphic violence. The fraction of cases in which they are used to suppress assigned material is small and largely anecdotal. Yet the anecdote travels because it confirms a story older readers want to believe about younger ones.

I will grant the critics this much: there is a strain of campus activism that genuinely confuses speech with violence and disagreement with injury, and it deserves intellectual pushback. But the right response is argument, not the moral panic currently on offer. When we lump trigger warnings, safe spaces, disinvitations, microaggression complaints and one student newspaper controversy into a single generational portrait, we are not analyzing; we are venting.

What will these students become as future leaders? Probably much like prior generations: a mix of the principled and the opportunistic, the rigid and the flexible, shaped more by the institutions they enter than by the slogans they chanted at twenty. The 1960s radicals became, variously, senators and stockbrokers and schoolteachers. Today's allegedly fragile undergraduates will, I suspect, turn out to be more durable than their critics assume — and more willing to defend speech they care about than the current discourse credits them for.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The free-speech debate on campus is not really about free speech. It is about who gets to define the terms of legitimate disagreement, and that is a question about power.

For most of American higher education's history, certain views were simply unthinkable in the classroom: that slavery was a national crime, that women belonged in the professions, that homosexuality was not a pathology, that the canon might be parochial. The exclusion of these views was not enforced by trigger warnings or safe spaces. It was enforced by the quieter mechanisms of who got hired, who got published, who got admitted, and what counted as a serious question. We did not call that censorship. We called it standards.

Now the demographic composition of the university has changed, and with it the range of views that students are willing to press. Some of those pressing tactics are admirable; some are illiberal; many are both. But the framing in which a relatively powerless 19-year-old demanding a content warning constitutes a "threat to free speech" — while a trustee quietly killing a program, or a donor pulling funds from a controversial center, does not — tells us more about whose speech we are accustomed to protecting than about any new generational vice.

The Wesleyan episode is instructive precisely because it cuts against the easy narrative. The students did not silence the op-ed; it was published. They responded with their own speech: petitions, debates, demands. The newspaper was not destroyed. The system, in other words, worked roughly the way a system of contesting speech is supposed to work, and the contest produced more discourse, not less.

That does not mean every campus controversy is benign. Shouting down a speaker is not argument, and demanding that professors be disciplined for assigned readings is genuinely corrosive. Students who do these things deserve to be told so, by their professors, their peers and the press. But the conversation should be conducted with some humility about how recent — and how partial — our supposed campus tradition of open inquiry actually is.

If these students become tomorrow's leaders, they will be leaders trained to notice whose voices have historically been excluded and to ask whether current arrangements perpetuate that exclusion. That is not the death of liberal democracy. It is, with all its excesses and missteps, one of the things liberal democracy was supposed to produce.

Watch carefully and you will see two different anxieties bundled together in the current campus speech debate, and they need to be unbundled before we can think clearly.

The first anxiety is psychological. It concerns the apparent fragility of young people: the rising rates of reported anxiety and depression on campus, the demand for therapeutic accommodations, the language of "harm" applied to ideas as well as actions. This is a real phenomenon and it has roots that go far beyond the university — overscheduled childhoods, the smartphone, helicopter parenting, the long shadow of the 2008 recession. It deserves serious attention from clinicians and sociologists, not just op-ed writers.

The second anxiety is political. It concerns the willingness of some campus activists to use institutional power — student government, Title IX offices, social-media campaigns — to penalize speech they find objectionable. This is a question about norms of discourse and the proper limits of administrative authority, and it has roots in the proliferation of university bureaucracies as much as in student temperament.

The two anxieties overlap but they are not the same. A student who asks her professor for a trigger warning before a discussion of sexual assault is not necessarily the same student who demands the firing of a colleague for an unfortunate email. The first is, at worst, asking for a courtesy. The second is participating in something more troubling. Treating them as one phenomenon — call it Coddled Youth — flattens distinctions that matter.

What does this say about the generation? Honestly, I think it says less than the punditry suggests. Surveys of young people consistently find them more supportive than older cohorts of letting unpopular speakers be heard. Surveys also find them more anxious, more medicated and more screen-bound. Both things are true. The question is which tendency will dominate as they age into institutional power.

My guess — and it is only a guess — is that the political habits acquired in college will fade as graduates enter workplaces where peers do not share their priors and where firing a colleague for an op-ed has real costs. The psychological tendencies will be harder to shed and will reshape institutions in subtler ways: more HR, more wellness, more cautious communication. Whether that is a tragedy or merely a change is a question I am not prepared to answer yet.

We should be honest about what is being mourned. The college campus of the older critic's imagination — a marketplace of ideas where any view, however heterodox, could be advanced and tested — never quite existed. It was always shaped by the prejudices of those in charge, the donors they courted, the trustees they answered to. The current generation has not invented intolerance on campus. They have, however, changed who is asked to tolerate whom, and the change is unsettling to people who liked the old arrangement.

That said, I will not pretend everything is fine. Some of what passes for activism in elite universities right now is genuinely illiberal. Listing trigger warnings on Ovid is, at best, faintly ridiculous. These habits, if carried into adult life, will produce timid editors, litigious managers and politicians who confuse offense with injury.

But here is the harder truth. The most powerful threats to free speech in America today do not come from college sophomores. They come from state legislatures passing laws to restrict what can be taught about racism in public schools, from billionaires using libel suits to bankrupt unfriendly publications, from police departments arresting journalists at protests, from a surveillance state that monitors activists' communications. If we are worried about future leaders being friendly to free expression, we might worry less about whether they request content warnings and more about whether they will resist subpoenas, fund investigative reporting and defend whistleblowers.

The generation now in college has grown up in a period of unusual transparency about American failures: the Iraq war, the financial crisis, Ferguson, Snowden. They are skeptical of institutions partly because the institutions have earned the skepticism. The same skepticism that makes them suspicious of a provocative op-ed also makes them suspicious of state power, corporate power and the press's complicity with both. That skepticism is, on balance, a civic virtue, even when it leads them into errors that older citizens did not commit at twenty.

What will they become? Probably leaders who define the borders of legitimate speech differently than we do — sometimes worse, sometimes better. The same has been true of every generation that ever took over.

The moral panic over “coddled” students fundamentally misunderstands what free speech has always required: an audience that can actually listen. Trigger warnings and safe spaces are not rejections of uncomfortable ideas; they are mechanisms that allow marginalized students — those who've historically been told their pain doesn't matter — to stay in the room long enough to engage. When a veteran with PTSD is warned that a class discussion will include graphic violence, we don't accuse him of being intolerant. We recognize that a brief notice lets him prepare psychologically, making genuine debate possible. So why, when women of color ask for the same consideration before a seminar on sexual assault, are they suddenly threats to the First Amendment?

The Wesleyan student newspaper controversy is instructive, not because a provocative essay nearly killed a publication, but because it revealed that many students wanted to *debate* the ideas, not silence them. Yes, some called for defunding — and they were wrong. But framing the entire generation as anti-speech because a few voices overcorrect ignores the hundreds of students who wrote letters, hosted forums, and wrestled publicly with views they found abhorrent. That is the very definition of intellectual engagement, the kind of messy, passionate discourse that a genuinely free culture produces.

What critics miss is that speech doesn't exist in a vacuum. For decades, marginalized groups were expected to bear the psychic costs of “robust debate” while their own voices were systematically excluded. Safe spaces and trigger warnings are an attempt to level that playing field — to say that if you want to argue about race, gender, or trauma, you must acknowledge the humanity of those most affected. This isn't coddling; it’s a more mature, honest form of free expression that refuses to pretend we all start from the same emotional or social position. The future leaders emerging from this generation are not fragile — they are teaching the country that free speech without empathy is just another form of tyranny. And that, perhaps, is exactly what makes their elders so uncomfortable.

Stop blaming the students. The real driver of campus illiberalism is the administrative machinery that has metastasized over the last two decades. When Wesleyan students called for defunding the *Argus*, the pushback and the ultimate preservation of the paper were led, in large part, by other students. The same is true across the country: surveys consistently show that while a vocal minority of undergraduates endorse speech restrictions, the majority still supports open debate. It is the adults in the room — the deans, the diversity bureaucrats, the risk-averse presidents — who translate student discomfort into rigid policies, speech zones, and speaker disinvitations.

Consider the origin of trigger warnings. They began as grassroots, informal tools used by feminist bloggers to alert readers to potentially distressing content. Faculty who voluntarily adopted them saw them as pedagogically useful. But the proliferation of mandatory, administrative-level warning policies is a different phenomenon entirely. It is driven by a confluence of legal liability, marketing imperatives, and the expansion of student services offices that need to justify their budgets. A student's offhand complaint about a class discussion becomes a Bias Incident Report, which feeds a data dashboard, which spawns a new mandatory training module. The student never asked for a speech code; the system provided one.

This distinction matters enormously when we project into the future. The generation currently in college is not being trained to censor by their peers. They are being conditioned by institutional structures that teach them that speech is a hazard to be managed, not a skill to be practiced. When these young people become leaders, they won't necessarily bring the illiberal instincts of the campus left. They will more likely bring the corporate and bureaucratic habits of the institutions that shaped them: the instinct to mitigate reputational risk, to quiet dissent through process, to treat all controversy as a PR crisis.

If we are serious about preserving free speech, the target of our criticism should be the administrators, trustees, and legislators who turn the fleeting furies of adolescence into permanent, risk-averse architecture. The students are often more resilient than the systems built in their name.
