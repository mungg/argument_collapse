Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Heckman's case for early childhood investment is powerful and, in its broad outlines, persuasive. The evidence from Perry, Abecedarian, and the Nurse-Family Partnership is genuinely impressive, and his insistence on non-cognitive skills corrects a real distortion in current education policy, which—under No Child Left Behind—has fetishized measurable test scores at the expense of the persistence, self-control, and sociability that we know matter for adult life. But I want to press on three points where I think the essay overreaches or papers over genuine difficulties.

First, the scaling problem. Perry enrolled 58 children; Abecedarian, 111. Both were boutique programs run by deeply committed researchers in the 1960s and 70s, with intensive home visits and unusually low child-to-teacher ratios. The leap from these demonstrations to the claim that "policy can matter" at a national scale is enormous. The closest thing we have to a scaled-up federal early childhood program is Head Start, and the picture there is sobering. The 2010 Head Start Impact Study, a randomized evaluation of roughly 5,000 children, found that initial cognitive and socio-emotional gains had largely faded by the end of first grade. Heckman cites Perry's IQ fadeout and reassures us that non-cognitive benefits persist, but the Head Start results raise the question of whether even those subtler effects survive when programs are run by ordinary administrators serving ordinary populations. A serious case for predistribution has to grapple with implementation, not just demonstration.

Second, the relationship between money and parenting is more entangled than Heckman allows. He repeatedly insists that "the scarce resource is love and parenting—not money," and he uses the Eastern Cherokee casino study to argue that income matters only through its effect on parenting behavior. But that study cuts both ways: parenting did improve when families received cash, which suggests that material resources and parenting quality are not cleanly separable. Greg Duncan and others have shown that exogenous income changes affect child outcomes in ways that look causal. To set "predistribution" against "redistribution" as though they were rivals is a false choice. Stressed, sleep-deprived, food-insecure parents working two jobs are not failing at love; they are failing at time. The EITC and the Child Tax Credit are arguably among the more effective "predistributional" tools we have, precisely because they relax the constraints under which parenting happens.

Third, Heckman's invocation of cultural sensitivity and the "sanctity of early family life" deserves more weight than he gives it. His own framing—that the "real determinants" of disadvantage are parenting quality, measured by things like words-per-hour spoken to a toddler—edges close to pathologizing poor and minority families. The Hart and Risley study, with its 42 families, has become a workhorse for this line of argument, but it is a slim empirical reed for a sweeping claim about what constitutes adequate parenting across class and culture. When Heckman writes that some program-induced changes "may run counter to the values of certain parents," he is acknowledging, almost in passing, a deep political problem: programs targeted at the parenting of the poor will be experienced, and resisted, as intrusions. The history of such interventions—from mid-century child welfare practices to contemporary disputes over home visiting—shows how quickly "helping kids" curdles into surveillance.

None of this undermines Heckman's central insight: early environments matter enormously, and the social returns to investing in them are likely high. But the policy implication is not "predistribution instead of redistribution." It is both, plus humility about what large-scale public programs can replicate from small, well-run experiments, plus seriousness about the political legitimacy of intervening in family life. Heckman has identified the target. He has not yet shown us a credible weapon.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

James Heckman’s diagnosis of America’s skills divide is powerful in many respects. He is right that the accident of birth now predicts life outcomes with alarming force, that non-cognitive skills matter at least as much as test scores, and that gaps in both kinds of abilities appear distressingly early. Yet his prescription, while wrapped in the language of rigorous evidence, gives too little weight to the most direct way that families shape those early environments: through their material resources. In downplaying income, the essay risks constructing a policy agenda that is both incomplete and unnecessarily modest.

The claim that “giving money to poor families does not, by itself, promote social mobility across generations” is stated as a hard lesson from the War on Poverty. But it is belied by a growing body of experimental and quasi-experimental evidence that was already well established by 2012. Several recent studies exploiting the Earned Income Tax Credit, for instance, show that increases in family income produce measurable improvements in children’s cognitive test scores and eventually in school achievement. Dahl and Lochner, in a 2012 paper, demonstrated that a $1,000 increase in income from the EITC raised combined math and reading scores by roughly 6 percent of a standard deviation, with effects concentrated among the most disadvantaged. Other work links the EITC to improved infant health and reductions in maternal stress. These findings matter because they isolate income’s role from the “quality of parenting” that Heckman rightly emphasizes. The most plausible pathway, supported by research on maternal depression and parenting stress, is that extra income gives parents the psychological and logistical bandwidth to provide the nurturing, stimulating environments Heckman champions. Income is not a rival to parenting quality; it is one of its key determinants.

Heckman’s celebration of small-scale experiments such as Perry Preschool and Abecedarian also prompts caution. Those programs were intensive, expensive, and run by committed researchers under artificial fidelity. The per-child cost of Abecedarian has been estimated at roughly $14,000 per year in 2012 dollars—far above what any scalable public program is likely to sustain. When early childhood education is taken to scale in the real world, as with Head Start, the results have been far more modest. The rigorous Head Start Impact Study, which released its third-grade follow-up in 2010, found that initial cognitive gains mostly faded out by the end of first grade, and few differences persisted on non-cognitive measures either. While Heckman would rightly note that Head Start varies greatly in quality, that is precisely the point: translating small-scale triumphs into a national system that consistently delivers high-quality interactions is a profound implementation challenge. The essay’s own call for a “menu of high quality programs from which parents can choose” acknowledges this difficulty without fully confronting it.

A more balanced approach would treat early intervention and income support as complements, not substitutes. The potent logic that “skill begets skill” cuts both ways: early environments are shaped in part by the financial strains that crowd out the very attentive, language-rich parenting that better-off children receive. The 1996 welfare reform that Heckman cites did not simply cut checks; it combined time limits with work requirements and dramatic expansions in supports like the EITC, essentially blending redistribution with predistribution. The evidence suggests that its most successful elements were precisely those that lifted take-home pay and reduced deep poverty among working families. To dismiss post-hoc redistribution as incapable of promoting mobility is to ignore this recent history.

None of this is to say that Heckman’s emphasis on early, character-focused intervention is wrong. It is vital, and the Perry and Abecedarian results are genuinely impressive. But a strategy that exclusively targets the parent-child interaction while leaving the economic pressures on parents intact sets an unnecessarily high bar for itself. The “scarce resource” may well be love and engaged parenting, but poverty taxes both. If we are serious about closing the gaps that open before kindergarten, we should expand high-quality early learning and simultaneously ensure that families have enough income to escape the corrosive stress that makes consistent nurturing so hard. A just society cannot choose between predistribution and redistribution; it needs both.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

James Heckman’s powerful case for early childhood intervention offers a vital corrective to the narrow way we usually discuss educational policy, inequality, and workforce development. By demonstrating that non-cognitive skills—such as perseverance, motivation, and socio-emotional regulation—are just as crucial as cognitive intelligence, Heckman rightly moves our focus from adolescent remediation to the earliest years of life. His synthesis of neuroscience, developmental psychology, and economics proves that waiting until high school to address inequality is waiting far too long.

However, in his zeal to champion early childhood programs, Heckman draws a sharp, flawed dichotomy between "predistribution" (early skill-building and parenting interventions) and "redistribution" (financial support for families). He argues that the scarce resource in disadvantaged homes is "love and parenting—not money," and warns against simply giving financial resources to poor families. This artificial separation fundamentally misunderstands the mechanics of poverty and the lived reality of disadvantaged parents.

Money and parenting quality are inextricably linked, primarily through the mechanism of stress. Extensive research in developmental psychology and pediatrics has firmly established the destructive nature of "toxic stress" on early brain architecture. When families face the relentless financial pressure of deep poverty—worrying about eviction, food insecurity, or unpaid utility bills—parents experience chronic, debilitating physiological stress. This stress severely depletes the mental bandwidth, patience, and energy required for the responsive, high-quality "serve and return" parenting that Heckman rightly identifies as essential for healthy child development. Parents working irregular shifts at multiple low-wage jobs do not lack love for their children; they lack the time, energy, and material security that allow middle-class parents to easily bathe their children in cognitive and emotional resources.

Ironically, Heckman’s own essay contains the very evidence that refutes his dismissal of financial redistribution. He cites a natural experiment involving an American Indian population that received a sudden influx of wealth from a newly opened casino. Heckman notes that "with more money, parental supervision of children improved, and there was greater parental engagement," leading to reduced disruptive behavior in the children. He concludes that "it was the changes in parenting that reduced disruptive behavior," using this to claim that parenting, not money, is the true lever of change. But this misses the obvious causal sequence: the *money* was the direct catalyst that allowed the parents to change their behavior. Financial resources relieved the suffocating pressures of poverty, creating the vital breathing room necessary for better parenting to emerge.

High-quality early interventions, such as the Perry Preschool Project, the Abecedarian Project, and the Nurse-Family Partnership, are undeniably effective and deserve the massive public investment Heckman advocates. Yet, we cannot expect early childhood educators and visiting nurses to entirely offset the punishing economic realities of the modern low-wage labor market. If we accept Heckman’s premise that the home environment is the primary engine of human skill formation, then stabilizing that home environment economically must be a central pillar of our social mobility strategy.

Ultimately, predistribution and redistribution are not competitors; they are necessary complements. Providing a menu of high-quality early childhood programs will undoubtedly boost both the cognitive and socio-emotional skills of the next generation. But if we truly wish to arrest the polarization of American society and ensure that the accident of birth does not become fate, we must also ensure that parents have the basic material foundation required to raise their children without the shadow of constant deprivation. A society that truly values the healthy development of its youngest citizens must be willing to provide both the developmental programs children need and the economic security their families require.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

James Heckman is right about the central fact: opportunity begins long before kindergarten. By the time children enter school, class differences in vocabulary, health, self-regulation, and confidence are already large. A society that waits until adolescence to repair those gaps is not practicing equality of opportunity; it is economizing on children and then blaming schools, courts, and job-training programs for the consequences. The evidence from Perry, Abecedarian, and nurse home visiting is powerful precisely because it shows that early disadvantage is not destiny.

But the case for early intervention becomes less persuasive when it is framed as an alternative to redistribution rather than as a complement to it. Heckman’s “predistribution, not redistribution” formulation risks drawing too sharp a line between the quality of parenting and the material conditions under which parenting occurs. Love is not money, but neither is love independent of money. Parents who work irregular hours, face eviction, lack health insurance, live in dangerous neighborhoods, or suffer untreated depression are not simply deficient suppliers of “nurturing.” They are raising children under conditions that make consistent nurturing harder.

Indeed, some of the evidence Heckman cites points in this direction. The casino-income study did not merely teach parents better values; it increased family income, and family relationships improved. The mechanism may have been parenting, but the intervention was money. Similarly, expansions of the Earned Income Tax Credit, food stamps, Medicaid, and housing assistance are not just static transfers. They can reduce stress, improve nutrition and health, increase residential stability, and make it more possible for parents to read, talk, supervise, and plan. To say that “giving families more money is not the same as enhancing children’s environments” is true. But it does not follow that money is unimportant to those environments.

The same point applies to family structure. Children in stable two-parent families often do better, but policy cannot simply wish such families into existence. Low wages, mass incarceration, unstable employment, poor health, and weak prospects for less-educated men all shape marriage markets and family life. If we want more stable families, we need not only parenting programs but also jobs that pay enough to support a household, less destructive criminal justice policies, and health care that reaches parents as well as children. A home-visiting nurse can do a great deal; she cannot by herself reverse the collapse of work for less-skilled men.

There is also a question of scale. Perry and Abecedarian were intensive, carefully staffed, and small. Their successes should inspire policy, but they cannot simply be multiplied by congressional appropriation. Head Start’s more mixed record is a warning that quality matters enormously. If early childhood programs are underfunded, staffed by poorly paid workers, or reduced to test-prep for four-year-olds, they will not reproduce the results of the model programs. The lesson is not that we should abandon large-scale early education, but that we should be honest about what it requires: well-trained teachers, low child-adult ratios, attention to health and family support, and stable financing.

Nor should enthusiasm for the early years lead us to understate the importance of schools. Heckman is correct that many gaps appear before first grade, but schools can still widen or narrow them. A child who enters kindergarten behind is not helped by attending a chaotic elementary school, a segregated middle school, and a high school with inexperienced teachers and few advanced courses. Early investment “begets” later learning only if later institutions are capable of receiving and reinforcing it. The right conclusion is not early childhood instead of school reform, but a developmental strategy that links prenatal care, home visiting, preschool, elementary instruction, after-school supports, and pathways into work.

The focus on non-cognitive skills is salutary, but it too requires care. Perseverance, self-control, and motivation are real contributors to success. Yet when public policy becomes fascinated with “character,” it can drift toward asking poor children to adapt to unjust conditions rather than changing those conditions. A teenager may need grit, but he also needs a safe school, a summer job, a reliable bus route, and some reason to believe effort will be rewarded. Non-cognitive skills should not become a polite way of relocating responsibility from institutions to children.

The strongest version of Heckman’s argument is not that early childhood policy can substitute for redistribution, school improvement, or labor-market reform. It is that any serious egalitarian politics must begin earlier than our politics usually begins. That is a profound correction. But social mobility is produced by an ecology: family resources, parental time, neighborhood safety, health, schools, wages, and public institutions. Early childhood programs are among the best investments we can make. They will work best when embedded in a broader commitment to reduce poverty and strengthen the worlds in which parents are trying to raise their children.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

James Heckman's essay offers a compelling synthesis of evidence on early childhood development and its lasting consequences for life outcomes. His central argument—that policy should shift toward early intervention rather than remediation for older populations—is grounded in rigorous experimental evidence from the Perry Preschool and Abecedarian programs. This evidence is difficult to dismiss. However, several dimensions of his analysis warrant critical scrutiny, particularly regarding the relationship between individual-level interventions and the broader structural forces that create inequality in the first place.

Heckman's critique of GED programs provides some of the essay's most persuasive moments. The point that GED recipients possess comparable cognitive abilities to traditional graduates yet earn at dropout wages effectively demonstrates the importance of non-cognitive skills. This analysis is valuable precisely because it moves beyond simplistic measures of "smartness" to examine what actually produces success in work and life. Yet there is an irony in Heckman's use of this example: the GED is itself a late intervention, designed for those who have already failed in the traditional K-12 system. If early childhood interventions are so crucial, why do we have such limited evidence about their long-term effects when implemented at scale rather than in highly controlled experimental settings?

This points to a central challenge with Heckman's predistribution thesis. The Perry and Abecedarian programs achieved remarkable results, but they were extraordinarily intensive. Perry involved daily sessions over thirty weeks plus home visits for fewer than sixty children. Abecedarian provided full-time, year-round care from infancy through age eight. These interventions achieved their outcomes because of their intensity, duration, and careful implementation by trained professionals. Replicating such programs universally would require resources and institutional capacity far beyond what current political conditions permit. The evidence that early intervention works in principle does not automatically translate into evidence that we can implement it effectively at scale.

More fundamentally, Heckman's framework largely accepts existing economic arrangements and focuses on developing individual capabilities to thrive within them. His discussion of "predistribution" explicitly contrasts with redistribution, arguing that enhancing early environments produces both efficiency and equity gains without the tradeoffs inherent in income transfer programs. But this framing may understate the extent to which the structure of the economy itself generates the conditions Heckman rightly identifies as problematic. The decline in high school graduation rates, the deterioration of family environments, and the growth of disadvantaged childhoods are not simply failures of individual capability development; they reflect changes in labor markets, the collapse of middle-wage jobs, the decline of unions, and decades of policy choices that have increased economic insecurity for working families.

A comprehensive mobility strategy would require not only high-quality early childhood programs but also attention to the broader determinants of family stability and childhood advantage: wages that permit stable family formation, labor protections that provide economic security, and housing and community policies that enable parents to provide nurturing environments. Without such structural attention, early childhood interventions may help individual children while leaving the underlying sources of inequality intact.

Heckman is correct that we should not "repeat the mistakes of the War on Poverty" by treating income transfers as sufficient. But the lesson may be that neither individual-level skill interventions nor income redistribution alone is adequate. We need both, alongside structural reforms that address the economy's capacity to provide secure, rewarding work to all who seek it.

Predistribution and Redistribution Are Not Substitutes

James Heckman has done as much as any economist alive to put early childhood on the policy map, and his case for intensive, well-designed interventions in the first years of life is largely persuasive. The longitudinal evidence from Perry and Abecedarian, the developmental neuroscience, the data on diverging family environments — all of it points the same direction. Where his essay goes wrong is in the rhetorical move at the end: dressing up predistribution as a substitute for redistribution, and presenting the 1996 welfare reform as vindication of the view that "giving money to poor families does not, by itself, promote social mobility."

That framing is too clean, and the evidence is more ambivalent than Heckman lets on.

Start with the Cherokee casino study he himself cites approvingly. When the Eastern Band of Cherokees began distributing per-capita casino payments, the children in newly non-poor households showed marked reductions in behavioral problems and improvements in school performance. Heckman reads this as a story about parenting: income changed, parenting improved, child outcomes followed. Fair enough. But notice what the study does not show — it does not show that you could have produced the same parenting improvements without the cash. The income was the intervention. To then conclude that "giving families more money is not the same as enhancing their children's environments" reverses the actual finding: in that experiment, giving families money was precisely what enhanced their children's environments.

The same logic applies to a growing body of work on the Earned Income Tax Credit, Section 8 housing vouchers, SNAP, and the Canadian and British child benefits introduced over the past fifteen years. Where careful identification has been possible, additional income to poor families has been linked with better birth weights, higher test scores, and reductions in maternal stress. These effects are not as large as the Perry results per dollar, but they are real, and they accrue across the entire low-income population — not the few thousand children a boutique program can serve.

Heckman's own framework helps explain why. If "the scarce resource is love and parenting — not money," we should ask what depletes love and parenting in poor households. The answer, much of the developmental literature suggests, is chronic stress: eviction, food insecurity, unstable work schedules, untreated maternal depression, the cognitive load of constant financial triage. Sendhil Mullainathan and Eldar Shafir's recent work on scarcity is directly relevant here. Income transfers do not buy nurturing directly, but they relax precisely the conditions that make nurturing hardest. To pit predistribution against redistribution is to ignore how the latter underwrites the former.

There is also a political-economy point Heckman glides past. The Perry Preschool intervention cost roughly $17,000 per child in 1960s dollars and required highly trained staff making weekly home visits. Scaling that nationally would be enormously expensive — easily comparable to a meaningful expansion of the EITC or a child allowance. If we are going to spend the money either way, the choice is not between costly redistribution and cheap predistribution, but between two equally serious commitments. The honest case for early childhood programs is that they complement income support, not that they let us avoid it.

This matters because Heckman's framing has a political afterlife he may not intend. His essay can be — and has been — quoted by people who want to defund welfare and call the savings "predistribution." That misreads his evidence. Nothing in the Perry or Abecedarian results tells us that food stamps are useless or that child poverty per se is harmless. They tell us that intensive cognitive and socio-emotional enrichment, on top of whatever else children have, pays off handsomely. The "on top of" is doing a lot of work.

I would also push back on the implicit causal story about welfare reform. Yes, employment among single mothers rose after 1996. But deep poverty also rose, particularly after the safety net was tested by the 2008 recession. Kathryn Edin's recent ethnographic work documents families living essentially on no cash income at all. If the goal is healthy early environments, exposing the most vulnerable children to that kind of destitution is not a triumph of social policy.

Heckman is right that you cannot solve the skills gap by writing checks. He is right that high-quality, early, sustained interventions yield extraordinary returns, especially when they engage parents directly. He is right that adolescent remediation is a poor substitute for early formation. But the right conclusion is not "predistribution, not redistribution." It is that a serious mobility agenda needs both: a generous floor of income and material security for families with young children, and a serious public investment in the developmental scaffolding — nurse home visiting, high-quality center-based care, paid parental leave, parenting support — that converts that security into nurturing capacity.

To borrow Heckman's own language: skill begets skill, but stability begets the conditions under which skill can be built. We should stop pretending we have to choose.

The Scaling Problem Heckman Won't Quite Face

James Heckman's essay is a tour of impressive findings tied together with a confident policy moral. I want to dwell on a question the essay raises but never really answers: can the results of the Perry Preschool Project and the Abecedarian Project be reproduced at scale? Because if they cannot, the entire policy structure built on those two studies is wobbling on a very narrow base.

Consider the sample sizes. Perry randomized 58 children. Abecedarian randomized 111. These are not typos; they are the actual populations from which we are extracting eye-popping internal rates of return and on which the case for a major redirection of federal social policy is being built. Both studies were run by committed researchers in specific communities — Ypsilanti, Michigan and Chapel Hill, North Carolina — using carefully recruited staff and curricula designed by their inventors. The Nurse-Family Partnership, similarly, achieved its strongest results in trials supervised by its founder, David Olds.

What happens when programs leave the hothouse? Head Start is the natural test. It has been running at scale since 1965 and serves nearly a million children annually. The 2010 Head Start Impact Study — a large, randomized national evaluation released by HHS during the Obama administration — found modest initial gains in cognitive skills that largely faded by the end of first grade. There were some persisting effects on parenting practices and on certain socio-emotional measures, but the eye-popping ROI numbers Heckman cites from Perry simply do not show up in the program we actually run. Tennessee's state pre-K, evaluated by Vanderbilt researchers, shows a similar pattern of early gains followed by convergence.

Heckman knows this. His standard response is that Head Start is underfunded, uneven in quality, and not as intensive as Perry or Abecedarian. That may be true. But it is exactly the point. The interventions that produced the famous returns were exceptional in their intensity, staff quality, and duration. Perry involved daily classroom time plus weekly 90-minute home visits by certified teachers. Abecedarian ran year-round, full-day, from infancy through age eight, with a 3:1 infant-teacher ratio. There is no reason to believe that the typical American child care worker, paid little more than minimum wage and turning over at high rates, can deliver what the Perry teachers delivered. Heckman's evidence is, in effect, that a Michelin-starred meal is nutritious and delicious. He has not shown that we can serve it in a school cafeteria.

There is a further problem with the cited rates of return. Most have been calculated by Heckman and his collaborators themselves, using particular assumptions about discount rates, the monetization of crime reduction, and projections of lifetime earnings effects from small early differences. None of these moves are illegitimate, but they are sensitive to assumptions, and as the original samples age, the "treatment" and "control" arms involve dozens of people each, not thousands. A handful of unusually successful or unsuccessful individuals can shift the headline numbers materially. We should be reading these effect estimates with wider confidence intervals than the essay implies.

I am not arguing against early childhood investment. I am arguing for honesty about what we know. Here is what I think the evidence actually supports: (1) severe early deprivation has lasting negative effects, as the Romanian orphanage data devastatingly show; (2) high-intensity, high-quality programs delivered to small numbers of severely disadvantaged children can produce meaningful gains; (3) less intensive programs delivered at scale produce smaller and more uncertain effects, particularly on cognitive measures.

This more modest summary has different policy implications than Heckman's. It suggests we should be cautious about promising voters or legislators large macroeconomic returns from universal pre-K. It suggests we should target the most intensive interventions on the most disadvantaged families, as Heckman himself recommends, rather than diffusing resources thinly across the entire under-five population. It suggests we should be investing in rigorous evaluation of new programs at scale, not assuming Perry's results will replicate. And it suggests humility about how much social mobility any preschool program, however good, can deliver against headwinds like deindustrialization, residential segregation, mass incarceration, and a stagnant low-wage labor market.

There is a final irony. Heckman criticizes the official high school graduation statistics for laundering bad outcomes through the GED. He is right to. But the analytic standards he applies to NCES data should apply to small-sample randomized trials too. We should be asking whether long-run Perry effects are robust to coding choices, attrition, and the inclusion of a handful of outliers. Some of the technical literature suggests they are not as stable as the headline numbers imply.

Heckman has reoriented an entire field toward early childhood. That is a real contribution. But the case for redirecting billions of dollars of federal spending needs more than two beautifully run demonstrations from half a century ago. It needs evidence that we can actually do this at scale — and that evidence, on the current record, is much thinner than his essay suggests.

Don't Talk Around the Family

Reading Heckman's essay, one is struck by how carefully it tiptoes around what its own data are saying. The single most powerful predictor of child disadvantage he identifies is the breakdown of stable, two-parent family life. The proportion of children born to never-married mothers has soared. Time-use data show married, educated mothers spending dramatically more time on enrichment than their less educated counterparts. The "diverging destinies" Sara McLanahan has documented are, at their root, diverging family structures. And yet Heckman's policy conclusion is essentially: let us design state-funded programs to compensate. The family itself, as an institution and a set of cultural norms, drops out of view.

This is a strange place to land, and I think it reflects a broader squeamishness in the social-policy literature about saying out loud what its own findings suggest. The Moynihan Report was right, and its critics were wrong. Children raised by their married biological parents do better, on average, on virtually every outcome we measure: educational attainment, employment, mental health, avoidance of incarceration, future marital stability. The differences survive controls for income and education. They show up across racial groups. They are large.

Heckman gestures at this, then immediately tries to defuse it. He notes the presence of a father "can be a net negative if he shows antisocial tendencies or if marital conflict is substantial." True, but a caveat is not a counterargument. The average effect of two parents in stable marriage is strongly positive, and the policy question is what to do about a culture in which such marriages have become a class privilege.

The class divergence in family structure is the central social fact of our era. Among college-educated Americans, marriage rates remain high, divorce rates have actually fallen since the 1970s, and almost all children are born within marriage. Among Americans without a college degree, marriage has collapsed and out-of-wedlock births now exceed 50 percent. Charles Murray's recent book Coming Apart documents this with painful clarity, even if one rejects parts of his interpretation. The "haves" practice traditional family norms with remarkable discipline; they just decline to preach what they practice.

Heckman's framework lets policy elites avoid that conversation. Rather than asking why working-class family life has eroded, or what cultural and legal changes might support its repair, we propose to professionalize child-rearing for the disadvantaged: nurse home visitors, intensive preschool, parenting curricula. There is nothing wrong with any of those programs in themselves. But they are downstream interventions trying to reproduce, imperfectly, what intact families produce naturally. We are essentially proposing to socialize, at public expense, the developmental functions that families used to perform on their own.

This raises questions Heckman does not engage. What signal does it send when society treats the early-childhood environment as a public-policy problem rather than a parental responsibility? Does it accelerate the very atomization it is trying to compensate for? When the state offers to take on more of the work of nurturing, does it lighten the load on struggling parents — or further erode the cultural expectation that parents will do that work themselves?

There are also serious questions about which family structures the programs will quietly normalize. Heckman is admirably explicit that programs must respect "cultural diversity" and "the sanctity of early family life." But the actual content of Perry and Abecedarian — and of nurse-visiting curricula — embeds particular middle-class assumptions about reading aloud, structured play, verbal interaction, and discipline. These are good practices. They are also the practices of intact, educated, two-parent families. Why we feel comfortable transmitting them through paid professionals but uncomfortable advocating the family structure that produces them organically is, I think, mostly a matter of contemporary taboo.

A more honest version of Heckman's argument would say: the best early childhood intervention we know of is a stable marriage between the child's biological parents, conducted by adults with the maturity and resources to invest in their children. Public policy should support that arrangement directly — through tax treatment, work-family policies, the removal of marriage penalties in transfer programs, and cultural advocacy by the institutions (schools, churches, media, professional associations) that shape norms. Where the underlying family is broken or absent, intensive programs can partially substitute. But they are second-best.

Welfare reform, which Heckman invokes approvingly, was animated partly by this insight: that policy structures shape family formation, and a system that subsidized non-work and non-marriage was producing more of both. We have not finished that work. The current tax code still penalizes marriage for low-income couples. Disability and Medicaid eligibility rules often make a working spouse a liability. These are predistributional levers Heckman does not discuss.

I am genuinely grateful for Heckman's evidence on the importance of early life. But the policy implication is not only "build more programs." It is also: take the family seriously as the institution his own data point to. The accident of birth includes whether one's parents stayed together. We should be willing to say so.

When "Quality of Parenting" Becomes a Polite Way to Blame the Poor

There is much to admire in James Heckman's essay, and I share his conviction that early childhood is a decisive period of human development. But I want to register a serious worry about the rhetorical center of his argument: the move from "income and family structure are crude proxies" to "the scarce resource is love and parenting — not money." That sentence does an enormous amount of political work, and most of it is in the wrong direction.

What Heckman is doing, intentionally or not, is reviving a culture-of-poverty framework dressed in the language of neuroscience and behavioral economics. The proximate cause of bad child outcomes, he tells us, is poor parenting. The Hart and Risley word-gap study is invoked. ACE scores are tallied. We are shown brain scans of neglected three-year-olds. By the end, the reader is meant to conclude that the children of the poor suffer mainly because their parents do not talk to them enough, do not read to them enough, do not regulate them enough — not because they lack money, housing, or stable work.

This is a half-truth that functions as a falsehood. The half that is true: yes, parenting practices vary by class, and yes, parenting matters for child development. The half that is false: that these parenting practices are independent causes, freely chosen, that could be altered without altering material conditions.

Consider what we actually know about why poor parents talk less, read less, and discipline more harshly. Decades of developmental research — much of it summarized by scholars like Vonnie McLoyd and Jeanne Brooks-Gunn — point to a few mechanisms: chronic stress depletes cognitive bandwidth and emotional patience; unstable, exhausting low-wage work leaves parents physically depleted at the end of the day; depression and anxiety, which are far more prevalent among the poor, dampen the sensitive responsiveness that babies need; cramped, noisy, unsafe housing makes reading aloud less feasible; food insecurity and eviction threats consume attention. Mullainathan and Shafir's recent work shows experimentally that the same person, placed under financial scarcity, exhibits measurable declines in executive function. These are not character defects. They are predictable responses to the conditions of poverty.

Once you see this, "quality of parenting" stops looking like an independent causal variable and starts looking like an intermediate outcome of the broader economic and social environment. Heckman acknowledges this when convenient — see his use of the Cherokee casino study, where unexpected cash transfers improved parenting and thereby improved child outcomes — but then he abruptly concludes that "giving families more money is not the same as enhancing the quality of the environments of disadvantaged children." On his own evidence, that is precisely what it was.

The framing matters because of where it leads in practice. If the problem is bad parenting, the policy response is to fix the parents — through nurse visits, parenting classes, and intensive early childhood programs administered by professionals. If the problem is the material and structural conditions that degrade parenting, the policy response is broader: a living wage, predictable schedules, paid family leave, child allowances, accessible mental health care, housing stability, and yes, programs like Perry and Abecedarian as part of a larger package. The two framings have very different fiscal and political implications, and conservatives have understood this for a long time. That is why Heckman's "predistribution, not redistribution" framing has been picked up so eagerly in policy circles that have no interest in funding either at the scale required.

There is also something to be said about who gets pathologized. The Perry and Abecedarian samples were poor, mostly Black children in particular communities. The studies were conducted with care and respect, but the larger discourse Heckman is sustaining is one in which the parenting of poor Black mothers becomes a national scientific object — measured, scored (the ACE), found wanting. Meanwhile, the affluent parents whose children outperform on every metric are not subjected to the same scrutiny, and their advantages — inherited wealth, residential sorting into high-resource schools, professional networks, the ability to absorb shocks without family disruption — are treated as the background condition rather than the object of analysis. We have, in effect, two literatures: one on what is wrong with poor families, and another on inequality among institutions. Heckman's framing belongs to the first.

To be clear: I support universal high-quality early childhood education. I support nurse home visiting. I support more federal investment in evidence-based parenting programs. What I oppose is dressing these up as substitutes for redistribution, and the implicit claim that American inequality of opportunity is at root a parenting problem rather than a political-economic one.

Heckman has the data; he just stops the analysis too early. The Cherokee children needed both the casino check and the better parenting it enabled. American children, on the whole, need both income security for their families and the developmental supports Heckman champions. Pretending we can have the second without the first is a politically convenient story. The evidence does not support it.

Don't Let Schools Off the Hook

James Heckman's case for early childhood investment is compelling, and I have spent enough years in classrooms to know that children arrive at kindergarten with vastly different vocabularies, attention spans, and emotional regulation. He is right that those gaps are largely formed before we ever meet the children. Where I part company with him is in the inference he draws: that K–12 schooling "plays only a minor role" in alleviating or creating test-score gaps, and that school reform is therefore largely beside the point.

This claim is overstated, and as policy guidance it is dangerous.

Look closely at the chart Heckman references — the one showing gaps by maternal education that are largely present at age six and persist through age eighteen. The persistence is doing rhetorical work. He reads it as evidence that schools cannot close the gaps. An equally plausible reading is that American schools fail to close the gaps because they are systematically less effective for the children who arrive most disadvantaged. The gap does not narrow because we have organized our school system in a way that virtually guarantees it will not narrow.

Consider how American public education is funded and organized. The bulk of school revenue comes from local property taxes, which means that children from disadvantaged neighborhoods attend schools with fewer resources, less experienced teachers, more turnover, larger classes for kids who need smaller ones, and weaker curricula. The poorest children get the weakest schools. If we then observe that gaps that opened early persist through schooling, we have not shown that schooling cannot matter; we have shown that this kind of schooling does not.

Compare international evidence. Finland, Korea, Singapore, and Canada all show smaller socioeconomic gradients in achievement than the United States. Their school systems differ from ours in important ways: more equal funding across districts, higher teacher selectivity and status, more coherent curricula, more support for struggling students built into the school day. None of these countries achieved their results primarily through early childhood programs — most of them invested in K–12 quality at least as much. The PISA data Heckman references are themselves evidence that schools can shape outcomes, because they show that countries with comparable family inequality produce very different educational outcomes.

There is also evidence from within the United States. The recent work on "no excuses" charter schools — KIPP, the Harlem Children's Zone Promise Academy, Boston's charter sector evaluated by MIT economists — shows that intensive, well-run schools serving poor children can produce large achievement gains, on the order of multiple grade levels over a few years. These results are contested in various ways, but they are not negligible, and they make it harder to assert that schooling is fundamentally powerless against early disadvantage. The Harlem Children's Zone's combination of early childhood programs and intensive K–12 instruction in fact illustrates the complementarity Heckman himself endorses in principle but downplays in his conclusion.

Heckman's own framework actually supports more investment in schools, not less. "Skill begets skill," he writes; the returns to later investment depend on the strength of the earlier foundation. Fine — but the converse is also true. The returns to early investment depend on what happens afterwards. The IQ gains from Perry faded; the lasting effects came through non-cognitive channels and were reinforced (or not) by the K–12 experience that followed. If we pour resources into ages 0–5 and then ship those children into mediocre elementary schools, we are squandering our early investment. Heckman acknowledges this in passing, but the headline of his essay — "Predistribution, Not Redistribution," and the dismissal of "reducing pupil-teacher ratios" as ineffective remediation — sends the opposite message.

The framing of class-size reduction as merely "remedial" is also questionable. The Tennessee STAR experiment, one of the largest randomized education studies ever conducted, found that smaller K–3 classes produced significant achievement gains, especially for minority and low-income students, with effects persisting through high school and into postsecondary outcomes. STAR was not aimed at adolescents trying to compensate for early disadvantage; it was an early-grades intervention. Heckman's blanket skepticism about class size doesn't fit the evidence.

I am not arguing against Perry or Abecedarian. I am arguing that policy elites who read Heckman as having proven the futility of school reform have misunderstood him — and that Heckman himself sometimes encourages the misunderstanding by his framing. The honest summary is this: early childhood matters enormously; schools matter substantially; later remediation matters at the margin. A serious mobility agenda invests in all three, with the heaviest weight on the earliest years.

Telling teachers and school reformers that their work is "minor" because gaps emerge before kindergarten is both empirically wrong and politically self-defeating. K–12 educators are an enormous constituency that can either reinforce or dissipate the gains of early childhood investment. Heckman should want them as allies, not as bystanders.

Another enters a contemporary low-wage service job with unpredictable scheduling, little training, no union, and high turnover. Their “non-cognitive skills” may be identical. Their life chances will not be. Institutions convert skills into security. When institutions weaken, even hardworking people struggle.

This is not an argument against early childhood investment. It is an argument against asking early childhood investment to do too much. Skill formation can improve the supply side of the labor market. It cannot by itself ensure that the labor market produces enough good jobs. A society of better-prepared children is still unjust if too many of them grow into adults competing for poverty-wage work.

The word “predistribution” is useful, but Heckman defines it too narrowly. Predistribution should mean shaping market outcomes before taxes and transfers, not only shaping children before school. Early childhood programs are one form of predistribution. So are labor laws, wage floors, collective bargaining rules, public employment, infrastructure investment, and regulations that prevent employers from shifting risks onto workers. If we care about mobility, we should intervene both before children enter school and before workers receive paychecks.

There is also a feedback loop between labor markets and families. Heckman rightly emphasizes parenting quality, but parenting quality is affected by job quality. A parent with a stable schedule can attend school meetings, cook dinner, supervise homework, and establish bedtime routines. A parent with unpredictable shifts cannot do these things as easily. A father with decent wages is more marriageable and more likely to remain connected to his children. A mother with paid sick leave is less likely to lose her job when a child has asthma. Thus labor policy is family policy, and family policy is child development policy.

Housing policy belongs in the same conversation. Children develop in neighborhoods as well as families. Exposure to violence, lead, eviction, and frequent moves undermines attention and emotional regulation. If early childhood programs teach self-control during the day while children return at night to chaos produced by housing insecurity, the programs are swimming upstream. Vouchers, affordable housing, and efforts to reduce concentrated poverty may not look like “skill policy,” but they help create the stable environments in which skills form.

Heckman’s treatment of immigration also deserves caution. He notes that unskilled migration increases the proportion of low-skilled workers, contributing to inequality and reducing productivity growth. That may be partly true in a narrow accounting sense, but immigrants are not merely skill units. They are workers, parents, entrepreneurs, and future citizens. Their children often make substantial educational gains. A humane mobility agenda should invest in immigrant children early, improve English-language learning, regularize the status of families where possible, and enforce labor standards so that employers cannot use vulnerable workers to undercut wages. The answer to low skill is not resentment of migrants but inclusion and institution-building.

Where Heckman is strongest is in demolishing the idea that equal opportunity can be achieved by opening a gate at age eighteen and inviting everyone to run. By then, the race has been structured by years of unequal development. But we should be equally skeptical of the idea that opportunity can be secured by early childhood investment alone. The metaphor of a race is itself misleading. People do not merely run with capacities formed in childhood; they move through terrain built by law, policy, and power.

The proper conclusion is a two-sided agenda. First, invest heavily and intelligently in early childhood: prenatal care, nurse visiting, high-quality child care and preschool, parent support, and attention to socio-emotional development. Second, rebuild the economic institutions that make effort pay: full employment policy, stronger wage standards, better community colleges and apprenticeships, labor protections, infrastructure jobs, and support for collective voice at work. Add housing and health care security, and the developmental benefits of early programs will be more likely to last.

Heckman is right that birth should not be fate. But neither should the labor market. A child saved by excellent early intervention should not grow up to discover that the only jobs available offer low wages, unstable hours, and no dignity. The promise of social mobility requires both capable people and a fair economy. We should build both.

James Heckman's essay presents a compelling case for early childhood intervention, and I largely agree with its central thesis. However, the essay understates a crucial challenge: even when the evidence for early intervention is strong, translating small-scale demonstrations into large-scale social policy is extraordinarily difficult. Perry Preschool and Abecedarian achieved impressive results, but these programs operated with intensive resources, highly trained staff, and careful monitoring that mass public programs rarely match. Before we commit to major policy shifts, we need to confront the scalability problem honestly.

Consider what made Perry and Abecedarian successful. Perry served 58 children over five years with a daily 2.5-hour program and weekly home visits. That is roughly $20,000 per child per year in 2012 dollars, accounting for inflation. The Abecedarian program was even more intensive—a full-day, year-round intervention from infancy through age eight with a 3:1 infant-to-teacher ratio. When Heckman reports 6-10 percent returns, he is measuring returns to programs that cost more per child than many public universities charge in annual tuition.

Now consider what a universal early childhood program would need to look like. The National Institute for Early Education Research estimates that quality preschool for all three and four-year-olds would cost between $50-75 billion annually. Extending that to infants and toddlers—as Abecedarian suggests we should—would cost considerably more. And this is assuming we can recruit and train the workforce needed to deliver these services at quality levels comparable to the demonstration programs. We currently face a shortage of qualified early childhood educators, and the working conditions and compensation in the field do not attract the talent required for high-quality interventions.

There is also the problem of institutional capacity. Perry and Abecedarian benefited from dedicated leadership, external evaluation, and ongoing program refinement over decades. Scaling these models nationally would require building infrastructure that does not currently exist. Head Start, our main federal early childhood program, has struggled for fifty years to reach quality standards, and evaluations consistently show that gains from Head Start fade by third grade—precisely the fadeout pattern Heckman attributes to inferior programs.

None of this means we should abandon early childhood investment. We should, however, be realistic about timelines and expect outcomes proportional to what we actually invest. A modest but well-designed expansion of evidence-based home visiting programs, combined with quality improvements in existing preschools, might achieve real results. Promising universal preschool immediately, based on demonstrations that cost multiples of what universal programs could realistically spend, sets up expectations we cannot meet.

Heckman is right that early intervention is more effective than later remediation. But effectiveness in controlled settings does not guarantee effectiveness at scale. Sound policy requires us to bridge that gap thoughtfully rather than assume that demonstration results will repeat themselves in imperfect real-world implementations. We should invest in early childhood, but we should also invest in the institutional capacity to make that investment count.

Heckman's analysis is largely accurate, but it neglects a crucial causal factor in the skills problem he identifies: changes in the labor market have fundamentally altered the returns to skill and reshaped American opportunity structures in ways that policy alone cannot reverse. Understanding these economic dynamics is essential for designing effective interventions.

The decline in demand for less-skilled labor has been underway for decades, driven by automation, globalization, and the rise of information-intensive economic activities. Jobs that once provided stable employment for high school graduates—manufacturing, construction, clerical work—have contracted. The workers who once filled these jobs face either long-term unemployment or absorption into low-wage service jobs with minimal advancement potential. This is not merely a skills problem; it is a structural transformation of the economy that outpaces the rate at which workers can adapt.

When Heckman notes that real wages for high school graduates have increased relative to dropouts, he correctly identifies the incentive structure. But incentives can only influence behavior if feasible options exist. For young men in communities where good jobs are scarce, finishing high school opens the door to unemployment rather than employment. The rational response to a labor market with few rewards for credentialing is to discount the value of credentials—and many young people do exactly that.

This suggests that early childhood interventions, however valuable, cannot by themselves solve the skills problem if the economy continues to generate few opportunities for less-skilled workers. Perry and Abecedarian succeeded partly because the children who benefited from them entered an economy that still offered substantial returns to the skills they developed. If the economy continues to polarize, early intervention beneficiaries may find their enhanced capacities are not fully utilized.

Policy must therefore address both sides of the problem. We need early childhood investment to build human capital, but we also need economic policies that ensure this capital is utilized productively. This might include industrial policy that preserves or rebuilds skilled manufacturing employment, apprenticeship programs that provide direct pathways to middle-class careers, or other mechanisms that connect skill development to economic opportunity.

The skills problem Heckman diagnoses is real, but it is embedded in an economic transformation that policy has not adequately addressed. Early intervention is necessary but not sufficient.

Early childhood programs work best when they engage families as partners, but they also inevitably involve state actors making judgments about parenting quality and child welfare. Who determines what constitutes adequate nurturing? On what basis? With what protections for parental autonomy?

The ACE studies Heckman cites provide the most troubling examples. Adverse childhood experiences—abuse, neglect, domestic violence—clearly harm children and predict poor adult outcomes. But identifying these experiences and intervening in family life raises questions that the essay does not address. What standard of evidence justifies state intrusion? How do we distinguish between parents who provide suboptimal environments due to their own limitations from parents who actively harm children? And what institutional structures can ensure that intervention helps rather than harms?

Historical examples suggest these questions are not merely academic. The child welfare system has disproportionately removed children from poor families and families of color on the basis of standards that reflected middle-class norms rather than objective evidence of harm. Early childhood programs designed to improve parenting could replicate this pattern if they are not carefully designed.

None of this means we should abandon early childhood investment. It does mean that effective policy requires clarity about the limits of state power in family life. Programs should support parents rather than replace them. Universal programs with voluntary participation are preferable to targeted programs that may stigmatize struggling families. And we should be skeptical of approaches that give the state extensive leverage over how parents raise their children.

Heckman is right that we should invest in early childhood. But we should do so in ways that respect both the children who need support and the families whose autonomy deserves protection. This balance is difficult to achieve, but it is essential for a just social policy.
