Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The case for an independent inspector general at the NYPD is strong, and the resistance to it from Commissioner Ray Kelly and Mayor Bloomberg reveals exactly why one is needed. The current oversight architecture has visible gaps. The Civilian Complaint Review Board investigates individual misconduct claims; the federal monitor proposed in the Floyd litigation would address a specific constitutional violation; internal affairs polices the rank and file. But no standing body has the authority, expertise, and independence to evaluate whether broad NYPD policies—stop-and-frisk quotas, Demographics Unit mapping of Muslim neighborhoods, social-media surveillance—are lawful, effective, and proportionate. That is the work an IG does.

The model is well established. The CIA, the Justice Department, and nearly every federal department of consequence have inspectors general who issue public reports without disrupting operations. Los Angeles created an Office of the Inspector General after the Rampart scandal and the consent decree that followed; by most accounts it has improved both accountability and the LAPD's relationship with communities of color without hobbling policing. New York's own intelligence and corrections systems have analogous offices. The notion that the NYPD is uniquely unsuited to such review—because terrorism is at stake, or because crime is down—proves too much. Every agency claims its mission is too sensitive for outside eyes; that is precisely the claim an IG exists to test.

Two objections deserve a serious answer. First, won't an IG chill aggressive policing? Only if "aggressive" means "legally indefensible." A good IG audits patterns, not split-second tactical decisions; officers on the street would scarcely notice the office exists. Second, isn't the federal monitor sought in Floyd enough? No—a court-appointed monitor is narrow, temporary, and reactive. An IG is permanent, proactive, and able to examine programs that may never produce a lawsuit, like the Demographics Unit, which appears to have generated no terrorism leads despite years of operation.

The stop-and-frisk numbers—roughly 685,000 stops at the 2011 peak, nearly 90 percent of those stopped neither arrested nor ticketed, the overwhelming majority black or Latino—indicate at minimum a policy that was never adequately audited from within. The same goes for the AP-revealed Muslim surveillance program. Other large cities have shown that independent oversight and effective policing are complements, not substitutes. New York should follow their lead.

The call for an independent inspector general for the NYPD is not simply a political posture; it is a necessary institutional response to a crisis of legitimacy. When a police department becomes the subject of repeated federal lawsuits, when its aggressive tactics consistently draw judicial rebuke, and when whole communities report feeling occupied rather than protected, the routine internal review mechanisms have demonstrably failed. An independent IG would provide the kind of systemic oversight that neither the department’s Internal Affairs Bureau nor the Civilian Complaint Review Board can offer, precisely because it would have the authority to examine patterns of policy and practice rather than just individual misconduct.

The stop-and-frisk program illustrates the gap. By 2013, the NYPD had conducted well over 4 million stops since 2002, with about 90 percent of those stopped being black or Latino, and roughly 90 percent found to have done nothing wrong. The numbers themselves, compiled by the department and analyzed by the New York Civil Liberties Union, reveal a program that is not a surgical crime-fighting tool but a blunt instrument that alienates minority communities. Internal quality assurance has not curtailed these disparities; in fact, the number of stops soared under Commissioner Kelly despite internal audits. An IG would have the independence to audit the operation’s outcomes against its stated objectives, demand data, and publicly report on whether the costs in community trust and constitutional violations outweigh the marginal crime reductions.

Similarly, the surveillance of Muslim communities, exposed by the Associated Press in 2011 and 2012, showed the department mapping neighborhoods, infiltrating student groups, and conducting intelligence operations without evidence of criminal activity. The NYPD’s own legal advisors have argued that the department is not bound by the same restrictions as federal intelligence agencies—a position that underscores the need for oversight that answers to the public, not the police hierarchy. An IG could review such programs for legality and discriminatory impact, ensuring that counterterrorism does not become a license for religious profiling.

Opponents argue that an IG would be a redundant layer of bureaucracy, interfering with operational decisions and compromising officer safety. They point to the Police Commissioner’s authority and the existing review boards. But these boards either lack subpoena power, independence, or the mandate to proactively audit policies. An IG does not direct tactical decisions; it evaluates systemic fairness. That is precisely what has been missing. Without it, the department’s claim that it polices itself rings hollow. If police departments truly have nothing to hide, they should welcome an independent body that can restore public confidence by demonstrating that aggressive tactics are not only effective but equitable. The mayoral candidates who embrace this idea are not being anti-police; they are being pro-accountability. And in a democracy, accountability is not an enemy of law enforcement—it is its foundation.

It is a massive, complex organization that functions not just as a local constabulary, but as a sophisticated intelligence agency. Yet, amid mounting controversies over the widespread use of stop-and-frisk and the surveillance of Muslim communities, the department lacks a fundamental mechanism of modern democratic governance: an independent inspector general. Establishing such an office is not a punitive measure against the police; it is a necessary step to restore public trust and ensure constitutional law enforcement.

Neither is equipped or authorized to conduct proactive, systemic audits of department-wide policies. When a strategy like stop-and-frisk yields hundreds of thousands of street stops annually—the vast majority of which target innocent black and Hispanic New Yorkers—the issue is not merely individual officer misbehavior. It is a matter of institutional policy that demands macro-level scrutiny.

An independent inspector general would fill this critical void. Empowered with subpoena power and direct access to department data, an IG could objectively assess whether policies are achieving their stated goals without violating civil liberties. For example, an IG could rigorously evaluate the efficacy and legal parameters of the NYPD’s Demographics Unit, or analyze the statistical justifications and community impacts of the stop-and-frisk program. Other major law enforcement and intelligence entities, including the FBI, the CIA, and the Los Angeles Police Department, have long operated with inspectors general. Their existence has not compromised public safety; rather, it has provided the transparency necessary to correct systemic failures before they metastasize into massive civil rights lawsuits.

Effective policing relies fundamentally on the consent and cooperation of the public. When communities feel they are being unfairly targeted by the very people sworn to protect them, trust evaporates, and criminal investigations suffer. Creating an independent inspector general for the NYPD would provide a vital check on executive power, ensuring that the department's strategies keep New Yorkers safe while simultaneously upholding the Constitution.

The NYPD is not just another city agency; it has extraordinary authority to detain, search, surveil and use force. That authority requires more than after-the-fact review of individual misconduct. It requires independent scrutiny of patterns and policies.

New York already has oversight mechanisms, but they are incomplete. The Civilian Complaint Review Board handles complaints about individual officers. Internal Affairs investigates corruption and serious misconduct. Courts can hear lawsuits, but litigation is slow, expensive and limited to the claims before a judge. None of these bodies is designed to conduct regular, expert, systemic review of departmentwide practices such as stop-and-frisk deployment, intelligence operations, training, data collection or compliance with constitutional rules.

Hundreds of thousands of New Yorkers have been stopped each year, overwhelmingly black and Latino, while only a small fraction of stops produce weapons. The legal question is not whether proactive policing is legitimate; it is whether stops are based on individualized reasonable suspicion rather than neighborhood, race or quota pressure. An inspector general could audit forms, analyze precinct patterns, evaluate supervision and recommend changes before public trust is damaged further.

The same is true of surveillance of Muslim communities. Police intelligence work is necessary in a city that remains a terrorist target. But necessity does not answer every question. If mosques, student groups, restaurants or neighborhoods are mapped or monitored without meaningful suspicion, the damage is not only to civil liberties but also to cooperation with law enforcement. An independent reviewer with security clearance and confidentiality rules could examine whether intelligence programs are focused, productive and consistent with law.

The case for an independent inspector general within police departments rests on a fundamental principle: oversight that is neither independent nor empowered to investigate is no oversight at all. When New York City is fighting simultaneous lawsuits over stop-and-frisk practices and the surveillance of Muslim communities, the problem is not simply these specific policies but the structural absence of genuine accountability mechanisms. An inspector general with meaningful authority—including subpoena power, access to internal records, and the ability to publish findings independently—would provide both external scrutiny and institutional expertise that current systems lack.

Critics will argue that such an office interferes with operational effectiveness and chain-of-command discipline. This concern deserves consideration, but it conflates oversight with micromanagement. A properly structured inspector general does not direct patrol operations or override tactical decisions. Rather, an I.G. reviews systemic patterns: whether stop-and-frisk practices are being applied consistent with constitutional standards, whether training aligns with legal requirements, whether complaint mechanisms are functioning. The New York Police Department's own monitoring has proven inadequate when departmental interests conflict with the public interest in lawful, non-discriminatory policing.

The broader question is whether police departments should be treated as other government agencies, which universally accept independent oversight of their operations and finances. The argument that law enforcement's unique challenges require exemption from standard accountability structures tends to become circular—itself a reason to wonder why such exemption exists. Municipal police departments exercise extraordinary coercive power over citizens daily; the argument for insulating them from independent review is considerably weaker than for agencies that do not carry guns and make arrests.

That said, an inspector general alone is insufficient. The office must have genuine independence from both the police commissioner and the mayor—appointed by a body with diverse representation, confirmed by a process that insulates against political pressure, and funded as a separate line item. Without structural guarantees, any inspector general becomes another potential instrument of politics rather than accountability.

The controversies currently plaguing the NYPD suggest that the status quo—self-monitoring punctuated by litigation—is neither cost-effective nor just. An independent inspector general will not solve every problem, but it creates an institutional voice for reform from within, grounded in evidence rather than politics.

Yes, the NYPD needs an inspector general — and the resistance to one tells us exactly why. When Commissioner Ray Kelly and Mayor Bloomberg insist that another layer of oversight will hamstring the department, they are essentially arguing that the country's largest police force, with a $4.6 billion budget and 35,000 officers, should be uniquely exempt from the kind of independent scrutiny applied to nearly every other major arm of government. The CIA has an IG. The Pentagon has an IG. The NYPD, which runs its own intelligence division and operates undercover units in other states, somehow does not.

The stop-and-frisk numbers alone justify the office. In 2011 the department recorded nearly 686,000 stops, roughly 87 percent of them of black or Latino New Yorkers, and roughly 88 percent resulting in no arrest or summons. The trial in Floyd v. City of New York, currently underway in federal court, has produced sworn testimony from officers describing quotas and pressure to stop young men of color in particular neighborhoods. Whether or not Judge Scheindlin ultimately finds a constitutional violation, the existence of such a sustained pattern — invisible to the City Council, contested by the department, and litigated only because the Center for Constitutional Rights brought a class action — is itself the argument for systemic, ongoing oversight.

Add to this the AP's revelations about the Demographics Unit's mapping of Muslim communities, the surveillance of Newark mosques and Muslim student associations at Yale and Rutgers, and the department's own admission that the program generated no leads. These were not rogue operations; they were sanctioned. Yet New Yorkers learned about them from reporters, not from any internal review process. An IG with subpoena power and a mandate to issue public reports would not have stopped a determined commissioner from launching such a program, but it would have forced a contemporaneous accounting.

Critics warn an IG would chill aggressive policing and erode the chain of command. But the proposed office, as outlined by Council Speaker Christine Quinn and Public Advocate Bill de Blasio, would not have operational authority. It would review policies, recommend reforms, and report findings — much as the NYPD's existing inspector general for internal affairs does, but covering the broader question of whether departmental practices comport with the Constitution and sound policy.

Police legitimacy depends on the public's belief that someone is watching the watchmen. CCRB complaints rise, lawsuit settlements mount, and trust in minority neighborhoods frays. An inspector general is a modest, structural fix. The remarkable thing is that New York has gone this long without one.

The push for a police inspector general is a well-intentioned mistake — one that mistakes a political grievance for a structural problem and risks weakening the very institution that has made New York the safest big city in America.

Consider the baseline. In 1990, New York recorded 2,245 murders. Last year it recorded 419. Major felonies are down roughly 80 percent over two decades. No other large American city has come close to matching this. That achievement was not accidental: it reflects a deliberate strategy built around CompStat accountability, hotspot deployment, and yes, proactive stops of suspicious persons in high-crime areas. To layer a new oversight bureaucracy on top of the existing structure — the Civilian Complaint Review Board, the Commission to Combat Police Corruption, the five district attorneys, the U.S. Attorney's Offices, the federal monitor in pending cases, the City Council's Public Safety Committee, and the courts themselves — is to assume that the marginal benefit of another investigator outweighs the marginal cost of distraction and risk aversion at headquarters.

The proponents have not made that case. They point to stop-and-frisk and to the Muslim surveillance programs, but both are already being litigated. Floyd v. City of New York may yet impose a federal monitor with far more authority than any city IG would possess. Handschu compliance is supervised by a federal judge. If the courts find constitutional violations, they have ample remedial tools. If they do not, what is the IG's mandate? To second-guess judicially blessed tactics on policy grounds? That is a job for the mayor and the commissioner he appoints — officials who answer to voters.

There is also a chain-of-command problem that proponents wave away too easily. Policing is unlike most municipal functions because split-second decisions, undercover operations, and counterterrorism intelligence cannot be conducted under the gaze of an independent reviewer with subpoena power and a press office. Commissioner Kelly's objection that an IG would compromise sensitive sources is not corporate self-protection; it is a practical concern shared by every major intelligence partner the department works with.

If New Yorkers want different policing, they have a direct remedy: this November's mayoral election. Bill de Blasio, John Liu, and Bill Thompson have all staked out reform positions. That is democratic accountability at work. Creating a permanent, unelected watchdog to override the elected executive's police commissioner is not reform — it is an end-run around accountability dressed up in its language.

The debate over an NYPD inspector general has gotten tangled in personalities — Kelly versus Quinn, Bloomberg versus the Council — when it should be about institutional design. The right question is not whether the NYPD has done something wrong, but whether the existing oversight architecture is adequate to the size and scope of what the department has become.

It isn't. And the reason has less to do with stop-and-frisk than with the quiet transformation of the NYPD into something resembling a domestic intelligence agency. Since 2002 the department has built an Intelligence Division with officers stationed in eleven foreign cities, a Demographics Unit that mapped Muslim daily life, and a counterterrorism apparatus that operates well beyond the five boroughs. These are not traditional policing functions, and the traditional oversight tools — civilian complaints about misconduct, district attorneys prosecuting crimes — were not designed to evaluate them.

The federal intelligence community offers a useful analogy. After abuses came to light in the 1970s, Congress created inspectors general at the CIA and other agencies precisely because external prosecution and internal discipline missed the systemic questions: Is this program effective? Is it lawful? Is it worth its costs in civil liberties? Those are exactly the questions one wants asked about the Demographics Unit, about license plate readers, about the Domain Awareness System co-developed with Microsoft. The CCRB cannot ask them. A jury in a § 1983 suit cannot really answer them.

A well-designed IG would have three features. First, statutory independence — appointed for a fixed term, removable only for cause, with a guaranteed budget. Second, real access — to documents, personnel, and classified material, with appropriate clearances. Third, a public reporting requirement, with carve-outs for genuinely sensitive operational details. Crucially, the IG should not have operational authority. The commissioner runs the department; the IG audits it.

What the IG should not be is a vehicle for relitigating tactical choices that fall within constitutional limits. If the office becomes a roving second-guesser of beat-level decisions, it will fail politically and operationally. Its value lies in the systemic view: pattern analysis, policy review, and the kind of historical accounting that we now have to wait for AP reporters or class-action plaintiffs to produce.

New York pioneered modern policing; it should also pioneer the modern oversight that policing on this scale requires. The choice is not between Ray Kelly and chaos. It is between ad hoc oversight after the fact and structured oversight in real time.

I want to register skepticism from a direction the debate hasn't much considered: the inspector general model itself. Communities most affected by aggressive policing — black and Latino neighborhoods in Brooklyn and the Bronx, Muslim communities in Queens — are being offered an IG as the solution to grievances that an IG is structurally ill-equipped to redress.

What does an inspector general actually do? It writes reports. It makes recommendations. It is, by design, advisory. The federal inspectors general invoked as models have a decidedly mixed record. The CIA's IG has not prevented torture, mass surveillance, or extraordinary rendition; those programs were exposed by leakers and journalists. The Justice Department's IG produces serious work but cannot compel the Attorney General to do anything. The TSA still operates as it pleases despite years of IG criticism. Why would an NYPD IG fare differently against a commissioner with the political backing of City Hall?

The deeper issue is that stop-and-frisk and Muslim surveillance are not the products of insufficient internal review. They are policies pursued openly and defended publicly by the commissioner and the mayor. Everyone has known the numbers for years. The City Council has known. The DAs have known. The mayor was reelected after the policy was in full swing. The problem is political will, not informational deficit, and an IG addresses informational deficit.

If the goal is meaningful change, the more potent tools are already on the table and largely being ignored in this debate. The Community Safety Act, which would create an enforceable ban on bias-based profiling and a private right of action, would do more in a year than an IG would do in a decade. Strengthening the CCRB by giving it final disciplinary authority — rather than letting the commissioner overrule its findings, which Kelly has done with striking regularity — would put teeth into existing oversight. Subjecting the intelligence division to genuine judicial supervision under a modernized Handschu agreement would address the surveillance issues directly.

I'm not opposed to an inspector general; on the margin it might help. But I worry that endorsing the IG lets politicians claim the mantle of reform while declining the harder fights. Speaker Quinn, in particular, has used the IG proposal to position herself as a reformer while continuing to support Commissioner Kelly. That is a tell. The communities pressing for change should demand more than an auditor. They should demand accountability that actually binds.

Look beyond New York. The most striking thing about this debate is how parochial it has become — framed as a battle over Ray Kelly's legacy when in fact the question of independent civilian oversight of police is being asked, and answered, in jurisdictions across the country.

Los Angeles has had an Inspector General for the LAPD since 1996, created in the wake of the Christopher Commission's post-Rodney King findings. The office reviews officer-involved shootings, audits internal affairs, and reports to the Police Commission. It has not abolished the LAPD; it has not handcuffed officers. Crime in Los Angeles has fallen alongside crime in New York. Whatever one thinks of the LAPD today, almost no one in Los Angeles seriously proposes abolishing the IG.

Washington, D.C., operates under federal oversight following a DOJ consent decree. Cincinnati emerged from one. Pittsburgh did. Detroit is currently subject to one. New Orleans is. Oakland is. In each case, an outside actor — usually a court-appointed monitor — performs essentially the functions a permanent inspector general would perform: auditing data, reviewing policies, reporting publicly. The empirical record from these jurisdictions is not that policing collapses; it is that data systems improve, complaint rates eventually stabilize, and use-of-force incidents tend to decline.

New York is unusual among large American cities in having avoided either a consent decree or a permanent independent monitor. This is sometimes cited as evidence that its policing is exemplary. But it may equally reflect that the DOJ has historically been reluctant to take on a department this large and politically connected, and that systemic civil rights litigation has, until Floyd, been hard to mount at the necessary scale. The pending federal trial may yet bring a monitor through the back door. An inspector general established by city law would be a more democratic, locally accountable way to get there.

There is a final consideration. Counterterrorism intelligence, which the NYPD now conducts on a scale rivaled by no other municipal force, has been the subject of structured oversight at the federal level for forty years — the FISA Court, the congressional intelligence committees, agency IGs. These mechanisms are imperfect, but they exist because we long ago decided that secret government activity targeting Americans requires more than the good faith of the officials conducting it. New York City has decided no such thing about its own intelligence apparatus, and that is the gap an IG would begin, modestly, to fill.

I support an inspector general for the NYPD, but I want to be honest about what it can and cannot do, because the reform debate has become unhelpfully polarized between people who treat the IG as a panacea and people who treat it as an existential threat to public safety.

What the IG can do is generate facts. That sounds small. It is not. Consider how much of what we know about the NYPD's most controversial practices has come from outside the department: stop-and-frisk demographics from RAND and the New York Civil Liberties Union, the Demographics Unit from the Associated Press, quota allegations from a handful of whistleblower officers willing to risk their careers. The department's own data is famously hard to obtain. FOIL requests drag on for years. The Inspector General's office, with statutory access, could produce the kind of routine, granular, public reporting that allows informed democratic debate. That alone is worth the cost.

What the IG cannot do is substitute for political accountability. The commissioner serves at the pleasure of the mayor; the mayor faces the voters. If New Yorkers want a different policing philosophy, they have to elect a different mayor. The IG is a tool for making that choice an informed one, not a substitute for making it.

I also want to push back gently on the strongest version of the pro-NYPD argument — that crime is down, therefore criticism is unwarranted. The decline in crime is real and impressive, but the causal claim that it required 686,000 stops a year in 2011, up from 97,000 in 2002, is not well established. Crime fell during periods when stops were far lower, and crime continued to fall in 2012 even as stops dropped sharply. The argument that aggressive stops are the load-bearing element of New York's safety story is an article of faith at One Police Plaza, not a finding established by evidence. An IG with the capacity to test such claims rigorously would be valuable precisely because it could move policing debates from theology to data.

Finally, on the chain-of-command worry: every major institution operates under some form of independent audit, from public companies to universities to military commands. The idea that policing alone is too operationally delicate to bear scrutiny is not credible. An IG done thoughtfully — independent, well-resourced, restrained in scope — is a modest correction to a power imbalance that has grown too large. New York should adopt it, and other large cities should follow.

An IG with civil liberties expertise could address those questions openly rather than letting them fester in litigation.

What an IG should not do is interfere with patrol operations, counterterrorism tactics, or active investigations. The structural design matters enormously. The inspector general should be appointed by the mayor with confirmation by the City Council, serve a fixed term, and have statutory access to department records and personnel. Critically, the office should be prohibited from investigating individual complaints — that must remain the CCRB's function.

This approach addresses the legitimate oversight concerns without creating the operational paralysis that critics rightly fear.

Behind the debate over an NYPD inspector general lies a deeper constitutional question that the city has not adequately grappled with: who watches the watchers?

The NYPD is the largest municipal police department in the country, with a budget in the billions and a mandate that touches every neighborhood in the city. Its stop-and-frisk practices, which resulted in hundreds of thousands of stops annually with a vast majority producing no arrest or summons, have been challenged in federal court as discriminatory. Its surveillance of Muslim communities has been documented in detail by the Associated Press. These are not allegations — they are documented practices. And they have proceeded with essentially no independent oversight.

The constitutional problem is structural. When the police commissioner reports to the mayor, and the mayor has a political stake in demonstrating effectiveness, there is a structural incentive to resist scrutiny of the most aggressive and visible tactics. That is not a character flaw in any particular official — it is the predictable result of how the system is designed. An independent inspector general corrects that structural problem.

I am also skeptical of the claim that existing mechanisms are sufficient. The CCRB can investigate individual complaints but cannot examine systemic patterns. Federal oversight is episodic and politically variable. State oversight through the attorney general is reactive. None of these mechanisms provides the ongoing, proactive, technically sophisticated review that a modern police department requires.

The 2013 mayoral race has put this issue front and center. That is healthy. New Yorkers should demand an answer to a simple question: if the NYPD's policies are fair and constitutional, why should the department fear independent review? If they are not, why would we not create the mechanisms to find out?

When we debate police oversight, we need to talk about what kind of democracy we want. Not merely what kind of policing we want — that framing already concedes too much ground to those who treat oversight as a threat to safety rather than a feature of accountable government.

An independent inspector general for the NYPD is not an anti-police proposal. It is a pro-constitution proposal. The Fourth Amendment requires that seizures and searches be based on individualized suspicion, not quotas, neighborhood demographics, or religious identity. The First Amendment requires that government not target religious practice preemptively. These are not negotiable values that police commanders can trade against crime rates. They are the floor below which no government action may fall.

The stop-and-frisk data tells a story that the department has been reluctant to acknowledge publicly. Black and Latino New Yorkers are stopped at rates dramatically disproportionate to any crime statistics. Most stops produce no contraband and result in no enforcement action. The program has generated hundreds of thousands of minor interactions between police and residents — interactions that, whatever their legality, shape how those residents experience their own city. That is a civil rights issue regardless of whether every individual stop met the legal threshold.

The Muslim surveillance program compounds these concerns. Surveillance of religious communities based on their religion and where they worship is exactly the kind of surveillance that the Constitution was designed to prevent.

An inspector general provides the institutional capacity to enforce constitutional standards that the political process has failed to enforce. That is not a radical idea. It is a conservative one — conservative in the sense of preserving the constitutional order that gives policing its legitimacy in the first place.

The practical case for an NYPD inspector general rests on one observation that should end the debate: the department has an inherent conflict of interest when it investigates itself.

This is not a theoretical concern. The NYPD has faced repeated findings of discriminatory or unconstitutional practices — from stop-and-frisk to the Demographics Registry targeting Muslim New Yorkers — and in each case, the investigation was conducted internally or by entities under the department's influence. The results have been predictable: patterns of concern have been minimized, statistics have been questioned, and constitutional questions have been reframed as tactical disagreements.

An independent inspector general changes the incentive structure. When an external office has jurisdiction to examine practices, the department has reason to self-correct before the external review forces the issue. The prospect of embarrassment before an independent investigator creates accountability that internal review cannot replicate.

The counterargument — that inspectors general create bureaucracy and interfere with operations — deserves a more precise response. Effective IG models, such as those at the federal level, are designed to avoid exactly those problems. They focus on systemic review, not individual case management. They have expertise in law and policy, not patrol tactics. They produce recommendations, not operational directives.

More fundamentally, the objection to an IG on efficiency grounds has it backwards. Unchecked policing produces litigation costs, settlements, court orders, and lasting damage to community trust. The cost of the Floyd lawsuit and similar cases runs into the hundreds of millions of dollars. An effective IG would likely pay for itself many times over by identifying problems before they become legal liabilities.

Efficiency and accountability are not in tension. They are complements. The city should create the inspector general position and let that be the argument.
