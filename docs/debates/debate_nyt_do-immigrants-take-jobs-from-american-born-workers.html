Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The claim that immigrants "take" jobs from American-born workers treats the labor market as a fixed pie, which most economists reject. Immigrants are not just workers; they are consumers, renters, entrepreneurs and taxpayers whose spending generates demand for additional labor. The Kauffman Foundation has documented that immigrants start businesses at roughly twice the rate of the native-born, and Census data show foreign-born workers are overrepresented at both the top (engineers, physicians) and bottom (agriculture, home health, hospitality) of the occupational ladder—precisely the segments where native-born labor supply is thinnest.

That said, David Frum is pointing at something real. The bulk of post-2008 employment gains have indeed gone disproportionately to foreign-born workers, and George Borjas's research has long suggested that less-skilled natives—particularly workers without high school diplomas and African-American men—face measurable wage pressure from immigration, on the order of several percentage points over the long run. David Card's studies of the Mariel boatlift and other natural experiments find smaller effects, but the disagreement is over magnitude, not direction at the very bottom of the skill distribution.

The honest answer, then, is "mostly no, but with an important exception." For the economy as a whole and for most native workers, immigration is a net positive: it raises GDP, complements native skills, slows the fiscal squeeze from an aging population, and creates rather than destroys jobs on net. For the shrinking minority of Americans competing directly with low-skilled newcomers, the effect can be genuinely harmful, and dismissing their concerns as nativism is both unfair and politically counterproductive.

The policy response is not to halt immigration but to tilt it toward higher skills, enforce labor standards that prevent undercutting, and invest seriously in adjustment assistance and education for displaced natives.

The image of immigrants “stealing” jobs from native-born Americans is an enduring political trope, but the reality, as most economists understood it by early 2015, is far more nuanced. David Frum’s observation that post-recession job growth has disproportionately benefited lower-paid immigrants is an important data point, yet it risks confusing a snapshot with the full picture. The labor market is not a fixed pie from which one group’s gain is necessarily another’s loss. While there is evidence of localized and sector-specific pressure, the broad consensus holds that the threat to American economic stability has been exaggerated, and that immigration’s overall effect on native employment is small and often positive over the long run.

The core of the economic argument rests on the fact that immigrants are not simply clones of American workers. They tend to cluster in occupations at both the high and low ends of the skill spectrum, often filling roles that complement rather than compete with the native-born. Research by Giovanni Peri and others has shown that an influx of less-educated immigrants can push native workers toward jobs requiring communication and coordination skills—supervisory or sales roles that are harder to automate and pay better. In this view, immigrants take the manual-intensive jobs, while native-born workers shift into positions that leverage their language and local knowledge, creating a more efficient division of labor.

However, this benign narrative does not apply uniformly. The work of George Borjas has consistently demonstrated that for the most vulnerable native workers—particularly high school dropouts and previous cohorts of immigrants—large inflows of low-skilled labor can depress wages and reduce employment opportunities in the short term. This is precisely the dynamic Frum highlights with his post-recession job growth figures. In a sluggish recovery that was already marked by weak demand and stagnant wages for the working class, it is plausible that native-born workers without a diploma faced stiffer competition for a shrinking pool of entry-level positions, from hospitality to construction. These workers bear the adjustment costs that broader national statistics often mask.

Yet, it is a mistake to attribute these hardships solely to immigration. The 2008 recession accelerated structural changes—automation, the decline of unionization, the offshoring of manufacturing—that had been eroding middle-skill jobs for decades. Immigrants, often more mobile and willing to accept lower wages and erratic schedules, were simply better positioned to snap up the precarious, service-sector jobs that the recovery produced. The problem, then, is less that immigrants are taking jobs that American-born workers are lining up to do, and more that the economy is not generating enough decent, stable employment for anyone, native or foreign-born. Framing this as a zero-sum conflict distracts from the deeper policy challenge of ensuring that aggregate growth translates into broad-based prosperity.

Rather than displacing Americans, this influx of labor allows domestic businesses to survive and expand, which in turn generates higher-level managerial, sales, and administrative jobs typically filled by native-born citizens.

This is not to say that labor competition is entirely nonexistent. Some economic studies indicate a slight downward pressure on wages for native-born Americans without a high school diploma. However, this localized effect is heavily outweighed by the broader economic benefits of immigration, including increased innovation, business formation, and lower prices for consumer goods. The genuine threats to working-class economic stability today are deep and structural: increasing automation, the decline of collective bargaining, and the broader forces of globalization. While David Frum's recent analysis deserves serious attention, conflating short-term post-recession dynamics with long-term structural effects risks misdiagnosing what is actually a more nuanced phenomenon.

Economists across the ideological spectrum have largely agreed that immigration expands the overall economy by increasing labor supply, entrepreneurship, and consumer demand. During cyclical downturns, any employment gains may appear concentrated among specific groups, but this reflects matching dynamics rather than displacement. Undocumented workers, often willing to accept less favorable terms, are frequently among the last hired and first fired. Their participation in recovery sectors—construction, food service, hospitality—bears little resemblance to the employment patterns of displaced manufacturing workers in the Midwest, whose losses stem primarily from automation and globalization.

That said, legitimate wage pressures exist in specific labor markets where less-skilled native workers and immigrants directly compete. The empirical evidence suggests these effects are modest and concentrated among recent arrivals to the country rather than established workers. Effective policy addresses such concerns through targeted workforce development rather than blanket restriction.

The framing of this question—as a binary between immigrants either taking jobs or not—obscures the real policy choices involved. Immigration, like trade, generates winners and losers. Acknowledging those losers does not require abandoning the substantial economic benefits that controlled immigration provides, nor does it justify the scapegoating that nativist framing encourages.

The Frum Hypothesis Deserves a Serious Hearing

David Frum's analysis touches a nerve precisely because the data are difficult to dismiss. Since the recession officially ended in 2009, a disproportionate share of net job growth has gone to foreign-born workers, even as labor force participation among native-born Americans—particularly men without college degrees—has continued its long slide. Whatever else this represents, it is not a picture of immigration costlessly complementing native labor.

The standard economist's rejoinder is the "lump of labor fallacy": there is no fixed number of jobs, immigrants create demand as well as supply, and in the long run wages adjust. All true—at the level of aggregates and decades. But the working-class American who lost a construction job in 2008 does not live in the long run. He lives in a specific labor market, in a specific region, competing for specific jobs. George Borjas of Harvard has shown repeatedly that when you disaggregate by skill and education, low-skill native workers—especially African-American men and workers without high school diplomas—experience measurable wage suppression from immigration of similarly skilled workers. The estimates aren't catastrophic, but they aren't trivial either, particularly stacked atop globalization and automation.

The H-1B program tells a parallel story at the high end. Tech firms insist they cannot find American engineers, yet wages in the relevant occupations have been remarkably flat. If a genuine shortage existed, prices would rise. The simpler explanation is that employers prefer workers tied to their visas.

None of this is an argument for closing the borders. Immigrants start businesses, fill genuine gaps, and on net almost certainly make the country richer. But "on net" obscures distribution. The gains from immigration flow heavily to immigrants themselves (rightly), to employers, and to consumers of immigrant-provided services. The costs are concentrated on workers who compete most directly with new arrivals—who happen to be among the most economically vulnerable Americans.

Honest immigration policy would acknowledge this trade-off rather than wave it away. A system that prioritized skills, enforced existing laws against employers, and modulated low-skill inflows according to labor-market conditions would capture most of immigration's benefits while protecting the workers least equipped to absorb its costs. Dismissing the question as xenophobic, as too much of the commentary does, only ensures that the political backlash—already visible—will grow uglier.

The Job-Theft Story Doesn't Survive Contact With Economics

The intuition that immigrants "take jobs" is powerful because it matches how an individual workplace feels: there are a limited number of slots, and if a newcomer fills one, a native doesn't. Extrapolated to a national economy, however, this intuition collapses. Economies are not fixed pies of employment. Immigrants buy groceries, rent apartments, pay for haircuts, and start businesses—all of which generate jobs. The very premise of Frum's framing, that we should worry when immigrants fill jobs, mistakes a flow for a zero-sum transfer.

Look at the actual evidence. The classic natural experiment is David Card's study of the Mariel boatlift, in which Miami's labor force expanded by 7 percent virtually overnight in 1980. Wages and employment for low-skilled native workers, including African-Americans, were essentially unaffected. Subsequent research—by Card, Giovanni Peri, and others—has repeatedly found that immigration's effect on native wages ranges from slightly negative to slightly positive, depending on the specification, but is consistently small. Peri's work suggests that immigrants and natives often specialize in different tasks, with immigrants doing manual work that frees natives to move into communication-intensive roles that pay more.

What about Frum's central statistic, that post-recession job growth has gone to the foreign-born? This is largely a demographic artifact. The native-born workforce is aging; baby boomers are retiring in enormous numbers, exiting employment counts. The foreign-born population skews younger and is still growing. When the labor force composition shifts, employment numbers shift with it. To call this "immigrants taking jobs" is to confuse accounting with causation.

The genuine problems of working-class Americans—stagnant wages, declining labor force participation, the hollowing-out of middle-skill occupations—have causes that long predate the recent immigration wave and that operate in countries with very different immigration profiles. Germany, with low immigration historically, and Canada, with proportionally more immigration than the United States, have both seen versions of these trends. The common thread is technology and trade, not the foreign-born.

Blaming immigrants is politically convenient because immigrants don't vote and make easy scapegoats. But the diagnosis is wrong, and the policy that follows from it—restriction—would harm the economy without meaningfully helping the workers it claims to protect. The honest response is to address wage stagnation directly: through education, labor standards, and redistribution, not border walls.

It's Not Immigrants. It's the Rules We've Built Around Them.

The question "do immigrants take jobs from Americans?" is poorly posed, because it treats immigration as a single phenomenon with a single effect. In reality, the labor-market impact of immigration depends almost entirely on the legal architecture surrounding it. A Salvadoran roofer working off the books, an Indian software engineer on an H-1B, a Filipino nurse on a green card, and a Canadian executive on an L-1 visa are not interchangeable inputs. They affect different labor markets in different ways, and our policy treats them inconsistently—often perversely.

Consider the undocumented workforce, roughly 8 million strong in the labor market. Their illegality is not a bug from the employer's perspective; it is a feature. Workers who cannot complain to OSHA, cannot unionize meaningfully, and cannot demand back wages without risking deportation are cheaper and more pliable than workers who can. This pushes down standards in construction, agriculture, meatpacking, and hospitality—industries where native workers once earned middle-class wages and now often do not. The harm here is real, but its source is the legal limbo, not the immigrants themselves. Legalization would do more to raise wages in these sectors than deportation ever could.

The H-1B program illustrates the same dynamic in a white-collar key. The visa ties the worker to a sponsoring employer, creating a captive labor pool that depresses wages and reduces mobility. Outsourcing firms have learned to game the lottery, importing workers specifically to replace American IT staff—as recent layoffs at Southern California Edison and elsewhere have shown. Again, the issue is not that talented foreigners come to America; it is that we've designed a program that hands employers leverage over them.

So when Frum points to post-recession job gains accruing to immigrants, the right response isn't "build a wall" or "open the borders." It's to ask which immigrants, in which legal status, in which industries—and what features of policy produced that pattern. Enforce labor standards against employers regardless of worker status. Legalize the long-settled undocumented. Replace employer-tethered visas with portable work authorization. Shift toward skill-based selection where the labor market genuinely needs it.

Immigration becomes a threat to working-class stability mainly when policy makes it one. Change the policy, and the supposed dilemma between welcoming immigrants and protecting native workers largely dissolves.
