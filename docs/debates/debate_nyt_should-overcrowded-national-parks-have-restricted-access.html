Should the Park Service more widely restrict access to protected areas, or does that contradict its mission to keep the lands open and accessible?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Organic Act of 1916 charges the Park Service with a famously awkward dual mandate: to provide for public enjoyment while leaving the parks "unimpaired" for future generations. When these goals collide—as they increasingly do at Yosemite Valley, Zion's Narrows, or the rim of the Grand Canyon—the statute itself tells us which side to favor. Enjoyment is the means; preservation is the end. A trampled meadow, a habituated bear, or an air-choked vista cannot be enjoyed by anyone, present or future.

That said, "restricted access" should not mean turning Americans away at the gate. The Park Service already has a sophisticated toolkit it should deploy more aggressively: timed-entry reservations (already used for Half Dome cables and Angels Landing-style permits at some sites), shuttle-only access into congested corridors as Zion pioneered in 2000, seasonal quotas for backcountry zones, and congestion pricing that nudges visitors toward shoulder seasons and lesser-known units. Of the 411 sites the agency manages, a handful absorb the bulk of the 307 million annual visits. Spreading demand is as important as capping it.

Equity concerns are real—reservation systems can favor the tech-savvy and the schedule-flexible—but they are solvable with lotteries, set-asides for walk-ups, and fee waivers. The greater inequity is allowing a few iconic landscapes to be loved into ruin while their statutory protectors shrug.

On its centennial, the Park Service should remember that "accessible" has never meant "unlimited." A park you cannot damage is the only park your grandchildren will inherit.

The National Park Service’s 1916 Organic Act established a notoriously difficult dual mandate: to conserve the nation’s natural scenery and wildlife, while simultaneously providing for public enjoyment. As the agency celebrates its centennial this year, this inherent tension has reached a breaking point, forcing a necessary debate over whether access to our most popular parks must be restricted.

The National Park Service was created in 1916 to preserve America's natural treasures "unimpaired for the enjoyment of future generations." That phrase contains a tension the agency has wrestled with for a century, but on this centennial we should be honest: when "enjoyment" actively damages what is being preserved, preservation must come first. Consider what overcrowding actually does. At Zion, the shuttle line for Angels Landing can stretch hours, and hikers trample fragile cryptobiotic soils to escape the queue. At Yellowstone, bison jams cause accidents and habituate wildlife to human proximity, which gets animals killed. At Yosemite, the Merced River has been so degraded by foot traffic and informal camping that the Park Service has spent decades and millions trying to restore it. The damage from a single overloaded weekend can take years to heal.

We already accept access restrictions everywhere else in American life — concert venues sell out, restaurants take reservations, the Statue of Liberty caps daily visitors. Yet we treat national parks as if they have infinite carrying capacity. They do not. The wilderness permit systems at parks like Rocky Mountain and the lottery for Half Dome cables show that rationing works. Visitors plan ahead, the experience improves, and the resource is protected.

The fairness objection — that parks belong to all Americans — actually cuts the other way. Right now, access is rationed by who can afford a hotel near Jackson Hole in July, who can take time off midweek, who can stomach a six-hour traffic jam. A reservation or lottery system is more democratic than the current chaos, because it distributes access by rule rather than by wealth and luck.

None of this means locking people out. It means timed entries at the most pressured sites, shuttle-only access in fragile corridors, and aggressive investment in the lesser-known 400-plus units of the system. Great Basin and North Cascades are achingly beautiful and nearly empty. The Park Service should be a traffic engineer as much as an interpreter, steering people across the calendar and the map. The alternative — loving Yosemite Valley to death in its second century — is not preservation at all.

The debate over restricting access to overcrowded parks is usually framed as a values clash — preservation versus access — but it's more useful to think of it as a design problem. What instruments do we have, and which ones produce the best outcomes at the lowest cost to the Park Service's dual mandate?

Outright caps are blunt. They protect resources but ration by luck or by who refreshes the reservation page at 7 a.m. Mountain time. Entrance fee hikes are also blunt: they ration by income, which is a poor proxy for who values the park or who will treat it well. Neither tool, used alone, is satisfying.

A smarter approach uses several instruments together. First, timed-entry reservations at the genuine pressure points — Arches in midsummer, Zion Canyon, the Yosemite Valley floor on holiday weekends. This is what Muir Woods did when it implemented its parking reservation system, and the early reports suggest it has reduced chaos without meaningfully cutting visitation. Second, mandatory shuttles in fragile corridors, which the Park Service has used successfully at Zion since 2000. Third, congestion pricing — higher fees on peak days, lower or zero fees on shoulder seasons and at underused parks. Fourth, real investment in the 350 units that aren't Yellowstone or Yosemite, so demand spreads.

Notice what this package does not require: closing parks, banning anyone, or abandoning the access mission. It just reflects what every transportation planner, utility, and airline knows — that peak demand has to be managed, and price and reservation signals work better than queues and damage.

The harder question is governance. The Park Service is chronically underfunded; its maintenance backlog runs into the billions. Without resources, even the best-designed access policy will fail at the implementation stage. Congress has consistently treated the agency as a beloved photo opportunity rather than a serious land-management enterprise. Until that changes, debates about whether to restrict access are arguing over deck-chair placement.

So yes, restrict access — selectively, transparently, with clear evidence of resource harm, and as part of a broader management package. The dichotomy of "open to all" versus "closed off" is a false one. The real choice is between thoughtful management and continued degradation by default.

The overcrowding crisis facing America's national parks this Memorial Day is a textbook example of the tragedy of the commons. When a highly desirable resource is vastly underpriced, demand will naturally outstrip supply, leading to congestion, degradation, and a diminished experience for everyone.
