Protecting Student Privacy in Online Learning

Schools are increasingly using online learning technology. Advocates tout its use in tailoring lessons to each child’s pace and ability as a revolution in education.

But privacy advocates have warned that the vast amounts of personal data students generate with the products can be misused. California, for instance, is set to pass the first law prohibiting companies from selling students’ personal information or using it for marketing purposes.

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The question is framed as a yes-or-no, but the more useful answer is: it depends entirely on who holds the data, what they do with it, and what consent looks like. Collecting data to help a fifth-grader master fractions is not the same as building a behavioral dossier that follows her into a credit application a decade later.

California's SB 1177 gets the framing right. It targets specific harms — selling student information, advertising to minors, building noneducational profiles — rather than treating all data collection as toxic. That distinction matters. Adaptive learning genuinely requires telemetry: keystrokes, response times, error patterns. Banning that would gut the technology's pedagogical premise.

The real problem is that K-12 students cannot meaningfully consent, and parents are presented with click-through agreements written for corporate convenience. FERPA, drafted in 1974, was not designed for cloud vendors aggregating data across millions of children, and the 2011 amendments arguably loosened it further. The inBloom collapse earlier this year showed that parents will revolt when they sense their kids being data-mined, even for ostensibly benign purposes.

So the collection itself isn't the invasion; the absence of enforceable limits on secondary use is. The right policy posture is purpose-limitation, deletion timelines, and audit rights — not a retreat from personalized learning altogether.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The promise of personalized learning — tailoring instruction to each student's pace and needs — depends on collecting data. That data can be as granular as a child’s keystrokes, reading speed, or specific wrong answers. When used ethically, it can illuminate where a student struggles and help a teacher intervene. So the question isn’t whether collecting data is itself an invasion; it’s whether the current ecosystem makes it one.

The unease stems from a lack of transparency and control. Once that information leaves the classroom, it can end up in hands that have no educational interest — marketers, data brokers, or even future employers. The 2014 implosion of inBloom, a Gates Foundation–backed nonprofit meant to store and share student data across states, is instructive. Parents and privacy advocates erupted over the prospect of a centralized vault holding sensitive information, and the venture folded under public pressure. That backlash wasn’t irrational; it was a response to a system where consent was shallow and lines of accountability were blurred. Federal student privacy law, chiefly FERPA, was written in the era of file cabinets and does little to restrict how education-technology companies use the data they gather from students online.

California’s newly passed legislation, which bars companies from selling students’ personal information or targeting them with ads based on school-issued devices, is a rational step. It doesn’t ban data collection; it bans the misuse that turns collection into a violation. The core problem isn’t the digital footprint itself — it’s that students and families have been left with no easy way to know what is collected, who holds it, and for what purpose. Without meaningful opt-outs and strict use-limits, the default becomes a kind of compulsory surveillance that many would indeed call an invasion. Until federal law catches up and forces ed-tech firms to be stewards of data rather than profiteers, the answer will remain, for too many families, a lamentable yes.

Collecting data in schools is not automatically an invasion of privacy. Teachers have always collected information: grades, attendance, reading levels, disciplinary records, notes on learning needs. Digital tools can make some of that information more useful, especially for students who need extra practice, faster advancement or early intervention.

But the scale and character of online learning data make the privacy question fundamentally different. A worksheet forgotten in a desk is not the same as a permanent, searchable profile containing keystrokes, mistakes, behavior patterns, disabilities, family information and predictive labels. Children cannot meaningfully consent to this collection, and families often cannot opt out without opting out of school itself. That makes the school’s duty especially strong.

The danger is not only identity theft or sloppy security, though those are real. It is also secondary use: marketing to children, selling profiles, sharing data with vendors beyond the educational purpose, or allowing algorithmic judgments made in elementary school to follow a student for years. FERPA and COPPA were not designed for the current ecosystem of cloud platforms, apps and subcontractors. The collapse of inBloom showed that public trust can disappear quickly when parents feel data systems are being built around them rather than with them.

The right standard is simple: collect only what is necessary, use it only for education, keep it only as long as needed, protect it rigorously, and make practices transparent to parents and students. Companies should not own student data, sell it or use it for advertising. With those limits, educational technology can serve children. Without them, it turns schooling into surveillance.

Yes, the routine collection of student data is an invasion of privacy—but the more pressing question is whose privacy is most at risk and what we should do about it.

Affluent families have options. If they distrust a district's ed-tech platform, they can complain at school board meetings, hire lawyers, transfer to private schools, or supplement at home. Lower-income families generally cannot. Their children are most likely to be funneled through "personalized learning" software as a substitute for teacher attention, precisely because such tools are cheaper than reducing class sizes. The privacy costs of ed-tech, in other words, fall disproportionately on the students with the least leverage to refuse.

This matters because the data being collected is not neutral. Behavioral analytics can flag children as struggling, distractible, or "at risk" in ways that shape how teachers and future schools see them. A label assigned by an algorithm in third grade can shadow a student through her entire educational career. For kids who already face stereotyping based on race or class, adding an algorithmic layer of judgment is not progress; it is amplification.

California's bill is welcome, and other states should follow. But prohibiting the sale of data is the floor, not the ceiling. We need rules about data minimization (collect only what is needed for the immediate educational task), retention limits (delete after the school year unless there is specific reason to keep it), transparency (parents should be able to see exactly what is collected and inferred about their children), and portability (families should be able to take data with them or have it destroyed).

So yes, this is an invasion of privacy. But it is also an equity issue, and treating it only as a matter of individual rights misses how unevenly the costs and benefits of educational surveillance are distributed.

Let's resist the temptation to answer this question with a confident yes or no. "Invasion of privacy" is a phrase that does too much work and not enough thinking.

Consider three different things that all get called "student data." First, there is information directly tied to instruction: which problems a child got wrong, where she paused while reading. Second, there is information about the child that the platform happens to collect: location, device, time of use, social interactions with classmates. Third, there is derived information: inferences and predictions the algorithm generates about the child's abilities, attention, or future performance.

These categories raise very different concerns. The first is hard to object to in principle; teachers have always kept such records. The second has nothing inherent to do with education and should probably be off-limits. The third is the most troubling because it can feel objective while embedding assumptions that the child and her parents never see. A reasonable privacy framework would treat these tiers differently rather than lumping them together under one alarming label.

The California legislation gestures at this kind of distinction by focusing on commercial uses—selling data, marketing to students—rather than banning collection outright. That is sensible. The harm of a textbook publisher quietly building advertising profiles of seven-year-olds is concrete. The "harm" of a math program tracking which exercises a student found difficult is, on its face, just teaching.

Where I would push regulators is on the second and third categories: limit ancillary collection, require disclosure and human review of algorithmic judgments, and give families meaningful access to whatever profile a vendor has built. Done well, this preserves the genuine pedagogical value of digital tools while curbing the practices that look like surveillance regardless of how they are dressed up. The right answer is neither outrage nor complacency, but careful line-drawing.
