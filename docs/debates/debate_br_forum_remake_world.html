Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Walter Johnson's essay accomplishes something important and, for that reason, deserves the kind of friction that comes from taking it seriously rather than nodding along. His indictment of the "dehumanization" trope is largely persuasive, his insistence on the entanglement of slavery and capitalism is by now (and not coincidentally) the most generative line of inquiry in the field, and his summons to ground a theory of justice in the history of slavery and racial capitalism rather than in twentieth-century European catastrophe is exactly the kind of provocation American intellectual life needs. But the essay's strongest moves are sometimes also its most overdrawn, and the version of "racial capitalism" it leaves us with risks becoming a master concept that, like the liberal-humanist frame it displaces, does too much work too easily. Let me press on three points.

First, on dehumanization. Johnson is right that the word, taken literally, performs a kind of accidental violence: it suggests that enslavers managed (somehow) to extract or suspend the humanity of their captives, and that historians' principal task is to demonstrate that this humanity persisted, was restored, or was reclaimed. That framing turns black humanity into a perpetually probationary fact and turns slaveholding into an inhuman aberration rather than a recognizable human practice. Johnson's reminder that slavery depended on the human capacities of the enslaved—their intelligence, their fertility, their sentience, their capacity to suffer in legible ways—is essential, and he is right to point out that violence presupposed rather than negated the humanity of its objects. The torturer needs a witness.

Still, I would resist the conclusion that "dehumanization" is simply a category error to be discarded. The term names something real about the social and legal status that slaveholders sought to fix on the enslaved: a condition in which one's status as a moral or jural person was systematically denied even as one's biological and cognitive capacities were exploited. Orlando Patterson's "social death," for all its limits, gestures at this gap between what was extracted from enslaved people and what was conceded to them. Saidiya Hartman, whom Johnson surely has in mind, has shown how the law's selective recognition of slave humanity—its acknowledgment of criminal agency, for instance, alongside the denial of contract or kinship—was itself a technology of subjection. "Dehumanization" need not denote the metaphysical erasure of personhood; it can name the political project of producing a being whose humanity is exploited without being recognized. Johnson's critique is best read as a warning about lazy usage, not as a verdict that the concept has no purchase.

Second, on racial capitalism. Johnson, following Cedric Robinson and Du Bois, argues that there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery: Manchester did not happen without Mississippi. As an empirical claim about the cotton economy of the long nineteenth century, this is now hard to dispute; the work of Sven Beckert, Walter Johnson himself in *River of Dark Dreams*, Edward Baptist, and the contributors to *Slavery's Capitalism* has made James Oakes's counterfactual ("would Britain have industrialized without slavery?") look exactly as ahistorical as Johnson says it is. And Du Bois's collapsing of the orthodox firewall between slavery and capitalism in *Black Reconstruction* deserves its belated canonization.

But it is one thing to insist that the historical capitalism we actually had was constituted through racial slavery, settler expropriation, and colonial extraction; it is another to argue, as Robinson sometimes does and as Johnson seems to here, that capitalism is *constitutively* racial—that race and capital are inseparable in principle, not just in fact. The first claim is a historical thesis. The second is closer to a philosophical one, and it carries a heavy burden. If racial differentiation is intrinsic to capital accumulation, what do we make of capitalist economies whose hierarchies of expendability run along non-racial lines (caste, sect, region, citizenship)? Are these to be reabsorbed into "race" by definitional fiat, or do we need a more capacious account of how capital opportunistically seizes upon whatever lines of human difference are at hand? I suspect the answer is the latter, and I suspect Johnson would agree, but the rhetoric of his essay tends to elide the distinction. The danger is that "racial capitalism" becomes a name for everything bad about modernity, at which point it loses its analytical edge precisely because it has become a synonym for the world.

Third, on rights. Here I think Johnson moves too quickly. He follows Samuel Moyn in dating the contemporary regime of human rights to the late twentieth century and in seeing it as the ideological scaffolding of liberal neo-imperialism. He then proposes that justice be reconceived not through rights but through reparations, restorative justice, and a critique of racial capitalism. The six "virtues" he enumerates at the end of the essay are genuinely attractive: a Global South standpoint, attention to extraction and distribution, an emphasis on the historical sedimentation of present privilege, sensitivity to gender and social reproduction, a unified analysis of race and class, and a link between the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature. These are virtues worth wanting.

But what is the relationship between this program and the rights tradition? Johnson sometimes writes as if rights talk is to be jettisoned in favor of something deeper; at other times as if it is to be supplemented or radicalized. The Movement for Black Lives platform he cites, after all, is not anti-rights; it makes rights claims (to reparations, to investment, to economic justice) that rest on universalist premises even as they are anchored in the particular history of slavery and Jim Crow. The history of abolition itself—from David Walker to Frederick Douglass to Anna Julia Cooper—was not a history outside the rights tradition; it was a history of forcing that tradition to live up to commitments it had betrayed. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction*, which Johnson rightly celebrates, treats the postwar amendments as both an unfinished revolution and a usable inheritance. The question is whether we treat the language of rights as a thin veneer over racial capitalism (to be peeled away) or as a contested terrain on which redistributive and reparative claims can be lodged. The latter strikes me as both more historically faithful and more politically generative.

A final note. Johnson ends by warning, with Du Bois, that there are moments propitious for remaking the world, and that the cost of missing them is paid in the blood of subsequent generations. Writing in February 2017, with a new administration committed to nativism, deregulation, and the open celebration of white grievance, the warning lands. If the "new history of capitalism" is to do more than enrich our footnotes, it must connect its account of how the world was made to an argument about how it can be unmade. Johnson's essay is one of the more serious efforts to draw that connection. Its arguments deserve to be tested, not embraced—which is, I take it, what he would want.

Walter Johnson’s essay offers a necessary and provocative intervention, insisting that the history of slavery must be centered not only in how we understand capitalism but in how we imagine justice. His rejection of the language of “dehumanization” and his turn to racial capitalism as a framework for reparative demands are compelling. Yet I want to complicate two aspects of his argument, not to dismiss it but to strengthen its reach. First, while the word “dehumanization” can indeed function in the harmful ways Johnson identifies, that does not exhaust its critical possibilities. Second, the thoroughgoing dismissal of human rights discourse, built into his claim that the “culturally dominant notion of human rights” is unresponsive to slavery’s legacies, risks discarding a language that has, in fact, been mobilized by the global South and by movements for racial justice in ways that align with Johnson’s own commitments. A more nuanced engagement with both concepts can sharpen, rather than blunt, the radical edge he seeks.

Johnson is right to warn that the notion of “dehumanization” can slide into a patronizing posture. When historians present their work as “proving” the humanity of enslaved people, they imply that such humanity was ever genuinely in question, and they unwittingly set themselves up as arbiters of who counts as fully human. This move, he argues, is deeply tied to a liberal politics of recognition that equates humanity with the exercise of will and choice, thereby alienating enslaved people from the historical contexts of their own actions. But the concept of dehumanization need not carry this baggage if understood not as an ontological claim about enslaved people’s actual selves, but as a description of a social and ideological process. To say that slavery dehumanized is to say that the institution systematically denied enslaved people the status of full persons in law, economy, and social life, and that this denial required an ideology that cast them as less than human. This is not the same as claiming that enslaved people internalized that denial or lost their inherent capacities; it is to name a political strategy of domination. Indeed, the very violence Johnson cites—swinging a baby against a post, jamming a hoe handle down a throat—was not made possible by a prior successful “dehumanization” that turned perpetrators into unseeing monsters. Rather, it was enabled by a culture that encouraged the perpetrator to regard the victim as an object of power, a person whose suffering could be ignored or even savored precisely because their humanity was recognized but derogated. The term dehumanization remains useful for capturing the ways that recognition of human sentience is twisted into a tool of terror—a point Johnson himself makes when he insists that slaveholders depended on the violated slave’s capacity to “bear witness.” That capacity is profoundly human, and the attempt to crush it while relying on it is a distinctive form of wrong that we might still call dehumanization, so long as we grasp that it refers to an active, ongoing social relationship, not a permanent alteration of the victim’s being.

This reconceptualization also addresses historians’ anxieties. When Philip Morgan writes that slaves “strove . . . to preserve their humanity,” he no longer needs to be read as suggesting that their humanity was a fragile thing that could be annihilated by masters. Instead, he records a struggle against a regime that constantly attacked their standing as human persons. To describe that regime as dehumanizing is not to doubt that the struggle was waged by human beings; it is to name what they struggled against. The problem, then, is less with the word itself than with the liberal framework that isolates a narrow notion of agency as the sole proof of humanity. Johnson’s own positive vision—rooted in the black radical tradition and attentive to the reproductive, ecological, and spatial dimensions of racial capitalism—actually furnishes a richer account of enslaved humanity without needing to abandon all talk of dehumanization as a harmful process inflicted upon them.

This brings me to the question of human rights. Johnson, drawing on Samuel Moyn’s periodization, argues that modern human rights discourse emerged from twentieth-century European history and has been structurally blind to the legacies of slavery, even serving as a vehicle for liberal neo-imperialism. In its place, he proposes a justice grounded in the history of racial capitalism, outlined through six virtues that emphasize redistribution, reparations, reproductive justice, and the inseparability of race and class. I share much of this vision, but I am unconvinced that it requires a clean break from the language of rights. By 2017, it is well established that the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and subsequent treaties were not simply a Western imposition; they were shaped by anticolonial leaders and critical voices from the Global South. The African Charter on Human and Peoples’ Rights (1981), for instance, codified collective rights to self-determination, development, and a satisfactory environment—rights that directly echo Johnson’s call for a justice attentive to extraction, empire, and the instrumentalization of nature. The Declaration on the Right to Development (1986) explicitly tied individual rights to the restructuring of global economic relations. These instruments emerged from the very histories of enslavement, colonization, and dispossession that Johnson highlights, and they represent an attempt to infuse rights discourse with the substance of historical repair. They are not, of course, unproblematic or fully realized, but they demonstrate that the “culturally dominant notion” Johnson critiques is only one version of human rights—and not the only one available to movements for racial justice.

Moreover, the Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson approvingly cites is shot through with rights claims. Its demands for an end to police violence, for full employment, for quality education, and for reparations are articulated in a language that combines moral urgency with legal and political assertion. Reparations, at its core, is a claim of right: the right to have past wrongs acknowledged and materially redressed. This is not the thin, procedural rights of classical liberalism that Marx critiqued, but a substantive, redistributive rights framework that arises from the specific experience of chattel slavery and its afterlives. Johnson’s own outline of a slavery-grounded justice implicitly relies on normative principles—the wrongness of natal alienation, the claim to land and resources, the refusal of racial hierarchy—that are, in effect, rights claims by another name. To call them “virtues” rather than “rights” may be a tactical choice to avoid cooptation, but it risks ceding the powerful mobilizing terrain of rights to those who would narrow its meaning.

The black radical tradition itself has never been uniformly hostile to rights talk. W. E. B. Du Bois, whom Johnson rightly places at the center of racial capitalism analysis, spoke of the “right to work,” the “right to culture,” and the “right to be a man,” even while exposing the hypocrisy of a nation that denied those rights to black people. C. L. R. James wrestled with the dialectic of formal rights and material inequality, and Cedric Robinson, in *Black Marxism*, did not abandon the language of recognition and justice so much as he demanded that it be rooted in the collective historical experience of black people. The alternative to a hollow universalism is not to abandon the universal, but to rebuild it from the ground up, taking seriously Du Bois’s insight that the problems of the twentieth century are those of the “color line” and using that standpoint to generate a genuinely inclusive conception of human flourishing. In that sense, Johnson’s six virtues can be seen as a powerful contribution to the ongoing reconstruction of human rights, not a rejection of them.

A justice rooted in slavery and racial capitalism must indeed go beyond the minimalist protections of liberal internationalism. It must encompass economic redistribution, gender and reproductive justice, ecological reparation, and the dismantling of racialized borders. But framing these demands in the language of rights—understood as collective and historically informed—can connect them to existing legal and political struggles globally, building solidarities that cross the North-South divide Johnson correctly highlights. The risk of a purely oppositional stance is that it leaves the field of rights to be defined by the very forces that produced racial capitalism in the first place. Far better to occupy and transform that field, insisting that human rights must mean the right to repair, the right to land, the right to recognition on terms that do not presuppose a neutral bourgeois subject. This is not the final form of human emancipation Marx spoke of, but it is a necessary step within the existing world order, a step that the black radical tradition has long been taking. Johnson’s essay is an indispensable map of the ground we must cover; the journey there will need all the tools we have, including a dehumanization concept that names the ongoing process of racial abjection and a rights discourse that has learned from those who survived the hold of the slave ship and built worlds in the quarters.

Walter Johnson begins his profound and challenging essay with a provocation that demands our immediate attention: the claim that the term “dehumanization,” when applied to the history of slavery, is misleading, harmful, and ought to be resisted. For many who study, teach, and mourn the history of chattel slavery, this assertion is startling. The concept of dehumanization has long served as our primary moral vocabulary for naming the specific, unique horror of reducing a human being to property. Yet Johnson insists that this linguistic habit inadvertently grants slaveholders too much power, separating the “inhuman” violence of slavery from the entirely human capacities upon which that system relied.

Johnson’s essay is a vital, paradigm-shifting intervention that brilliantly resurrects the framework of racial capitalism—pioneered by Cedric Robinson and W. E. B. Du Bois—to map the intertwined material realities of white supremacy and global capitalist development. However, his rapid dismissal of “dehumanization” and his deep skepticism toward the discourse of human rights risk discarding the very moral vernacular that marginalized people have historically utilized to demand justice. As we stand in early 2017, confronting a resurgent white nationalism and a profoundly unequal global economy, we desperately need Johnson’s material critique of racial capitalism. But realizing his ambitious vision for justice requires us to radically redefine, rather than entirely abandon, the language of human dignity and rights.

Let us first examine Johnson’s critique of dehumanization. He argues that slaveholders did not need to blind themselves to the humanity of the enslaved in order to torture, exploit, or commodify them. On the contrary, slavery was entirely predicated on the human attributes of the enslaved: their sentience, their intelligence, their capacity for fear, and crucially, their reproductive potential. To say that slaves were “dehumanized,” Johnson suggests, is to imply an ontological alienation—a literal loss of humanity—that renders black humanity intellectually probationary and centers the moral feelings of the observer over the lived reality of the enslaved.

This is a powerful historiographic corrective, but it perhaps misreads the vernacular function of the word “dehumanization.” When descendants of the enslaved, activists, and critical scholars describe slavery as dehumanizing, they are rarely suggesting that enslaved people literally lost their human essence or that slaveholders operated under a genuine cognitive delusion regarding the biological humanity of their captives. Rather, they are naming a legal, social, and political project of violent exclusion. Dehumanization, in this sense, is not an ontological state achieved by the oppressor; it is the enforced deprivation of the *conditions* of humanity—the stripping away of legal personhood, bodily autonomy, and social standing.

When the law defines a person as chattel, capable of being mortgaged, insured, and inherited alongside livestock and machinery, it is engaging in a process of dehumanization. The fact that this project was a legal fiction, and that the slaveholder intimately knew the enslaved person was human, does not make the term “dehumanization” inaccurate; it simply highlights the grotesque hypocrisy and calculated malice of the slave system. If we abandon the concept entirely, we risk losing the specific vocabulary necessary to distinguish the unique moral injury of chattel slavery from other severe forms of labor exploitation. We can acknowledge, as Johnson rightly demands, that slavery relied on human sentience and reproduction, without sacrificing the language that condemns the systemic reduction of a person to a price tag.

This debate over language bleeds into Johnson’s broader critique of human rights. Drawing on Samuel Moyn’s timeline, which locates the ascendance of modern human rights in the mid-twentieth century, and Karl Marx’s critique of bourgeois liberal rights, Johnson argues that the dominant human rights framework is untethered from the history of slavery. Because contemporary human rights discourse arose largely in response to the European atrocities of the twentieth century, it often functions as a vehicle for liberal neo-imperialism—an “imaginary universality” that masks ongoing material inequalities and the enduring legacies of colonialism.

Johnson’s skepticism of this dominant framework is well-founded. A human rights regime that focuses solely on the political freedoms of the individual—speech, conscience, democratic participation—while ignoring the deeply racialized global maldistribution of wealth is woefully inadequate. But here, too, we must be careful not to throw out the baby with the bathwater. The Black radical tradition, which Johnson so compellingly centers, has a long history of engaging with and subverting the language of rights.

From the Haitian Revolution to the colored conventions of the nineteenth century, to the civil rights struggles of the mid-twentieth century, marginalized people have frequently seized the rhetoric of universal rights—not as a naive endorsement of classical liberalism, but as a powerful crowbar to pry open closed systems of power. They understood that the universality claimed by the Enlightenment was a hypocritical fiction, but they wielded that fiction to demand material, structural transformation. Even the Movement for Black Lives, whose 2016 platform Johnson praises for its demand for reparations, frames many of its material demands within a broader appeal to the fundamental right to life, dignity, and self-determination. Walter Johnson's essay performs a series of intellectual gestures that deserve to be examined separately, because they do not all travel together as easily as he suggests. Whether labor is alienated by sale of labor-power or by sale of the laborer is not a minor technical difference. It changes the dynamics of class formation, the politics of resistance, the temporal structure of exploitation, even the rate at which capital can respond to price signals. Walter Johnson's essay is so wide-ranging and so eloquent that any response risks pedantry. Where I want to push back is on his treatment of human rights — both as an analytical category in the historiography of slavery and as a political horizon for present-day struggles for justice. Johnson treats the liberal vocabulary of rights as a kind of trap. I think it remains one of the more powerful instruments we have, and that abandoning it is a mistake the left repeatedly makes to its cost.

Begin with Johnson's specific complaint about the historiography. He argues that historians who frame enslaved people's actions in terms of "agency," "independent will," and the like are anachronistically projecting the bourgeois liberal subject onto people whose actions were structured by quite different idioms. He cites Philip Morgan's *Slave Counterpoint* as an example. The criticism is not without bite. There is a tendency in some scholarship to treat any act of enslaved self-assertion as a vindication of universal liberal selfhood, as though the only meaningful question about the enslaved is whether they pass the test of resembling us.

But I think Johnson overstates the trap. The reason historians keep returning to questions of agency and humanity is not that they have been seduced by liberal anachronism. It is that the historical archive they work with — produced overwhelmingly by people who insisted, both legally and ideologically, on the non-personhood of the enslaved — generates an interpretive obligation. If your sources call a human being "wool" or "hand," and your discipline once took those sources at their face, then the work of demonstrating personhood is not paternalist; it is corrective. Johnson treats this work as if it placed black humanity "on probation." I think it is better described as releasing black humanity from the probation that the archive imposed upon it. The frame Johnson criticizes is the frame from which historians are escaping, not the frame in which they are stuck.

A similar point applies to the broader category of human rights. Johnson, following Moyn, dates the dominant contemporary regime of human rights to the mid-twentieth century, severs it from the longer history of antislavery, and treats it as the intellectual architecture of liberal neo-imperialism. There is something to this. The selective invocation of human rights as a pretext for military intervention is a real and ugly feature of the post-Cold War order. But to move from this critique to the suggestion that we should look elsewhere for the conceptual basis of justice — that the language of universal rights is fundamentally unresponsive to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery — is to give up too much.

Consider what rights actually do, at their best. They name a floor below which no human being is to be pushed, and they make that floor enforceable by institutions external to the immediate parties of any dispute. The reason the language of rights has been useful to enslaved and formerly enslaved people, again and again across the centuries, is not that it perfectly captures their experience or worldview. It is that it provides a vocabulary that can be addressed to those who hold power, in terms those powerholders are committed (however hypocritically) to recognizing. Frederick Douglass did not need to be reminded that the framers' invocation of universal rights was bourgeois, partial, and stained by complicity with slavery. He nevertheless saw, more clearly than many of his successors, that holding the bourgeois at his own word was the most effective lever available. The Civil Rights Movement made similar use of the same lever a century later.

Johnson would respond, I think, that rights-based reform produces only partial, fragile, and easily reversible gains, and that the deeper structures of racial capitalism remain untouched. The historical record gives this argument some weight. The dismantling of legal segregation has not produced economic parity. The formal end of slavery did not end the global pattern of racial extraction. Fair enough. But the question is whether the inadequacy of rights-based emancipation is grounds for moving past it, or grounds for taking it more seriously and pushing it further. Marx, whom Johnson quotes, did not argue that political emancipation should be discarded; he called it "a big step forward" and described it as the most complete form of emancipation available within "the hitherto existing world order." The implication is that you do not skip the step. You build on it.

I worry that Johnson's program — restorative justice and reparations rooted in a standpoint of the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed" — risks becoming a substitute for, rather than a supplement to, the slower work of universal rights enforcement. Reparations, to be politically effective, will need a framework of universal rights to operate within. The argument for reparations is fundamentally an argument about historical violations of rights that human beings hold simply by being human. Without that universal premise, the reparations claim collapses into either a claim of group entitlement (which is politically weaker) or a claim of historical particularity (which is politically narrower). The strongest moral case for reparations runs through, not around, the universalist tradition Johnson treats as exhausted.

Consider the geography of human-rights mobilization over the past two decades — Tahrir Square, Hong Kong, the indigenous rights movements of Latin America, the Movement for Black Lives itself. None of these are content with the neo-imperial deployment of human rights by Western governments, but neither do they reject the underlying universalist vocabulary. They claim it, often more rigorously than the governments that nominally champion it. The vocabulary of rights is, at this moment in history, one of the few cross-cultural moral idioms with real political traction. To abandon it because it has been put to bad uses is to surrender one of the few weapons that consistently embarrasses the powerful.

I do not want to be misread as defending the neoliberal status quo, which has weaponized rights talk against substantive equality in any number of ways. Johnson is right that property rights, freedom of contract, and the like have done immense work in entrenching the inequalities he describes. The libertarian appropriation of rights discourse is a genuine problem. But the response is to fight for a richer conception of rights — one that includes the social and economic rights long recognized in international declarations but persistently weakened in domestic implementation — not to abandon the discourse to its libertarian appropriators.

So I read Johnson's essay as offering a powerful historical reframing that does not, in the end, require the political conclusion he draws. The history of racial capitalism is real, and any adequate response to it must reckon with the global patterns of extraction Johnson describes. But the moral and political resources for responding to those patterns include, ineliminably, the universal language of rights — not as the final form of human emancipation, but as the most powerful tool available within the world order we actually inhabit. The choice is not between rights and reparations. It is between a rights tradition that includes reparative obligations and one that does not. Johnson's history should be marshaled in service of the former, not against the rights tradition as such.

The Reproductive Hinge: What Johnson Gestures Toward and Does Not Quite Hold

Walter Johnson writes, near the heart of his essay, that "racial capitalism swung on a reproductive hinge." It is a striking phrase, and the paragraph it introduces — invoking Deborah Gray White on sexual violation, reproductive invigilation, and natal alienation as "elementary aspects of slavery, and thus of racial capitalism" — is one of the essay's most important. I want to praise this gesture and then to argue that Johnson does not quite carry through on what it implies. The reproductive analytic is mentioned, given a sentence or two, and then largely set aside. Among the six "principal virtues" of his proposed framework, gender appears as the fourth item, presented as if it were one ingredient in a recipe. But the reproductive hinge is not one ingredient. It is the hinge. And the moment we take it seriously, the architecture of the argument has to be rebuilt.

Let me first say what Johnson gets right, because it is a great deal. The political economy of the antebellum United States, after the closing of the international slave trade in 1808, was uniquely dependent on enslaved women's reproductive capacity. The expansion of cotton across the Deep South — the very expansion that built Manchester and Manhattan in the way Johnson describes — would have been impossible without the natural increase of the enslaved population. The internal slave trade that moved people from the Upper South to the cotton frontier was, at its base, a trade in the children of enslaved women. The legal doctrine of *partus sequitur ventrem* — that the status of the child follows that of the mother — quietly performed an enormous amount of economic work: it converted reproductive labor into capital accumulation. Johnson, by citing DeBow's proposal to reopen the Atlantic trade as an alternative to natural increase, registers this dynamic. But he does not develop it.

What does developing it look like? It looks, first, like taking seriously that the racial-capitalist accumulation Johnson describes is not analogous to gendered exploitation; it is constitutively gendered exploitation. The category of the slave was, from the eighteenth century forward, structured around a specifically feminine reproductive function that was simultaneously extracted (as labor power), commodified (as the production of new slaves), and rendered invisible (as "natural" increase). Hortense Spillers, working in a register Johnson does not quite enter, has argued that under slavery the very categories of "mother" and "father" are denied to the enslaved in ways that produce a distinctive kinship grammar — what she famously calls the "American grammar." The point is not merely that enslaved women were exploited as women; it is that the gendered structure of slavery generated a set of categories that have continued to structure black social life and its representation long after formal emancipation.

If we take this seriously, several of Johnson's framings need adjustment.

Consider his critique of the "dehumanization" thesis. Johnson is right that slavery depended on the human capacities of enslaved people — labor, sentience, sociality. But the reproductive analytic suggests that what slavery did was not dehumanize so much as ungender. Spillers's argument is that the violence of slavery produced a category — the "flesh," as distinct from the "body" — that lay outside the gendered hierarchies of the bourgeois social order, available for forms of violation and use that the bourgeois subject was supposed to be protected from. The enslaved woman was not simply a worker who happened to be female; she occupied a position that was neither the wage-laborer of orthodox Marxism nor the domestic woman of bourgeois ideology. She was something else, and that something else is what racial capitalism required. Johnson's argument that we should "use the history of slavery as the source rather than the subject of knowledge" applies with particular force here: a political economy reconstructed from the standpoint of the enslaved woman would look quite different from one reconstructed from the standpoint of the male field hand.

Consider, next, Johnson's account of the relationship between capital and labor. He observes, correctly, that the enslaved were both capital and labor at once — that their double economic aspect could not be separated. But the case is even stranger when we add reproduction. The enslaved woman was capital, labor, and the means of producing new capital-laborers. Her body was a productive site, a workplace, and a commodity, all at once and frequently in violent simultaneity. No category of orthodox political economy — neither Adam Smith's nor Marx's — can hold this configuration. To name this we need a vocabulary that thinks production and reproduction together, and that thinks both as racially structured. The work of black feminist scholars — Angela Davis in *Women, Race and Class*, Hazel Carby, Saidiya Hartman, Jennifer Morgan's *Laboring Women* — has been developing this vocabulary for decades. Johnson cites a few of these scholars, but the engagement is more nominal than substantive.

Consider, finally, Johnson's program for justice. The fourth of his principal virtues is that an account of justice rooted in racial capitalism would be "attentive to questions of gender and sexuality, on the ways that reproductive invigilation and natal alienation . . . were core features of the human wrongs of slavery." Yes. But what does this mean concretely? If reparations are to take the form Johnson gestures toward, they must reckon with harms that are not easily monetized: the destruction of kinship relations, the long history of medical experimentation on black women's reproductive bodies, the ongoing structures of differential maternal mortality, the criminalization of black motherhood, the carceral targeting of black families. These are not addenda to a reparations program. They are the spine of one. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson cites includes provisions that move in this direction — demands around reproductive justice, around the freeing of incarcerated black mothers, around the family policing system. The framework Johnson articulates needs to internalize these as central rather than as derivative.

There is one further consequence of the reproductive analytic that I want to draw out, because Johnson does not. The sixth of his principal virtues is the relationship between the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature. He frames this primarily in terms of resource extraction — the elephants whose teeth become billiard balls, the land whose fertility is mined out by the cotton crop. But the deepest connection between racial capitalism and ecological domination runs through reproduction. The plantation was an experiment in industrialized biological production — of cotton plants, of livestock, of human beings. The techniques of selective breeding, the optimization of yield, the management of bodies for productive output: these were developed on the plantation before they were generalized across modern agriculture and modern industry. Donna Haraway and others have begun to trace the lines from plantation biopower to contemporary techniques of biological management. The "instrumentalization of nature" Johnson invokes is not parallel to the instrumentalization of slaves; it grew out of it, and the connecting tissue is the reproductive management of bodies, human and otherwise.

I have been pressing Johnson, but I want to close by acknowledging that his essay is genuinely opening a door. The phrase "reproductive hinge" alone is worth a great deal. What I am urging is that the door be walked through with greater willingness to let the destination be reshaped by what is found on the other side. The tradition Johnson invokes — Du Bois, Robinson, James, Williams, Rodney, Davis — is in fact richer on these questions than his essay quite registers. Angela Davis's work in particular has long insisted that the analysis of capitalism cannot proceed without the analysis of reproduction, and that the racial structure of capitalism is inseparable from the gendered structure of reproductive labor. Johnson cites Davis, but more as a name in a list than as a presence in the argument.

The stakes are not merely academic. The form an account of justice takes determines what kinds of redress it makes thinkable. An account rooted in the labor of field hands tends toward redress in the currency of labor — jobs, wages, working conditions. An account rooted in the reproductive labor of enslaved women, and in the ongoing afterlives of natal alienation, tends toward redress in the currency of kinship, bodily autonomy, and the social conditions of mothering and care. We need both. But the second has been chronically subordinated to the first, in the scholarship and in the politics. If Johnson's essay is to live up to its own promise — if the history of slavery is really to be the source rather than the subject of knowledge — then the reproductive hinge has to be more than a phrase.

A Marxist Worries About Racial Capitalism

I write as someone who has spent a career working in a broadly Marxist tradition and who has watched, with a mixture of admiration and concern, the rise of "racial capitalism" as a master category in the new history of American slavery. Walter Johnson's essay is one of the most ambitious recent statements of this position, and it deserves a serious response from those of us who are not persuaded that the orthodox tools have been exhausted. My disagreements are not, I want to stress, with Johnson's politics, which I largely share. They are with the analytical move that subordinates the category of capital to the category of race, and with what I think is lost when we do so.

Let me start with what is right in Johnson's account. The claim that slavery and capitalism were historically entangled is correct and overdue. The empirical work of Beckert, Baptist, Bonnie Martin on slave-mortgaged credit, and Johnson's own *River of Dark Dreams* has made it impossible to maintain the older view in which slavery is a precapitalist or noncapitalist formation. Du Bois saw this in 1935 and we are still catching up. The integration of plantation, factory, and counting house into a single account of nineteenth-century accumulation is a real historiographic achievement.

What I am skeptical about is the further step that Johnson, following Robinson, takes: the claim that capitalism was, from the start, "racial capitalism," and that race is not a contingent feature of historical capitalism but a constitutive one. This is a strong metaphysical claim, and it has political implications I think need to be examined.

Consider, first, the actual argument in Robinson's *Black Marxism*. Robinson's claim is that European feudalism was already structured by what he calls "racialism" — hierarchical distinctions among Europeans (Slavs, Irish, Roma, Jews) that were articulated in proto-racial terms before the encounter with Africa. Capitalism, on this view, inherited racialism from feudalism and projected it outward in the age of conquest and slave trade. This is an interesting historical claim, but it has the peculiar effect of detaching "race" from the specific history of anti-black racism that the framework was developed to illuminate. If everything from medieval anti-Irish prejudice to nineteenth-century anti-black ideology counts as "racialism," then the category becomes too broad to do the work of differentiation that historical analysis requires. We end up with a thesis — capitalism is racialism all the way down — that can absorb any counterexample by reclassifying it as a variant of racialism.

The orthodox Marxist approach, by contrast, treats race as a historically specific ideological formation that emerged in particular conditions to do particular work, and which can be analyzed in terms of its functions, limits, and contradictions. This is, I would argue, what Du Bois actually does in *Black Reconstruction*, despite Robinson's appropriation of him. Du Bois's famous account of the "wages of whiteness" is an analysis of how the racialization of the white working class served specific functions in the consolidation of post-Reconstruction capitalism — functions that can be understood, criticized, and potentially undone. To turn this into a thesis that capitalism is permanently and constitutively racial is to remove the analytical leverage that Du Bois's account provided. It also tends to obscure the fact that the racialization of capitalism takes different forms in different conjunctures: the racialization of Irish labor in the nineteenth-century United States is not the racialization of African labor under apartheid, and the differences matter for the politics of solidarity.

There is a related worry about the political consequences of the racial capitalism thesis. If race and class are, as Johnson puts it, "functionally the same," then the prospect of class-based solidarity across racial lines becomes theoretically suspect. The white worker is, on this account, not simply a member of a class with objective interests that align (in important ways) with those of black workers; he is a beneficiary of racial capitalism whose interests are structured against those of the dispossessed. This is the thesis of the "psychological wage" extended into a permanent feature of the system. The trouble is that it makes multiracial working-class politics a more difficult project than the historical record suggests it has to be. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* contains, alongside the analysis of the wages of whiteness, a sustained meditation on the moments when interracial labor solidarity was within reach and was sabotaged by political and economic forces. The lesson is not that white workers are permanently disqualified from solidarity; it is that solidarity is a political achievement that has to be built against the forces that work to prevent it.

Johnson's essay does not deny this, exactly, but its framing tilts in the direction I am worried about. The fifth of his "principal virtues" — that the framework "correlates both the entitlement and vulnerability of the white working class with the subjection of the 'dark proletariat'" — treats the white working class principally as beneficiary rather than as both beneficiary and victim of the racial structure. The vulnerabilities of the white working class are real (declining life expectancy, opioid epidemic, deindustrialization, the very phenomena that are being weaponized into white nationalism in our current political moment). Treating these as continuous with rather than opposed to the subjection of the dark proletariat is precisely the analytical task — and it is a task that requires the orthodox Marxist tools of class analysis, even as it requires the additional analytical work that race demands.

A second worry concerns the relation between capitalism and slavery as productive systems. Johnson is right that slavery in the nineteenth-century South was integrated into the global capitalist economy and that the financial structures that made cotton production possible were continuous with those that organized industrial capitalism. But the social relations of production on the plantation were not the same as those in the Lancashire mill, and the differences mattered for the trajectory of the system. The plantation, despite its integration with global markets, was a fundamentally different productive form from the factory: it relied on direct coercion rather than the market in labor power, it organized labor through gang work and the lash rather than through clock discipline, and it tended toward forms of social organization (paternalist, hierarchical, anti-democratic) quite different from those of industrial capitalism. These differences are why Marx, despite his evident moral condemnation of slavery, treated it as a distinct mode of production rather than as a variant of capitalism.

To say this is not to retreat to the position that slavery was "precapitalist." It is to insist that the relationship between modes of production within a world-system is more interesting than a simple identification. Eugene Genovese, despite the limitations of his analysis, was onto something when he treated the slave South as a society that contained capitalist elements but was not reducible to them. The seigneurial culture of the planter class, the resistance to commercial agriculture in the modern sense, the politics of honor — these were not epiphenomena of capitalism but features of a specifically slaveholding social formation that had its own logic, even as it was articulated with the broader capitalist world. James Oakes's worry that calling everything "capitalism" obscures these distinctions is not pedantic conservatism; it is a defense of the analytical specificity that historical materialism requires.

Where does this leave us? I want to be clear that I am not advocating a return to the older periodization in which slavery is the antechamber to capitalism proper. The empirical case for integration is settled. What I am advocating is a more disciplined use of the integrative framework — one that recognizes the integration of slavery and capitalism without collapsing them into a single undifferentiated category, and one that retains the analytical tools of class analysis even as it incorporates the analytical demands of race.

There is also a methodological observation. The strongest version of the racial capitalism thesis tends to work by accumulation rather than by argument: by piling up examples of racial violence within capitalist accumulation until the cumulative effect produces conviction. This is not nothing — the cumulative effect can be epistemically powerful — but it is not the same as having demonstrated the constitutive thesis. The constitutive thesis would require showing that capitalism could not have arisen, or could not function, without racial differentiation. The orthodox view, which I still find more persuasive, is that capitalism has typically operated through some form of social differentiation (race, gender, nationality, religion, caste) and that the specific form it takes in any conjuncture is historically variable. Race has been, in the modern North Atlantic, the dominant axis of differentiation. It is not therefore the only one capitalism could have used.

The political stakes here are real. If racial capitalism is constitutive, then anti-racism and anti-capitalism become a single struggle, and the question of which strategies advance both becomes correspondingly easier (or seems to). If racial capitalism is conjunctural, then the relations between anti-racist and anti-capitalist struggle are matters of political construction, and the question of strategy becomes correspondingly harder — but also more open to the possibility of cross-racial coalition built on shared material interest. I think the second view is both more analytically defensible and more politically generative. Johnson's essay leans toward the first, and I want to register that lean as a concern.

Settler Colonialism and the Limits of Racial Capitalism

I want to thank Walter Johnson for an essay that, more than any recent intervention in the slavery-capitalism debate, takes seriously the entanglement of African slavery with Indigenous dispossession. Toward the end of his essay he turns to *Johnson v. McIntosh* and writes that "the foundation of the law of property in the United States combines, at once, the imperial assertion of U.S. sovereignty and the identification of that project with continental racial governance." That sentence is doing a great deal of work, and I want to spend this response unpacking it — both to credit what is right about it and to push on what I take to be the limits of the racial capitalism framework when it comes to Indigenous experience and the politics of land.

The basic claim that the cotton economy was built on stolen Indigenous land is not new to specialists, but its integration into the mainstream history of American capitalism remains uneven. The standard narrative of "King Cotton" tends to elide the Removal Era — the brutal expulsion of the Creek, Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole nations that opened the lands of what we now call the Deep South to the cotton-and-slave complex. Without Removal, there is no cotton frontier; without the cotton frontier, there is no antebellum boom; without the antebellum boom, the entire economic and political history Johnson describes does not occur. To say that the political economy of nineteenth-century American slavery rested on the dispossession of Indigenous peoples is to state a basic and underappreciated fact.

What I want to press, though, is whether the conceptual apparatus of racial capitalism — even in the expanded form Johnson proposes — can adequately hold the analysis of Indigenous dispossession alongside the analysis of African enslavement. There is a temptation, when these histories are integrated, to treat them as two species of a single genus: two forms of "racial" subordination that capitalism produced and required. The result is a tidy taxonomy in which African Americans are exploited for their labor, Indigenous people are dispossessed of their land, and both are "racialized" in service of accumulation. This taxonomy has the virtue of acknowledging the centrality of both groups. It has the vice of obscuring the structural differences between the two systems of domination and, more importantly, of subsuming Indigenous politics into a framework that may not be Indigenous politics' framework of choice.

Patrick Wolfe argued, in his influential work on settler colonialism, that the "logic of elimination" structuring the relationship between settlers and Indigenous peoples is distinct from the "logic of exploitation" structuring the relationship between capital and enslaved or wage labor. Settler colonialism, on Wolfe's account, does not primarily want Indigenous bodies — it wants Indigenous land, and it wants that land cleared of its prior inhabitants. The settler relationship is therefore eliminationist in a way that the slave relationship is not. The slaveholder requires the continuous reproduction of enslaved bodies; the settler requires their disappearance. This is a difference in the basic structural orientation of the two systems, not a variant within a single system.

If Wolfe is right, then "racial capitalism" risks framing Indigenous dispossession as a kind of subspecies of racial capitalist exploitation, when in fact it is something different: an ongoing project of replacement that operates by a distinct logic and toward distinct ends. The risk is not merely taxonomic. It is political. Indigenous political demands have centered on the recovery of land, the recognition of sovereignty, and the restoration of treaty rights. These demands do not map neatly onto the framework of reparations Johnson invokes. They are not primarily demands for redistribution within an existing polity; they are demands that put the existing polity itself in question. Land Back, as the contemporary slogan goes, is not a redistributive demand; it is a sovereignty demand.

This matters for Johnson's argument because the six principal virtues he articulates at the end of his essay, though framed as virtues of an account rooted in racial capitalism, do not obviously generate a politics adequate to Indigenous claims. Consider his second virtue: "focuses on the extraction and distribution of resources between classes and areas of the world." This is a redistributive framework. It treats land and labor as resources to be more fairly distributed. But for many Indigenous peoples, the issue is not the more equitable distribution of resources within the U.S. polity; the issue is the legitimacy of U.S. sovereignty over their lands at all. A framework that begins from the question "how should resources be distributed?" has already conceded the prior question — "by what right does this polity claim the authority to distribute resources on these lands?" — that Indigenous politics has insisted on raising.

A related point concerns Johnson's treatment of *Johnson v. McIntosh*. He reads the decision correctly as establishing that Native American lands had to pass through the public domain of the United States before becoming private property — and thus as embedding U.S. sovereignty into the foundation of the property regime. But the decision also did something deeper. It invoked the so-called Doctrine of Discovery — the medieval-canonical principle that Christian European nations could claim sovereignty over lands inhabited by non-Christians — as the source of U.S. claims to Indigenous lands. The Marshall Court did not justify U.S. claims primarily in terms of racial capitalism; it justified them in terms of a longer European tradition of Christian-imperial sovereignty. Racial categories certainly did work in the decision and its aftermath. But the deeper structure is one of imperial sovereignty operating through religious-civilizational categories that predate the racial capitalism of the nineteenth century. To collapse this into "racial governance" is to miss part of what is going on.

Let me put the broader point this way. The history of the Americas involves at least three distinct, interlocking, but irreducible structures of domination: the enslavement of Africans, the dispossession of Indigenous peoples, and the exploitation of (initially European, later globally diverse) wage labor. These structures share certain features and frequently reinforce one another, but they have distinct logics and generate distinct political demands. Racial capitalism, as a framework, can hold all three only if it is sufficiently capacious — and the more capacious it becomes, the more it risks losing analytical specificity. The advantage of the framework is that it forces us to see the connections; the disadvantage is that it can homogenize the differences.

What I would urge, then, is that Johnson's framework be supplemented rather than treated as sufficient. The history of capitalism is also the history of settler colonialism, and settler colonialism is not just a variant of racial capitalism. Glen Coulthard's work on the Dene experience in northern Canada, Audra Simpson's work on Mohawk political theory, and the broader emergence of Indigenous studies as a field with its own analytical commitments — these traditions need to sit alongside the Robinson-Du Bois tradition Johnson invokes, not be folded into it.

The implications for politics are significant. If Indigenous dispossession is not just another instance of racial capitalism but a distinct structure with its own logic, then the politics of decolonization is not just another instance of racial capitalist redress but a distinct political project that may have different demands, different timeframes, and different relations to the existing state. A reparations program rooted in the history of African enslavement is not, by itself, a decolonial program. The two may be compatible — and at their best they should be — but they are not interchangeable. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson cites is, importantly, in conversation with Indigenous movements (the platform was developed with input from Indigenous organizers and includes recognition of Indigenous sovereignty), but the conversation is precisely between distinct movements with distinct demands. The framework Johnson articulates needs to register this distinctness.

I want to close with a more constructive observation. The strongest version of Johnson's argument — the one in which the history of slavery becomes the "source rather than the subject of knowledge" — should, by parity of reasoning, extend to the history of Indigenous dispossession. What if we used the history of dispossession as a source of knowledge about modern political economy? It would generate a different set of insights than the ones Johnson develops. It would foreground the relationship between sovereignty and property, the violence of the state in producing the conditions for private accumulation, the role of treaty and its breaking in the constitution of the modern legal order, and the question of what is owed when what was taken is land and life rather than labor. These insights are not incompatible with Johnson's; they are complementary to them. But they require their own intellectual genealogy, and that genealogy includes Vine Deloria Jr., Robert Williams, Taiaiake Alfred, and a growing body of Indigenous scholarship that has its own answers to the questions Johnson is raising.

The propitious moment Du Bois invoked, the moment for remaking the world, is also a moment when the question of Indigenous sovereignty is being raised in new and powerful ways — Standing Rock has, in the months leading up to this writing, made this unmistakable. An adequate account of justice will not arrive at the right answers if it begins from a framework that has not internalized this question. Johnson's essay opens the door. The next step is to walk through it with Indigenous interlocutors and let the framework be transformed by what they have to say.

In Defense of "Dehumanization," Cautiously

Walter Johnson opens his essay with a striking claim: that the language of "dehumanization," as applied to slavery, is misleading, harmful, and worth resisting. The argument is provocative, and Johnson develops it with the care his readers have come to expect. But I think he is wrong, or at least more wrong than he is right, about this particular term — and I want to spend my response defending a chastened version of "dehumanization" against his critique.

Johnson's argument against the term has two main parts. First, it is misleading because slavery in fact depended on the human capacities of the enslaved — their labor, their sentience, their fear, their pain. Second, it is harmful because the language imposes on the enslaved a kind of secondary injury: it suggests they were alienated from their humanity, and it places observers in a paternalist position of judging when and whether that humanity has been restored. Both criticisms have force. But I think the force can be acknowledged without abandoning the term, and there are good reasons not to abandon it.

Consider the first criticism. Johnson is right that slavery did not require the enslaved to actually become non-human in any descriptive sense. The slaveholder needed the slave to be human in order to exploit, fear, manipulate, and torture her. But this is not what the term "dehumanization" denotes when philosophers and historians use it carefully. Dehumanization, in the philosophical literature, is typically not a descriptive claim about the actual ontological status of the dehumanized; it is a claim about the perceptual and ideological structure of the dehumanizer. To dehumanize is to perceive, or to ideologically construct, the other as less than fully human — even while continuing to depend on capacities that one's perceptual frame officially denies. The contradiction is internal to the perpetrator, not to the victim.

This is, I think, the structure of most serious accounts of dehumanization in the literature on mass violence — David Livingstone Smith's work, for example, or the older sociological literature on dehumanization in modern warfare. The dehumanizer is engaged in a kind of perceptual self-deception, holding the other as both human (in capacities) and sub-human (in moral standing). The contradiction is not resolved; it is sustained, and it is what allows the violence to proceed. To say that slavery dehumanized the enslaved, on this understanding, is not to say that the enslaved actually became less than human. It is to say that the slaveholder operated within a perceptual and ideological frame that classified them as less than human, even as it depended on their human capacities. This is closer to what historians like Philip Morgan are typically trying to say, even when they put it less precisely.

Now Johnson might respond that even this more carefully formulated version of dehumanization smuggles in a problematic normative claim — namely, that there is a stable category of "the human" against which dehumanization can be measured, and that the project of restoring or affirming the humanity of the enslaved presupposes our ability to occupy that category in a way the enslaved supposedly could not. This is the deeper version of his second criticism, and it is the one I want to engage with most seriously.

There is something right in the worry. The category of "the human" has been a fraught one in Western thought, repeatedly defined in ways that excluded those who did not fit a particular cultural or biological template. The history of "humanism" is, as Sylvia Wynter and others have argued, a history of differentiated humanity, in which "Man" — typically European, male, propertied, rational — has been positioned as the model against which all other humans are measured. To invoke "the human" as the standard against which dehumanization is to be measured is, on this view, already to be operating within a problematic framework.

But notice what happens if we follow this critique all the way down. We end up with a position in which there is no stable notion of the human from which moral claims about slavery can be made. Slavery cannot be called "inhuman" because there is no category of "the human" to contrast it with. The violations of slavery cannot be called violations of human capacity because the category of human capacity is itself ideological. This is, I think, an untenable position. The reason "dehumanization" has persisted as a category in spite of its difficulties is that it names something real and important: the construction of moral hierarchies that classify some human beings as less deserving of moral consideration than others. To abandon the term is to lose a vocabulary we cannot yet do without.

The question, then, is whether we can hold on to the term while addressing Johnson's legitimate concerns. I think we can. The first move is to distinguish carefully between dehumanization-as-description and dehumanization-as-perception. The enslaved were not, descriptively, dehumanized; they remained fully human throughout, in all the capacities that constitute humanity. But they were subjected to a system that perceived and treated them as less than fully human. The dehumanization is in the system, not in the persons. This is a subtle distinction but a crucial one, and it preserves the moral force of the term without imposing on the enslaved the secondary injury Johnson worries about.

The second move is to be careful about the relationship between dehumanization talk and the project of historical recovery. Johnson is right that there is something unsatisfying about a historiography that frames its central task as "discovering" the humanity of the enslaved, as if that humanity were ever in genuine question. But I do not think most contemporary historians are doing exactly this. What they are doing — what Morgan is doing in the passages Johnson quotes — is reconstructing the lifeworlds, agency, creativity, and moral seriousness of the enslaved against an archive that systematically obscured these things. The point is not to prove that the enslaved were human (which no one with any sense doubts) but to give weight, texture, and specificity to lives that the archive was designed to flatten. Johnson conflates these two projects, and the conflation does damage to his argument.

There is also a generational point worth making. The historiographic moves Johnson criticizes — the insistence on the agency and humanity of the enslaved — were developed in the 1960s and 1970s against an older historiography (Phillips, Elkins) that had genuinely treated the enslaved as objects of history rather than its subjects. The work of John Blassingame, Eugene Genovese, Lawrence Levine, and others was a corrective to a tradition that did need correcting. Johnson is in a sense complaining about the success of that corrective: now that the agency of the enslaved is established, perhaps the framework that established it has become a confinement. There is something to this, but it is worth noting that the framework remains contested in some quarters, and that its dismantling in academic discourse may not be received in the same way in public discourse, where the older paternalist framings retain considerable life.

Where I think Johnson is most powerful is in his observation about the implicit liberalism of the "agency" framework — the way it tends to assimilate enslaved action to the model of the bourgeois liberal subject choosing among options. Singing a spiritual, breaking a tool, fomenting a revolution: these can be read as expressions of liberal selfhood, and there is something thin and anachronistic about that reading. The cultural and religious idioms within which enslaved action took place do not reduce to the framework of intentional choice. A richer historiography would attend to the specific moral and cosmological universes the enslaved inhabited — and there has been important work in this direction, by scholars like Stephanie Camp, Walter Rucker, and others.

So my position is this: Johnson is right that the language of agency, humanity, and dehumanization has been deployed in ways that can flatten the specificity of enslaved experience and impose anachronistic frameworks. He is wrong that this requires abandoning the language. A more careful use of these terms — one that distinguishes perceptual from descriptive dehumanization, that does not frame the historian's task as proving humanity, and that attends to the specific cultural idioms of enslaved life — preserves what is valuable in the inherited vocabulary while addressing what is problematic.

There is one final point I want to register, which concerns the politics of vocabulary. Johnson's critique of "dehumanization" is being mounted at a moment when the actual political defense of the humanity of black people remains, in the United States and elsewhere, an ongoing struggle. The Movement for Black Lives takes its very name from an insistence on the basic moral status of black life that significant portions of the polity continue to deny. In this context, the philosophical refinement of our vocabulary needs to be calibrated against the political work that vocabulary continues to do. "Dehumanization" remains a useful word in public discourse because it names, with rhetorical force, a moral failure that is still being committed. To strip it from the vocabulary on philosophical grounds, however well-reasoned, is to risk disarming a discourse that needs the weapons it has.

The Reparations Question: A Friendly Skeptic Responds

Walter Johnson's essay is, among other things, an argument for reparations — though it does not put the argument in those words, and it does not engage in detail with how a reparations program might be designed or implemented. He cites the Movement for Black Lives platform, whose call for reparations is unambiguous, and he frames his "six principal virtues" of a justice rooted in racial capitalism in ways that lead unmistakably in a reparative direction. I want to engage Johnson as a friendly skeptic on the reparations question — friendly because I think the moral case for reparations is essentially correct, skeptic because I worry that the political case has not been adequately made, and that Johnson's framework, while powerful as historical analysis, generates fewer answers than it appears to about the form reparations should take.

Let me begin by stipulating what I take Johnson's historical analysis to establish. The wealth of the United States, and of the broader North Atlantic world, was built in significant part on the enslaved labor of Africans and their descendants and on the dispossessed land of Indigenous peoples. The continuing disparities in wealth, health, education, incarceration, and life expectancy between white Americans and African Americans are not accidents but the predictable consequences of centuries of accumulated extraction and exclusion. The case for some form of compensatory redress is, on these premises, very strong. I am not interested in litigating the moral case against people like David Horowitz, whose objections seem to me to dissolve on contact with the historical record. The moral case is, in my view, settled.

What remains contested are questions of design and implementation, and it is here that I want to press Johnson. Reparations programs face a series of practical and political challenges that have to be confronted on their own terms.

First, who pays? Johnson's framework points toward "the dark and dispossessed" as the standpoint of justice and toward something like the metropolitan beneficiaries as the bearers of the obligation. But the actual distribution of contemporary wealth in the United States and globally is more complex than this binary suggests. The white working class that Johnson, following Du Bois, treats as having received "wages of whiteness" is also, by other measures, increasingly economically immiserated. The current political moment, with its rising white nationalism, is being read by many as a response to that immiseration. A reparations program funded by a broad tax base will fall on people who are themselves struggling, and the political result is predictable. A reparations program funded narrowly by the very wealthy faces a different problem: the very wealthy are politically powerful, the financial system protects them from taxation, and even small steps in this direction (the Obama estate tax adjustments) have proven politically costly.

Second, who receives? The category of those owed reparations is harder to define than it might first appear. Are reparations owed to all African Americans, including recent immigrants from Africa or the Caribbean whose ancestors were not enslaved in the United States? Are they owed only to descendants of those enslaved in the United States, and how is such descent to be established when the historical record was so deliberately incomplete? Are they owed to Indigenous peoples on a separate track, or together with African Americans, and how are the different forms of redress (land back, monetary compensation, structural reform) to be weighted? Johnson's framework gestures at the unity of these claims but does not work out their differences.

Third, what form? Reparations can take many forms — direct payments to individuals, payments to communities, structural investments in housing, education, and health, the cancellation of debt, the return of land, or some combination. Each form has different distributive consequences, different political implications, and different vulnerabilities. Direct payments have the virtue of treating the recipients as autonomous moral agents but face the criticism that they may be quickly absorbed back into structures of disadvantage. Structural investments may produce more durable change but raise questions about who controls the funds and how priorities are set. Land returns to Indigenous communities raise sovereignty questions that direct payment programs do not. Johnson's framework is suggestive but does not resolve these design questions.

Fourth, how much? The numbers in serious reparations proposals range widely — from the relatively modest sums implied by truth-and-reconciliation models to the figures generated by full accounting of unpaid labor over centuries (William Darity's work has been important here). The choice of figure has enormous practical consequences. A small program can be implemented within existing fiscal frameworks but may be inadequate to the harm. A large program is potentially transformative but raises questions about economic stability, inflation, and political backlash that have to be faced.

Fifth, and most importantly, what is the political path? Reparations have been on the table as a serious proposal in U.S. political discourse since at least the 1969 Black Manifesto. They have not advanced significantly in fifty years, and the recent revival of the discussion (Ta-Nehisi Coates's 2014 essay was a milestone) has not so far translated into legislative momentum. H.R. 40, the bill to study reparations introduced by John Conyers every Congress since 1989, has yet to pass. The political moment, with a new administration that ran on a platform explicitly antagonistic to the racial-justice frame, looks worse rather than better for the project.

I raise these questions not to dismiss reparations but to suggest that the gap between Johnson's historical and theoretical analysis and the political work of building a reparations coalition is considerable. The analysis tells us why reparations are morally owed. It does not tell us how to build the political force capable of delivering them.

There is an alternative framework worth considering, and I want to lay it out not as a substitute for reparations but as a possible complement. The framework is what we might call universal social democracy with reparative weighting. Rather than building a politics around a category of recipients defined by descent, we might build a politics around universal entitlements — to health care, housing, education, employment, a basic income — that are sized and structured in ways that close the gaps Johnson rightly identifies. A robust public-housing program that builds where the need is greatest will disproportionately benefit black and Indigenous communities. A jobs guarantee will disproportionately benefit communities most affected by deindustrialization and incarceration. A wealth tax of sufficient scale will disproportionately fall on those whose wealth derives most directly from the histories of extraction Johnson describes.

The advantages of this framework are political. It builds a broader coalition by attaching the redress of historical injury to programs that also benefit the white working class, whose interests are objectively more aligned with anti-racist politics than with white nationalism. It avoids the legal and definitional challenges that haunt reparations design. It can be implemented incrementally without waiting for a transformative political moment. And it operates within institutional channels (Congress, federal agencies, state governments) that exist and have track records.

The disadvantages are moral. Universal programs do not name the specific historical wrong they are responding to. They do not provide the symbolic vindication that explicit reparations offer. They allow beneficiaries who are not victims of the historical wrong to participate equally in the redress, which some will find offensive. And they may be insufficient: the wealth gap between white and black Americans is large enough that universal programs may not close it.

I am genuinely uncertain about how to weigh these considerations, and I suspect the most defensible position is some combination — a universal social-democratic program plus a targeted reparations program, with the targeting designed to address the residual gap that universalism does not close. But this combination requires a politics that can hold both elements together, and I do not yet see what that politics looks like.

Johnson's essay does not engage with these questions, and I do not fault him for that — they are not the questions his essay is asking. But I think the essay would be more useful if it were more candid about what its framework does and does not deliver. The framework establishes that reparations are owed. It does not, by itself, build the political coalition capable of paying them. The work of building that coalition is, in my view, the central political task for those who agree with Johnson's analysis. And that work will require engagement with constituencies — including the white working class — that the racial capitalism framework, in some of its formulations, tends to treat as obstacles rather than as potential allies.

The propitious moment Du Bois invoked is not, I suspect, our moment. The political winds are running the other way. The task for those committed to Johnson's analysis is to prepare the ground for a moment that may come — to build the institutions, the analyses, and the coalitions that can move when an opening appears. This is patient work, and it is harder than the writing of essays. But it is the work the moment requires.

Plantation, Anthropocene, Earth

The sixth of Walter Johnson's "principal virtues" is the one I want to take up: the suggestion that an account of justice rooted in racial capitalism can connect a critique of the instrumentalization of human beings to a critique of the instrumentalization of nature, "over and against many recent efforts which assert that a forthright treatment of global environmental history requires the elevation of the categories of the 'human' and the 'Anthropocene' over and against other historical categories." This is an important intervention, and I want to develop it further than Johnson's essay has space to do.

The debate Johnson is alluding to is the debate over the Anthropocene — the proposed geological epoch defined by human impact on the Earth system. Scholars working in environmental humanities have, over the past decade, raised the question of whether "the human" is the right unit of analysis for the planetary crisis we are confronting. The argument that has emerged from critical quarters — Donna Haraway, Jason Moore, Kathryn Yusoff, T. J. Demos, and others — is that the Anthropocene framing obscures more than it illuminates. The "anthropos" that has driven planetary transformation is not humanity in general but particular human populations, organized in particular economic systems, pursuing particular forms of accumulation. To say "humans" caused climate change is to occlude the historical fact that the carbon emissions in question were generated overwhelmingly by particular polities (the industrialized North Atlantic), particular classes (the owners of fossil capital), and particular projects (industrial capitalism, colonial extraction, the postwar consumer society).

Alternatives have been proposed. Moore has argued for "Capitalocene," locating the geological signature of human impact in the rise of capitalism specifically rather than in human activity generally. Haraway has proposed "Chthulucene" and, more pointedly for our purposes, "Plantationocene," arguing that the plantation — as a specific form of biological and labor organization — is the deeper origin of the planetary patterns we are now living with. Yusoff, in work that is just beginning to circulate, has argued that geology itself, as a science and as a mode of relating to the Earth, is implicated in the racial and colonial structures that the Anthropocene framing tends to obscure.

Johnson's essay sits comfortably alongside these alternatives without quite endorsing any of them by name. What he offers is a framework — racial capitalism — that can do similar work to "Capitalocene" or "Plantationocene" but with more historical specificity than either. The strength of Johnson's framework, for environmental purposes, is that it locates the origin of the planetary transformation we are now living through in a specific set of historical processes: the integration of African enslavement, Indigenous dispossession, and fossil-powered industrial production into a single global system of accumulation. The plantation, the mill, and the railroad were not three separate developments that happened to coincide; they were components of a single system that, taken together, set the planet on its current trajectory.

Let me develop this with some specifics. The cotton plantation of the antebellum South was, by the standards of its time, one of the most ecologically transformative institutions on Earth. The clearing of forest for cotton cultivation, the exhaustion of soil that drove the constant westward expansion of the plantation frontier, the displacement of native ecologies by monocultural production, the introduction of new species (cotton itself, but also the various plants and animals that traveled with the plantation system) — these were not side effects of plantation production but constitutive features of it. The plantation was, among other things, a machine for converting ecological diversity into commodity uniformity, with consequences for soil, water, biodiversity, and climate that we are still living with.

Crucially, the plantation also pioneered modes of biological and labor organization that have been generalized far beyond their original site. The disciplined, time-managed, output-optimized organization of labor that we associate with industrial capitalism was, as historians like Caitlin Rosenthal have argued, developed first on the plantation. The use of detailed accounting techniques to measure and increase labor output; the systematic breeding of plants and animals (and, monstrously, of human beings) for productive characteristics; the management of large populations in confined spaces — these technologies of biopower were refined on the plantation before they were generalized to the factory, the feedlot, and the modern industrial farm. Haraway's term "Plantationocene" tries to capture this lineage: the planetary patterns of biological management we now live within have a specific origin in the plantation form, and that form was racial from its inception.

If we follow this line of thought, the planetary environmental crisis is not detachable from the racial-capitalist history Johnson narrates. The carbon emissions that are now altering the Earth's climate were generated by an industrial economy whose original capitalization came from plantation slavery. The species extinctions we are now witnessing trace back, in significant part, to land-use patterns established by plantation agriculture and colonial extraction. The vulnerabilities to climate disruption are distributed along racial and colonial lines: the populations most exposed to rising seas, intensifying storms, agricultural disruption, and heat stress are predominantly the populations whose ancestors bore the brunt of the racial-capitalist accumulation that generated the crisis in the first place. This is not coincidence. It is the continuing operation of the system Johnson describes.

What follows for the politics of climate? Several things, I think.

First, climate justice cannot be reduced to a question of emissions reduction abstractly considered. The "we" of humanity-in-general that the Anthropocene framing posits is not the relevant political subject. The relevant subjects are differentiated: those most responsible for emissions, those most vulnerable to consequences, those whose forms of life have been most destroyed by the system that generated the crisis. A climate politics adequate to the moment must hold these differentiations at its center.

Second, climate redress has a reparative dimension that is continuous with the reparative dimension of the racial-capitalist accumulation that produced the crisis. The Global North owes the Global South not just emissions reductions but transfers — of resources, of technology, of accumulated knowledge — that respond to the historical fact of asymmetric extraction. The "loss and damage" framework that has been struggling for traction in international climate negotiations is one institutional expression of this. So is the broader argument that climate finance must be additional to, not substitutive for, development aid that was itself inadequate compensation for colonial extraction.

Third, the response to the climate crisis cannot be technocratic in a way that leaves the underlying system intact. Carbon capture, geoengineering, market-based emissions trading — these can be parts of a response, but they cannot be the whole response, because they leave intact the patterns of differential extraction and vulnerability that constitute the deeper problem. A genuinely transformative climate politics has to be a politics of system change, in something close to the sense the climate-justice movement has been arguing.

Fourth, the relationship between ecological domination and racial domination is not, as Johnson rightly insists, analogical. It is historical and structural. The same processes that produced the racialization of human populations also produced the instrumentalization of nature, and the categories that organized one organized the other. Sylvia Wynter has argued that the Western conception of "Man" — bourgeois, rational, propertied, white — was constructed in opposition not just to non-Western humans but to nature, and that the same conceptual operation that produced racial hierarchy produced the domination of the natural world. The undoing of one is bound up with the undoing of the other.

This is where I think Johnson's framework can do more than he claims. It can serve not just as a framework for understanding the history of racial capitalism but as a framework for understanding the planetary crisis we are now confronting. The "world-wasting race to the end of time" Johnson invokes is not a metaphor; it is a description of the trajectory the system is on. The plantation, the slave ship, the cotton mill, the coal pit, the oil well, the suburban subdivision, the data center — these are episodes in a single history, and that history is now bringing us up against planetary limits.

The political implication is not despair. It is that the work of dismantling racial capitalism and the work of addressing the planetary crisis are, properly understood, the same work. The frameworks that treat them as separate — environment over here, justice over there — are inadequate to both. The frameworks that treat them as continuous — racial capitalism, Plantationocene, climate justice — are doing the deeper diagnostic work the moment requires. Johnson's essay points in this direction. The next step is to make the connection explicit and to develop the politics that follows from it.

Moyn, Antislavery, and the Vexed History of Human Rights

Walter Johnson's essay treats the history of human rights with a glancing touch. He cites Samuel Moyn's *The Last Utopia* approvingly for its argument that the dominant contemporary regime of human rights is a creature of the mid-twentieth century rather than a continuation of the longer antislavery tradition. He then uses this argument to license a broader claim: that the human-rights framework is inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, and that we should turn instead to a justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism. I want to spend my response unpacking the history of human rights more carefully, because I think Johnson's quick adoption of Moyn's periodization obscures both what Moyn gets right and what Johnson's argument actually requires.

Moyn's argument in *The Last Utopia* is, in brief, that "human rights" as a globally circulating moral and political vocabulary emerges late — in the 1970s rather than in 1948 (the Universal Declaration), 1789 (the French Declaration), or 1776 (the American Declaration). The earlier moments Moyn treats as essentially nationalist or revolutionary projects that used the language of rights instrumentally; what is distinctive about human rights properly understood, on Moyn's account, is the appeal to a transnational moral authority that sits outside and above the nation-state. That appeal, Moyn argues, becomes politically operative only with the collapse of competing utopias (Marxist, anticolonial, nationalist) in the 1970s, when human rights becomes the "last utopia" for a generation disillusioned with state-based projects.

This is a controversial argument, and Moyn's critics — Lynn Hunt, Micheline Ishay, others — have argued that the longer history of human rights, including antislavery, has more continuity with the contemporary regime than Moyn allows. The debate is unresolved. But for our purposes, what matters is what Johnson does with Moyn's argument and whether the argument can bear the weight Johnson places on it.

Johnson uses Moyn to license the claim that the contemporary human-rights regime is "not significantly inflected by the history of slavery, although it would be better if it were." This is a much stronger claim than Moyn himself makes, and it deserves scrutiny. Even if we grant Moyn that human rights as a transnational vocabulary emerges in the 1970s, it does not follow that the substantive content of that vocabulary is uninflected by antislavery. The 1948 Universal Declaration, which Moyn de-emphasizes but does not dismiss, contains provisions (the prohibition of slavery, the prohibition of torture, the protection against arbitrary detention) that have clear genealogical relations to abolitionist arguments. The framing of human rights as "inherent" in the human person draws on a long tradition that the antislavery movement helped to develop. The institutional architecture of contemporary human rights (treaty bodies, special rapporteurs, the U.N. system) was built by people who took the abolitionist precedent seriously even when they did not invoke it explicitly.

What is true, and what Moyn is right about, is that the contemporary human-rights regime does not foreground the history of slavery in the way it foregrounds, say, the Holocaust or twentieth-century totalitarianism. The genealogy of the Universal Declaration runs more directly through the experience of Nazi atrocity than through the experience of Atlantic slavery. The institutional development of human rights in the postwar period was shaped by Cold War politics in ways that often suppressed engagement with the colonial and racial dimensions of the order it claimed to regulate. These are real points, and they constrain what human rights as a vocabulary can do.

But they do not establish what Johnson needs them to establish — namely, that the human-rights framework is fundamentally inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, such that an alternative framework rooted in racial capitalism is required. The relationship between the human-rights vocabulary and the project of racial justice is more dialectical than Johnson's brisk treatment allows.

Consider the actual deployment of human-rights frameworks by movements working against the legacies of slavery. The NAACP brought a petition to the United Nations in 1947 (the "Appeal to the World," edited by Du Bois himself) and another in 1951 (the "We Charge Genocide" petition, brought by the Civil Rights Congress). These efforts used the emerging human-rights vocabulary to indict the United States for its treatment of African Americans. They were not entirely successful — Cold War politics suppressed serious engagement with the petitions — but they established a precedent that has been picked up repeatedly since. The U.S. CERD reports, the periodic engagement of U.S. civil-rights groups with U.N. human-rights bodies, the recent reparations claims being developed by CARICOM nations under international law — all of these use the human-rights framework as a vehicle for racial-justice claims that domestic frameworks have been inadequate to advance.

The Movement for Black Lives platform, which Johnson cites, contains within it a complex relationship with the human-rights vocabulary. It draws on human-rights language in places (the language of dignity, of self-determination) while also developing claims that exceed the human-rights frame (the reparations demand in its specificity, the abolitionist demand). The platform is, in this sense, neither a vindication of the human-rights framework nor a rejection of it; it is a strategic and substantive elaboration of how a movement rooted in the history of racial capitalism can use human-rights tools without being captured by them.

This is, I think, the more productive way to think about the relationship Johnson is gesturing at. The human-rights framework is not adequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, but it is not inadequate in the simple sense Johnson's brisk dismissal suggests. It is partially adequate. It captures some dimensions of the injustice (the violation of bodily integrity, the denial of political participation, the imposition of arbitrary state violence) while occluding others (the structural extraction of resources across generations, the racialization of life chances, the entanglement of race and class). The political task for movements working in the inheritance of slavery is to use the framework where it serves them, to push it where it can be pushed, and to develop additional frameworks where it cannot.

The development of additional frameworks is where Johnson's contribution lies, and I want to credit it. The history of racial capitalism does generate analytical and moral resources that the human-rights framework does not. The standpoint of the Global South, the focus on extraction and distribution, the emphasis on reparations rather than synchronic rights — these are real additions, and they enrich the moral vocabulary available to contemporary movements. What I am resisting is the framing that treats the new framework as a replacement for, rather than a supplement to, the human-rights tradition.

There is also a strategic point worth making. The human-rights framework, for all its limitations, has institutional purchase that newer frameworks do not. Treaty bodies, U.N. mechanisms, regional courts, NGO networks — these exist, they have track records, and they can be deployed in service of racial-justice claims even when their original architects did not anticipate that deployment. Movements that operate purely within new vocabularies face the additional burden of building institutional infrastructure from scratch. Movements that operate within the human-rights framework can leverage existing infrastructure even as they critique and transform it.

This is not an argument against developing the framework Johnson proposes. It is an argument for developing it in addition to, and in dialogue with, the existing framework. The history of social movements suggests that the most effective approaches typically operate on multiple vocabularies at once — using each where it serves, holding them in productive tension rather than picking one and discarding the others.

There is a deeper point lurking here about the relationship between historical genealogy and political utility. Moyn's argument is in significant part a historical one: human rights as we know them are a recent invention. Even if this is correct as history, it does not settle the question of political utility. A vocabulary can be useful for purposes its inventors did not envision, and the abolitionist tradition that Moyn de-emphasizes can be retroactively reclaimed as an ancestor of contemporary human rights even if the actual historical lineage is more discontinuous than such retrieval suggests. The question for contemporary movements is not "what did the framers of human rights actually think they were doing?" but "what can the human-rights vocabulary do for us now?" The answer to that question is not "nothing."

I want to close with an observation about the politics of the present moment. The new administration in Washington is, by all appearances, hostile to the human-rights framework — both in its rhetoric and in its early policy moves. The international institutions that have housed the human-rights regime are under pressure from multiple directions. The framework Johnson critiques is, in a sense, on the defensive. In this context, the question of whether to defend the framework or to move beyond it is not merely academic. Defending it imperfectly may be more useful, at this moment, than developing alternatives that will not have political traction for years to come. The framework Johnson proposes is important for the long term. The framework Moyn analyzes is what we have to work with in the short term. The relationship between the two is a strategic question, and I do not think Johnson's essay quite reckons with that question's urgency.

Theory from the Movement Floor

I write as someone who has spent most of my working life in organizing rather than in scholarship, and I want to offer a response to Walter Johnson's essay from that vantage. Johnson is a scholar I respect, and the framework he develops — racial capitalism rooted in the history of slavery, oriented toward reparative justice — resonates strongly with how movement people I know talk about what we are trying to do. But there are also things the essay gestures at without quite capturing, and I want to put them on the table.

The first thing to say is that the essay matters because it takes seriously what the Movement for Black Lives platform actually argues for. The platform, released in August 2016, was an enormous collective achievement — months of work by dozens of organizations to articulate a vision that goes well beyond the immediate police-violence concerns that brought the movement to public attention. The reparations plank is the most ambitious part of the platform, and it has been the most controversial. Liberal commentators have treated it as a maximalist demand that distracts from achievable reforms. Scholars in the Beckert/Baptist tradition have engaged with the history but rarely with the political demand. Johnson's essay is one of the few academic interventions that takes the demand seriously as a serious demand, and that engagement matters.

What movement people I know are trying to do, in our better moments, is hold together three things at once: an analysis that names the deep structural causes of the harm we are responding to, a politics that can build the power needed to address those causes, and a practice that responds to the immediate suffering of our communities while we build that power. Johnson's essay is strongest on the first of these, and it is the second and third that I want to talk about.

On the politics of power-building: Johnson's framework, as articulated, places the standpoint of justice in the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed." This is true to the historical reality his essay describes. But it raises a question that organizers face every day: what is the relationship between the standpoint of justice and the building of political majorities? In a country where black Americans are roughly 13 percent of the population, the political math of racial-justice organizing requires alliances. The question is what kind of alliances, on what terms, with whom.

Johnson's framework tilts in a direction that some movement people I work with find generative and others find restricting. The generative reading is that it provides a clear analytical basis for treating racial justice as central rather than peripheral to the broader work of building a more just society — that it resists the chronic temptation of multiracial movements to relegate racial issues to the margins in pursuit of "broader" coalitions that turn out to be predominantly white. The restricting reading is that it can be used to justify forms of movement-building that treat potential allies (Latino communities, Asian-American communities, white working-class communities, working-class people generally) as either subordinate constituencies or as not really part of the project at all.

The most successful movement work I have seen does not resolve this tension cleanly. It holds it. It centers the experience and analysis of black communities in a way that takes the racial-capitalism framework seriously, while also building real alliances across racial lines on the basis of overlapping material interests. The Fight for $15 has been a useful example — a campaign that has black workers at its core, that takes seriously the racial dimension of low-wage work, but that builds across racial lines because the material interest in higher wages is widely shared. The Movement for Black Lives platform itself is more open to coalitional politics than the racial-capitalism framework, narrowly construed, would suggest — it explicitly engages with environmental justice, immigrant rights, queer and trans liberation, and other movements that are not principally about racial justice but that are bound up with it.

On the practice that responds to immediate suffering: Johnson's framework points toward long-term structural transformation, and that is appropriate to the moral analysis he is offering. But movement work cannot wait for structural transformation. We have to feed people now, defend people from deportation now, get people out of cages now, respond to police violence now. The question is whether the framework Johnson articulates is compatible with this immediate work, or whether it implicitly demands a kind of revolutionary patience that defers immediate response in favor of long-term analysis.

I think the framework is compatible — Johnson's own writing on Ferguson and similar moments suggests he sees the immediate and the structural as connected — but the essay does not develop this connection. What movement people I know need from scholars is not just analysis of the deep structures but help thinking about how immediate work can be informed by and contribute to the longer-term project. The bail funds, the mutual-aid networks, the rapid-response teams, the participatory-defense projects — these are the daily work, and they are also the building blocks of something larger. Connecting the daily work to the structural analysis is intellectual labor that the academy could be doing more of.

There is a related question about the role of the academy in movement work. Johnson cites a number of scholars whose work is central to the racial-capitalism tradition — Gilmore, Kelley, Lipsitz, Hartman, others. Many of these scholars are also movement-engaged in significant ways, and their scholarship is shaped by movement work even as it shapes it. This is good. What is harder, and what I think the essay does not fully address, is the gap between the scholarly conversation and the conversation in organizing spaces. The vocabulary of the academic conversation — "racial capitalism," "the dark proletariat," "natal alienation," "the carceral state" — has migrated into organizing spaces, sometimes productively and sometimes in ways that produce more confusion than clarity. Building real connection between scholarly and movement work requires more than the migration of vocabulary; it requires sustained relationships, mutual accountability, and a willingness on the scholarly side to take direction from movements rather than only providing analysis to them.

A final point. Johnson invokes, in his closing paragraph, Du Bois's argument about moments propitious for change — moments when the world might be remade in a better image. The political moment we are entering does not look propitious in any obvious sense. The new administration is hostile to virtually everything the racial-capitalism analysis would prescribe. Federal institutions that could be levers for racial-justice work are being staffed by people committed to the opposite. The political coalitions that delivered marginal but real gains under the previous administration are demoralized and disorganized.

But Du Bois's point about propitious moments was not just about how to recognize them; it was about the cost of failing to act when they arrive. The next four years will be a defensive period for movement work in important ways. The work of preserving what can be preserved, of protecting people who are immediately threatened, of building infrastructure that can survive the period — this is the work that the moment demands. But it is also possible to do more than defense in a defensive period. The conditions under which a future propitious moment will arrive are being shaped now, and the analysis and organizing we do now will determine whether we are ready when that moment comes.

Johnson's essay contributes to that readiness. It gives us a framework that goes beyond the inherited liberal vocabularies, that takes seriously the history of the harm we are responding to, and that orients us toward redress that is commensurate with that harm. What movement people I know need to do with the framework is what we always do with intellectual resources — take them, test them against our work, adapt them as needed, and put them in service of the projects we are building. The essay is useful to that work. We should say so, even as we keep our own counsel about how to use it.

Some Conservative Doubts About Racial Capitalism

I am writing this response from a perspective that does not, I suspect, share much ground with most of Walter Johnson's other respondents. I am a conservative — by which I mean a person who is skeptical of large-scale schemes for social reconstruction, who values inherited institutions, and who is suspicious of historical narratives constructed to support comprehensive political demands. I am also a person who takes the history of slavery seriously and who believes that the moral weight of that history places real obligations on the American polity. I want to engage Johnson's essay seriously, from this perspective, because I think the conservative tradition has something to say about it that the broader conversation is not hearing.

Let me start by conceding what is conceded. The history of African slavery in the Americas is among the great moral failures of human civilization. The political economy of the antebellum United States was built on enslaved labor and on the dispossession of Indigenous peoples. The wealth that the American nation has enjoyed since emancipation was built in significant part on the unpaid labor of generations of African Americans, and the wealth disparities we observe today have real continuities with that history. None of this is in dispute, and a serious conservative response to Johnson cannot evade it.

What I want to dispute is the move from these historical premises to the political and analytical conclusions Johnson draws. Several moves in the essay strike me as problematic, and I will take them in turn.

First, the move from history to total system. Johnson's framework, following Robinson, treats racial capitalism as a comprehensive system that integrates plantation, factory, counting house, and colonial extraction into a single object of analysis. The analytical impulse here is totalizing — it wants to bring everything under a single conceptual umbrella. The conservative impulse is the opposite: to insist on the irreducibility of particulars, the multiplicity of institutions, the resistance of historical reality to schematization. I do not think Johnson is wrong that the various elements of the nineteenth-century world economy were related. But I think he is wrong to suppose that the relationships are best understood through a single integrating concept. The relationships were specific, contingent, often contradictory, and frequently the elements of the system worked against each other (the abolitionist movement in Britain, for instance, was not simply the ideological extension of British capitalism; it operated, in important respects, against the immediate interests of British capital). A more modest framework, one that recognized the connections without insisting on their unity, would do better justice to the historical complexity.

Second, the move from system to standpoint. Johnson argues that the appropriate standpoint of justice is that of the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed." This is a standpoint-epistemological claim, and it bears scrutiny. The claim that the experience of the oppressed gives privileged access to social truth has a long history in radical political theory, but the conservative tradition has reasons to be skeptical of it. Different people, even among the oppressed, draw different conclusions from their experience. The standpoint of the dispossessed in the early twentieth century gave us Du Bois but also Marcus Garvey, Booker T. Washington but also Ida B. Wells. Among the contemporary descendants of the enslaved are conservatives, libertarians, religious traditionalists, and political moderates whose political conclusions diverge significantly from those Johnson's framework would license. Privileging a particular interpretive frame and calling it "the" standpoint of the dispossessed is, at the very least, a contestable move.

Third, the move from standpoint to comprehensive program. The six principal virtues Johnson lists at the end of his essay constitute, in effect, a political program: a politics of redistribution rooted in the experience of the Global South, oriented toward reparations and restorative justice, organized around the entanglement of race and class, attentive to gender and ecology, hostile to the categories of liberal individualism. Each of these elements has been contested in mainstream American politics for as long as such politics has existed, and the framework Johnson articulates does not so much resolve the contestation as decide it by analytical fiat. If you accept the racial capitalism framework, the political program follows. But that is a large "if," and the program does not become more defensible because the framework has been adopted.

Fourth, the move from program to political imperative. Johnson invokes Du Bois on propitious moments and on the cost of failing to act when they arrive. The implication is that we are at or near such a moment and that the costs of failure will be paid in the blood of future generations. This is rhetorically powerful but politically dangerous. The history of comprehensive political programs justified by appeal to historical urgency is not a happy one. The conservative instinct here — to be suspicious of political imperatives that present themselves as beyond the ordinary processes of deliberation, accommodation, and compromise — is, I think, a sound one. Democratic societies do not get better by being told that the time for deliberation is past; they get better by working through their disagreements within institutions designed for that purpose.

This is not to say that nothing should be done. The conservative tradition is capable of acknowledging real wrongs and supporting real redress; the question is the form that redress should take. Edmund Burke, after all, was a critic of British colonial misrule and a defender of the rights of subject peoples; he was not an opponent of redress as such but a critic of the particular form of redress (the wholesale reconstruction of society) that revolutionary politics preferred. A similar distinction applies here. One can take the history of slavery seriously, support targeted policies that address its continuing consequences, and still resist the comprehensive program Johnson's framework licenses.

What would such a moderate position look like? Several elements come to mind.

It would acknowledge the historical wrongs in clear terms and support official measures of acknowledgment — a formal apology, the inclusion of the history in public commemoration, the preservation of historical sites, the funding of museums and educational programs. These are not trivial measures; the absence of clear official acknowledgment continues to shape American racial politics in damaging ways.

It would support targeted policies that address specific continuing harms — discrimination in housing, education, and employment; the disparate operation of the criminal justice system; the persistent underinvestment in historically black communities. These policies have a moral basis that does not require subscribing to the racial capitalism framework; they can be defended on grounds available to any serious participant in American political life.

It would be more cautious about the broader reparations program, not because the underlying claim lacks moral force but because the program's design raises difficulties (about recipients, amounts, mechanisms, political coalition-building) that have not been adequately resolved. A study commission of the sort H.R. 40 has proposed for nearly thirty years would be a useful next step, and the resistance to even that modest measure tells us something about the current political moment.

It would maintain a robust commitment to the universalist tradition of American politics — the tradition of equal citizenship, equal protection, equal opportunity. This tradition has been imperfectly realized, but its commitments are the basis on which civil-rights advances have been made, and abandoning them in favor of a framework that emphasizes group-based historical claims risks losing more than it gains. The conservative defense of universalism is not a denial of group-based history; it is an insistence that the political response to that history should operate through institutions and principles that command broad assent.

It would, finally, take seriously the conservative case for institutional preservation. The American political order, for all its failures, has been the framework within which significant racial-justice progress has been made — emancipation, Reconstruction (incomplete though it was), the civil-rights revolution, the long if uneven development of equal protection doctrine. The framework Johnson articulates is, in its more ambitious moments, suspicious of these institutions and the universalist tradition they embody. A conservative response would urge that progress is more likely through the institutions we have than against them — that the institutions are works in progress that have produced real if inadequate results, and that dismantling them in favor of an alternative framework is more likely to produce political chaos than substantive justice.

I want to register one more thing, which is the worry I have about the political dynamics of the racial capitalism framework in the current moment. The framework, in its more polemical forms, treats the white working class principally as beneficiary of racial capitalism — as a constituency that has been bought off by the "wages of whiteness" and whose interests are aligned against those of black and brown Americans. Whatever the analytical merits of this view, its political consequences in a moment of rising white nationalist politics are not encouraging. The white working class is, by other measures, in the midst of a serious economic and social crisis. Treating that crisis as either irrelevant or as a deserved consequence of racial privilege is not, I think, a politically productive stance. A more capacious framework would recognize that the white working class, too, has been damaged by the broader processes Johnson describes, and that any politics adequate to the moment must speak to its situation as well as to the situation of black and brown Americans.

Let me close with a concession. The argument I am making is a moderate one, and the moderate position in politics is often the position that ends up being run over by events. Du Bois's argument about propitious moments has real force; sometimes incremental change is inadequate, and the conservative instinct toward institutional preservation can be a way of preserving real injustices. I take this seriously. But I would suggest that the test of whether a moment is propitious in Du Bois's sense is not whether a comprehensive analytical framework licenses comprehensive demands; it is whether the political conditions actually support transformative change. The conditions of February 2017 do not, by my reading, support the transformations Johnson's framework envisions. They support, perhaps, the patient work of building toward a future moment when more becomes possible — and that work is best done within institutions and traditions that can sustain it across the long haul.

Whether Johnson's framework or something like the more modest one I have sketched is the right basis for that patient work is the question on which thoughtful people disagree. I have tried to give my own answer with the respect that Johnson's serious essay deserves. The disagreement, I hope, is itself a sign of the kind of public discourse that the moment requires.

The left has been burned many times by utopian projects that turned into nightmares; by blueprints for a just society that, when implemented, produced new forms of injustice. Better, perhaps, to focus on exposing the injustices of the present than to risk repeating the mistakes of the past. But this posture of permanent critique has costs. It cedes the terrain of the future to those who are perfectly willing to offer their own visions: the libertarian who promises freedom through markets, the nationalist who promises security through walls, the techno-optimist who promises abundance through innovation. If the left cannot say what kind of world it wants, it should not be surprised when other people fill the silence.

He has cited a tradition of thinkers—Du Bois, Williams, Robinson, Davis—who, between them, have provided the intellectual resources for a radical critique of the existing order. The deeper problem, I think, is that Johnson is trying to derive a normative theory of justice from a historical analysis of injustice. This is a category mistake. Knowing how the world became unjust does not, by itself, tell us what a just world would look like. It requires an account of what human beings need to flourish, and of what we owe each other as a matter of principle. The liberal rights tradition that Johnson dismisses has, for all its flaws, attempted to provide such an account. It has said, in various formulations, that every person has a claim to certain basic liberties, certain material minimums, and a certain degree of social recognition. If I am the descendant of enslaved people, I can understand why reparations are owed to me. What do I say to the Hmong refugee whose family was decimated by the CIA’s secret war in Laos? What do I say to the white Appalachian whose ancestors were exploited by coal companies but never owned slaves? This is not to say that a universalist account of justice is easy to construct, or that the liberal tradition has gotten it right. Far from it. The liberal tradition has been blind, again and again, to the ways that its universalism has been undercut by racism, sexism, and imperialism. But the solution to that blindness is not to abandon universalism; it is to make it more genuinely universal—to demand that the circle of concern be expanded to include those who have been left out, and that the principles of justice be tested against the experience of the most vulnerable. That work has been done, brilliantly, by thinkers like Amartya Sen, Martha Nussbaum, and Charles Mills, who have taken the liberal tradition seriously while exposing its failures. Johnson, by contrast, seems to want to throw the whole tradition overboard and start fresh from the history of slavery. I am not convinced that this is possible, or desirable.

Let me conclude with a suggestion. Johnson’s six “virtues” of an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery are, in fact, excellent criteria for evaluating any theory of justice. Any adequate theory should be attentive to global inequalities, to historical legacies, to gender, to the entanglement of race and class, to the relationship between human and environmental exploitation. These are not alternatives to universalist principles; they are tests that any universalist principle must pass. The task before us is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, but to bring them into productive tension—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and hypocrisies in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to generalize the insights gained from the history of slavery. This is not, I think, an accident. The intellectual tradition Johnson draws on has always been stronger on critique than on strategy. Du Bois, for all his brilliance, was never a successful organizer; his various political projects—the Niagara Movement, the NAACP, the Pan-African Congresses, the Peace Information Center—had mixed results at best. C. L. R. James’s revolutionary politics were, by his own later admission, out of step with the actual conditions of the mid-twentieth century. Cedric Robinson was a theorist, not a movement-builder. This is not to diminish their contributions; it is simply to note that a theory of justice is not the same thing as a plan of action.

What would a plan of action look like, if it took Johnson’s analysis seriously? I can sketch only the barest outline here, but let me try. The starting point, as Johnson argues, is the recognition that the history of slavery and racial capitalism is not just a matter of past wrongs but of ongoing structures. This means that the struggle for justice is not primarily a struggle for recognition or apology or even monetary compensation, but for the transformation of the institutions that reproduce racial inequality: the financial system that channels capital away from black communities, the criminal justice system that incarcerates black people at staggering rates, the education system that underfunds black schools, the housing market that perpetuates segregation. Each of these institutions has a history that runs back through Jim Crow to slavery, and each requires a specific set of remedies: not just more money or better policies, but a fundamental redesign of the way they operate.

This is an overwhelming agenda, and it cannot be accomplished all at once. The question, then, is how to sequence it—where to start, what to prioritize, how to build momentum. The Movement for Black Lives platform, which Johnson cites approvingly, is one attempt to answer this question. It is a comprehensive document, covering everything from reparations to police reform to environmental justice to education. But it is also, in a sense, a wish list: a catalog of demands that the current political system is nowhere close to meeting. The distance between the platform and the possible is vast, and the question for anyone who takes Johnson’s analysis seriously is how to close that gap.

One path is the path of disruptive protest. The protests in Ferguson, Baltimore, and elsewhere in recent years have succeeded in placing police violence and racial inequality at the center of public debate in a way that decades of policy advocacy did not. This is a real achievement, and it vindicates something important in Johnson’s argument: the idea that the perspectives of the oppressed, when expressed collectively and forcefully, can reshape the political landscape. But protest is a limited tool. It can force issues onto the agenda, but it cannot, by itself, enact legislation or build institutions. The history of social movements suggests that protest must be combined with other forms of political action—electoral organizing, legal advocacy, coalition-building with labor and environmental groups—if it is to produce lasting change.

Another path is the path of electoral politics. The Movement for Black Lives has, to a significant degree, embraced this path, with activists running for office at local, state, and national levels. The idea is to take power within the existing system and use it to enact the reforms that the platform calls for. This is a plausible strategy, but it runs up against the very structures that Johnson’s analysis identifies: the entrenchment of white supremacy in the American political system, the disproportionate influence of money in politics, the gerrymandering and voter suppression that dilute black political power. It also runs up against the limits of reformism: even if black candidates are elected in larger numbers, they will still be operating within a capitalist economy and a carceral state that were built, in part, on the foundations of slavery. How much can be changed from within?

A third path is the path of building alternative institutions. This is the path most deeply rooted in the black radical tradition, from the mutual aid societies of the nineteenth century to the Black Panther Party’s survival programs to the contemporary experiments in worker cooperatives and community land trusts. The idea is to create spaces of autonomy and self-determination that are not dependent on the white-dominated institutions of the larger society. Johnson’s invocation of the “democratic practices and revolutionary thought of black people living under conditions of racial capitalism” points in this direction. But here, too, there are limits: alternative institutions can provide important forms of immediate relief and models for the future, but they cannot, on their own, transform the structures of racial capitalism that operate at the level of the global economy and the nation-state.

My own view is that all three paths are necessary, and that the most important task for the present moment is to build the kind of multiracial, cross-class coalition that can pursue them simultaneously. This is not a new insight. It was the insight of the Popular Front in the 1930s, of the civil rights movement in the 1960s, of the Rainbow Coalition in the 1980s. Each of those coalitions had its limits and its failures, but each also achieved real gains that improved the lives of millions of people. The challenge for the current moment is to build a coalition that is durable enough to survive setbacks, inclusive enough to hold together diverse interests, and radical enough to demand the systemic changes that Johnson’s analysis shows are necessary.

Johnson’s essay does not, in the end, tell us how to do this. But it does something almost as important: it reminds us of the stakes. If this is indeed a propitious moment, and if we fail to take advantage of it, then the cost, as Du Bois warned, will be reckoned in the blood of our children and grandchildren. That is a heavy burden, and Johnson has done us the service of bringing it into view.

There is an old saying among lawyers: hard cases make bad law. The idea is that a particularly extreme or emotionally freighted set of facts can lead judges to craft rules that work poorly when applied to more ordinary circumstances. The case of a child burned by a defective product, for instance, might lead to a rule of strict liability that makes sense for that case but creates perverse incentives in other areas of the law. The solution is not to ignore the hard case, but to think carefully about how the principles it demands can be generalized without doing violence to other important values.

I thought of this maxim while reading Walter Johnson’s essay. Johnson is, in effect, arguing that the history of slavery is a hard case for liberal theories of justice—so hard that it cannot be accommodated within the existing framework of rights, liberties, and distributive fairness. The wrongs of slavery, he argues, are not just extreme instances of injustice that can be remedied by extending existing rights to the enslaved and their descendants. They are qualitatively different: a form of “racial capitalism” that turned human beings into capital, that rested on the reproductive exploitation of women, that was inseparable from Native dispossession and global imperialism, and that continues to structure the distribution of wealth and power in the present. A justice adequate to this history cannot be a justice of individual rights and formal equality; it must be a justice of reparations, redistribution, and fundamental transformation.

I am not unsympathetic to this argument. Johnson is right that the liberal tradition has struggled to account for the legacy of slavery, and right that the remedies it has offered—the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments, the Civil Rights Acts, affirmative action—have been partial and inadequate. But I want to push back against the implication that the liberal tradition is therefore bankrupt. Hard cases, I want to suggest, do not always make bad law. Sometimes they reveal the true meaning of the principles we already hold.

Take the principle of equal citizenship. The liberal tradition, from Locke to Rawls, has insisted that all persons have a fundamental moral claim to be treated as free and equal members of the political community. Johnson sees this principle as inherently limited, because it abstracts away from the material inequalities that make formal equality a mockery for the descendants of enslaved people. But the principle of equal citizenship, properly understood, does not require abstraction from material conditions. On the contrary, it requires that the state take affirmative steps to ensure that all citizens have the means to participate effectively in political and social life. This is the insight behind Rawls’s difference principle, which holds that inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least advantaged. It is the insight behind the idea of “substantive” as opposed to “formal” equality of opportunity—the idea that it is not enough to remove legal barriers to advancement; one must also provide the resources, the education, the health care, and the social supports that make advancement genuinely possible. And it is the insight behind the growing body of scholarship on “structural” or “systemic” discrimination, which recognizes that racial inequality is not simply the product of individual prejudice but of institutional arrangements that reproduce disadvantage across generations.

If we take these insights seriously—and I believe they are firmly within the liberal tradition, even if they have not always been honored in practice—then the case of slavery does not require us to abandon the framework of equal citizenship. It requires us to deepen it. The descendants of enslaved people are not just citizens who happen to be poor; they are citizens whose poverty is the direct result of a centuries-long program of state-sponsored expropriation, exploitation, and exclusion. Equal citizenship for them requires not just formal equality but remedial action: action that acknowledges the specific history of their disadvantage and aims to eliminate its ongoing effects. This is, in its essentials, the argument for reparations, and it is an argument that can be made entirely within the language of liberal justice—indeed, some of its most eloquent exponents, from Thurgood Marshall to Ta-Nehisi Coates, have done exactly that.

Johnson might reply that this is precisely the problem: that the liberal tradition can be stretched to accommodate reparations, but only by losing its distinctiveness. If liberal justice requires the wholesale redistribution of wealth and power, then what distinguishes it from the radical tradition Johnson favors? But this reply misses something important. The radical tradition, as Johnson presents it, is grounded in the particular experience of one group—the enslaved and their descendants—and it calls for remedies that are tailored to that experience. The liberal tradition, by contrast, is grounded in general principles that apply to everyone. The advantage of the liberal approach is precisely that it is general: it provides a framework within which the claims of different groups can be weighed against one another, and it provides a language in which those claims can be justified to people who do not share the same history.

Let me give an example. Johnson’s six virtues include the claim that a justice rooted in the history of slavery should focus on “the extraction and distribution of resources between classes and areas of the world: on the relationship of African American history to Native American history, for example, or on the relationship of either or both of those to the history of the white workers (and merchants and bankers) in the financial and manufacturing centers of the United States and Europe.” This is a commendably expansive vision. But it raises a host of practical questions. How do we balance the claims of African Americans, whose ancestors were enslaved, against the claims of Native Americans, whose ancestors were dispossessed and genocidally slaughtered? Are these claims in competition with one another, or can they be harmonized? What about the claims of more recent immigrants, whose ancestors may have been victimized by other forms of colonialism or oppression but who arrived in the United States after the end of slavery? What about the claims of the white working class, which Johnson acknowledges is both a beneficiary of racial privilege and a victim of capitalist exploitation?

These questions do not answer themselves. They require a framework for adjudication—a set of principles that can tell us, however imperfectly, how to weigh competing claims. The liberal tradition, for all its flaws, has at least attempted to provide such a framework. It has proposed principles of desert, of need, of equality, of reciprocity. These principles are far from settled, and their application to the specific case of slavery and its legacies is a matter of ongoing dispute. But the dispute is productive. It forces us to be precise about what we think justice requires, and to justify our conclusions to others in terms they can, at least in principle, accept.

Johnson’s alternative, by contrast, is to derive justice from a particular historical experience. But this raises the question of authority: why should someone who does not share that experience accept the conclusions that follow from it? If the answer is simply “because the experience reveals truths that you cannot otherwise see,” then we are in the territory of an epistemic privilege that is not easily squared with democratic deliberation. If the answer is “because you are complicit in the structures that produced that experience, whether you know it or not,” then we need an argument about complicity that Johnson does not provide.

None of this is to deny the power of Johnson’s critique. The history of slavery is indeed a hard case for liberal justice, and coming to terms with it may require transforming liberal justice in ways that are hard to predict. But the solution, I want to suggest, is not to abandon the liberal tradition for a particularist account of justice grounded in the history of one group. It is to do the hard work of reconstructing the liberal tradition from within—to use the history of slavery as a test case for our principles, and to revise those principles until they can meet the test. This is a slower, messier process than the one Johnson envisions. But it is, I believe, more likely to produce a justice that can be shared.

But Johnson’s treatment of reproduction raises as many questions as it answers, and I want to open some of them up here.

The first question is conceptual. What does Johnson mean by “reproduction”? The term is slippery, and it has a complex history in Marxist and feminist theory. In the Marxist tradition, “reproduction” refers to the processes by which a mode of production maintains itself over time: not just the production of commodities, but the reproduction of the labor force (through food, shelter, child-rearing) and the reproduction of the social relations that make production possible. In the feminist tradition, “reproduction” has been used to highlight the often-invisible work of bearing and raising children, caring for the sick and elderly, and maintaining households—work that is disproportionately done by women and that is systematically devalued under capitalism. These two uses of “reproduction” overlap but are not identical, and Johnson does not always make it clear which sense he has in mind.

When he says that enslaved women’s bodies were the foundation of racial capitalism because of “their ability to reproduce capital,” he seems to mean something like: enslaved women gave birth to children who were themselves enslaved, and those children became the labor force of the next generation of plantations. This is true, and important. But it is also too narrow. Enslaved women did more than give birth. They worked in the fields alongside men; they cooked, cleaned, sewed, and nursed; they raised children not only their own but those of the master’s family; they provided emotional and sexual labor to both white and black communities. All of this work was essential to the functioning of the plantation economy, and all of it is part of what “reproduction” should mean in an adequate account of racial capitalism.

Johnson nods toward this broader sense when he cites J. D. B. DeBow’s argument for reopening the Atlantic slave trade: either import more slaves, DeBow warned, or “await with folded arms the coming of population and of labor which will be the result of natural increase.” The alternative to the slave trade, in other words, was the reproductive labor of enslaved women. This alternative was cheaper than the slave trade—that was the point—but it was not costless. It required the systematic sexual violation of enslaved women, the breaking of family ties, the constant threat of sale that made every mother a potential childless mourner. The violence of slavery was not just about extracting labor from bodies; it was about controlling reproduction, and that control was both a means of economic accumulation and a site of particular, gendered forms of terror.

Should there be specific remedies for the ongoing effects of reproductive exploitation, such as the disproportionate rates of maternal mortality among Black women in the United States today? These are not rhetorical questions; they are questions that any serious reparations program will have to address, and they are far from settled within the Movement for Black Lives or the broader left.

The right to control one’s own body, to choose whether and when to bear children, to be free from sexual violence and coercion—these are rights claims that have been central to the movement for reproductive justice, a movement that was pioneered by women of color precisely because they understood that reproductive oppression was inseparable from racial and economic oppression. The name of that movement is important: reproductive *justice*, not just reproductive rights. The shift from “rights” to “justice” signals a recognition that formal legal equality is not enough, that the conditions under which women make reproductive decisions—the availability of health care, the absence of coercion, the presence of social support—are as important as the decisions themselves. This is, in many ways, exactly the kind of justice that Johnson is calling for: historically grounded, attentive to the intersections of race and class, focused on the material conditions of life rather than on abstract entitlements.

And yet Johnson seems to want to distance himself from the language of rights entirely, even as his own argument depends on a notion of bodily integrity and self-determination that is, at bottom, a rights claim. When he describes the violation of enslaved women’s bodies as a core wrong of slavery, he is appealing to a standard that goes beyond the particular experience of the enslaved and calls on a principle that is, I would argue, universal. The principle is this: to use a person’s body against their will for the purposes of another is a fundamental violation of that person’s dignity. This principle is not the exclusive property of the liberal tradition; it can be found in many cultures and many philosophies. But it has been articulated most clearly and defended most systematically within the rights tradition, and I do not think we should abandon that tradition just because it has been historically allied with imperialism and capitalism.

The challenge, then, is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, or between particularism and universalism. The challenge is to hold them together—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and silences in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to give moral force to the demands that emerge from the history of slavery. Reproductive justice is a model for how this can be done. It begins with the specific experiences of women of color—their histories of forced sterilization, of coerced contraception, of inadequate health care, of family separation—and it uses those experiences to articulate demands that are framed in the language of rights while also challenging the limits of that language. The result is a politics that is both radical and legible, both particular and universal, both historical and forward-looking.

Johnson’s essay is a call for such a politics, even if it does not fully achieve it. He is right that the history of slavery is a standpoint from which we can see the inadequacies of liberal justice more clearly. He is right that reproductive exploitation was a core feature of racial capitalism and must be a core concern of any movement for repair. What remains is to do the work of translation: to turn the history into a politics, the critique into a program, the memory of the dead into a promise to the living. That work is not yet done, and Johnson’s essay is an invitation to take it up. I am grateful for the invitation, even as I recognize how much remains to be done.

It is an argument for reparations and decolonization to be thought together.

Native dispossession is not simply one example of racial capitalism. It is the founding condition of the settler polity within which racial capitalism developed in North America.

This internal colonial relation complicates any map that imagines exploitation as something that happens over there while metropoles sit over here. The colony is also here.

If we begin only from slavery, we may miss sovereignty and settler replacement. If we begin only from class, we miss race and nation. If we begin only from identity, we miss political economy. The challenge is to hold these histories together without forcing them into a single hierarchy.

A new administration has taken power amid intensified nationalism, border militarization, hostility to Muslims and immigrants, and promises to expand fossil fuel extraction. Native nations will face renewed pressure on land and water. Black communities will face intensified policing and backlash against movements for racial justice. Immigrants will face raids and exclusion. These struggles are connected, but connection should be built through respect, not absorption.

The truth that enslaved people were human, and therefore had rights no one could justly violate, remains the strongest condemnation of slavery we possess. We should deepen that truth, not discard it.
