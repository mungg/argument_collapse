Is the energy used worth the speed and ease we now have on the Internet, or should we wean ourselves from the idea that information and a fast connection should be available instantly, at all times?

The framing of this debate is misleading. The question pits convenience against conservation, but the real issue is engineering discipline, not consumer expectation. Asking users to tolerate slower load times in order to reduce server idling would deliver a rounding error in global emissions while inconveniencing billions. The waste lies upstream, in how data centers are designed and operated.

Consider that the industry average power usage effectiveness (PUE) still hovers near 1.8 or 2.0, meaning nearly as much electricity goes to cooling and overhead as to actual computation. Yet Google reports PUEs around 1.12, and Facebook's Open Compute facility in Prineville is comparable. The technology to halve data center overhead exists; it simply isn't uniformly adopted because electricity has historically been cheap relative to the cost of an outage. Similarly, server utilization rates of 6–12 percent — widely cited by McKinsey and the Uptime Institute — reflect overprovisioning for peak load, not user impatience. Virtualization and better workload scheduling can address this without anyone noticing a slower click.

So yes, the energy is worth it — but that doesn't excuse the waste. Rather than asking consumers to ration information, regulators and large customers should push for transparency on PUE, utilization, and the carbon intensity of the grids powering these facilities. The fix is industrial, not behavioral.

But the 90 percent waste figure cited in the recent New York Times investigation is not a consumer problem—it is an industry problem, and specifically a problem of misaligned incentives.

Data center operators waste electricity because the cost of an outage vastly exceeds the cost of overprovisioning. A retailer that loses its site for an hour during a holiday sale loses millions; a utility bill that runs 30 percent higher than necessary is a rounding error. So servers idle at 6 to 12 percent utilization, diesel generators hum on standby, and battery rooms wait for failures that almost never come. The waste is rational from the operator's perspective and irrational from society's. That is the textbook definition of an externality.

The right response is not asceticism but policy. Three measures would do more than any consumer behavior change. First, require large data centers to publicly report Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) and total consumption, the way we require corporate financial disclosures. Sunlight has already pushed Google and Facebook to compete on efficiency; expanding disclosure would extend that competition to laggards like the financial-services and government data centers that drag the industry average down. Second, price carbon. Much of the cited waste is dirty because data centers cluster in coal-heavy grids like Virginia and the Carolinas; a carbon price would shift siting decisions toward cleaner regions and renewable contracts. Third, reform utility regulation so that operators can more easily buy directly from wind and solar producers, as Google has done in Oklahoma.

Asking individuals to "wean themselves" from instant information is both futile and beside the point. The marginal user has essentially no leverage over data center design. Meanwhile, the same digital infrastructure that consumes this electricity enables videoconferencing that displaces flights, telecommuting that displaces commutes, and logistics optimization that displaces truck miles. The net environmental ledger of the Internet is almost certainly positive. What we need is not less Internet, but a less wasteful version of it—and that requires regulators and engineers, not penitent users.

A search result that arrives in 800 milliseconds instead of 200 is not noticeably worse for any human purpose, yet the engineering required to guarantee the 200-millisecond figure—redundant servers, geographic replication, idle capacity waiting for traffic spikes—is what produces most of the waste the Times documented.
