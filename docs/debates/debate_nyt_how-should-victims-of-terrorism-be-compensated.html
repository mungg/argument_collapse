How Should Victims of Terrorism Be Compensated?

After each national tragedy, the American people ask: How can we help? But the answer changes each time. After the terrorist attacks in 2001, Congress set up a taxpayer-financed fund to compensate victims and their families – and to prevent lawsuits against the airlines. After the Newtown shootings in 2012, nonprofit groups distributed (or did not distribute) donations. After the Boston Marathon bombing last year, a single fund collected contributions, and its administrator decided how to divide them up.

Should the U.S. government compensate victims of mass violence like bombings and shooting sprees? Or should compensation come from other sources, like lawsuits and private donations?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The instinct to "do something" for victims is generous, but it has produced an inconsistent patchwork that ends up being deeply unfair. The 9/11 fund paid families an average of over $2 million each; the families of the 26 killed at Sandy Hook had to fight over donations from a charity, and survivors of everyday gun violence in Chicago or New Orleans get essentially nothing beyond state crime-victim funds that cap payouts in the tens of thousands. If we believe victims deserve compensation as a matter of justice, we cannot keep distributing it by the emotional gravity of the news cycle.

There is a principled case for a permanent federal compensation program, but only if it covers all victims of mass violence — terrorism, school shootings, workplace rampages — on roughly equal terms. Ken Feinberg, who administered both the 9/11 fund and the One Fund Boston, has himself acknowledged that the 9/11 model was a one-off born of a specific fear: airline bankruptcy. Treating Boston survivors generously while ignoring the dozens shot in a single weekend in any major city sends an indefensible message about whose suffering counts.

Lawsuits are a poor substitute. Terrorists are usually dead or judgment-proof; suing gun manufacturers is largely barred by the 2005 PLCAA; suing landlords or schools for inadequate security produces erratic results and punishes the wrong parties. Private donations, meanwhile, are driven by media attention and produce both windfalls and bitter disputes over allocation, as we saw painfully at Newtown.

The better path is to expand and standardize state crime-victim compensation, federally backstopped, with a supplemental tier triggered by federally designated mass-casualty events. Compensation should focus on documented economic losses — medical bills, lost wages, long-term care — rather than speculative valuations of a life. That approach treats victims as citizens entitled to a safety net, not as lottery winners whose payout depends on how telegenic their tragedy was.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The impulse to help victims of mass violence is as immediate as it is noble, yet the way we channel that compassion reveals deep-seated tensions over fairness, responsibility, and the role of the state. The patchwork of responses after 9/11, Newtown, and the Boston Marathon bombing shows that ad hoc charity and litigation are poor substitutes for a clear, principled system. The federal government should establish a permanent compensation fund for victims of terrorism and mass shootings, not as an exclusive remedy, but as a reliable floor beneath which no victim should fall.

Private donations, however generous, are inherently arbitrary. After the Sandy Hook shootings, more than $11 million in donations sat undistributed for months while committees debated eligibility, angering families who had immediate funeral costs and medical bills. Some funds went directly to the school district for a memorial, not to the families who lost children. In Boston, the One Fund was more streamlined, but it still relied on the subjective judgments of a single administrator—Kenneth Feinberg—who had to weigh the worth of a lost limb against a lost life, a calculus that no private citizen should be forced to make alone. Charitable giving also follows media attention; victims of less photogenic tragedies are routinely left behind.

Lawsuits offer equally uneven justice. The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund was created partly to shield airlines from crippling litigation, but it also guaranteed swift, no-fault payments to families who would otherwise have faced years of legal wrangling with uncertain outcomes. Those with skilled lawyers or sympathetic juries might win massive awards, while others—especially the uninsured or low-income victims—would get little. Litigation channels public grief into adversarial contests that deepen trauma rather than heal it.

A government fund, by contrast, can provide immediate, standardized relief based on clear criteria: loss of life, permanent disability, medical expenses, and lost income. It ensures that compensation reflects societal solidarity, not the luck of the charity draw or the lottery of litigation. This is not a radical idea; many nations already operate victim compensation schemes for violent crime, recognizing that the state’s core duty is to protect its citizens. When that protection fails in the face of terrorism or mass violence, the state has a moral obligation to respond directly.

To be sure, a federal fund should not become the sole avenue for redress. Private donations can still cover gaps—scholarship funds for children, mental health services not covered by insurance—and lawsuits should remain an option when government negligence demonstrably contributed to the tragedy, as in cases of intelligence failures or security lapses. But the baseline must be public, predictable, and insulated from the whims of donors and the capriciousness of the courts. As we mark another year since Boston and twelve years since Newtown, the question is no longer whether the government should act, but why it hasn’t yet created a permanent mechanism to do so. The patchwork has failed; we owe survivors more than a lottery.

Whenever tragedy strikes, Americans reflexively open their wallets. But as the stark disparities between the aftermath of 9/11, Newtown, and the Boston Marathon bombing demonstrate, our ad-hoc approach to compensating victims of mass violence is fundamentally inequitable. Relying on the unpredictable winds of public attention or the grueling grind of the civil justice system leaves too many victims behind. To ensure fairness, the United States needs a standardized, federally backed compensation framework for victims of terrorism.

Lawsuits are an agonizingly slow and uncertain remedy. Victims and their families can spend years in court fighting well-funded corporate or institutional defendants over liability, often compounding their trauma without any guarantee of financial relief. Private donations, while noble, are similarly flawed as a primary safety net. The One Fund Boston successfully distributed tens of millions of dollars, largely because the bombing occurred in a major media market during a beloved international event. Lesser-known tragedies simply do not generate the same philanthropic outpouring, creating an unequal hierarchy of victimhood where compensation depends heavily on media coverage rather than actual human need. Furthermore, as the aftermath of the Newtown shootings showed, delegating distribution to disparate nonprofits can lead to severe bureaucratic delays, lack of transparency, and bitter community disputes.

A permanent federal compensation fund, triggered by official declarations of terrorism or mass violence, would resolve these deep inequities. The September 11th Victim Compensation Fund proved that a centralized administrative process, guided by a neutral special master, can distribute aid swiftly and fairly. While the 2001 fund was uniquely designed to protect the airline industry from financial ruin, its core mechanism—offering guaranteed, rapid financial support in exchange for waivers of certain litigation rights—should be adapted as a permanent model.

Private philanthropy will always play a vital role in national healing, and citizens should absolutely continue to give. But charity cannot replace sound public policy. When American citizens are targeted in acts of terrorism or mass violence, their physical and financial recovery should not depend on the whims of the 24-hour news cycle or the endurance to survive a decade-long lawsuit. The government has a duty to provide a predictable, equitable baseline of support.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

The first principle should be that compensation is not the same as charity, tort liability or a memorial. It is a public acknowledgment that, when people are killed or maimed in attacks aimed at a community, the injury is partly civic. That does not mean every victim can be “made whole,” or that grief can be priced. But it does mean we should not leave assistance to the accident of where a donation drive catches fire or whether a solvent defendant can be sued.

The United States already has pieces of a better system. State crime-victim compensation programs, supported in part by the federal Crime Victims Fund, help with medical bills, funeral costs and lost wages. After Sept. 11, Congress created a much broader fund, partly to stabilize the airline industry by limiting litigation. After Boston, private giving was impressively swift, but the distribution of millions of dollars rested largely on ad hoc judgments by an administrator. Newtown showed the opposite problem: donations can be fragmented, delayed and disputed. These cases differ, but the moral claim of the badly injured child, runner, office worker or teacher should not depend so heavily on improvisation.

A sensible approach would create a standing national framework for mass violence: limited, prompt federal compensation for death, catastrophic injury, serious physical injury, and documented economic losses, available whether the perpetrator is a foreign terrorist, a domestic extremist or a mass shooter. The threshold should be high enough to avoid turning every crime into a federal compensation case, but not so narrow that victims of politically undefined atrocities are excluded. Payments should be transparent, with published categories and procedures, and coordinated with state programs, insurance and workers’ compensation to avoid duplication.

Private donations should remain welcome, but they should supplement rather than substitute for a public baseline. Donors may want to help with counseling, scholarships, community rebuilding or needs that formulas miss. Lawsuits should also remain available when there is genuine negligence or misconduct — for example, against a security provider, gun seller or institution that violated a legal duty. But litigation is slow, uncertain and often mismatched to urgent needs. It also distributes money according to provable fault, not necessarily according to harm.

The Sept. 11 fund was justified in part by exceptional economic concerns, so it should not become an unlimited precedent. Still, the lesson should not be that only some tragedies merit national responsibility. The better lesson is that compassion needs rules before the next attack, not after it.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The question of how to compensate victims of mass violence admits no single right answer, but the historical record suggests that a government role—even a limited one—offers advantages that private mechanisms alone cannot replicate.

Pure private charity, as seen in Newtown, produces inconsistent results. Distribution depends on which organizations receive donations, how efficiently they operate, and often on which victims attract media attention. Families who suffered equally but quietly sometimes received far less. This arbitrary distribution is not merely unfair; it compounds trauma by forcing victims into competition for sympathy.

Lawsuits provide compensation but only for those with the resources and patience to litigate, and often only against parties with deep pockets. After 9/11, lawsuits against airlines threatened the industry's survival while providing uncertain, delayed recovery to families. Tort litigation also creates perverse incentives: plaintiffs seek defendants with insurance, not necessarily those most responsible.

The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund demonstrated what government action can accomplish. It provided guaranteed, relatively quick payments without forcing families into adversarial litigation. The tradeoff—limiting lawsuits against airlines—reflected a policy judgment that broader economic interests justified streamlined compensation.

Going forward, a tiered approach makes sense. Government should provide a baseline of immediate support—medical care, funeral expenses, basic living assistance—through emergency funds established in advance of disasters. This removes the chaos of ad hoc fundraising and ensures that no victim is forgotten for lack of media attention.

Private donations and civil litigation should supplement but not replace this foundation. Victims retain the right to sue responsible parties, but initial compensation comes from public sources designed to deliver it quickly and fairly. This acknowledges that mass casualties are not merely private misfortunes but events that strain communities and implicate public interests in security and recovery.

What we should avoid is the current pattern of improvisation. Each tragedy produces a new debate about how to help, leaving victims uncertain and officials reacting instead of responding. A standing framework—with clear government responsibilities and defined roles for private action—would serve both efficiency and justice.

**Create a Permanent Federal Fund — and Stop Improvising**

The pattern is by now familiar and embarrassing. A tragedy occurs, the nation grieves, and then we scramble to invent a compensation scheme from scratch. After 9/11, Congress hurriedly stood up a special master's fund. After Newtown, a confused patchwork of nonprofits collected money that some families never saw. After Boston, Ken Feinberg was pulled out of semi-retirement to do once again what he had done before. Each time, victims and donors are left wondering whether their case will be treated like the last one or invented anew.

This is no way to run a civilized response to mass violence. The United States should establish a permanent federal compensation program for victims of terrorist attacks and mass shootings, with clear eligibility rules, a published benefits schedule, and a standing administrator. Australia, the United Kingdom and Israel all have versions of this. We have one too, technically — every state runs a Crime Victim Compensation program funded partly by the federal Victims of Crime Act — but the caps are absurdly low (often $25,000 or less) and few people know it exists.

A permanent federal fund would do three things at once. It would give victims certainty: they would know in the chaotic days after an attack that medical bills, lost wages, and funeral costs will be covered, without having to wait for a charity drive or a lawsuit. It would protect against the inequities that emerge whenever ad hoc funds divide donations: the Boston fund paid amputees who lost one leg differently from those who lost two, generating heartbreak and resentment. And it would remove the political pressure that distorts each new crisis, where the size of the payout depends on how telegenic the tragedy was.

The standard objection is fairness: why should a person killed by a terrorist receive more than a person killed by a drunk driver? It is a serious question, but the answer is that mass violence is different in kind — it is an attack on the polity itself, an event for which the state bears some responsibility for prevention. Compensating its victims is not charity; it is an acknowledgment that they were harmed in our place. We should institutionalize that acknowledgment rather than reinvent it after every funeral.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**No Special Treatment for Terror Victims**

There is something deeply uncomfortable about the premise that victims of terrorism deserve a different — and usually far more generous — response than victims of ordinary violence. The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund paid an average of roughly $2 million per death. The family of a child killed by a stray bullet in Chicago receives nothing remotely comparable. The mother of a soldier killed in Iraq receives a $100,000 death gratuity. Why is the manner of one's death the measure of one's worth?

The honest answer is that it isn't, and we should stop pretending otherwise. The 9/11 fund was not a principled exercise in victim compensation; it was a political bargain to shield the airlines from ruinous litigation. Congress essentially bought a tort waiver from the families. That deal made some sense in the unique aftermath of an event that threatened the solvency of an entire industry, but it set a precedent we cannot afford and should not want.

What we need instead is a level baseline. Every American who is killed or seriously injured by a violent crime — terror or otherwise — should be eligible for the same package of benefits through existing state crime-victim compensation programs, which should be modestly expanded and better publicized. Health insurance and disability insurance should cover the rest, as they would for any other catastrophic injury. Where private actors have been negligent — a marathon organizer that ignored security warnings, a school district that left doors unlocked — let the tort system do its work, with no special carve-outs.

Beyond that, private philanthropy can and does respond when the public is moved. Americans gave tens of millions to Newtown and Boston families. That generosity is admirable, but it should not be confused with a coherent public policy. Letting it remain voluntary acknowledges what it actually is: an expression of solidarity, not a statutory entitlement.

The deeper problem with bespoke terror funds is that they encourage us to rank victims by the political salience of their deaths. That is a moral hierarchy we should be ashamed to maintain. Equal protection of the laws ought to mean equal treatment after the worst happens, too.

**Let Lawsuits Do Their Job**

Before we reach for the taxpayer's wallet, we should remember that the United States already has a sophisticated system for compensating people who are wrongfully harmed: the civil courts. The tort system, for all its flaws, has real virtues that government compensation funds lack. It investigates fault. It deters negligence. It forces institutions — landlords, security firms, gun sellers, event organizers — to internalize the costs of the risks they create. And it pays for itself.

The 9/11 fund deserves more skepticism than it usually receives precisely because it short-circuited this process. In exchange for compensation, families had to waive their right to sue the airlines, whose lax cockpit-door policies and screening lapses had been criticized for years. We will never know what discovery in those lawsuits might have revealed, or what safety reforms litigation might have driven. Congress decided the airlines were too important to fail and used taxpayer money to make that decision palatable to grieving families. That is a precedent, not a model.

Boston is instructive in the opposite direction. The One Fund Boston distributed roughly $61 million in private donations without preempting anyone's legal claims. Victims can still pursue the bombers' estates (admittedly thin), negligent parties if any are identified, and their own insurers. The charity money fills a gap; it doesn't foreclose accountability.

This is the right division of labor. Private philanthropy provides immediate relief in the days and weeks after a tragedy, when people need money for travel, funerals, and medical co-pays. Insurance — health, life, disability — handles long-term costs, which is what insurance is for. And litigation pursues whoever bears legal responsibility, whether that's a gun manufacturer that marketed irresponsibly or a building owner that ignored security warnings.

Government compensation should be reserved for cases where the state itself bears clear responsibility — soldiers, first responders, victims of failures by federal agencies that had specific intelligence and failed to act. Otherwise, taxpayer-funded compensation is a tempting shortcut that absolves the actually-culpable and inflates the public's sense that Washington can solve any problem by writing a check. It can't, and shouldn't try.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Strengthen What We Already Have: State Victim Compensation**

Lost in nearly every conversation about terrorism compensation is the fact that the United States has been quietly compensating crime victims for decades. Every state runs a Crime Victim Compensation program, most of them funded by a mix of court fees, criminal fines, and federal money from the Victims of Crime Act. These programs paid out roughly half a billion dollars last year to victims of assaults, sexual violence, drunk-driving crashes, and yes, mass shootings. They are unglamorous, bureaucratic, and they work.

The problem is that they are too small and too obscure. Most state programs cap benefits at $25,000 to $50,000 per victim, which is fine for funeral expenses but inadequate for catastrophic long-term injury. Many require victims to exhaust other sources first and to navigate a paperwork-heavy claims process. Awareness is dismal: surveys consistently find that the majority of eligible victims never apply.

The right reform is to build on this existing infrastructure rather than invent new mechanisms after each headline-grabbing attack. Raise the caps. Expand eligibility to include mental health treatment, which mass-shooting survivors often need for years. Fund outreach so that hospitals and police departments routinely inform victims of their rights. And create a federal supplemental program that automatically tops up state benefits whenever a mass-casualty event overwhelms a state fund's capacity.

This approach has several advantages over the bespoke-fund model. It treats the victim of a workplace shooting in Wichita the same as the victim of a bombing in Boston, which is more morally defensible than our current habit of compensating the famous tragedies generously and the obscure ones not at all. It removes the political theater of debating each new compensation scheme during a moment of grief. And it preserves a role for private charity to address the genuinely unusual needs that no statutory program can anticipate.

The 9/11 fund will probably always stand as a unique response to a unique event. But Boston, Aurora, Newtown, Tucson, Virginia Tech — these are tragically not so unusual anymore. We need a system, not a series of improvisations. The bones of that system already exist; what's missing is the political will to give them flesh.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**The Real Lesson of Boston: Trust Civil Society**

Watching how Boston handled the marathon bombing should make us cautious about expanding federal compensation programs. Within hours, neighbors were offering their homes to stranded runners. Within days, a fund had been organized. Within weeks, more than $60 million had been raised and Ken Feinberg had agreed to administer it without taking a fee. By the one-year mark, every dollar had been distributed under a transparent protocol that the public broadly accepted as fair.

This is what a functioning civil society looks like, and it is something American communities are unusually good at. We should think hard before we replace it with a federal program, because government compensation tends to crowd out private generosity. When Washington is on the hook, donors give less. When professional bureaucrats run the show, communities lose the rituals of collective response — the bake sales, the benefit concerts, the GoFundMe pages — that are themselves part of how a wounded community heals.

This doesn't mean the federal role should be zero. There is a legitimate case for a backstop in truly catastrophic cases like 9/11, where the scale exceeds any plausible private response. There is a case for ensuring that no victim of a federally preventable attack goes bankrupt from medical bills, which is mostly an argument about health insurance generally. And there is a strong case for compensating first responders and federal employees harmed in the line of duty.

But for the run of mass-violence events — the shootings and bombings that, terribly, recur every few months — the right model is the one Boston demonstrated. Private fundraising, transparently administered, supplemented by existing state crime-victim programs and the tort system where culpable parties can be found. The government's role is to coordinate, not to write checks.

There is also something to be said for the moral meaning of voluntary giving. A check from the Treasury says: the state owes you this. A check from millions of strangers says: we, your fellow citizens, choose to stand with you. Both are valuable. But in a country where civic ties feel increasingly thin, we should be careful not to nationalize one of the few remaining occasions when Americans reliably show up for each other.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Stop Pretending This Is About Compensation**

Let's be honest about what these funds actually do. They do not "compensate" anyone in any meaningful sense. No amount of money compensates a parent for a murdered child or a runner for blown-off legs. The Victim Compensation Fund did not make 9/11 families whole; nothing could. What these funds do — and what they are really for — is to express, in the most tangible idiom available to a modern state, the proposition that the rest of us see what happened and refuse to look away.

Once we understand the funds as expressive rather than restorative, the policy debate looks different. The question isn't "what's the right algorithm for valuing a life?" — Ken Feinberg has spent fifteen years explaining that there isn't one. The question is: what gesture do we, the political community, want to make when violence tears one of our own?

Seen that way, the case for federal action in cases of mass terrorism is strong, because terrorism is a political act aimed at the polity itself. When someone is killed in a robbery, the state has failed in its general duty to keep order, but the victim was incidental. When someone is killed in a terrorist attack, the victim was instrumentalized — chosen precisely because of what they represented. The state's response should reflect that distinction, not by paying more, but by paying at all in the name of the public.

For ordinary mass violence — the shootings that are tragically not political in motivation — the case for federal compensation is weaker, and the expressive work is better done by communities and charities. The patchwork we have, where Newtown families received millions and a single homicide victim in Baltimore receives nothing comparable, is genuinely ugly. But the solution is not to extend lavish compensation to every victim of violence; the solution is to be honest that what we are doing in these special cases is making a political statement, and to confine the statement to events that warrant one.

The frame of "compensation" obscures all of this. We should drop it. Call it solidarity, call it recognition, call it national mourning made material. Just don't call it making victims whole. They never are.

A national wound demands a national response. When terrorists strike or a gunman turns a school into a slaughterhouse, we confront a collective failure—of security, of mental health systems, of social cohesion. In those moments, the government should step forward with a compensation fund, not as a generous gift, but as an acknowledgment of shared responsibility. A society that cannot prevent every act of mass violence should at least guarantee that its victims are not left financially ruined. That is not a concession to failure; it is a basic pact of citizenship.

The impulse to have the government write checks after every mass shooting or bombing is understandable but misguided. It nationalizes private grief, turns taxpayers into insurers of last resort, and erodes the very mechanisms meant to deter negligence and distribute responsibility: the tort system and voluntary charity. The government’s proper role is to prevent crime and punish perpetrators, not to compensate victims from the public purse every time horrors occur.

The tort system, for all its flaws, serves a vital function. When a property owner fails to provide adequate security, when a gun manufacturer knowingly markets weapons to dangerous people, or when an airline lets a terrorist slip through screening, lawsuits force accountability. They generate discovery, expose negligence, and drive safer practices. The 9/11 fund, by shielding the airlines from litigation, arguably short‑circuited that accountability. A blanket government fund for mass violence would similarly let potential defendants off the hook—why fix security if Uncle Sam will pick up the tab?

Private charity, meanwhile, is flexible, decentralized, and genuinely voluntary. After the Boston Marathon bombing, One Fund Boston raised over $60 million and distributed it with relative speed and transparency. Yes, there were debates about how to allocate funds, but those debates reflected genuine community deliberation, not a bureaucratic formula imposed from Washington. Charity allows donors to target their support to causes and communities they care about, and it preserves the personal, human dimension of aid—neighbors helping neighbors.

There is also a moral hazard in making the state the perpetual compensator. If every tragedy comes with a guaranteed payout, we risk normalizing mass violence as just another insurable event, and we blunt the urgent question of why these attacks happen in the first place. Compensation should not become a sedative that quiets demands for real prevention. Instead, we should strengthen the ability of victims to sue those responsible and encourage a robust culture of private giving, so that assistance flows from authentic solidarity rather than a government mandate. Let the state focus on policing and security, and let citizens—freely and directly—care for the wounded.

The question conflates two distinct tragedies: terrorism and mass shootings. The government should compensate victims of terrorism because such attacks are assaults on the nation itself; mass shootings, however horrific, are primarily crimes against individuals and communities, and should be addressed through other means. This distinction is neither cold nor legalistic—it reflects the unique character of political violence.

When a bomb explodes at the Boston Marathon, the target is not just three dead and hundreds wounded. It is an attack on our public life, our open society, our civic rituals. The perpetrators seek to terrorize the entire populace. In responding, the state acts not as a charitable bystander but as the entity directly challenged. The 9/11 fund was justified not because flying planes into buildings is worse than a school shooting, but because the attack was an act of war by a foreign enemy. A nation at war compensates its wounded; it is the least we can do for those who took the shrapnel meant for all of us.

Mass shootings, on the other hand, while often politically motivated, arise from a different set of failures. They involve domestic criminals, frequently with untreated mental illness, wielding guns they legally or illegally obtained. The state has a role in preventing these tragedies through gun laws and mental health services, but it does not have the same obligation to indemnify their victims. Here, civil lawsuits against negligent parties (schools that ignored warning signs, dealers who sold firearms illegally) and private donations are more appropriate. The Newtown donations, despite administrative hiccups, ultimately delivered over $11 million. The outcry over distribution was itself part of a necessary communal conversation about what we owe one another after a local catastrophe, not a national one.

A government terrorism fund—permanent, well‑administered, and strictly limited to political violence—would eliminate the ad‑hoc scramble after each attack. It would signal that when our nation is struck, we stand together. For all other mass tragedies, let communities, charities, and courts carry the burden. That keeps the state’s role focused where it belongs: on the external and ideological threats that target the very fabric of our democracy.

We should not be asking whether victims of terrorism deserve government compensation. We should be asking why only the victims of spectacular, cable‑news tragedies get any help at all. Every year, thousands of Americans are shot, stabbed, or otherwise violently assaulted. Most receive nothing beyond what their own insurance covers—if they have any. The uneven, ad‑hoc response to Newtown, Boston, and Aurora reveals a profound injustice: our compassion is triggered by body counts and headlines, not by need.

A just society would establish a universal violent‑crime compensation fund, available to every victim of criminal violence, regardless of whether their suffering aired on CNN. Such a fund would operate on clear, needs‑based criteria: covering medical expenses, lost income, rehabilitation, and funeral costs, with a floor and a cap to ensure equity. This is not a radical idea; many states already run victim compensation programs, but they are chronically underfunded and limited in scope. The federal government could provide a backstop, funded perhaps by a modest surcharge on firearms and ammunition, or by fines levied on corporations that violate safety regulations, linking the cost to the sources of harm.

The current patchwork—a special congressional appropriation after 9/11, chaotic donations after Newtown, a privately administered pool after Boston—creates perverse incentives. It encourages survivors to become public advocates for their own tragedy to draw donations, while quietly ignoring that a drive‑by shooting in Chicago left a family equally devastated. It also fosters resentment: why did the marathon victims get more than the families of children murdered in a classroom? Arbitrariness breeds cynicism about the entire process.
