Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet have produced one of the most bracing diagnoses of the contemporary liberal predicament I have read in a long while. Their core observation—that mid-century liberals tried to wall off both constitutional interpretation and economic policy from democratic politics, and that conservatives never accepted the bargain—is correct, and it explains an enormous amount about how we got here. The "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition they recover from the Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Dealers is a genuinely usable past, and their three-part program (reclaiming the First Amendment, re-fusing race and political economy, repoliticizing antitrust and corporate law) sketches the outlines of a constitutional politics worth fighting for. And yet I want to press on three points where their argument feels too confident, or too thin, given what the next few years look likely to demand.

First, the asymmetry problem. The essay treats the diffusion of constitutional argument into public debate as a kind of equalizer: if progressives just stop ceding the field to courts and lawyers, the people themselves can reshape what the Constitution means. But the conservative legal movement spent fifty years doing exactly this work *while also* capturing the federal judiciary and the Republican Party's judicial-selection apparatus. They did not merely make better arguments at town halls; they built the Federalist Society, packed lower courts, blockaded Merrick Garland, and rammed through three Trump appointees. Popular constitutionalism without institutional power is a sermon. The lead essay is right that not speaking about the Constitution cedes ground to courts—but speaking about it without the votes to confirm judges, the seats to expand the bench, or the procedural willingness to strip jurisdiction simply enriches the rhetorical environment in which a 6-3 majority continues to do whatever it likes. The Dobbs draft leaked earlier this month is a fair preview. The authors mention judicial reform mostly to dismiss restorationist nostalgia, but they say strikingly little about court expansion, term limits, or jurisdiction-stripping—all of which would be the muscle behind the rhetoric they propose.

Second, the historical tradition is more equivocal than the essay lets on. The Jacksonian anti-oligarchy tradition was inseparable from Indian removal and the herrenvolk democracy of the white republic; its "many" was conspicuously not everyone. The New Deal labor settlement, as the authors themselves know, was bought partly with the exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers, a concession to Southern Democrats that helped lock in Black economic subordination for another generation. Reconstruction is the cleanest precedent, and even there the political economy program (land redistribution, federal enforcement) was abandoned within a decade. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is real, but it is also a tradition of partial coalitions and racial bargains. Reviving it as constitutional argument requires confronting, not just incorporating, that history—otherwise the appeal to "American traditions of constitutional argument" risks reproducing the very compromises that made those traditions politically viable in the first place. The authors gesture at this in their section on race and the ACA, but the tension between coalitional politics and racial justice deserves more weight than it gets.

Third, the shield-versus-sword distinction is more porous than the essay suggests. The authors say conservatives wield constitutional political economy as a sword to strike down legislation, while progressives should use it as a shield to defend legislation. This is rhetorically attractive but probably unstable. If progressives genuinely believe that something like the ACA is constitutionally *required*, that wealth concentration violates the anti-oligarchy commitments of the Constitution, that the First Amendment imposes affirmative duties on the state to build countervailing worker power—then those claims have offensive implications too. They imply, for example, that a state's failure to expand Medicaid is itself unconstitutional, or that Taft-Hartley's restrictions on secondary boycotts are constitutional violations Congress must repeal. Once you say the Constitution *requires* a certain political economy, you have armed future courts—including hostile ones—to enforce that requirement on their terms. The Lochner Court was, in its own self-understanding, defending a constitutional political economy. The history the authors recover is precisely a history of legislators arguing against judicial supremacy, but that argument only worked when the elected branches were strong enough to face the Court down. It is not obvious that "departmentalism" is a stable equilibrium rather than a transitional posture en route to either court reform or court capitulation.

None of this is fatal to the project. The authors are right that liberals' technocratic retreat has been a strategic disaster, that the First Amendment has been weaponized in ways that demand a constitutional response and not just a doctrinal one, and that antitrust and corporate law are constitutional terrain whether or not we admit it. The PRO Act, H.R. 1, a robust wealth tax with backstops—these are exactly the kinds of measures progressives should be pushing, and pushing in explicitly constitutional terms. But the constitutional argument needs to be paired with the institutional argument it has been studiously avoiding. Make progressive politics constitutional again, yes. But also: reform the Court, or be prepared to watch the constitutional argument be answered by five robed men.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The lead essay by Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet offers a bracing historical narrative and a provocative strategic recommendation: progressives must re-embrace constitutional argument about political economy, drawing on the anti-oligarchy tradition running from the Jacksonians through Reconstruction and the New Deal. The authors warn that by ceding the terrain of constitutional meaning to courts, liberals have disarmed themselves, while conservatives have methodically weaponized the First Amendment, federalism, and other doctrines to dismantle egalitarian legislation. They call for a deliberate effort to show that the Constitution not only permits but compels the broad distribution of power and opportunity, and they point to promising developments in labor law, antitrust, and racial-justice advocacy as evidence that this revival is already underway. As a diagnosis of our present predicament, the essay is incisive and historically rich. But as a strategy for confronting an archconservative Supreme Court, it warrants deeper scrutiny. In particular, the essay understates the institutional and cultural obstacles to the constitutional politics it envisions, overestimates the persuasive power of progressive constitutional narrative inside the courtroom, and underestimates the risk that this very approach might further entrench judicial supremacy rather than challenge it.

The authors’ central historical claim is that during periods of reform, elected officials and movements openly contested the Court’s interpretations and asserted their own constitutional visions, and that these public arguments helped shift legal doctrine and political possibility. The New Deal is the key example: Roosevelt and his allies argued that industrial despotism threatened constitutional democracy, and that federal labor protections were an affirmative governmental duty. Yet the analogy to our moment falters in crucial respects. The New Deal Court realignment occurred only after a protracted fight that included court-packing threats, massive electoral victories, and a judiciary already divided and, in part, open to change. Today’s conservative supermajority is younger, more ideologically cohesive, and structurally insulated, with lifetime tenure and a jurisprudence that treats many of the very areas the authors want to reclaim—campaign finance, labor rights, federal regulatory power—as settled against progressive intervention. The argument that lawmakers have a “constitutional duty” to preserve democratic political economy, even if passionately made in the public square, would almost certainly be treated by this Court as a mere policy preference, not a binding legal command. The justices would not need to be persuaded because they have already constructed an elaborate doctrinal apparatus that translates conservative economic premises into constitutional imperatives: that spending money is speech, that protecting corporate property is a takings concern, that states possess “equal sovereignty.” Mere invocation of an older tradition is unlikely to dislodge these edifices; it risks being dismissed as a flavor of living constitutionalism the majority explicitly rejects.

This points to a deeper tension in the essay’s strategic vision. The authors say they want to show “that all branches of government, and the people themselves, have the authority and duty to debate what our constitutional principles require.” That is an argument against judicial monopoly on constitutional meaning, yet many of their specific recommendations—such as devising legislative fallbacks if a wealth tax is struck down, or crafting campaign-finance reforms that might survive current doctrine—seem to accept the Court’s finality. The “shield” metaphor, in which progressive constitutional arguments are wielded primarily to defend legislation, implicitly acknowledges that the courts will remain the ultimate arbiters. The danger is that re-constitutionalizing politics in this defensive mode reinforces, rather than undermines, the notion that judicial review is the legitimate umpire of these fundamental contests. Historically, the democracy-of-opportunity tradition was often accompanied by direct challenges to the Court’s authority: Roosevelt’s court-packing plan, congressional jurisdiction stripping, Reconstruction’s restructuring of federal courts. The lead essay notes these precedents but does not advocate similar measures, perhaps because the present political conditions make them nearly unthinkable. But without a credible threat to the Court’s power, a purely rhetorical reclamation of constitutional discourse may end up supplying the conservative justices with more opportunities to authoritatively reject progressive meanings, deepening the very impression of judicial legitimacy that the authors wish to counter.

Moreover, the essay’s treatment of the First Amendment illustrates a further risk. It correctly observes that the Court has reframed campaign-finance and labor cases as clashes between a lone speaker and the regulatory state, erasing the collective self-government interests of working people. The authors urge progressives to articulate an alternative First Amendment vision centered on countervailing power. But the difficulty is that the current Court’s First Amendment jurisprudence is not simply a mistaken reading that can be corrected by better historical or theoretical arguments; it is an ideological commitment embedded in a larger vision of liberty that treats economic inequality as natural and voluntary associations as threats to individual autonomy only when they are collective. Even if elected officials and movements embrace a pro-labor, anti-oligarchy First Amendment, the justices can dismiss it as constitutionally irrelevant, as they did when they effectively imposed right-to-work on public-sector unions in *Janus*. The lead essay acknowledges that arguments inside the court may seem futile, but suggests that the real work is to shift public understanding and ultimately influence judicial outcomes. That may be a long-term project, yet the urgency of the problems the essay identifies—climate change, voting rights, economic inequality—suggests that time is short. One might ask whether the energy spent developing a constitutional counter-narrative would be better directed toward building political power to pass legislation despite the Court, or toward more aggressive structural reforms such as court expansion or term limits, which many progressives by 2022 were seriously debating.

Another concern is that the essay’s invocation of constitutional obligation might inadvertently constrain progressive policy ambition. The argument that the Constitution *compels* certain egalitarian measures could cut both ways: if the political branches fail to enact them, does that constitute a constitutional failure? And if the Court strikes down such measures, does that signal that the constitutional vision itself is legally untenable? The New Deal reformers succeeded not by simply asserting a constitutional duty but by creating new institutional realities—social security, labor rights, regulatory agencies—that eventually became woven into the fabric of expectations and legal acceptability. The essay’s examples, such as the PRO Act and the wealth tax, are precisely the kind of legislative initiatives that can reshape the baseline on their own terms. Wrapping them in constitutional rhetoric might be inspiring to some constituencies, but it also gives hostile courts an opening to define the constitutional limits more sharply. A more cautious path might be to pursue these policies as exercises of statutory authority under the Commerce Clause or the spending power, employing the full scope of existing doctrinal openings without prematurely inviting constitutional adjudication.

That said, the essay’s recovery of the anti-oligarchy tradition is a genuine intellectual contribution, and its call to reconnect racial justice with political economy is especially timely. The observation that the Medicaid-expansion ruling and the gutting of the Voting Rights Act both rest on newly invented principles of state sovereignty, with racially disparate impacts, illuminates how racial hierarchy is embedded in the architecture of constitutional federalism. Yet even here, the strategic question remains: what would a progressive constitutional politics look like that doesn’t simply rehearse these insights in briefs and legislative findings, but actually changes the balance of interpretive power? Perhaps the answer lies less in persuading the current Court than in shaping the appointment process and the broader judicial ideology over decades. The essay’s own examples of conservative success—the patient construction of a movement that installed reliable judges—suggest that constitutional change requires capturing institutions, not just winning arguments.

In the end, “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again” is a powerful diagnostic tool and a welcome invitation to recover a lost political language. Its strength is that it treats political economy as a constitutional subject, refusing the long-standing liberal separation of law from economics. Its weakness is that it does not fully reckon with the practical reality that the current Court is prepared to treat any progressive constitutional vision as illegitimate by definition. To reclaim the Constitution, progressives may need not only to speak in constitutional terms but to build the political power necessary to alter the composition and jurisdiction of the courts themselves—a project that, in 2022, remains deeply uncertain. Without that, the democracy-of-opportunity tradition may remain a library of noble arguments, all of them overruled.

Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, and their co-authors offer a compelling and desperately needed diagnosis of the left’s current ideological malaise. For too long, the post-war liberal consensus has artificially severed constitutional law from political economy. By outsourcing economic policy to technocratic experts and constitutional interpretation to a specialized legal elite, progressives unilaterally disarmed. They allowed the conservative legal movement to aggressively fill the void, weaponizing the Constitution to dismantle campaign finance regulations, weaken labor unions, and constrain the administrative state. The authors’ call to revive the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition—arguing that the Constitution actively requires the elected branches to prevent oligarchy—is a potent intellectual corrective.

However, while their proposed rhetorical shift is intellectually satisfying, relying on “re-constitutionalizing” progressive politics underestimates the immediate, intractable barrier of a 6-3 conservative Supreme Court. Furthermore, it overestimates the political utility of constitutional rhetoric for the modern progressive coalition. Shifting our narrative is necessary, but without coupling this new constitutional vision with aggressive, structural reforms to the judiciary itself, the anti-oligarchy agenda will remain trapped in academic thought experiments.

The authors propose using the Constitution as a “shield” for egalitarian legislation, hoping to demonstrate to judges and the public why such laws should survive constitutional attack. But in the reality of 2022, a shield is fundamentally useless if the referees are explicitly hostile to the very concept of the defense. The conservative legal movement did not just win the rhetorical battle of constitutional interpretation; it systematically seized the machinery of the judiciary. Decisions like *Citizens United* and *Janus* are not merely the result of superior conservative arguments that progressives failed to counter; they are the result of a decades-long, heavily funded project to install movement judges who view the protection of capital and corporate speech as their primary jurisprudential duty.

Making popular, anti-oligarchic arguments outside the Court is a noble endeavor, but it will not magically sway Justices Alito, Thomas, or Gorsuch, who hold life tenure and operate insulated from the very democratic pressures the authors wish to mobilize. The authors suggest that progressives must explain to the people why constitutional challenges to laws like the PRO Act or wealth taxes should fail. But public persuasion does not automatically translate into judicial restraint. When the Court strikes down a democratically enacted wealth tax, public outrage grounded in the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition will provide little immediate comfort if the progressives lack the tools or the will to override the Court’s decree.

This brings us to the historical precedents the authors cite. They note that Jacksonian Democrats, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Deal Democrats challenged hostile courts. But these historical actors did not merely make better constitutional arguments in the public square; they wielded raw political power against the judiciary. Abraham Lincoln openly defied Chief Justice Roger Taney. Franklin D. Roosevelt won his constitutional revolution not just through rhetorical persuasion, but by explicitly threatening to pack the Supreme Court, forcing a doctrinal retreat. Today, the doctrine of judicial supremacy—the belief that the Supreme Court alone dictates the final meaning of the Constitution—is so deeply internalized by the political establishment that even modest proposals for term limits or court expansion are met with institutionalist panic.

If progressives are to take the authors’ advice and assert that elected branches have a co-equal duty to interpret the Constitution, they must be willing to engage in the kind of institutional hardball that makes that assertion real. Reclaiming a progressive constitutional political economy requires stripping the courts of their outsized veto power. Without advocating for jurisdiction stripping, court expansion, or the willingness of the executive and legislative branches to aggressively check judicial overreach, asking progressives to fight a conservative juristocracy using only the power of a "shield" narrative is sending them into a gunfight armed with a history book.

Furthermore, asking today’s progressive coalition to invest heavily in constitutional veneration carries its own political risks. The authors want to wrap progressive policies in the mantle of the Constitution, particularly regarding racial justice. But for many in the modern progressive base—especially racial justice advocates and younger voters—the Constitution is viewed with deep, justified skepticism. It is a document rooted in compromises with enslavers, originally designed precisely to protect elite property rights against the redistributive impulses of democratic majorities. Demanding that activists revere this document as a fundamentally anti-oligarchic text might ring hollow. For many organizers, making straightforward moral and material arguments for universal healthcare, labor rights, and environmental protection is vastly more effective than attempting to reverse-engineer a constitutional mandate out of a document that historically codified their subjugation.

Where the essay shines brightest is in its granular application of political economy to modern legal battles. Reclaiming the First Amendment from its current status as a tool for corporate deregulation, reviving the Brandeisian vision of antitrust law to break up monopolies, and recognizing that racial justice cannot be achieved without massive public investment are the exact policy goals the left must pursue. The authors are absolutely right that we cannot allow the right to monopolize the language of fundamental rights and constitutional fidelity.

Ultimately, Fishkin, Forbath, and their co-authors have provided a vital blueprint for *what* progressives should argue. The Constitution is indeed the charter of a republic, and vast inequality threatens the foundations of that republic. But winning the battle for the nation's political economy will require more than just recovering lost historical arguments. It will require the political courage to confront, and ultimately restructure, the unaccountable judicial institutions that currently stand in democracy's way.

The lead essay is right about the central asymmetry in American constitutional politics. Conservatives have never treated the Constitution as a lawyerly settlement to be administered by neutral experts. They have treated it as a charter of political economy. They have argued, organized, appointed judges, funded litigation, and educated voters around a substantive account of freedom, property, religion, federalism, and speech. Progressives, by contrast, have too often responded as if the problem were merely bad judging: if only courts would respect precedent, if only doctrine were more technically sound, if only nominees had better temperaments. That posture is not enough for the moment we are in.

The Supreme Court is now poised to exercise enormous power over voting rights, labor law, reproductive freedom, climate regulation, gun regulation, campaign finance, affirmative action, and the administrative state. The leaked draft in Dobbs has made especially vivid what many already knew: appeals to precedent and institutional modesty are weak reeds when a majority has a constitutional project. If progressives answer that project only with nostalgia for a less partisan Court, they will lose. They need a constitutional politics of their own.

But the strongest case for reviving progressive constitutional argument is not that it will quickly persuade this Court. It probably will not. The point is that constitutional meaning in the United States has never been made by courts alone. Abolitionists, labor organizers, suffragists, civil rights activists, New Dealers, and opponents of the Vietnam War all made constitutional claims outside the judiciary. Sometimes courts eventually ratified those claims; sometimes they resisted them; often the more important victories came through statutes, constitutional amendments, administrative action, and changed public common sense. The Constitution has always been a language through which Americans argue about who counts, who rules, and what kind of power is compatible with republican government.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition the authors describe is therefore worth recovering. It reminds us that economic inequality is not merely a regrettable social fact to be corrected after constitutional law has done its work. It is itself a constitutional problem when it enables domination: employers over workers, monopolists over markets, donors over elections, states over disenfranchised citizens, police and prisons over racially subordinated communities. A republic cannot be reduced to formal rights possessed by isolated individuals. It requires institutions that allow ordinary people to exercise power together.

That point is especially compelling in the First Amendment context. The current Court’s free speech jurisprudence often treats economic power as constitutionally innocent and democratic regulation as constitutionally suspect. In campaign finance cases, this means that efforts to prevent great wealth from drowning out ordinary citizens become illicit attempts to “level” speech. In labor cases, the same amendment that once protected picketing and association becomes a weapon against unions’ ability to sustain themselves. The result is not neutrality. It is a choice to protect some forms of power—corporate, managerial, plutocratic—while disabling others.

Progressives should say this plainly. A small-donor public financing system is not a technocratic tweak; it is an effort to vindicate political equality. Labor law reform is not simply industrial policy; it is a way to make freedom of association meaningful for people who otherwise face employers alone. Antitrust enforcement is not only about prices; it is about preventing private governments from emerging inside the economy. These claims may not fit neatly into the doctrinal boxes law students memorize, but they fit the deeper constitutional concern with domination and self-government.

Still, there is a danger in saying that progressive reforms are not only permitted by the Constitution but required by it. The danger is not that such claims are illegitimate. Conservatives make equivalent claims constantly. The danger is that progressives may reproduce the very judicial supremacy they oppose if every major policy preference is redescribed as a constitutional command. A constitutional politics of democracy should enlarge the space for democratic judgment, not replace one set of judicially enforced absolutes with another.

For that reason, the language of “constitutional necessity” should be used carefully. It is one thing to say that Congress has a constitutional responsibility to protect voting rights, counter oligarchy, and secure the material bases of citizenship. It is another to say that the Constitution dictates a particular health care system, wealth tax, campaign finance formula, or corporate governance model. The first claim mobilizes constitutional values in democratic politics. The second risks collapsing prudential disagreement into constitutional betrayal.

This distinction matters because the progressive coalition itself contains real disagreements. Some favor single-payer health care; others prefer expanded public options or Medicaid-like programs. Some want aggressive breakups of dominant firms; others emphasize regulation, public utility models, or worker power. Some support race-conscious remedies; others stress universal programs designed with racial justice in mind. A democracy-of-opportunity Constitution should be capacious enough to guide these debates without pretending to settle all of them.

The authors’ account also needs to confront more directly the exclusions embedded in the very traditions they wish to reclaim. Jacksonian anti-oligarchy was often bound up with white male democracy, settler expansion, and Indigenous dispossession. New Deal constitutionalism empowered industrial workers while leaving many agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately Black workers and women—outside key protections. Even some Progressive Era attacks on monopoly coexisted with racial hierarchy, immigration restriction, and imperial ambition. These facts do not make the tradition unusable, but they mean it cannot simply be restored. It has to be reconstructed.

That reconstruction should place race, gender, and empire at the center rather than treating them as later additions to a primarily class-based story. The lead essay does important work in connecting Medicaid, voting rights, and federalism to racial political economy. That insight should be extended. The constitutional meaning of labor law looks different when domestic workers, care workers, incarcerated workers, and gig workers are placed at the center. The constitutional meaning of public investment looks different when viewed from communities subjected to redlining, environmental racism, school segregation, and predatory policing. The constitutional meaning of anti-oligarchy looks different when one remembers that concentrated private power has often worked through racialized markets in land, credit, labor, and punishment.

The practical question is how such a constitutional politics can gain traction. It cannot be limited to law reviews, judicial opinions, or Supreme Court confirmation hearings. It must appear in legislative findings, presidential speeches, union campaigns, state constitutional litigation, ballot initiatives, agency rulemaking, party platforms, and movement demands. Congress should not merely pass voting rights legislation; it should explain that political equality is a constitutional duty. Labor reformers should not merely cite economic studies about wages; they should argue that democracy requires countervailing worker power. Antitrust officials should not merely invoke consumer welfare; they should name domination by concentrated capital as a republican danger.

At the same time, institutional reform remains indispensable. A revived progressive constitutional politics should not become an excuse to ignore the structure of the Court itself. Jurisdiction stripping, ethics rules, term limits, expansion, supermajority requirements for invalidating federal statutes, and other reforms all deserve serious debate. If the Court is exercising political power, then democratic actors are entitled to ask how that power should be checked. Constitutional argument and institutional reform are complements, not substitutes.

The lead essay’s most important contribution is to reject learned helplessness. For too long, liberals have spoken as though the Constitution belongs to judges and the economy belongs to experts. That division has left citizens with too little democratic authority over the basic structures that govern their lives. Recovering the Constitution as a language of democratic political economy will not by itself defeat an entrenched conservative judiciary or reverse decades of inequality. But without such a recovery, progressive politics will continue to sound merely managerial while conservative politics sounds foundational.

The Constitution should not be a museum piece guarded by courts, nor a weapon reserved for the powerful. It should be a site of democratic struggle over the conditions of freedom. Progressives should enter that struggle without apology—but also without imagining that constitutional rhetoric can spare them the hard work of organizing majorities, building institutions, and governing well. A republic is not saved by doctrine alone. It is saved, if at all, by people who understand that concentrated power is a constitutional problem and who build the democratic power needed to overcome it.

The lead essay's call to "make progressive politics constitutional again" offers a compelling historical recovery and a necessary challenge to liberal timidity about constitutional argument. Yet the proposal raises significant questions about strategy, institutional design, and the risks of entrenching a vision that may prove as constraining as it is liberating.

The authors are right that the postwar liberal consensus on constitutional interpretation was a strategic error. Treating constitutional meaning as the exclusive province of courts while ceding economic policy to technocrats handed conservatives a profound advantage. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition recovered here—stretching from Jacksonians through Reconstruction to the New Deal—reminds us that generations of reformers understood the Constitution as a charter for democratic self-governance against oligarchy. This is genuine intellectual history, not mythmaking.

But the essay understates the risks of constitutionalizing economic policy debates. The authors acknowledge that "constitutional confrontations over rival visions of our political and economic future are inevitable." Yet they seem more confident than they should be that a progressive constitutional politics can remain a "shield" rather than becoming a "sword." In practice, arguments about what the Constitution requires are arguments about what courts may strike down. If progressives succeed in establishing that the Constitution compels particular economic arrangements, they create precedents that future courts—perhaps less friendly than the present moment's courts would be to progressive goals—could wield against different reforms. The Lochner era offers a cautionary tale: conservative courts once read the Constitution to protect liberty of contract and strike down labor protections. Progressive constitutionalism risks creating a mirror image, in which judicial review becomes a mechanism for locking in whatever economic arrangements the current interpretive consensus favors.

The more lasting contribution of the democracy-of-opportunity tradition may be not a set of specific constitutional doctrines but a broader understanding of what constitutional democracy requires. The authors rightly note that constitutional arguments move between courts and public debate, shaping possibilities in both spheres. But this cuts both ways. If progressives succeed in making anti-oligarchy arguments central to constitutional discourse, they may also make those arguments vulnerable to being captured, diluted, or turned to unexpected purposes. The Tea Party's invocations of constitutional originalism suggest that populist constitutional argument can be weaponized in unexpected directions.

The essay's treatment of racial justice is particularly thoughtful, recovering the connection between economic redistribution and the struggle for equality that mainstream liberal discourse has often obscured. The observation that the Medicaid expansion's destruction fell disproportionately on the former Confederacy, and on Black Americans, is an important reminder that constitutional doctrine operates in a political economy shaped by centuries of racial hierarchy. Yet here too the analysis could go further. The history of Reconstruction demonstrates not only that economic transformation was necessary for racial justice, but that racial exclusion was deliberately used to prevent economic reform. White elites weaponized racial fear to fragment populist coalitions. This history suggests that even a revived democracy-of-opportunity tradition will face the same challenge: building multiracial solidarity against concentrated economic power requires more than constitutional argument.

The proposal for progressive constitutional politics faces a practical obstacle the essay acknowledges but does not fully reckon with: the current configuration of political power. The Senate's structure, the filibuster, and the composition of the Supreme Court all create veto points that constitutional argument alone cannot overcome. The authors are right that "there is no future for the liberal idea of a sharp separation between constitutional arguments in court and political arguments outside the courts." But there is also no future for constitutional politics without the political power to act on constitutional claims. The PRO Act, antitrust reform, and campaign finance innovation all require not merely compelling constitutional arguments but the votes to enact them. Constitutional argument is most powerful when it reinforces and legitimizes political mobilization, not when it substitutes for it.

Finally, the essay's confidence that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition can be recovered and deployed may underestimate how thoroughly that tradition was displaced. The postwar settlement that replaced it—combining economic growth with limited social insurance and a color-coded welfare state—was not simply imposed by elites. It reflected the limits of progressive coalition-building and the durability of racial exclusion in American politics. Recovering the tradition requires not only intellectual work but the reconstruction of political capacity. The authors are correct that this intellectual work is necessary. Whether it is sufficient is another question.

The Court Won't Be Argued Out of Power

Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet have written a bracing essay, and one I largely admire. They are right that postwar liberalism took a wrong turn when it ceded constitutional political economy to the courts and economic policy to technocrats. They are right that conservatives never made that mistake, and right that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition offers progressives a usable past. But I worry that the essay rests on a hopeful premise about constitutional argument that the present moment will not bear: that if progressives speak in a more constitutional register, courts will eventually respond, the public will eventually rally, and the political branches will eventually feel empowered to push back. I am not so sure.

Consider what the authors are actually asking elected officials and movements to do. They want Congress to pass a wealth tax with a backup inheritance tax in case it gets struck down. They want a robust labor reform that anticipates First Amendment and federalism attacks. They want voting rights statutes that openly defy Shelby County’s logic. They want antitrust reform that confronts takings and contract challenges head-on. In each case, progressives are supposed to win the legislative fight, then win the litigation, or, failing that, mobilize public sentiment to delegitimize an adverse ruling. That is an enormous amount of work, and it presupposes a Congress capable of passing such laws in the first place. The same antidemocratic structures that the essay rightly identifies—the Senate, the filibuster, gerrymandered House districts, the Electoral College—stand between us and the legislative victories that would be needed to even tee up these constitutional confrontations.

The harder question the essay sidesteps is what to do when the legislative path is blocked not just by ideological opponents but by structural features of the system that themselves enjoy constitutional cover. The Senate gives Wyoming and California equal weight. The Electoral College has handed the presidency to popular-vote losers twice in two decades, and each of those presidents reshaped the Court. The federal judiciary is staffed for a generation by a movement that lost the popular vote in seven of the last eight presidential elections. You cannot argue your way out of that. You can only restructure it.

This is where I part company with the authors, not in their diagnosis but in their menu of responses. They are right that judicial reforms premised on restoring a nonpartisan past are based on a myth. But the alternative is not merely to recover a richer tradition of constitutional argument. It is to use the powers the elected branches still have to change the shape of the judiciary itself. Court expansion, jurisdiction-stripping, supermajority requirements for invalidating federal statutes, term limits enforced through circuit-riding rotation, even straightforward statutory overrides under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment—all of these belong on the table. The authors gesture toward popular constitutionalism but seem reluctant to embrace the institutional weapons that would actually disarm a hostile Court. They want a battle of arguments. The right is fighting a battle for control of an institution.

There is a deeper theoretical problem too. The essay’s rhetoric implies a roughly symmetrical situation: conservatives have a constitutional political economy, progressives need one. But the asymmetry is enormous. Conservatives have spent fifty years building the Federalist Society, capturing law schools’ feeder pipelines, training clerks, grooming nominees, and constructing a doctrinal apparatus (originalism, textualism, the major questions doctrine, anti-commandeering, equal sovereignty of states) that operates as a coherent ideology. Progressives have... law professors writing books. I do not mean to be glib; this book and this essay are valuable contributions. But constitutional argument does not move judges who are confident in their power and their numbers. It moves judges who feel insecure—who fear public backlash, institutional delegitimation, or being overruled by political action. The lesson of 1937 is not that FDR persuaded the Court of a richer constitutional political economy. It is that he threatened to pack it, and they blinked.

I also want to register a more uncomfortable doubt about the “democracy-of-opportunity tradition.” The essay traces a noble lineage: Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, Progressives, New Dealers. But several of these movements were built on racial exclusion, and the New Deal famously bought Southern support by carving Black workers out of social insurance. Aziz Rana himself acknowledges in the passage they quote that the “high tides” of democracy of opportunity coincided with territorial expansion. That is not incidental. The American political economy has often expanded the franchise of citizenship by drawing tighter borders around who counts. If we are going to revive this tradition, we have to grapple seriously with how to extract its anti-oligarchic energy without inheriting its exclusionary scaffolding. The essay gestures toward this in its section on race and political economy, but the gesture is too brief given the weight the tradition is being asked to bear.

None of this is to say the authors are wrong about the importance of constitutional argument. They are right that the liberal silence about the Constitution outside of court is a strategic catastrophe. They are right that when Stevens spoke of a “democratic duty” to regulate corporate political spending, he was invoking a register that has gone almost entirely missing from mainstream Democratic rhetoric. The reclamation project the essay proposes is necessary. I just do not believe it is sufficient.

What might a more adequate strategy look like? It would combine the constitutional argument the authors propose with three additional elements. First, structural reform of the Court and of democracy itself: statehood for D.C., abolition of the filibuster, court expansion or jurisdiction-stripping, federal voting rights protections enforced through means that do not require the Court’s acquiescence. Second, building durable institutions—not just movements, which come and go, but the kinds of mass-membership organizations that once made labor a force the political system could not ignore. The PRO Act is good, but legislation alone will not rebuild unions that have been hollowed out for forty years. Third, a willingness to engage in confrontational popular politics around adverse rulings: not just disagreeing with Citizens United or Shelby County, but treating them as illegitimate exercises of power that the political branches have an affirmative obligation to work around and, where possible, override.

The authors come close to this last point when they write that “workers will need to exercise their rights to organize, strike, and act in solidarity in contexts where this is now illegal, in the face of judicial injunctions, fines, and jail time.” That is exactly right, and it is the most radical sentence in the essay. But it sits oddly with the surrounding emphasis on constitutional argumentation. Civil disobedience is not really an argument about constitutional meaning; it is a wager about political power. The 1930s labor movement won not because it persuaded the Hughes Court of a better reading of the Commerce Clause, but because sitdown strikes in Flint and elsewhere made the cost of resistance higher than the cost of accommodation. The constitutional revolution followed the political revolution, not the other way around.

So here is my friendly amendment to the authors’ project. Yes, retrieve the democracy-of-opportunity tradition. Yes, teach progressives to speak constitutionally about wages, monopoly, race, and political voice. Yes, make the case in legislation, in litigation, and in public argument that egalitarian reforms are constitutional necessities. But do not imagine that argument alone can constrain an unelected, life-tenured majority that views itself as the vanguard of a counter-revolution. The Court will be brought back in line, if at all, by a combination of political organization, institutional restructuring, and yes, the kind of confrontational lawmaking and movement politics that forces a choice between legitimacy and obstruction.

The essay’s subtitle could be “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again.” I would add a second clause: “And Be Prepared to Make the Constitution Political Again, Too.” The Court has acted politically for fifty years while being treated as an oracle. The remedy is not more sophisticated oracular interpretation. It is to puncture the mystique, demonstrate that the emperor is naked, and use the tools democracy still gives us to constrain him. The authors have written a book that helps with the first half. We need the second half too.

The Risks of Constitutionalizing Everything

There is much to admire in the lead essay, and I want to begin by acknowledging it. The authors are correct that the Roberts Court has politicized constitutional law in ways that demand a response. They are correct that Citizens United, Janus, Shelby County, and NFIB v. Sebelius rest on constitutional theories that did not exist a generation ago and that systematically disadvantage egalitarian legislation. They are correct that liberals have ceded too much ground by treating the Constitution as the courts’ private property. And they are right to call attention to a forgotten tradition of constitutional argument that linked political democracy to economic structure. These are real contributions.

But I want to enter a dissent from the broader project. The authors are urging progressives to do what conservatives have done for fifty years: develop a substantive constitutional vision of political economy and press it in every domain of public life. I think this is a mistake, both strategically and as a matter of constitutional principle. Politicizing constitutional law symmetrically does not restore balance. It accelerates the very degradation the authors deplore.

Begin with the strategic point. The authors’ implicit theory is that constitutional argument is a tug-of-war: one side has been pulling hard, the other side hasn’t pulled at all, and the rope has consequently moved. Pull back, and equilibrium can be restored. But constitutional argument is not really a tug-of-war. It is more like a public norm whose stability depends on widespread agreement that there are some questions law cannot answer. When everyone treats every contested policy question as a constitutional question, the consequence is not that egalitarian policies become constitutionally entrenched. It is that the very idea of constitutional law as something other than naked policy preference collapses. And when constitutional law collapses into policy, the side with more institutional power—judicial nominations, Senate seats, state legislatures—wins. That is the side conservatives are on, and have been on for some time.

There is a reason postwar liberals tried to depoliticize constitutional law: they had just lived through the Lochner era and seen what happened when the Court treated its preferred political economy as constitutionally required. The response was not to develop a counter-Lochnerism. It was to insist on judicial humility—the idea, articulated in cases like Williamson v. Lee Optical and Carolene Products, that elected branches should generally be allowed to legislate as they saw fit on economic questions. The authors call this “depoliticization,” and treat it as a defeat. I would call it a hard-won settlement: the Court would protect democracy and discrete minorities, but stay out of the policy disputes that elected branches were better suited to resolve. This settlement broke down because the right walked away from it, not because it was incoherent.

The authors’ proposal is essentially to walk away from it from the other direction. They want progressives to argue, in court and out, that the Affordable Care Act is constitutionally required, that labor reform is constitutionally required, that aggressive antitrust enforcement is constitutionally required, that wealth taxes are constitutionally required. Take the long view here. Suppose this argumentative campaign succeeds in some future court. We would then have a constitutional regime in which judges enforce a left-wing political economy. The next time the political winds shift—and they always do—the right will inherit that machinery and use it for their own ends. Indeed, that is essentially what happened with substantive due process: a doctrine progressives developed to defend liberty has, in Dobbs and elsewhere, become a vehicle for conservative outcomes. We should learn this lesson, not repeat it.

There is also a democratic objection. The authors invoke democracy throughout the essay—democracy of opportunity, democratic self-rule, democratic political economy. But the practical effect of constitutionalizing political economy is to remove these questions from democratic contestation. If a wealth tax is constitutionally required, then voters who oppose it are constitutionally in the wrong. If aggressive antitrust is a constitutional necessity, then a future Congress that prefers a different approach is violating the Constitution. This is precisely the move conservatives have made with respect to property and contract, and the authors rightly criticize it. But the criticism cuts equally against the proposed progressive version. Either elected branches should be able to set economic policy in a wide range of ways, or they should not. The authors want it both ways—democratic discretion when progressives are in power, constitutional necessity to entrench their gains.

A more candid response to the Roberts Court is to fight it on the terrain where it is most vulnerable: not constitutional theory, but democratic legitimacy. The Court today is unpopular, and increasingly seen as a partisan institution. The right response to this is not to construct a competing constitutional ideology, but to make the Court behave more like a court—through structural reforms (term limits, ethics rules, expansion if necessary), through political pressure, and through a renewed commitment by the political branches to act under their own constitutional authority rather than waiting for permission. The authors note, correctly, that Congress can override Janus through legislation reshaping labor law, that Congress can rebuild the Voting Rights Act, that Congress can respond to Citizens United with public financing. Good. These are democratic responses to anti-democratic rulings. They do not require progressives to construct a parallel constitutional ideology; they require progressives to act as if elected branches have the powers the Constitution gives them.

I worry, too, about the historical narrative on which the essay leans. The “democracy-of-opportunity tradition” is presented as a coherent constitutional lineage running from Jackson through Reconstruction through the New Deal. But each of these movements had a very different relationship to the Constitution. Jacksonians were hostile to the Court and to federal power generally; Reconstruction Republicans wielded federal power against state oligarchy in ways Jacksonians would have abhorred; New Dealers built an administrative state that some Progressives had championed and others, including Brandeis, distrusted. Treating these as moments in a single tradition smooths over genuine conflicts and risks producing a usable past that is more retrospective construction than historical reality. There is nothing wrong with constructing a usable past—every political movement does it—but we should be honest about what we are doing.

Let me close by saying what I think a better response to the Roberts Court looks like. First, restore judicial humility as a constitutional ideal. Not the false neutrality the authors rightly criticize, but a genuine principle that on most contested questions of economic policy, the Court should defer to elected branches. This is a constitutional argument too—arguably the central one of the post-1937 era—and it deserves to be defended, not abandoned. Second, support structural court reform to address the asymmetry that has produced the current Court, including term limits and possibly expansion. Third, pursue an aggressive legislative agenda that takes seriously Congress’s own constitutional authority to interpret the Fourteenth Amendment and to legislate under the Commerce Clause and the Spending Clause. Fourth, defend democracy itself through federal voting rights legislation, anti-gerrymandering reforms, and an end to the filibuster. Fifth, build the political coalitions and movements that can actually win elections at every level.

None of this requires progressives to mirror the conservative legal movement’s ideological project. It requires progressives to do the unglamorous work of democratic politics. The authors are right that the courts are not nonpartisan and never were. But the answer is not to treat constitutional law as partisan all the way down. The answer is to limit the role of courts in the questions that democracy is supposed to settle—and to win those democratic battles when they come. Constitutionalizing progressive politics, in the end, would make progressives more dependent on courts, not less. That is a trade we should refuse.

The Argument Is Right; The Subjects Are Missing

The lead essay performs a genuine service by recovering a constitutional tradition that mainstream liberalism has neglected for two generations. Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet make a compelling case that the postwar settlement—courts handle the Constitution, economists handle the economy, politics fills in the residual blanks—was always a bad deal for working people, and that today’s right-wing court has cashed in the bill. I want to push their argument in a direction they only partially explore: toward the question of who, exactly, is going to make these constitutional arguments and through what social formations.

The essay is rich with prescriptions for what Congress should do, what doctrine should look like, what legislative bills should anticipate. It is much thinner on the question of where the political force comes from. The authors note, almost in passing, that “workers will need to exercise their rights to organize, strike, and act in solidarity in contexts where this is now illegal.” This is the single most important sentence in the essay, and it deserves to be the center rather than an aside. Constitutional traditions do not assert themselves. They are asserted by organized people with the leverage to make their assertions stick.

Consider the historical episodes the authors invoke. The Jacksonian assault on the Bank of the United States was not a triumph of constitutional reasoning. It was an outcome of a mass party with deep roots in local political culture, capable of mobilizing voters around an explicit anti-oligarchy program. Reconstruction’s constitutional achievements—the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments—were imposed at gunpoint by an army on a defeated South, and they collapsed when the political will to enforce them collapsed. The New Deal’s constitutional revolution was made possible by industrial unions whose sitdown strikes in Flint, Akron, and elsewhere demonstrated that the existing legal order could not be maintained at acceptable cost. In each case, constitutional argument followed political force; it did not precede it.

What this means is that the authors’ project—and I do think it is a project worth pursuing—will succeed only insofar as it is connected to organizations capable of generating political force. The contemporary American left has very few such organizations. Unions represent about 10 percent of the workforce, and only 6 percent of the private sector. Civil rights organizations have professionalized to the point of becoming, in many cases, donor-driven nonprofits rather than mass-membership institutions. The Movement for Black Lives, which the authors cite, has produced remarkable policy platforms, but it is a network of local groups without the durable institutional infrastructure of, say, the NAACP in its mid-century prime. The Democratic Party, especially since the Clinton-era turn toward college-educated professionals as its core constituency, is in many ways structurally incapable of carrying the kind of program the authors describe.

So the constitutional argument the authors want to make has, at present, no organized subject. It has authors—several distinguished ones, who have written a book—and it has scattered legislative champions, and it has a sympathetic but inattentive base. What it lacks is the social muscle to make adverse court rulings unenforceable, to make legislative leaders fear the consequences of inaction, and to make constitutional arguments resonate in workplaces, neighborhoods, and churches as well as in seminar rooms.

I do not say this to belittle the authors’ contribution. The intellectual work is necessary. A movement without ideas is no better off than ideas without a movement. And there is a real chicken-and-egg problem here: it is hard to organize without a vision, and hard to articulate a vision without an organized base to test it against. The authors have done the harder of the two preliminary tasks. But the next steps belong to organizers, not professors.

What might those next steps look like? Let me sketch three.

First, the labor movement needs to recover the constitutional self-understanding the essay describes, and quickly. The PRO Act is a good bill, but it will not pass under current conditions. What might shift those conditions is a wave of strikes and organizing drives that frame themselves explicitly in constitutional terms—not just as wage disputes, but as exercises of fundamental rights to collective action that the current legal regime unjustly restricts. The recent organizing victories at Amazon and Starbucks suggest that something is stirring. If those campaigns can be linked to a broader narrative about democracy, oligarchy, and constitutional self-rule, they become more than economic actions; they become a politics. The authors’ framework is exactly what such campaigns need.

Second, civil rights organizations and racial justice movements need to make the kind of political-economy turn the essay describes, but as organizing strategy as well as theory. The brilliance of Reverend Barber’s Poor People’s Campaign is precisely that it links racial justice to economic transformation in a way that builds multiracial coalitions in places (the South, in particular) where the politics of racial division has long been used to defeat economic reform. This is not just a policy program; it is a theory of how to assemble a political majority capable of overcoming the structural barriers to reform. Constitutional argument can be a glue here, providing a shared vocabulary that crosses identity lines.

Third—and this is the hardest—progressives need new institutional forms to replace the mid-century mass institutions that have decayed. The labor movement’s decline is the most visible example, but parallel decay has affected churches, civic organizations, and political parties at the local level. Without thick institutions, constitutional arguments cannot travel from law reviews to ordinary politics. The right has its own infrastructure—Federalist Society, evangelical churches, talk radio, gun clubs, increasingly social media networks—and this infrastructure carries constitutional ideas about religious freedom, gun rights, federalism, and limited government into millions of households. The left needs analogous infrastructure, and the lack of it is the deepest reason its constitutional arguments fail to resonate.

I want to add one more observation that the essay underplays. The authors treat the constitutional argument as something to be conducted in elite venues—courts, Congress, op-eds, books like the one this essay condenses. But constitutional argument in the historical episodes they cite happened largely in popular venues: pamphlets, mass meetings, election campaigns, even church sermons. Frederick Douglass made constitutional arguments to crowds of thousands. Eugene Debs made them from a prison cell. The Knights of Labor and the Populists made them in farmhouse organizing meetings across the Midwest and South. There is something a little ironic about an essay calling for popular constitutionalism that is itself addressed largely to other law professors and policy elites. The work the authors describe needs to be vernacularized, and that is not something legal scholars can do alone.

I close on a hopeful note. The conditions for the kind of politics the authors envision are better than they have been in decades. Inequality is at levels not seen since the Gilded Age. Public skepticism of corporations and billionaires is widespread and bipartisan. The pandemic exposed the cruelty of the existing economic order in ways that briefly made universal social provision politically thinkable. Young people, in particular, hold views about capitalism, democracy, and the role of government that align closely with the democracy-of-opportunity tradition the authors describe. There is, in short, a constituency for these arguments. What remains is to organize it.

The authors have given us the script. Now we need a theater company, a stage, and an audience. None of these will assemble themselves. Constitutional politics, like all politics, is built by people in rooms, in workplaces, in neighborhoods, in the slow accumulation of institutional capacity. The book this essay distills is one piece of the larger project. The rest will be made not by writing but by organizing, and the gap between the elegance of the argument and the meagerness of our current organized base is the gap that must be closed.

A Conservative's Reply: Whose Constitution, Whose Tradition?

I read Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet’s essay with interest, and a fair amount of irritation. As someone who has spent a career thinking about constitutional law from the right, I want to engage their argument seriously rather than dismiss it. They are right about some things. But the essay is built on a series of moves that, examined closely, do not work.

Begin with the central rhetorical trick: the authors describe the Roberts Court’s jurisprudence as a “politicization” of constitutional law, a betrayal of the postwar settlement. But the postwar settlement, as they themselves note, was a particular liberal achievement, not a neutral baseline. Brown v. Board, the Warren Court’s criminal procedure revolution, Roe v. Wade, the expansion of First Amendment doctrine to protect flag burning and pornography—all of these were political and contested constitutional moves made by judges. Conservatives spent fifty years building a movement to respond to them. To describe that response as a “politicization” of what was previously nonpolitical is to misdescribe the entire postwar period. The Court was always doing politics in constitutional clothing. Conservatives just got better at it.

Second, the authors’ historical narrative is selective in ways that should give a careful reader pause. They invoke the “democracy-of-opportunity tradition” as a continuous lineage from Jacksonians through Reconstruction Republicans to New Dealers. But Jacksonians defended slavery and Indian removal; their anti-oligarchic rhetoric coexisted comfortably with white supremacy. Reconstruction Republicans were the party of high tariffs and protective legislation that the Populists later attacked. New Dealers crafted the Social Security Act to exclude domestic and agricultural workers because Southern Democrats demanded it. The “tradition” the authors describe is not a tradition at all but a retrospective construction, assembled from compatible fragments while inconvenient elements are excluded. This is what every political movement does with its history, of course, but the essay’s rhetorical force depends on persuading the reader that something coherent and recoverable exists where in fact a curated anthology has been substituted.

Third, the essay’s portrayal of the Roberts Court’s key decisions is tendentious in ways that obscure rather than illuminate. Take Citizens United. The case did not, as the essay implies, license unlimited corporate domination of politics. It held that the government could not criminalize the showing of a political documentary because the production company was incorporated. The dissenting view—that the government has constitutional authority to prohibit corporate political speech—would also have allowed it to prohibit speech by the New York Times, by Planned Parenthood, by labor unions, by the ACLU, all of which are corporations. The narrowing distinctions the authors prefer (between “media” corporations and others, between PACs and treasuries) have no obvious constitutional grounding and create serious line-drawing problems. The Court’s decision was about whether the government can pick and choose which speakers get to address the public on political matters. That is not a small First Amendment question, and dismissing it as “pro-oligarchy” argument-by-label is not engagement.

Take Janus. The case held that public-sector workers cannot be forced to subsidize a union’s political speech as a condition of employment. The authors describe this as the Court turning the First Amendment into a tool against democratic self-government. But public-sector unions are not just private collective bargainers; they negotiate with the government over policy questions—school curricula, public safety, pension obligations—that have significant political content. Compelling workers to fund advocacy on those questions raises serious First Amendment concerns that the Abood compromise papered over rather than resolved. One can disagree with Janus, but the case is not the mask-off betrayal the authors describe. It rests on a defensible reading of compelled speech doctrine that liberals themselves have endorsed in other contexts.

Take Shelby County. The decision did not “destroy” Section 5 of the Voting Rights Act; it held that the preclearance formula in Section 4(b), based on data from the 1960s and 1970s, no longer corresponded to current conditions and that Congress would need to update it to continue subjecting certain jurisdictions to extraordinary federal supervision. That is a defensible position. One can argue (as I would) that Congress should have updated the formula. The Court did not prevent that; it required it. The political failure to do so is not the Court’s fault.

Now let me concede what the essay gets right. The authors are correct that liberals have been intellectually lazy about constitutional theory. For decades, mainstream liberal constitutionalism has been content with results-oriented common-law reasoning, dressed up as principle but in fact responsive to elite policy preferences. Conservatives have at least tried to articulate a coherent theory of constitutional interpretation—originalism, with all its problems—and that theory has provided both rhetorical force and intellectual discipline. Liberals lack an equivalent. The essay is a partial attempt to provide one, and that effort deserves respect even where its substance is wrong.

The authors are also right that the line between constitutional argument and political argument is porous, and that pretending otherwise is both naive and self-defeating. Conservatives have always known this; the right’s long march through the institutions of legal academia, judicial selection, and movement infrastructure was conducted with eyes open. If liberals want to compete, they will have to fight on the same terrain.

But here is where the conservative response gets sharper. The essay’s proposed program—constitutionalizing labor rights, social insurance, antitrust, wealth taxation, racial reparation—is not a return to anything. It is a project to read into the Constitution a substantive vision of political economy that has no clear textual or historical grounding. The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments do not, on any honest reading, require a wealth tax. The First Amendment does not, on any honest reading, mandate public financing of campaigns or restrict private campaign spending. The Constitution is not silent on these questions; it leaves them, mostly, to democratic politics. That is a feature, not a bug.

What the authors are really arguing for is not constitutional interpretation but constitutional rewriting through judicial reinterpretation, justified by a constructed tradition. This is the same complaint conservatives have lodged for decades against living constitutionalism, and the authors’ response—essentially, “the right does it too, so we should also”—concedes the point. If the standard for constitutional argument is “does it advance my preferred political economy,” then constitutional law has nothing left to say to citizens who disagree with that political economy. It becomes simply another arena of political combat, and the side with more power wins.

Conservatives have built their constitutional movement around a different aspiration: that there is something the Constitution actually says, that judges should try to apply that something rather than their preferred policy outcomes, and that democratic politics should be left to handle the rest. We have not always lived up to that aspiration. But it is at least an aspiration that can in principle constrain judicial behavior. The authors’ proposed approach has no comparable constraint. It is constitutionalism as movement strategy, all the way down.

If progressives adopt this approach, they will deserve the reaction they get. Conservatives will use the same tools when their turn comes—and their turn will come. The current moment of conservative judicial ascendancy is not eternal; political winds shift, judges retire, elections happen. The constitutional regime the authors are building, if it ever comes into being, will be wielded by future judges of various ideological commitments to entrench whatever political economy they happen to prefer. That is not a desirable destination. It is, in fact, what conservatives have been warning against since Lochner. The authors have inadvertently confirmed our diagnosis while disagreeing about the cure.

The honest path forward is not for either side to constitutionalize its policy preferences. It is for both sides to recommit to a constitutional law that is genuinely about the Constitution—text, structure, history, precedent—and to let political economy be settled, as it should be, by political means. The authors have written a candid manifesto. I just hope, for the country’s sake, that it does not become the playbook.

The Missing Half of the Story: International Political Economy

The lead essay is a powerful and largely persuasive case for recovering an indigenous American tradition of constitutional political economy. I want to add a dimension that the essay touches on but does not develop: the international and imperial context in which both American constitutional law and American political economy have always operated. Without that dimension, the recovery project the authors propose risks reproducing some of the limitations of the very tradition it seeks to revive.

Aziz Rana, one of the lead authors, has himself argued elsewhere that American democracy has historically expanded by drawing on the resources and exclusions of empire. The quotation from him in the essay—“the actual historical high tides for the domestic experience of democracy of opportunity have very clearly occurred during periods of territorial and global expansionism”—is the most important sentence in the piece, and it deserves to be taken seriously rather than registered and moved past. If the democracy-of-opportunity tradition has historically been parasitic on territorial expansion and global power projection, then a revival of that tradition in a world of constrained American power and ecological limits faces challenges the essay does not fully reckon with.

Consider Jacksonian democracy. Its anti-oligarchic energy was made possible in part by westward expansion, which provided an apparently inexhaustible supply of land—taken from Native peoples—and thus a material basis for the yeoman ideal. The artisan republicanism that informed early American political thought presumed access to productive property; when industrialization closed off that access, the tradition struggled to adapt. Reconstruction’s democratic moment was made possible by federal military power directed against the defeated South, but it collapsed in part because the same federal power was being redeployed against Native peoples on the plains and, soon after, in overseas adventures in Cuba, the Philippines, and the Caribbean. The Progressive Era’s anti-monopoly politics coexisted with Jim Crow and with the consolidation of an American overseas empire. The New Deal’s social democracy was funded in part by an industrial economy whose raw materials came from a global periphery shaped by colonial and neocolonial extraction, and its labor settlement carved out the workers—domestic and agricultural, disproportionately Black and brown—whose exclusion made the deal politically possible.

I do not raise these points to indict the democracy-of-opportunity tradition, which contains real resources for egalitarian politics. I raise them to ask: what does it mean to revive this tradition in a twenty-first-century context where the conditions that made its previous victories possible no longer obtain?

The United States today operates in a global economy where capital is mobile in ways it was not in the 1930s, where supply chains run through dozens of countries, where the dollar’s reserve status both subsidizes American consumption and constrains American monetary policy, and where the climate crisis imposes hard limits on the kind of growth-based political economy that previous progressive movements assumed. American workers compete not just with each other but with workers across the world; American corporations are often more accurately described as transnational entities than national ones. Any serious attempt to discipline corporate power, redistribute wealth, or rebuild labor faces challenges that the New Deal did not face, because the boundaries of the political economy no longer align with the boundaries of the polity.

The authors’ essay is almost entirely silent on this. They write about antitrust, labor law, and corporate governance as if these were domestic policy questions to be resolved within the American constitutional frame. But the firms they propose to regulate are global; the workers they propose to empower are often abroad; the wealth they propose to tax can be moved across borders with a keystroke. A genuinely twenty-first-century democracy-of-opportunity tradition would have to be, in part, an internationalist tradition—one that recognized the impossibility of building a humane domestic political economy on the basis of exploitation elsewhere, and that took seriously the constitutional implications of American economic and military behavior beyond its borders.

This points to a deeper limitation of the essay’s constitutional framing. The Constitution the authors invoke is the constitution of a particular polity, with particular borders, particular citizens, and particular interests. Constitutional argument is powerful precisely because it draws on shared traditions and texts. But the questions of political economy that the authors want to address are increasingly transnational, and the constitutional vocabulary they offer is by definition national. There is a mismatch between the scale of the problem and the scale of the proposed solution.

The tradition the essay celebrates was marked by deep contradictions—around race, gender, and empire—that the essay mentions but does not adequately reckon with. These contradictions are not mere historical curiosities; they limit the tradition's applicability to contemporary politics and reveal the risks of selective historical retrieval.

Consider the Reconstruction era, which the essay treats as a high point of democracy-of-opportunity politics. The essay praises Reconstruction Republicans for linking Black political participation to economic security, particularly through proposals for land redistribution. But the essay does not adequately grapple with why these proposals failed. Land redistribution was blocked not only by conservative resistance but by disagreements within the Republican coalition, by the prioritizations of Freedmen's Bureau officials who emphasized wage labor over land ownership, and by the broader economic interests of Northern investors who wanted Southern integration into a national market on terms favorable to capital. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition during Reconstruction was always partial and contested, not simply defeated by forces external to it.

More troubling is the essay's treatment of territorial expansion. The essay quotes Aziz Rana observing that "the actual historical high tides for the domestic experience of democracy of opportunity have very clearly occurred during periods of territorial and global expansionism." This is a significant concession that the essay does not develop. If democracy-of-opportunity required empire—if the Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Deal Democrats all depended on external expansion to manage internal contradictions—then the tradition is not simply available for contemporary appropriation. Contemporary progressives cannot pursue a twenty-first-century Freedom Budget through territorial expansion. The tradition's connection to expansionism reveals a structural limit that the essay does not address.

The New Deal era raises similar concerns. The essay rightly celebrates New Deal labor law as embedding workers in constitutional democracy. But it does not adequately address the racial exclusions built into New Deal programs. Social Security originally excluded agricultural and domestic workers, predominantly Black jobs. The Wagner Act's protections were least effective in the Jim Crow South, where Black workers faced employer resistance and union racism that the federal government did not effectively counter. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition during the New Deal was a class-inclusive vision that was racially exclusive in practice, a pattern that the essay acknowledges in passing but does not adequately theorize.

The contemporary implications are significant. The essay proposes that progressives reconnect racial justice with political economy, drawing on a "radical tradition of Black thought" from Du Bois through the Movement for Black Lives. This is a better and more honest approach than simply appropriating the Reconstruction or New Deal tradition. But the essay then pivots to proposals—the ACA Medicaid expansion, wealth taxes, antitrust enforcement—that are framed in broadly inclusive terms without adequately addressing how these policies interact with racial hierarchy in the present.

The Medicaid expansion, as the essay rightly notes, had a profound racial dimension—but in ways that complicate the democracy-of-opportunity framing. The states that opted out of the expansion were concentrated in the former Confederacy, where white political majorities chose to deny health coverage to low-income residents, many of them Black. This is a story about racial politics, but it is also a story about democracy working as designed.majorities in those states preferred to deny benefits to racial minorities rather than expand coverage. The constitutional question is not simply whether federal power can override state choice, but whether democratic majorities can be compelled to provide for the less fortunate when they prefer not to. This is a harder constitutional question than the essay suggests.

The essay proposes that Congress "undo the Court's constitutional intervention" through statutory reforms. But statutory reforms can be undone by future Congresses and do not address the underlying question of whether democratic majorities can be compelled to redistribute. If Southern whites prefer to deny benefits to Black residents rather than expand coverage for all, a federal statute mandating expansion does not resolve the democratic deficit; it overrides it.

The essay concludes by asserting that dispersed political and economic power are "constitutional necessities." But this framing does not adequately address the tensions within the democracy-of-opportunity tradition between inclusion and exclusion, between democracy and empire, between class and race. A constitutional tradition that required expansionism to manage internal contradictions is not simply available for contemporary appropriation. The contemporary radical tradition the essay invokes—the Movement for Black Lives, the Poor People's Campaign—has developed its own constitutional vision, rooted in different historical experiences and drawing on different resources. Progressives would do better to support and learn from these contemporary movements than to retrieve a selectively remembered past.

The lead essay diagnoses correctly that conservatives achieved constitutional supremacy partly because they made constitutional argument central to their politics, while liberals imagined constitutional law as an autonomous domain best left to lawyers. The prescription—to retrieve a democracy-of-opportunity tradition and make progressive politics constitutional again—is intellectually compelling and historically grounded. But the essay underestimates how much this strategy depends on political conditions that currently do not exist and cannot be created by argument alone.

The essay's core insight is that constitutional argument is not a separate sphere from politics but part of the broader contestation over political economy. This is true and important. But the essay does not adequately reckon with the gap between argument and institutional power. When the essay argues that "there is not only constitutional power, but a constitutional duty" to preserve democratic political economy, it assumes that this argument will land with some audience capable of acting on it. The Court has demonstrated that it will not. The political branches are constrained by filibuster rules and the composition of the Senate. The people themselves are divided by polarization and unequal access to political participation. Making constitutional arguments does not fill these gaps.

The essay identifies three arenas where progressive constitutional argument is needed: First Amendment (campaign finance and labor law), racial political economy (Medicaid, voting rights, reparations), and antitrust/corporate law. These are the right arenas. But the essay does not adequately address what happens when constitutional arguments are made and rejected by a hostile Court. The ACA narrowly survived because Chief Justice Roberts chose to uphold it on a highly strained interpretation. The Medicaid expansion was gutted. The Voting Rights Act provision was invalidated. Public financing schemes face certain challenge. Labor law reform faces certain challenge. A wealth tax faces certain challenge. The antitrust revival faces certain challenge from corporate litigants who have the resources and incentives to contest it.

In each of these arenas, the essay proposes that progressives make constitutional arguments to defend their proposals against judicial invalidation. But the constitutional arguments are not what will save these proposals. What will save them is political power: sustained majorities committed to these goals, capable of withstanding judicial invalidation through override statutes, constitutional amendments, structural reforms to the courts, or simply the passage of time and accumulation of precedent. The constitutional arguments matter, but they matter as part of a broader political strategy, not as the central element.

The essay's treatment of the PRO Act illustrates the problem. The essay celebrates this legislation as embodying the democracy-of-opportunity tradition and argues that it should be understood as exercising constitutional rights rather than merely statutory authority. This is a sound rhetorical move. But the PRO Act will face judicial challenge regardless of its constitutional grounding. The essay acknowledges that conservatives "will find First Amendment arguments against efforts to restrain employers' anti-union activity." They will also find Takings Clause arguments, federalism arguments, and whatever other doctrines they can construct. These challenges will succeed or fail based on Court composition and doctrine, not on the quality of progressive constitutional argument.

The essay concludes by arguing that progressives should "mine this rich vein of U.S. constitutional history" to prepare for "confrontations with a far-right court." This framing is apt but suggests that the goal is to win constitutional arguments before the Court. This is not a realistic strategy. The goal should be to build the political power that makes constitutional argument effective—to expand the Court, to constrain its jurisdiction, to override its decisions, or to wait it out while building durable majorities. Constitutional argument is one tool among many, valuable but not sufficient.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition the essay retrieves was effective when it was embedded in mass politics. The Jacksonians organized millions of small farmers against Eastern moneyed interests. The Reconstruction Republicans built a coalition linking formerly enslaved people to white yeomen through promises of land and citizenship. The New Dealers constructed a coalition of labor, farmers, and urban immigrants that sustained Democratic dominance for decades. These coalitions did not simply make constitutional arguments; they built political power that constitutional argument served.

Contemporary progressives face a more fragmented landscape. Labor has been decimated. The South is no longer a potential site of biracial democracy but a region of concentrated conservative power. The regulatory state has been captured by the industries it was supposed to regulate. The electoral system is structured to favor rural, white, and wealthy interests. The constitutional framework is stacked against majoritarian governance. These conditions cannot be changed by constitutional argument alone. They require sustained organizing, coalition-building, and the patient accumulation of power.

The lead essay offers a valuable contribution by recovering the democracy-of-opportunity tradition and arguing that progressives must reclaim constitutional discourse. But the essay should be read as a complement to, not a substitute for, the harder work of building political power. Constitutional arguments become powerful when they are made by people with institutional standing, coalition support, and the capacity to impose costs on those who reject them. Progressives should develop the constitutional arguments the essay proposes—but they should do so in service of broader political projects, not as the central element of a strategy that cannot succeed without them.
