Is the Hijab Worth Fighting Over?

The Ukrainian group Femen restarted the international debate about Muslim veils when it declared “Topless Jihad Day” on April 4, staging protests against the hijab and in support of an activist in Tunisia. A group of Muslim women then organized a counterprotest called “Muslimah Pride Day,” defending the hijab.

Is either party dealing with a serious issue? Is the hijab worth fighting over?

The hijab is worth talking about seriously; whether it is worth "fighting" over in the manner Femen has chosen is another question entirely. Femen's topless protests presume that the veil is universally a symbol of female subjugation, and that bare-breasted Western women are uniquely positioned to liberate their Muslim sisters. This is condescension dressed as solidarity. It collapses the wildly different conditions of women in Saudi Arabia, Iran, Tunisia, France and Dearborn into a single image of oppression, and it ignores the women — many of them feminists — who say they cover by conviction.

That said, Muslimah Pride Day's rejoinder, while a welcome corrective, risks the opposite error: treating any criticism of veiling practices as Islamophobia. There are real issues here. Amina Tyler faced genuine threats in Tunisia. Iranian women are jailed for showing hair. French Muslim girls have been pushed out of schools by a law many of them did not ask for. These are serious matters of coercion — by states, families and communities — that deserve sustained attention.

The useful fight, then, is against compulsion in either direction. The hijab itself is a garment; what surrounds it is what matters.
