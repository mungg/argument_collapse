Prescription drug abuse has become the fastest-growing drug problem in the United States, with the CDC reporting that opioid painkillers now kill more Americans than heroin and cocaine combined. But the solution isn't to retreat from treating pain — millions of patients with legitimate chronic conditions are already undertreated. What's needed is a smarter infrastructure around prescribing.

The single most effective step would be a fully interoperable, mandatory prescription drug monitoring program. Most states now have PDMPs, but they don't talk to each other, and in many states physicians aren't required to check them before writing scripts for controlled substances. A doctor in New Jersey should be able to see in seconds whether a patient filled an oxycodone prescription in Pennsylvania last week. "Doctor shopping" thrives in the seams between these systems.

Second, prescriber education must change. Surveys consistently show that most physicians receive only a few hours of pain-management training in medical school, and almost none on addiction medicine. Continuing education tied to DEA registration — as Massachusetts and a few other states have begun exploring — is a reasonable lever.

Third, we need easier, less stigmatized disposal of leftover pills. The DEA's take-back days are a start, but most diverted drugs come from family medicine cabinets, given freely to teenagers and relatives.

Finally, expand access to addiction treatment, including buprenorphine. Crackdowns on "pill mills" in Florida and elsewhere are necessary, but enforcement without treatment simply pushes dependent users toward heroin — a substitution we are already beginning to see.

Prescription drug abuse is a quiet epidemic, killing more Americans than heroin and cocaine combined, yet our response remains dangerously fragmented. The challenge is not simply to lock away medications; it is to build a system that distinguishes pain patients from those seeking a high, without erecting barriers that leave legitimate suffering untreated.

The single most effective step we can take right now is universal, real-time prescription monitoring. Many states already operate databases that allow physicians and pharmacists to see a patient’s controlled-substance history before writing or filling a prescription. The problem is that too many states still do not mandate their use. A doctor in Florida might unknowingly prescribe opioids to a patient who just picked up the same drug in Georgia. Requiring prescribers to check these databases, and linking them across state lines, would instantly flag doctor-shopping and pill-mill patterns while protecting patients with verifiable pain conditions. Privacy concerns are manageable with proper safeguards, and the technology already exists.

Monitoring alone, though, cannot fix a medical culture that was trained to treat pain as a “fifth vital sign” and to reach for opioids as the first line of defense. We need aggressive continuing education for prescribers, not just on addiction risks but on the full spectrum of pain management alternatives—physical therapy, nerve blocks, non-opioid medications, and integrated mental health care. Too many physicians were never adequately trained to distinguish acute from chronic pain or to recognize early signs of misuse in their own patients. At the same time, patients must be taught that a pain-free existence is not always attainable and that all pills carry risk. Public awareness campaigns should shift from vague “just say no” messages to clear, evidence-based information about safe storage, the dangers of sharing medications, and the fact that leftover pills in the medicine cabinet become a public hazard. These measures, layered together, can push back the tide without abandoning the patients who genuinely need relief.

The most effective lever we have against prescription drug abuse is also the most under-used: state-run prescription drug monitoring programs (PDMPs). When a patient can walk into five different clinics in a week and walk out with five different opioid prescriptions, the problem isn't really demand — it's a system that refuses to talk to itself.

Forty-eight states have authorized PDMPs, but the real-world implementations vary wildly. Some states require prescribers to check the database before writing a controlled-substance prescription; most do not. Some share data across state lines through hubs like NABP's PMP InterConnect; many sit on isolated islands. Florida, long the pill-mill capital of the country, only got its database operational in late 2011 — and the effect on diversion patterns in Appalachia and the Southeast has already been visible to law enforcement.

Three reforms would make these systems actually work. First, mandatory query: a prescriber should not be able to write for oxycodone, hydrocodone, or alprazolam without first pulling up the patient's recent controlled-substance history. Second, real-time reporting from pharmacies — weekly batch uploads, still common in some states, are useless against a determined doctor-shopper. Third, full interstate interoperability, because addiction does not respect the Mason-Dixon line.

Skeptics raise two concerns worth taking seriously. The first is privacy: prescription records are sensitive, and rolling them into a queryable database creates real risks of breach and misuse. Strict access controls, audit trails, and limits on law-enforcement access without a subpoena can mitigate this, but the concern is legitimate and should shape implementation. The second is the worry that doctors, faced with database friction and DEA scrutiny, will simply stop prescribing opioids to patients who genuinely need them. This is the harder problem, and it's why monitoring must be paired with clear clinical guidelines that protect physicians who prescribe responsibly.

What monitoring will not do is solve addiction itself. Once a patient is dependent, cutting off the supply without offering treatment just pushes them toward heroin — a substitution we are already seeing in pockets of the Northeast. So PDMPs have to be one piece of a larger response that includes expanded access to buprenorphine, methadone, and behavioral treatment. But as a first line of defense against the diversion that fuels the epidemic, nothing else we have is as practical or as ready to deploy.

Before we talk about curbing prescription drug abuse, we should be honest about a tension that most policy discussions paper over: any policy aggressive enough to meaningfully reduce abuse will also leave some patients in untreated pain. Pretending otherwise is the surest way to design bad policy.

Chronic pain affects roughly 100 million American adults. For many of them, opioid medication is the difference between functioning and not functioning. These are not the patients on cable news; they're the 62-year-old with degenerative disc disease, the cancer survivor with neuropathy, the construction worker whose back gave out at 45. They already face suspicion at pharmacy counters, urine tests at pain clinics, and physicians who increasingly refuse to prescribe at all because the regulatory risk has become intolerable. Every new restriction — tighter scheduling, prescription monitoring, prescription limits, mandatory in-person visits — falls on them too.

That doesn't mean we should do nothing. It means we should be modest and targeted. Three principles should guide reform.

First, focus on the supply side of diversion, not the demand side of legitimate use. Pill mills — clinics that exist solely to write opioid prescriptions for cash — are not a hard regulatory problem. They are visible, their prescribing patterns are statistically obvious, and states that have actually gone after them (Florida finally did) have seen results. The DEA and state medical boards know who these prescribers are.

Second, regulate the manufacturers more aggressively. Abuse-deterrent formulations — pills that can't easily be crushed and snorted or injected — should be the standard, not the premium product. The FDA has the authority to require this; it should use it.

Third, decouple addiction treatment from the criminal justice system. The biggest barrier to people getting off opioids isn't access to pills, it's access to buprenorphine, methadone, and counseling. Federal caps on the number of patients a physician can treat with buprenorphine are arbitrary and counterproductive.

What we should not do is impose blanket prescription caps, mandatory tapering schedules, or DEA quotas that treat every chronic-pain patient as a presumptive addict. The opioid problem is real, but it is not best solved by making the medical system less responsive to suffering. Whitney Houston's death is a tragedy; turning every cancer patient into a suspect would be another one.

The framing of this debate — how to curb prescription drug abuse "while ensuring patients still receive the medications they need" — assumes that the two goals are in tension. They are, but only because we have built a treatment infrastructure that addresses pain almost exclusively through pills. Change the infrastructure, and the tension eases.

Consider what a typical American patient with chronic lower back pain encounters. A 15-minute primary care visit, an X-ray, a referral that may take weeks, and in the meantime a prescription for hydrocodone. Physical therapy is often covered grudgingly, with high copays and limited visits. Cognitive behavioral therapy for chronic pain — well-supported by evidence — is rarely covered at all. Acupuncture, massage, mindfulness-based stress reduction: out of pocket. The cheapest, fastest option for both doctor and insurer is the prescription pad.

Compare this to how chronic pain is handled in much of Western Europe, where multimodal pain clinics integrate physical therapy, psychological treatment, and judicious pharmacology. Opioid prescribing rates are a fraction of ours; chronic pain outcomes are comparable or better.

The policy implication is that curbing prescription drug abuse is largely a problem of health-care financing. As long as our reimbursement system pays generously for a pill and stingily for everything else, doctors and patients will reach for the pill. Medicare and Medicaid set the tone for private insurers; if CMS expanded coverage for evidence-based non-pharmacological pain treatments and bundled chronic pain care, the private market would follow.

This wouldn't eliminate opioid prescribing — for acute pain, post-surgical pain, and end-of-life care, opioids remain indispensable. But it would shrink the population of patients on long-term opioid therapy for conditions where the evidence of long-term benefit is, candidly, thin. Fewer chronic users means fewer leftover pills in medicine cabinets, fewer opportunities for diversion, fewer accidental dependencies.

The other piece is take-back programs. The DEA's National Prescription Drug Take-Back Days have collected hundreds of tons of unused medication; making such collection routine, at every pharmacy, would close one of the largest pipelines to recreational use. According to SAMHSA data, most teenagers who misuse prescription opioids get them from a friend or family member — usually from a medicine cabinet, not a dealer.

Curb the supply at its origin, treat pain with more than pharmacology, and the celebrity overdose headlines will become rarer.

Let's stop pretending that prescription drug abuse is fundamentally a medical or regulatory problem. It is a problem about how Americans cope with psychic and physical pain — and the only durable answers are cultural, not pharmaceutical.

Whitney Houston, like Michael Jackson, Anna Nicole Smith, Heath Ledger, and the long list before them, did not die because some rogue doctor invented a new way to write a prescription. She died, almost certainly, because a particular kind of suffering met a particular kind of available chemistry, and because the people around her — and the culture around them — treated pharmacological self-management as normal. So do millions of non-celebrities. The mom with Xanax for "stress," the executive on Ambien for sleep and Adderall for focus, the teenager who treats his parents' Percocet as a weekend recreation — these are not aberrations. They are mainstream behaviors in a country that increasingly believes there is a pill for every discomfort.

You cannot regulate your way out of a cultural condition. You can make doctor-shopping harder; we should. You can crack down on pill mills; we should. You can require tamper-resistant formulations; we should. But none of this addresses the underlying demand, which is the willingness — even the eagerness — of ordinary Americans to medicate moods, sensations, and life problems that previous generations endured, talked through, or worked around.

What might actually change the culture? Honest public health messaging would help, the kind we eventually got around to about tobacco. Insurance parity for mental health treatment, so that depression and anxiety get talked about with a therapist rather than numbed with a benzodiazepine, would help more. The grim irony of celebrity overdose coverage is that it reinforces the very mindset that produces it: drugs as glamorous, drugs as escape, drugs as the natural medium of intense feeling. If we want fewer such deaths — and the tens of thousands of anonymous ones — we will have to be a different kind of country, not just have a different prescribing protocol.

Most of the proposals you'll hear in response to this question — monitoring databases, prescriber education, tighter scheduling — treat prescription drug abuse as a problem of supply leakage. But there is one piece of low-hanging fruit that has been sitting on the FDA's desk for years: require abuse-deterrent formulations as the default for high-risk opioids, and pull the easily-abusable versions from the market.

The model is OxyContin. When Purdue Pharma reformulated it in 2010 to be harder to crush, dissolve, and inject, the change was driven less by public-spiritedness than by patent considerations. But the public health effect was real and rapid: surveys of opioid abusers documented a sharp drop in OxyContin abuse, with users switching to other formulations or, troublingly, to heroin. That substitution is a serious problem, but it also shows that formulation matters — making a drug harder to abuse meaningfully reduces its abuse.

The FDA has the legal authority to require abuse-deterrent properties for new opioid approvals and to encourage reformulation of existing ones through labeling and exclusivity incentives. It has been slow to use this authority aggressively, partly because the science of "abuse deterrence" is genuinely complicated — a pill that can't be crushed can still be swallowed in handfuls — and partly because industry has lobbied against standards that would advantage some manufacturers over others.

That's a regulatory failure that can be fixed. The agency should set clear, tiered standards for abuse-deterrent properties, require new opioid approvals to meet them, and provide a finite phase-out window for legacy formulations. Generic manufacturers, which currently have little incentive to invest in deterrent technology, should be required to match the deterrent properties of the branded versions they copy.

This won't solve the demand problem; nothing single-handedly will. People determined to abuse opioids will find ways, and as the heroin substitution data show, restricting one supply can divert users to another. But the marginal abuser — the curious teenager, the back-pain patient sliding into dependence, the recreational user who doesn't yet know how to inject — is exactly the population most affected by friction. Make abuse harder and slower, and many such users never progress to addiction in the first place.

That is a meaningful win, and one within reach of existing regulatory authority. We should use it.

If we truly want to curb prescription drug abuse without denying needed medications, we must follow the money. The epidemic of accidental overdose deaths in America is not a story of weak individuals or lax doctors alone; it is the direct, predictable result of a pharmaceutical industry that spent the past fifteen years deliberately downplaying addiction risks and aggressively marketing opioids for everyday, chronic pain.
