Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Sabel and Victor have written one of the most clarifying diagnoses of the climate diplomacy stalemate in years. Their core insight—that the architects of the UNFCCC misread the Montreal Protocol, taking from it a global treaty form while leaving behind the sectoral problem-solving machinery that actually made it work—deserves to be central to any serious post-Paris reckoning. So does their argument that experimentalist governance, with its braided top-down framework goals and bottom-up technical learning, fits the structure of decarbonization better than either the Kyoto-style mandate or the Paris-style voluntarism. And yet several features of the climate problem make me less optimistic than they are that experimentalism, even applied with the discipline they describe, can carry the load they want it to carry.

Begin with the Montreal analogy itself. The chlorofluorocarbon problem was extraordinary in two respects that make it a poor template for greenhouse gases. First, ODS were produced by a remarkably concentrated industry—a handful of chemical companies in a handful of countries—and used in a finite set of applications. The "sectoral committees" Sabel and Victor celebrate could canvass essentially every relevant engineer on the planet. Carbon dioxide, by contrast, is the joint product of nearly every economic activity humans undertake, generated by hundreds of millions of point sources, and embedded in capital stocks—buildings, power plants, industrial boilers, vehicles, land-use patterns—with replacement cycles measured in decades. Second, and decisively, DuPont's defection from industry orthodoxy turned on the fact that fluorochemical alternatives to CFCs were patentable and potentially more profitable than the products being phased out. The penalty default worked because the technological substitution was, for the dominant incumbent, a business opportunity. ExxonMobil and Saudi Aramco face nothing remotely comparable. Their core asset—proven reserves—is what decarbonization renders worthless. There is no analogue to DuPont's "break ranks" moment available to a major oil company without dissolving the company. Sabel and Victor acknowledge that decarbonization is "much more modest" in scale than ODS phaseout—they have it backwards; it is vastly larger—and they do not adequately reckon with what this asymmetry means for the politics of penalty defaults.

This connects to a second worry: penalty defaults need an enforcer. In the Montreal case, the United States and the European Community could credibly threaten trade sanctions and exclusion from lucrative markets because they were the markets, and they were aligned. The world Sabel and Victor describe in their opening pages—of fraying global order, of a paralyzed WTO, of receding American hegemony, of mistrust between China and the West—is precisely a world in which credible cross-border penalty defaults are hard to assemble. A carbon border adjustment mechanism of the kind the EU is contemplating may eventually serve this function for some industries, but the political coalition needed to wield it consistently across steel, cement, aluminum, shipping, and aviation does not yet exist, and Sabel and Victor do not say much about how to build one. Their argument tends to treat the penalty default as a technology that can be installed once a sectoral institution is up and running. In practice it is the scarcest resource in the entire scheme.

A third point concerns time. Experimentalist governance is, by design, iterative and patient: thin consensus thickens through cycles of local trial, peer review, and adjustment. Montreal took roughly thirty years from the Vienna Convention to near-universal phaseout. We do not have thirty years for the steel sector. The IPCC's 1.5°C pathways require global emissions to be cut roughly in half by 2030—nine years from now—and to reach net zero around mid-century. Sabel and Victor sometimes write as if the alternative to experimentalism is the failed Kyoto template; but the actually pressing alternative, which they barely engage, is aggressive carbon pricing, sectoral mandates, and large-scale public investment in deployment. These tools are blunter than experimentalist learning, but they have the virtue of moving capital quickly. A more honest framing would be that experimentalism is essential for the hard-to-abate sectors where the technology is genuinely undetermined—green steel, sustainable aviation fuel, cement chemistry—and supplementary in the sectors where the technology is already known and the bottleneck is deployment finance and political will. Power generation in most large grids is now the latter case, not the former.

Fourth, I think the authors are too dismissive of what Paris can become. They concede Paris a single function—legitimating sanction by activists and governments against laggards—but the Agreement also institutionalized a five-year ratcheting cycle and a global stocktake whose first iteration begins in 2023. If the stocktake were paired with sectoral assessments analogous to Montreal's technical and economic panels—organized by industry and by region rather than by country—the existing Paris architecture could be made considerably more experimentalist than Sabel and Victor allow. The proposals are out there: the various initiatives under the Climate and Clean Air Coalition, the Mission Innovation framework launched in 2015, the IEA's sectoral roadmaps, the Nordhaus-style climate clubs that link trade access to participation. Sabel and Victor's framework is most useful, I think, when read as a guide for how to wire these existing efforts into a coherent feedback loop, rather than as a reason to consign Paris itself to symbolic status.

Finally, a Deweyan point Sabel and Victor invoke but do not fully cash out. They are right that decarbonization will reach into everyday life and that the legitimacy of expert-driven decisions depends on integrating where the shoe pinches. But the pinching here is profoundly unequal. Coal communities in West Virginia or Shanxi, smallholder farmers in palm oil regions, and informal-sector workers in fossil-dependent megacities will bear costs that knowledge workers in coastal cities will not. Experimentalist governance has historically been better at convening producers and regulators than at incorporating the people who lose their jobs and tax bases. The Green New Deal vision the authors gesture toward, for all its governance vagueness, is centrally a claim that distributional commitments must be hardwired into climate institutions, not negotiated downstream. An experimentalism that does not absorb that claim will reproduce the elite distrust the authors themselves identify as a structural obstacle.

None of this refutes Sabel and Victor; it sharpens what their proposal must do. Experimentalist governance is the right meta-theory. The harder question is whether we can install its missing parts—penalty defaults, sectoral institutions, distributive guarantees—on a calendar the atmosphere will accept.

The essay by Victor and Sabel offers a bracing diagnosis of three decades of diplomatic failure on climate change and advances a tempting alternative—one rooted in a pragmatic, piecemeal experimentalism that learns from the unexpected success of the Montreal Protocol. Their core insight is that the search for a binding global treaty from the start has been a recipe for paralysis, and that building consensus through on-the-ground problem solving, sector by sector, is more likely to disrupt the status quo. Yet while their critique of the orthodox approach is largely persuasive, the solution they champion underestimates the sheer political and economic power of entrenched fossil fuel interests and glosses over the gulf between the ozone crisis and the far deeper transformation that decarbonization demands. Experimentalist governance, for all its appeal, risks becoming a technocratic retreat from the kind of muscular public action and global solidarity that climate change still requires.

The Montreal Protocol is an irresistible model, but its very success rests on features that climate change does not share. The ozone crisis implicated a handful of industrial chemicals for which substitutes could be developed at manageable cost and, crucially, produced at a profit by the same firms that made the offending substances. DuPont broke ranks because it recognized that a phase-out could yield commercial advantage, and because the reputational liability of destroying the ozone layer threatened its broader chemical business. In the climate arena, by contrast, the core business of the world’s most profitable and politically potent corporations—fossil fuel extraction, refining, and distribution—is incompatible with decarbonization. There is no equivalent of DuPont: a major oil company has no pathway to profitability by simply swapping crude oil for a benign substitute while retaining its corporate identity. The penalty default that Victor and Sabel describe requires that once one innovator moves, laggards face market exclusion. But in climate, the laggards are not niche chemical manufacturers; they are nation-states whose entire rentier economies depend on oil and gas revenues, and whose refusal to cooperate cannot be disciplined by trade sanctions without fracturing the global trading system on which experimentalist governance itself depends.

This brings us to a deeper tension in the essay’s argument. Victor and Sabel note the paralysis of the World Trade Organization and the fraying of global order. Yet the penalty defaults that make the Montreal model work rely on powerful states—mainly the United States—threatening trade sanctions against violators. In a world of rising nationalism, great-power rivalry, and a hollowed-out WTO, the credibility of such threats is vanishingly low. The United States may threaten China or India with trade penalties for climate obstruction, but in practice such threats would ignite retaliatory spirals that no administration, least of all one facing domestic polarization, is likely to sustain. The experimentalist cycle assumes that once local innovations succeed, they can be codified as standards and enforced through market access, but without a hegemonic enforcer willing to punish laggards, the penalty default collapses into a voluntary scheme that the least ambitious can ignore. The very geopolitical fragmentation the authors lament thus erodes the foundations of their proposed solution.

Equally troubling is the democratic deficit that experimentalist governance can conceal. The essay invokes John Dewey’s vision of collaboration between expert and citizen, but the actual institutions it celebrates—Montreal’s sectoral committees, California’s Air Resources Board, the EU’s Water Framework Directive—are overwhelmingly technocratic bodies in which corporate and regulatory elites negotiate standards, often with minimal public participation. The Maersk example in the shipping sector is instructive: the largest container firm coordinates demonstration programs and helps write the rules inside the International Maritime Organization. That may accelerate innovation, but it also cements the influence of incumbent players who can steer standards to their own advantage, protecting themselves from nimbler disruptors while doing just enough to appear climate-friendly. Without robust mechanisms of democratic accountability, experimentalist governance can degenerate into the kind of elite consensus that populist movements rightly distrust—and that has already inflamed climate politics in many countries. The Green New Deal is mentioned as a vision strong on mandates but silent on governance, yet the opposite danger is equally acute: an elaborate governance architecture that lacks the democratic mandate for the trillions in public investment and the industrial transformation that deep decarbonization requires.

Indeed, the essay’s silence on the scale of public finance and the politics of a just transition is a conspicuous gap. The Multilateral Fund that smoothed developing-country participation in the Montreal Protocol was tiny relative to the costs of climate mitigation and adaptation. The funding required to phase out coal in India, to build renewable grids across Africa, or to compensate fossil fuel workers in West Virginia and Alberta is orders of magnitude larger. Victor and Sabel hint that a Biden administration might dramatically increase resources, but they offer no governance framework for mobilizing that capital or for ensuring that it reaches the communities that fear—with good reason—that decarbonization means economic devastation. Experimentalist governance excels at iterative learning, but it does not, by itself, generate the massive fiscal commitments that will determine whether climate action is perceived as fair or as yet another elite imposition. Without a narrative of shared sacrifice and shared benefit, and without the institutional muscle to redistribute costs and gains, the thin consensus that gets experimentalism started is likely to shatter when the losers begin to organize.

Finally, the essay’s dismissal of the Paris Agreement as little more than a norm-setting forum underestimates the potential of that framework to evolve. Paris’s reliance on nationally determined contributions and periodic review is indeed weak, but it also creates a transparency mechanism and a five-year ratchet cycle that, combined with growing civil society pressure and the logic of peer accountability, can tighten over time. The “rulebook” that Victor and Sabel deride as obstructive is precisely the kind of information infrastructure that experimentalist governance needs to compare efforts and identify laggards. Rather than jettisoning Paris in favor of a fragmented sectoral approach, the wiser course would be to embed experimentalist initiatives within the Paris framework—allowing sectoral clubs and transnational networks to generate credible pathways, but anchoring them in a universal process that preserves the norm of common but differentiated responsibility and maintains political pressure on all nations. The symbolic power of global goals, however unenforceable in the short term, creates the penalty default writ large: it empowers protesters, shareholders, and litigants to demand that firms and governments act in line with declared ambitions, and it provides a baseline against which experimentalist achievements can be measured.

Victor and Sabel are right that the top-down, consensus-or-bust model of climate diplomacy has failed and that learning from local and sectoral successes is indispensable. But the climate crisis is not merely a coordination problem under uncertainty; it is a confrontation with the most powerful industrial complex in history. Overcoming it demands not only the ingenuity of experimentalist governance but also the raw political power to break the stranglehold of incumbents and the fiscal capacity to make the transition just. The Montreal Protocol worked because the affected industries could be redirected with relatively modest pressure and because rich countries paid the tab. Climate change will require a far more formidable combination of public investment, democratic mobilization, and global burden-sharing. For all the elegance of their framework, Victor and Sabel offer only half the story, and the missing half is the one that will determine whether their quiet revolution gets started at all.

David G. Victor and Charles Sabel offer a deeply pragmatic, structurally sound diagnosis of the failures of global climate diplomacy. By correctly identifying that the top-down, consensus-driven model of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is ill-equipped to handle the immense complexities of deep decarbonization, they clear the air of a pervasive diplomatic delusion. Their proposed alternative—experimentalist governance—provides a compelling operational blueprint. Shifting our focus from monolithic, legally binding global treaties to sector-by-sector innovation, driven by frontline actors and enforced by "penalty defaults," is precisely the strategic pivot the climate crisis demands.

However, while the architecture of the Montreal Protocol serves as an instructional model for this kind of governance, its political economy is dangerously misleading when applied to the fossil fuel economy. Victor and Sabel accurately extract the governance mechanisms that made Montreal work, but they underestimate the sheer gravitational pull of the carbon economy. To make experimentalist governance succeed in the 2020s, we must recognize that the "penalty defaults" required to break the fossil fuel status quo will need to be vastly more muscular, confrontational, and economically disruptive than anything attempted in the 1980s.

The core limitation of the Montreal analogy lies in the nature of the pollutant and the industries that produce it. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and other ozone-depleting substances were discrete industrial chemicals utilized in specific, largely peripheral sectors: refrigerants, aerosols, and solvents. When the ozone hole was discovered, the chemical manufacturers—most notably DuPont—initially resisted regulation. But once the science became undeniable and the regulatory writing was on the wall, these firms realized they could pivot. In fact, DuPont championed the phase-out of CFCs precisely because they held the patents for the hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) alternatives. The transition was a manageable, and ultimately profitable, technological substitution for a concentrated sector.

Greenhouse gas emissions, by contrast, are not a byproduct of a specific chemical process; they are the fundamental energy backbone of modern industrial civilization. Decarbonizing the global economy does not mean asking a few chemical companies to switch their product lines; it means rendering trillions of dollars of fossil fuel reserves permanently stranded. It presents an existential threat to multinational oil majors, massive utility companies, and entirely petroleum-dependent sovereign states.

Consequently, Victor and Sabel’s vision of sectoral working groups, where industry regulators and producers collaborate to find alternatives, risks profound regulatory capture if applied naively to fossil fuels. The fossil fuel industry has spent the last three decades systematically funding disinformation, capturing legislative bodies, and delaying action. Unlike the makers of metered-dose inhalers or refrigerators, the incumbent giants of the carbon economy will not sit at the table in good faith to engineer their own demise. If they are invited to co-design the transition, they will use the language of experimentalism—pilot projects, feasibility studies, and transition schedules—to institutionalize delay.

This means that for experimentalist governance to work, the "penalty defaults"—the threats that make inaction riskier than innovation—must be external and incredibly severe. Victor and Sabel note that penalty defaults destabilize the status quo, transforming obstruction into a risky bet. But where do these defaults come from today? The Paris Agreement, as they note, provides a moral and political authorization for action, but it lacks teeth. In November 2020, as we look to rebuild the post-pandemic global economy, the necessary penalty defaults must be forged in the realms of hard economic policy: international trade, financial regulation, and aggressive industrial policy.

At the international level, the most potent penalty default currently under development is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM). As the European Union moves forward with its European Green Deal, the implementation of a CBAM would effectively impose a tariff on carbon-intensive imports from countries that fail to price or regulate emissions. This perfectly aligns with Victor and Sabel’s framework: it does not demand global consensus at the WTO or the UNFCCC, but rather uses the leverage of a massive economic bloc to force a sectoral penalty on laggards. If the EU penalizes imported steel or cement made with high-carbon energy, producers in China, India, and the United States will be forced to innovate simply to maintain market access. This is experimentalist governance with real geopolitical leverage. The incoming Biden administration should immediately look to coordinate with the EU on such border adjustments to create a transatlantic penalty default for high-emitting sectors.

A second vital domain for penalty defaults lies in the global financial system. Central banks, financial regulators, and massive asset managers are slowly waking up to the systemic risks of climate change. Efforts to mandate climate-risk disclosures and perform climate stress-tests on major financial institutions operate precisely on the logic of experimentalist governance. By requiring banks to transparently model how their portfolios would fare under rapid decarbonization scenarios, regulators force the market to price in the risk of stranded assets. If the cost of capital for coal plants and oil exploration skyrockets because investors fear future regulation, the market itself becomes a penalty default. Frontline actors—in this case, investors and corporate boards—are forced to shift capital toward clean energy, not out of altruism, but out of financial self-preservation.

Furthermore, applying experimentalist governance to the global climate crisis requires a massive escalation of the funding mechanisms Victor and Sabel praise. The authors rightly highlight the Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund, which subsidized the transition for developing nations and helped build local administrative capacity. It was an essential tool for preventing diplomatic deadlock. However, replacing CFCs globally cost roughly a few billion dollars over a couple of decades. Transitioning the Global South away from fossil fuels, adapting to the locked-in impacts of a warming planet, and building out zero-carbon grids will require trillions of dollars in new capital.

If we are to piece together partial successes across diverse nations, the Bretton Woods institutions—the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank—must be radically repurposed. Developing nations cannot be expected to act as laboratories for green innovation if they are simultaneously suffocated by sovereign debt and starved of capital. A true climate experimentalism requires debt forgiveness tied to climate action, massive green technology transfers, and the issuance of Special Drawing Rights to finance clean infrastructure. Without a financial engine scaled to the problem, the Global South will simply be locked out of the sectoral innovations occurring in Europe, California, or China.

Domestically, the political dynamics of experimentalist governance also require a more forceful approach than Victor and Sabel imply. They note that progressive calls for massive public investments, like the Green New Deal, are "often silent on governance." While this may be true of the broadest rhetorical frameworks, the underlying political economy of these movements is vital for providing the political pressure that makes experimentalism possible. The youth climate movement and progressive insurgents have done more to threaten the social license of the fossil fuel industry than decades of technocratic tinkering.

As the Biden administration takes office, it will face a divided, polarized Congress. Victor and Sabel are correct that progress will likely depend on aggressive administrative action and place-based solutions at the state level. But these executive actions—whether tightening fuel efficiency standards, utilizing the EPA to regulate power plant emissions, or using federal procurement to guarantee markets for clean steel and electric vehicles—must be explicitly designed to fracture the political coalition of the fossil fuel industry. By directing federal subsidies and research grants toward clean energy sectors, the government can incubate new, powerful green constituencies that will fight to maintain and expand those regulations over time.

Ultimately, Victor and Sabel are right to abandon the illusion that a single, legally binding global treaty will save us from climate catastrophe. The top-down versus bottom-up dichotomy has indeed paralyzed us for too long. Sector-by-sector problem solving, driven by a thin initial consensus andratcheted up through continuous review, is the only historically proven way to manage complex, technological transitions under deep uncertainty.

But as we embrace experimentalist governance, we must do so with our eyes wide open to the brute realities of power. The fossil fuel economy will not be phased out through polite committee work alone. The successes of the California Air Resources Board or the European Water Framework Directive were achieved because regulators were willing to bring the hammer down on entrenched interests. To fix the climate in the decades ahead, we must pair the agile, iterative structures of experimentalist governance with the raw political and economic power necessary to make the continuation of the fossil fuel era impossible. We have the governance model; now we must forge the penalty defaults that will force the world to use it.

Victor and Sabel are right to puncture the comforting myth that a single global bargain will “solve” climate change. The climate regime has too often been judged by the elegance of its summit declarations rather than by tons of carbon dioxide kept out of the atmosphere. Their central insight—that decarbonization will advance through iterative, sectoral problem solving rather than one grand act of diplomatic will—is indispensable. But experimentalist governance, if it is to be more than a sophisticated name for muddling through, must be joined to a more explicit account of power, justice, and political economy. The climate crisis is not only a problem of uncertainty. It is also a problem of entrenched wealth, unequal vulnerability, and organized obstruction.

The Montreal Protocol is indeed the best example we have of a successful global environmental regime. Its virtues are exactly as Victor and Sabel describe: sectoral knowledge, repeated revision, financial assistance for poorer countries, and a credible threat that laggards would lose access to markets. It also shows that policy can create facts. Once governments signaled that ozone-depleting substances would be phased out, firms searched harder for substitutes; once substitutes existed, regulation could tighten. The apparent “cost” of environmental protection changed as innovation unfolded.

Climate policy needs this dynamic view of costs. For decades, opponents of climate action treated the energy system as if it were fixed: any reduction in emissions must therefore mean a reduction in prosperity. That assumption has already been shattered in parts of the economy. The cost of solar power, wind power, and batteries has fallen dramatically over the last decade, not by magic but through deployment, learning, supply chains, standards, and public support. California’s zero-emission vehicle rules, Germany’s feed-in tariffs, Chinese industrial policy, and U.S. federal tax credits have all helped create markets in which firms learned how to produce clean technologies at scale. The lesson is not that markets alone will save us. It is that markets respond to rules, subsidies, procurement, and expectations.

Still, the Montreal analogy has limits that should make us cautious. Ozone-depleting chemicals were important, but they were not the foundation of modern political economy. Fossil fuels are. Coal, oil, and gas are embedded in electricity, transport, agriculture, buildings, trade, geopolitics, and public finance. They generate not only emissions but rents: revenues for states, profits for companies, wages for workers, and cheap energy for consumers. The world does not merely need substitutes for a class of chemicals; it needs to transform the material basis of development.

That difference matters because obstruction in climate politics is not simply a temporary information problem. It is often a rational strategy by incumbents whose assets lose value in a decarbonizing world. Oil and gas companies may invest in low-carbon technologies, but many have also funded doubt, delayed regulation, and lobbied to preserve demand for their products. Coal interests have fought air pollution rules as well as carbon rules. Automakers have alternately cooperated with and resisted tighter standards. Utilities have embraced renewables where regulation rewards them and resisted them where business models are threatened. Experimental governance must therefore include conflict, not just learning.

The phrase “penalty default” is useful here, but it should be sharpened. Penalties do not appear automatically when innovators demonstrate feasibility. They are made by politics. Trade measures, procurement standards, clean electricity mandates, phaseout dates for internal combustion engines, methane rules, financial disclosure requirements, and public litigation are not merely technical devices. They are exercises of public authority that change the bargaining position of incumbents. Without them, experimentation can become a refuge for delay: pilot projects without phaseouts, voluntary pledges without accountability, innovation funds without emissions cuts.

The Paris Agreement is weaker than many of us would like, but its weakness should not be overstated. Paris did not create a world government; it created a framework for expectation, comparison, mobilization, and ratcheting. That is not enough, but it is not nothing. The very fact that governments, cities, investors, and companies now argue in the language of “net zero” reflects a change in the political field. The European Union has moved toward a Green Deal. China has announced an aim of carbon neutrality before 2060. Japan and South Korea have announced mid-century net-zero goals. These pledges are not self-executing and may prove hollow. But they create openings for domestic actors to demand plans, budgets, standards, and investments.

This is where Victor and Sabel’s sectoral approach can strengthen Paris rather than bypass it. The problem with nationally determined contributions is not simply that they are national. It is that they are too aggregated. A national pledge can conceal whether a country has a credible plan for steel, cement, transport, buildings, methane, land use, and electricity. Sectoral review would make the Paris process more real. Instead of asking only whether a country has promised a 30 or 50 percent reduction by a given date, reviewers should ask: How fast is coal power retiring? Are new buildings being required to meet high efficiency standards? Is the grid being prepared for variable renewables? Are industrial plants testing hydrogen, electrification, or carbon capture where appropriate? Are methane leaks measured and regulated? Are public funds supporting workers and communities through transition?

Such questions would also expose a deeper issue: the capacity of states. Experimentalism requires capable public institutions. It needs agencies able to gather information, convene firms and workers, set standards, monitor results, revise rules, and resist capture. CARB is not powerful because it is experimental in the abstract; it is powerful because California built a durable regulatory apparatus with legal authority, technical expertise, and political backing. Many governments lack that capacity. Some are dependent on fossil revenues. Some face debt burdens, weak grids, informal economies, or basic development needs that make decarbonization harder. If climate cooperation is to work, it must finance not only technologies but institutions.

That is why the Multilateral Fund in the Montreal regime is so important—and why climate finance remains a moral and practical test. Wealthy countries promised to mobilize $100 billion annually for climate action in developing countries by 2020. Even if the accounting is disputed, the scale is plainly inadequate relative to the need. A serious climate regime must help countries build clean power systems, adapt to unavoidable warming, protect forests without impoverishing local communities, and avoid locking in high-carbon infrastructure. Loans alone will not do. Nor will a model in which poor countries are asked to bear the risks of experimentation while rich countries capture the patents and supply chains.

Justice is not an ornament to climate policy; it is a condition of durability. In the United States, the politics of decarbonization will fail if it is perceived as a project by professionals for professionals, with costs dumped on workers and benefits captured by investors. Coal miners, refinery workers, oilfield communities, and manufacturing towns have reason to distrust promises of “transition” when previous waves of economic change left many places abandoned. A Green New Deal, whatever one thinks of the slogan, names a real political requirement: climate policy must visibly improve people’s lives. Cleaner air, cheaper energy bills, good jobs, reliable transit, healthier housing, and investment in neglected communities are not side benefits. They are the social foundation of ambitious decarbonization.

Here experimental governance has something valuable to offer progressives as well as technocrats. Grand spending bills can fail if they do not learn. Weatherization programs, transit investments, grid upgrades, and industrial policy all require feedback from the people who use and implement them. A national clean energy standard or large public investment program should not be designed as a rigid blueprint from Washington. It should set goals, fund local and regional experimentation, compare performance, spread successes, and correct failures. But it must also have deadlines and consequences. Learning without obligation becomes drift; obligation without learning becomes backlash.

The same is true internationally. Sectoral clubs may be essential, especially in steel, cement, aviation, shipping, methane, and vehicles. Border carbon adjustments, if carefully designed, may become part of that strategy. But clubs must not become green protectionism in disguise. The immediate task, in late 2020, is to turn the new political opening into institutions that can survive polarization. The federal government should use its purchasing power to create markets for low-carbon steel, cement, vehicles, and electricity. Victor and Sabel’s most important contribution is to redirect attention from climate diplomacy as theater to climate governance as practice. They remind us that uncertainty is not an excuse for paralysis; it is a reason to build institutions that learn. But the climate problem is urgent enough that learning must occur under pressure. We need experimentation, yes—but experimentation disciplined by emissions targets, public investment, enforceable standards, and justice. We need sectoral problem solving, but also mass politics capable of overcoming those who profit from delay. We need Paris, not as a savior, but as a platform on which more concrete regimes can be built.

The diagnosis that Victor and Sabel offer of international climate diplomacy is largely compelling. Experimentalist governance captures something real about how effective regulation often works: iterative adjustment, feedback from practitioners, and graduated pressure rather than all-or-nothing commitments. The authors are right that this contrasts sharply with the top-down, bottom-up dichotomy that has constrained climate diplomacy. But translating these principles from successful domestic regulatory contexts like CARB or the EU's Water Framework Directive to global scale encounters fundamental obstacles they underestimate.

The Montreal Protocol succeeded in part because the problem it addressed was comparatively tractable. Deep decarbonization involves transforming entire energy systems, transportation infrastructure, industrial processes, and consumption patterns—changes that dwarf anything involved in phasing out CFCs. The authors acknowledge this scale difference but perhaps not its implications for governance. When transformation requires dismantling fossil-fuel-dependent systems that constitute the backbone of modern economies, the political resistance is categorically different.

The Montreal Protocol was sponsored by the United States and other wealthy nations that provided both the Multilateral Fund and the political cover for trade sanctions against laggards. The penalty defaults that made the regime work depended on asymmetries of power that no longer obtain in the same form. Today's global landscape features a fragmented hegemon, a rising China that is simultaneously essential to any climate solution and increasingly willing to contest Western-led governance, and a constellation of emerging economies with divergent interests. Designing penalty defaults that function across these power dynamics is a far more daunting task than the authors' confident prescription suggests.

Their sector-based approach is intuitively appealing but faces its own challenges. Industrial sectors like steel and cement are indeed more globally integrated than, say, building codes, and international coordination within these sectors may be more feasible than country-level agreements. Yet sectors are not apolitical abstractions. The steel industry has spent decades building institutional capacity to resist exactly the kind of intrusive regulation the authors envision. The transportation sector encompasses everything from zero-emission vehicle mandates to airline emissions—domains with very different political economies. Identifying which sectors are amenable to experimentalist governance and which will resist it requires attention to political geography that the authors' framework does not fully provide.

The authors' treatment of the Paris Agreement is perceptive but perhaps too dismissive of its continued relevance. They argue that Paris serves only to authorize protest and punish laggards—a limited but essential function. Several cities, states, and corporations have used Paris not just as a symbolic reference point but as a legal and practical framework for their own commitments. Dismissing this as insufficient misses the possibility that the agreement's value lies precisely in providing legitimacy to actors the authors would likely support: state-level regulators, firms experimenting with clean technology, and civil society groups demanding accountability.

What the authors call experimentalist governance has much to recommend it as a heuristic for thinking about institutional design. The emphasis on iterative learning, the rejection of premature closure on means and ends, and the insistence on connecting technical expertise to democratic accountability are all valuable. The Montreal Protocol worked because there was a moment—a hole in the sky, an engaged U.S. government, a corporate outlier willing to break ranks—when the politics aligned with the technical possibilities. Sabel and Victor have written the most clear-eyed assessment of climate diplomacy that has appeared in a long time, and their experimentalist alternative deserves to be taken seriously by anyone who has watched the pageant of UNFCCC summits with mounting despair. The essay invokes "penalty defaults" repeatedly, and rightly so. Montreal had something analogous, even if it is rarely described this way: the implicit "price" on chlorofluorocarbons was infinite, because they were being phased out entirely on a known schedule. Fair enough. The principles are good. The problem is that Montreal worked not because of these principles in the abstract but because of the particular political and technical conditions that made the principles effective. Consider what the ozone problem actually looked like in 1987. The number of significant CFC producers in the world was perhaps a dozen, concentrated in a handful of advanced industrial economies. The substances were used in identifiable consumer products (aerosols, foams, refrigerators) and industrial processes (cleaning, fire suppression) that represented a small share of total economic activity. Substitutes were technically demanding but conceptually straightforward: find a molecule that performs the same function without the chlorine atom that destroys ozone. The economic value of the substances being phased out was tiny relative to the value of the host industries continuing to operate. DuPont made most of its money from things other than CFCs; it could afford to be a hero. And — this is decisive — the United States was politically dominant, technologically leading, and committed to the regime. American sanctions threats were credible because America had something the rest of the world wanted access to.

Now consider carbon. The number of significant emitters is, depending on how you count, every firm on Earth or every household on Earth. Fossil fuels are not a marginal input into a few consumer products; they are the energetic basis of the entire economy. The substitutes are not chemically analogous molecules but entirely different physical infrastructures (grids, fleets, building stocks) that must be built at unprecedented speed and scale. The economic value of the substances being phased out is many trillions of dollars in reserves on the balance sheets of the largest companies on Earth, plus sovereign wealth in petrostates whose entire fiscal structure depends on continued extraction. There is no DuPont here — no major fossil producer that can credibly say it makes most of its money in something else and can afford to be the hero. Begin with what Sabel and Victor identify as the indispensable function of "penalty defaults." In their Montreal account, these took several forms: domestic electoral pressure on Western governments, brand-and-license pressure on consumer-facing firms like DuPont, and trade sanctions threatened by powerful Northern states against laggard Southern ones. These pressures worked because they were credible, because the targets had something to lose, and because the costs of compliance were modest enough that the threatened parties could be brought around without political revolution.

The trouble with applying this to climate is that the analogous pressures are diffuse, uncoordinated, and often unenforceable. Domestic electoral pressure exists in some countries (Germany, the UK, Denmark) but not in others (Russia, Australia, Saudi Arabia, and large portions of the United States). Brand pressure works on B2C firms but not on commodity producers selling into anonymous markets. Trade sanctions are theoretically available but politically toxic and legally fraught under the WTO regime that the authors themselves note is paralyzed.

What a climate club does is consolidate these scattered pressures into a single, predictable, rule-based instrument: differential market access. Members of the club adopt a common carbon price (or its regulatory equivalent), apply a border adjustment to imports from non-members, and condition further trade integration on emissions performance. Non-members face a clear choice — adopt comparable measures and gain access to the club's markets, or pay the border adjustment as the price of standing apart. The penalty default is no longer an ad hoc threat issued by individual states; it is the standing structure of the trade regime that members have built among themselves.

This is, I would argue, the most plausible path to making Sabel and Victor's sectoral experimentalism work at the speed and scale we need. Consider how it interacts with each layer of their framework. At the level of frontier industrial sectors — steel, cement, ammonia, aluminum — the club's border adjustment raises the cost of dirty imports to club markets, which makes investments in clean production (like the HYBRIT steel project in Sweden or the various low-carbon cement initiatives in the EU) commercially viable. Sabel and Victor's sectoral committees can do their patient work of testing alternatives and codifying standards, but the commercial logic that makes the work matter comes from the price differential the club creates. Without the differential, the patient work produces interesting demonstration projects that do not propagate; with it, the work produces templates that competitors race to adopt.

At the level of place-based sectors — buildings, grids, distribution — the club's effect is more indirect but still consequential. Club members are forced by their own internal carbon prices to find ways to decarbonize their domestic economies, which drives demand for the kinds of place-based experiments and local re-elaborations Sabel and Victor describe. The club does not directly govern these efforts, but it creates the macro-conditions under which they are funded and pursued.

At the level of hybrid sectors — forestry products, palm oil, agricultural commodities — the club's border adjustment can be extended to embedded emissions and to certified sustainability schemes. The EU's deforestation regulation, then being prepared, points in this direction. I also want to note what the essay gets exactly right: the critique of the top-down/bottom-up dichotomy that has paralyzed climate diplomacy. Paris was adopted in reaction to Kyoto's failure, which was understood as a failure of top-down binding commitments. Paris's voluntarism was thus presented as the opposite of Kyoto's approach. But the opposite of a failed approach is not necessarily a successful one; it may simply be a different failure mode. Victor and Sabel's observation that "the opposite of a failure does not make a success" applies not just to Paris but to much of climate discourse, which has oscillated between binding commitments and voluntary approaches without finding a third way.

The Dewey quotation is well chosen. Dewey's pragmatism emphasized that democratic governance must integrate expert knowledge with citizen participation. The Montreal Protocol's sectoral committees approximated this integration by bringing together industry, academia, and regulators. Climate governance needs more of this kind of working collaboration and less of the formal diplomacy that has dominated to date.

My final concern is about equity. Victor and Sabel note that the Multilateral Fund helped developing countries participate in Montreal by covering compliance costs. They do not ask whether this arrangement was sufficient or equitable. In practice, Montreal's framework allowed wealthy countries to maintain their technological advantages while requiring developing countries to adopt standards set by others. The technology transfer was real, but it reinforced rather than challenged existing patterns of dependency.

Climate change raises these equity concerns more acutely. Deep decarbonization in developing countries cannot be achieved through technology transfer alone; it requires comprehensive transformation of energy systems, industrial processes, and economic structures. The investment needs are staggering. Unless wealthy nations commit to financing this transformation at scales comparable to the problem, experimentalist governance in the Global South will remain incomplete.

Victor and Sabel might respond that experimentalism's emphasis on local adaptation and capacity-building addresses these concerns. Perhaps. But local adaptation without adequate resources is adaptation to poverty rather than adaptation to climate. A truly equitable experimentalist framework would combine local flexibility with global solidarity—recognizing that decarbonization is a shared responsibility requiring burden-sharing commensurate with capacity.

Despite these concerns, I endorse the essay's core argument. Global climate diplomacy has failed because it was designed around assumptions—universal binding commitments, formal consensus, top-down implementation—that do not match the nature of the problem. Experimentalist governance offers an alternative: thin consensus on goals, devolved problem-solving, peer review, and penalty defaults that make obstruction costly. This framework has worked in domains like ozone protection and water quality; it might work for climate change.

But "might" is the operative word. The essay provides a vision of what successful climate governance could look like. Building the political conditions for that vision is the work that remains. It is the harder work, and the essay's silence on how to accomplish it is its most significant limitation.
