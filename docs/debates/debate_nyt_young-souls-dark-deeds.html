Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Charging a 12-year-old as an adult is almost never defensible, and the impulse to do so reflects prosecutorial convenience more than justice. The premise of adult criminal law is that the defendant possesses the cognitive and moral capacities of an adult: the ability to foresee consequences, control impulses, resist peer influence, and understand the gravity of harm. By every measure we have — developmental psychology, neuroscience of the still-maturing prefrontal cortex, and the Supreme Court's own reasoning in Roper, Graham, and Miller — children that young simply do not.

The Michigan and Milwaukee cases illustrate the point rather than rebut it. A boy who stabs a playmate and then sobs into a 911 phone that he wants to die is not a calculating adult criminal; he is a child in some kind of crisis whose actions are catastrophic but whose mind is unformed. The Wisconsin girls, reportedly egged on by a fictional internet bogeyman called Slender Man, displayed exactly the magical thinking and susceptibility to suggestion that distinguishes preadolescents from adults. Treating their delusion as adult premeditation is a category error.

That doesn't mean nothing should happen. The juvenile system can confine a dangerous child, mandate intensive psychiatric treatment, and supervise him for years — in serious cases, with the possibility of extended jurisdiction into the mid-twenties. What it won't do is brand a seventh-grader a murderer for life, drop him into adult prison where the rates of sexual assault and suicide for juveniles are notoriously high, and foreclose any plausible rehabilitation. Most states still permit transfer to adult court for teenagers accused of homicide; extending that machinery down to twelve crosses a line that ought to be bright.

If we believe children are different — and our drinking, voting, driving, and contract laws all say we do — we cannot suspend that belief the moment the crime becomes terrible.

The proper response is not to throw them into a punitive adult apparatus, but to deploy intensive, secure juvenile facilities equipped with forensic psychiatry and long-term therapy. To insist on treating them as juveniles is not to be soft; it is to insist that justice demands proportionality and a honest reckoning with what a child’s mind can fathom.
