Advocates for women who say they have been treated callously while their rapists have gone unpunished are disturbed that false accusations are getting serious attention. But others have written that the problem of campus rape has been exaggerated, and that the accused are often mistreated.

How can women who say they have been raped be treated supportively and sensitively while their accounts are fully investigated and the accused get fair treatment?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The collapse of the Rolling Stone story is a setback for survivors, but it shouldn't obscure what we already know: campus rape is common, badly underreported, and badly handled when it is reported. The right response is not to choose sides between believing women and protecting the accused — it is to build a process serious enough to do both.

That starts with separating the support function from the adjudicative one. A student who reports an assault needs immediate access to medical care, counseling, academic accommodations, and a confidential advocate. None of those services should depend on whether her account is ultimately substantiated, and none of them require the university to have pre-judged the case. The mistake schools keep making is conflating compassionate treatment of a complainant with a finding against the respondent.

The adjudication itself should look more like a real investigation and less like a faculty committee improvising. That means trained, full-time investigators (ideally drawn from outside the campus disciplinary office), prompt collection of physical and digital evidence, written findings, and parallel cooperation with police when the complainant chooses to go that route. The accused should receive timely written notice of the allegations, access to the evidence, the right to an adviser, and a genuine opportunity to respond. The preponderance standard mandated by the 2011 Dear Colleague letter is defensible for a school proceeding that can expel but not imprison — but it only holds up morally if procedures are otherwise rigorous. Cross-examination through the panel, rather than direct confrontation, can protect complainants without gutting the respondent's ability to test the case.

Finally, universities should stop pretending they are courts. Their job is to decide whether a student may remain enrolled, not to pronounce on guilt. Honest framing of that limited question — paired with real support services and competent investigation — would do more for both accusers and accused than the current oscillation between credulity and backlash.

The unravelling of a high-profile magazine story has exposed a painful truth: the current system too often forces a false choice between supporting those who report sexual assault and safeguarding the rights of the accused. We can and must do both. The path forward lies not in taking sides, but in building institutional processes that are trauma-informed, procedurally fair, and rigorously independent.

First, support and investigation must be structurally separated. A reporting student should immediately be connected to a confidential victim advocate—someone who is not a college employee tasked with fact-finding—who can explain options, arrange counseling and academic accommodations, and provide support without triggering a formal inquiry. The advocate’s role is to listen, not to weigh evidence. Only when the student chooses to proceed does a formal investigation begin, preserving the complainant’s agency while avoiding the impression that the institution is reflexively adversarial.

For that investigation to be fair, colleges need trained, impartial investigators who are not embedded in the dean’s office or campus security. The 2011 “Dear Colleague” letter rightly demanded prompt and equitable response, but it also inadvertently encouraged an overcorrection: some schools adopted truncated processes that denied accused students meaningful notice of the allegations or the chance to present witnesses. A fair system gives both parties timely, written notice of the charges, access to the full evidence file, and the opportunity to suggest questions for witnesses. Cross-examination need not be a live confrontation—trauma-informed models allow each student to submit questions through an intermediary, preserving dignity while testing consistency.

Crucially, these proceedings should be governed by a clear, consistent standard of proof, and the preponderance-of-evidence standard remains appropriate for educational discipline. What must change is the perception that this means a presumption of guilt. The investigator’s report should be reviewed by a panel that includes faculty and trained community members, not students alone, to insulate decisions from campus politics. Both sides deserve written findings that articulate the reasoning, and both should have a right to appeal on procedural grounds.

This is not a zero-sum contest. When institutions invest in skilled investigators, transparent protocols, and separate advocacy resources, they can honor the pain of survivors without abandoning the fundamental principle that a disciplinary finding must rest on evidence, not sympathy or suspicion. Fairness and sensitivity are not opponents; they are the twin pillars of justice.

Universities should not be running rape tribunals. This is the uncomfortable conclusion that the current debate keeps circling without quite landing on. The well-meaning impulse to make campuses "safer" by giving deans the power to adjudicate felonies has produced a system that satisfies nobody: survivors get sanctions that bear no relation to the gravity of the harm, the accused face life-altering findings without basic procedural protections, and serious criminals—if they are criminals—face no real legal consequences at all.

This is justice for no one. It privatizes a public crime, transforms universities into quasi-courts they are not equipped to be, and lets the criminal justice system off the hook for its real failures in investigating sexual assault. A more honest division of labor would look like this: universities provide robust support services, academic and housing accommodations, and no-contact orders without requiring a finding of guilt. This will be resisted from both sides. Advocates fear, with reason, that the criminal system will fail survivors as it long has. The Rolling Stone fiasco is one symptom of a system structurally incapable of producing trustworthy outcomes. The answer is not to tweak it but to rethink what it is for.

What is missing from most of these discussions is the experience of the women who actually go through campus reporting processes. I have spent the last several years talking with students at half a dozen universities about what happens when they tell someone they were assaulted, and the picture is far bleaker than the recent backlash suggests.

A student tells a resident adviser she was raped at a party. She is sent to a Title IX coordinator who, depending on the school, may or may not be trained, may or may not be neutral, and may or may not even tell her about her options. She is asked, sometimes in those exact words, what she was wearing and how much she had to drink. If she chooses to pursue a hearing, she may wait months. She will see her assailant in the dining hall, in class, in her dorm. She may be discouraged, subtly or overtly, from going to the police, because the school does not want a crime statistic. If a hearing happens, she may be cross-examined—directly or through a panel—by people who have no idea how trauma affects memory. If she "wins," her assailant may receive a one-semester suspension and graduate on time.

This is the system that critics now describe as stacked against the accused. It is worth asking who is actually being protected here.

That said, the recent attention to false accusations is not entirely unwelcome, because it forces clarity about what good process actually looks like. Belief and investigation are not opposed. A trauma-informed interviewer can take an account seriously, document it carefully, and still gather evidence that may corroborate or complicate it. A hearing panel can presume innocence as a legal matter without treating the complainant as a liar. These are professional skills, and the central failure of campus systems is that they are run by amateurs.

The reforms that would help everyone are unglamorous: trained investigators, written findings, consistent timelines, written procedures available to both parties, the right to see the evidence, the right to an adviser, and standards that distinguish coercion from regret. None of this requires choosing sides in the culture war that has formed around the Rolling Stone story.

The danger of the current moment is that one bad story will be used to justify rolling back protections that were hard won and remain inadequate. Survivors and the accused both deserve professionalism. They are not getting it now, and pretending the problem is bias in one direction or the other will not produce it.

The framing of "support for survivors" versus "fairness to the accused" makes for a tidy debate, but it misses how these cases actually unfold. The hardest campus rape cases are not the ones where a stranger drags a woman into the bushes—those are rare, and when they happen, everyone agrees on what should occur. The hard cases involve two people who knew each other, who had been drinking, who may have had prior sexual contact, and who give sharply different accounts of what consent looked like that night. In those cases, the question is not whether to "believe" anyone in the abstract. It is how to make a decision under genuine uncertainty.

This is where the preponderance standard, properly applied, is actually defensible. We use it in every other civil context where serious interests are at stake—employment discrimination, custody, professional discipline. The objection is not really to the standard but to the surrounding procedures: who decides, what evidence they see, whether both sides can test it. A 50.01% standard applied by trained adjudicators with full information is very different from the same standard applied by an ad hoc panel working from a one-sided file.

So the reforms that matter are procedural rather than substantive. Both parties should have access to the same evidence. Both should be able to submit questions to be put to the other (direct cross-examination is traumatizing and often counterproductive, but written questions through a panel work). Both should have an adviser who can actually advise. Findings should be written and reasoned, so that appeals mean something. Decisions should be made by people who do this work regularly, not by rotating committees of faculty.

Where I part ways with some reformers is on the proposal to push everything into the criminal system. That sounds principled, but it ignores why campus processes exist in the first place: the criminal system has, for generations, refused to take these cases seriously. Prosecutors decline them; juries acquit. A woman whose assailant cannot be convicted beyond a reasonable doubt may still have a legitimate claim that he should not live across the hall from her. Civil-style adjudication of campus conduct is not a substitute for criminal justice; it is a parallel system addressing a different question—not whether someone goes to prison, but whether they get to remain in a particular community.

Done well, that system can serve both parties. Done as it is now, in too many places, it serves neither. The Rolling Stone affair should focus attention on the procedures, not on whether to believe women in general. The question is always about a specific case.

In the bitter tug-of-war between advocates for victims and defenders of the accused, one possibility is almost never discussed: what if the entire adversarial framework is the problem? Both criminal trials and campus disciplinary panels are designed to produce winners and losers. One party is declared a liar or a predator; the other is vindicated or shattered.
