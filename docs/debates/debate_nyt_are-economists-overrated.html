One in 100 articles in The New York Times over the past few years have used the term “economist,” a much greater rate than other academic professions, according to a recent article in The Upshot. Economic analysis and pronouncements are crucial to most policy decisions and debates.

But given the profession’s poor track record in forecasting and planning, and the continued struggles of many Americans, have we given economists too much authority?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The premise of the question conflates two very different things economists do: forecasting and analysis. Economists are indeed bad at forecasting—few predicted the 2008 crisis, and the IMF's recent admission that it badly underestimated fiscal multipliers during the eurozone austerity push is a humbling case in point. But forecasting macroeconomic turning points may simply be impossible in the way predicting earthquakes is impossible. Judging the profession by that standard is like dismissing seismology because it can't tell you when the next quake will hit.

What economists do well, and what justifies their outsized presence in policy debates, is causal analysis of specific interventions. We know more than we did a generation ago about the employment effects of minimum wage increases (thanks to Card and Krueger and the natural-experiment revolution), about how health insurance affects outcomes (the Oregon Medicaid study), about the returns to education, about how cash transfers change behavior in developing countries, and about the design of auctions, kidney exchanges, and school-matching systems. This is real, accumulated knowledge, and policymakers ignore it at their peril.

The deeper worry isn't that economists are consulted too often but that the wrong economists are consulted, or that their tentative findings get laundered into confident pronouncements by journalists and politicians. Macroeconomic punditry—where ideological priors most contaminate the work—gets the airtime, while careful microeconomic empiricism does not. When an op-ed cites "economists say," it is rarely citing the median finding of a literature; it is citing whoever returned the reporter's call.

So the answer is: economists qua forecasters and macro-pundits are overrated; economists qua empirical analysts of specific policies are arguably underused. The fix isn't to demote the profession but to be more discriminating about which claims warrant deference. A humble economist saying "we ran the experiment and here's what happened" deserves more authority than a confident one telling you where GDP will be in 2017.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The sheer volume of economists in public discourse says little about their actual usefulness. One in a hundred articles in The New York Times might mention them, but that frequency reflects a modern ritual—invoking a credentialed seer to bless or curse policy ideas—rather than a track record of clairvoyance. The painful truth is that economists are overrated, not because their analytical tools are worthless, but because the authority we hand them far exceeds their ability to deliver reliable foresight or to capture the lived experience of ordinary people.

Start with forecasting, the most visible product of the profession. The failure to anticipate the 2008 financial crisis is only the most spectacular example. Mainstream macro models, with their assumptions of rational expectations and smoothly functioning markets, largely missed the housing bubble, the systemic risk in shadow banking, and the depth of the coming recession. As the Queen of England famously asked during a 2008 visit to the London School of Economics, “Why did nobody notice it?” The candid answer—that economists had become too confident in elegant mathematical fantasies—haunts the field. Even outside crises, the record of consensus forecasts for growth, inflation, and unemployment is strewn with large and persistent errors. Yet media coverage continues to treat every Federal Reserve projection or IMF outlook as news, rarely acknowledging how often those numbers are revised.

The authority problem extends beyond prediction into policy design. For decades, a particular brand of economics—emphasizing efficiency, free trade, deregulation, and the idea that a rising tide lifts all boats—has dominated Washington and global institutions. The result has been policies that, while boosting aggregate GDP, have often widened inequality and left entire communities behind. Many Americans’ continued struggles, from stagnant wages to job insecurity, are partly a consequence of taking economists’ aggregate welfare functions too literally and ignoring distributional costs. The profession has itself begun to reckon with this: Thomas Piketty’s recent work jolted the discipline by showing how capital accumulation can leave workers behind, a reality that too many policy economists had downplayed. Yet the dominant voices in public debate still routinely dismiss concerns about inequality or deindustrialization as unsophisticated protectionism, revealing a thin understanding of social disruption.

This is not to say economists are useless. Rigorous empirical work has improved antitrust enforcement, environmental policy, and poverty alleviation. But the appropriate role for the economist is as one voice among many—offering data, clarifying trade-offs, and testing assumptions—not as a priestly caste whose models validate preordained policy choices. When a single profession’s jargon crowds out historical, sociological, and moral reasoning, we all lose.

The outsized presence of economists in public discourse is a symptom of how we currently approach governance: we crave certainty in a deeply uncertain world. By applying complex mathematical models to human behavior, economists offer the comforting illusion that public policy can be managed as an exact science. However, treating economists as modern-day oracles has indeed led us astray, largely because we expect them to perform tasks their discipline is ill-equipped to handle.

The primary indictment against the economics profession is its catastrophic failure to foresee the 2008 financial crisis. This blind spot was not a minor oversight but a fundamental failure of macroeconomic models that assumed rational actors and perfectly efficient markets. When we ask economists to act as forecasters, we inevitably set ourselves up for disappointment. The global economy is a complex, chaotic system driven by human psychology, political turbulence, and unforeseen shocks. Models that rely on historical data to predict the future often fall apart the moment human panic or irrational exuberance takes hold.

Furthermore, elevating economists to the role of ultimate arbiters in policy debates risks disguising moral and political choices as mere technical calculations. Whether debating the expansion of the Affordable Care Act, the stagnation of middle-class wages, or the implementation of a higher minimum wage, economic analysis can tell us about potential costs and trade-offs. It cannot, however, tell us what kind of society we ought to build. That is a democratic imperative, not a mathematical one.

Yet, it would be a mistake to dismiss the profession entirely. While economists make terrible fortune tellers, they are indispensable accountants of cause and effect. The analytical frameworks they provide—concepts like opportunity cost, moral hazard, and systemic incentives—are vital for crafting functional legislation and avoiding unintended consequences.

Ultimately, economists are only overrated if we expect them to possess a crystal ball or if we let their models override our democratic values. If we demote them from prophets to advisors, utilizing their tools to clarify trade-offs while acknowledging the limits of their foresight, their outsized voice in the public square remains justified.

Economists are overrated when they are treated as seers, but underrated when they are treated as mere pundits. The problem is not that economic analysis has too much influence; it is that a narrow kind of economic authority is too often mistaken for neutral, comprehensive wisdom.

The profession plainly earned some of the skepticism it now faces. Many leading economists failed to anticipate the financial crisis, underestimated systemic risk, or placed too much confidence in elegant models that assumed away panic, fraud, leverage and institutional fragility. Before 2008, the “Great Moderation” encouraged a complacent belief that central banking and modern finance had tamed the business cycle. After the crash, economists also disagreed sharply over stimulus, austerity, monetary policy and the causes of slow recovery. To citizens facing stagnant wages, underwater mortgages or long-term unemployment, expert debate could look less like science than like dueling ideologies with equations attached.

But forecasting failure does not make economics useless. Meteorologists miss storms; doctors disagree on treatments; neither fact implies that weather maps or clinical trials should be ignored. Economists have helped clarify real trade-offs in taxation, health care, antitrust, unemployment insurance, education, environmental policy and monetary policy. Ideas like incentives, opportunity cost, externalities and marginal analysis are indispensable. It matters, for example, whether a carbon policy uses prices, whether a minimum wage increase is large or modest, whether a mortgage subsidy mainly helps buyers or sellers, and whether austerity during a slump worsens unemployment. Good economics can puncture wishful thinking.

The danger comes when economists are asked questions their discipline cannot answer alone. “Will this policy raise GDP?” is not the same as “Is this policy just?” A cost-benefit analysis may tell us how to price risk, but not whose risk counts most. Measures like GDP can rise while insecurity, inequality or community decline worsen. Distribution is not a side issue; it is often the issue. Yet public debate sometimes gives the most airtime to economists who speak as if efficiency settles moral and democratic questions.

So yes, economists have been given too much authority when they function as high priests of inevitability: globalization requires this, markets demand that, the model proves there is no alternative. They deserve less deference and more interrogation. But public life needs more serious economics, not less: more attention to evidence, uncertainty, institutions and distribution; fewer confident predictions; and more humility about where economics ends and politics begins.

The prominence of economists in public life reflects not merely their own ambitions but the genuine complexity of the issues we face. When we debate stimulus versus austerity, trade agreements, minimum wages, or financial regulation, we are fundamentally asking how incentives shape behavior, how markets distribute resources, and where government can improve on market outcomes. Economists bring trained frameworks for thinking about these questions that most journalists, politicians, and citizens lack. This expertise is real, even if it is often overextended.

The criticism that economists have a "poor track record in forecasting" deserves serious attention. The profession largely failed to anticipate the 2008 financial crisis despite years of apparent warning signs. Individual predictions about unemployment, growth, and inflation frequently prove wrong. These failures matter. When policymakers act on forecasts that don't materialize, real people bear the consequences. The continued struggles of many Americans— stagnant wages, persistent unemployment in certain regions, widening inequality— suggest that economic policy has not delivered on its promises. It is reasonable to ask whether we have placed too much faith in experts whose models failed to capture crucial dynamics.

Yet we should be careful not to throw out useful analysis because it is imperfect. Economic thinking has also shaped valuable reforms: antitrust enforcement, environmental regulation through market mechanisms, social insurance programs designed with behavioral insights, and microeconomic analysis of how people actually respond to incentives. The problem is rarely economics itself but rather the hubris of those who mistake theoretical elegance for predictive accuracy or who forget that markets are embedded in social and political institutions.

The appropriate response is not to sideline economists but to deploy them more wisely. Forecasts should come with honest uncertainty estimates. Models should be tested against historical evidence. And policymakers should remember that economics illuminates tradeoffs but cannot resolve political disagreements about values. Respect for the profession need not mean surrender to its pronouncements.

Yes, Economists Are Overrated — And Dangerously So

The Times's own data tells the story: economists appear in its pages at rates that dwarf sociologists, historians, anthropologists, or political scientists combined. This would be defensible if the profession had earned that privilege through predictive accuracy or policy success. It has not.

Consider the record. In 2007, the IMF's World Economic Outlook forecast tranquil global growth into the indefinite future. The Federal Reserve under Ben Bernanke famously dismissed subprime concerns as "contained." Queen Elizabeth's now-legendary 2008 question to LSE economists — "Why did no one see it coming?" — still hasn't received a satisfying answer. And once the crisis hit, mainstream economists split bitterly over austerity versus stimulus, with the Reinhart-Rogoff debt threshold paper — cited by deficit hawks across continents — turning out to contain an Excel error. That isn't a minor embarrassment; it is a window into how thin the empirical foundations of confidently dispensed policy advice often are.

The deeper problem is that economists trade on the prestige of mathematical rigor while smuggling in contestable value judgments. A "Pareto improvement" sounds technical; a recommendation to liberalize trade, deregulate labor markets, or means-test a program is political. By dressing political claims in equations, the profession claims a neutrality it does not possess. Sociologists and historians who study the same questions — inequality, unemployment, financial instability — are not granted the same deference, despite often having sharper diagnoses.

This matters because authority distorts deliberation. When a CBO score or a DSGE model lands in a policy debate, it tends to close down argument rather than open it. Other forms of knowledge — ethnographic, historical, moral — get crowded out. Six years into a recovery that has left wages stagnant and labor force participation depressed, the Americans who continue to struggle have good reason to wonder whether the technocrats narrating their economy actually understand it.

None of this means economics should be banished from public life. Careful empirical work — Raj Chetty on mobility, the Card-Krueger tradition on minimum wages, the randomistas in development — has genuinely advanced understanding. But the discipline's status in journalism and policymaking is wildly out of proportion to its track record. A healthy public conversation would treat economists as one expert voice among many, subject to the same skepticism we apply to any group that combines high confidence with modest results. Right now, we don't.

No — If Anything, Good Economists Are Underused

The complaint that economists are "overrated" tends to conflate two different things: the prominence of economic language in public discourse, and the actual influence of trained economists on policy. The first is high; the second is often surprisingly low, and where it is low, we tend to pay the price.

Start with what economists are actually good at. Microeconomics — the study of how incentives, prices, and institutions shape behavior — has a strong empirical track record. Auction theorists designed the spectrum auctions that raised tens of billions for the U.S. Treasury. Market designers like Al Roth built the kidney exchange that saves lives every year. Development economists running randomized trials have transformed what we know about deworming, microcredit, and conditional cash transfers. Labor economists' careful work on the minimum wage, immigration, and education has reshaped debates that used to run on pure ideology. None of this is overrated; much of it is barely known to the general public.

Even on the macro side, the caricature of cluelessness is unfair. Yes, almost no one forecast the precise timing of the 2008 crisis — but forecasting recessions is genuinely close to impossible, and the same critics who mock economists for missing it would mock them harder for crying wolf. What economists did do, once the crisis hit, was draw on Bernanke's own scholarship on the Great Depression to prevent a repeat. Compare the U.S. recovery, imperfect as it is, with the eurozone's austerity-driven double dip — where economic advice was overridden by political imperatives. The lesson there is not that economists failed; it is that they were ignored.

The deeper point is that the alternative to economic analysis is not no analysis. It is folk economics: zero-sum thinking about trade, intuitions that minimum wages can't possibly cost jobs or can't possibly fail to, conspiracy theories about the Fed, beliefs that deficits are always catastrophic or never matter. Politicians and pundits will fill the vacuum with worse reasoning if trained economists step back.

The right critique isn't that economists have too much authority but that the wrong kind of economist — the cable-news macro pundit with strong priors and weak humility — gets the microphone, while the patient empirical worker does not. Fix the selection problem in public discourse, and the answer to the headline question becomes obvious: we should be listening to more economists, not fewer.

It Depends Which Economists, and For What

The question "are economists overrated?" is too blunt to answer well. The profession is not monolithic. A development economist running a randomized trial in Kenya, a macroeconomist building a DSGE model at a central bank, a financial economist calibrating risk at a hedge fund, and a think-tank pundit writing op-eds about the deficit are all called "economists." Lumping them together produces a debate that goes nowhere.

Where economists deserve their influence: well-identified microeconomic empirical work. The credibility revolution of the past two decades — natural experiments, regression discontinuities, randomized trials — has produced reliable knowledge about questions that matter: what schools work, how taxes affect labor supply, whether health insurance improves health, how cash transfers affect poverty. This is real science, slow and cumulative, and policy is better when it draws on it.

Where economists deserve much less deference: macroeconomic forecasting and grand structural pronouncements. The dismal record of predicting recessions, the public quarrels over multipliers and austerity, the Reinhart-Rogoff fiasco, and the inability of standard models even to incorporate financial intermediation before 2008 all point to a field whose confidence has consistently outrun its evidence. When a senator cites a macroeconomist's forecast that a tax cut will pay for itself, we are not in the realm of science.

Where economists deserve outright skepticism: when they leave their lane. Economic imperialism — applying price-theoretic reasoning to marriage, crime, religion, addiction — has produced genuine insights but also a lot of overreach. The world has problems that aren't optimization problems, and the assumption that everything reduces to incentives misses much of what sociologists, historians, and psychologists understand about institutions, culture, and power.

The Times statistic the prompt mentions — one in 100 articles citing an economist — actually reflects this mixed picture. Some of those citations are exactly right: an economist explaining tax incidence or auction design. Some are misuse: a macro forecaster treated as an oracle. Some are crowd-out: an economist asked about questions that historians or political scientists could answer better.

So: overrated in some venues, properly rated in others, underrated in a few. The reform that actually matters isn't taking economists down a peg in general. It's developing better public literacy — among journalists especially — about which economic claims are well-grounded and which are educated guesses dressed up in math.

Don't Blame Economists; Blame the People Quoting Them

The premise of this debate quietly shifts responsibility. If economists appear in one of every hundred Times articles and shape policy decisions across Washington, who decided that? Not, mostly, the economists themselves. Reporters chose them as sources. Editors elevated their quotes. Politicians and staffers solicited their testimony. The profession's public footprint is largely a creation of journalism and politics, not of academic economists hustling for influence.

This matters because the failures the prompt invokes — bad forecasts, sluggish recoveries, persistent inequality — are not really failures of academic economics so much as failures of how a complicated discipline gets translated into public discourse. Academic economists publish papers full of caveats, confidence intervals, identification assumptions, and acknowledged limitations. By the time their work reaches a Times article, the caveats are gone and the point estimate has become a headline. By the time it reaches a senator's speech, the point estimate has become a slogan. Whose fault is that?

Consider macroeconomic forecasting, the standard exhibit for the prosecution. Serious academic macroeconomists will tell you that recessions are nearly impossible to predict, that forecasts more than a couple of quarters out are barely better than naive extrapolation, and that policy models have wide error bands. None of this stops cable news from asking them to predict next year's GDP, or stops viewers from treating the answers as authoritative. The misuse is the public's; the embarrassment somehow accrues to the discipline.

Or consider the 2008 crisis. The economists who saw the housing bubble — Robert Shiller, Dean Baker, Raghuram Rajan — were ignored not by their profession but by politicians and journalists who preferred a comforting consensus. When the system blew up, the same political class blamed economists collectively for a misreading that better economists had warned against.

The healthy reform here is not to demote economists. It is to demand better economic journalism — reporters who understand the difference between a well-identified causal estimate and a back-of-the-envelope projection, and who are willing to ask hard methodological questions instead of just collecting authoritative-sounding quotes. It is also to expect more of policymakers, who should be able to engage with evidence rather than shopping for the economist whose conclusion they already wanted.

Economists aren't overrated. They're over-quoted by people who don't read past the abstract.

The Real Problem Is Economic Imperialism

Yes, economists are overrated — but not for the usual reasons. The standard indictment focuses on bad forecasts and the 2008 crisis. The deeper issue is that economics has colonized intellectual territory that belongs to other disciplines, and our public conversation has shrunk as a result.

Look at how policy questions get framed today. Should we expand pre-K? The debate centers on rate-of-return estimates from Heckman-style studies. Should we reform criminal justice? Out come the cost-benefit calculations of incarceration. Should we let in more immigrants? Cue the labor-market wage effect estimates. Climate change? Discount rates and integrated assessment models. In each case, questions that are fundamentally about values, history, power, and the kind of society we want to live in have been recoded as optimization problems with technical answers.

This is what scholars have called economic imperialism, and it has been astonishingly successful. Gary Becker extended price theory to families, crime, and discrimination. Freakonomics turned the move into a brand. Today, a political scientist who wants to be taken seriously runs regressions; a sociologist who wants policy influence learns to speak in elasticities. The other social sciences haven't disappeared, but they have been demoted in public esteem, and with them have gone modes of analysis — historical, interpretive, ethnographic, normative — that we badly need.

The cost shows up in our public deliberation. Inequality, for instance, was for decades almost invisible in mainstream economic discussion because the dominant models treated distribution as either solved or uninteresting. It took Piketty — significantly, a historically-minded economist working with long-run data — to drag it back to the center. The Americans the prompt mentions, the ones who continue to struggle, were largely missing from elite economic discourse not because economists are stupid but because the questions that would have made them visible weren't the questions the profession was asking.

So when the Times quotes economists in one of every hundred articles, the loss isn't just that some of those quotes are wrong. It's that ninety-nine other framings — moral, political, historical, sociological — got squeezed out. The fix isn't to silence economists. It's to remember that they answer a particular kind of question with particular tools, and that most of what matters about how we live together lies outside their domain. Until we relearn that, yes: economists are wildly overrated.

The charge that economists are overrated is itself overrated. It rests on a fundamental confusion between the job of an economist and the job of a fortune teller. No economist worth her salt claims to predict the precise date of the next recession or the closing level of the Dow. The financial crisis did not discredit economics. The field is more self-critical and empirically grounded today than ever before. We need better economics, not less of it. We are having the wrong conversation. Asking whether economists are overrated is like asking whether doctors are overrated in the era before germ theory. The answer is that a vital craft is in the midst of a painful but exhilarating transformation, and we should be cheering it on, not writing its obituary. The models were filled with hyper-rational agents who never made cognitive errors, possessed perfect information, and inhabited markets that miraculously cleared. The revolution in behavioral economics, led by figures like Kahneman and Thaler, has reintroduced actual human psychology into the core of the discipline. Policymakers face choices about taxation, regulation, trade, and monetary policy that have quantifiable effects on millions of lives. Someone needs to analyze those trade-offs with rigor. The alternatives—purely political calculations, intuition-based decision-making, or ignoring economic evidence entirely—have proven far more catastrophic. The Great Depression, stagflation of the 1970s, and the Asian financial crisis all involved periods when economic expertise was ignored or overridden with disastrous results.

Critics point to 2008 as evidence of failure, but this misreads history. Many economists warned about systemic risk and housing bubbles beforehand. The models were wrong in specifics, but they correctly identified the danger. Meanwhile, the profession's response—unconventional monetary policy, targeted fiscal stimulus—likely prevented a depression far worse than what actually occurred. Would critics prefer we had relied on pure ideological instinct?

The frequency of economist citations in the Times reflects not blind deference but recognition that economic analysis is indispensable to understanding modern governance. Yes, the profession has limitations. Models are simplifications; human behavior introduces uncertainty; complex systems resist precise prediction. But these are not reasons to marginalize economic expertise—they're reasons to use it wisely alongside other forms of knowledge. Dismissing economists wholesale because they don't possess crystal balls would be the real error.

The evidence is damning: economists failed to predict the worst financial crisis in 80 years, offered wildly divergent forecasts about stimulus effectiveness, cannot agree on basic questions about minimum wages or trade, and have presided over decades of stagnant median wages while their theories justified policies that enriched the top percentile. Yet we defer to them on everything from interest rates to healthcare to education policy. This is not a minor professional shortcoming—it's a profound epistemic failure that has real human consequences.

Consider the track record since the crisis. Economists told us austerity was necessary; it wasn't. They told us the unemployment rate would fall faster with aggressive QE; it didn't fall as predicted. They told us trade would lift all boats; it didn't. They told us financial innovation made the system safer; it didn't. At what point does accumulated failure become disqualifying?

The problem runs deeper than prediction. Mainstream economics built its authority on claims of scientific rigor—mathematical models, statistical analysis, equilibrium concepts—while producing theories that systematically ignore power, institutions, history, and psychology. When your framework can't explain why 1% of Americans hold 40% of wealth, or why identical policies produce different results in different countries, your framework is failing you, not just your predictions.

Media outlets bear responsibility too. The ritual of consulting economists for instant analysis—"what does this jobs report mean?"—creates an illusion of precision that the profession itself rarely claims. Journalists elevate a narrow band of credentialed voices while ignoring heterodox economists who sometimes saw crises coming or questioned dominant models. Institutional capture has created an echo chamber.

Americans are right to be skeptical. Economic expertise should inform policy debates, not dominate them. Democratic deliberation about values and priorities deserves equal weight with technical analysis. The profession's influence should be proportional to its demonstrated accuracy—and by that measure, it's been vastly overweighted for too long.

The question of economist authority is really a question about what we expect from expertise in a democracy—and whether we've conflated complexity with mystique. Economics is a young science grappling with extraordinarily difficult subject matter. Judging it too harshly by forecasting standards, or too deferentially by credentialing standards, both miss the mark.

The honest answer is that economic expertise is indispensable but systematically misused. Economists offer crucial analytical tools: cost-benefit reasoning, incentive analysis, general equilibrium thinking, empirical methods for identifying causal effects. Graduate training genuinely equips people to think rigorously about trade-offs that non-economists often overlook. This has real value.

The problem emerges when this expertise is translated into policy pronouncements beyond its legitimate reach. Economics can tell us the mechanical effects of a tax change on employment or output; it cannot tell us whether those effects matter more than distributional values we might hold. It can model incentive structures; it cannot adjudicate between competing visions of human flourishing. Yet the public role of economists often blurs this distinction, implying that economic science has answers to fundamentally political questions.

There's also the "expert problem" that afflicts many fields. Specialized knowledge creates asymmetric credibility. Economists often speak with authority their actual expertise doesn't support—offering predictions as if they were analyses, or generalizing from models that make unrealistic assumptions. The profession is beginning to grapple with reproducibility issues and model limitations, but public communication lags far behind.

The solution isn't to dismiss economic insight but to contextualize it. Economic analysis should be one input among others—alongside ethical reasoning, historical perspective, democratic input, and lived experience. When economists are positioned as neutral technocrats rather than advocates with methodological priors, everyone loses. Their real contribution lies in disciplined analysis of trade-offs, not oracle-like pronouncements on what policy should be.

The economist question reveals something structural about how modern governance operates rather than something inherent about the discipline's value. We need to examine not just whether economists are right, but why they've become so prominent and who benefits from that prominence.

Post-Cold War, economics became the legitimating discourse for a particular vision of governance: market-friendly, trade-open, financially deregulated, central bank independent, fiscal conservative. This wasn't an accident—it reflected the ideological preferences of those who set policy agendas. Economists provided the technical veneer that made politically contested choices appear scientifically necessary. "There is no alternative" got dressed up in supply and demand curves.

The 2008 crisis disrupted but didn't dismantle this arrangement. Emergency measures borrowed heavily from heterodox thinking, but the subsequent regulatory response remained captured by financial sector interests using economic arguments to resist meaningful change. Economists who questioned deregulation before 2008 found themselves marginalized; those who validated it kept their platforms.

The media amplifies this dynamic. "Economist says" carries authority regardless of the economist's actual credentials, track record, or potential conflicts of interest. Financial industry economists, think tank fellows, and academic ideologues get equal billing with scholars who might offer more skeptical perspectives. The result is a distorted public conversation that presents contested economic theories as settled science.

What's needed is not fewer economists but different economics—more attention to institutional variation, historical specificity, power dynamics, and the limits of optimization frameworks. And it needs different politics: governance that treats economic expertise as one input among many rather than a trump card. The profession's overrated status isn't about individual incompetence so much as systemic misuse of technical authority to foreclose democratic debate about values that are fundamentally contested.

Looking at the economist question historically helps clarify what's at stake. Two centuries ago, "political economy" was understood as a branch of moral philosophy—concerned with the good society, not just market mechanics. Economists like John Stuart Mill or Alfred Marshall saw their work as part of broader philosophical inquiry. The profession's narrowing into technical modeling came later, partly in pursuit of scientific status that would lend authority to its prescriptions.

This transformation created genuine gains: rigorous empirical methods, sophisticated theoretical frameworks, better data analysis. But it also created losses—particularly the severing of economic analysis from ethical and political context. Modern economists often speak as if efficiency were the only value that matters, or as if positive analysis naturally implies normative conclusions.

We're now seeing the consequences. The profession's technical authority has been deployed to depoliticize choices that are fundamentally political—who gets what, whose interests matter, how we balance security against liberty, growth against sustainability. Economic expertise provides a veneer of objectivity to what are actually contested value judgments.

The proper remedy involves recognizing economics as what it actually is: one form of inquiry among many, valuable for certain purposes but neither comprehensive nor final. This doesn't mean ignoring economic analysis; it means situating it correctly. Economists can illuminate trade-offs and identify second-order effects. They cannot resolve disagreements about what kind of society we want.

What's required is intellectual humility from the profession and better calibration from the public. Economists should speak as experts offering analysis, not as scientists revealing necessary truths. The public should demand that economic expertise be deployed democratically rather than technocratically—as informing deliberation rather than overriding it. The current moment offers an opportunity for this recalibration, if we have the institutional creativity to seize it.
