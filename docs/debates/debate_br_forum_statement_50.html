Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Tom Hayden's reflection is generous, lyrical, and — like the document it honors — admirably free of bitterness about the distance between aspirations and outcomes. Reading him in the spring of 2012, after a winter that saw Zuccotti Park cleared, the Wisconsin recall fight grind into its electoral phase, and the killing of Trayvon Martin spark a new wave of marches, I find his catalog of present-day "spirits of the '60s" moving. But I also find it too quick. The Port Huron Statement's call for participatory democracy is not so much timeless as perennially unfinished, and the unfinished parts are precisely the ones that the past six months have most painfully exposed.

Start with Occupy, since Hayden cites it as the freshest evidence of the Statement's afterlife. The general assemblies in lower Manhattan, in Oakland, in dozens of other cities, were as faithful an attempt to enact "decision-making aimed at achieving consensus" as American politics has seen in a generation. They produced something real — a shift in the national conversation about inequality that the Democratic Party did not produce in three years of crisis. They also produced, by late autumn, a familiar set of pathologies: meetings consumed by procedural disputes, blocks deployed by tiny minorities, an inability to articulate demands precisely because doing so would have required exclusions a consensus body could not bear to make. I say this as a sympathizer. The honest lesson is not that Occupy "embodied" participatory democracy in some pure form Port Huron foresaw, but that participatory democracy, when stretched to govern a heterogeneous, open-ended movement, hits limits the Statement gestured past. Hayden mentions consensus in a single clause and moves on. The clause deserves an essay of its own.

Then there is the capaciousness problem. Hayden writes that "liberals, radicals, libertarians, populists, Marxists, feminists, anarchists, progressive entrepreneurs, poets, musicians, unions, parties, non-governmental organizations, and even conservatives (sometimes)" can fit under the tent of participatory democracy. This is meant warmly, but it is also where the concept threatens to dissolve. The Tea Party, after all, is a participatory mass movement. Its activists show up at town halls, run for school boards, run primary challenges, organize on the ground, and use new media to bypass mainstream outlets. They invoke "We the People" with no less fervor than Occupy invokes "the 99 percent." If participatory democracy is just citizens energetically participating, then it describes a procedural value to which the right has at least as plausible a claim as the left right now. Port Huron meant something thicker — a substantive politics of equality, of economic redistribution, of dismantling racial caste — but the thickness came from those commitments, not from "participation" per se. Hayden's framing risks letting the procedure carry weight the substance should bear.

The inside-outside formulation has the same problem, and the 2008 Obama campaign is a strange example to invoke in its favor. Hayden calls it "a moving upswell of commitment," and so it was. But what happened to that upswell? Organizing for America was folded into the DNC and largely demobilized. The grassroots energy that elected Obama was not deployed to push him on health care, on financial reform, on immigration, or on the foreign wars where his early supporters expected the sharpest break. The countermovement Hayden warns about did not emerge after victories were consolidated; it emerged in the vacuum the demobilized base left behind, and it took the form of the Tea Party. If the inside-outside strategy means anything operational, it has to include some account of how outside pressure is sustained against an inside ally who needs to be pushed. The Obama years so far have been an object lesson in how hard that is, and Hayden's brief paragraph does not reckon with it.

I would also press Hayden on the Statement itself, which he treats with a light touch on its limitations. The Port Huron Statement was written largely by white, largely male students at a moment when SNCC was already in the field doing more difficult and more dangerous work, and when the women in SDS were beginning to articulate the critique that would explode by mid-decade. The Statement's account of race is thin, its account of gender is essentially absent, and its vision of "the university" presumes an undergraduate experience that was not available to most Americans of color in 1962. Hayden acknowledges this in passing — "it was written before the modern women's movement" — but the gap is more than chronological. Participatory democracy as the early SDS practiced it had to be forced open, sometimes painfully, by people the original authors had not centered. Carl Wittman's gay liberation manifesto is one beautiful example; the women's caucus walkouts of 1965–67 are a harder one. The Statement's legacy is inseparable from the people who corrected it.

A word, too, on the Internet. Hayden writes that "the Internet and social media now promise the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history." By April 2012 we have enough evidence — from Tahrir Square to Tehran's Green Movement to the role of Twitter in Occupy's coordination — to take that promise seriously. We also have enough evidence to be cautious. The same platforms enable surveillance by states and employers, the commodification of political attention, and the substitution of expressive gestures for organizational commitment. Wael Ghonim's Facebook page helped bring people into the streets, but the discipline that followed came from labor unions, the Muslim Brotherhood, and neighborhood committees with deep prior roots. The medium amplifies; it does not organize.

None of this is meant to dismiss Hayden's argument. The deepest sentence in his essay is the last one: that participatory democracy is "the political expression of the universal human need to be involved in the joint-creation of our community." That is the right way to honor Port Huron — as a statement of a need, not a recipe. The harder work, fifty years on, is to ask which institutions can actually carry that need: which workplaces, which schools, which unions, which parties, which platforms, which kinds of property, which kinds of citizenship. Hayden's spirits of the '60s are real, but spirits cannot vote, cannot bargain, and cannot govern. The next fifty years will be about the bodies that house them.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Tom Hayden’s evocative fifty-year retrospective on the Port Huron Statement captures something genuine about the rhythms of American dissent. The sight of young undocumented students risking deportation, the mass marches for immigrant rights, the pepper-sprayed protesters on university quads—these do echo the moral urgency of the early 1960s. And there is no question that the language of participatory democracy, first articulated by a handful of students in a Michigan union hall, has become a touchstone for movements that its drafters could scarcely have imagined. Yet for all its emotional resonance, Hayden’s account reads more like a celebration of a spirit than a reckoning with a political strategy, and that distinction matters enormously. If the goal is not merely to honor the past but to give the present generation a usable framework, we need to ask harder questions about what has changed in the terrain on which participatory democracy must now operate.

The single greatest difference between 1962 and 2012 is the nature of the power that movements confront. The Port Huron Statement was drafted in an era of ascendant welfare-state liberalism, a time when the federal government was widely seen as a potential vehicle for progressive change—a view reinforced by the civil rights victories that came through federal courts and legislation. Today’s activists face a political economy shaped by four decades of neoliberal restructuring, financialization, and the hollowing out of democratic institutions. The state that Tom Hayden and his fellow students wanted to make more participatory has itself been substantially captured by concentrated wealth. The Citizens United decision in 2010, which opened the floodgates to unlimited corporate political spending, merely codified a long-standing reality: that the formal channels of representative democracy are increasingly unresponsive to popular majorities. When Hayden points to the initiative, referendum, and recall in Wisconsin as examples of participatory tools, he neglects to note that the Wisconsin uprising of 2011, for all its impressive grassroots energy, was triggered by a governor who rode a corporate-funded wave into office and who has not been stopped by recall petitions alone. The recall effort against Scott Walker is even now uncertain; the institutional deck is stacked.

This shift in the structure of power exposes a central ambiguity in the concept of participatory democracy itself. Hayden is right that the term has broad appeal—anarchists, populists, progressive entrepreneurs, and even some conservatives can rally around it. But that very breadth is also a weakness. A Tea Party activist occupying a town hall meeting in 2009 to protest the Affordable Care Act was practicing a form of direct action and demanding a more responsive government, yet the policy outcome they sought was the opposite of what most New Left veterans envisioned. Participatory democracy does not contain an inherent ideological valence; it is a method, not a program. The Port Huron Statement, in its original form, rooted its call for participation in a critique of both corporate capitalism and the Cold War garrison state, a synthesis that gave it moral clarity. Today’s movements often lack that clarity, substituting a generalized demand for inclusion for a substantive analysis of the economic and military structures that distort democratic life.

The celebration of the Internet and social media as the “greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history” likewise deserves more skepticism than Hayden offers. In the fourteen months since the Arab Spring erupted, we have seen both the power of digitally coordinated protest and its profound limits. Social media can help assemble crowds in Tahrir Square or Zuccotti Park, but it is far less effective at the grinding, long-term work of building organizational capacity, negotiating with entrenched interests, or crafting legislative alternatives. Moreover, the same platforms that activists use are owned by massive corporations that profit from surveillance and data extraction; the SOPA/PIPA protests in January 2012 demonstrated that online mobilization can sometimes block bad legislation, but the power of Silicon Valley to shape policy in its own interest remains largely unchecked. The public sphere that the Internet creates is fragmented, algorithmically curated, and susceptible to misinformation. It is not a modern equivalent of the participatory town meeting; it is a new kind of public square, one whose architecture is not under democratic control.

There is also an uncomfortable gap between the inside-outside strategy that Hayden praises and the record of the last four years. He cites the “upswell of commitment” to Barack Obama in 2008 as an example of participatory democracy manifesting in the electoral arena. Yet many of the same young people and African-Americans who poured their hopes into that campaign have spent the time since watching a presidency that, on issues from drone strikes to Wall Street accountability to the public option, has governed far more conventionally than they expected. The movement energy that helped elect Obama was not so much integrated into a sustained participatory structure as it was demobilized after the election, leaving the field open to the very corporate and national-security interests the Port Huron Statement once condemned. This is not a failure of spirit but a reflection of how the inside-outside dynamic actually works in an age of professionalized politics: the energy of the margins is absorbed and neutralized by the center, producing symbolic victories and incremental reforms that rarely alter the basic distribution of power. Hayden himself acknowledges that “the establishment embraces participatory democracy’s reforms when pragmatic representatives realize that it is better to concede on the issues than allow the popular surge to become more radical,” but he seems to assume that this cycle is a kind of two-step forward, one-step back progression. What the Occupy movement’s trajectory suggests is that the forces of containment are now so sophisticated that they can preempt genuine transformation, offering a media spectacle of concession while leaving the underlying structures untouched.

None of this is an argument to abandon participatory democracy as an ideal. The instinct to demand a say in the decisions that shape one’s life is, as Hayden says, a universal human need, and the movements he describes are filled with courage and moral witness. But if the Port Huron Statement’s fiftieth anniversary is to be more than an occasion for nostalgic admiration, it must also prompt a candid assessment of why the vision has so often stalled. Part of that assessment must involve reviving the kind of structural analysis that the original statement contained—an analysis that named the economic system, the military-industrial complex, and the racial hierarchy as intertwined obstacles. Another part must involve building organizational forms that can sustain movements beyond the adrenaline rush of street protests and viral videos. Participatory democracy cannot live by consensus process and social media alone; it needs durable institutions, a willingness to engage with the messy work of policy, and a strategy for when the countermovement pushes back. The spirit of the ’60s is real, but spirits gain power only when they become flesh.

Tom Hayden is right to recognize the echoes of the Port Huron Statement in the political uprisings of our current moment. Arriving at its fiftieth anniversary, the Statement’s rallying cry for “participatory democracy” feels remarkably prescient. From the student encampments of Occupy Wall Street to the historic protests in Madison, Wisconsin, and the sprawling digital networks that facilitated the Arab Spring, the desire of ordinary people to claim a direct voice in the decisions that shape their lives has reemerged with thrilling force. Yet, while Hayden beautifully captures the moral energy linking the 1960s to today, his optimism glosses over the severe practical limitations of participatory democracy as we are witnessing it practiced right now.

Hayden celebrates the tactics of direct action and consensus-based decision-making. If we look closely at the Occupy movement, however, we see both the inspirational highs and the exhausting lows of this model. The General Assemblies in Zuccotti Park and across the country were marvels of horizontal organization, utilizing the "human microphone" to ensure every voice was heard. But this strict adherence to absolute consensus frequently paralyzed the movement. When every participant effectively holds veto power, formulating specific, actionable political demands becomes nearly impossible. Activists today are relearning the hard lessons articulated in the 1970s by feminist scholar Jo Freeman in "The Tyranny of Structurelessness": when formal leadership is abolished, informal and unaccountable hierarchies inevitably take its place. As the winter of 2011 set in and the encampments were cleared, Occupy struggled to transition from a powerful symbolic protest into a durable political force. Participatory democracy excels at naming a crisis, but it often falters when tasked with the messy, bureaucratic work of governing and legislating.

This brings us to Hayden’s invocation of the “inside-outside” strategy. He argues that participatory democracy relies on both mass moral energy on the outside and courageous politicians on the inside. Historically, he is correct; the civil rights movement utilized direct action to force the hand of institutional powers, culminating in landmark legislation. Today, however, the left’s outside movements suffer from a profound, perhaps fatal, cynicism regarding the "inside." Many Occupy activists explicitly reject electoral politics, viewing the entire legislative apparatus as irreparably corrupted by corporate money.

By contrast, look at the Tea Party movement. While ideologically opposed to Hayden’s progressive vision, they successfully weaponized grassroots anger by seamlessly integrating it with an inside strategy, primarying establishment Republicans and reshaping Congress in 2010. If today’s progressive movements hope to achieve the lasting impact of the 1960s civil rights or environmental movements, they cannot rely on horizontal assemblies alone; they must eventually engage with the formal levers of representative power that they currently despise.

Furthermore, Hayden’s assertion that the internet and social media “promise the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history” requires a heavy dose of skepticism. It is true that platforms like Facebook and Twitter have drastically lowered the barriers to mobilization. The speed with which protests can now be organized is unprecedented. However, mobilization is not the same as deep, sustained democratic participation.

The digital sphere often fosters what critics have dubbed "slacktivism"—superficial engagement that provides the emotional satisfaction of political action without the requisite risk or sacrifice. The recent, viral explosion of the "Kony 2012" campaign last month perfectly illustrates this phenomenon: tens of millions of views and clicks, translating into practically zero real-world institutional change. Real participatory democracy requires the slow, tedious building of trust and relationships across deep differences. The architecture of social media, which monetizes outrage and creates echo chambers, is often antithetical to the kind of coalition-building Hayden envisions.

The Port Huron Statement remains a vital historical document because it articulated a fundamental human yearning: the desire not to be treated as mere cogs in a university machine, a corporate economy, or a geopolitical chess game. Hayden’s vision of a society where individuals jointly create their communities is as compelling today as it was in 1962. But to honor that legacy today requires more than just celebrating the resurgence of street protests. It demands that we critically examine the structural weaknesses of structurelessness, bridge the widening chasm between grassroots activists and electoral politics, and recognize that a retweet is no substitute for the hard, unglamorous work of institutional reform.

Tom Hayden is right that the Port Huron Statement still speaks with unusual freshness. Its language is earnest in a way that can embarrass our more ironic age, but its central complaint remains familiar: ordinary people are asked to live with decisions made far from them, by institutions that treat them as clients, consumers, targets, or data points rather than as citizens. Fifty years later, the “loneliness, estrangement, isolation” named by Students for a Democratic Society has not disappeared. It has been reorganized by debt, precarious work, gated expertise, permanent campaigning, and screens that connect us constantly while often leaving us politically powerless.

But if participatory democracy is to be more than a beautiful inheritance, we need to be clearer about both its promise and its limits. Hayden presents it as a broad democratic horizon capable of joining immigrant youth, climate activists, anti-tuition protesters, Occupy Wall Street, unions, civil libertarians, and reform-minded politicians. That is partly true. Yet participation by itself does not settle the hardest questions: who participates, under what conditions, with what resources, through which institutions, and to what binding effect? Without answers, participatory democracy can become a mood rather than a politics.

The Port Huron Statement’s enduring contribution was to reject the cramped politics of spectatorship. It insisted that democracy was not exhausted by voting every few years, not reducible to interest-group bargaining, and not safe in the hands of technocrats who claimed to know better than the people affected by their decisions. That critique has only gained force. The Iraq War, financial deregulation, the mortgage crisis, mass incarceration, climate inaction, and the expanding national-security state have all revealed, in different ways, how insulated decision makers can impose enormous costs on people who had little meaningful say.

The recent financial crisis is perhaps the clearest example. Millions lost homes and jobs after a set of practices—subprime lending, securitization, reckless leverage, rating-agency failure—flourished in a world most citizens could barely see, much less govern. The public was later told that the same institutions that had helped create the disaster were too large and too interconnected to be allowed to fail. This was the opposite of participatory democracy: risk socialized after profit had been privatized, with democratic consent arriving late, confused, and under duress. Occupy’s great achievement was to make that arrangement morally visible. “We are the 99 percent” was not a policy program, but it was a democratic intervention of real power.

Still, Occupy also illustrates the problem. Its assemblies, hand signals, working groups, and refusal of conventional leadership embodied the participatory impulse in a remarkably disciplined way. At its best, Occupy created spaces in which people long treated as politically irrelevant—students, the unemployed, debtors, homeless people, precarious workers—could speak in their own names. But the movement’s very commitment to horizontalism sometimes made it difficult to decide, negotiate, prioritize, or be accountable. Consensus can protect minorities from being steamrolled; it can also empower the most patient, the most available, the most verbally confident, or the most obstructionist. Informal hierarchy does not disappear because formal hierarchy is renounced. Jo Freeman’s old warning about the “tyranny of structurelessness” remains pertinent.

This is not an argument against participatory democracy. It is an argument for structures that make participation real rather than symbolic or exhausting. People working two jobs, raising children, caring for parents, or navigating immigration courts cannot attend endless meetings. The affluent and educated are usually better positioned to “participate” unless institutions are deliberately designed to counteract inequality. A neighborhood forum, a public hearing, or an online comment process may look democratic while amplifying the voices of those who already possess time, confidence, and connections. Democracy requires participation, but participation requires material supports: leisure, education, health, transportation, childcare, legal protection, and freedom from retaliation at work.

That is why unions, whatever their flaws, remain indispensable democratic institutions. A union meeting is not a utopia, and union bureaucracies can become rigid or self-protective. But collective bargaining gives ordinary people some power over the place where many spend the largest part of their waking lives. It also trains citizens in argument, representation, compromise, and solidarity. The attack on public-sector unions in Wisconsin is therefore not only a budget fight. It is a fight over whether working people will retain institutions through which they can act collectively. The recall campaign now underway in Wisconsin shows one face of participatory democracy; the deeper question is whether democratic participation will survive inside the workplace as well as at the ballot box.

Hayden is also right to connect participatory democracy with the initiative, referendum, and recall. Yet here we should be cautious. Direct democracy has a progressive history, but it has also been used to impose tax revolts, restrict minority rights, and lock in fiscal decisions that legislatures then struggle to reconcile. California’s Proposition 13 did not deepen democracy simply because voters approved it; it helped cripple public finance and empowered a politics of permanent scarcity. Proposition 8 showed that majority participation can threaten equal citizenship. A democratic politics worthy of the name must combine popular power with constitutional protections, deliberation, and a commitment to those who are outnumbered or stigmatized.

The same caution applies to the Internet. Hayden describes social media as promising the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history. Perhaps. Certainly digital tools have lowered the cost of communication, allowed rapid mobilization, exposed police violence, circulated independent journalism, and helped movements from Cairo to Madison to Zuccotti Park find one another. Undocumented students, antiwar veterans, climate activists, and debt resisters can now tell their stories without waiting for permission from network television or metropolitan newspapers.

But the Internet is not inherently democratic. It is also a marketplace of attention dominated by private platforms; a surveillance infrastructure; a rumor mill; a device for sorting citizens into self-confirming publics; and a tempting substitute for organization. Clicking, forwarding, and posting can support movements, but they do not automatically build durable power. The same tools that help protesters assemble help authorities monitor them. The same networks that spread democratic testimony can spread paranoia, scapegoating, and cruelty. Participatory democracy will not be delivered by technology. It will depend on who owns the platforms, who controls the data, and whether online association can be translated into institutions capable of contesting power.

The experience of the Obama campaign is instructive. In 2008, millions of people, especially young people and African-Americans, felt that electoral politics had opened into something larger than a campaign. The small-donor fundraising, house meetings, volunteer networks, and disciplined hopefulness of that year were genuine democratic energies. But once the campaign became an administration, much of that energy was demobilized or repurposed into support for decisions made elsewhere. The health-care fight, the stimulus, financial reform, Afghanistan, deportations, and civil liberties all revealed the distance between movement participation and governing power. It is not enough to elect leaders who have benefited from participatory enthusiasm. Movements need independence from the politicians they help elect.

Here the “inside-outside” strategy Hayden endorses is essential, but it must be genuinely two-sided. Without inside allies, movements can be ignored, repressed, or translated into harmless symbolism. Without outside pressure, inside allies become isolated or absorbed. The civil rights movement needed federal legislation and court orders, but those would not have come without sit-ins, freedom rides, local organizing, jail-going, boycotts, and the willingness of ordinary people to face terror. The labor movement needed the Wagner Act, but the Wagner Act would have meant little without strikes and shop-floor organization. The disability rights movement needed statutes, but Hayden rightly reminds us that the Americans with Disabilities Act also rested on direct action by people who refused invisibility.

Participatory democracy therefore needs a theory of power. Moral witness matters, but power decides whether witness changes law, budgets, ownership, and administrative routines. The original Port Huron Statement sometimes spoke as if authentic dialogue could overcome domination. Dialogue is necessary, but not sufficient. Segregationists did not yield because they were invited into better conversation. Employers do not surrender unilateral control because workers express themselves eloquently. Fossil-fuel interests will not abandon profitable reserves because citizens deliberate sincerely about the atmosphere. Participation must be linked to leverage: strikes, votes, litigation, boycotts, civil disobedience, public financing, regulatory authority, and alternative institutions.

The next democratic project should take Port Huron’s moral aspiration and give it institutional muscle. That means automatic or same-day voter registration, protection against voter suppression, public financing of elections, and an end to partisan manipulation of election rules. It means reversing the logic of Citizens United by preventing concentrated wealth from drowning out citizens’ voices. It means stronger unions and new forms of worker representation in sectors where traditional collective bargaining has withered. It means participatory budgeting in cities, community control over policing with real authority, open government data that citizens can use, and universities governed less like corporations and more like public trusts. It means immigration reform that brings millions out of civic darkness, because democracy cannot thrive with a large population living and working in fear.

It also means cultivating democratic habits. Participatory democracy is not merely a mechanism; it is a discipline. It asks people to listen, lose votes, revise opinions, share credit, accept responsibility, and distinguish between compromise and betrayal. Movements often romanticize spontaneity, but democracy requires preparation. The Freedom Rides looked spontaneous to some observers, but they depended on training, organization, legal strategy, and extraordinary courage. The same is true today. A durable movement for climate justice, immigrant rights, economic equality, or peace will need not only indignation but schools, media, leadership development, research, alliances, and memory.

At fifty, the Port Huron Statement should not be treated as scripture. Its blind spots were real, and many of the movements that later transformed American life had to push beyond the assumptions of the young men and women who gathered in 1962. Feminism, gay liberation, Black Power, Chicano and American Indian activism, environmentalism, and disability rights all enlarged the meaning of democracy in ways Port Huron only partly anticipated. Yet that is a point in its favor. A democratic manifesto should invite its own revision.

Hayden’s deepest claim is that people create themselves through the joint creation of their communities. That remains a radical proposition in a society that tells us we are mainly consumers, competitors, debtors, or spectators. The task now is to make that proposition practical. We should celebrate the spirit of participation wherever it appears—in the streets, online, in recall drives, in occupations, in classrooms, in workplaces, in campaigns. But we should also ask the harder question that follows every awakening: how will this energy become power, and how will that power remain democratic?

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Tom Hayden's anniversary meditation on the Port Huron Statement is, in its own way, a testament to the very participatory democracy he extols. Here is a founding member of Students for a Democratic Society, now in his seventies, still reaching outward to connect contemporary movements with an older vision. That continuity of commitment is admirable. But I think the essay understates some of the tensions that make the relationship between the Port Huron legacy and today's activism more complicated than Hayden allows.

Hayden is right that the language of participatory democracy has proven remarkably generative. The Arab Spring uprisings, the Wisconsin protests against the rollback of collective bargaining rights, the Occupy movement's General Assemblies—all drew explicitly or implicitly on the idea that ordinary people should participate directly in decisions that shape their lives. The Dream Act activists Hayden mentions have indeed demonstrated the same willingness to risk consequences for collective goods that characterized civil rights sit-ins and antiwar protests. These connections are real and worth celebrating.

Yet Hayden's framing tends to flatten important distinctions among the movements he invokes. The Port Huron Statement emphasized a critique of concentrated power in both state and corporate hands—a critique that, crucially, did not treat the two as equivalent threats. "The American economic and political system," the Statement declared, "will not now be reconstructed by those whose concern is to patch out-dated institutions." This was a radical statement in 1962, and its implications remain contested. By 2012, the relationship between Occupy and the Democratic Party, between protest and electoral politics, between movements for economic justice and those focused on other concerns, remained deeply unresolved. Hayden himself acknowledges that "the establishment embraces participatory democracy's reforms when pragmatic representatives realize that it is better to concede on the issues." But if this pattern is structural rather than accidental—if inclusion in the system always risks defusing radical energy—then the path from margin to mainstream is less a tide rising than a series of strategic compromises that partially transform both the movements and the institutions they seek to influence.

Hayden's claim that participatory democracy can unite "liberals, radicals, libertarians, populists, Marxists, feminists, anarchists, progressive entrepreneurs, poets, musicians, unions, parties, non-governmental organizations, and even conservatives" understates how differently these groups understand what participation means. For some, it means local assemblies and consensus-based decision-making. For others, it means strengthening regulatory agencies to check corporate power. For still others, it means reducing the size of government itself. These are not merely tactical disagreements about means; they reflect fundamental disagreements about ends and about where legitimate authority resides. The language of participatory democracy is capacious enough to accommodate all of these, but that very capaciousness risks becoming empty rhetoric that everyone can claim but no one need define.

There is also the question of scale. The Port Huron era's participatory structures—community organizing, local newspapers, neighborhood meetings—operated at a human scale that allowed genuine face-to-face deliberation. The Internet and social media that Hayden celebrates as "the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history" have enabled new forms of coordination, but also new forms of fragmentation, distraction, and superficial engagement. The question of whether digital tools genuinely deepen democratic participation or merely simulate it remains genuinely open. We have seen, in the months since Occupy began, both the power of networked coordination and the difficulty of translating online energy into durable offline institutions.

Finally, Hayden's essay sidesteps a challenge that the Port Huron Statement itself acknowledged: the tension between participation and effectiveness. Participatory processes tend to be time-consuming and difficult to scale. Movements that succeed in mobilizing large numbers of people often find that the demands of maintaining participation drain energy from other goals. The Statement itself was a product of a small, relatively homogeneous group of activists. Replicating that experience at larger scale, or in more heterogeneous communities, requires not just rhetorical appeal but institutional innovation that remains largely unrealized.

The Port Huron Statement's vision endures because it names a real human aspiration. But honoring that vision requires acknowledging the obstacles it has faced and continues to face. The movements Hayden celebrates are hopeful signs, not because they fulfill the Port Huron vision but because they keep working toward it in conditions the Statement's authors could not have anticipated.

I write as someone who disagrees with most of what Tom Hayden has fought for over the last half century. What was admirable was the seriousness. Read alongside the campus manifestos that have come after it, the Port Huron Statement is striking for its earnestness, its evident reading, and its willingness to talk about virtue and character. The famous opening line—"We are people of this generation, bred in at least modest comfort, housed now in universities, looking uncomfortably to the world we inherit"—is not the cant of grievance. It is the moral self-examination of young people who suspect that they have been formed by institutions whose ends they do not endorse. A conservative ought to recognize this gesture, because it is fundamentally a Burkean one: the recognition that we are creatures of the orders that have made us, and that the question of how to act within and upon those orders is the great question of an adult life.

Where the Statement went wrong, in my view, and where Hayden's essay continues to go wrong, is in its assumption that "participatory democracy" names a clear good rather than a deeply ambiguous procedure that can serve many masters. Consider that Hayden lists, among the expressions of participatory democracy he admires, "progressive tools of referendum, recall, and initiative." Set aside the fact that these were originally Progressive Era reforms championed by good-government types whom the New Left would otherwise have despised as managerial liberals. Note instead that the most consequential recent uses of those tools have produced California's Proposition 13, term limits, bans on affirmative action, and the recall of Gray Davis—outcomes that, whatever their merits, did not flow from the political sensibility Hayden represents. Direct democracy is a knife that cuts every way. It produced Brown v. Board, and it produced Proposition 8. It dethroned a sitting Wisconsin Supreme Court justice over collective bargaining politics, and it has been used to entrench tax limitations that have starved the states Hayden cares about.

If you genuinely believe in participatory democracy, you have to be prepared for the participating public to want things you abhor. Hayden's essay does not really reckon with this. His examples are all cases in which "the people," properly mobilized, want what the author wants. But of course the Tea Party is also a form of participatory democracy. The pro-life movement, which has built one of the most impressive grassroots organizing infrastructures in modern American politics, is participatory democracy. The Mormon volunteers who flew to California to door-knock for Proposition 8 were practicing participatory democracy. So were the parents who showed up at school board meetings to challenge the textbooks chosen by their children's teachers. Hayden's vision is procedurally neutral but substantively partisan, and the gap between the two has only widened since 1962.

The deeper conservative concern is that "participatory democracy," as the New Left understood it, was less a political theory than a therapeutic one. The Port Huron Statement repeatedly invokes "alienation," "loneliness," "estrangement," and presents participation as a remedy for these existential conditions. Hayden's closing sentence is unmistakably in this vein: participation as "the political expression of the universal human need to be involved in the joint-creation of our community and, through that collective activity, the creation of ourselves." This is not really a claim about how to govern a continental republic of three hundred million people. It is a claim about how to be a person. And while I have nothing against personal flourishing, I would gently submit that treating politics as a venue for self-creation is precisely how politics becomes both totalizing and unserious. The young person who needs to find herself in a general assembly is in for some disappointment when the general assembly turns out to be full of other young people who need to find themselves. Meanwhile the sewer system needs maintenance, the schools need to teach reading, and somebody has to make the trains run.

The genius of the American constitutional order—a genius that the Port Huron Statement consistently failed to appreciate—is that it presupposes neither civic transcendence nor universal participation. It assumes that citizens will be partially attentive, mostly self-interested, and frequently wrong, and it designs institutions that can produce passable government anyway. James Madison, not Tom Hayden, is the relevant guide here. Madison did not need every citizen to be a constant participant in the joint-creation of the community. He needed enough of them, often enough, to throw the bums out. That is a more modest, and a more achievable, ideal.

I will end where I began, with some grudging respect. The Port Huron Statement was, whatever its errors, a serious effort by serious young people to think their way into adult responsibility. They believed they were inheriting a country whose institutions needed honest scrutiny, and they were not wrong about that. They were not wrong, either, that civic life had thinned, that mass media had infantilized, that the academy had become servile, that the military-industrial complex (Eisenhower's phrase, not theirs) was a genuine threat to republican government. A conservative could endorse much of that diagnosis.

Where conservatives and the New Left parted ways, and must continue to part ways, is over the question of what mediating institutions are for. Hayden's tradition wanted to dissolve the established institutions—church, family, hierarchical labor union, professional association, traditional party—in favor of direct, face-to-face, consensus-driven participation. Conservatives believe that those mediating institutions are exactly what makes free self-government possible, because they discipline participation, transmit memory, and protect the citizen against both the state and the mob. Fifty years on, with mediating institutions hollowed out and politics increasingly direct, plebiscitary, and televised, I would suggest that the conservative reading has worn rather better than the New Left's. Hayden remembers the wheelchairs in the federal building. I remember the Rotary, the parish council, the Knights of Columbus, and the Elks Lodge, and I notice that they are mostly empty now. The country is the worse for it.

This is not a small point. Hayden himself, to his enduring credit, has always acknowledged the influence of SNCC on his thinking. He drove to Albany, Georgia. He was beaten in McComb. He listened. Participatory democracy, as a phrase, came to Hayden from Arnold Kaufman, his teacher at Michigan. But as a practice, it came from Ella Baker. It was Baker who insisted, against Martin Luther King Jr. and the SCLC ministerial establishment, that the Raleigh students should form their own organization rather than being absorbed into an existing one. It was Baker who taught that strong people do not need strong leaders, who modeled group-centered leadership against leader-centered groups, and who built a politics around the idea that ordinary black people in the rural South could analyze their own conditions and act on them. SNCC's practice of long meetings, consensus seeking, and decentralized direct action was not borrowed from anyone north of the Mason-Dixon. It came out of the black freedom struggle, drawing on the prophetic tradition, on Highlander Folk School pedagogy, and on the hard practical wisdom of organizers like Baker, Septima Clark, and Bob Moses.

To say this is not to begrudge Port Huron its honors. The Statement is a real document, and the white students who gathered at the UAW retreat in Michigan were genuinely brave and genuinely thoughtful. But to read Hayden's essay you would not necessarily know that there had been a year of sit-ins, a year of freedom rides, a year of jail-no-bail, all before a single delegate arrived at Port Huron. You would not necessarily know that the Statement's most resonant phrase had a longer, blacker genealogy than the document itself acknowledges. You would not necessarily know that SNCC field organizers in Mississippi in 1964 were practicing a participatory democracy more radical, more dangerous, and more substantively transformative than anything the white New Left ever managed in the North.

I dwell on this because the question of credit shapes the question of inheritance. If we tell the story of the Sixties as a story radiating outward from SDS, we will look for our successors in the campus movement, in Occupy, in the comfortable spaces where mostly-white college students rediscover horizontalism every few years. If we tell the story as one radiating outward from SNCC—and from the broader black freedom struggle of which SNCC was one expression—we will look elsewhere. We will look at the Dream Act youth, yes, but we will also look at the National Domestic Workers Alliance, at the Coalition of Immokalee Workers, at the formerly incarcerated organizing against the carceral state, at the Movement for Black Lives that is even now stirring on social media in the wake of Trayvon Martin's killing in Florida six weeks ago.

Hayden does mention the Dream Act youth. But the analytic apparatus of his essay—participatory democracy as a "framework" that can include "liberals, radicals, libertarians, populists, Marxists, feminists, anarchists, progressive entrepreneurs, poets, musicians"—is curiously color-blind. The framework as he describes it is everyone's and therefore no one's in particular. The actual lineage I am tracing, by contrast, is rooted in specific communities engaged in specific struggles, and that rootedness is what made it powerful. Ella Baker's participatory democracy was not a general framework. It was a specific pedagogy for specific people facing specific dangers.

There is also a hard question about what participatory democracy has done and not done for black America in the decades since. If you measure by the metrics that matter—the racial wealth gap, school resegregation, the mass incarceration of black men, the persistence of housing segregation, the gutting of the Voting Rights Act preclearance regime that the Supreme Court is preparing to revisit—the post-Sixties record is grim. Participation has flowered in some respects (we have a black president, after all, an unimaginable fact fifty years ago) and withered in others (black voter suppression is more sophisticated now than it was when Bob Moses walked into the McComb courthouse). The Sixties was not won. Large parts of it were lost, and other parts were repealed.

Hayden's "inside-outside strategy" is the right name for what has to be done. But the inside has been very hard for black activism to occupy on stable terms, and the outside is being criminalized in ways that the white left has been slow to recognize. The wheelchairs in the federal building secured the ADA. The black bodies in the streets of Sanford, Florida, demanding justice for Trayvon Martin, are being met with a different kind of attention.

So: let us by all means honor Port Huron. Let us also remember who was already doing the work, who taught the teachers, and whose freedom struggle is still unfinished business in the country we share. The Statement's fiftieth anniversary is a good occasion not just to celebrate participatory democracy as an ideal but to ask, soberly, who has had the chance to participate, who hasn't, and what we owe each other now. Ella Baker, who taught us most of what we know about how to organize, would want us to spend the day on that question rather than on commemorations.

The modern women's movement did not arrive after the Port Huron Statement by some accident of historical scheduling. It arrived, in part, because women in SDS, in SNCC, and in the broader New Left found that the participatory democracy they had been promised stopped at the kitchen door of the movement office. Casey Hayden—Tom's former wife—and Mary King wrote one of the foundational documents of second-wave feminism in 1965, the "Kind of Memo" to women in the peace and freedom movements, precisely because they had discovered that the men who talked about democracy did not really mean to share it. Stokely Carmichael's notorious quip about the position of women in SNCC ("prone") is the most famous artifact of this discovery, but it was hardly the most painful. The painful part was the daily reality: the long meetings in which women took notes and made coffee while men theorized, the assumption that movement leadership was male, the political analysis that treated "the worker" and "the citizen" as implicitly male categories whose female complements were domestic and secondary.

This is not ancient gossip. It bears directly on how we should evaluate Hayden's claim that participatory democracy is a unifying framework, available to all and disqualifying none. Participatory democracy is only as inclusive as the participants make it, and the New Left's participants—especially in its most heroic years—were notoriously bad at noticing whose voices were not in the room. Consciousness-raising, the most important political innovation of second-wave feminism, was in part a corrective to participatory democracy's blind spots: a deliberate method by which women created spaces where their experience could be named, theorized, and politicized without male mediation. CR was participatory in the deepest sense, but it had to be built outside the structures that called themselves participatory because those structures were not, in fact, hospitable to women's full participation.

I dwell on this not to indict the men of Port Huron, who were creatures of their moment and many of whom (including Hayden) have done important work since to support feminist causes. I dwell on it because the lesson of the New Left's gender failures is directly applicable to the movements Hayden celebrates in 2012. Occupy, for all its participatory rhetoric, was beset from its early weeks by reports of sexual assault inside the encampments, by general assemblies in which women were spoken over and dismissed, and by a stubborn tendency for the most-quoted "voices of Occupy" to turn out to be young white men. The Dream Act youth movement has been better, in part because it has been more aware—immigrant women organizers have insisted on it—that "undocumented" is not a gender-neutral condition. But the general lesson stands: procedural participation does not automatically deliver substantive equality, and the assumption that it will is itself a form of male innocence.

There is also a substantive question that the Port Huron Statement could not really ask, and that Hayden's anniversary essay still does not quite ask. What if "the political" is not coterminous with the spaces the Statement called political? What if much of the work of democracy actually happens in households, in care relationships, in the unwaged labor that reproduces the conditions under which "public" politics is possible? Feminist political theorists from Carole Pateman to Joan Tronto have argued that any serious account of democracy has to include the domestic sphere, the carework economy, and the gendered division of labor that makes possible the very leisure to engage in public life. The Port Huron Statement, like most political documents written by young men in 1962, treated the household as a given background condition rather than a contested political site. Hayden's essay does the same. It celebrates the manifold sites of participatory democracy—workplaces, neighborhoods, classrooms, the streets—without quite asking why those spaces have always been more available to some bodies than to others.

The reproductive rights movement is instructive here. It is one of the most successful participatory movements of the late twentieth century, in the sense that it converted private experience into public demand with extraordinary effectiveness. It is also under sustained, well-organized counterattack at this moment in 2012, with state legislatures passing waiting periods, ultrasound mandates, and clinic regulations designed to choke off access, and with a Supreme Court that may well take up new challenges to Roe before the decade is out. If we are looking for the front lines of contemporary democratic struggle, the fight over women's bodily autonomy is at least as important as anything happening in the campus or the encampment. Hayden's essay does not mention it. I think this is not malice but a kind of categorical blindness inherited from the document he is commemorating.

What would a Port Huron Statement written by women in 1962 have looked like? We cannot know, but we can imagine. It would probably have spent less time on the existential alienation of the comfortable student and more time on the alienation of the domestic worker, the secretary, the housewife with a graduate degree she was not permitted to use. It would have noticed that "the system" had specifically gendered features, and that the same Cold War liberalism that built the military-industrial complex also built the suburban nuclear household as a containment strategy. It would have asked who cleans up after participatory democracy goes home.

I do not say this as a rebuke to Hayden, who has earned the right to celebrate his document. I say it as a friendly amendment. Participatory democracy is a real and durable contribution. But the practice of participation does not automatically produce its preconditions. Those preconditions—care, recognition, the equal capacity to occupy public space without harassment or violence—have to be built deliberately, and the movement that built them, more than any other, was the women's movement that the Statement was written just before. Fifty years on, that movement's work is unfinished, and the framework Hayden offers will only be adequate to the present if it lets feminism finish writing the sentences that Port Huron left incomplete.

The Romance of Participation: A Liberal Skeptic Replies

I want to register a friendly dissent from Tom Hayden's celebration of participatory democracy. I share many of Hayden's commitments—on civil rights, on immigration, on climate, on the dangers of corporate power—but I think the framework he proposes for understanding political action is misleading at best and, at worst, actively counterproductive to the causes he and I both support.

Start with the empirical record. The two greatest legislative achievements of American liberalism in the last seventy-five years are the New Deal and the Great Society. Neither was a triumph of participatory democracy in any meaningful sense. The New Deal was the work of a charismatic president, a Democratic Congress with overwhelming majorities, and a corps of policy intellectuals (Frances Perkins, Harry Hopkins, Robert Wagner) who designed the welfare state from the top down. There were mass movements in the background—the CIO organizing drives, the unemployed councils, the bonus marchers—but those movements did not "participatorily" produce the Social Security Act. The Act was produced by experts working in legislative committee rooms and signed by a president whose political genius was as much theatrical as participatory.

The Great Society was similar. The Civil Rights Act and the Voting Rights Act were undeniably products of pressure from the civil rights movement, which was an extraordinary mobilization. But that movement succeeded because Lyndon Johnson, working the Senate with brutal effectiveness, found the votes. Medicare passed because Wilbur Mills decided to let it pass. The Elementary and Secondary Education Act passed because a former schoolteacher in the White House and a particular configuration of congressional committee chairs made it possible. None of these accomplishments is intelligible as a triumph of horizontalism. They are intelligible as triumphs of representative institutions, working under unusual pressure and with unusually skilled leadership, to enact substantive reforms.

Hayden's essay treats representative institutions almost as an embarrassment, a place where the real energy of participatory democracy occasionally arrives but where the action does not really happen. I would suggest the opposite: representative institutions are where almost all the action happens, where they work, and the question of how to make them work better is the central question of democratic politics. Hayden's "inside-outside strategy" formally acknowledges this—he thanks Bella Abzug and Russ Feingold, and rightly—but the energy of his essay is overwhelmingly on the "outside," and his examples of participatory democracy are overwhelmingly extra-electoral.

Here is the problem with that emphasis. American electoral participation, by international standards, is mediocre. We have low turnout in midterm and primary elections, high disengagement from local politics, and an off-year electorate that is significantly older, whiter, and more conservative than the country as a whole. The single most consequential thing that progressives could do to advance the causes Hayden cares about is to turn out more progressive voters in more elections at more levels of government. This is not glamorous work. It does not produce manifestos. It does not lend itself to anniversary essays. But it is, quite literally, the work that determines whether the country will have a livable climate policy, a functioning health system, and a Supreme Court that does not roll back civil rights for the next generation.

By contrast, much of what Hayden celebrates as participatory democracy looks, to a sympathetic outside observer, like organizing for organizing's sake. The Occupy general assemblies were extraordinary spectacles, but they did not produce a legislative program, a candidate, a slate, or a durable institutional residue. The encampments dispersed under police pressure, the activists scattered, and the immediate political legacy was a vocabulary—"the 1 percent," "the 99 percent"—that has been useful but is hardly transformative. Meanwhile, the Tea Party, which is in many ways Occupy's mirror image, made the choice to enter electoral politics directly. They primaried incumbent Republicans, took over state party committees, won seats in Congress, and remade one of the two major parties in their own image. They have been incomparably more politically effective than Occupy, and the asymmetry is precisely about their willingness to engage with representative institutions rather than to perform an alternative to them.

I am not arguing that mass mobilization is irrelevant. I am arguing that mass mobilization is a complement to representative politics, not a substitute for it, and that the Port Huron tradition has consistently tended to invert this relation. Participatory democracy, in its New Left inflection, has often been suspicious of leadership, suspicious of policy detail, suspicious of compromise, and suspicious of the institutions through which political change actually has to be channeled if it is to last. These are temperamental rather than analytical commitments, and they have repeatedly led good people into political dead ends.

There is also a deeper philosophical disagreement. Hayden ends his essay with the claim that participatory democracy is "the political expression of the universal human need to be involved in the joint-creation of our community and, through that collective activity, the creation of ourselves." This is a beautiful sentence, and it is also, I think, a category error. Politics is not primarily for self-creation. Politics is for the management of collective problems under conditions of disagreement, scarcity, and uncertainty. The expectation that politics will deliver existential meaning is one of the great pathologies of modern public life, and it produces both the mass movements of the right and the burnout cycles of the left. A healthy democratic citizen is one who participates without expecting that participation to redeem her life, and who recognizes that most of the time, most of politics is going to be boring, technical, and frustrating—and that's okay.

The Port Huron Statement is a beautiful document. I would not want a country in which young people did not write such documents. But I would also not want a country whose political theory was written by twenty-year-olds. The young Tom Hayden saw clearly what was wrong with the United States in 1962. He saw less clearly how to fix it. The mature Hayden of 2012, looking back, is gracious about the achievements of participatory democracy and largely silent about its limits. A liberal friend would say: the achievements are real, but the limits are exactly what we need to learn from now.

The Libertarian Case for Participation, Properly Understood

I am a libertarian, which means that in the political schema of Tom Hayden's essay I belong in the parenthetical list, somewhere between "progressive entrepreneurs" and "anarchists." Hayden is correct that libertarians can and should fit under a banner of participatory democracy, but only if "participatory democracy" is understood in a particular way that I suspect is not quite Hayden's way. I want to spend this response making that distinction, because I think there is a genuine convergence to be found between libertarian thought and the best of the Port Huron tradition, and a genuine divergence that is worth being honest about.

The convergence is real, and it begins with a shared diagnosis. The Port Huron Statement was, among other things, a protest against bigness: the big corporation, the big union (yes, the big union—reread the Statement, which is sharply critical of labor bureaucracy), the big university, the big military, the big political party. The Statement called for human-scale institutions in which actual persons could exercise meaningful agency rather than being reduced to inputs in a managed system. This diagnosis is, with adjustments, recognizably libertarian. F.A. Hayek, no friend of the New Left, made a parallel argument from a different starting point: that the great enemy of human flourishing is the concentration of power, whether in the state, the cartel, or the corporate-state nexus. Wilhelm Röpke, Robert Nisbet, even (in his way) Murray Rothbard could shake hands across a fairly wide ideological gap with the young Hayden on the proposition that mid-twentieth-century American institutions had grown too large, too remote, and too unaccountable.

So far, so good. Where libertarians and Port Huron part ways is over the question of what to do about it.

The New Left's instinct was to democratize the large institutions: take over the universities, take over the workplaces, take over the foreign policy apparatus, and run them all by consensus. The libertarian instinct is to shrink them: replace the large institutions with smaller, voluntary, exit-friendly alternatives. The difference is not trivial. To "democratize" General Motors means to give the assembled population—workers, consumers, environmental stakeholders, government appointees—a vote on how it is run. To replace General Motors with a network of smaller firms operating under genuine market discipline means to give every individual the power of exit, which is in many ways a more potent form of accountability than voice. Albert Hirschman's famous taxonomy, "Exit, Voice, and Loyalty," is the relevant guide here. The New Left was a voice tradition. Libertarians are an exit tradition. Both are forms of participation, properly understood, but they have very different institutional implications.

Hayden's essay slides between these without quite acknowledging the tension. He celebrates the underground press and independent media of the Sixties, which were genuinely participatory and which arose, as he says, because the mainstream press was indifferent. But he then celebrates "the Internet and social media" as the next great expansion of participatory democracy, as if these were continuous with the underground press. They are not. The underground press was a voluntary, distributed, plural ecology of small actors. The Internet, as we are encountering it in 2012, is rapidly consolidating into a handful of advertising-supported platforms—Facebook, Google, Twitter, Amazon—whose business model is the monetization of user data and whose governance is opaque and corporate. To call this an expansion of participatory democracy is half right and half disastrously wrong. Yes, more people can publish more things to more audiences than at any previous moment in history. No, this does not in itself constitute democratic empowerment, because the terms of participation are set unilaterally by the platforms, with no meaningful voice and limited exit.

The libertarian response to this concern is structural: we should be skeptical of any concentration of power, including private concentrations, and we should design our institutions and our regulatory regimes so as to preserve genuine competition, genuine alternatives, and genuine exit options. Antitrust enforcement, for the libertarian-of-the-Brandeisian-strain at least, is not an enemy of liberty but a precondition of it, because monopoly is itself a form of unaccountable power. I suspect Hayden would not disagree, but his framework, with its emphasis on participation through voice within existing institutions, does not naturally produce this analysis.

The deeper libertarian concern about the Port Huron tradition is its tendency to treat participation as intrinsically good regardless of what is being participated in. There are domains in which I do not want a participatory decision-making process. I do not want my neighbors voting on what books I read, what religious community I join, whom I marry, what I eat, what I say, what I sell, or what risks I take with my own body. The classical liberal tradition's great achievement was to carve out a private sphere of individual sovereignty that is protected against majority decision, however participatory. The Port Huron Statement was alert to some of these concerns—it was, after all, a document by people who had felt the boot of the McCarthyite consensus on their necks—but its successors have not always been. The contemporary impulse to politicize every domain of life, from the dinner table to the bedroom to the workplace, is not a friend of liberty even when it is conducted with impeccably participatory procedures.

So here is the libertarian's friendly amendment to Hayden's essay. Participation, yes. Self-government, yes. Decentralization, yes. Resistance to the unaccountable concentration of power, absolutely yes. But pair these with a robust commitment to the private sphere, to exit as a form of accountability at least as important as voice, and to the recognition that not all of life is or should be political. If "participatory democracy" can hold these commitments alongside its instincts toward voice and consensus, then count this libertarian among its supporters. If it cannot, then we will have to part ways, regretfully, somewhere short of the general assembly.

I suspect Hayden, who has lived through enough of the twentieth century to know what happens when politics colonizes everything, would not entirely disagree. The Port Huron Statement was wiser than many of its successors. It would be a fitting tribute, on its fiftieth anniversary, to recover the wisdom and discard the excesses. There is a coalition there, if we want one.

Don't Mistake the Internet for the Movement

Tom Hayden's essay contains one prediction that deserves more scrutiny than it has received. "The Internet and social media," he writes, "now promise the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history." This claim is now a commonplace; it appears in op-eds, in TED talks, in State Department speeches about Internet freedom, and in foundation-funded reports on the future of civic life. I want to dissent from it, not because the Internet is irrelevant to democracy—it is plainly transformative—but because the relation between the technology and the democratic ideal is much more ambiguous than the cheerful version allows.

Begin with the empirical record so far. The most celebrated recent demonstration of the Internet's democratic potential is the Arab Spring, in which Facebook and Twitter were said to have enabled the overthrow of autocratic regimes in Tunisia and Egypt. A year on from those events, the picture is considerably more complicated. Egypt is now governed by a military council that has shown every sign of intending to retain power, with the Muslim Brotherhood and Salafists having dominated the parliamentary elections that have actually been allowed to occur. Syria is in the middle of a brutal civil war. Libya is unstable. The networks that mobilized the Tahrir Square crowds did not produce institutions capable of governing the transition, and the regimes that initially fell have either reconstituted themselves or been replaced by forces no friendlier to liberal democracy. Whatever Facebook did, it was not a sufficient condition for democratic consolidation.

Closer to home, the empirical record is equally mixed. The same Internet that powered Howard Dean's insurgency in 2003-2004 and the Obama campaign's small-donor operation in 2008 has also powered the Tea Party's mobilization, the proliferation of climate-denial misinformation, the harassment campaigns that drive women and minorities off public platforms, and the ongoing concentration of advertising revenue away from local newspapers, leaving us with a public information environment thinner and more fragmented than the one we had a generation ago. Local journalism in the United States is in a state of free fall. The Internet did not cause all of this, but it has plainly accelerated it, and "participatory democracy" as a description of the resulting environment is at best half-true.

There are three distinct problems with the technological optimism Hayden voices, and they are worth disentangling.

First, the architecture problem. The Internet of 2012 is not the distributed, peer-to-peer network it was on the way to becoming a decade ago. It is increasingly an oligopoly of advertising-supported platforms—Facebook is preparing to go public next month at a valuation north of $100 billion—whose business model depends on capturing user attention and selling it to advertisers. This is not a neutral substrate for democratic participation. It is a particular kind of attention economy with specific biases: toward outrage over deliberation, toward the visceral over the rigorous, toward virality over verification. To call this an expansion of participatory democracy is to confuse participation with engagement, where engagement is a metric optimized by the platform for its own ends.

Second, the slacktivism problem. There is real research—Evgeny Morozov's recent book *The Net Delusion* is the most pointed example, though one need not endorse all of his views to take the empirical claim seriously—suggesting that low-cost online participation can substitute for, rather than catalyze, higher-cost offline participation. Signing an online petition feels like action; it is not the same as showing up. Sharing an angry article on Facebook feels like organizing; it is not the same as making a phone call to a wavering legislator's district office. In some accounts, the easy availability of expressive online action is actively eroding the muscle of more demanding civic engagement. This is hard to measure cleanly, but it is at least a real possibility, and it deserves more attention than it gets in cheerful "Internet equals democracy" essays.

Third, the surveillance problem. The Port Huron generation cared deeply, and rightly, about the FBI's COINTELPRO operations, the surveillance of dissident organizations, the infiltration of student groups, and the way state security agencies treated political dissent as a security threat. Anyone alive to that history should be deeply alarmed by what the post-9/11 surveillance apparatus, in collaboration with the major Internet platforms, has been quietly constructing. The 2008 FISA Amendments Act, the explosion of fusion centers since 2003, the surveillance contracts revealed in the Wikileaks "Spy Files" releases in late 2011, the ongoing construction of the NSA's massive data facility in Utah—these are signs of a state security architecture wrapped around exactly the platforms Hayden celebrates as democratic. The Internet may be an instrument of participation. It is also, demonstrably, an instrument of surveillance, and the same features that enable the first enable the second.

None of this is an argument that the Internet is bad for democracy. The Internet has plainly enabled remarkable democratic actions, from the Wisconsin uprising's livestreaming to the Occupy movement's distributed media production to the global organizing around climate change. I have used these tools myself; I am writing this response on one of them. The argument is that the technology is profoundly ambivalent, that its democratic potential is in active tension with its commercial and surveillance structures, and that the political work of making it actually serve democracy has barely begun.

What would that political work look like? Some of it is technical: support for distributed protocols, for end-to-end encryption, for open standards that resist platform lock-in. Some of it is regulatory: antitrust enforcement against the emerging Internet monopolies, real privacy law of the sort the European Union is beginning to attempt, transparency requirements for political advertising. Some of it is economic: public funding for journalism and civic infrastructure that the advertising-supported model is failing to provide. Some of it is cultural: education in critical media literacy, in the slow practices of verification and deliberation, in the recognition that an engaged citizen is not the same thing as an active user.

The Port Huron Statement was prescient about the dangers of corporate media in 1962. Its inheritors should be at least as prescient about the dangers of platform media in 2012. Hayden's essay treats the Internet as an obvious friend of his project. I think the truth is that the Internet is a contested terrain, and that the question of whether it will end up as an instrument of participatory democracy or as the most powerful surveillance and manipulation infrastructure in human history is genuinely open. We had better fight about it, because the platforms certainly are not going to give us the answer we want for free.

Scale, Time, and the Limits of Participation: A Political Scientist's Note

I have spent my professional life studying democratic institutions, and I want to offer a few observations on Tom Hayden's anniversary essay from that perspective. They are not intended to debunk the Port Huron Statement, which I admire, but to clarify what participatory democracy can and cannot reasonably be expected to do.

The first observation concerns scale. The participatory practices that Hayden's tradition celebrates—general assemblies, consensus decision-making, neighborhood councils, direct deliberation among affected parties—work tolerably well in groups of, roughly, the size of a hunter-gatherer band. They begin to strain at the size of a New England town meeting (somewhere between several hundred and a couple of thousand participants). They break down catastrophically at the size of a city, a state, or a nation. This is not a contingent fact about democratic culture; it is a consequence of basic communicative arithmetic. If every adult in the United States spoke for two minutes on a single national question, the discussion would take more than nine hundred years. Participation, in the rich sense Hayden means it, has to be exercised somewhere, and the question of where it can be meaningfully exercised at what scale of decision is the central question of democratic design.

The American constitutional system, for all its many sins, was designed with this scale problem in mind. Federalism distributes decision-making across multiple levels so that some questions can be settled locally (where participation can be meaningful) and others nationally (where it cannot, but where the consequences of decisions ramify across the whole country). Representation, the much-maligned solution to the scale problem, allows for indirect participation at scale by delegating to chosen agents, with mechanisms (elections, primaries, recall, lobbying, public hearings) for ongoing influence. None of this is as exciting as a general assembly, but all of it is necessary, and a serious theory of democratic participation has to engage with it rather than treat it as a fall from grace.

Hayden's essay actually gestures at this when he mentions referendum, recall, and initiative as expressions of participatory democracy at scale. These tools were pioneered by the Progressive Era reformers a century ago for exactly the reasons I have just described: they were attempts to inject elements of direct citizen decision into a system that was, by design, indirect. The record of these tools is genuinely mixed. Initiative and referendum have produced both progressive triumphs (state minimum wage increases, some environmental protections) and reactionary disasters (Proposition 13 in California, which has crippled state finances for thirty-five years; the wave of state same-sex marriage bans in the mid-2000s). The lesson is not that direct democracy is bad. The lesson is that direct democracy at scale has predictable pathologies—susceptibility to well-funded campaigns, low-information voting, the tyranny of slogans over deliberation—that have to be addressed institutionally if it is to work.

The second observation concerns time. Participatory politics, of the kind Hayden celebrates, runs on a particular temporal rhythm: mobilization, action, exhaustion, demobilization, sometimes followed by re-mobilization around a new issue. This is a real and powerful rhythm, and major historical changes have ridden it. But democratic governance has to happen on a different rhythm: the regular meeting of legislative bodies, the slow accretion of administrative rule, the continuous monitoring of executive action, the gradual interpretive work of courts. Participatory mobilization can punctuate this slower rhythm—and at its best, it does—but it cannot replace it. The civil rights movement could not, on its own, administer the Civil Rights Act once passed; that required a federal Department of Justice with a Civil Rights Division, an EEOC, a long roster of career civil servants, and an interpretive ecosystem of lawyers and judges. The movement's energy was necessary but not sufficient. The bureaucracy was necessary too. So was the law school. So was the regulatory state.

The Port Huron tradition has been notably uncomfortable with this second set of necessities. Bureaucracy is treated as a pathology, professionalism as a corruption, the regulatory state as an apparatus of distant elites. There is some truth to all of this—bureaucracies do calcify, professionals do develop guild interests, regulators do get captured. But the alternative to badly functioning institutions of administration is not no institutions of administration. It is well-functioning institutions of administration, which require sustained democratic attention to design, staffing, oversight, and reform. This is not the kind of work that calls forth Port Huron Statements. It is the kind of work that makes democratic government possible.

The third observation concerns the relation between intensity and breadth of participation. There is a real tension, well documented in the political science literature, between the depth of participation by the most engaged citizens and the breadth of participation by the citizenry as a whole. The kind of activists who show up to every neighborhood council meeting are not, demographically or attitudinally, representative of the population. They are older, wealthier, more educated, more ideologically extreme, and more likely to be homeowners than renters. The neighborhood council that empowers these citizens to make zoning decisions will reliably produce decisions that disempower renters, young people, and the working poor. This is not a hypothetical. It is the documented pattern in cities across the country, where progressive procedural participatory institutions reliably produce regressive substantive outcomes. The remedy is not to abandon participation; it is to design participatory institutions with deliberate attention to who is being excluded and why.

Hayden's essay does not really engage with any of these design questions. It treats participation as if its goodness were self-evident and its scaling unproblematic. A more useful tribute to the Port Huron Statement, I think, would treat the document as the opening of an institutional design question rather than the answer to one. How do you scale meaningful participation? How do you maintain it over time? How do you design participatory venues that do not systematically over-represent the already-empowered? How do you marry the energy of mobilization to the durability of administration? These are difficult questions and there are no general answers. But the questions are the questions, and democratic theorists from Madison through Tocqueville through Dewey through Robert Dahl have wrestled with them. The Port Huron Statement, for all its virtues, did not really wrestle with them. Its inheritors have to.

I close with a friendly note. Political scientists are not, generally, the people who write manifestos. We are the people who tell manifesto-writers why their proposals will not work as advertised. This is a tedious role and we are not, on the whole, popular at parties. But the work of making democracy actually function, at the scale and over the time scales that matter, requires both the manifesto-writers and the killjoys. Hayden has done his half. Let those of us in the other half do ours, in the spirit of the genuinely participatory conversation he himself recommends.
