Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The debate between balanced literacy and direct, explicit instruction tends to be framed as a clash of pedagogies, but the evidence accumulated over the past two decades points toward a more practical synthesis. The National Reading Panel's 2000 report, still the most comprehensive review we have, identified five pillars of effective reading instruction: phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. None of those pillars is optional, and the trouble with pure balanced literacy as practiced in many New York classrooms is that it tends to underweight the first two.

Lucy Calkins's workshop model, which Chancellor Fariña is reintroducing, has real virtues. Children write daily, choose books at their level, and confer with teachers about their thinking. For students who arrive at school already steeped in print, this looks wonderful. But for the roughly one-third of American children who struggle to crack the alphabetic code—disproportionately poor children and English learners—discovery-oriented approaches and "three-cueing" strategies that encourage guessing from pictures and context can entrench bad habits. Cognitive scientists like Mark Seidenberg and Stanislas Dehaene have shown that skilled reading is built on rapid, automatic decoding, and that decoding has to be taught, systematically, to most kids.

The phonics-versus-whole-language framing, however, obscures what may be the bigger problem: comprehension. Once children can decode, what holds them back is not a generic "comprehension skill" but knowledge. E.D. Hirsch has been making this argument for thirty years, and recent work by Daniel Willingham reinforces it: you cannot comprehend a passage about the Civil War without knowing something about the Civil War. The relentless focus on "strategies" like predicting and inferring, divorced from substantive content, leaves students—again, especially disadvantaged ones—unable to make sense of grade-level texts as they move into the upper elementary grades and beyond.

The right approach, then, is neither Calkins's workshop nor a return to drill-and-kill. It is systematic, explicit phonics instruction in the early grades, paired with a content-rich curriculum that builds vocabulary and background knowledge across history, science, and the arts. Independent reading and writing matter, but they belong on top of that foundation, not in place of it.

The debate over reading instruction often frames the choice as a stark one: joyful, student-led exploration versus rigid, skill-and-drill phonics. This is a false dichotomy that has, for too long, allowed a romantic vision of literacy to eclipse the realities of cognitive science. The balanced literacy programs making a resurgence in New York City promise the best of both worlds, but in practice, they frequently shortchange the very skills that make authentic reading possible—leaving too many children, particularly those from less language-rich homes, adrift.

By 2014, the evidence base is clear and voluminous. The National Reading Panel’s synthesis, replicated by subsequent research, demonstrates that explicit, systematic instruction in phonemic awareness and phonics is not an enemy of comprehension; it is its indispensable foundation. For the novice reader, the brain must forge new neural pathways, connecting visual symbols to the sounds of spoken language. This process does not happen by osmosis through immersion in quality literature alone. Skimming over letter-sound correspondences in a "teachable moment," as many balanced literacy models encourage, simply does not provide enough repetitive, structured practice for the majority of students to achieve automaticity. Without that automaticity, cognitive bandwidth is consumed by decoding, leaving little room for meaning-making.

Understanding the importance of background knowledge also complicates the romantic narrative. Comprehension is not an abstract skill that can be applied to any text once a child "learns to love reading." It is intricately tied to factual knowledge about the world. An approach that privileges student choice and minimizes direct instruction in history, science, and the arts inadvertently widens the knowledge gap between students who absorb such facts at home and those who do not. Those who advocate for a purely student-led approach sometimes mistake a child’s natural curiosity for a self-sufficient curriculum. A true commitment to equity demands that we systematically build the store of vocabulary and facts students need to grasp complex texts in the later grades, not simply hope they acquire it during independent reading time.

Restoring the balance in literacy does not mean banishing authentic literature or killing a love of stories. It means acknowledging the science of how children actually learn to read. It means recognizing that a teacher’s most profound act of empowerment is not to be a silent facilitator beside a reading nook, but to be an expert guide who directly, unapologetically, and joyfully equips every child with the code and the knowledge to unlock the world of ideas.

New York City’s decision to revive the "balanced literacy" approach relies on a romantic but ultimately flawed vision of how children learn to read. Balanced literacy is undeniably appealing in its rhetoric. It emphasizes independent reading, student choice, and cultivating a lifelong love of literature. However, while surrounding children with engaging books is a wonderful goal, it is not a substitute for directly teaching them how to read the words on the page.

Decades of cognitive science and the findings of the 2000 National Reading Panel have settled the debate on how the human brain acquires written language. Unlike speaking, reading is not a biologically natural process that children absorb simply by being immersed in a text-rich environment. Children must learn to decode the English language by mapping sounds to letters. For the vast majority of students, this requires explicit, systematic phonics instruction, not a brief "mini-lesson" where phonics is treated as just one of several optional strategies for guessing a word based on context clues or pictures.

The shift away from direct instruction is profoundly an equity issue. Some children arrive at kindergarten having been read to thousands of times, possessing the rich vocabulary and print awareness to seemingly "catch" reading with minimal explicit guidance. Balanced literacy often works well enough for them. But for students from disadvantaged backgrounds, who may lack that early exposure, the absence of systematic phonics is devastating. Direct, teacher-led instruction levels the playing field, ensuring that every child is explicitly taught the fundamental mechanics of reading.

Furthermore, literacy extends beyond decoding; it requires deep comprehension. Critics of balanced literacy are right to point out that students need "facts." Comprehension is heavily dependent on background knowledge and vocabulary. When reading instruction consists primarily of students practicing abstract strategies—like "finding the main idea"—on isolated books they choose themselves, they miss out on the systematic knowledge-building that comes from a shared, content-rich curriculum.

We all want students to love reading, but they cannot love what they cannot do. True literacy requires a solid foundation built on explicit phonics and rich, factual content. Pretending otherwise will only continue to leave our most vulnerable students behind.

The right approach is a genuinely balanced one: explicit, systematic instruction in the alphabetic code in the early grades, coupled with abundant reading, writing, discussion and knowledge-building across literature, science, history and the arts.

Principals should not be left to choose between ideology and test preparation. Phonics is not a pedagogical preference; it is the scientifically established foundation of reading. The National Reading Panel's 2000 report, building on decades of cognitive science by researchers like Marilyn Jager Adams and Keith Stanovich, found unambiguously that systematic phonics instruction produces better outcomes than embedded or incidental approaches—especially for struggling readers and children from low-income homes. Balanced literacy, in practice, too often means phonics whenever the teacher feels like getting to it, which is to say not systematically and not enough.

The romance of "student-led" discovery in reading is particularly damaging because reading, unlike spoken language, is not a natural human capacity. Our brains did not evolve to decode symbols; we have to be taught, painstakingly, to map graphemes to phonemes. Children who are read to by educated parents and surrounded by print may appear to pick this up "naturally," but they are in fact getting heavy implicit instruction at home. The children who do not get that home advantage are precisely the ones balanced literacy fails. Lucy Calkins's workshop model, which the new New York City chancellor Carmen Fariña is reinstating, assumes a level of self-direction and background knowledge that many eight-year-olds simply do not possess.

Equally damaging is balanced literacy's neglect of content knowledge. E.D. Hirsch has argued for thirty years that reading comprehension is not a transferable "skill" you practice on any old text; it is largely a function of what you already know about the topic. A child who knows nothing about the Civil War cannot comprehend a passage about Gettysburg no matter how many "main idea" worksheets she has completed. Yet balanced literacy classrooms famously assign students to read "just right books" of their own choosing, fragmenting the shared knowledge base a class might otherwise build.

The New York City experience under Joel Klein offered a natural experiment of sorts. Schools that adopted Core Knowledge curricula—with explicit instruction and shared content—outperformed balanced-literacy comparison schools on later reading tests, as the 2012 pilot study suggested. That is consistent with what we see in the highest-performing charter networks and in nations like Finland and Singapore, where direct instruction in decoding precedes the pleasures of independent reading.

None of this means classrooms should be joyless drill factories. Skilled teachers weave phonics into rich read-alouds, vocabulary development, and writing. But the sequence matters: decode first, comprehend always, discover later. Returning balanced literacy to the center of New York's instructional approach, without the structural correctives Klein-era reformers tried to install, is a step backward dressed up as progressivism. The children who will pay the price are the ones who can least afford it.

The debate between "balanced literacy" and "phonics-and-facts" is largely a manufactured polarity, and it has been recycled every fifteen years since at least the 1950s, when Rudolf Flesch wrote "Why Johnny Can't Read." Every generation, reformers discover that children need phonics; every subsequent generation rediscovers that children also need to love books. The truth is unsurprising: they need both, in proportions appropriate to their age and their individual development.

What I find more interesting than the methodological war is how little of it is really about method. The Calkins workshop model done well—by a teacher who has been trained for years, who knows how to confer with individual students, who keeps meticulous running records of each child's progress—is a beautiful thing. The same model done badly, by a teacher handed a binder and told to implement it, is a disaster of children pretending to read while the teacher pretends to teach. The same is true of scripted phonics programs. Direct Instruction in the hands of a master teacher is rigorous and warm; in the hands of a tired one, it's a hostage situation.

In other words, the variable that matters most is teacher capacity, and the United States has spent thirty years pretending otherwise. We adopt curricula and call it reform. We tinker with reading wars while our schools of education produce graduates who often cannot themselves articulate what a phoneme is, or name the books a third grader ought to be reading. Finland, the perennial example, doesn't have a secret pedagogy; it has a teaching corps drawn from the top tier of university graduates and trained at master's level before being trusted with children.

So when New York City swings back toward balanced literacy under Carmen Fariña, the right question isn't "is balanced literacy good?" The right question is: will the city's roughly 75,000 elementary teachers receive the sustained coaching, planning time, and substantive professional knowledge they need to execute it? If yes, we'll see gains. If no—if it's another mandate from Tweed delivered through a PowerPoint—we'll get the same mediocre results we got the last time, and we'll blame the method rather than the implementation.

A sensible literacy program in 2014 would include systematic, explicit phonics in K–2; rich, content-heavy read-alouds drawn from a coherent curriculum like Core Knowledge; substantial independent reading of texts at varied levels; and writing every day. None of this is controversial among reading researchers. The controversy is political. It is time we noticed that.

I taught fourth grade in the Bronx for seven years, and I want to push back on the way this debate is being framed. The critics of balanced literacy talk as though the alternative—"closer instruction from teachers and more work on phonics and facts"—is self-evidently rigorous, and as though workshop classrooms are loose, undisciplined places where children drift. That is not what I saw, and it is not what my colleagues did.

A well-run reading workshop is one of the most demanding instructional formats there is. The teacher delivers a focused minilesson, then circulates and confers with individual students, diagnosing in real time what each child needs—this one is skipping over multisyllabic words, that one is decoding fine but missing inferences, a third has stalled at chapter books because she lacks stamina. The teacher keeps running records, plans small strategy groups, and adjusts daily. This is not the absence of instruction. It is instruction at a level of differentiation that whole-class phonics drilling cannot match.

The caricature of balanced literacy as "let the children discover reading" comes mostly from people who have not spent recent time in good balanced-literacy classrooms. Phonics is taught in those rooms—through word study, through interactive writing, through shared reading. It is just not the only thing taught, and it is not taught as an end in itself.

What went wrong in New York under the prior administration was not that balanced literacy was tried and failed. It is that it was layered on top of a high-stakes testing regime that pushed teachers toward test prep, and rolled out without enough trained literacy coaches to support new teachers. When a brand-new teacher with no mentor is told to "do workshop," of course it falls apart. That is a problem of investment, not of philosophy.

The phonics-first crowd points to scientific consensus, and they are right that decoding instruction matters, particularly for the youngest students and for children with dyslexia or other reading difficulties. No serious balanced-literacy advocate disputes this. The disagreement is about what comes next—whether a child who can decode is a reader, or whether reading also requires the dispositions of choice, stamina, response, and authentic writing. I believe it requires those dispositions, and I believe they cannot be programmed in from a script.

Chancellor Fariña's return to workshop methods is welcome, provided it comes with what was missing before: smaller class sizes, full-time literacy coaches in every school, and time for teachers to plan. Method without support is theater. With support, balanced literacy is, in my experience, what good teaching looks like.

Both sides of this debate share a quiet assumption that I want to name and challenge: that the chief obstacle to American children becoming readers is the school's choice of instructional method. It is not. The chief obstacle is what happens, or fails to happen, in the first five years of a child's life, before any teacher gets to them.

Betty Hart and Todd Risley's well-known research documented a thirty-million-word gap in vocabulary exposure between children of professional families and children on welfare by age three. By kindergarten entry, the gap in oral language, background knowledge, and print exposure is enormous. The PIRLS and NAEP data we have on American reading achievement consistently show that the variance in outcomes is overwhelmingly driven by socioeconomic factors that precede schooling and persist alongside it. We are arguing about whether to use phonics workbooks or leveled readers while the actual determinants of literacy—parental education, household stress, access to early childhood programs, summer learning loss, the presence of books in the home—go largely unaddressed.

This is not to absolve schools or to say method doesn't matter. It does, at the margins, and the margins are where many individual children live or die academically. But policy debates conducted entirely at the level of in-school pedagogy systematically overestimate what schools alone can do, and they let everyone else—legislators, employers, housing authorities, the people who set the minimum wage—off the hook.

If I were designing a literacy strategy for New York City, I would spend less energy adjudicating between Lucy Calkins and E.D. Hirsch and more energy on: universal high-quality pre-K (which Mayor de Blasio is, to his credit, attempting), home visiting programs for new parents, expanded summer reading programs in public libraries, and serious investment in the wages of early childhood workers so that the people interacting with three-year-olds are not the lowest-paid people in the education system. When we look at the highest-achieving school systems in the world, what distinguishes them is rarely a pedagogical magic bullet. It is broader social policy: lower child poverty, stronger family supports, more equitable funding. The reading wars are, in part, a displacement activity. They let us argue passionately about something inside school walls because the harder conversation—about what's outside those walls—is one we keep refusing to have.

The deepest problem with the way we teach reading in America is that we treat "reading" as a skill divorced from anything in particular to read. Both phonics enthusiasts and balanced-literacy advocates, in different ways, fall into this trap. Phonics gives you the keys but not the house. Balanced literacy gives you a house tour but no maps. What children desperately need is a coherent, sequenced body of knowledge built over years.

Consider what happens when a fifth grader is asked to read a passage about the Constitutional Convention. If she does not know who the framers were, what the Articles of Confederation failed to do, what "ratification" means, why slavery shadowed the proceedings—then no amount of "reading strategies" will rescue her comprehension. Daniel Willingham, the cognitive scientist, has been making this point for years: reading comprehension is largely knowledge in disguise. The strategies and skills approach—"find the main idea, make a prediction, ask a question"—works only for students who already possess the relevant background knowledge. For everyone else, it is empty calisthenics.

This is why E.D. Hirsch's Core Knowledge curriculum, in the small but rigorous trials we have, has produced striking results. It is not because Core Knowledge is more "rigorous" in some vague sense. It is because it commits to teaching specific things in a specific order—ancient Egypt in second grade, the American Revolution in fifth, the cell in seventh—so that by the time a student encounters a difficult text, she has the conceptual scaffolding to make sense of it.

Balanced literacy's preference for "leveled readers" chosen by student interest is actively pernicious here. A class of thirty children reading thirty different books cannot build shared knowledge. A teacher cannot weave references back to a common touchstone. The vocabulary that is the engine of comprehension—the academic and domain-specific words children meet only in texts—is dispersed and forgotten.

The right approach, then, is neither phonics-versus-whole-language nor workshop-versus-direct-instruction. It is a curriculum-first approach: a clearly specified, knowledge-rich sequence taught to every child, K–8, in history, science, geography, art, and literature, with reading instruction (including systematic phonics) folded into it. The pedagogical methods can vary by teacher and child. What cannot vary, if we are serious about literacy, is the substance. The American obsession with reading as a content-free skill is, in the end, the deepest cause of our mediocre comprehension scores. Fix the curriculum, and many of these methodological arguments will look quaint.

There is something almost theological about the reading wars. Each side has its scripture (the National Reading Panel report; "The Art of Teaching Reading"), its prophets (Reid Lyon; Lucy Calkins), and its narrative of fall and redemption. As someone who has watched this argument cycle through American education since the 1980s, I want to offer a more skeptical view: maybe the truth is that we do not actually know nearly as much about how to teach reading at scale as either camp claims.

Consider the evidence base. The National Reading Panel, often cited as settling the phonics question, examined a narrow slice of experimental studies and reached its strongest conclusions about decoding in the earliest grades. Its findings on comprehension, fluency, and vocabulary were considerably weaker. Studies of balanced literacy and workshop approaches at scale are sparser still, and confounded by the fact that the very name "balanced literacy" covers wildly different practices in different classrooms.

The high-quality evidence we have tells us a few things with confidence: systematic phonics in K–2 helps, particularly for at-risk readers; reading volume matters; vocabulary and background knowledge matter enormously for comprehension. Beyond that, we are largely arguing from anecdote, ideology, and small studies that don't replicate well.

Given this uncertainty, what should policy do? I'd suggest three principles. First, mandate the things we actually know: explicit, systematic phonics instruction in the early grades, regardless of what else a school does. Second, leave room for genuine professional variation in everything else—because we do not have evidence strong enough to justify imposing a single comprehension pedagogy on every classroom. Third, invest heavily in measurement, not just of student outcomes but of what teachers are actually doing, so that we can learn over time what works for whom.

What New York City has done for two decades is the opposite: swing between centralized mandates, each one declared the answer, each one reversed by the next administration. Joel Klein imposed balanced literacy, then partially retreated from it. Carmen Fariña is now restoring it. Whoever follows her will probably reverse it again. The whiplash itself is a major contributor to mediocre outcomes, because teachers spend their careers being retrained in successive orthodoxies rather than deepening their practice.

Humility is in short supply in this debate, but it is what the evidence actually warrants. Teach phonics, surround children with books and knowledge, hire and retain good teachers, measure honestly, and stop pretending that any of us has discovered the One True Method. We haven't.

I want to make an argument that will sound strange in this debate: the most important variable in reading instruction may not be the curriculum at all, but the amount of time children spend actually reading.

Richard Allington has spent decades documenting a startling pattern. In typical American elementary classrooms, children spend only a small fraction of the language arts block reading connected text. They spend much more time on worksheets, transitions, test prep, listening to the teacher, doing "centers," and various other activities that look like reading instruction but involve almost no eyes-on-text. In schools where children become strong readers, by contrast, they read—a lot—every day. The volume effect is enormous and shows up in study after study.

This reframes the phonics-versus-balanced-literacy fight. Phonics programs, especially the more scripted ones, can crowd out reading time with drill on isolated sounds and words. Balanced literacy, done in the worst way, can crowd out reading time with elaborate minilessons, conferences, and "share" sessions that leave only ten minutes for the books. Both pathologies produce the same result: classrooms where children talk about reading, prepare for reading, get assessed on reading, and rarely read.

The remedy is not a methodological reversal but a structural one. Protect long, daily blocks of independent reading time, with a variety of books at varied levels in every classroom, and with teachers conferring quietly with students about what they are actually reading. Pair this with explicit phonics instruction in the earliest grades for those who need it—and most kindergartners and first graders do—but keep that instruction short and crisp, not the centerpiece of the morning.

This matters especially for children from homes with few books. The "summer slide" research shows that children from poor households fall behind largely because they don't read between June and September. Schools that send children home with stacks of self-selected books over the summer—a small, cheap intervention—have shown meaningful effects on fall reading scores. This is balanced literacy's best instinct: that becoming a reader requires reading, daily, in volume, with some measure of choice and interest.

So the right approach is to stop talking about approaches for a moment and look at the clock. How many minutes a day is each child in our schools actually reading? In most schools, the honest answer is shockingly few. Until we fix that—through scheduling, through book access, through the protection of independent reading time from the encroachments of testing and pedagogy alike—no instructional war we wage will produce the literate citizenry we say we want.

There is a dangerous nostalgia creeping into our reading debates. When critics attack balanced literacy as too loose, too student-led, and insist on a return to teacher-directed, phonics-heavy instruction, they forget what school felt like for millions of children under the old factory model. Balanced literacy, at its best, treats reading not as a set of drills to be endured but as a deeply human act of making meaning. She pulls a small group to work on vowel digraphs using a beloved picture book, or she nudges a writer to stretch out a word in his story. This responsive teaching respects child development: some five-year-olds crack the code with minimal direct instruction, while others need repeated, systematic exposure. A skilled teacher in a well-stocked balanced literacy classroom can provide exactly that, without making every child march in lockstep.

In New York City, where balanced literacy is regaining ground, the goal is to elevate teacher expertise, not impose a one-size-fits-all method. And let us not forget motivation. Children who see reading as a source of joy and agency — who get lost in a series book or pour their heart into a personal narrative — read more. Volume of reading is one of the strongest predictors of long-term achievement. The return of balanced literacy in New York City schools is a troubling step backward. After decades of cognitive research, we know with certainty how skilled reading develops. It is not a natural process, like learning to speak. Reading is an artificial construction that requires explicit, systematic instruction in the alphabetic code. When we embrace a student-led philosophy that privileges meaning and context over the mechanics of decoding, we fail the very children who most need our teaching — and we widen the achievement gap.

The evidence is overwhelming. The National Reading Panel’s report, now well over a decade old, confirmed the necessity of phonemic awareness and systematic phonics for beginning readers. More recently, functional brain imaging has shown that effective reading instruction literally rewires the brain, creating neural pathways that integrate visual, phonological, and semantic processing. This rewiring does not happen through osmosis or by guessing at words from pictures. It requires deliberate practice in connecting letters to sounds, blending those sounds into words, and building fluency through repeated reading of decodable text. Balanced literacy programs may nod at phonics, but they treat it as a minor ingredient, sprinkled in when a child “needs” it. For many children — particularly those without a print-rich home environment — that is far too little, far too late.

Equally worrying is the neglect of facts and background knowledge. In a well-meaning effort to put students at the center and follow their interests, many balanced literacy classrooms devote hours to independent reading and strategy-based mini-lessons, while pushing history, science, and the arts to the margins. Yet comprehension is not a free-floating skill. A child who can flawlessly decode a paragraph about the Civil War but has no mental model of slavery, sectionalism, or Abraham Lincoln will understand little. We need curricula steeped in rich content, not just “just-right” books chosen by reading level alone. The Common Core State Standards recognized this by insisting on complex, knowledge-building text. Balanced literacy’s emphasis on student choice and teacher-crafted units often undermines that coherent knowledge progression.

The solution requires more than curriculum adoption. The reading wars will continue indefinitely because we keep looking for methodological magic bullets while ignoring the systemic conditions that shape educational practice. The research is more settled than the debate suggests, and policymakers who keep reopening the reading wars are either ill-informed or using the debate for other purposes. Let me be direct: the evidence supports an approach that combines explicit phonics instruction with systematic development of comprehension strategies, all within a framework that builds knowledge and vocabulary over time.

The National Reading Panel's 2000 report synthesized decades of research and found consistent evidence that phonemic awareness instruction, systematic phonics, guided repeated reading, and comprehension strategy instruction all produce reliable gains. Subsequent research has confirmed these findings. What works is explicit instruction in the alphabetic principle combined with extensive practice reading meaningful text and direct instruction in comprehension strategies like questioning, summarizing, and monitoring understanding.

This evidence has been available for over a decade, yet American schools continue to oscillate between ideological extremes. Why? Because evidence-based practice requires infrastructure—assessment systems to identify students who need intervention, professional development to prepare teachers, and curriculum materials aligned with research. Ideological positions require only conviction and rhetorical skill.

The New York City context is instructive. When the Bloomberg administration pushed balanced literacy in the early 2000s, research already suggested that explicit phonics instruction produced better outcomes for struggling readers. When the de Blasio administration now signals a return to balanced literacy, the research is even clearer. What is missing is the political will to invest in evidence-based implementation rather than ideological fashion.

Improving literacy requires following evidence where it leads, not retreating to comfortable ideologies. The evidence says: teach phonemic awareness explicitly, teach phonics systematically, provide time for authentic reading and writing, teach comprehension strategies directly, and build knowledge through content-rich curriculum. This is not balanced literacy or phonics instruction—it is evidence-based reading instruction. That is the right approach.

I want to offer a perspective that the reading wars render invisible: that of the classroom teacher who must somehow navigate between competing demands while serving real children with complex needs.

I need assessment systems that help me understand each child's specific needs and monitor progress over time. I need time—time to plan, time to collaborate with colleagues, time to work with small groups.

The reading wars are exhausting because they treat teaching as a matter of choosing the right script rather than developing professional expertise.
