What Is the Purpose of Lent?

For many Christians, Lent is a profound spiritual tradition, symbolic of Christ’s 40 days of temptation in the wilderness. But even for nonbelievers, it can be a solemn ritual or a chance for self-improvement.

So what is the purpose of Lent? Is there a point in giving something up?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Lent began as a baptismal preparation in the early church, a forty-day catechumenate culminating at the Easter Vigil. Only later did it become a general season of penance for the whole community. Knowing that history matters, because it tells us what the practice is actually for: not self-improvement, but conversion. The point is to be turned, gradually and uncomfortably, toward something larger than oneself.

This is why the modern habit of giving up chocolate or Facebook, while harmless, misses what the tradition is doing. Fasting, almsgiving, and prayer—the classical triad—are meant to work together. You deny yourself something small so that you notice how attached you are to it; you give the money or time you save to someone who needs it; and you pray, because without prayer the whole exercise collapses into a diet or a productivity hack. The discomfort is diagnostic. It reveals the disordered loves you didn't know you had.

For nonbelievers, I think there is still something to learn here, though it requires honesty about what's being borrowed. A secular Lent can cultivate genuine discipline and gratitude. But stripped of its theological anchor—the conviction that we are being prepared for a particular death and a particular resurrection—it tends to become another form of self-optimization, which is the opposite of what penance is.

So yes, there is a point in giving something up. The point is to discover, for forty days, that you are not what you consume.

Lent’s purpose is preparation. For Christians, the 40 days mirror Jesus’ wilderness fast—a time of testing and prayer. Giving something up, whether food or a habit, becomes a physical reminder of dependence on God and a small participation in Christ’s sacrifice. This self-denial trains the soul to resist temptation and opens space for prayer and service. Historically, the season has been a collective journey of penance, aligning the faithful with the story of salvation leading to Easter.

But the ritual holds meaning beyond church walls. In a consumer culture that promotes constant indulgence, voluntary renunciation becomes a countercultural act of self-mastery. Behavioral research confirms that self-control operates like a muscle; periodic fasting from a chosen pleasure—caffeine, television, online shopping—can strengthen overall willpower. For the nonbeliever, Lent offers a structured framework for mindfulness, an annual opportunity to disrupt unconscious habits and reassess what is truly necessary.

The practice also cultivates empathy and community. Traditional Lenten disciplines tied fasting to almsgiving, so that what was saved through self-denial could be shared with those in need. Even today, giving up something reveals our hidden dependencies and can spark gratitude. When undertaken in a group—whether a church congregation or a circle of friends—the shared fast creates accountability and a sense of common purpose, turning personal sacrifice into a collective journey.

Thus, the point is not deprivation for its own sake. Giving something up creates space—for reflection, for recalibrating priorities, for attending to what matters. Whether the goal is closeness to God or a clearer sense of self, Lent’s purpose is to add depth and intentionality to life—a kind of spiritual spring cleaning. In a distracted age, that is a gift anyone can receive.

The purpose of Lent is not deprivation for its own sake, nor a religiously decorated version of a New Year’s resolution. At its best, Lent is a season for telling the truth about desire: what governs us, what comforts us, what distracts us, and what we would rather not face.

That is why giving something up can matter. A fast from meat, alcohol, sweets, shopping, Facebook or television is not morally impressive by itself. One can give up chocolate and become only more vain, more irritable or more self-satisfied. But a chosen absence can reveal dependence. It interrupts habit long enough to ask whether the thing I call “mine” has quietly become my master. The point is not to prove willpower, but to make room for repentance, prayer and mercy.

In Christian terms, Lent looks toward Easter by way of the wilderness and the cross. It recalls Jesus’ 40 days of temptation, but also Israel’s long schooling in dependence. The traditional disciplines — fasting, almsgiving and prayer — belong together. Fasting without generosity becomes self-improvement. Prayer without sacrifice can become sentiment. Almsgiving without conversion can become philanthropy at a safe distance. Lent joins them so that the believer’s body, time and money are all implicated.

There is also a public value here, even for those outside the church. A consumer culture trains people to treat every appetite as urgent and every inconvenience as an injustice. Lent insists that limits can be chosen, not merely suffered; that freedom is not identical with having options; that some hungers should be educated rather than indulged.

Still, Lent should not be romanticized. For the poor, the sick or the overburdened, “giving something up” may be the daily condition, not a spiritual exercise. The season’s deeper question is therefore not “What have you quit?” but “What are you being freed for?” If the answer is greater humility, clearer attention and more love of neighbor, then Lent has served its purpose.

The purpose of Lent, ultimately, is to interrupt our automatic living. In suspending small pleasures, we wake up to the world we've been sleepwalking through. That waking, whether framed religiously or secularly, remains worth practicing.

Lent is, first and last, a theological discipline, and we lose something important when we try to repackage it as a generic self-improvement regimen. The forty days are not arbitrary. They echo Israel's forty years in the wilderness, Moses on Sinai, Elijah's journey to Horeb, and most directly Christ's forty days of fasting before his ministry. To observe Lent is to enter, liturgically, into that wilderness — to confront one's own appetites, distractions and idols in preparation for the Paschal mystery of Good Friday and Easter.

Strip out the resurrection and the practice loses its shape. Giving up chocolate or Facebook because it might make you a marginally healthier or more focused person is fine, but it isn't Lent; it's a New Year's resolution with a Catholic accent. The Christian point of fasting is not to feel virtuous, nor even to build character, but to create space — hunger, silence, inconvenience — in which prayer and repentance can do their slow work. The traditional triad has always been prayer, fasting and almsgiving, and the last is telling: the money saved from the steak or the wine is supposed to go to the poor. The discipline is oriented outward and upward, not inward.

That said, I don't begrudge nonbelievers who borrow the season. There is real wisdom in any cultural practice that asks affluent people to deny themselves something, however small, on a fixed schedule. In a society that treats every craving as a right to be satisfied immediately, even a secularized Lent registers a useful dissent. But Christians should resist the temptation to sell the season on those terms. Lent's purpose is not to make us better versions of ourselves; it is to remind us that we are dust, that we are mortal, and that we need a Savior. Everything else — the discipline, the simplicity, the small daily renunciations — flows from that.

The honest answer is that Lent, for most of the people who observe it today, no longer has a single purpose — and that is not necessarily a bad thing.

Survey the practice in 2013 and you find an extraordinary range. There are devout Catholics who fast and abstain because the Church asks them to and because they believe it conforms them to Christ. There are mainline Protestants who use the season as a kind of spiritual spring cleaning. There are lapsed Christians who give up beer for forty days because their grandmother did. And there are plenty of nonbelievers who, noticing that Ash Wednesday has become a minor Twitter holiday, give up sugar or social media as a sort of secular discipline. The Pew studies on American religion have been documenting this kind of "believing without belonging" — and "belonging without believing" — for years.

Rather than scolding any of these groups for doing it wrong, it's worth asking what the practice actually accomplishes in each case. For the believer, Lent is preparation for Easter and an exercise in repentance. For the cultural Christian, it is a tether to a tradition that might otherwise dissolve. For the secular faster, it is an experiment in self-knowledge: you learn surprising things about yourself when you can't reach for the thing you habitually reach for. Behavioral psychologists like Roy Baumeister have written persuasively about how small, repeated acts of self-denial actually strengthen the capacity for self-control over time.

So yes, there is a point in giving something up, and the point varies with the giver. The deeper question Lent poses — religious or not — is whether we are in charge of our desires or whether our desires are in charge of us. Forty days is long enough to find out, and short enough that almost anyone can try.

What I find most interesting about Lent is how stubbornly it has survived in a culture that has supposedly outgrown such things. Most of the old liturgical calendar has faded from public consciousness — Ember Days, Rogation, Septuagesima — but Lent persists, and not only among the devout. Why?

My hypothesis is that Lent answers a need that secular modernity has not figured out how to meet on its own: the need for collective, time-bound, embodied discipline. We are very good at telling individuals to improve themselves, but very bad at offering them a shared framework in which to do it. Lent provides what the self-help industry cannot: a fixed start (Ash Wednesday), a fixed end (Easter), a community of people doing it with you, and a set of practices handed down rather than invented. Émile Durkheim would have recognized exactly what was going on.

This is why I think the question "is there a point in giving something up?" slightly misses the mark. The point is not really the thing given up. The point is the structure — the fact that on a specific Wednesday, millions of people simultaneously agree to take on a small, deliberate constraint, and that on a specific Sunday they will collectively release it. That rhythm of restraint and feast, lack and abundance, is something older than Christianity and probably older than agriculture. Christianity baptized it; secular culture has nothing comparable.

For believers, of course, the structure carries the substance: Lent prepares the heart for the death and resurrection of Christ. But even for the nonbeliever who participates, the form does its quiet work. You learn that your wants are not your needs. You learn that you can wait. You learn that there are seasons. In a culture organized around the instantaneous and the individual, this may be the most countercultural thing Lent has left to offer.
