Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Robert Pollin's call to make full employment the organizing principle of economic policy is bracing and overdue. After two years in which official unemployment has hovered above 9 percent and a broader U6 measure near 17 percent, the political conversation has nonetheless drifted toward deficit hawkery and a premature pivot to austerity — witness the recent Bowles-Simpson report and the ECB's tightening posture even as European peripheral economies bleed. Pollin is right that the choice is not between stimulus and a return to "normal," because the pre-crisis normal was a debt-fueled bubble economy that delivered stagnant median wages for three decades. The question is whether his prescription can carry the freight he assigns to it.

Start with the green-jobs arithmetic, which is the empirical centerpiece. The PERI input-output exercise is useful, but it elides a crucial distinction between gross and net job creation. A million dollars shifted from oil refining to weatherization generates more direct hires per dollar, but some of the displaced oil-sector workers will not be quickly absorbed, and the differential is partly a labor-intensity story that is another way of saying productivity is lower in the receiving sector. Over the long run, an economy whose marginal investments yield more jobs per dollar but less output per worker is one in which living standards rise more slowly. That tradeoff may well be worth making — especially when carbon externalities are priced in — but it should be named rather than absorbed into a headline figure of 4.8 million net jobs. A 25 percent cut to the Pentagon is also presented as if it were simply a fiscal switch; in practice it would generate dislocations concentrated in particular congressional districts, which is precisely why such cuts have proven politically unattainable since Eisenhower warned about them.

Second, the essay underweights what is probably the binding constraint right now: aggregate demand and the zero lower bound on nominal interest rates. The Fed's second round of quantitative easing, announced in November, is an admission that conventional monetary policy is exhausted. In that environment, the multiplier on fiscal expansion is unusually high, and the case for a much larger stimulus — not just a reallocation of existing spending — is overwhelming. Pollin nods at this but spends most of his pages on a longer-run structural agenda. The danger is that the structural agenda becomes a substitute for the cyclical fight rather than a complement to it. If we accept 7 percent unemployment as the baseline from which his green-jobs program subtracts 3 points, we have already conceded too much.

Third, the Swedish detour is illuminating but the conclusion drawn from it understates the institutional gap. The Rehn-Meidner model rested on centralized wage bargaining, solidaristic wage compression, and union density above 70 percent. American private-sector union density is now under 7 percent. Asking U.S. unions to "design a workable full-employment program" that internalizes inflation discipline is asking a movement without coordination capacity to do what only encompassing labor federations have managed. Without first rebuilding the institutional substrate — card check, sectoral bargaining, something — the Swedish lesson is more cautionary than instructive. Pollin's own evidence that Sweden itself abandoned the model under EU pressure should give pause.

Fourth, Pollin too quickly dismisses the trade-deficit channel. He is right that a competitive devaluation race is unappealing, but a persistent current-account deficit of 3–5 percent of GDP, sustained by Chinese reserve accumulation and an undervalued renminbi, is a steady drain on tradable-goods employment that no domestic reallocation can offset. A serious full-employment program needs an external strategy — coordinated rebalancing, perhaps via the kind of capital-flow management Keynes proposed at Bretton Woods — not just a domestic one.

These are friendly amendments. Pollin has the fundamental diagnosis right: the natural-rate framework has functioned as an ideological tranquilizer, persuading policymakers that 6 or 7 percent unemployment is a fact of nature rather than a policy choice. Recovering the moral and analytic standing of full employment as a goal is the necessary first step. The harder work — confronting the Fed's inflation orthodoxy, rebuilding labor power, taming finance, and coordinating internationally — still lies ahead.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Robert Pollin is right to insist that full employment must return to the center of American economic policy. The human toll of the Great Recession demands an equally ambitious response. Yet the specific path he charts—a massive reallocation of $330 billion per year from military and fossil fuel spending into education and clean energy—rests on a series of empirical, political, and theoretical assumptions that are far more fragile than his confident rhetoric suggests. Pollin frames his proposal as a clean break from neoliberalism, but the details reveal a plan that may be neither politically attainable nor economically sufficient to deliver the full-employment economy he envisions. In what follows, I argue that the challenges of job quality, inflation, globalization, and political will are deeper than Pollin acknowledges, and that a more credible approach must work with, rather than against, the realities of the U.S. economy in 2011.

The centerpiece of Pollin’s proposal is the sectoral spending shift. Drawing on his own research, he calculates that $330 billion transferred annually to education and clean energy would create about 4.8 million net new jobs. The numbers are seductive, but they deserve careful scrutiny. The job multipliers he uses—29 jobs per $1 million in education, 17 in clean energy—are statistical averages derived from input–output tables. They say nothing about the quality of those jobs, the skill mismatches that would emerge, or the time it takes to retrain a welder from the oil fields to install solar panels. Many of the clean-energy jobs counted are in construction and manufacturing, fields that are notoriously cyclical and vulnerable to the next downturn. Education jobs are more stable, but they depend overwhelmingly on state and local budgets, which are being crushed precisely because the recession vaporized tax revenues. Simply throwing federal money at classrooms does not circumvent the fact that the states must eventually sustain those positions once the initial infusion runs out. Nor does Pollin address the regional concentration of job losses: slashing the military and fossil-fuel sectors by a quarter would crater employment in places like Texas, Oklahoma, and the defense-heavy suburbs of Washington, D.C. The workers displaced are not interchangeable Lego pieces that can be slotted smoothly into schools and wind farms; they are middle-aged people with specific skills, mortgages, and community ties. Without a massive and costly system of wage insurance, relocation subsidies, and retraining—far beyond what Pollin sketches—the transition would generate its own brand of economic misery and political backlash.

Then there is the question of political feasibility, which Pollin himself admits is the ultimate obstacle. But he treats it almost as an afterthought, a matter of summoning enough “political will.” In the Washington of 2011, where the new Republican House majority is already demanding deep spending cuts and a balanced budget amendment, a proposal to strip $330 billion from defense and fossil fuels while expanding government programs is a nonstarter. The military budget, as bloated as it is, supports a vast constellation of contractors, bases, and congressional districts. The fossil-fuel industry is among the most powerful lobbying forces in the country. Even if one could muster a coalition for clean energy and education, it would fracture as soon as the specifics of which bases to close, which oil leases to cancel, and which districts to starve came to the table. Pollin’s Sweden relied on a social democratic consensus that simply does not exist in the United States, where organized labor represents less than 7 percent of private-sector workers and where the prevailing ideology is still heavily tilted toward market solutions. To treat this political reality as a mere detail is to ignore the most important lesson of the past two years: that even a relatively modest stimulus package of $787 billion, enacted in the depths of crisis with a Democratic Congress, sowed the seeds of a furious anti-government reaction. Doubling down with a permanent reallocation on that scale would be political suicide for any administration.

The inflation question deserves more than the ambivalent treatment Pollin gives it. He acknowledges that a full-employment economy will generate upward pressure on wages and prices, and he points to the Swedish model of union restraint as a possible mechanism for managing it. But American unions are in no position to play such a role. They lack the centralized wage-setting authority of their Swedish counterparts; they cover a tiny fraction of the workforce; and they have been unable to prevent decades of real wage stagnation even in sectors they dominate. The Phillips Curve may have been misapplied in the 1970s—the oil shocks were clearly a major factor—but that does not mean the relationship has vanished. With the Federal Reserve’s post-Volcker mandate focused single-mindedly on price stability, any sign of rising inflation would provoke interest-rate hikes that would choke off the very recovery Pollin seeks. And inflation, if it takes hold, is not a neutral force: it erodes the real wages of the workers full employment is meant to help, and it falls hardest on those on fixed incomes. The Swedish model worked for a time not just because of union restraint, but because the central bank and government were fully committed to accommodating full employment even at some inflationary cost—a commitment that no plausible U.S. administration or Federal Reserve chair in 2011 would make.

Pollin’s handling of globalization also reveals a tension he never fully resolves. He rightly argues that the trade deficit and immigration are not insurmountable barriers, and he points to the late 1990s as proof that near-full employment can be reached in an open economy. But that period was fueled by the dot-com bubble and an extraordinary surge in private investment that followed the productivity boom associated with information technology. The investment environment today, by contrast, is still weighed down by the aftermath of the housing bust, deleveraging, and deep uncertainty about future demand. Nor did the late 1990s solve the problem of wage stagnation: even at 4 percent unemployment, the median worker’s real wage barely budged, and the gains were concentrated at the top of the distribution. Worse, Pollin underestimates the corrosive effect of offshoring threats on worker bargaining power. When firms can credibly threaten to relocate production or services to low-wage countries, even very low unemployment may not translate into real wage gains—the reserve army has simply gone global. His own data on the divergence between productivity and wages since 1973 makes that clear, yet his policy response, a domestic spending shift, does nothing to alter those international power dynamics. A truly effective assault on unemployment would require rethinking the terms of trade, investment treaties, and corporate governance—issues Pollin only glances at.

Most fundamentally, Pollin’s plan relies on an engine of job creation that is overwhelmingly public-sector driven. Government spending, no matter how well targeted, cannot be the sole generator of sustainable full employment in a $15 trillion economy. The majority of the 140 million jobs that exist today are in the private sector, and any durable return to full employment must be led by a revival of private investment in productive, non-speculative activities. Pollin mentions financial regulation to channel credit away from the Wall Street casino, but he offers no concrete mechanism. Without a surge in business confidence and plant-and-equipment spending, the jobs created by government outlays will remain dependent on continued deficits. That is a recipe for a permanent state of semi-stimulus, with all the political vulnerability that implies. A more balanced strategy would couple targeted public investments in infrastructure and clean energy with a genuine commitment to industrial policy—something the United States has never had the institutional capacity to manage—and would address the fundamental sources of private-sector stagnation, including the overvaluation of the dollar, the dysfunctional corporate tax code, and the financial system’s bias against long-term risk-taking.

None of this is to say that Pollin’s moral imperative is wrong. A society that denies its citizens the right to a decent job is failing on the most basic level. But the road from here to there is far more treacherous than his essay suggests. America in 2011 is not Sweden in 1965: its political institutions are fragmented, its labor movement is enfeebled, its global position is less dominant, and its public is skeptical of grand government schemes. The challenge is to build a full-employment coalition that begins with the realistic possibilities of the Obama presidency—extending the payroll tax cut, maintaining aid to the states, investing in infrastructure through a national infrastructure bank—while laying the intellectual groundwork for the longer-term transformation of labor-market institutions and trade policy. Pollin’s call to arms is a necessary provocation, but it is an opening bid in a debate, not a blueprint. The real work of reconstruction will be slower, messier, and more deeply tied to the difficult business of restoring the private sector’s will to invest and the public’s trust in collective action.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Robert Pollin offers a refreshing and morally necessary provocation: the United States must return full employment to the center of its macroeconomic agenda. In the devastating wake of the 2008 financial collapse, with official unemployment still hovering near 9.5 percent and millions more relegated to involuntary part-time work or pushed out of the labor force altogether, the human cost of the Great Recession is staggering. Pollin rightly argues that our current economic suffering is not an unavoidable act of nature, but the culmination of a decades-long neoliberal consensus that prioritized inflation control and deregulation over the livelihoods of working people.

However, while Pollin’s ultimate destination is compelling, the roadmap he provides to get us there relies on political and economic assumptions that fracture upon contact with the current reality of 2011. Specifically, his proposal to generate 4.8 million jobs by redirecting $330 billion annually from the military and fossil-fuel sectors into education and clean energy underestimates the severe frictions of labor mobility and ignores the immediate, hostile political climate in Washington.

First, we must address the glaring political elephant in the room. As we enter the new year, the political winds have shifted violently away from the stimulus mentality of 2009. The November 2010 midterm elections delivered historic gains to a Republican party fueled by a Tea Party movement that is singularly obsessed with austerity, deficit reduction, and slashing the size of government. In this freshly divided government, finding a coalition willing to slash the Pentagon budget by 25 percent and aggressively divest from fossil fuels is not merely a heavy political lift; it is a legislative impossibility. The immediate battle in the 112th Congress will not be about launching massive new domestic investments, but rather about defending the remnants of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act and basic social safety nets from draconian cuts.

Beyond the political headwinds, Pollin’s economic mechanics treat human labor as far more fungible than it actually is. By relying on input-output models to show that $1 million spent on education generates roughly 29 jobs compared to only five in the fossil-fuel sector, he presents a mathematically elegant solution to a deeply messy human problem. But labor is not a liquid asset that can be seamlessly poured from one sector into another.

If we rapidly draw down defense spending and fossil-fuel extraction, we will inevitably displace hundreds of thousands of workers—machinists at defense contractors in the Rust Belt, oil rig workers in the Gulf, and logistics personnel scattered across the country. Can a displaced 50-year-old defense manufacturing worker easily transition into a role as an elementary school teacher or a solar-panel installer? The “skill mismatch” generated by such a sudden, massive sectoral shift would trigger a surge in frictional and structural unemployment. Pollin acknowledges that there would be "skill shortages in some areas and labor gluts in other areas," but he vastly understates the localized economic devastation this would cause in communities reliant on the defense and energy sectors.

To bridge the gap between our current unemployment crisis and a sustainable, high-employment economy, we must focus heavily on a sector Pollin largely bypasses: traditional infrastructure. The 2008 crash was uniquely destructive to the construction and manufacturing industries, heavily tied to the bursting of the housing bubble. Millions of blue-collar workers with physical construction, welding, and engineering skills are currently idle. A massive public works program focused on rebuilding roads, bridges, water systems, and modernizing the electrical grid would absorb the specific demographic of workers who have actually lost their jobs in this recession, without requiring them to completely retrain for the education or high-tech clean energy sectors. Furthermore, traditional infrastructure retains a glimmer of the bipartisan appeal that education and climate-change initiatives currently lack.

Finally, Pollin’s strategy for managing the inflationary pressures of full employment relies on a revival of the Swedish model, wherein labor unions restrain their wage demands in exchange for government commitments to job creation. This is an intriguing historical parallel, but it is deeply mismatched with the realities of the modern American workforce. In Sweden during the 1960s and 1970s, labor union density was remarkably high, allowing centralized labor federations to negotiate on behalf of the broader working class. In the United States today, union membership has fallen to roughly 12 percent overall, and less than 7 percent in the private sector. We simply do not possess the institutional framework for organized labor to credibly orchestrate the kind of macroeconomic wage restraint Pollin envisions.

Pollin is absolutely correct that full employment should be our moral and economic lodestar. The failure of the neoliberal model is laid bare in the shuttered factories and foreclosed homes across the country. But to achieve a high-employment economy, our solutions must grapple with the actual skillsets of the currently unemployed and the fierce realities of an austerity-crazed Congress. Reaching full employment requires not just a shift in accounting, but a grounded strategy for transitioning workers and navigating a deeply fractured political system.

Robert Pollin is right to insist that “full employment” cannot mean merely the statistical disappearance of idleness. A society in which people survive by juggling temp work, involuntary part-time hours, unpaid family labor, and desperate self-employment has not solved unemployment; it has disguised it. The moral center of his argument is therefore powerful: a decent job is not only a paycheck but a source of bargaining power, social participation, and security. In the present crisis, with long-term unemployment at postwar highs and state and local governments cutting teachers, transit workers, and social services, treating joblessness as a regrettable side effect of deficit reduction is both economically foolish and politically corrosive.

But Pollin’s strongest claims also point to the hardest questions. He proposes a long-term redirection of spending from the military and fossil fuels toward education and clean energy. I agree that this is the right direction. Education is labor-intensive, locally rooted, and socially productive. Energy retrofits, transit, smart-grid investment, and renewable power would employ people while addressing climate change and reducing oil dependence. In principle, this is a better jobs program than another round of tax cuts tilted toward households likely to save rather than spend.

Still, the transition he describes cannot be the whole answer to the employment emergency of 2011. Millions of workers need jobs now, not after the political system has reorganized the Pentagon, rewritten energy policy, and built new financing channels for green investment. The immediate crisis calls for a more direct commitment: federal aid to states and cities to prevent layoffs; extension of unemployment insurance and food assistance; infrastructure repair with “use it or lose it” deadlines; and, if private hiring remains weak, public employment programs targeted at distressed communities. The Works Progress Administration is not a perfect model for a modern economy, but its premise remains sound: if the private sector will not hire idle workers and useful public work is waiting to be done, government should connect the two.

Pollin also underestimates the political difficulty of his proposed reallocation. Cutting military spending by 25 percent is economically defensible, especially as the Iraq war winds down and Afghanistan policy comes under scrutiny. But defense spending is not merely a budget category; it is a congressional jobs system, a foreign-policy ideology, and a network of contractors with deep local influence. Likewise, reducing fossil-fuel spending is not simply a matter of shifting dollars from dirty energy to clean energy. It means confronting consumers’ dependence on cheap gasoline, regional economies built around coal and oil, and companies whose profits are tied to existing reserves. A full-employment coalition will have to offer transition guarantees to workers in these sectors, not just promises about future green jobs.

The inflation question deserves similar realism. Pollin is right that the 1970s should not be reduced to a morality tale about excessive worker power; oil shocks mattered enormously. But the United States today lacks Sweden’s union density, coordinated bargaining, and social-democratic institutions. Asking labor to help manage inflation is attractive, yet American labor is too weak in many sectors to perform that role. A full-employment strategy must therefore include institutional rebuilding: easier union recognition, stronger labor-law enforcement, wage boards in low-wage industries, and public standards attached to federal contracts and subsidies. Otherwise “green jobs” may become just another slogan covering low pay and insecurity.

The deeper issue is whether full employment should be treated as an outcome we hope markets eventually deliver or as a governing obligation. The Federal Reserve has a dual mandate, but in practice unemployment is too often tolerated in the name of credibility, while inflation fears are treated as urgent even when wages are stagnant. Congress, meanwhile, is already pivoting toward austerity despite the economy’s weakness. That is the wrong lesson from the stimulus. The Recovery Act was not proof that fiscal policy failed; it was proof that fiscal policy was too small, too temporary, and too compromised by tax cuts to fill the hole left by the financial collapse.

Pollin’s essential contribution is to widen the debate beyond “recovery” to the kind of economy worth recovering. Full employment will require more than growth. It will require public investment, financial restraint, worker power, and a willingness to measure success by decent jobs rather than by stock prices or deficit targets alone. That is ambitious, but the alternative is to accept mass unemployment as normal. We should refuse that bargain.

While Pollin is right that the human cost of unemployment demands urgent attention, his proposed remedy—a large-scale reallocation of spending toward education and clean energy—faces political and economic obstacles that his essay understates. Even accepting the moral case for full employment, we should be skeptical that the kind of spending shift he proposes can deliver the results he predicts.

Pollin is correct that the stimulus's failure to achieve a robust recovery reflects inadequate scale rather than a fundamental flaw in fiscal activism. But his specific proposal to redirect $330 billion annually from military and fossil fuel spending toward education and clean energy underestimates how difficult such a reallocation would be to enact. In early 2011, with Republicans having gained significant seats in the midterm elections and the political discourse shifting toward deficit concerns, the likelihood of Congress approving a major new spending program—let alone cuts to defense—appears slim. Pollin writes as though the main obstacle is merely articulating a compelling vision, but the political economy of budget allocation involves entrenched interests and constituencies with resources to defend their positions.

The Swedish model that Pollin champions offers an important theoretical insight, but his own evidence ultimately undermines its applicability. He acknowledges that Sweden abandoned its commitment to full employment in the early 1990s under pressure from European integration and globalization. If the Swedish model proved vulnerable to these forces, what makes us think it could survive in the much larger and more globally exposed U.S. economy? Pollin suggests adapting Swedish principles to American conditions, but he does not explain how the institutional preconditions—powerful unions willing to moderate wage demands, a political consensus supporting full employment over price stability—could be rebuilt in a country where those conditions have eroded for decades.

There is also a tension in Pollin's argument about labor's role. He calls for workers and unions to accept wage restraint as part of a bargain to maintain full employment, drawing on the Swedish experience. But this assumes that unions have the capacity to deliver such restraint on behalf of workers. In the United States of 2011, private sector union membership has fallen to historic lows, and the political environment is hostile to collective bargaining. Without union density, there is no mechanism to enforce the kind of coordinated wage moderation that made the Swedish model work. Pollin asks labor to participate in managing inflation, but gives little guidance on how labor could rebuild its own organizational strength in the interim.

Pollin's dismissal of the natural rate theory deserves scrutiny as well. He attributes the rise of Friedmanite economics to a misreading of 1970s inflation, arguing that oil shocks, not labor market tightness, drove the inflationary spiral. But even if the oil shocks were the proximate cause, the experience of the 1970s did demonstrate that economies could face simultaneous inflation and stagnation—a phenomenon the Phillips Curve framework struggled to explain. The intellectual failure of Keynesian economics in that era reflected not just a misinterpretation of events but genuine difficulties in managing aggregate demand. Pollin's confidence that we can now manage these tensions better seems optimistic given that the basic challenge—preventing wage-price spirals while maintaining high employment—remains unresolved.

Finally, Pollin frames full employment as a moral imperative, but this framing may be counterproductive in the current political environment. Arguments framed primarily in moral terms often fail to persuade those who start from different premises about the proper scope of government. A more effective case might emphasize the economic costs of unemployment—both the direct loss of output and the human capital deterioration that occurs during prolonged spells out of work. These economic arguments do not replace the moral case, but they may build broader coalitions for action.

None of this diminishes the urgency of the jobs crisis or the need for creative thinking about solutions. But moving from diagnosis to prescription requires confronting political and institutional realities that Pollin's essay treats too lightly.

Pollin's Diagnosis Is Right, but His Cure Underestimates the Money Problem

Robert Pollin has written a bracing and humane essay, and on the central moral point he is correct: an economy that consigns thirty million people to idleness or underemployment is not a decent economy, and "full employment" deserves to return to the center of policy debate. Where I want to push back is on the macroeconomic plumbing. Pollin treats inflation control largely as a matter of wage restraint, corporatist bargaining, and sectoral reallocation. He says relatively little about the institution that actually decides, day to day, whether the United States operates near full employment: the Federal Reserve.

Consider the recent past. The 1990s episode Pollin cites approvingly — unemployment dipping below 4 percent, wages finally rising for non-supervisory workers, poverty falling — did not happen because the labor movement won a wage-restraint compact or because Washington redirected hundreds of billions toward education and clean energy. It happened because Alan Greenspan, against the advice of much of his own staff, refused to raise interest rates when conventional Phillips Curve models said he should. Greenspan suspected (correctly) that productivity growth had accelerated, and he let the labor market run hot. That single discretionary choice did more for American workers in the late 1990s than any piece of legislation passed in that decade.

The lesson is uncomfortable for both left and right. For the right, it shows that the "natural rate" is not a fixed number engraved on a stone tablet — estimates that put NAIRU at 6 percent in the mid-1990s were wrong by at least two percentage points. For the left, it shows that even in the absence of stronger unions or industrial policy, monetary policy alone can deliver something close to full employment, at least temporarily. Pollin's essay barely engages with this. He mentions the Fed once, almost dismissively, as something whose authority over inflation should be shared with labor.

But the Fed's mandate is, on paper, already a dual mandate: maximum employment and stable prices. The problem is that since Paul Volcker, the institution has interpreted that mandate asymmetrically, treating any inflation above 2 percent as a five-alarm fire while tolerating 9.5 percent unemployment as a regrettable side effect of "structural" forces. If we want full employment, the first and cheapest reform is to insist that the Fed actually weight both halves of its mandate equally. We could even go further and adopt a nominal GDP target, which would mechanically commit the central bank to easing when nominal spending collapsed — as it did catastrophically in 2008–09.

This brings me to my second worry about Pollin's essay: the deficit framing. He notes, almost apologetically, that the stimulus produced $1.4 trillion deficits "of a magnitude the United States hasn't seen since World War II." This concedes too much rhetorical ground to the austerity camp. The relevant question is not the size of the deficit but whether the economy is operating near its productive capacity. With unemployment at 9.5 percent, factories idle, and the ten-year Treasury yielding around 3 percent, the United States is screaming for more public borrowing, not less. Pollin knows this — he calls for "new rounds of major job-generating measures" — but by treating the deficit as a problem to be managed rather than a non-issue at the zero lower bound, he leaves himself rhetorically exposed.

Third, I think Pollin's enthusiasm for sectoral reallocation, while attractive on its own terms, gets the causality backward when it comes to employment. Shifting $330 billion from the Pentagon and oil companies to schools and wind turbines may well create 4.8 million jobs in a slack economy. But in a full-employment economy, those workers would have to come from somewhere else — they would be bid away from other jobs, putting upward pressure on wages and prices. The 4.8 million figure is a slack-economy multiplier; it is not a sustainable feature of a steady-state full-employment economy. Pollin's own essay implicitly acknowledges this when he admits that "skill shortages" and "inflationary pressures" would have to be "managed creatively." This is doing an enormous amount of work in one sentence.

The clean-energy and education arguments stand on their own merits — climate, human capital, the moral worth of teaching — and Pollin should make those arguments without overloading them with the additional burden of being the country's primary employment engine. That engine is, and will remain, aggregate demand management plus a well-functioning financial system.

Fourth, on Sweden: Pollin is honest that the Swedish model "largely abandoned" full employment in the 1990s, and he attributes this to EU accession and globalization. Fair enough. But this points to a deeper problem with corporatist bargaining models. They depend on encompassing unions, social-democratic governments, and a degree of cultural homogeneity that no longer exists even in Sweden, let alone in a continental, fractious, multi-ethnic, weak-labor United States where union density has fallen below 12 percent. Pollin acknowledges this and says American labor should "design a workable full-employment program" anyway, but he doesn't tell us how a labor movement representing less than one-eighth of the workforce is supposed to do that.

None of this is meant to dismiss the essay. Pollin's moral framing is exactly right, and his insistence that involuntary unemployment is the central scandal of the present moment cannot be repeated often enough. But if we want to actually get to full employment from here, we need to start with the institution whose decisions most directly determine the unemployment rate — the Federal Reserve — and demand that it stop treating 5 or 6 percent unemployment as acceptable. Everything else, including the worthy reallocation Pollin proposes, follows more easily once monetary policy is willing to let the labor market breathe.

The Forgotten Tool: A Federal Job Guarantee

Robert Pollin's essay does many things well. It restores full employment to its proper place as a moral and economic priority. It demolishes the lazy story that immigrants or trade deficits are responsible for the jobs crisis. It correctly identifies neoliberalism, not globalization per se, as the policy regime that broke the postwar bargain. But for an essay titled "Back to Full Employment," there is a glaring omission: Pollin never seriously discusses the policy that would most directly deliver full employment, which is a federal job guarantee.

The idea is straightforward. The federal government commits to hiring, at a fixed wage with benefits, any adult who wants to work and cannot find a private-sector job. The work is useful — care for the elderly, environmental restoration, tutoring, infrastructure maintenance, public art, community gardens, the kinds of things market provision systematically underprovides. The wage acts as an effective minimum for the entire economy, since no private employer can pay less than the guarantee and still attract workers. When the private economy is booming, the guarantee program shrinks because workers are pulled into private jobs; when the private economy slumps, it expands automatically, providing a powerful counter-cyclical buffer.

The intellectual lineage here is venerable. Hyman Minsky proposed it for decades. William Vickrey, the Nobel laureate, endorsed it. The Argentine Jefes program in 2002–03 implemented something very much like it in response to that country's economic collapse, with measurable success in reducing extreme poverty and stabilizing communities. Pavlina Tcherneva, Randall Wray, and Stephanie Kelton have developed the modern American version in considerable detail. None of these names appear in Pollin's essay.

Why does this matter? Because Pollin's proposed mechanism — reallocating $330 billion from the Pentagon and fossil fuels to education and clean energy — has two weaknesses that a job guarantee does not.

First, it is indirect. Pollin estimates 4.8 million new jobs from his reallocation, which would bring unemployment from a hypothetical 7 percent down to about 3.9 percent. That's a great outcome, but it depends on a long chain of assumptions: that Congress will pass the reallocation, that the spending will flow into the predicted sectors with the predicted multipliers, that the newly created jobs will be matched to currently unemployed workers, that skills will transfer, that geographic mismatches will sort themselves out. Each link in the chain can break. A job guarantee, by contrast, hires people directly, where they live, at a wage they can count on, with no middleman.

Second, it does not solve the problem at the margin. Even if Pollin's plan worked exactly as advertised, you would still have several million people involuntarily unemployed and several million more underemployed. Their fate would still depend on whether the macroeconomy ran hot enough to absorb them. A job guarantee, by definition, leaves no involuntary unemployment. The "reserve army" that Marx, Kalecki, and Pollin all rightly identify as the source of capital's bargaining advantage is dissolved at the root, because no worker is forced to compete for survival.

Pollin worries — correctly — about Kalecki's insight that full employment threatens capital's political control. A job guarantee is the most direct realization of Kalecki's nightmare: workers would always have an outside option. Capital would lose much of its disciplinary power. This is exactly why a job guarantee will be politically difficult, but it is also why it would actually work, where weaker measures fail.

The standard objection is inflation. If everyone is guaranteed a job at, say, $12 an hour with benefits, won't this drive up wages throughout the economy and ignite an inflationary spiral? Here Pollin's Swedish example is actually instructive in ways he does not develop. The Swedish model achieved 2.1 percent unemployment with manageable inflation precisely because the job-creation mechanism included an active labor market policy that funneled workers into productive uses, combined with wage coordination. A U.S. job guarantee, by setting a fixed wage rather than chasing private-sector wages upward, functions as an inflation anchor, not an accelerant. The wage floor is set; it does not bid against itself.

A second objection is fiscal: where will the money come from? But this is precisely backward. The United States issues its own currency. The constraint on a job guarantee is not finance but real resources — workers, materials, energy. At 16.5 percent broad unemployment, real resources are precisely what we have in surplus. Funding the program is the easy part. The deficit hawks Pollin (rightly) criticizes will of course object, but they would object to anything.

I should be clear that the job guarantee is complementary to Pollin's proposals, not a replacement. The clean-energy transition is essential for reasons that go far beyond employment. Better-funded schools are essential. Cutting the Pentagon's bloat is essential. But the through-line that ties an actual full-employment policy together — that says "no one who wants to work will be denied a job" — is the guarantee. Without it, "full employment" remains a target we aim at, rather than a right we deliver.

Pollin closes by asking whether there is "the political will, in the United States, to fight for something as basic as the right to a decent job." It is a good question. But fighting for "something" is much easier when that something is concretely specified. A job guarantee — a real, administrable, federal-budget-line program with a wage and benefits package and an enrollment office — is the kind of thing people can rally for, vote for, and demand. "Reallocate Pentagon spending toward education and clean energy with a Swedish-style corporatist bargaining structure" is, by comparison, a graduate seminar.

If we want full employment, let's name the policy that delivers it.

The Inflation Problem Pollin Wants to Wish Away

I admire Robert Pollin's commitment to the unemployed, and I share his disgust with the cavalier way mainstream economics has come to accept 5 or 6 percent unemployment as "natural." But I want to register a sharp dissent from the way his essay handles inflation. Pollin treats the 1970s as a kind of forgivable accident — "the failure of the advanced capitalist economies… to contain inflation in the 1970s" was caused, he tells us, by "the two oil price shocks," and not by low unemployment or the policies that pursued it. This is too easy. It lets the full-employment agenda off the hook for what was its most damaging real-world failure, and it underestimates how much harder the inflation problem will be to manage in a globalized, financialized, deunionized American economy.

Let me take the 1970s argument first. It is true that oil shocks contributed materially to the inflation of 1973–75 and 1979–81. But inflation began rising well before either shock. The U.S. CPI inflation rate had climbed from 1.3 percent in 1964 to 5.9 percent by 1970 — before OPEC was a household word. The proximate causes were Vietnam war spending stacked on top of Great Society spending without offsetting tax increases, combined with a Federal Reserve that accommodated the spending. This was textbook demand-pull inflation under low unemployment. The oil shocks made things much worse and added a supply-side dimension, but the inflation was already chronic. The Phillips Curve worked, in the bad direction, for nearly a decade before it broke.

What did the Phillips Curve "breaking" mean? It meant that once inflation expectations became embedded — once workers, firms, and lenders all assumed that prices would rise 6 or 8 percent every year — the unemployment cost of getting inflation back down was brutal. Volcker's recession in 1981–82 pushed unemployment to nearly 11 percent. Millions of manufacturing jobs disappeared, and many never came back. The political backlash against the Democratic Party that resulted from this episode is arguably still shaping American politics thirty years later. To wave this away as a misreading of "the primary cause of high inflation in the 1970s" is to ignore the actual lived experience of two generations of American workers.

Now: is inflation really a serious risk today, with unemployment at 9.5 percent and the CPI barely above zero? Of course not. Pollin and I agree that the immediate problem is grossly insufficient demand, and that worrying about inflation in 2011 is like worrying about sunburn during a blizzard. But Pollin's essay is not framed as a short-run stimulus argument. It is framed as a long-run case for full employment as a policy regime, drawing analogies to Sweden in the 1960s and the United States in the late 1960s. And in a long-run regime, inflation control is the central technical question, the question on which everything else depends. Pollin gives it about three pages, mostly devoted to Sweden, and then moves on.

The Swedish answer was a particular combination of corporatist wage bargaining, active labor market policy, and union restraint. Pollin acknowledges that this model "could not be transplanted intact into the U.S. economy today, since the current U.S. labor movement is far less powerful than the Swedish movement of the 1960s–1970s." Indeed: U.S. private-sector unionization is around 7 percent, compared with Swedish unionization north of 70 percent in the period in question. The mechanism Sweden used to control inflation under full employment is unavailable to us. Pollin then says "the U.S. labor movement should take it upon itself to design a workable full-employment program today." But what is the design? The essay does not say.

Without a credible inflation-control mechanism, a serious push for sub-4-percent unemployment in the United States runs significant risks. Consider how the economy now actually behaves. Real estate, commodity, and asset-price bubbles have replaced general consumer-goods inflation as the principal manifestation of excess demand. We have just lived through two of them — dot-com in the late 1990s and housing in the 2000s — and the late-1990s episode that Pollin praises was itself bubble-driven, a point he concedes in passing. The "good" late-1990s low unemployment was unsustainable not because consumer prices were rising too fast but because asset prices were. A genuine full-employment policy would have to grapple with the fact that financial bubbles are the new wage-price spiral, and that the tools for popping bubbles without crashing the labor market are not well developed.

Pollin gestures at this when he calls for "financial-market regulations and incentives intended to force credit markets away from hyper-speculative practices." Good. But this is a separate, enormous policy area, and the gesture is hand-waved through.

Then there is globalization. Pollin argues, optimistically, that "we can approach full employment with rising wages even after allowing for current levels of global integration, immigration, and trade deficits." Maybe. But the threat-effect mechanism he himself describes — the credible threat that employers will outsource — is precisely what holds down wage inflation in tradable sectors. Take that threat away, raise wages aggressively, and you accelerate exactly the offshoring the policy is trying to forestall, with corresponding declines in domestic employment. Pollin cannot have it both ways: either offshoring threats are real and constraining (in which case wage pressure won't show up much in inflation, but won't show up much in wages either), or they aren't (in which case full employment will produce wage inflation that will need to be controlled by something).

I am not arguing against the full-employment goal. I am arguing that we need to be honest about how hard it is. The Swedes succeeded for a generation through an institutional infrastructure that took a century to build and that they themselves abandoned when conditions changed. Pollin's essay reads, despite his caveats, as if we can have the benefits of that model without the institutional preconditions. We can't. Either we rebuild American labor — a multi-decade project — or we find a fundamentally different inflation-control mechanism, perhaps based on monetary policy plus financial regulation plus tax-based incomes policy of some kind. Either way, the work is just beginning.

Pollin asks whether Americans have the political will to fight for full employment. The harder question is whether the economics profession has the intellectual will to figure out, honestly, how full employment could actually be sustained.

Pollin Is Right — and Doesn't Go Far Enough

Reading Robert Pollin's essay is bracing precisely because it is one of the few mainstream-adjacent pieces of policy writing in 2010 that takes mass unemployment seriously as a moral catastrophe. Thirty million people. Twice the combined populations of New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago. We are now nearly two years into the Obama administration, and the official conversation in Washington has moved on to deficit reduction. Pollin's reminder that 16.5 percent of American workers are unemployed or underemployed is exactly what is needed.

But I want to push the argument further, because Pollin, for all his radicalism on full employment, remains too cautious in his diagnosis of why we are not getting it. He treats the failure as ideological and political — the rise of neoliberalism, the weakness of American labor, Federal Reserve culture. All of this is true. But there is a deeper structural fact that the essay only glances at, and it is this: the American capitalist class has, for forty years, organized its political power specifically to prevent full employment, because full employment threatens its profits and its power. This is Kalecki's argument, which Pollin cites and then sets aside almost too politely.

Kalecki's 1943 essay "Political Aspects of Full Employment" is one of the most prescient documents of the twentieth century. He observed that businessmen oppose full employment not because they don't understand the Keynesian arithmetic — they understand it perfectly well — but because they understand the politics. Sustained full employment changes the balance of power on the shop floor. It changes the balance of power in collective bargaining. It changes the balance of power between renters and landlords, debtors and creditors, workers and managers. In a tight labor market, the boss who treats people badly loses them. In a slack labor market, the boss who treats people badly knows they have nowhere to go. Capital, as a class, prefers a slack market. Always. Everywhere.

This is not a left-wing fantasy. The minutes of Federal Reserve meetings over the past several decades, the editorial pages of the Wall Street Journal, the lobbying record of the Business Roundtable, the campaign-finance flows that punish politicians who push too hard on labor issues — all of it lines up. When unemployment falls below 5 percent, the financial press fills with warnings about "wage pressures" and "overheating." When unemployment is at 9 percent, the same press writes about the urgent need to cut social spending and restrain the deficit. The asymmetry is so consistent that it cannot be coincidence.

Pollin acknowledges this in his peroration: "Putting an end to neoliberalism will require nothing less than an epoch-defining reallocation of political power away from the interests of big business and Wall Street and toward the middle class, working people, and the poor." Yes. But he then offers as his concrete program a reallocation of $330 billion across four sectors, plus financial regulation, plus implied union revival. Will this reallocation by itself produce the necessary shift in political power? I doubt it. The reallocation can only be enacted by a political coalition that has already won the power struggle. Otherwise it won't get through Congress.

So what does the power struggle actually look like? Here are some directions Pollin's essay points toward but does not fully develop.

First, we need a massive rebuilding of American labor. Not just the existing AFL-CIO unions, but new forms of worker organization — sectoral bargaining, works councils, worker-cooperative networks, alt-labor organizations like the Restaurant Opportunities Centers and Domestic Workers United. The labor-law reform that died in 2009–10 with the failure of the Employee Free Choice Act needs to come back, in stronger form, and to extend to the millions of workers (independent contractors, gig workers, domestic workers, agricultural workers) currently outside the NLRA's protections entirely.

Second, we need to break up the banks. Pollin alludes to this — "financial-regulatory regime that channels private funding toward productive, employment-generating activities" — but he soft-pedals. The financial sector at its current size and political power is structurally incompatible with full employment, both because it diverts capital from productive uses and because it serves as the chief political enforcer of austerity discipline. The financial sector's share of corporate profits roughly tripled between 1980 and 2007, and this growth was directly correlated with the stagnation of wages. Either finance is brought back under firm regulatory control — Glass-Steagall, leverage limits, a financial transactions tax, public banking options — or it will continue to dominate macroeconomic policy.

Third, we need to think about workplace democracy as well as employment levels. Even if we achieved Pollin's 3.9 percent unemployment goal, an American worker would still spend most of their waking hours under conditions of authoritarian control. The boss decides everything: hours, breaks, what tasks you do, whether you can talk during work, often whether you can pee. Full employment in a context of workplace despotism is better than mass unemployment in the same context, but it is not full freedom. Worker representation on boards (the German model), expanded worker cooperatives, and stronger workplace rights deserve to be on the agenda alongside the employment level.

Fourth, and most fundamentally, we need to stop thinking of "the economy" as a neutral machine that policy adjusts and start thinking of it as a contested social institution. Pollin almost gets there — the Marx and Kalecki sections of his essay are excellent — but he keeps returning to a technocratic vocabulary of sectoral multipliers and labor-market interventions. The point of full employment is not that it is technically optimal; the point is that it is morally required, and politically possible only if working people organize themselves to demand it.

Pollin ends by asking whether Americans have "the political will to fight for something as basic as the right to a decent job." It is the right question. But political will is not a free-floating quantity. It is built by organizations, in struggles, over years. The right asks the same question constantly — "Do Americans have the will to cut taxes / restrict abortion / deregulate finance?" — and it builds, year after year, the institutions that answer "yes." The left needs to do the same for the right to a job, which means starting now, with the people we have, where they actually live and work.

Full employment is achievable. It will not arrive as a gift from a sympathetic technocracy. It will be taken.

A Conservative Defense of Some of What Pollin Says

Robert Pollin will not expect a sympathetic hearing from someone like me, but on a few of his central points I find myself nodding along, and I want to explain where I agree and where I think he goes badly wrong. I write as someone who believes in markets, in price stability, and in the discipline that fiscal restraint imposes on government. None of these commitments is incompatible with caring about whether ordinary people can find work.

Start with what is right in the essay. Pollin is correct that 9.5 percent unemployment is a national disgrace and that the political class in Washington has, with remarkable speed, lost interest in the problem. He is correct that work is not merely an economic transaction but a source of meaning, dignity, and social connection — points that conservatives, of all people, should be the first to insist upon. The Catholic social tradition, on which much modern conservatism draws, treats labor as a fundamental dimension of the human person; Pope John Paul II's Laborem Exercens is closer to Pollin on this point than to many free-market conservatives I know.

Pollin is also correct that immigrants are not the cause of the current unemployment crisis. The arithmetic he cites — that the immigrant share of the labor force was comparable in the late 1990s when unemployment fell below 4 percent — is dispositive. Conservatives who want to blame immigration for the jobs crisis are blaming a constant for a variable. I wish more people on my side of the aisle would acknowledge this honestly.

And Pollin is correct that we should not be panicked by the deficit at this exact moment. I am, in general, a deficit hawk. But the deficit hawk's standard argument is that government borrowing crowds out private investment and raises interest rates. In 2010, with the ten-year Treasury yielding around 3 percent, there is no plausible crowding out happening. Whatever one thinks about the long-run fiscal trajectory — and I think it is genuinely worrying — short-run austerity in the middle of a depression is bad economics, and I am willing to say so even if my political allies are not.

Now to where Pollin goes wrong.

First, the romantic invocation of Sweden. Pollin acknowledges, but quickly minimizes, the fact that Sweden itself abandoned its full-employment model in the 1990s. He attributes this to EU accession and globalization, as if these were exogenous shocks rather than responses to a system that was, by the late 1980s, generating serious problems. The Swedish model in its heyday relied on high marginal tax rates, an enormous public sector, and a degree of social and ethnic homogeneity that made wage solidarity politically possible. By the late 1980s, growth was sclerotic, the public sector was overextended, and Sweden was experiencing a banking crisis that required substantial market-oriented reform. The reforms worked. Sweden today is a more market-oriented economy than the one Pollin admires, and arguably a better one for it. Holding up the 1960s–1970s Swedish model as a template ignores why even the Swedes left it behind.

Second, the indictment of "neoliberalism." This is a slippery term. If by "neoliberalism" Pollin means the deregulation of finance, I largely agree that the United States went too far in the 1990s and 2000s, and that the 2008 collapse was an indictment of that overreach. But "neoliberalism" as he uses it covers everything from free trade to welfare reform to inflation targeting, and these are not a single bundle. Free trade has lifted hundreds of millions of people in the developing world out of dire poverty. Inflation targeting brought the Great Inflation of the 1970s to an end, restoring price stability that benefits workers and retirees most of all. The pre-1979 monetary regime that Pollin nostalgically remembers ended with 13 percent inflation and 7 percent unemployment simultaneously. That is the actual track record of Keynesian demand management when allowed to run unchecked, and it is not a record worth restoring.

Third, the sectoral reallocation proposal. Pollin wants to shift $330 billion from the military and fossil fuels to education and clean energy. The military number I have no particular objection to in principle, though the politics are terrible. But the clean-energy-versus-fossil-fuel claim deserves more scrutiny than he gives it. Clean energy creates more jobs per dollar largely because clean energy is, currently, less productive per dollar — it takes more workers to produce a given quantity of energy via solar panels or wind turbines than via natural gas or coal. This is not actually a benefit. If we needed energy and nothing else mattered, we would want fewer jobs per unit of energy, because that would mean those workers were free to do something else. Counting "jobs created" as an unalloyed good leads to absurdities — we could "create jobs" by banning combine harvesters and going back to horse-drawn plows.

There are good reasons to support clean energy: carbon emissions and climate change. There are good reasons to support military restraint: the unsustainable cost of empire. Make those arguments on their merits. The "jobs per million dollars" framing is a rhetorical sleight that obscures more than it reveals.

Fourth, and most importantly, Pollin underestimates how much of the current unemployment problem is structural rather than cyclical. The 2008 collapse destroyed enormous amounts of human capital — construction workers in particular, whose skills are not easily transferred — and dropped them into a labor market that doesn't need them. Many of these workers will not return to work, regardless of macroeconomic policy. The right response is not more stimulus indefinitely; it is investment in retraining, mobility, and the kind of dynamic labor market that allows people to find new niches. Some of Pollin's proposals (more education spending) could plausibly contribute to this. Others (corporatist wage bargaining à la Sweden) would actively impede it by locking labor markets into rigidity.

What I share with Pollin is a sense that mass unemployment is a moral failure that demands a serious response. What I do not share is his diagnosis that the response should be to relitigate the social-democratic settlement of 1945–1979. That settlement broke down for reasons; we should learn from those reasons, not pretend they didn't happen.

Where Are the Women, the Caregivers, and the Public Sector?

Robert Pollin's essay covers a lot of ground, and I do not want to fault it for what it could not cover in 5,000 words. But I do want to register what is missing, because the absences shape the argument in important ways.

First, the essay barely mentions women. There is a passing reference to the late 1960s, when full employment brought "less job discrimination against women, African Americans, and other historically marginalized populations." That's it. And yet women's labor force participation is one of the largest economic transformations of the postwar period, and one of the most pertinent to a discussion of unemployment. Women's participation rose from about 33 percent in 1948 to 60 percent today. Most of this represents real liberation — women gaining economic autonomy, the ability to leave bad marriages, careers that match their talents. But some of it reflects economic necessity — the wage stagnation that Pollin documents for male workers from 1973 onward was masked, at the household level, by the addition of a second earner. The "American family income" did not collapse the way the male wage did, because women went to work.

This matters for the full-employment debate in several ways. First, the appropriate measure of "full employment" today must include women, not just men. The 1960s model that Pollin reaches back toward was implicitly a male-breadwinner model. We cannot and should not return to that. Second, the kinds of jobs that have been growing in the American economy — healthcare, education, retail, food service — are disproportionately filled by women and disproportionately undervalued in compensation. Full employment that left those jobs at their current low wages would not be a great victory. Third, the work of social reproduction — childcare, eldercare, household management — is overwhelmingly done by women, frequently unpaid, and is largely invisible in conventional labor statistics. A serious full-employment agenda has to engage this work, either by socializing it (publicly funded childcare, eldercare) or by valuing it (paid family leave, fair compensation for care workers, social security credits for caregiving years).

Pollin's reallocation proposal directs new money to "education and clean energy." Education is good, but the proposal as described — smaller class sizes, more financial aid, building improvements — is heavily K–12 and college-focused. What about early childhood education and childcare, where the United States lags far behind every other rich country? A full-funded universal pre-K and childcare system would do more for women's labor force participation, for child development, and yes, for employment (childcare workers, teachers) than reducing average class size from 23 to 19. It would also unlock the labor of women currently kept out of the workforce by the cost of childcare, which is now greater than college tuition in many states.

Second, the essay underplays the public sector. Pollin treats "education" as a sector to be invested in, but he frames the investment as flowing through ordinary fiscal channels — federal money to states, money to school districts, etc. The deeper structural question is that the United States runs an unusually small public sector by rich-country standards. Public employment in the U.S. is about 16 percent of total employment, compared with 25–30 percent in Scandinavian countries and around 20–22 percent in continental Europe. Much of the gap is in exactly the sectors Pollin highlights: healthcare, education, social services. If U.S. public employment as a share of the workforce were merely at the OECD average, we would have several million more public-sector jobs.

This points to a structural answer to the unemployment problem that Pollin gestures at but does not embrace. Rather than (or in addition to) shifting funding between sectors, simply expand the public sector. Hire more teachers, more public health nurses, more parks and recreation staff, more librarians, more social workers, more inspectors of all kinds. The work is needed; the workers are available. The reason it doesn't happen is not technical but ideological — the United States has, since the 1980s, treated public-sector employment as suspect, parasitic, and economically inferior to private-sector employment.

The current recession has actually been a public-sector contraction. State and local governments have shed several hundred thousand jobs since 2009, almost entirely because of revenue shortfalls and balanced-budget requirements. Federal aid to states would have prevented most of these layoffs. We laid off teachers, librarians, and bus drivers in the middle of a depression because of an artificial fiscal constraint. This is policy malpractice on an enormous scale. Pollin's essay, focused on the longer-term reallocation question, does not register the immediate public-sector emergency with the urgency it deserves.

Third, the essay treats globalization and immigration in a single brief paragraph each, in a way that, while correct on the headline conclusions, misses the gendered and racialized dimensions of both. The jobs most exposed to outsourcing are not evenly distributed across the workforce; the jobs most filled by immigrants are not the ones outsourcing pressures most directly affect. A real analysis of who gains and who loses from globalization and immigration policy needs to disaggregate by race, gender, sector, and region. The blunt aggregate claim "globalization is not the problem" is technically defensible but politically inert. The problem is which workers in which places are bearing the costs of how the U.S. has chosen to integrate with the world economy.

Fourth, and finally, Pollin's framing of full employment as "decent jobs" — the move he makes against the Bolivian Ministry of Planning economists who counted street hawkers as employed — is exactly right, but it deserves more development. What makes a job "decent"? Pay above some threshold, yes. Benefits, certainly. But also: hours that allow a life outside work, schedules that are predictable enough to permit caregiving, safety from harassment and physical danger, dignity at work. By any of these measures, large numbers of currently "employed" Americans hold "indecent" jobs. The fast-food worker on an unpredictable schedule, the retail worker without benefits, the Amazon warehouse worker on a brutal pace, the home healthcare aide making $9 an hour with no overtime protection — these are technically employed people, but they are not what most of us mean by the right to a decent job.

A full-employment agenda for the twenty-first century has to be not just about quantity of jobs but about the structural transformation of what work means in America. Pollin opens the door to this question and then walks past it. We should walk through.

The Climate Connection Pollin Almost Makes

The most interesting and underdeveloped argument in Robert Pollin's essay is the claim that full employment and decarbonization can be joined as a single political project. He says this early — "creating a full-employment economy can be joined effectively with another fundamental policy aim: ending our dependence on fossil fuels and creating an economy powered by clean energy" — and returns to it when proposing his $330 billion reallocation. But the strategic logic of the alliance gets less attention than it deserves. I want to spell it out, because I think it is the most promising path forward for both causes.

Climate change is the defining long-term threat of our era. The science is unambiguous: without rapid, aggressive emissions reductions, we face temperature increases that will displace tens or hundreds of millions of people, flood coastal cities, accelerate species extinction, and disrupt agricultural systems on which billions depend. The minimum stabilization target Pollin cites — 80 percent reduction below 2000 levels by 2050 — requires, in essence, the wholesale rebuilding of the energy system in less than forty years. Every power plant, every building's heating system, every car, every industrial process must be reimagined. This is the largest physical undertaking in the history of the human species.

Meanwhile, the American economy is unable to put fifteen million unemployed people to work, with another fifteen million working less than they want or having given up entirely. The Federal Reserve, terrified of inflation that does not exist, sits on its hands. Congress, terrified of deficits that are entirely affordable, gestures at austerity. Tens of millions of able workers are wasted.

Climate change is not a problem about polar bears. Unemployment is not a problem about statistical aggregates. Both are problems about what kind of country, and what kind of species, we want to be. The climate-jobs nexus offers a story that connects the daily anxieties of ordinary people to the largest civilizational stakes imaginable. A Skeptic's Note on the Numbers

Let me play a slightly different role from the other commentators and focus narrowly on some of Robert Pollin's quantitative claims, because I think the policy debate would be better served by some careful skepticism about figures that, taken at face value, do an enormous amount of rhetorical work in his essay.

Claim one: the stimulus "prevented" unemployment from reaching 17 percent. Pollin cites the Blinder-Zandi study. I have looked at this study, and I find it suggestive but not as definitive as Pollin implies. Blinder and Zandi use a structural macroeconomic model — Zandi's proprietary model at Moody's Analytics — and run counterfactuals. Counterfactuals are inherently speculative. They depend on the model's specification of the multiplier, its assumptions about monetary policy response, its assumptions about state and local government behavior, and many other parameters. Different reasonable models give different answers. There are serious economists (John Taylor, Robert Barro, others) who have run their own analyses and found much smaller stimulus effects.

I am not saying Blinder and Zandi are wrong. I am saying the question is genuinely contested, and citing the upper end of credible estimates as if it were established fact misleads readers. A more honest framing would be: "Mainstream estimates suggest the stimulus prevented unemployment from rising several percentage points, though the precise magnitude is debated."

Claim two: 29 jobs per $1 million in education, 17 in clean energy, 12 in military, 5 in fossil fuels. These numbers come from Pollin's own work at the Political Economy Research Institute, using input-output methods. Input-output analysis is a venerable tool but it has well-known limitations. It assumes fixed production coefficients — that the relationships between inputs and outputs in each sector remain constant. It does not handle changes in factor prices that result from large-scale reallocation. It does not generally account for general equilibrium effects: if you suddenly demand many more clean-energy workers, their wages rise, the cost of clean-energy projects rises, and the actual jobs-per-dollar figure falls below the input-output estimate.

This matters because Pollin's policy recommendation — reallocate $330 billion per year — is precisely the kind of large-scale reallocation where these effects become serious. At the margin, his numbers are probably roughly right. At the scale he proposes, they are likely overstated, perhaps significantly.

Claim three: a 25 percent military cut returns spending to "pre-Iraq and Afghanistan levels." This is mostly accurate, though it depends on whether you measure in nominal or real terms and how you treat various supplemental appropriations. But the more important point is the political feasibility. The defense budget has constituencies in every congressional district, defense contractors with armies of lobbyists, and the institutional inertia of an enormous bureaucracy. Cutting 25 percent is not impossible but it is a multi-decade project, not a "redirect this year's budget" matter. Pollin's essay reads as if Congress could simply vote on Tuesday to make the switch. The political-economy obstacles deserve to be foregrounded, not bracketed.

Claim four: the 1990s show that "we can approach full employment with rising wages even after allowing for current levels of global integration, immigration, and trade deficits." Pollin himself admits that the 1990s expansion was bubble-driven. But the conclusion he draws — that the structural conditions are compatible with full employment — depends on whether the late-1990s tightening was a sustainable equilibrium or a temporary deviation made possible by unsustainable financial conditions. The fact that the expansion ended in a crash should give us more pause than Pollin offers. Perhaps the only way to push unemployment below 4 percent in our economy is via asset bubbles. That would be a deeply uncomfortable conclusion, but it is not ruled out by the evidence Pollin provides.

Claim five: 20–30 percent of U.S. jobs are potentially outsourceable, per Blinder's Foreign Affairs piece. This is a real and important finding. But Blinder himself emphasized that the threat-effect on wages, rather than actual mass outsourcing, was the main mechanism. Pollin uses the figure to suggest both that outsourcing is a serious problem and that it can be policy-managed. These are in tension. If 30 percent of American jobs are potentially deliverable from Bangalore for one-fifth the price, no amount of clever sectoral policy is going to prevent enormous downward wage pressure in the affected occupations. The honest answer is that we may need to accept significantly lower wages in tradable services, or significantly higher import barriers, neither of which Pollin endorses.

Claim six, the most consequential: the proposed reallocation would create 4.8 million net new jobs. Even granting the input-output coefficients, this is a gross job creation number that needs to be net of job destruction. Cutting Pentagon spending by 25 percent eliminates jobs in defense industries. Cutting fossil-fuel spending by 25 percent eliminates jobs in oil and gas. Pollin acknowledges the differential — clean energy creates 17 jobs per million, fossil fuels 5, so net jobs from this swap alone are positive — but the calculation assumes perfect labor mobility, which is precisely what we don't have. A laid-off coal miner in West Virginia does not become a solar installer in Arizona costlessly. The transitional unemployment from such a reallocation could be substantial and long-lasting, and the political coalition that would have to support the policy would have to be willing to bear these costs.

None of this means Pollin's policy direction is wrong. The case for reducing military and fossil-fuel spending in favor of education and clean energy can be made on multiple grounds — climate, geopolitics, fiscal sustainability, social investment. But the case should rest on those grounds, supported by careful uncertainty analysis, rather than on a single headline figure — 4.8 million new jobs — that is more precise than the underlying analysis can support.

The full-employment argument deserves to be made more carefully. Honest acknowledgment of uncertainty is not a concession to the opposition; it is the precondition of being taken seriously by anyone whose mind is actually open. Pollin's broad direction is right. The numbers do not need to be presented as more certain than they are to make that case.

Robert Pollin is right to put full employment back at the center of our national conversation, and his proposal to shift spending from the military and fossil fuels toward education and clean energy is a welcome step in the right direction. Such a program would not only reduce the misery of joblessness immediately but would also build the public goods and infrastructure that a green, equitable economy requires.

Pollin’s analysis of job creation by sector is illuminating, but it reveals a fundamental limitation: even his proposed reallocation of $330 billion per year would, by his own estimates, create 4.8 million jobs and bring unemployment down to around 6.5 percent over several years. An economy that tolerates unemployment at 5 or 6 percent as “full employment” is not a decent society, especially when so many of the jobless are from communities of color, the young, and the less educated, for whom unemployment rates are often double the national average.

A direct public employment program, modeled on the WPA of the 1930s but updated for the 21st century, could close this gap. The federal government would serve as employer of last resort, offering a job at a living wage—say, $15 an hour plus health benefits—to anyone ready and willing to work. The jobs would be in areas of genuine public need: retrofitting public buildings for energy efficiency, building and maintaining mass transit, providing child and elder care, restoring parks and wetlands, staffing libraries and after-school programs, and carrying out the very education and clean-energy investments Pollin champions. A government hiring program of this sort could put millions directly to work within months, not years, and it would be targeted precisely at the places and populations suffering the highest joblessness.

Critics will scream about costs and deficits, but they ignore that unemployment itself is enormously costly. Mass unemployment depresses tax revenues, increases spending on safety-net programs, and inflicts long-term scars on workers and communities—losses that economists like Alan Blinder estimate run into hundreds of billions annually. A WPA-style program would pay for itself in part through these savings, and the multiplier effects of putting paychecks into the pockets of those most likely to spend them would boost private demand and tax receipts further. Moreover, the federal government can borrow at historically low interest rates—around 3 percent on 10-year Treasury bonds—and the real risk is not inflation but a Japanese-style lost decade of deflation and stagnation if we fail to act.

The deeper objection is that such a program would crowd out private employment or create a permanent class of government dependents. But the historical experience with the WPA suggests the opposite: by putting a floor under wages and aggregate demand, it helped to pull the entire economy out of depression. The program was temporary and flexible, expanding in downturns and shrinking as private employment recovered. Today, with the private sector still shedding state and local government jobs and adding only a trickle of new positions, there is no risk of overheating. A public jobs guarantee would instead set a de facto minimum wage and benefit standard that private employers would have to meet, ending the race to the bottom that has driven wages down even as productivity soared.

Pollin rightly warns of the political obstacles, but they are not insurmountable. The original WPA was born of a crisis even more severe than today’s and was pushed through by a mobilized labor movement and mass protests of the unemployed. That kind of pressure is again necessary, and a direct jobs guarantee is a demand that can unite the jobless, labor unions, community organizations, and the climate movement. Without it, Pollin’s noble vision of full employment will remain a distant dream as millions continue to suffer needlessly. If we are serious about the right to a decent job, the government should become the employer of last resort—not as a permanent feature of a transformed economy, but as a bridge to a future where private and public investment together provide abundant, high-quality work for all.

Robert Pollin’s manifesto for full employment is an ambitious throwback to a bygone era of Keynesian demand management, but it is founded on a fundamental misunderstanding of how labor markets actually function. The tragic unemployment in the wake of the Great Recession is not a problem of insufficient spending or a lack of federal jobs programs; it is largely a consequence of labor market rigidities, perverse incentives, and a prolonged policy-induced uncertainty that has hamstrung private investment. If we truly want a high-employment economy, the path is not to double down on government intervention but to unleash the dynamism of the private sector by removing the barriers that prevent wages from adjusting and workers from moving into productive jobs.

Pollin’s history of economic thought is revealing. He sets up a clash between the Keynesian view, which he endorses, and the Friedmanite natural-rate framework, which he caricatures. But the Friedmanite insight—that unemployment largely reflects voluntary choices and structural impediments, not a chronic shortage of aggregate demand—is more relevant than ever. Milton Friedman’s concept of the natural rate of unemployment is not a claim that unemployment is always zero; it is the recognition that, in a dynamic economy, there are frictions: workers between jobs, skill mismatches, geographic immobility, and the inevitable dislocation that comes from technological change and global competition. The natural rate of unemployment in the United States today, given the depth of the housing bust and the mismatch between the skills of the long-term unemployed and the jobs available, is probably somewhere between 6 and 8 percent. Trying to force unemployment below that level through massive government spending and sectoral reallocation would lead not to permanent prosperity but to accelerating inflation and a misallocation of resources that would ultimately destroy jobs.

Pollin dismisses the 1970s inflation as a result of oil shocks, but the evidence shows that the wage-price spiral was already in motion before the 1973 embargo, driven by overly accommodative monetary and fiscal policy aimed at pushing unemployment below its natural rate. Even if the input-output math is correct in a static sense, it ignores the general-equilibrium effects: the jobs created in education and green energy would be offset by the destruction of jobs in other sectors as resources are forcibly reallocated by political fiat rather than market signals. The prospect of even larger deficits would push up long-term interest rates, crowd out private investment, and further delay the recovery.

The Obama administration’s stimulus may have blunted the recession, but its aftereffects—an explosion of new financial, health-care, and environmental regulations, combined with the scheduled expiration of the Bush tax cuts and the looming costs of the new health-care law—have created a cloud of uncertainty. Small businesses, which generate the majority of new jobs, are sitting on record amounts of cash because they are terrified of what comes next. Reducing the corporate tax rate, simplifying the tax code, reining in the growth of entitlements, and signaling a credible commitment to fiscal discipline would do far more to restore the animal spirits Keynes talked about than any government “jobs program.”

Construction workers, who made up a disproportionate share of the job losses, cannot simply retrain overnight to build wind turbines or teach elementary school. The fastest way to reduce unemployment would be to accelerate the foreclosure process, allow the market to clear, and free up labor mobility. Government programs to modify mortgages have only prolonged the pain.

The correct policy response is not to wall off trade but to accept that the wage structure must adjust, and to remove the floor of minimum wages, occupational licensing, and union protections that prevent it. In a flexible market, everyone who is willing to work at the market-clearing wage can find a job. The path back to full employment lies through market flexibility, not government central planning.

Beyond Reform: Why Capitalism Cannot Deliver Full Employment

Robert Pollin’s eloquent plea for full employment is a courageous intervention in a public debate that has been narrowed by decades of neoliberal orthodoxy. As much as we might wish otherwise, full employment, in the meaningful sense Pollin invokes, is incompatible with the capitalist mode of production itself. The historical experience he cites, including the rise and fall of the Swedish model, demonstrates not that full employment is a matter of political will, but that capital will always and everywhere subvert full employment when it threatens profitability and class dominance.

Pollin’s own account of the debate between Marx, Keynes, Friedman, and Kalecki provides the theoretical tools for this critique, yet he does not follow them to their logical conclusion. Marx’s concept of the reserve army of labor is central: capitalism, by its very nature, generates a fluctuating surplus population of unemployed and underemployed workers, which serves to discipline labor, keep wages in check, and secure the extraction of surplus value. For Marx, full employment is not a stable equilibrium but a transient moment of high accumulation that inevitably brings a profit squeeze and a subsequent crisis of investment, restoring the reserve army. Pollin acknowledges this point through Kalecki’s political argument that capitalists oppose full employment because it emboldens workers and threatens their control. But Kalecki went further, arguing that even with technically sound Keynesian policies, capitalists would use their political power to block genuine full employment. The solution? A fascist state that could force workers to work and suppress their demands, but that is hardly the “decent society” Pollin envisions.

Pollin attempts to circumvent the Kalecki problem with the Swedish model, where strong unions voluntarily restrained wages in exchange for a solidaristic full-employment policy. But as he himself notes, the Swedish model was abandoned in the early 1990s, precisely when capital went on the offensive. With the liberalization of capital markets and the acceleration of globalization, Swedish business no longer needed to tolerate a high-employment compact that constrained profits. The threat of capital flight, the pressures of European Union convergence criteria, and a concerted ideological assault by business interests dismantled the Meidner-Rehn framework. The lesson is not that the Swedish model was a transferable template but that capital’s power to exit and to shape the rules of the game makes any national full-employment project vulnerable to sabotage.

His plan to shift $330 billion from the military and fossil fuels to education and clean energy assumes that the political system can override the interests of the most powerful sectors of American capital: the military-industrial complex, the oil majors, and the financial institutions that profit from both. Even if a political movement could achieve such a shift, the response of the affected industries would be fierce: capital flight, investment strikes, capital market turmoil, and a relentless campaign to reverse the policy. And the green jobs created—wind turbine technicians, retrofitters, bus drivers—would immediately face downward pressure on wages as capital seeks to reimpose labor discipline. Pollin’s hope that labor unions could play a leading role in managing inflation ignores that union density in the private sector is under 7 percent, and the last attempt at labor law reform, the Employee Free Choice Act, died in a Democratic-controlled Congress under ferocious business opposition.

Moreover, the entire framework of managing aggregate demand to sustain full employment assumes a closed national economy with a relatively autonomous state. In the globalized world Pollin acknowledges, any attempt to run a high-pressure labor market will quickly run up against a balance-of-payments constraint. Imports will surge, the dollar will weaken, and financial markets will demand higher interest rates or impose capital flight, forcing the Fed to jack up rates and induce a recession to restore “confidence.” The experience of François Mitterrand’s France in the early 1980s is instructive: a Keynesian stimulus in one country, in a world of mobile capital, ended in austerity and the abandonment of the program. The neoliberal globalization Pollin criticizes has been constructed precisely to prevent the kind of autonomous full-employment policy he advocates.

This is not an argument for despair, but for a clear-eyed recognition that the fight for full employment is inseparable from the fight against capitalism itself. The right to a decent job requires a transformation of property relations and economic democracy far beyond sectoral spending shifts. We need public ownership of the commanding heights of the economy, particularly in finance and energy, so that investment decisions can be made on the basis of social need rather than private profit. We need a radically shorter workweek to share the available work, and a robust welfare state to decommodify labor and thereby weaken the reserve army effect. And we need to break decisively with the logic of endless growth, recognizing that a full-employment economy on a finite planet is an ecological impossibility.

Pollin’s essay is valuable as a spur to debate, but it ultimately offers a technical fix where a systemic challenge is required. The Great Recession has demonstrated beyond doubt that neoliberalism is a dead end. It is time to build a movement that demands not just full employment within capitalism, but a socialism that makes decent work and a livable planet its first principles.

The Wall Street banks that caused the crisis remain too big to fail and are still siphoning resources out of the real economy through predatory lending, excessive fees, and shareholder demands for short-term profits. A financial transactions tax, strict capital controls, and the break-up of the mega-banks would give public authorities and unions more leverage over the allocation of credit and dampen the destructive boom-bust cycle that always punishes workers.

This is a far more powerful tool for raising wages across the board than hoping that a tight labor market alone will do the job.

Pollin is correct that the fight for full employment is fundamentally a fight against neoliberalism. Undoing it will require rebuilding the collective power of workers to demand not only jobs, but good jobs, a say in how the economy is run, and a guarantee that no one is left behind. The grand ideological struggle against neoliberalism will take a generation, not a single legislative session. For now, we must rescue what we can and build the political alliances that might one day make full employment thinkable again.

Robert Pollin is correct to insist that full employment must be a central goal of American economic policy, and his plan to boost education and clean-energy spending is a valuable contribution. But his essay fundamentally misjudges the most powerful force shaping job prospects in the United States today: the inexorable relocation of production to low-wage countries, and the trade policies that have enabled it. You cannot simply “approach full employment with rising wages after allowing for current levels of global integration,” as Pollin asserts, because that global integration has been deliberately structured to undermine labor’s bargaining power and to promote a massive trade deficit that bleeds domestic demand out of the economy. Any serious full-employment strategy must begin by reindustrializing America, managing trade, and breaking the stranglehold of global corporations that have no loyalty to the U.S. labor force.

Pollin’s own data reveal the depth of the problem, even if he draws the wrong conclusions. He notes that average wages for non-supervisory workers have fallen 7 percent since 1972 while productivity doubled. This is not a coincidence: it is the direct consequence of a corporate strategy, made possible by free-trade agreements and the open-door policies toward China, to use the threat of offshoring to suppress wages even in good times. The Blinder estimate that 20 to 30 percent of U.S. jobs could be performed in poor countries is not just a theoretical curiosity; it shapes every wage negotiation, every plant-location decision, and every investment calculation. When companies such as General Electric, Caterpillar, and IBM are building their new facilities in China and India rather than in Ohio or South Carolina, no amount of domestic spending on education and wind turbines is going to absorb the millions of displaced manufacturing workers and their communities.

Pollin points to the late 1990s, when unemployment fell below 4 percent despite a trade deficit of similar magnitude, as evidence that globalization need not prevent full employment. But the 1990s were an exceptional period, fueled by the twin bubbles of the dot-com frenzy and a housing market that had not yet crashed, and by a temporary surge in private investment that masked the long-term erosion of the industrial base. Even then, the recovery was profoundly unequal: the bulk of job growth came in low-wage services, and the manufacturing jobs that were lost in the early 1980s and early 1990s never returned. By the time the Great Recession hit, the American economy had already been hollowed out, with millions of families dependent on the bubble’s phantom wealth. To suggest that the 1990s provide a replicable model for full employment is to ignore that the underlying productive capacity of the nation was being dismantled all the while.

A genuine full-employment policy must therefore include a radical reorientation of trade policy. This means, first, a rejection of the “free trade” consensus that has dominated both parties since the 1990s. The United States should impose a surcharge on imports from countries with systematically undervalued currencies, above all China, whose manipulation of the renminbi has given it an unfair competitive advantage estimated at 25 to 40 percent. We need a managed exchange-rate system, negotiated with our major trading partners, that prevents the destructive race to the bottom in which every nation tries to export its unemployment. Without currency stability, any fiscal stimulus will be siphoned off into a rising trade deficit, as it was in 2009 and 2010.

Second, we must directly challenge the power of multinational corporations to pit workers against one another. The tax code should be rewritten to penalize companies that outsource production and reward those that invest in domestic jobs. A border-adjustable value-added tax, common in Europe, would tax imports and exempt exports, thereby removing the incentive to shift production offshore. The World Trade Organization rules that prohibit such policies should be renegotiated, and if necessary, ignored.

Third, we need an explicit industrial policy to rebuild the manufacturing sectors that provide the foundation for mass middle-class employment—not just in green energy, but in advanced machinery, transportation equipment, and high-tech components. Pollin’s job multipliers rightly highlight the domestic content of clean energy, but many of the components for wind turbines and solar panels are produced in Asia. A robust domestic-content requirement, combined with public procurement, can ensure that the green transition creates jobs in American factories, not just on construction sites.

Pollin’s fear of a currency war is misplaced. The currency skirmishes he worries about are already happening, and the United States is losing precisely because it refuses to match the aggressive industrial and trade policies of China, Germany, and other surplus nations. Accepting the current “level of global integration” as a given is to accept a permanent downward pressure on wages and a structural ceiling on employment. A high-wage, full-employment United States cannot be built on a foundation of runaway shops and chronic trade deficits. The first step toward Pollin’s vision is to bring the jobs back home.

Full Employment on a Finite Planet: The Green Contradiction

Robert Pollin deserves credit for integrating the imperative of a clean-energy transition into his full-employment program, and his job-multiplier analysis demonstrates convincingly that climate protection can be a source of abundant work. But his essay rests on a hidden assumption that is both ecologically unsustainable and ultimately at odds with his call for a decent society: the assumption that the American economy must continue to grow in order to absorb its labor force. Pollin’s full employment is the full employment of a growth machine, and on a planet already in ecological overshoot, that machine is pushing us toward catastrophe. A truly moral and realistic employment policy must break the link between jobs and growth, embracing a steady-state economy with shared work, shorter hours, and a redefinition of prosperity itself.

Pollin’s own numbers point toward the contradiction. He proposes to cut fossil-fuel spending by $165 billion per year as part of a climate stabilization effort, but he acknowledges that to meet an 80 percent reduction target by 2050 we would need to cut far more. Yet the entire thrust of his employment strategy is to increase overall investment and consumption—to grow the economy back to “full employment,” defined as a low unemployment rate within the existing structure of production and mass consumption. The green jobs he enumerates—wind turbine manufacturing, home retrofitting, mass transit—are certainly less carbon-intensive than the fossil-fuel activities they replace, but they still require vast material throughput: steel, concrete, rare earth minerals, and transportation networks that themselves emit greenhouse gases. The green growth he envisions is better than brown growth, but it does not escape the fundamental limits of a finite biosphere. Climate scientists tell us that a stable climate requires global emissions to peak within the next decade and decline sharply thereafter; that cannot happen if the United States and other rich countries are simultaneously trying to push GDP growth rates back to 3 or 4 percent.

Moreover, Pollin’s focus on creating jobs through additional spending ignores a more elegant and ecologically sound approach: work-time reduction. If we gradually shortened the standard workweek—say, from 40 hours to 30 hours—and expanded paid vacation time, we could spread the available work over a larger number of workers without needing to grow the economic pie. This was the central demand of the labor movement in the early twentieth century, and it helped to bring about the weekend and the eight-hour day. Today, many European countries with shorter average working hours, such as Germany and the Netherlands, have weathered the recession with lower unemployment rates than the United States, despite having less robust GDP growth. A shorter workweek, combined with a robust social safety net, would allow people to choose leisure and family time over consumption, reducing the economy’s material footprint even as it ensures that everyone who wants a job can have one.

Pollin treats the Swedish model of the 1960s and 1970s as an inspiration, but he fails to note that even Sweden’s remarkable achievements were built on a high-growth, high-resource-throughput economy that was itself ecologically unsustainable. The post-1990 abandonment of full employment in Sweden, which he laments, was in part a response to the limits of that growth model. The real challenge today is to construct a new Swedish model for the twenty-first century—one that combines full employment with a shrinking material economy. That can be done through a combination of work sharing, universal basic services (such as free health care, education, and public transit), a job guarantee in socially useful, low-impact sectors like care work and ecological restoration, and a gradual phase-out of the most damaging industries.

The political obstacles to such a transformation are enormous, as Pollin would rightly note. But they are no greater than the obstacles to his own proposal, which requires taking on the military-industrial complex and the fossil-fuel lobby simultaneously. A politics of sufficiency might, in fact, have broader appeal in a society weary of overwork, consumer debt, and environmental decline. It offers a vision of the good life that is not predicated on endless accumulation.

Pollin concludes that full employment is a moral imperative for a decent society. I agree. But a decent society on a finite planet cannot be one that equates a good job with ever-rising GDP. We need an economy that provides meaningful work for all within the ecological limits of our world. That is the true challenge of our time, and it requires us to think beyond the growth paradigm that even well-intentioned progressives like Pollin still take for granted.

The Simplest Path: Guarantee a Job at a Living Wage

Robert Pollin’s essay is a powerful indictment of the economic and moral failures of the neoliberal era, and his sectoral proposal to invest in education and clean energy is a sensible long-term vision. But his approach is needlessly indirect and ultimately relies on a Rube Goldberg machine of sectoral targets, negotiated wage restraint, and political trench warfare. There is a far simpler, more direct way to reach full employment and sustain it, one that avoids the thickets of inflation management and the entanglements of corporate power: the federal government should simply commit to providing a job at a living wage to anyone who wants one. This employer-of-last-resort (ELR) approach, long advocated by post-Keynesian economists, would directly eliminate involuntary unemployment, set a floor for wages and working conditions, and transform the politics of labor relations overnight. Pollin flirts with the idea when he discusses the Swedish state’s active labor-market policies, but he stops short of the bold step that could actually deliver on his promise.

The ELR concept is straightforward. The federal government would announce a wage—say, $15 per hour plus basic health and retirement benefits—at which it would hire any ready, willing, and able worker who cannot find private or regular public-sector employment. The program would be administered locally, in partnership with community organizations and municipalities, and the jobs would be designed to meet unmet social needs: environmental cleanup, elder care, public arts, urban gardening, afterschool tutoring, and the retrofitting projects Pollin champions. Because the wage is fixed, the program acts as an automatic stabilizer: in a recession, private-sector workers would flow into the ELR pool, maintaining their incomes and stabilizing demand; in a boom, employers would hire workers out of the pool by bidding wages above the ELR floor, and the program would shrink naturally. Inflation is contained because the ELR wage does not chase market wages upward; instead, it anchors the price system by setting a buffer stock of employed labor that can be drawn upon as needed.

Pollin worries about inflation and the need for unions to restrain wage demands, but his own analysis shows that the Phillips Curve trade-off is largely driven by class conflict over distribution, not by some mechanical law of nature. An ELR program breaks that conflict by mopping up unemployment directly, without relying on overheating the entire economy. The ELR wage becomes the de facto minimum wage, and because it comes with a job guarantee, workers are never in a position of having to accept substandard private employment just to survive. This gives them genuine bargaining power, but within a framework that does not threaten a destabilizing wage-price spiral: if unions push private-sector wages too high and cause unemployment, the affected workers can simply step into the ELR program at its stable wage, putting a ceiling on the damage. The ELR thus solves the Kalecki problem—capitalist resistance to full employment—by creating a permanent institutional structure that removes the threat of a rising reserve army.

Politically, an ELR might seem far-fetched, but it actually has advantages over Pollin’s more complex plan. It is simple to understand and can be framed as a right: the right to work. It avoids the charge of “big government” picking winners in the energy and education sectors, and it directly addresses the crisis of the long-term unemployed that neither conventional stimulus nor sectoral reallocation can reach quickly. The cost, while substantial, is manageable: if 10 million people entered the program at an average total cost of $40,000 per job (including materials and administration), the gross price tag would be $400 billion per year, but the actual net cost would be far lower because it would substitute for existing spending on unemployment insurance, food stamps, social services, and the productivity losses of idleness. Pollin’s own 4.8 million job creation proposal, by contrast, would involve $330 billion in shifted spending without directly guaranteeing that those jobs go to the hardest-to-employ.

The experience of the Great Depression and the wartime mobilization demonstrates that the federal government is fully capable of being the employer of last resort when the political will exists. The WPA and related programs directly employed millions and built the infrastructure that underpinned the postwar boom. The environmental and social challenges we face are no less urgent than those of the 1930s and 1940s. Pollin has shown that we can afford a full-employment economy and that the moral case is unanswerable. It is time to complete the thought and demand that the government fulfill its most fundamental obligation: ensuring that anyone who wants to work can work, at a wage that supports a decent life. Nothing less will do.

Robert Pollin's essay makes a valuable contribution to the debate over economic policy in the aftermath of the Great Recession. The author is right to argue that full employment should be a central policy objective, and his critique of neoliberalism is largely on target. Where the essay falls short is in providing a realistic path to achieving this goal.

The most practical obstacle to full employment is political, not economic. Pollin acknowledges this at the end of his essay when he asks whether there is the political will to fight for decent jobs. But he does not analyze where this political will might come from or how it might be built. The American political system is designed to block transformative change, and the interests opposed to full employment are well-organized and well-funded.

A more pragmatic approach might focus on achievable incremental steps rather than a comprehensive full employment agenda. Extension of unemployment benefits, targeted infrastructure spending, and financial reform are all within reach. These measures would not achieve full employment as Pollin defines it, but they would reduce unemployment and improve workers' bargaining position.

Pollin's specific proposal to redirect military and fossil fuel spending is unlikely to pass Congress in its current form. But the underlying principle—that public investment should create good jobs—is worth pursuing through more politically feasible channels. Clean energy investment has bipartisan support in some form, and education funding is popular even in an era of fiscal anxiety.

The essay's greatest strength is its insistence that the choice between full employment and low inflation is a false dichotomy. The Swedish model shows that both goals can be achieved through coordinated policies that give workers a stake in inflation control. This lesson deserves wider dissemination, even if the specific institutional arrangements that made it work cannot be easily replicated.

Pollin ends with a question: whether there is the political will to fight for full employment. The answer will depend on the mobilization of working people and their allies in the years ahead. The essay is a valuable contribution to this effort, even if it does not provide all the answers.
