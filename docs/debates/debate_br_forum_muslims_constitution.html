Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The most important move Akbar and Theoharis make is refusing the framing of Trump's executive order as rupture. The seven-country list did not spring from Steve Bannon's head; it was lifted from the Obama administration's 2015 and 2016 amendments to the Visa Waiver Program. The premise of "extreme vetting"—that Muslim travelers carry a presumption of risk requiring affirmative rebuttal—has been operationalized at borders, airports, and consulates for fifteen years. The chant "this is not who we are," however emotionally necessary at JFK and Dulles, was empirically wrong. This is precisely who we have been.

I want to extend the authors' argument with three concrete observations, and then press on two places where I think their case needs sharpening.

First, on continuity: the legal architecture of the post-9/11 security state is more durable than the authors even suggest. Consider *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* (2010), in which a 6–3 Supreme Court majority—an opinion written by Chief Justice Roberts and joined by Justice Stevens—upheld the material support statute against a First Amendment challenge brought by a human-rights group that wanted to train the PKK and LTTE in nonviolent dispute resolution and U.N. petitioning. The Court accepted the government's premise that even pure speech "coordinated with" a designated organization could be criminalized because it might "free up" the group's resources for violence. This is the case Akbar and Theoharis gesture toward when they describe the material support ban as a "black box." Its breadth is why a U.S. citizen like Tarek Mehanna could be sentenced to seventeen and a half years primarily on the basis of translating jihadist texts; why the Holy Land Foundation prosecution criminalized charitable giving through zakat committees the U.S. government itself had previously worked with; why the Newburgh Four—poor, mentally vulnerable men from upstate New York—were nudged toward a bombing plot by an FBI informant offering a quarter million dollars and a BMW. Trevor Aaronson's reporting and the 2014 Human Rights Watch/Columbia Law School study documented that informants played a central role in roughly half of post-9/11 federal terrorism prosecutions; in nearly thirty cases, the informant arguably engineered the plot. Whatever the next four years bring, this is the inherited toolkit.

Second, on surveillance: Edward Snowden's leaks captured the public imagination because they implicated everyone. But the more sustained scandal—the NYPD's Demographics Unit, exposed by the Associated Press beginning in 2011—targeted Muslims qua Muslims. Plainclothes officers mapped mosques, halal butchers, Muslim Student Associations at Yale and Penn, and entire neighborhoods on the theory that ordinary religious and social life was a "radicalization indicator." The unit produced not a single lead, by the NYPD's own admission, before it was disbanded in 2014. *Hassan v. City of New York* is still working its way through the Third Circuit. The point Akbar and Theoharis make—that white liberal alarm at NSA bulk collection coexisted with indifference to the Demographics Unit—is exactly right, and the asymmetry has political consequences. A coalition built only on the privacy interests of the universal "American" will not protect those whose surveillance is justified precisely by their particularity.

Third, the historical frame should reach further back than 2001. The federal government's anti-Muslim apparatus has genealogies in COINTELPRO's surveillance, infiltration, and disruption of the Nation of Islam in the 1950s and 1960s; in the Palmer Raids and the Red Scare deportations of putatively dangerous immigrants; in the internment of Japanese Americans under *Korematsu*, a case the Supreme Court has never overruled. Khizr Khan's pocket Constitution is a moving prop, but the Constitution it invokes is the same document that authorized chattel slavery, accommodated Jim Crow, and supplied no obstacle to Executive Order 9066. The authors are right that the romance of the document obscures more than it reveals. The relevant question is not whether the law is on our side—often it is not—but whether we can build the political power to bend it.

Now the pushbacks.

The authors are correct that the airport mobilizations rested on a particularized notion of Muslim innocence: the doctor returning from a conference, the grandmother visiting family, the Iraqi interpreter who had served U.S. forces. They are correct that this framing leaves intact the category of the "dangerous Muslim" who might justly be excluded. But I am not sure the choice they pose—innocence-based defense versus structural critique—is quite the right one for this moment. Mass mobilizations need legible victims; the airport scenes broke through because the people detained were ones the government's own categories had pre-cleared. The strategic question is how to use that opening to indict the categories themselves—to say, in effect, that if these people can be detained, then no one is safe, and the system that made them detainable was never legitimate to begin with. This is the move civil rights lawyers eventually made about the Japanese internment cases, and the move ACLU and CCR lawyers have been making in their challenges to the no-fly list and the terrorism watch list. The "innocent traveler" framing is not the destination; it can be the entry point.

The second pushback concerns equivalence. Akbar and Theoharis are emphatic, and rightly so, that Trump did not invent these policies. But there is a risk in the continuity argument: that it flattens distinctions that still matter. Obama's drone program was lawless and lethal, and his administration prosecuted whistleblowers more aggressively than any predecessor. It was also subject to internal legal review of a kind that does not appear to constrain the new administration; it operated within rhetorical limits that, however hypocritical, retained the form of universal citizenship. Trump's contribution is not the apparatus but the explicit avowal of religious targeting, the public licensing of vigilante harassment, the appointment of figures like Bannon and Sebastian Gorka who have argued that Islam itself is the enemy, and the restructuring of CVE to drop even the pretense of attending to white-supremacist violence at the moment when Dylann Roof's massacre and a wave of mosque arsons make that violence undeniable. The authors register these developments. But the response cannot only be "this was always here." It must also be: this is a regime that intends to use what is here in qualitatively new ways, and the legal infrastructure that seemed tolerable when administered by lawyers with a sense of constitutional shame will not be tolerable now.

The agenda the essay implies is therefore the right one and more urgent than it has ever been: repeal or narrow the material support statute; dismantle CVE; abolish the no-fly list or subject it to adversarial process; end the suspicionless border searches of electronic devices; rein in the 2001 AUMF and the unreviewed targeted-killing program; defend the right of Muslim communities to be politically dissident, religiously conservative, and unsurveilled all at once. The airports were a beginning. Whether they become anything more depends on whether the publics that showed up are willing to defend Muslims who are not, by anyone's measure, innocent of having opinions about American power.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The essay by Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis is a searing and necessary indictment of the interlocking legal, surveillance, and military apparatus that has constrained and distorted Muslim life in the United States for more than fifteen years. Their insistence that Islamophobia is not a Trumpian aberration but a deeply embedded bipartisan project, nourished by a public willing to sort “good” Muslims from “bad,” is thoroughly convincing. They rightly demand that we examine the casual brutality of the No Fly List, the pretzel logic of material support prosecutions, the everyday humiliation of borders, and the fiction that our domestic criminal process is fundamentally just. Yet as compelling as their diagnosis is, the essay’s analytical frame sometimes overreaches, collapsing the distinction between illegitimate targeting and the legitimate, if deeply flawed, effort to prevent violence. In doing so, it risks weakening the very moral and political ground on which a new, rights-respecting security order could be built.

Nowhere is this more apparent than in the treatment of the Khizr Khan speech. The authors are correct that the public’s effusive embrace of the Khan family was drenched in a provisional logic: a Muslim had to offer a son’s military sacrifice to be fully heard. But reading that moment solely as an iteration of the “good Muslim/bad Muslim” dichotomy overlooks what else was happening. When Khan brandished his pocket Constitution and invited Trump to search it for the words “liberty” and “equal protection,” he was not merely performing a romanticized Americana for a liberal audience. He was invoking a long tradition of marginalized groups demanding that the nation live up to its professed ideals. From Frederick Douglass’s searing Fourth of July oration to the Black civil rights marchers who recited the Declaration of Independence, appeals to national creed have been subversive, not simply complicit. To dismiss Khan’s act as “showing America through a romantic filter” nearly strips Muslim Americans of the agency to define their own belonging on their own terms. The more devastating problem is not that the public celebrated the Khans, but that so many other Muslim claims to equal protection—by people who have not served in the military, or who criticize U.S. foreign policy, or who wear a hijab and say nothing at all—are dismissed. A critique that can honor the Khan family’s witness while demanding the same dignity for all Muslims is more powerful than one that risks treating any heartwarming national moment as a trap.

A similar slippage marks the discussion of counterterrorism tools like the material support ban and the paradigm of radicalization. Akbar and Theoharis rightly show that these instruments have been grotesquely overbroad, criminalizing association, charity, speech, and even dissent. They have overwhelmingly been deployed against Muslims and have enabled sting operations that look more like entrapment than protection. But the essay’s framing often seems to suggest that the very concept of cutting off material support for terrorism, or of paying attention to ideological pathways toward violence, is intrinsically Islamophobic. That is a difficult argument to sustain in early 2017, when the country was still processing a series of attacks in which perpetrators claimed Islamist inspirations—Orlando only eight months earlier, San Bernardino the year before—and in a world where civilians in Baghdad, Jakarta, and Istanbul were slaughtered by groups like ISIS. The challenge is not to abandon the state’s responsibility to prevent mass murder, but to demand a counterterrorism that follows the Constitution, that does not profile, and that is based on real evidence of criminal conduct rather than on the kinds of piety or politics the FBI finds unsettling. By conflating the existing, rights-abusing apparatus with the very idea of counterterrorism itself, the essay may inadvertently cede the most crucial terrain: the construction of a security policy that Muslim communities themselves can help shape and that protects them from the extremists who threaten their lives and their religion.

This concern extends to the essay’s treatment of surveillance. The authors are correct that Edward Snowden’s revelations laid bare a surveillance state with a disproportionately heavy footprint on American Muslims, and that the widespread public anger focused largely on the government spying on “all Americans” rather than on the communities most affected. But the picture they paint of unrelenting, broadly unchallenged advancement is not quite complete. The USA Freedom Act of 2015, while modest, ended the bulk collection of telephone metadata and imposed some new transparency requirements. It passed because a coalition of civil libertarians and some Muslim advocacy groups worked with skeptical legislators on both the right and the left. That coalition arose precisely because people began to grasp the kind of structural harm the essay describes. To imply that the No Fly List is simply “celebrated” by Democrats, citing John Lewis’s sit-in for gun control, is an uncharacteristically sloppy generalization that elides a more productive story: many actors across the political spectrum do object to the list’s secret, unaccountable nature, and that dissent is a resource to be mobilized, not dismissed. Recognizing that small but genuine contested spaces exist does not minimize the crisis; it points toward the possibility of actually dismantling the machinery.

Something similar must be said about the essay’s international framing. The authors note that the United States bombed six of the seven countries in Trump’s ban, a devastating fact. But the argument that these wars are simply a manifestation of the same domestic anti-Muslim logic runs the risk of flattening the genuinely complex, tragic nature of conflicts in Syria, Iraq, and beyond. The violence there has many engines—authoritarian regimes, regional proxy wars, and the very sort of extremist groups whose victims are overwhelmingly Muslim. The proper progressive response is not to imply that all U.S. military action is reducible to Islamophobia, but to demand a foreign policy that stops fueling the fires and that centers human security over geopolitical competition. A critique that fails to distinguish between the bombing of a hospital and the narrow, multilateral defense of a Yazidi community under siege (had such ever occurred) may find itself without the moral grammar to oppose the worst while leaving open a door for the genuinely protective.

The great strength of Akbar and Theoharis’s essay is that it insists we see the whole sprawling system: the connection between domestic surveillance and foreign war, between the war on drugs and the war on terror, between the conditional hospitality offered to some Muslims and the permanent suspicion cast on most. My concern is that in its rush to denounce the entire edifice as a seamless project of anti-Muslim prejudice, it sometimes leaves the impression that the very act of trying to prevent terrorism is a mask for bigotry. That will not serve the broad, durable coalition needed to undo the damage. The Americans who packed airports to protest the ban included people who still believe that al-Qaeda and ISIS pose a real threat. Many of them want security policies that are both effective and just. If we tell them that the tools they instinctively support are beyond redemption, we risk losing them entirely. The more difficult but necessary task is to show why the current approach does not actually make us safer—why blowing up mosques with informants and draining civil liberties fuels the very narratives extremists exploit—and then to build out the alternative. Akbar and Theoharis have done us an immense service by illuminating the horrors of the status quo. The next step is to avoid foreclosing the very possibility of a counterterrorism that is neither Islamophobic nor incompetent, because without that vision, we will be left forever protesting the latest ban without ever wrenching the law away from the logic of the trial.

The images that emerged from international airports across the United States in the waning days of January were undeniably moving. Thousands of Americans, holding hastily made cardboard signs, flooded arrival halls to protest Donald Trump’s executive order banning travel from seven Muslim-majority nations. Lawyers sat cross-legged on terminal floors, drafting habeas corpus petitions on their laptops. For a brief moment, a spontaneous and vibrant coalition stood in direct opposition to the new administration’s overt Islamophobia, culminating in a temporary victory when the federal courts halted the ban’s implementation. It was a heartening display of civic resistance. Yet, as Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis so powerfully argue in their lead essay, this outpouring of outrage risks obscuring a much darker, far more entrenched reality. If our resistance begins and ends with Donald Trump, we will have failed to confront the sprawling, bipartisan national security apparatus that made his presidency—and his policies—possible in the first place.

Akbar and Theoharis correctly identify a historical amnesia at the heart of the current liberal “resistance.” The outrage over Trump’s travel ban has frequently been framed as a defense of American norms against a sudden, unprecedented authoritarian aberration. But the truth, however uncomfortable it may be for defenders of the previous administrations, is that Trump did not build the machinery of the modern surveillance and security state. He merely inherited it. By focusing our political energies exclusively on the vulgarity of Trump’s rhetoric and the cruelty of his immediate executive actions, we grant a retroactive absolution to the architects of the post-9/11 War on Terror—architects who belong to both major political parties.

Nowhere is this dynamic more insidious than in the liberal establishment’s embrace of the “good Muslim” archetype, a phenomenon Akbar and Theoharis brilliantly dissect through the lens of Khizr Khan’s speech at the Democratic National Convention. The rapturous applause that greeted Khan’s invocation of the Constitution revealed a deeply conditional framework for Muslim belonging in the United States. To be accepted as fully American, a Muslim must offer a blood sacrifice. They must either lose a child in one of America’s endless overseas wars, or they must enthusiastically collaborate with domestic law enforcement. As Bill Clinton explicitly stated in his own DNC remarks, Muslims are welcome so long as they “help us win.”

This is not a framework of equal rights; it is a framework of probation. It demands that American Muslims endlessly prove their loyalty to a state that fundamentally views them as a suspect class. By anchoring the defense of Muslim Americans in narratives of exceptional patriotism or absolute, helpless victimization—such as the elderly refugees and young children detained at airports—we implicitly validate the state’s right to police, surveil, and deport the “bad Muslims.” We accept the premise that constitutional rights must be earned through perfect behavior, rather than inherently possessed by virtue of humanity.

This dichotomy between the “good” and “bad” Muslim has been aggressively operationalized by federal law enforcement for the last fifteen years. While liberals rightly condemn Trump’s proposed restructuring of the Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) program, we must reckon with the fact that CVE was a hallmark of the Obama administration. Under the guise of community empowerment and public-private partnerships, the Obama-era CVE programs effectively deputized teachers, social workers, and community leaders to monitor Muslim youth for signs of “radicalization.” The indicators of this supposed radicalization were shockingly broad, often encompassing protected First Amendment activities: growing a beard, becoming more observant in daily prayers, or expressing political anger over U.S. foreign policy and drone strikes.

CVE programs, alongside the widespread dragnet surveillance enacted by the NYPD’s now-defunct Demographics Unit and the FBI’s sprawling network of informants, created an environment where the most intimate aspects of Muslim life were criminalized. When the state treats conservative religious practice or political dissent as a precursor to terrorism, it creates a chilling effect that hollows out the community from the inside. We did not need Donald Trump to teach us how to view Muslims with suspicion; the federal bureaucracy has been training local police and civil servants to do exactly that for a decade and a half.

Furthermore, as the lead essay emphasizes, we must critically examine the domestic legal infrastructure that has allowed these abuses to flourish with minimal judicial pushback. The current celebration of the federal judiciary for halting the travel ban glosses over a bleak judicial record regarding Muslim civil rights. For years, the courts have shown immense, often cowardly deference to the executive branch on matters of national security.

The most potent weapon in this legal arsenal is the “material support” statute (18 U.S.C. § 2339B), vastly expanded by the USA PATRIOT Act. The material support statute has become a black box of preventative prosecution, allowing the government to secure decades-long prison sentences for individuals who have never committed an act of violence. In 2010, the Supreme Court ruled in *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* that even peaceful speech, legal advice, and human rights advocacy can be criminalized as "material support" if directed toward a designated foreign terrorist organization. This disastrous ruling eviscerated First Amendment protections and provided the FBI with a blank check to conduct entrapment-style sting operations. For years, the FBI has targeted economically vulnerable or mentally ill Muslim men, providing them with the plot, the fake explosives, and the transportation, only to arrest them with great fanfare to justify counterterrorism budgets. The courts have routinely rubber-stamped these manufactured conspiracies, dismissing clear evidence of entrapment. If Trump decides to utilize these same tools, he will find a judiciary that has already spent fifteen years sharpening the blade for him.

Akbar and Theoharis also challenge us to recognize the deep intersectionality between the War on Terror and the broader regimes of mass incarceration and border militarization. It is a profound failure of imagination to treat the defense of Muslim civil rights as a discrete silo, separate from the movement for Black lives or the fight for undocumented immigrants. The legal and technological mechanisms of oppression are deeply intertwined. The same Department of Homeland Security grants that funded counterterrorism units in major cities were used to purchase the armored vehicles and military-grade weaponry deployed against Black protesters in Ferguson and Baltimore. The ideological conflation of Blackness with inherent criminality operates on the exact same frequency as the conflation of Islam with inherent terrorism. Both paradigms are designed to justify state violence and preemptive policing against communities of color.

Finally, we cannot confront domestic Islamophobia without confronting American imperialism. It is the height of hypocrisy for politicians to wring their hands over the plight of Syrian or Yemeni refugees stranded at JFK Airport while simultaneously voting to authorize the drone strikes and military interventions that forced those refugees to flee their homes in the first place. The United States has dropped thousands of bombs on the very countries listed in Trump’s travel ban—and Obama’s earlier visa restriction list—destabilizing the region and generating a massive humanitarian crisis. The domestic War on Terror is merely the homecoming of this violent foreign policy. We cannot successfully demand an end to the racial profiling of Muslims in New York or Los Angeles while remaining silent about the extrajudicial assassination of Muslims in Sana’a or Kabul.

As we move forward in this new and frightening political era, the lead essay by Akbar and Theoharis provides a vital roadmap. It demands that we refuse the comfort of easy nostalgia. The goal of the current resistance cannot simply be a return to January 19, 2017. To long for the days of the Obama administration is to long for a polite, bureaucratized Islamophobia—one managed by eloquent lawyers rather than crude demagogues, but one that destroys lives nonetheless.

If we truly wish to defend Muslim communities, we must dismantle the infrastructure of suspicion in its entirety. We must demand an end to the secretive No Fly List, the repeal of overbroad material support statutes, the defunding of CVE programs, and a radical scaling back of American military intervention abroad. We must recognize that the civil rights of Muslim Americans are not contingent upon their willingness to fight our wars or validate our constitutional myths. They are inherent. Donald Trump’s presidency presents a terrifying escalation of the War on Terror, but it also offers a moment of profound clarity. The mask of American benevolence has slipped. We now have an opportunity to look clearly at the carceral, militarized state we have built over the last two decades, and, finally, to begin the hard work of tearing it down.

Akbar and Theoharis are right to insist that the Muslim ban cannot be treated as an aberration that fell from the sky on January 20. The order’s cruelty and constitutional infirmity are urgent, but its plausibility rests on a much older architecture: secret lists, broad immigration powers, material support statutes, informant networks, watchlisting, expansive surveillance, and a political culture in which Muslims are routinely asked to prove that they are not the enemy. If the response to Trump is only to say “this is not who we are,” we will fail to see how much of this moment has been made by policies defended in more respectable language by both parties.

The central achievement of the essay is to shift the question from Trump’s animus to the conditions that made his program administratively and politically possible. That matters because the travel ban is not simply a symbolic insult. It operates through institutions that already know how to sort, detain, interrogate, and exclude Muslims and people perceived to be Muslim. It is easy to condemn a campaign promise of “a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States.” It is harder to confront the bureaucratic normality of secondary inspection, watchlists, immigration delays, pressured informant recruitment, and “extreme vetting” when they are described as ordinary security measures. But the latter is what gives the former teeth.

Still, the critique should not lead us to abandon constitutional argument or the language of equal citizenship. The authors are wary of Khizr Khan’s pocket Constitution, and understandably so. His reception did reveal the narrow terms on which Muslim belonging is often recognized: the grieving Gold Star father is embraced because his family has given the nation what the nation recognizes as the highest proof of loyalty. Bill Clinton’s convention line—Muslims may “stay here” if they love America and hate terror—was indeed an appalling formulation, as though American Muslims were guests whose lease depended on good behavior.

Yet Khan’s appeal also did something important. He made visible that Muslims are already within the constitutional “we.” He did not ask for tolerance as a favor; he demanded accountability from a presidential candidate who presumed to define the nation against him. The Constitution is not self-enforcing, and it has often been interpreted to permit slavery, segregation, exclusion, internment, and surveillance. But it is also a language through which excluded groups have made claims, exposed hypocrisy, and organized power. To criticize constitutional romance is necessary; to discard constitutional struggle would be a mistake. The question is not whether we invoke the Constitution, but whether we do so in a way that protects only the sympathetic, the patriotic, and the innocent, or in a way that challenges the state’s authority to distribute rights according to suspicion.

The innocence frame is particularly dangerous in national security politics. The first weekend after the executive order, the stories that circulated most widely were of infants, elderly travelers, translators who had helped U.S. forces, students, doctors, and families. Those stories deserved attention. They cut through abstraction and showed the human cost of policy made by stereotype. But a politics built only on the obviously blameless will not protect the person with unpopular views, the mosque-goer whose religious practice is deemed “conservative,” the charity donor whose recipient is later scrutinized, the young man baited by an informant, or the noncitizen with an old conviction. Civil liberties are tested precisely when the person claiming them is not perfect.

This is where the material support regime is so troubling. The law’s breadth blurs the line between aiding violence and engaging in association, speech, humanitarian work, or conflict mediation. After the Supreme Court’s decision in Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, even coordinated training in peaceful dispute resolution with a designated group could be criminalized. That logic gives the executive enormous power because designation decisions are themselves political and often opaque. If the Muslim Brotherhood were designated as a foreign terrorist organization, as some in the Trump orbit have urged, the consequences could radiate far beyond any actual security threat, sweeping in charities, civic organizations, scholars, and ordinary believers through guilt by association. Such powers should disturb conservatives and liberals alike.

The same is true of watchlisting. In recent months Democrats have sometimes embraced the No Fly List as a tool for gun control, asking why someone too dangerous to board an airplane should be allowed to buy a firearm. But that argument assumes what it should question: that the list reliably identifies dangerous people and that those listed have meaningful process. A secret executive list with vague criteria, predictable errors, and inadequate avenues for redress should not become the basis for depriving people of additional rights. The answer to the gun violence crisis cannot be to launder one civil liberties failure through another. If the state can restrict movement, religious practice, employment, or arms possession on secret evidence, the issue is not merely which right is at stake; it is whether law has become an instrument of unreviewable suspicion.

At the same time, an adequate response must speak honestly about violence committed in the name of jihadist movements. The existence of anti-Muslim prejudice does not mean there are no threats, just as the racist history of policing does not mean there is no violence in need of public response. ISIS, al-Qaeda, and their sympathizers have killed Muslims and non-Muslims in large numbers, and governments have a duty to prevent attacks. The problem is not that the state seeks security. It is that the dominant framework has too often equated Muslim identity, Muslim dissent, and Muslim religiosity with danger, while producing methods—mass surveillance, informant manipulation, overbroad prosecution—that are both unjust and often counterproductive. A rights-respecting security policy would focus on conduct, credible evidence, and individualized suspicion. It would not treat religious communities as pools of latent threat.

Countering Violent Extremism programs illustrate the dilemma. Presented as community partnership, they often ask teachers, social workers, religious leaders, and parents to become intelligence gatherers trained to detect supposed signs of radicalization. But the social science behind such indicators is weak. Political anger, religious conservatism, alienation, or criticism of U.S. foreign policy does not predict violence in any reliable way. Indeed, treating these as warning signs risks criminalizing adolescence, piety, and dissent. It also undermines trust in precisely the institutions—schools, mosques, clinics, community centers—that should remain places of support rather than surveillance. If the Trump administration narrows CVE to focus explicitly on “radical Islamic extremism,” it will make explicit what many communities already experienced: a program of ideological monitoring aimed primarily at Muslims.

Akbar and Theoharis are also right to connect the War on Terror to immigration enforcement and mass incarceration. These systems share habits of thought: dangerous classes, predictive policing, preventive detention, plea pressure, deference to prosecutors, and the conversion of social problems into security problems. They also share personnel, technology, and rhetoric. The militarized police response in Ferguson did not emerge from nowhere; it reflected decades in which domestic policing absorbed the equipment and posture of war. Likewise, immigration law’s civil label has long allowed severe punishments—detention, family separation, exile—without the protections associated with criminal law. For Muslim, Arab, South Asian, Black, and immigrant communities, these regimes overlap in daily life.

But the intersectional frame should be more than a coalition slogan. It should sharpen our analysis of who is harmed and how. Muslims in the United States are not a single racial or ethnic group. They are Black, Arab, South Asian, white, Latino, immigrant, native-born, wealthy, working-class, incarcerated, undocumented, secular, devout. Anti-Muslim policy often operates through race, but not always in identical ways. A Black Muslim may experience Islamophobia through the preexisting criminalization of Blackness; an Iranian student may encounter it through sanctions, visas, and suspicion of foreign ties; a Somali refugee may face the combined force of anti-Blackness, xenophobia, and counterterrorism scrutiny. A serious movement must be attentive to these differences rather than imagining a generic Muslim subject.

The foreign policy dimension is indispensable. The United States cannot bomb, raid, sanction, arm, occupy, and drone across Muslim-majority societies and then pretend that domestic suspicion of Muslims is unrelated. War produces categories of enemy identity. It circulates images of Muslim spaces as zones of chaos and Muslim bodies as risks to be managed. It also produces refugees, some of whom are then described as threats when they seek safety. To oppose the ban while refusing to discuss Iraq, Afghanistan, Yemen, Somalia, Libya, Syria, Pakistan, and the broader machinery of U.S. power is to treat symptoms while preserving causes.

Here again, precision matters. Criticizing American militarism does not require denying the brutality of Assad, ISIS, al-Qaeda, sectarian militias, or authoritarian regimes. Nor does it require imagining that nonintervention is always simple. But it does require recognizing that military force has repeatedly been oversold as liberation and underexamined in its consequences. The 2003 invasion of Iraq remains the central catastrophe in this history, destabilizing a region and helping create conditions in which later violence flourished. Drone strikes and special operations may appear cleaner than ground wars, but they normalize a global battlefield with little transparency and few constraints. Civilian deaths, mistaken targeting, and the psychological terror of drones hovering overhead are not incidental to how affected populations understand American power.

What, then, should resistance demand? First, the travel ban should be defeated not only because it was clumsily drafted or because it harmed visa holders and permanent residents, but because religious disfavor and nationality-based collective suspicion are incompatible with equal citizenship and a lawful immigration system. Courts should not avoid the anti-Muslim purpose evident in Trump’s campaign statements and the order’s design.

Second, watchlisting and no-fly procedures require real due process: notice, reasons, meaningful opportunities to contest placement, and independent review. Secret law cannot be the ordinary condition of modern governance.

Third, material support statutes should be narrowed so that they punish intentional assistance to violence, not speech, association, humanitarian engagement, or religious and political activity. Designation processes need transparency and checks.

Fourth, surveillance should return to individualized suspicion and judicial oversight. Mosques should not be mapped as threat sites; students should not be monitored for political opinions; border searches should not become fishing expeditions into a person’s digital life.

Fifth, CVE programs should be rejected where they deputize community institutions into ideological policing. Resources would be better spent on education, employment, mental health, anti-violence work, and community-led initiatives not tied to intelligence gathering.

Finally, opposition to Islamophobia must include opposition to the wars and security partnerships that sustain it. Otherwise we will defend Muslims at the airport while ignoring the bombs that made many of them refugees.

The danger of the Trump era is not only that new abuses will be invented. It is that old abuses will be intensified, renamed, and cheered. The opportunity is that more people now see what Muslim communities have been saying for years: emergency powers do not remain confined to emergencies, and suspicion built into law becomes ordinary life. The task is to build a politics capacious enough to defend the child detained at the airport, the dissident imam, the imperfect defendant, the refugee abroad, the citizen on a watchlist, and the community that refuses to purchase belonging through silence.

Akbar and Theoharis have written the essay that needs to be read right now, and the most important sentence in it may be the most easily overlooked: "Trump's easiest route to achieve his aims is therefore to amplify, extend, and revisit practices already in place." The lead essay properly catalogs the bipartisan machinery built since 2001: material support prosecutions, watchlists, CVE, sting operations dressed up as preemption, drone strikes in countries where Congress has never authorized force. But naming this machinery is not the same as committing to dismantle it. And much of the liberal opposition to Trump right now is busy trying to restore the machinery to its pre-Trump operators—to put the gun back in Obama's hand, where it supposedly belonged.

Former Bush and Obama national security officials, several of them personally responsible for signing off on torture memos, kill lists, or the metadata dragnet, are being treated as the adults in the room because they oppose Trump. James Clapper, who lied under oath to the Senate about NSA collection in 2013, is now a folk hero in some quarters because he signed a January assessment about Russian interference. That is not victory; that is the trap. The pre-Trump status quo was deportation at record rates, the FBI sending informants into mosques to coax mentally ill young men into terrorism plots the bureau itself manufactured, and a CVE program that hired Muslim community leaders to surveil their own neighbors in exchange for grant money. But politically it expressed an exchange: Muslim belonging mediated through blood sacrifice for American wars. Khan's son died in Iraq, in a war that killed hundreds of thousands of Iraqis and that the Khans' co-religionists are still fleeing—including, indeed, from one of the seven countries on Trump's list. The image of the pocket Constitution worked because it offered the audience a way to be on the right side of Trump without questioning anything about the wars, the surveillance, the prosecutions, or the bombing campaigns those Constitutions have been read to authorize. That reading list does not fit in a pocket.

The Limits of Continuity

Akbar and Theoharis present a clear and morally serious case, and on many specifics they are right. The material support statute is overbroad. CVE was a poorly designed program built on contested premises. The No Fly List has glaring due process problems that the Ninth Circuit has only begun to address. The federal terrorism prosecution apparatus has produced disturbing cases—the Newburgh Four, the Liberty City Seven—where the line between detection and entrapment looks vanishingly thin. The brunt of post-9/11 surveillance has indeed fallen on American Muslim communities, and the legal architecture built by Bush and continued by Obama deserves the scrutiny the authors demand. Where I part company with them is in the central rhetorical move of the essay: the insistence that Trump's executive order is essentially continuous with prior policy, and that liberal opposition to the ban which does not also repudiate the entire counterterrorism apparatus is hypocritical or naive.

This continuity thesis is analytically attractive and politically counterproductive, and I think it gets the moral structure of the situation wrong.

Begin with the executive order itself. The authors note, correctly, that the seven countries on Trump's list were drawn from a 2015 Obama-era law restricting the Visa Waiver Program for travelers who had visited those countries. But the Obama policy did not bar entry to nationals of those countries. It required them—and dual nationals of waiver-program countries who had traveled there—to go through the regular visa process rather than the streamlined waiver. That is a very different policy. It is the difference between additional screening and categorical exclusion, between treating travel history as a risk factor and treating national origin as a disqualification. To call the ban a mere "amplification" of Obama policy is to elide a categorical change in kind.

Similarly, while it is true that the Obama administration deported large numbers of people and built much of the surveillance architecture the authors decry, the orientation of that administration toward Muslim Americans differed in ways that matter. Obama personally and repeatedly affirmed that the United States was not at war with Islam. He visited mosques. He spoke at Muslim community events. His Justice Department prosecuted hate crimes against Muslims. His CVE program, whatever its real flaws, was at least notionally framed as a partnership with Muslim communities rather than a confrontation with them. Trump's first month has been categorically different: the appointment of advisors who have called Islam a "cancer," the reported plan to rename CVE "Countering Radical Islamic Extremism," the explicit campaign promises of a Muslim registry and a ban. To treat these as equivalent because both administrations operate within the same legal architecture is to ignore that the same architecture, in different hands, produces different harms.

The authors' answer to this would be: yes, but the architecture exists, and Trump can now wield it. That is true and it is the strongest version of their argument. But it is not the same as saying that Obama and Trump are interchangeable, or that the airport protestors are dupes for failing to demand abolition of the entire national security state. Most political reform happens incrementally, by mobilizing public concern around specific abuses, and the implicit contempt in the lead essay for "particularized notions of Muslim innocence" misreads how movements actually build.

Take the No Fly List. The authors criticize liberals for celebrating John Lewis's gun-control sit-in, which proposed using the list to bar firearm purchases. The criticism has merit—if you think the list is a due process disaster, you should not want to extend it to a new domain. But the appropriate response is to argue, as the ACLU did, that the list itself needs reform, not to dismiss the gun control effort as Islamophobic. The civil libertarian and the gun control proponent can be in conversation; they need not be enemies, and the authors' framing makes that conversation harder, not easier.

The same is true of the "innocence" framing. The lead essay treats public outrage focused on detained children and elderly travelers as a defect, because it implicitly cedes the legitimacy of harsher treatment for some "dangerous" Muslims. But this is how moral persuasion works. People become invested in a category of victims through specific, sympathetic cases; the category then expands as the underlying analysis develops. The civil rights movement did not begin by demanding the abolition of all racial classifications in law; it began with lunch counters and bus seats and incremental claims that brought ordinary white Americans into a moral relationship with Black plaintiffs. To insist that protestors at JFK must, on day one, articulate a fully developed critique of the AUMF or they are part of the problem is to set a purity test that no real political movement can meet.

There are also matters of fact and emphasis where the lead essay reads too tendentiously. The claim that CVE focuses "exclusively on Muslim extremism" under Trump (which is plausible) is paired with criticism of Obama's CVE for emphasizing Muslim communities and devoting "little emphasis" to white power or neo-Nazi extremism. This is partly true and partly unfair: the Obama DHS in fact funded several non-Muslim CVE grantees including programs targeting white supremacist recruitment, and the intelligence community produced reports on right-wing extremism (notably the 2009 DHS report that was rescinded after conservative pushback). The disproportion was real, but it was contested within the bureaucracy, not unanimous. Trump's reported decision to formally exclude white extremism is a real and meaningful change that the continuity thesis obscures.

On material support, the authors do not mention that the statute has also been used—rightly, in my view—to prosecute Americans who provided money, fighters, or logistical aid to groups like ISIS and al-Shabaab that have engaged in mass atrocities against Muslims. The case for narrowing the statute (particularly after Humanitarian Law Project, which extended liability to nonviolent advocacy training) is strong. The case for repealing it entirely is much weaker and would require ignoring real harms.

Finally, on the global picture: the authors are right that domestic and foreign policies are connected, and they are right that the bombing campaigns in Muslim-majority countries are part of the context. But the suggestion that opposing the ban requires confronting "the role of the United States around the world" can flatten important distinctions. The U.S. has bombed Syria—on behalf of, among others, the Syrian civilians being slaughtered by the Assad regime, which has killed more Muslims than any actor in the conflict. The U.S. has fought al-Shabaab in Somalia, which has killed thousands of Somali Muslims. The honest position acknowledges that American military power has done grave harm and also that withdrawing it entirely is not a costless choice for the populations involved.

What the moment calls for, I would suggest, is something subtler than the lead essay allows: a defense of liberal-constitutional values that is honest about how those values have failed Muslim Americans, committed to specific reforms (narrowing material support, due process for the No Fly List, ending entrapment-style stings, ending the CVE program in its current form), and unwilling to be paralyzed by the demand that the only authentic opposition to Trump is a comprehensive rejection of the modern American state. The airport protestors were not naive. They saw something specific and outrageous, and they showed up. That is how political mobilization starts. It is not how it ends, and Akbar and Theoharis are right that more is needed. But it is also not nothing, and it is not the same as the apparatus they protested.

What the Authors Get Wrong About Threat

I read Amna Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis's essay with the discomfort of someone who agrees with parts of their indictment and finds the larger framework unpersuasive. Yes, the executive order signed on January 27 was poorly drafted, badly executed, and likely unconstitutional in its initial form. Yes, there are real abuses in the federal terrorism prosecution system that civil libertarians of all stripes should oppose. But the lead essay is not really an argument about reforming specific tools. It is an argument that the entire post-9/11 national security state is illegitimate, that the threat which produced it was largely a pretext for racial domination, and that anyone who supports any element of counterterrorism policy is complicit in something resembling Jim Crow for Muslims. This is a serious claim. It deserves a serious answer.

The first thing to say is that the threat was, and remains, real. Between 2001 and 2017, jihadist terrorism—I use the term because "radical Islamic terrorism" has become a partisan shibboleth but the underlying phenomenon is what it is—killed roughly 100 people on American soil in attacks ranging from Fort Hood to San Bernardino to Orlando to the Boston Marathon. That number is, thankfully, smaller than many feared on September 12, 2001, but it is small in significant part because of the apparatus the authors want to dismantle. Globally, organizations professing some variant of jihadist ideology have killed hundreds of thousands of people, the overwhelming majority of them Muslims, in attacks ranging from the Beslan school siege to the Yazidi genocide to weekly suicide bombings in Baghdad, Kabul, and Mogadishu. ISIS at its peak controlled territory the size of Britain and ran an industrial-scale system of sexual slavery. Boko Haram has displaced two million people in the Lake Chad basin. Al-Shabaab routinely massacres Somali and Kenyan civilians. To write a 2,500-word essay on Islam and U.S. policy and barely acknowledge any of this is to omit the load-bearing fact of the entire policy debate.

The authors will respond that American policy did not cause but exacerbated this violence, and there is truth in that—the Iraq War was a catastrophic mistake whose consequences are still unfolding. But they cannot have it both ways. Either jihadist violence is a small problem that does not warrant the apparatus built to address it, or it is a large problem that American foreign policy has contributed to and which has independent existence. If the latter, then the question is what an appropriate response looks like, not whether response is necessary.

The lead essay does not engage this question seriously. The closest it comes is the implication that surveillance, prosecution, and military action are all manifestations of "Islamophobia" rooted in "the history of colonialism." But this framing collapses analytic categories. A federal investigation of an American citizen who has traveled to Syria, joined a designated terrorist organization, and returned to the United States is not the same kind of act as Trump's executive order. One is targeted at conduct; the other at national origin. One has been used, in many cases, to prevent demonstrable plots—the Najibullah Zazi case, in which an Afghan-American trained by al-Qaeda was apprehended before attempting to bomb the New York subway, is one of dozens. The other was a sloppy and probably unconstitutional categorical measure. Treating these as instances of the same phenomenon makes the critique easier to write and harder to defend.

The authors are particularly unfair to the surveillance debate. They suggest that civil libertarian outrage after Snowden focused only on spying on "all Americans" rather than on the disproportionate impact on Muslims. This is partly true and represents a real defect in white-led civil liberties advocacy. But the underlying NSA programs that Snowden exposed were not designed as anti-Muslim programs. The Section 215 metadata program collected indiscriminately. PRISM targeted foreign intelligence targets, the overwhelming majority of whom were not American Muslims. The authors' framing turns every counterterrorism program into a racial program by virtue of who the terrorists happen to be, which is a category error.

On material support, I share the authors' concern about overreach, particularly after Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project extended liability to nonviolent training and advocacy. That decision was wrongly decided. But the core of the statute—criminalizing the provision of money, weapons, personnel, and logistical aid to designated terrorist organizations—is necessary and right. It is the legal mechanism by which we prosecute Americans who try to join ISIS. The authors would, I take it, narrow the statute substantially. Fine, let's debate the specifics. But the implication that the statute itself is illegitimate, that prosecuting an American who provides cash or fighters to a group that beheads aid workers and burns prisoners alive is somehow analogous to enforcing Jim Crow, is wrong.

The Khan family discussion in the lead essay troubles me most. The authors imply that public celebration of the Khans was racist because it required a Muslim family's sacrifice as the precondition for sympathy. Set aside the awkwardness of criticizing a man whose son died serving his country, in front of his country, by suggesting his moment of public grief was insufficiently politically radical. The deeper problem is that the Khan moment was politically valuable precisely because it disrupted the categorical thinking the authors elsewhere claim to oppose. Khizr Khan stood on a stage and said: my family is American, my son was American, the Constitution is ours too. This is not a "good Muslim/bad Muslim" frame. It is a refusal of the frame. The authors read it as accommodationist; I read it as a claim of belonging. Both readings are available, but the authors' reading requires them to insist that no public expression of Muslim American patriotism can be authentic, which is itself a kind of essentialism.

There is also a strange silence in the lead essay about Muslim American agency in the counterterrorism conversation. American Muslims are not a monolithic community, and a significant portion of American Muslim leaders—imams, civic organizations, family members of radicalized young people—have actively cooperated with law enforcement, not under coercion but because they share the goal of preventing their kids from being recruited by ISIS. To frame all such cooperation as the product of CVE-style coercion or "good Muslim" performativity is to deny these Americans their own political agency. It treats them, ironically, exactly the way the authors accuse the U.S. government of treating Muslim women in the orientalist script: as objects without voice.

I do not want to be misread. The Trump executive order was wrong and I am glad the courts have, so far, enjoined it. The federal terrorism prosecution system has serious problems. CVE in its first incarnation was poorly conceived. The drone program needs much greater legal and democratic oversight. American policy in the Middle East has often been disastrous. These are real critiques and the authors are right to press them.

But the broader argument—that the threat is largely fictive, that the apparatus is fundamentally Islamophobic in design rather than in some specific applications, that liberal opposition to Trump is hypocritical unless it embraces a comprehensive critique of post-9/11 American statecraft—is not, I think, sustainable. It asks American Muslims to bear the cost of an analytic frame that denies the actuality of jihadist violence, including violence against Muslims. It asks American policy to renounce tools that have, on balance, prevented attacks. And it treats those who disagree as morally compromised rather than as participants in a legitimate disagreement about means.

A better essay would have acknowledged the threat, distinguished good tools from bad ones, and made the case for specific reforms. Akbar and Theoharis are too smart to have missed these moves. They chose a different essay, and the choice matters. The danger of their framing is not that it will be adopted in policy—it won't be—but that it will harden the perception, already widespread on the right, that the only critique of Trump's Muslim policy on offer from the left is one that denies the underlying problem exists. That perception is wrong, but the lead essay does not help correct it.

That framing brought hundreds of thousands of people to airports and pressured judges and lit up congressional offices. That is not nothing. It is, in many ways, the most public Muslim defense the United States has seen in fifteen years.

But the framing came at a cost, and the cost falls on people in my community who do not look like the doctor or the grandmother. It falls on the Yemeni teenager whose Twitter feed includes angry posts about Saudi airstrikes. It falls on the Pakistani graduate student who attended a conference where a speaker said unkind things about U.S. drone policy. It falls on the Somali kid in Minneapolis whose older brother went to fight—on the wrong side of a confusing civil war—and who is now an "associate" in some FBI file. The protests at JFK were not for them. The judge in the Eastern District of New York did not enjoin their predicament. They remain in the place American Muslims have lived since 2001: under suspicion, with their innocence forever provisional, awaiting the next ambiguous social media post or unwise friendship that will turn them into the case study.

This is the deeper point Akbar and Theoharis are making, and it deserves more elaboration than the essay gives. The category of the "good Muslim" is not just a frame imposed from outside. It is a discipline internalized within our communities, often in painful and divisive ways. The imam learns to preach a sermon that will read innocuously if transcribed by an informant. The mother counsels her son to stay away from political activity, to avoid Palestine rallies, to never share an opinion about Syria online. The civic organization writes its mission statement to include endless invocations of "American values" and quietly removes from its programming any speaker who has been critical of U.S. foreign policy. These are not just rhetorical adjustments. They are the slow, granular reshaping of religious and political life under conditions of surveillance, and they happen whether or not anyone is actually watching.

CVE was perhaps the most insidious version of this discipline because it offered grant money. The Obama administration's pilot programs in Boston, Los Angeles, and Minneapolis invited Muslim community organizations to apply for funds to address "radicalization," which in practice meant accepting the premise that radicalization was the primary issue facing our youth—not Islamophobic bullying, not police harassment, not the trauma of having a parent in immigration detention, not the ordinary travails of growing up Muslim in post-9/11 America, but radicalization, full stop. Some organizations took the money and tried to redirect it. Others refused on principle. The split tore at communities. People who had worked together for years stopped trusting each other. The Trump administration's reorientation of CVE makes this worse, but the damage was already done.

I want to register one place where I think the lead essay could have gone further. The authors note correctly that "Muslims and terrorism are seen as inseparable" in much the same way that "blackness and criminality have been conflated." This is right and important. But the framing risks treating Muslims and Black Americans as parallel rather than overlapping categories. A quarter or more of American Muslims are Black—descendants of enslaved Africans, members of historically African American Muslim communities, immigrants from East and West Africa. For these Muslims, the war on terror and the war on drugs have never been separate. They live both at once. The Black Somali kid in Minneapolis who is profiled by Minneapolis PD on the way to school and surveilled by JTTF in the evening does not experience two distinct regimes; he experiences one continuous regime of suspicion calibrated to his particular intersection.

The lead essay gestures at this with the line about Ferguson tanks, but I would push harder. The tools of mass incarceration—prosecutorial overcharging, plea coercion, pretrial detention, civil asset forfeiture, gang databases—translate almost seamlessly into the terrorism context. The terrorism informant program is genetically related to the drug informant program. The DEA built it first; the FBI scaled it up. The targeting of mosques in the NYPD's Demographics Unit was performed by officers trained in the same techniques used to target housing projects. We will not understand any of this if we separate the regimes analytically just because we encounter them through different bureaucracies.

Where I find myself slightly hesitant about the lead essay is its tone toward the airport protestors. The authors are scrupulous, but the implication—that the protests rested on a problematic frame—reads, in places, as a critique of the mobilization itself. I would frame it differently. The airport protests were a beginning, not an end. They were what was politically possible in the first 72 hours of a shocking executive action. The work of expanding that solidarity outward—to the No Fly List, to the prosecutions, to the drone war, to the deportation of Somali asylum seekers, to the ongoing CVE program—is the work of the next several years, and it will be done in part by the people who were at the airports. To begin that work by telling them their first instinct was tainted is, I worry, bad politics, even if the underlying analysis is right.

What I would have wanted in the lead essay is a clearer sense of where the political coalition goes next. The airport protests reached people who had not previously been activated. Many of them are open to the deeper critique. Many of them have Muslim coworkers, neighbors, friends, in-laws. Many of them are now reading their first articles about the No Fly List, the material support statute, FISA, Special Administrative Measures. The pedagogical opportunity is enormous. What we need now are organizers, lawyers, scholars, and journalists who can take the energy of the airports and walk it through the corridors of the federal courthouse, the FISA Court, the immigration detention center, the CVE office, the drone targeting room.

Akbar and Theoharis have written, I think, the opening argument of that pedagogy. I am grateful for it, and I am hopeful, with the kind of hope that comes from having seen our communities survive worse and from believing that this country is capable of telling itself a better story than the one it has been telling about us for the last fifteen years.

The Courts Were Always the Problem

Akbar and Theoharis's lead essay is a powerful indictment of the political branches and a useful corrective to the post-airport-protest mood. But I want to add a doctrinal layer that the essay touches on only briefly: the role of the federal judiciary in producing, not merely failing to check, the legal architecture of the war on terror. The authors are right that Khizr Khan's pocket Constitution did not protect Muslim Americans from fifteen years of rights violations. But the deeper question is why. The Constitution did not fail on its own. It was interpreted by federal judges, in case after case, in ways that gave the executive branch nearly everything it asked for. Any serious response to the lead essay's call to action has to reckon with judicial doctrine, because the doctrine is what made the rest possible.

Start with the cases the authors mention only obliquely. In Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project (2010), the Supreme Court held that the material support statute could constitutionally be applied to people who wanted to provide nonviolent training—in international human rights law and peaceful conflict resolution—to designated foreign terrorist organizations. The vote was 6-3. The majority, written by Chief Justice Roberts, held that the government's national security interest justified the limitation on First Amendment activity, and that courts owed substantial deference to executive judgments about which organizations were dangerous and what kinds of support might be fungible. Justice Breyer, dissenting, observed that the majority's reasoning would criminalize teaching a designated organization to file petitions with the United Nations. He was right, and the majority's logic has since been used to prosecute Americans for translating documents, sharing social media posts, and conducting religious instruction.

Holder is the keystone case for the modern terrorism prosecution apparatus. It is also a First Amendment case decided by a Supreme Court that, in other contexts, is among the most speech-protective in American history. The same court that struck down restrictions on corporate political spending in Citizens United, that protected the Westboro Baptist Church's right to picket military funerals in Snyder v. Phelps, that invalidated California's restriction on violent video games in Brown v. Entertainment Merchants—that court, in Holder, decided that translating a treatise on nonviolent conflict resolution was not protected speech if the recipient was on a list maintained by the State Department. This is not a minor doctrinal anomaly. It is a categorical exception, carved out of the First Amendment, for Muslims (in practice; the statute is technically neutral). It is the kind of exception the Court has historically made only in moments of acute panic and has later disavowed: Schenck, Korematsu, Dennis.

Now consider the standing cases. In Clapper v. Amnesty International USA (2013), the Supreme Court held that journalists, lawyers, and human rights workers who feared their international communications were being surveilled under Section 702 of FISA lacked standing to challenge the program because they could not prove their communications had actually been intercepted. The catch: the government would not tell them, the FISA Court's opinions were classified, and any specific surveillance was itself secret. The Court effectively held that the existence of a classification regime is sufficient to prevent constitutional challenge to that regime. Six months later, Edward Snowden's leaks revealed that the plaintiffs in Clapper had been precisely the kind of people the government was surveilling. The Court has not revisited the standing question.

Consider also the No Fly List litigation. In Latif v. Holder (2014), Judge Anna Brown of the District of Oregon held that the procedures for redress of No Fly designations violated due process. This was a significant win, but it has been slow to produce meaningful relief. The government's response was to create a marginally improved redress process and to litigate aggressively against further claims. The D.C. Circuit and other courts have been substantially more deferential, applying the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test in ways that consistently favor the government's claimed security interest over the individual's interest in not being barred from flying.

The pattern across these cases is consistent. Where Muslim plaintiffs raise claims about counterterrorism programs, federal courts have applied unusually deferential standards of review, accepted government affidavits invoking the state secrets privilege with minimal scrutiny, and constructed standing and pleading doctrines that make merits review nearly impossible. This is not the same as saying the courts have endorsed the underlying programs. It is to say that the courts have erected procedural and doctrinal barriers that prevent merits review. The functional result is the same: programs proceed, harms accumulate, plaintiffs lose.

So when Akbar and Theoharis note, with cautious optimism, that the judiciary's willingness to push back against Trump's executive order is "a heartening development," I share the cautious part more than the optimism. The injunction issued by Judge Robart in Seattle and the Ninth Circuit's refusal to stay it are real. They reflect a judiciary that, at least at the district and circuit levels, retains the capacity to act when an executive action is sufficiently outrageous and sufficiently visible. But the executive order was sufficiently outrageous because it was crudely executed, transparently religious in motivation, and procedurally chaotic in implementation. It was, in other words, the easy case. The hard cases—the material support prosecution that turns on a translation, the No Fly designation that turns on an algorithm fed by classified data, the FISA Court order that no defendant will ever see—are the cases the federal judiciary has consistently declined to scrutinize seriously.

What would meaningful judicial engagement look like? Several things. First, narrowing of Holder, either by Congress (through amendment to the material support statute) or by the Court itself if a sufficiently sympathetic case reaches it. The doctrinal vehicle is available: the majority opinion in Holder expressly disclaimed application to "independent advocacy" and left room for narrowing through future cases. Second, revisiting Clapper's standing analysis in light of what we now know about Section 702 collection. Third, more aggressive application of the Due Process Clause to administrative deprivations in the security context, including watchlists, suspicious activity reports, and CVE assessments. Fourth, much greater scrutiny of the state secrets privilege, which has metastasized from a narrow evidentiary rule into a near-categorical bar on Fourth and Fifth Amendment claims involving classified programs.

None of this will happen on its own. It will happen, if at all, through sustained doctrinal advocacy by groups like the ACLU, the Center for Constitutional Rights, Muslim Advocates, and CAIR, supported by academic work that takes counterterrorism law seriously as a domain of constitutional doctrine rather than as a sui generis emergency regime. The legal academy has been better on these issues than the bench, but academic critique only translates into doctrine through cases, and cases require plaintiffs, lawyers, and a judiciary willing to engage.

Here I want to register a partial disagreement with the lead essay's framing. Akbar and Theoharis treat the courts as part of the problem and as occasional, contingent participants in the solution. That is descriptively accurate. But it understates the leverage that doctrinal change can have on the broader political economy of counterterrorism. The reason the executive branch built so much of its program around legal authorities that the Supreme Court had blessed (or that the FISA Court had blessed in secret) is that legal authority matters. Programs that lose their legal foundation become much harder to sustain politically. The Bush administration's warrantless wiretapping program was, in the end, brought into the FISA framework precisely because its legal vulnerability made it politically vulnerable. If Holder were narrowed by the Supreme Court, dozens of pending and future prosecutions would have to be reconsidered. If Clapper were revisited, the Section 702 program would face constitutional challenge on the merits for the first time.

The lead essay's call to "push the courts" is exactly right. But pushing requires a theory of what to push toward, and the theory has to be doctrinal as well as moral. Muslim Americans need lawyers who can articulate, in terms the federal judiciary will recognize, why the post-9/11 framework violates the Constitution. They have many such lawyers already. What they need from the rest of us is the kind of sustained public attention that makes courts feel they have room to rule against the government. That attention, again, is what the airport protests provided in microcosm. The question is whether we can sustain it through the longer, less televisable litigation that will determine whether the next fifteen years look like the last fifteen.

The Word "Islamophobia" Is Doing Too Much Work

Akbar and Theoharis have written an essay that I largely agree with but that I think is partially undone by a single word: Islamophobia. The word recurs throughout the piece as a unifying analytic. It links the Trump executive order, the material support statute, the No Fly List, CVE, drone strikes, surveillance, and the bipartisan rhetoric of figures from George W. Bush to Bill Clinton to Khizr Khan's critics. The authors describe Islamophobia as "a historical phenomenon rooted in the history of colonialism" and "a widely supported, entrenched social practice." I want to argue that this framing, while capturing something real, also obscures distinctions that matter for both diagnosis and remedy.

Consider the range of phenomena the word is being asked to cover. On one end, there is the explicit anti-Muslim animus of figures like Frank Gaffney, who believes the Muslim Brotherhood has infiltrated the U.S. government, or Pamela Geller, whose "Draw Muhammad" contests are designed to provoke. On another end, there are the bureaucratic outputs of programs designed without explicit anti-Muslim intent but which, because of how terrorism is defined and where threats are perceived, disproportionately affect Muslim communities. Between these poles sit a range of phenomena: the orientalist tropes about Muslim women that the authors rightly identify; the rhetorical "good Muslim/bad Muslim" sorting that Mahmood Mamdani analyzed; the public-opinion polling showing significant minorities of Americans willing to endorse discriminatory policies; the elite consensus around counterterrorism tools; the structural features of post-9/11 law.

Calling all of these "Islamophobia" risks suggesting they are all manifestations of the same underlying phenomenon, driven by the same causes, susceptible to the same critique. I do not think they are. The bureaucratic disproportion of counterterrorism enforcement is real, but it is not produced primarily by anti-Muslim animus among FBI agents or federal prosecutors. It is produced by the way the post-9/11 threat picture was defined, the way resources were allocated, the way statutes were drafted, the way courts deferred. Many of the individual actors involved would, if asked, sincerely affirm that they bear no hostility toward Islam or Muslims. Some of them are Muslims themselves. The disproportion is structural, and structural problems require structural diagnoses.

By contrast, Trump's executive order is a different kind of object. It was animated, by all available evidence, by explicit anti-Muslim motivation. Rudy Giuliani gave an interview describing how Trump asked him to figure out how to do a "Muslim ban" legally. The order itself contained provisions giving preference to "minority religions"—understood to mean Christians—from the affected countries. The animus is on the surface. This is a different phenomenon than the bureaucratic disproportion of the No Fly List, even if both can be described as "Islamophobic" in their effect.

Why does the distinction matter? Because the remedies are different. The remedy for explicit animus is exposure, political mobilization, and constitutional litigation under the Establishment Clause and equal protection doctrine. The judiciary has been good at recognizing animus when it is explicit; Judge Robart's injunction and the Ninth Circuit's affirmance, as well as the developing case law on the Trump administration's "Muslim ban" history, rely on precisely this kind of analysis. The remedy for structural disproportion is much harder. It requires statutory reform, doctrinal change, prosecutorial reorientation, and changes in resource allocation that the political system rarely produces absent crisis.

Collapsing these into a single category called "Islamophobia" can lead to two kinds of analytical mistakes. The first is the mistake of imagining that defeating the explicit-animus version (the ban, the campaign rhetoric, the Bannon ascendancy) will fix the structural version. This is the mistake the airport protestors are sometimes accused of making, and Akbar and Theoharis are right to push back against it. The second mistake, less often noticed, is the mistake of imagining that the structural version is just a less-honest expression of the explicit-animus version—that Obama's counterterrorism policy was simply Trump's policy minus the candor, that all the federal prosecutors and FBI agents involved are crypto-Gaffneys hiding their true views. This is, I think, the framing the lead essay leans toward, and it is wrong in ways that matter.

The reason it matters is that the structural version of the problem is harder to fix precisely because it is not animated by animus that can be exposed and shamed. The material support statute is supported by serious people for serious reasons: they believe that prosecuting Americans who provide cash and fighters to designated terrorist groups is necessary, and they are willing to live with the over-inclusivity at the margins. The CVE program was designed by people who believed, in good faith if often wrongly, that they were helping Muslim communities address a real problem. The No Fly List was expanded by officials who believed they were responding to actual intelligence gaps revealed by the underwear bomber case. Disagreeing with these people, persuading them they are wrong, building political coalitions to override them—this is the work of structural reform. It is not the work of denouncing Islamophobia.

There is a related point about the lead essay's treatment of bipartisan continuity. Akbar and Theoharis are right that Bush and Obama built the apparatus together and that Trump has inherited it. But the bipartisan consensus they describe is not, I think, primarily about anti-Muslim animus. It is about a particular theory of counterterrorism that emerged after 9/11 and that has had bipartisan adherents: that terrorism is a problem of catastrophic risk requiring preemption rather than reaction, that the legal architecture of ordinary criminal law and immigration is insufficient to that task, and that some erosion of civil liberties is an acceptable cost. This theory is wrong, in my view, or at least overdrawn. But it is a theory, defended by serious people with reasons, and arguing against it requires engaging the reasons rather than reducing them to prejudice.

The authors come closest to this in the Sixth Misapprehension, which acknowledges the role of the United States around the world and the connection between the global wars and the domestic security regime. This is important and underdeveloped. American counterterrorism policy is, in part, a function of American foreign policy commitments: to allies in the Middle East, to a particular configuration of force projection, to an open-ended understanding of the 2001 AUMF that has been used to justify operations in countries far from the original Afghan theater. Changing the domestic regime requires, ultimately, changing the foreign policy regime that produces the threats and shapes how they are understood. This is a much larger project than fighting Islamophobia. It is the project of changing American grand strategy.

I want to end by registering what I take to be the lead essay's most important contribution. The authors are right that the airport mobilization, while important, has rested on a frame that may not extend naturally to the harder cases. They are right that the post-Trump liberal mood is at risk of restoring the apparatus rather than dismantling it. They are right that the courts have been part of the problem more than they have been part of the solution. They are right that the war on drugs and the war on terror share a structure of policing and prosecution that disproportionately harms communities of color. These are important arguments and the lead essay makes them well.

My disagreement is with the unifying analytic. I do not think "Islamophobia," even understood structurally, captures the full range of what needs to be diagnosed and changed. Some of what needs changing is, in fact, about anti-Muslim animus, and we should name it as such. But much of what needs changing is about American grand strategy, about the political economy of national security, about the doctrinal failures of federal courts, about the bipartisan elite consensus on preemption, about the relationship between counterterrorism and mass incarceration. Each of these requires its own diagnosis and its own coalition. Folding them all under one term may feel rhetorically powerful, but it can obscure the specific work each requires.

The lead essay closes with the question of what may go "radically wrong" if we do not speak up now. Speaking up is necessary. Speaking up clearly, with the right diagnoses for the right problems, is what will make the speaking effective.
