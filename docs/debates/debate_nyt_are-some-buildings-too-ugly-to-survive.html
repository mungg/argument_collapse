Are Some Buildings Too Ugly to Survive?

The Orange County government building in Goshen, N.Y., has a leaky roof, faulty ventilation and mold and, in the eyes of many, is just plain ugly. Officials shut it down last year and would like to demolish and replace it. But it is a prime example of Brutalism, from the noted architect Paul Rudolph. Many want to preserve it, even though, like many examples of Brutalism, it has not aged well. Do even ugly, unpopular buildings deserve to be saved if they are significant? Or should a community, or owner, be allowed to eliminate architectural mistakes?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The question of whether to save Paul Rudolph's Orange County Government Center poses a false choice between aesthetic preference and historical significance. The harder, more honest question is who gets to decide, and on what timescale.

Brutalism is the architectural movement we love to hate. The very nickname, derived from Le Corbusier's *béton brut* but absorbed into English as a near-pun, has been an albatross around the movement's neck. Yet the same was once true of Victorian buildings, which mid-century modernists tore down with gusto, and of Art Deco, which preservationists had to fight to save in the 1970s. Penn Station was demolished in 1963 because mid-century New Yorkers found it bloated and old-fashioned; today its loss is the touchstone case for historic preservation. Taste cycles. The buildings we condemn as eyesores at age forty are often the ones our grandchildren plead to keep. Rudolph's Goshen building, completed in 1967, is right at the dangerous age—too old to feel fresh, too young to feel historic.

That doesn't mean every concrete pile deserves a reprieve. Rudolph is not a marginal figure; he chaired Yale's architecture school and his Yale Art and Architecture Building is canonical. The Orange County Government Center is one of his more ambitious civic experiments, an interlocking composition of 87 roofs meant to dramatize the workings of democratic government. Whatever one thinks of the result, it is the kind of building that scholars and architects will be studying long after the leaks are forgotten. Significance is not a popularity contest, and preservation law exists precisely because majorities in any given decade cannot be trusted to recognize what later generations will value.

At the same time, communities are not museums, and a county government cannot be conscripted into permanent stewardship of a failing building it never wanted. The roof leaks, the ventilation is broken, mold is colonizing the offices. If Orange County is asked to bear preservation's costs, preservationists need to come to the table with money, with adaptive-reuse plans, and with realistic answers about how the building can serve a working function. The DOCOMOMO chapter and the World Monuments Fund, which has put the building on its watch list, ought to be partners, not just protesters.

The right standard, then, is neither "save everything significant" nor "let owners eliminate mistakes." It is: significant buildings carry a presumption against demolition, but that presumption can be rebutted when the costs of preservation fall unfairly on a public that needs functioning facilities and when no credible alternative use emerges. Ugliness, in itself, should never be the deciding factor. We are simply too unreliable as judges of it.

Architecture is a strange art: it must not only be meaningful but also useful. The Orange County government building in Goshen, designed by Paul Rudolph, is now unloved and unusable—a leaky roof, mold, and faulty ventilation plague it. Its Brutalist style, once celebrated for its bold honesty, now strikes many as oppressive and cold. Yet, to demolish it simply because it is “ugly” would be a mistake, as tastes change. Victorian Gothic was once reviled, now cherished. But when a building fails so completely in function, the question shifts from aesthetics to practicality.

Preservationists argue that significant architecture deserves protection regardless of current fashion. Rudolph’s work is undeniably important; it represents a key moment in postwar modernism. But architecture exists for people, not just for history books. A building that is actively harming its occupants and draining public funds cannot be sustained indefinitely just because of its pedigree. The community’s voice matters: if the people who use it every day find it dysfunctional and demoralizing, their judgment should carry weight.

That said, demolition should not be the automatic response. A thoughtful community might instead commission a replacement that acknowledges the original’s place in history—perhaps incorporating salvaged elements or documenting the building thoroughly before it goes. We should not be too quick to erase our architectural past, even its mistakes. But we also should not be so beholden to architectural significance that we force people to live with a building that has, in every practical sense, failed. Sometimes, even significant buildings are, indeed, too ugly—and too broken—to survive.

The debate over the Orange County Government Center in Goshen, New York, captures a perennial tension in urban planning: the clash between architectural history and civic utility. Paul Rudolph’s Brutalist design is undoubtedly significant to architectural scholars. Yet, to the people who must work in and pay for it, the building is largely seen as an imposing, ugly failure plagued by leaks, mold, and faulty ventilation. While preservationists are right to argue that we should not demolish buildings based solely on fleeting aesthetic trends, a community must have the right to replace structures that fundamentally fail to serve their purpose.

History warns us against destroying buildings just because they have fallen out of fashion. Architectural tastes are notoriously cyclical. Victorian homes were once dismissed as haunted monstrosities, and Art Deco masterpieces were frequently razed to make way for generic glass boxes. Today, Brutalism is enduring its own period of intense public disdain. With its heavy, unyielding concrete forms, the style is undeniably alienating to the general public, making it easy to hate. Preservationists rightfully point out that if we tear down every Brutalist structure today, future generations will lament the loss of an entire era of architectural history.

However, architecture is fundamentally distinct from painting or sculpture because it must be inhabited. A government building is not merely a museum piece; it is a functioning workplace and a public utility. When a structure fails on basic metrics of health, safety, and operational efficiency, the argument for preservation weakens considerably. Orange County employees are not just facing an unpopular facade, but rather mold, poor air quality, and chronic water damage. When a building becomes a physical hazard and an unsustainable financial drain, its historical status must be weighed against the well-being of the community.

Furthermore, Brutalist buildings are uniquely difficult to retrofit. Their massive poured-concrete walls and rigid spatial organizations resist easy modernization. If a municipality cannot practically or economically update a structure to meet current environmental and workplace standards, forcing them to maintain it elevates academic architectural theory over the day-to-day civic realities of the people who rely on the space.

Not every building designed by a famous architect is a masterpiece, and not every example of a controversial movement deserves eternal life. We should actively identify and preserve the finest, most adaptable examples of Brutalism to protect our architectural heritage. But a community should not be held hostage by a failing structure just to appease architectural historians and critics from afar. When a building can no longer safely or effectively serve the public, demolition is not a cultural crime—it is a practical necessity.

Yes, some buildings are too unhealthy, wasteful or obstructive to survive. But “too ugly” is a dangerous standard. Ugliness is often the first verdict rendered on new architecture and the last one to be revised. Victorian ironwork, Art Deco towers and even cast-iron commercial buildings all had periods of being considered expendable. If preservation had always followed public taste at its most impatient, many cities would be poorer.

The Orange County Government Center is not just any inconvenient old office block. Paul Rudolph was a major American architect, and the building is a serious example of a postwar civic ideal: muscular, complex, anti-monumental in the classical sense, and meant to make government visible as a set of interlocking public functions. That does not make the leaks, mold or ventilation failures irrelevant. A public building must serve the public before it serves architectural history.

Can it be adapted to contemporary needs? Would replacement truly be cheaper over its life cycle, including demolition, new construction and the loss of embodied energy? Is there a way to preserve the most significant portions while improving or adding to the rest?

Reverence for an architect should not become indifference to employees who worked under a leaking roof.

Communities should be allowed to remove genuine failures. But they should be wary of calling a challenging building a mistake merely because it is unfashionable. A significant ugly building deserves a fair trial: evidence, alternatives and imagination before the wrecking ball.

The Orange County government building in Goshen represents a genuine dilemma, but the framing of the question—asking whether "ugly" buildings "deserve" preservation—contains a conceptual trap. A building's worth cannot be reduced to its immediate visual appeal, and communities that equate preservation with sentimentality often miss the point entirely.

Paul Rudolph was one of the defining figures of American modernism, and the Goshen building stands as a significant example of his mature Brutalist work. These are not merely buildings that failed to age gracefully; they are architectural documents of a particular moment when architects believed that raw material honesty and sculptural mass could express civic ambition. That vision may now seem dated or unappealing, but that judgment reflects our current taste, not some timeless aesthetic standard. The buildings we now cherish from earlier periods—Gothic cathedrals, Victorian civic structures—were often regarded as eyesores or inappropriate in their own time. Preservation, at its best, protects buildings not because everyone finds them beautiful but because they embody ideas worth remembering.

This does not mean every unpopular structure warrants rescue. The question of significance matters enormously. Rudolph's work, the Yale Art and Architecture Building in New Haven, the Boston City Hall complex—these have sparked genuine architectural discourse and influenced generations of designers. Their preservation advocates are not simply sentimentalists; they recognize that demolishing such buildings would impoverish our built environment and erase evidence of an important design philosophy. By contrast, a mediocre structure from any era, however beloved by locals, rarely justifies the resources and effort of preservation.

Practical considerations also complicate the picture. A building with genuine structural problems, accessibility deficiencies, or prohibitive maintenance costs presents a real dilemma. Yet these are often questions of investment and political will rather than architectural irrelevance. Rudolph's buildings have always been expensive to maintain; that was true when they were new. The solution to underfunding is not demolition but rather a serious commitment to stewardship.

Communities should not be forced to maintain buildings against their wishes, but neither should they be encouraged to view preservation as mere nostalgia. The Goshen building's problems—leaky roof, mold—are technical challenges, not evidence that the building lacks value. Demolishing it would solve an immediate problem while destroying something that, once gone, cannot be recovered. That trade-off deserves more careful consideration than officials eager to replace "ugly" architecture with something more conventionally palatable seem willing to give.

We have photographs, drawings, models, and scholarly monographs documenting Rudolph's work. The Yale Art and Architecture Building survives. Every generation hates the architecture of the one before it. Victorians demolished Georgian terraces. Modernists tore down Victorian piles by the thousand, dismissing them as fussy, dishonest, and unhygienic. Penn Station — now mourned as one of the great civic crimes of the twentieth century — was bulldozed in 1963 by people who found it gloomy and obsolete. If we had let popular opinion in 1963 decide what survived, we would have lost most of what we now consider the irreplaceable fabric of American cities.

This is the context in which the Orange County building should be judged. Brutalism is roughly where Victorian Gothic was in 1930 or where Art Deco was in 1965: out of fashion, associated with discredited ideas, expensive to maintain, easy to mock. That is precisely the moment when buildings are most vulnerable, and precisely the moment when preservation has to do its hardest work. By the time consensus comes around — and with Brutalism it is already beginning to, as a younger generation rediscovers Rudolph, Goldberg, and the Smithsons — the best examples are usually gone.

The Goshen building is not just any Brutalist box. Paul Rudolph was one of the most consequential American architects of the postwar period, chair at Yale, mentor to Norman Foster and Richard Rogers. His civic work is rare; most of his commissions were institutional or private. Losing the Orange County government center subtracts something specific and unrepeatable from the record.

The maintenance problems are real but not unusual for any fifty-year-old public building that has been deferred-maintained for decades. The leaks and mold did not happen because Rudolph designed them in; they happened because successive county administrations chose patch jobs over investment. Demolition is being framed as a forced choice, but it is a political choice dressed up as engineering necessity.

The debate over the Goshen building keeps getting framed as a contest between aesthetics and significance, as if those were the only two values in play. But there is a third value, and it is the one that ought to govern public architecture: usefulness to the public that paid for it.

A county government building exists to house the offices, records, and meetings of local government. When it cannot do that — when employees are getting sick, when records have to be moved out, when the public has to conduct its business in trailers — the building has failed at its primary job. Architectural significance does not exempt a structure from this basic accountability. If anything, civic buildings carry a higher obligation to serve, because they are not optional. Citizens cannot take their business elsewhere.

I do not think this means every troubled landmark should be flattened. It means preservation arguments have to take seriously the cost of preservation, and who bears it. In Goshen, that cost falls on a county of roughly 380,000 people, many of them not wealthy, who never asked for a Paul Rudolph in the first place. The renovation estimates that have circulated run into the tens of millions of dollars, and even then would not produce a building as functional as new construction at similar cost. Asking residents to pay a premium so that architecture students and critics elsewhere can have a study object is a hard sell, and it should be.

There are middle paths. Partial preservation — retaining a wing, a facade, or the dramatic interior staircases — could honor the building's significance without committing to the full carrying cost. Adaptive reuse by a private buyer, with public functions relocated, is another option that has worked elsewhere with Brutalist structures. These compromises take imagination, but they exist.

What I resist is both extremes: the reflexive demolitionist who treats the entire postwar period as a mistake to be erased, and the absolutist preservationist who treats every signed building as untouchable regardless of cost. Goshen deserves a serious cost-benefit conversation, not a moral one. The building's ugliness is beside the point; its functionality, and the price of restoring it, is not.

I want to defend a slightly heretical position: ugliness itself can be a reason to preserve a building.

Most of what we save, we save because we already like it. Beautiful buildings, charming buildings, beloved buildings — these have constituencies that lobby for them. The harder and more interesting question is whether a society can preserve things it finds difficult, alienating, or even repellent, because they tell the truth about a moment in its history.

Brutalism is one of those truths. It was the architectural language of postwar public ambition: universities expanding to absorb the baby boom, cities building new civic centers, governments asserting through raw concrete that they were modern, serious, and permanent. The style failed in many ways — it ignored climate, it ignored pedestrians, it ignored the fact that concrete weathers badly — but its failures are themselves historically eloquent. They tell us what midcentury Americans believed about authority, transparency (Rudolph loved the visible structure), and the relationship between government and citizen.

Demolishing the Goshen building because it is ugly is a way of pretending that period never happened, or that we have always had the tastes we have now. It is the architectural equivalent of editing the family album to remove the awkward decade. A mature culture keeps the awkward decade.

I would extend this further. We routinely preserve Civil War battlefields, slave quarters, tenement museums, and Cold War missile silos precisely because they are unlovely. Their discomfort is the point. Brutalism is not morally analogous to those things, of course, but the principle holds: significance is not synonymous with beauty, and a preservation regime that only saves the pretty is not a preservation regime at all — it is just curated nostalgia.

None of this requires Goshen to swallow a non-functional building forever. Fix the roof. Fix the ventilation. Treat it as you would treat any aging public asset with character: invest, adapt, and live with it. The argument that it is too ugly to be worth fixing is, in the end, an argument that we should only invest in what already pleases us. That has never been how a serious culture treats its inheritance.

The question "are some buildings too ugly to survive?" smuggles in an assumption worth challenging: that "ugly" is a stable category we can act on. It isn't. Within living memory, brownstones were ugly, cast-iron facades were ugly, Art Deco was kitsch, and the Eiffel Tower was a national embarrassment that prominent Parisians petitioned to remove. Aesthetic verdicts are the least durable thing about a building. The roof, the steel, and the concrete will all outlast our opinions about them.

That said, I do not think this means anything significant should be saved automatically. The honest answer to the Goshen question requires separating three issues that critics keep tangling together.

First, the functional issue. A building that cannot be made habitable for a reasonable sum is a different problem from a building that is merely unloved. The Orange County government center's condition is the result of decades of deferred maintenance more than of Rudolph's design, but the bill for catching up is real, and it is fair for a community to weigh it.

Second, the significance issue. Rudolph is a major figure, and Goshen is a substantial example of his civic work. There are not many of these. If we are going to have a meaningful record of mid-century American architecture in fifty years, buildings like this are the evidence. Documentation alone is not a substitute, any more than a photograph of a painting is a substitute for the painting.

Third, the democratic issue. Local communities do have standing to shape their own civic spaces, and outsiders should be humble about telling Goshen what to keep. But democratic legitimacy is not unlimited; we do not, for instance, let every generation revote on whether to keep Independence Hall. Some things belong to a longer conversation than a county budget cycle.

My own view: find a way to keep it. Not because it is beautiful — I do not find it so — but because the cost of being wrong about demolition is irreversible, and the cost of being wrong about preservation is only money and inconvenience. We can always tear it down in twenty years if we still want to. We cannot bring it back. Asymmetry of regret should guide us when taste cannot.

The Goshen debate has been framed as a clash between elite taste and popular will, but the real issue is neither ugliness nor significance—it is stewardship. A building can be both a masterpiece and a disaster, as Paul Rudolph’s Orange County Government Center painfully demonstrates. The brutalist icon is undeniably significant: its cascading volumes and corduroy-concrete walls represent a visionary attempt to make democratic governance spatially dramatic. Yet it is also undeniably failing, with water intrusion that predates the recent roof troubles and an interior plan so dysfunctional that it impeded the very civic engagement it meant to symbolize. The question should not be “Should we save it at all costs?” but “Can we save it, and to what end?”

The binary of preservation versus demolition misses the far more constructive middle ground of adaptive reuse. The building’s problems are, to a large extent, technical, not inherent to its brutalist form. A new roof membrane, updated mechanicals, and a careful reconfiguration of interior partitions could solve the functional crises while preserving the sculptural exterior and the most dramatic public spaces. This is not an untested model: Boston’s City Hall, another brutalist monument, has been the subject of serious rehabilitation studies, and Yale’s Rudolph-designed Art and Architecture Building underwent a celebrated restoration after a devastating fire. The cost would be higher than a generic box, but not necessarily higher than demolition, abatement, and new construction. The choice is not between a moldy relic and a clean slate; it is between a thoughtless write-off and a creative reinvestment in civic memory.

That said, preservation cannot become an absolute. If a rigorous engineering analysis shows that the concrete structure is itself compromised beyond repair, or if the cost of truly making it watertight and accessible proves genuinely prohibitive for this small county, then a respectful demolition may be the only honest path. In that case, the site should host a design competition that explicitly engages Rudolph’s legacy—perhaps a new building that incorporates salvaged elements or echoes the original’s spatial drama. The goal should never be to simply bulldoze an “ugly” mistake, but to engage with architectural history as a living, adaptable dialogue. A building’s survival should hinge not on whether it pleases the eye of a passerby, but on whether its fabric can be made to serve the future while honestly acknowledging the past.
