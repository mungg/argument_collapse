The South’s Enduring Conservativism

A recent survey by the Public Religion Research Institute found that while Gov. Mitt Romney is thought to have a solid lead over President Obama among white working class voters, that’s almost entirely because of his lead among those voters in the South, where he has a 40 percent edge. The survey, like a recent Gallup poll, found white working class voters in the South far more conservative on several social and political issues.

Decades after the apparent arrival of a post-racial New South, and after years of economic and social changes, why does the Deep South remain so much more conservative than the rest of the country?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The South's distinctiveness isn't a puzzle so much as the residue of a long, layered history that economic modernization has not dissolved. Three forces, working together, explain the persistence.

First, religion. The Deep South remains the most evangelical region in the country, and white evangelical Protestantism since the 1970s has been tightly fused to a politics of social traditionalism—on abortion, gay marriage, school prayer, gender roles. PRRI's own surveys consistently show that religiosity, more than income, predicts conservative positioning among Southern whites. A working-class voter in Birmingham attends church at rates a counterpart in Akron does not.

Second, the unfinished business of racial realignment. The Voting Rights Act and the Southern Strategy converted the Solid Democratic South into a Solid Republican one, but the conversion was generational. As V.O. Key argued decades ago, Southern politics has long been organized around race, and scholars like Earl and Merle Black have shown that white racial conservatism still structures partisanship below the Mason-Dixon line in ways it doesn't elsewhere. Obama's presidency has, if anything, sharpened that cleavage.

Third, political economy and geography. The "New South" boom enriched Atlanta, Charlotte, and the Research Triangle, but vast stretches of Mississippi, Alabama, and rural Georgia were bypassed. Union density collapsed, populist economic appeals withered, and right-to-work norms hardened. Working-class whites there encounter Democrats as cultural strangers, not as economic allies.

Post-racial New South notwithstanding, religion, race, and a stunted labor tradition continue to make the region's politics genuinely exceptional.

The Deep South’s enduring conservatism confounds predictions of a post-racial New South because it is rooted in a fusion of history, religion, and racial anxiety that economic modernization alone cannot dissolve. Forged in the crucible of the Civil War and Reconstruction, the region’s political identity carries a deep-seated suspicion of federal authority and a defiant regional pride. For a century, the “Solid South” was Democratic, but that loyalty was transactional—it rested on segregation and white supremacy. When the national Democratic Party embraced civil rights in the 1960s, white Southerners began a long migration to the Republican Party. Cemented by Nixon’s Southern strategy and Reagan’s states’-rights appeals, the realignment made the South the GOP’s base.

Today, evangelical religion is the visible glue of this conservatism. The Bible Belt’s churches underwrite a moral framework that venerates traditional family structures, opposes abortion and same-sex marriage, and regards secular government as a threat. These convictions mobilize white working-class voters who view Democrats as hostile to their values. Beneath religiosity, racial attitudes remain potent, if less openly expressed. The Obama presidency has heightened a sense of cultural displacement among Southern whites, who resent a Democratic coalition perceived as prioritizing minorities. Economic anxiety amplifies these resentments: as manufacturing jobs vanish, the GOP’s message of self-reliance and limited government resonates more than liberal calls for expanded social programs. The Sun Belt’s growth has created liberal urban pockets, but the region’s rural and small-town heartland remains unshaken. The South is not simply conservative by inertia; its politics emerge from a history and culture that modern economic change has yet to rewrite.

The enduring conservatism of the white working class in the Deep South is not an anomaly defying recent history, but rather the predictable culmination of fifty years of political realignment. While the "New South" has undoubtedly experienced profound economic modernization and demographic shifts, its core cultural and historical pillars remain largely intact, anchoring the region firmly to the right.

First and foremost is the inescapable influence of evangelical Protestantism. The South remains the American Bible Belt, where conservative religious institutions shape not just Sunday worship, but an everyday worldview. For these voters, social issues—particularly abortion, marriage, and the preservation of traditional family structures—are not peripheral policy debates; they are central moral imperatives. The modern Republican Party has successfully branded itself as the political vehicle for these evangelical values, cementing a fierce loyalty that economic arguments from Democrats often struggle to penetrate.

Furthermore, we cannot ignore the ongoing reality of racial polarization. Following the civil rights movement, the massive exodus of conservative white Southern voters from the Democratic Party to the GOP fundamentally reorganized the region’s politics. Today, the Southern Democratic Party is heavily reliant on a strong base of African American voters. Consequently, for many white working-class Southerners, voting Republican has evolved into a deeply ingrained cultural marker—a default expression of regional and group identity that distinguishes them from the progressive, multi-ethnic coalition of the national Democratic Party.

Finally, there remains a historical, distinctly Southern skepticism of federal intervention. Dating back well before the current administration, white Southerners have harbored a cultural distrust of national elites dictating local norms. The region’s conservatism persists because it is a powerful synthesis of religious traditionalism, deeply polarized racial demographics, and an enduring preference for local autonomy.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

The Deep South’s conservatism is not a historical accident that somehow survived modernization; it is partly a product of the way modernization occurred there. The region industrialized later, with weaker unions, lower wages, fewer large immigrant communities and a political economy built around low taxes, business recruitment and skepticism toward federal labor and welfare regulation. Those conditions did not produce the same white working-class politics that emerged in much of the North and Midwest.

Race remains central, though not always in crude or explicit form. For much of the 20th century, white Southern politics was organized around resistance to federal intervention, first in defense of segregation and later in opposition to busing, affirmative action, voting-rights enforcement and social programs perceived as benefiting “others.” The civil rights revolution shattered the old Democratic South, and the parties gradually sorted themselves: black voters became overwhelmingly Democratic, while many white voters moved Republican. That racial sorting reinforced ideological sorting.

Religion also matters. White evangelical Protestantism is more institutionally powerful in the Deep South than in most other regions. Churches are not just places of worship; they are social networks, moral authorities and political information systems. On abortion, same-sex marriage, school prayer and gender roles, they help sustain a worldview in which national liberalism appears alien and threatening.

Finally, conservatism in the South is cultural as well as economic. Many white voters see federal power through a long regional memory of outside coercion, even when they rely on federal spending. Appeals to self-reliance, gun rights, military service and local control resonate because they are tied to identity.

None of this means the South is static. Atlanta, Charlotte, the Research Triangle and minority population growth complicate the picture. But among white working-class voters, history, religion, race and party identity still reinforce one another with unusual force.

The persistence of Southern conservatism defies simple explanation, rooted in a confluence of historical memory, cultural identity, and economic structure that has proven remarkably durable. The Civil War and Reconstruction left deep psychological and institutional scars; Southern whites developed a fierce independence from federal authority that later fused with Republican conservatism. This political identity solidified through the mid-twentieth-century realignment, when Democratic support for civil rights legislation forced a wholesale party switch that left the region's political infrastructure intact under a new partisan banner.

Beyond partisan politics, the South's religious landscape—dominated by evangelical Protestantism with its emphasis on personal morality, individual responsibility, and traditional family structures—provides a cultural framework aligned with conservative ideology. The region's weaker social safety net has also meant greater reliance on family, church, and community networks, reinforcing values of self-reliance that complement conservative economics.

Perhaps most significantly, racial attitudes remain a potent force. Decades of political strategy have linked Democratic liberalism with federal overreach on race, even as actual racial conditions have evolved. Economic anxieties, particularly among working-class whites facing deindustrialization, easily attach to cultural grievances about changing social norms.

None of these factors exists in isolation. Together they create a self-reinforcing political culture where conservative positions on social issues, economics, and race interlock. Demographic and economic change alone have proven insufficient to dissolve these attachments—identity, memory, and community prove more enduring than pundit predictions of imminent transformation.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The simplest explanation for the South's enduring conservatism is also the most uncomfortable one: race. The PRRI numbers don't show some peculiar Southern fondness for low marginal tax rates or a region-specific anxiety about Iranian centrifuges. They show a 40-point gap among white working-class voters between a white challenger and the first black president. That is not a coincidence, and pretending otherwise requires heroic feats of euphemism.

Political scientists have spent decades documenting what happened after the Civil Rights Act and the Voting Rights Act. The Solid Democratic South, built on Jim Crow, did not survive integration. It migrated, slowly at first under Nixon's Southern Strategy, then en masse under Reagan, into the Republican column. Recent work by Larry Bartels, Thomas Edsall, and others has consistently found that racial attitudes — not economic populism, not religion alone — best predict the partisan realignment of Southern whites.

The post-racial New South was always more of a marketing campaign than a sociological reality. Atlanta got skyscrapers and a CNN headquarters; rural Georgia still has counties where black and white residents attend different churches, different proms, and now vote for different parties at rates that would have been familiar to V.O. Key. The economic transformation of the Sun Belt brought transplants and capital, but it didn't dissolve the underlying racial structure of Southern politics — it gave that structure new vocabulary about "welfare," "states' rights," and "taking our country back."

None of this means individual Southern conservatives are bigots. Most aren't, and most would be genuinely offended at the suggestion. But political behavior is a mass phenomenon, and the mass pattern is stark. When you control for income, education, and religiosity, being a white Southerner remains an independently powerful predictor of conservative voting. Something specific to the region is doing work, and the historical candidates for that "something" are not mysterious.

Until commentators are willing to name this honestly, we will keep writing the same puzzled essay every four years, marveling at the South's "distinctiveness" while carefully avoiding the one variable that has organized its politics since Reconstruction.

The framing of this question annoys me, and I think it should annoy anyone who actually lives in the South. The assumption baked into "why is the South so conservative?" is that liberal coastal politics represents some default human condition from which Southerners have inexplicably deviated. Flip the question: why does the Northeast keep voting for politicians who can't balance a state budget? Why does California keep electing people who've presided over the hollowing-out of its middle class? Nobody writes those essays.

Southerners are conservative because conservatism, broadly understood, actually works as a way of organizing a life. Get married before you have kids. Go to church. Work hard. Don't expect the government to fix what your own bad choices broke. Own a firearm and know how to use it. These aren't exotic regional folkways; they're the operating instructions for a stable society, and they happen to be ones that secular coastal elites have spent fifty years sneering at.

The PRRI poll showing a Romney lead in the South isn't evidence of pathology. It's evidence that one part of the country has resisted a set of cultural changes — the collapse of marriage norms, the deference to credentialed expertise, the casual contempt for religious practice — that another part has embraced. Whether that resistance is admirable or backward depends entirely on whether you think those changes have been good for the country. Looking at illegitimacy rates, debt levels, and the general tenor of public life, I'd say the case for the Southern position is stronger than its critics admit.

It's true that race plays some role in the partisan gap. It would be strange if it didn't, given history. But the constant reduction of Southern conservatism to racial resentment is itself a kind of bigotry — a refusal to credit tens of millions of people with the capacity to hold political views for the same reasons anyone else does. Southerners are perfectly capable of disliking Obama's healthcare law on its merits, just as Vermonters are capable of liking it on theirs.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Religion is the variable most often underweighted in these discussions. The Deep South is not just conservative; it is the most religiously observant region in a country that is itself an outlier among wealthy democracies. Evangelical Protestantism — particularly the Southern Baptist Convention and the constellation of independent and Pentecostal churches around it — saturates daily life in a way that is genuinely difficult to convey to people who haven't lived it.

This matters politically for at least three reasons. First, on the specific social issues that move working-class voters — abortion, same-sex marriage, school prayer, the role of religion in public life — evangelical theology produces conservative positions almost automatically. Second, church networks are extraordinarily efficient at political mobilization. A pastor's casual remark on Sunday reaches a precinct's worth of voters by Sunday afternoon. Third, and most subtly, a worldview rooted in personal sin and individual redemption tends to be inhospitable to structural explanations of poverty or inequality. If your basic frame is "people fail because they sin and succeed because they're saved," then progressive policy arguments land on stony ground.

The PRRI survey, like most good polling on this, finds that once you control for religious attendance and affiliation, the regional gap shrinks substantially — though it doesn't disappear. White working-class voters in the South aren't just Republicans; they're churchgoing Republicans, and the churchgoing does a lot of the explanatory work.

This also helps explain why economic changes haven't moved the needle the way commentators keep expecting. The South has urbanized, industrialized, and integrated into the global economy, but the religious infrastructure has if anything intensified. Megachurches dot the suburbs of Atlanta and Dallas. Christian radio dominates the rural dial. The Bible Belt did not buckle under modernity; it absorbed modernity and kept its theology.

To understand Southern political behavior, in other words, you have to take seriously what Southerners themselves usually say when asked: their faith comes first, and their politics follow from it. That is not a story journalists from secular newsrooms find easy to write, which is why it tends to come in a distant second to racial explanations that are partially, but only partially, correct.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

We should be careful about treating "the South" as a monolith in the first place. Northern Virginia votes like Maryland. Atlanta votes like a Northern city. The Research Triangle in North Carolina helped deliver that state to Obama in 2008. South Florida is its own universe. What the PRRI poll is really capturing is not "the South" but a particular slice of it: the rural and small-town Deep South, plus the working-class white populations of the Appalachian and Gulf Coast regions.

That clarification matters because it points to an explanation that is less about Southern exceptionalism and more about a national pattern. Across the entire country, rural and small-town white voters without college degrees have moved sharply Republican over the last generation. They've done so in Pennsylvania, Ohio, upstate New York, downstate Illinois, and rural Oregon. The South looks distinctive in aggregate polls only because it has, proportionally, more of these voters than other regions do.

In other words, the puzzle may not be "why is the South so conservative" but "why does the South contain so much of the demographic that has become conservative nationwide?" And that question has straightforward answers. The South industrialized late, urbanized late, and retained a larger rural population for longer. Its educational attainment rates remain below national averages. Its economy still leans heavily on manufacturing, agriculture, and extractive industries — sectors that have been pummeled by globalization and that breed exactly the cultural anxieties Republicans have learned to mobilize.

This reframing doesn't dissolve the role of race or religion; both are real and both are amplified in the South. But it does suggest that the appropriate comparison isn't "South vs. rest of country" but "rural working-class whites everywhere vs. everyone else." When you make that comparison, the South looks less like a regional aberration and more like the leading edge of a national class-and-geography realignment that's reshaping politics from West Virginia to Wisconsin.

The Deep South is conservative because it is disproportionately the kind of place — economically, demographically, culturally — that has been trending conservative across the entire United States.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

History is sticky. That is the unfashionable lesson of Southern politics, and it's why each generation of commentators is surprised anew that economic growth and demographic change haven't produced the predicted convergence with the rest of the country.

Consider what we are actually asking the South to do. We are asking a region that lost a war 150 years ago, endured a punitive Reconstruction, built and then was forced to dismantle a comprehensive racial caste system, and watched its agricultural economy collapse twice in the twentieth century — we are asking that region to develop the same political culture as Massachusetts. The wonder is not that the Deep South remains distinctive but that anyone ever expected otherwise.

Path dependence in politics is well established. Robert Putnam's work on Italy showed that the civic patterns of medieval city-states still predict regional governance outcomes today. Closer to home, work by economists like Nathan Nunn has documented that American counties with higher historical concentrations of slavery still have worse contemporary outcomes on a striking range of measures, including political attitudes among white residents. The institutions are gone; the cultural residue persists across generations.

What does this residue look like in practice? A deep skepticism of federal power, born in Reconstruction and renewed by the civil rights era. A populist suspicion of Northern elites that translates easily into suspicion of coastal media, universities, and Democratic politicians. A honor-based culture, documented by Nisbett and Cohen, that responds to perceived insults with disproportionate force — and that finds in modern conservatism a vocabulary of cultural defense. A particular Protestant tradition shaped by frontier revivalism rather than by the more establishmentarian Northern denominations.

None of these things turn off when a Mercedes plant opens in Alabama or when Atlanta builds another light rail line. Economic modernization changes the surface of Southern life; the underlying political culture changes on a much slower clock, measured in generations rather than business cycles.

The PRRI numbers, then, are less a snapshot of 2012 than a long-exposure photograph in which the nineteenth and twentieth centuries are still faintly visible. Expecting them to fade quickly is to misunderstand how political cultures work.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Let me offer a more cynical answer than the others on offer: the Deep South is conservative because the Republican Party has invested enormous resources, over four decades, in making it so, and Democrats have largely conceded the ground.

Political behavior isn't just a reflection of underlying culture. It's a product of organization, recruitment, messaging, and money. The Republican Party in the 1970s and 1980s made a deliberate strategic decision — call it the Southern Strategy, call it whatever you like — to invest in evangelical outreach, talk-radio infrastructure, state legislative races, and judicial appointments across the South. That investment compounded. Today, a young white Southerner grows up in a media environment dominated by conservative talk radio and Fox News, attends a church whose denominational leadership is openly aligned with the GOP, and is represented at every level of government by Republicans who deliver concrete benefits to their constituents.

Meanwhile, the Democratic Party essentially abandoned the white South after 1994. With a handful of exceptions, the party stopped recruiting credible candidates for rural Southern districts, stopped investing in state parties, and stopped trying to construct an economic message that might compete with cultural conservatism. The result is a region where, in many counties, no Democrat has appeared on a ballot for years. Voters cannot choose what is not offered to them.

This is not the whole story — race, religion, and history all play their roles — but it is a part of the story that gets systematically underweighted because it implicates the present rather than the past. The PRRI poll showing Romney up 40 points among Southern white working-class voters reflects forty years of one party showing up and the other not bothering.

It also suggests, contrary to most of the fatalistic commentary, that the situation is not permanent. Political conditions made by deliberate organizational choices can be unmade by deliberate organizational choices. The South was solidly Democratic for a century, then it flipped. To assume it cannot flip again, or at least become genuinely competitive, is to mistake a recent equilibrium for a natural law.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Class, not just race or religion, deserves more attention here. The PRRI survey is specifically about white working-class voters, and that demographic specificity matters. White college-educated voters in the South, while still right-of-center, are nothing like 40 points for Romney. The regional gap is largely a working-class gap.

What does it mean to be working-class in the contemporary South? It often means working in a non-union manufacturing job, or in service work, or in the public sector at the lower rungs. It means living in a state where the safety net is thinner than almost anywhere else in the country, where wages are lower, and where the gap between economic precarity and the rhetoric of self-reliance is enormous. Paradoxically, these are exactly the conditions one might expect to produce left-populist politics — and historically, in the era of Huey Long and the Southern Farmers' Alliance, they sometimes did.

They don't now, and the reasons are revealing. The Democratic Party, since the 1970s, has gradually reconfigured itself as a coalition of educated professionals and minority voters. Its cultural style — secular, urban, university-credentialed — is alienating to working-class whites everywhere, but especially in a region where those cultural markers map onto a long-standing resentment of outsiders. Add to that the genuine economic policy convergence between the parties on trade, finance, and labor law, and the Democrats simply don't offer Southern working-class whites a compelling economic alternative to vote for. So they vote their cultural identity instead, and that identity has been ably curated by conservatives.

The deeper tragedy is that this leaves the most economically vulnerable region of the country with the politics least equipped to address its vulnerability. Mississippi has the worst poverty in America and the most conservative congressional delegation. Alabama leads the nation in disability claims and elects politicians who want to cut disability. This is not because Southerners are foolish; it is because no one is currently offering them a politics that takes both their economic interests and their cultural commitments seriously.

Until someone does, the PRRI numbers will keep looking like they do.

The South’s enduring conservatism cannot be understood apart from race. The region’s white working class did not suddenly become more conservative in 2012 — it has always been animated by a racial order under threat. The Public Religion Research Institute survey that finds Mitt Romney with a 40-point lead among these voters in the South is not primarily about tax policy or even religion; it is about which party is seen as defending a traditional hierarchy. From Reconstruction through the Civil Rights era, Southern whites forged a political identity around resisting federal intervention and black advancement. The parties have realigned, but the emotional architecture remains. When a white working-class voter in Mississippi says government is too big, he often means a government that enforces desegregation, affirmative action, and anti-discrimination laws. Obama’s presidency, however post-racial the New South was supposed to be, has revived those old anxieties. The racial resentment scales sociologists use show that these feelings are far more predictive of conservative views in the South than in the North. Economic change — the decline of textile mills, the rise of low-wage service jobs — only sharpens a sense of scarcity. Fear that “they” are getting something “we” are not feeds a politics that wants to pull up the drawbridge. Decades of racialized messaging by Republican strategists have locked in this alignment. So long as the GOP can signal, subtly or overtly, that it stands with the heritage and social status of white Southerners, the region will remain a deep red fortress. The “post-racial” New South was a media mirage; the survey merely confirms that the old cleavages, modernized but never truly healed, still run down the spine of the old Confederacy.

Religion is the engine of Southern conservatism. The Gallup and PRRI findings on white working-class voters track almost perfectly with a map of evangelical Protestantism. Across the Deep South, church attendance far outpaces the rest of the country, and the theological orientation is predominantly conservative. For tens of millions of Southerners, the Bible is the lens through which every social issue is interpreted. Abortion, same-sex marriage, and the perceived erosion of the traditional family are not merely political planks; they are existential battles between godly and secular worldviews. This religious identity translates directly into the ballot box. Mitt Romney, a Mormon, nonetheless benefits because Southern evangelicals see the Republican Party as the last viable vehicle for their moral agenda, especially with a president they view as hostile to religious liberty. The Affordable Care Act’s contraception mandate, for example, is framed in Southern pulpits as an assault on Christian conscience. Moreover, the cultural infrastructure of the South reinforces this. The church is not just a Sunday commitment but the community’s social center, where views on education, parenting, and authority are repeatedly normalized. The so-called economic anxieties of the white working class are often filtered through a moral narrative that blames liberal permissiveness for family breakdown and poverty. Until this religious soil changes — and secularization is far slower here than in the Northeast or West — the political conservatism will stay stubbornly intact. The God gap is the conservatism gap.

We are looking at the wrong map. The notion of an enduring, monolithic Southern conservatism crumbles when you adjust for urban and suburban growth. By 2012, states like Virginia, North Carolina, and Florida are genuine swing territory, while Georgia and Texas are trending less monolithic as metro Atlanta, Houston, and Dallas diversify. The PRRI and Gallup surveys emphasize “white working class in the South,” but that’s an increasingly narrow slice. Decades of in-migration from the North, Latino immigration, and the rise of a college-educated, information-sector workforce have transformed the political geography. What looks like static conservatism from 30,000 feet is actually a tale of two Souths: an older, rural, less-educated South that remains deeply conservative, and a fast-growing, suburban and urban South that is far more moderate, even liberal on some social issues. The flashy headlines about a 40-point Romney lead among working-class whites obscure the fact that those voters are shrinking as a share of the electorate. Presidential elections hinge not on massive margins among a declining demographic but on turnout and coalition-building. In 2008, Obama won North Carolina, Virginia, and Florida. In 2012, Virginia may well go blue again. The South is not static; it is being outgrown by its own metropolitan centers. The real story is not “the South’s enduring conservatism” but the long, slow nationalization of Southern politics, which erodes regional exceptionalism more every cycle.

Diversified DeepSeek V4 Pro

To understand why the white working class in the Deep South is so conservative, follow the money — or, more precisely, follow the economic structure that has defined the region for a century. The South’s political economy was built on low-wage, low-regulation, anti-union labor markets. From the plantation system through the textile mill villages to today’s auto plants in right-to-work states, the region’s elites have cultivated a workforce hostile to government intervention because government intervention means unions, minimum wages, and safety nets that might empower labor. The white working-class voters whom Romney commands by 40 points are not irrational; they are responding rationally to a narrative that has been institutionalized: that taxes and regulation threaten the very industries that keep small towns alive, that collective bargaining is a Northern import that sows discord, and that individual effort, not government aid, is the path to dignity. This anti-statist ethos is deeply racialized, of course, but it cannot be reduced to racism alone. The South’s poverty rates historically meant that many whites were one bad harvest or one mill closing away from destitution. The New Deal and Great Society programs offered a lifeline but also came with federal mandates that local elites resented.
