Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Miguel's essay is a model of cautious optimism, and the caution is well-placed. But I want to push back on the framing of the question—"Is it Africa's turn?"—and on some of the causal stories embedded in his answer. The risk in this kind of essay is that we mistake favorable tailwinds for structural transformation, and then design policy as if the transformation were real.

Consider the growth story. Miguel rightly notes that sub-Saharan Africa is averaging roughly 3 percent per capita growth since 2000—respectable, but, as he concedes, achieved without the industrial transformation that distinguished the East Asian miracles. Strip out the resource exporters—Angola, Nigeria, Sudan, Equatorial Guinea, Chad—and the picture looks considerably less impressive. The continent's terms of trade have improved dramatically because Chinese demand has bid up oil, copper, and (to a lesser extent) coffee and cotton. This is a price effect, not a productivity effect. Dani Rodrik and others have argued for years that sustained growth requires moving labor from low-productivity agriculture into tradable manufacturing. By Miguel's own admission, that is not happening. African manufacturing as a share of GDP has, if anything, drifted downward since 1990. A commodity boom that ends—and they always end—could expose how little of this growth was structural.

Second, the democratization-causes-growth link deserves more scrutiny than Miguel gives it. The Freedom House aggregate has improved, but much of that improvement reflects the easy transition from outright tyranny to competitive-authoritarian regimes—what Levitsky and Way have described as hybrid systems that hold elections without delivering accountability. Kenya's December 2007 debacle is exhibit A, but it is hardly unique: Ethiopia's 2005 elections, Nigeria's 2007 vote, Uganda's manipulated term limits, Zimbabwe's ongoing horror show. The Sen argument about famines and free press is powerful, but it concerns the prevention of catastrophic policy failure, not the engineering of sustained growth. Botswana and Mauritius—Africa's genuine success stories—were democratic from the start. The question is whether the new democracies will consolidate or hollow out, and on present evidence the latter is at least as likely.

Third, I think Miguel is too sanguine about Chinese engagement, even as he flags the Chambishi mine and Sudan. The deeper problem is not labor practices but the political economy of resource-for-infrastructure deals. When Beijing finances a railway in exchange for mineral concessions, it does so on terms negotiated with incumbent governments that are not obliged to disclose them. This deprives African legislatures and civil society—the very institutions Miguel celebrates—of leverage over their own resource endowments. The "resource curse" literature (Collier, Humphreys, Sachs and Warner) suggests that opaque resource rents tend to corrode institutional development, fund patronage networks, and foster the kind of winner-take-all politics that produced Kenya's electoral crisis in the first place.

Fourth, Miguel's RCPS proposal is intriguing, and I'd endorse expanding it. But the binding constraint on conflict prevention is rarely money; it's the absence of credible commitment devices among rival elites. Botswana's Drought Relief Program works because Botswana already had a functioning state. Exporting the program without exporting the institutions may not yield the same dividend.

None of this is to deny that things are better than they were in 1995. They clearly are. But "Africa's turn" is the wrong frame. The right question is whether the current window—high commodity prices, Chinese capital, debt relief, modest democratization—is being used to build the productive and institutional foundations that will outlast it.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Edward Miguel’s lead essay captures the tantalizing sense of possibility that many African watchers felt in the mid-2000s. The Busia he revisits is emblematic: paved roads, new businesses, and political opening. Yet his own account undercuts the idea that these improvements amount to a genuine turning point. The violence that followed Kenya’s 2007 election, the fragility of manufacturing, and the persistence of armed conflict across the continent all suggest that what looks like an economic miracle may be more a commodity-driven bubble than a structural transformation.

Miguel rightly notes that Africa’s recent growth has been powered largely by soaring demand and prices for oil, copper, coffee, and other raw materials. Rising commodity prices put money into the pockets of exporters and, through multiplier effects, into border towns like Busia. But this kind of growth is notoriously fickle. The last time commodity prices surged in the 1970s, African economies also enjoyed a brief expansion—only to collapse when prices fell in the 1980s. As Miguel acknowledges, manufacturing remains a small share of output in most African countries. The erosion of textile gains under AGOA after Chinese competition intensified shows how dependent even new sectors were on temporary trade preferences rather than genuine competitiveness. The Chambishi mine story he relates, with its deadly working conditions and sub-minimum wages, suggests that the boom’s benefits remain unevenly distributed and ethically questionable. If China’s growth slows or global demand softens, many African economies will have little to fall back on. The fact that foreign aid, as Miguel demonstrates, does not correlate with growth only reinforces the point: the current uptick is driven by external windfalls, not by deep institutional or technological change.

The political opening is equally fragile. Miguel’s own Freedom House figures show that the typical African country still ranks only a “4” on a scale where 1 is most democratic—hardly a consolidated democracy. The relapse in Kenya is not an isolated horror story but a reminder that elections without strong institutions can become flashpoints for ethnic violence. Côte d’Ivoire and Zimbabwe, once touted as success stories, imploded similarly when leaders refused to accept electoral outcomes. Miguel’s historical parallel with post-independence Latin America is instructive but cuts both ways: Latin American states took nearly a century to stabilize after independence, and many remained trapped in cycles of dictatorship and economic stagnation well into the twentieth century. If Africa is following a similar path, we should not confuse a few years of rising GDP with a permanent escape from poverty and conflict.

The conflict data Miguel presents underscores the persistent danger. Even as some wars end, new ones erupt, and the overall proportion of African countries at war has not declined since 1995. His proposal for “rapid conflict prevention support” is sensible, but it is also an admission that the economic success stories remain acutely vulnerable to drought, price drops, or political miscalculation. The fact that Kenya’s crisis occurred during good economic times suggests that growth alone does not defuse ethnic tensions or elite power struggles. Botswana’s drought insurance succeeded partly because it was embedded in a long-standing, reasonably well-governed democracy—a rare combination in the region.

None of this means that Africa is doomed. Countries such as Ghana, Senegal, and post-conflict Mozambique are making incremental progress, and technological changes like the spread of cell phones bring real efficiency gains. But the leap from patchy improvement to a continent-wide “development miracle” requires a far more demanding transformation: the building of accountable states, diversified economies, and cross-ethnic political bargains that can survive the end of a commodity boom or the heat of a contested election. Miguel ends by acknowledging that it is “still too early to know” if Africa’s time is now. The evidence he assembles, read carefully, suggests the honest answer in 2008 is probably no—at least not yet.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Edward Miguel provides a sobering yet cautiously optimistic assessment of sub-Saharan Africa’s recent trajectory. By grounding his analysis in the tangible, albeit fragile, economic renaissance of towns like Busia, he captures the duality of the continent in 2008: unprecedented growth rates and democratic gains, shadowed by the persistent threats of political violence and climate vulnerability. However, while Miguel rightly identifies the structural weaknesses in Africa's current boom—most notably its reliance on commodity exports and a stagnant manufacturing sector—he perhaps understates just how deeply these factors threaten the sustainability of this "development miracle."

Miguel correctly identifies the voracious appetite of a rising China as a primary engine of Africa’s recent economic growth. Surging prices for copper, oil, and agricultural goods have undoubtedly enriched nations like Zambia and Angola. Yet, history is littered with developing regions that mistook a cyclical commodity boom for permanent structural transformation. By Miguel’s own admission, industrial transformation is not driving Africa’s growth, and the manufacturing sector remains as small today as it was in 2000. This is not merely a footnote to the continent's success; it is the central vulnerability of the current narrative.

The nature of Sino-African trade actively reinforces this vulnerability. While Miguel astutely points out the human rights and labor abuses associated with Chinese foreign direct investment, the broader macroeconomic dynamic is equally troubling. China is effectively replicating the colonial economic model: extracting raw, unrefined resources while flooding African markets with cheap manufactured goods. As Miguel notes, the 2005 expiration of the Multi-Fiber Agreement allowed low-cost Chinese factories to outcompete African textile producers. Without the ability to compete in global manufacturing, African economies cannot climb the value-added ladder necessary for long-term, self-sustaining development. When the current commodity super-cycle inevitably cools, economies built solely on resource extraction will face severe contractions.

Given this precarious economic foundation, Miguel’s skepticism toward the "big push" foreign aid models championed by Jeffrey Sachs is well-founded. The historical disconnect between massive aid influxes and sustained economic growth in the 1980s and 1990s suggests that simply pouring more money into weak state institutions is not a panacea.

Instead, Miguel’s proposal for Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS) stands out as a highly pragmatic, evidence-based policy alternative. By treating aid as an insurance mechanism against the specific economic shocks—such as droughts or commodity price crashes—that precipitate civil violence, the international community could protect fragile states during their most vulnerable moments. The Botswana model of drought relief proves that targeted social safety nets can prevent the desperation that fuels conflict. As climate models predict increasingly severe conditions for the Sahel, RCPS offers a proactive, rather than reactive, approach to regional stability.

Yet, an insurance mechanism, no matter how intelligently designed, is not an engine for growth; it is a shock absorber. For Africa’s current progress to solidify into a true development miracle, the continent must transition away from an over-reliance on primary resource extraction. The international community can assist this transition, as Miguel suggests, by dismantling the hypocritical agricultural subsidies in the United States and Europe that depress global prices and punish African farmers.

Ultimately, the energy and optimism witnessed in Kenya before the tragic 2007 election violence demonstrate the profound resilience of the African people. But unless African nations can leverage this current commodity windfall to build diversified, industrialized economies, this decade's growth may ultimately be remembered as a fleeting boom rather than the dawn of Africa’s turn.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Edward Miguel is right to resist both Afro-pessimism and easy celebration. The Busia he describes—new roads, supermarkets, mobile phones, and cross-border trade—is real, and it matters. Too many discussions of Africa still proceed as if nothing has changed since the worst years of famine, structural adjustment, and civil war. Yet the central question is not whether Africa has improved since 2000. It has. The question is whether the improvement rests on foundations strong enough to survive bad politics, bad weather, and bad luck.

On that point, I would put more emphasis than Miguel does on the character of growth. A continent can grow rapidly for several years without undergoing a development transformation. Higher oil, copper, coffee, and other commodity prices have filled government coffers and raised some rural incomes. Debt relief and better macroeconomic management have also helped. But commodity booms are not the same as industrialization, and they can even postpone it. They strengthen currencies, draw talent toward government and extractive sectors, and tempt rulers to distribute rents rather than build states. Nigeria and Angola may record impressive growth rates, but if oil revenue does not generate jobs, power, roads, courts, and accountable public finance, then the boom will remain politically dangerous.

The missing link is employment. Africa’s demographic future will be decided not by GDP averages but by whether young people can find work outside subsistence farming and informal petty trade. Miguel notes the disappointing performance of manufacturing, and this deserves to be at the center of the debate. East Asia’s miracle was not simply export growth; it was labor-intensive export growth, accompanied by learning, discipline, and rising productivity. In many African countries, the factory floor remains too expensive because electricity is unreliable, transport costs are high, customs procedures are slow, credit is scarce, and contract enforcement is weak. A paved road to Busia is not a symbol only of progress; it is a reminder of how much of the continent’s potential is still trapped by logistics.

Democracy is part of the answer, but here too caution is necessary. Elections can discipline leaders, expose corruption, and give citizens a peaceful way to remove failed governments. Ghana, Benin, Botswana, Mali, Senegal, South Africa, and others have shown that African constitutional politics is not an illusion. But Kenya’s crisis reveals a harder truth: elections in societies with winner-take-all presidencies can become censuses of ethnic fear. When control of the state means access to land, contracts, jobs, police protection, and impunity, losing an election can look existential. The remedy is not less democracy, but deeper democracy: independent electoral commissions, credible courts, devolved authority, professional police, public broadcasting rules, and constitutional limits on presidential power.

Miguel’s proposal for rapid conflict-prevention support is imaginative and deserves attention. If droughts and commodity collapses predict violence, then aid should arrive before militias form and refugees flee. Botswana’s drought relief program is a useful example of social insurance reinforcing citizenship. Still, outside donors should be humble about their ability to identify and stabilize fragile states. Money sent quickly to governments under stress can be captured quickly as well. Conflict-prevention aid should therefore be transparent, rule-bound, and as automatic as possible: triggered by rainfall, food prices, or export shocks, and delivered through public works, school feeding, local infrastructure maintenance, or cash support where administrative capacity exists. Insurance should strengthen the social contract, not become another discretionary fund for incumbents.

The discussion of China also needs a sharper distinction. China is not simply a new imperialist or a benevolent partner. It is a commercial and strategic actor pursuing oil, minerals, markets, and diplomatic support. African governments can benefit enormously from Chinese roads, telecoms, and construction capacity, especially after decades in which Western investors saw only risk. But “no strings attached” finance is not neutral when the missing strings concern labor rights, environmental damage, procurement transparency, and repression. The answer is not to demand that Africa choose between Beijing and Washington. It is for African parliaments, unions, courts, journalists, and regional bodies to raise the price of bad deals.

Finally, rich countries have obligations that go beyond aid. Miguel is especially persuasive on cotton subsidies and agricultural protection. It is absurd to preach markets to Mali or Burkina Faso while protecting American and European farmers from competition. Trade reform, peacekeeping support, climate adaptation, and serious action on greenhouse-gas emissions may matter more than another round of grand aid pledges.

So is it Africa’s turn? Perhaps—but not in the sense of an inevitable continental takeoff. There is no single African trajectory. Some countries are consolidating peace and growth; others remain trapped by predation and war. The hopeful fact is that the distance between success and failure is increasingly political, not civilizational or geographical. The danger is that recent growth will be mistaken for transformation before institutions, jobs, and resilience have caught up. Africa’s turn will come not when the world discovers its promise, but when African citizens can reliably hold power to account and turn booms into broad-based opportunity.

Miguel's essay presents a carefully balanced assessment of Africa's prospects, acknowledging both the genuine progress of recent years and the profound vulnerabilities that remain. His optimism about democratization and economic growth is warranted, but I would push back on several of his conclusions and emphasize factors he underweights.

First, while the 3 percent annual per capita growth since 2000 is encouraging, we should be cautious about attributing it primarily to internal African reforms. Miguel himself notes that commodity prices and Chinese demand are driving much of this growth. This creates a troubling dependency: Africa's current recovery is partly exogenous, dependent on factors beyond African control. If Chinese growth slows or commodity prices fall, as they did repeatedly during Africa's lost decades, the structural weaknesses Miguel identifies could resurface quickly.

Second, on democratization, the Kenyan crisis deserves more weight than Miguel accords it. While he rightly notes that opposition media and civil society have strengthened, the December 2007 election revealed something fundamental: African elections remain deeply susceptible to ethnic manipulation and institutional capture. The ability of Kibaki's government to steal an election and survive, even with power-sharing, suggests that democratic norms are still thin. Other "success" countries—Côte d'Ivoire, Zimbabwe—have shown similar fragilities. Democratization without strong independent institutions is election cycles without substance.

Third, I am more skeptical than Miguel about Chinese investment's long-term benefits. He acknowledges the Chambishi labor abuses and Sudan's Darfur complicity, but frames these as unfortunate exceptions. I would argue they represent the logical endpoint of Chinese engagement: Beijing explicitly prioritizes resource access over governance and human rights. African governments receiving Chinese money face less pressure to reform, and citizens have fewer levers to demand accountability. The short-term infrastructure gains may come at the cost of longer-term institutional development.

Fourth, Miguel's argument about foreign aid's limited role is compelling, but it understates aid's potential when well-targeted. Botswana's drought insurance worked because it addressed a specific, predictable risk through capable institutions. The key insight is not that aid fails but that aid fails when it ignores institutional context. Simply withdrawing aid because it hasn't worked in corrupt environments concedes the problem rather than addressing it.

Finally, climate change deserves greater urgency. Miguel treats it as a distant threat, but for Sahelian farmers, changing rainfall patterns may be affecting livelihoods right now. The eighty-year horizon in climate models masks immediate adaptations already underway—migration, changed planting patterns, conflict over shrinking arable land.

Africa's progress is real, but sustainability requires addressing the structural weaknesses that caused the long decline: weak institutions, over-reliance on commodities, and poor governance. Growth without these reforms is a foundation built on sand.

**The Commodity Mirage**

Edward Miguel's essay paints an inviting picture of African renewal, but I worry it mistakes a commodity windfall for structural transformation. The data he himself cites should give us pause: oil prices tripled since 2000, copper soared five-fold, coffee nearly tripled. Of course African growth rates picked up. The harder question is whether anything beneath the price boom has actually changed—and on the evidence Miguel marshals, the answer is mostly no.

Consider what Miguel concedes almost in passing near the end of his essay: "in most African countries, the manufacturing sector remains as small today as it was in 2000." This is an extraordinary admission buried in an otherwise hopeful narrative. Every previous development success story—Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, now China and Vietnam—has been driven by industrial transformation: moving labor out of low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity manufacturing, and then climbing the technological ladder. Africa has done none of this. It has simply ridden a wave of Chinese demand for the raw materials it happened to be sitting on.

This matters enormously because commodity booms end. The history of resource-dependent economies is a history of boom-and-bust cycles that leave nothing durable behind. Nigeria has been pumping oil since the 1960s, and what does it have to show? Decaying infrastructure, mass poverty, and a kleptocratic political class. Zambia has been mining copper for nearly as long, with similar results. The "resource curse" literature pioneered by Sachs, Warner, Collier, and others suggests that natural-resource booms can actually undermine long-term development by encouraging rent-seeking, distorting exchange rates (Dutch disease), and weakening incentives for governments to build broad-based tax systems accountable to citizens.

Miguel acknowledges the Sudan and Angola problems, but he treats them as exceptions rather than as warnings about the underlying logic. When Chinese state-owned enterprises pay African governments directly for resource extraction rights, they are essentially funding regimes without requiring them to develop productive economies or accountable institutions. The fiscal independence this provides authoritarian elites is precisely the opposite of what produced democratic accountability in Western history, where rulers needed to bargain with productive citizens for tax revenue.

Compare this with the East Asian model Miguel implicitly invokes when he talks about China and India "transforming." Those countries did not grow rich because the world wanted their commodities. They grew rich by making things—first cheap things, then better things—and selling them on global markets. They invested obsessively in education, infrastructure, and technology absorption. South Korea's per capita income was lower than Ghana's in 1960. Today it is around twenty times higher, not because the world started wanting more Korean iron ore, but because Korea built shipyards, steel mills, semiconductor fabs, and Hyundai.

What would it take for Africa to follow that path? At minimum, it would require the kind of industrial transformation that the textile data Miguel cites shows isn't happening. The expiration of the Multi-Fiber Agreement gutted nascent African textile production almost as soon as it began. Chinese competition is now ferocious in exactly the labor-intensive manufacturing sectors that historically have served as the entry point for poor countries. Africa missed the window.

This is not Africa's fault—it is partly an accident of timing, partly a consequence of geography (landlocked countries face brutal transport costs), and partly a result of weak institutions that make manufacturing investment risky. But it means the optimistic story Miguel tells is built on sand. If Chinese growth slows—and no economy grows at 10 percent forever—commodity prices will tumble, African budgets will collapse, and the construction cranes in Nairobi will go silent.

I also think Miguel is too sanguine about the democratization story. Yes, Freedom House scores improved. But the African democratization wave has produced a long list of disappointments: Kenya's stolen 2007 election, Nigeria's fraud-riddled 2007 polls, Ethiopia's crackdown after 2005, Zimbabwe's catastrophe, and the routine extension of presidential term limits across the continent. The institutional substance of democracy—an independent judiciary, professional civil service, neutral security forces—has not kept pace with the procedural form of elections. When elections become merely the periodic means by which one ethnic coalition captures the state's rents, they can deepen conflict rather than channel it.

What I would emphasize, against Miguel's narrative, is that we have been here before. In the 1960s and early 1970s, African growth rates were also impressive. African democratization had its first wave. Optimistic essays were written. Then commodity prices crashed in the late 1970s and 1980s, debt mounted, structural adjustment slashed public services, and a quarter-century of decline ensued. The structural features that made Africa vulnerable then—commodity dependence, weak institutions, ethnic fragmentation, limited human capital—have not fundamentally changed.

I don't want to be entirely dismissive. Cell phone penetration is genuinely transformative, and Miguel is right that it has improved market functioning, especially for smallholders. The end of several civil wars is real progress. Anti-retroviral rollout has saved lives. And the post-Cold War political opening, however fragile, gave space to a vibrant civil society in many countries that did not exist before.

But "is it Africa's turn?" implies a structural break, and I see only a cyclical upturn. The real test will come when commodity prices fall—as they must eventually—and we discover whether anything has been built that can survive the downturn. My fear is that on present evidence, not enough has. Africa's leaders, with help from international partners, should be using the current windfall to invest in education, infrastructure, and institutional reform that could enable genuine industrial transformation. That is happening too rarely. The boom is being consumed, not invested. When it ends, we will likely be writing the same essays we wrote in the 1980s, wondering what went wrong this time.

**Don't Underestimate the Aid Story**

Edward Miguel's lead essay is admirably balanced and rich with on-the-ground texture, but on one major question—the role of foreign aid—I think he is too quick to dismiss its importance and too credulous of the Easterly position. Let me try to make a fuller case for why aid has been more significant in Africa's recent turnaround than Miguel allows, and why his statistical argument doesn't actually establish what he thinks it does.

Miguel's central empirical move is to compare aid levels across periods and regions and to note that Africa receives far more aid per capita than China or India, yet has historically grown more slowly. He also notes that aid to Africa fell by half between 1990 and 1995 with no apparent effect on the growth trajectory. He concludes that aid has played "a rather small role" in Africa's recent recovery.

But these comparisons prove much less than he suggests. The China-India versus Africa comparison is contaminated by every conceivable confounder: starting institutional quality, geography, market size, ethnic homogeneity, colonial legacies, Cold War history, neighborhood effects. Saying "Africa got more aid and grew less" tells us essentially nothing about the counterfactual—what Africa would have looked like with less aid. The relevant question is not whether aid is sufficient for growth (clearly it isn't), but whether at the margin specific aid interventions improve specific outcomes.

On that more tractable question, the evidence is increasingly positive. The randomized-controlled-trial revolution in development economics—a movement to which Miguel himself has contributed substantially—has identified numerous high-return interventions that have been scaled through aid: deworming, insecticide-treated bed nets, conditional cash transfers, school feeding programs, free textbooks targeted appropriately. These don't always show up in short-run national accounts, as Miguel acknowledges, but they accumulate. A generation of African children with reduced disease burdens, better cognitive development from improved early nutrition, and more years of schooling is precisely the kind of human-capital foundation that future industrial transformation will require.

The HIV/AIDS rollout deserves more emphasis than Miguel gives it. The President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief (PEPFAR), launched in 2003, has put well over a million people on anti-retroviral therapy in Africa, almost entirely funded by foreign aid. In a country like Botswana or Swaziland, where adult HIV prevalence approached or exceeded 25 percent, the aid-funded ART rollout has literally rescued the productive workforce from demographic catastrophe. The Global Fund and PEPFAR have together prevented what would have been one of the great economic and humanitarian disasters of the century. None of this happens without aid.

Second, Miguel's 1990-1995 aid drop-off argument is actually evidence in favor of aid mattering, when you look more carefully. African economic performance was poor in the 1980s and remained poor through the mid-1990s, when aid was falling. Performance picked up after 2000—after the Monterrey Consensus, the launch of the MDGs, the establishment of the Global Fund (2002), PEPFAR (2003), and the doubling of aid to Africa that followed. The timing actually fits an aid-matters story reasonably well. Correlation isn't causation, but Miguel can't have it both ways: if the 1990s aid drop didn't hurt, why should we credit anything else with helping in the 2000s?

Third, Miguel underweights the structural reforms that aid conditionality—however clumsy—has helped to drive. The HIPC and MDRI debt relief programs, conditional on macroeconomic and governance reforms, have given many African states fiscal breathing room they wouldn't otherwise have. The end of hyperinflation in places like Uganda and Mozambique, the macroeconomic stabilization in Tanzania and Ghana—these reforms have foundations in IFI engagement, even when the IFIs got specifics wrong.

I do agree with Miguel that aid is not a silver bullet and that the Sachs view—pour in enough aid and the trap breaks—oversimplifies. Aid that flows through corrupt governments is often wasted or worse. Aid that distorts local labor markets by paying inflated salaries to NGO staff has real costs. Aid that creates dependency among recipient governments and substitutes for domestic accountability is corrosive.

But the binary framing of "aid champions versus aid skeptics" obscures the more useful question: what kinds of aid, delivered through what channels, produce what outcomes? The most rigorous evidence we have—much of it from work Miguel himself helped pioneer—suggests that targeted health and education interventions delivered through credible institutions produce returns far higher than almost any alternative use of those resources.

I also want to push back on Miguel's enthusiasm for his proposed "Rapid Conflict Prevention Support" (RCPS) program. The idea is appealing in theory—use rainfall and commodity-price triggers to deliver aid to vulnerable states. But it suffers from the same problems as conventional aid, magnified. Who decides which country qualifies? How do you prevent the funds from being captured by the regime whose mismanagement may itself be the conflict risk factor? Botswana's Drought Relief Program works partly because Botswana already has good institutions; it's not clear the program would have prevented conflict in Sudan or Côte d'Ivoire even if it had existed. The lesson of Botswana is institutional, not technocratic.

Finally, Miguel's framing largely treats aid and growth as if they were the main outcomes that matter. But aid's most important function in many places is keeping children alive who would otherwise die. If aid prevents a million child deaths a year in Africa—roughly the order of magnitude of plausible effects from current health programs—that is an enormous moral achievement even if GDP per capita is unmoved.

Africa's turnaround is multi-causal. Trade, democratization, the end of several civil wars, commodity prices, and yes, aid, have all contributed. Dismissing aid because the cross-country regressions are muddled gets the answer backwards. We should be asking how to do more of what works, not whether to do less.

**The China Question Deserves More Skepticism**

Edward Miguel rightly identifies China's economic rise as a critical factor in Africa's recent prosperity, but his treatment of China is too genteel by half. The Chinese engagement with Africa is not just another foreign-investment story with a few rough edges; it is a fundamental challenge to the entire post-1989 vision of how poor countries can develop, what governments owe their citizens, and what the international community can demand from sovereign states. Africa's "turn" is happening within a Chinese embrace whose long-term implications we are only beginning to understand.

Consider what Western engagement with Africa looked like by the late 1990s. After the Cold War ended, donors and lenders increasingly conditioned assistance on a package of liberal reforms: multiparty elections, anti-corruption measures, transparency, human rights observance, environmental safeguards, labor protections. This conditionality was sometimes hypocritical (the U.S. coddled friendly dictators when convenient), sometimes counterproductive (structural adjustment imposed too narrowly), and sometimes paternalistic. But it pushed in a recognizable direction: toward accountable, rights-respecting governance, however imperfectly realized.

China has now offered African governments an exit option. No conditions. No press freedom requirements. No environmental impact assessments. No questions about how the president's son acquired his mansion. In exchange for resources, China offers cash, infrastructure built by Chinese workers, and diplomatic cover at the United Nations. From the perspective of an African elite, this is an extraordinarily attractive deal. From the perspective of African citizens hoping for accountable government, it is a disaster.

Miguel cites the Chambishi mine in Zambia as an illustrative case, but the pattern is much broader. In Sudan, Chinese arms have enabled the genocidal Bashir regime to wage war in Darfur while Chinese oil revenues fund the slaughter. In Angola, Chinese loans backed by oil revenues have allowed the dos Santos regime to bypass IMF transparency conditions and continue running one of the world's most corrupt petro-states. In Zimbabwe, when the international community sought to isolate Mugabe, China provided diplomatic and material support. In Equatorial Guinea, Chinese investment has helped sustain the Obiang kleptocracy.

The structural problem here is that Chinese engagement systematically reduces the leverage that ordinary Africans, opposition movements, and reform-minded factions within governments have against entrenched elites. When Western firms or governments insisted on anti-corruption measures, that was an external pressure that domestic reformers could use. When the IMF demanded transparency, that empowered local auditors and journalists. When the World Bank required environmental assessments, that gave civil society a tool. Chinese engagement removes these tools.

Miguel's discussion of this dynamic is too narrowly framed around labor conditions and PR. The deeper issue isn't that Chinese firms have bad press; it's that the Chinese government has demonstrated, with consequences, that the international order's commitment to liberal norms is contestable. African autocrats now know they have an alternative patron, and they are governing accordingly. The reformist energies that Miguel celebrates—the journalists, the civil-society activists, the anti-corruption campaigners—are fighting an uphill battle made steeper by the Chinese alternative.

There is also a longer-term economic concern that Miguel touches on but doesn't fully develop. The pattern of Chinese-African trade is classically neocolonial: raw materials flow out, manufactured goods flow in. Chinese textiles, electronics, and household goods are crushing the small industrial bases that some African countries have managed to build. The AGOA-driven textile sector in Kenya, Lesotho, and Madagascar has been hammered by Chinese competition since the Multi-Fiber Agreement expired. This is not a trading relationship that builds African productive capacity; it is one that locks Africa into commodity dependence while flooding African markets with Chinese consumer goods.

In this sense, the Chinese engagement may actually be worse than the colonial era, because at least colonial powers eventually invested in some infrastructure suited to long-term economic development—rail networks, ports, educational systems—even if for self-serving reasons. Chinese infrastructure investment is sometimes substantial but often disconnected from any coherent national development strategy: a stadium here, a presidential palace there, a road that serves a Chinese-operated mine.

I don't want to romanticize the Western record. European colonialism was brutal, post-independence Western engagement was often cynical, and the structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s caused enormous suffering. The case against Chinese engagement is not that the West has been a saint. It is that the move toward conditional, rights-based, transparency-oriented engagement that emerged in the post-Cold War period represented a genuine if incomplete advance—and that China is rolling it back.

What should be done? I am not optimistic about any direct response. Sanctioning Chinese firms would be politically impossible and probably counterproductive. Lecturing Beijing about norms is unlikely to change much. The most useful response is probably to compete: rich democracies should make their own engagement more attractive by being less hypocritical, less paternalistic, faster-moving, and more willing to fund infrastructure rather than just consultants. The African Development Bank and similar regional institutions should be strengthened. African civil society should be supported in demanding transparency from all foreign investors, Chinese included—and there are growing signs that activists in Zambia, Senegal, and elsewhere are organizing in this direction.

Miguel's essay treats China as a mixed blessing on net positive. I read the evidence as more troubling. Africa's commodity windfall is real, but it is being delivered through a relationship that systematically empowers the worst actors on the continent and weakens the institutional reformers Miguel celebrates. If Africa is going to have its turn, it will be despite, not because of, the terms on which China is engaging it.

**The View from Inside: An African Skeptic's Response**

I read Edward Miguel's essay with mixed feelings. On the one hand, it is a thoughtful and largely well-informed account of recent trends on the continent, and it avoids many of the worst tropes of "Afro-pessimism" that have dominated Western commentary for decades. On the other hand, like so much writing about Africa by outsiders—however sympathetic—it reproduces a certain frame that I find limiting: Africa as object of analysis, its fortunes shaped by external winds (Chinese demand, Western aid, climate change), with African agency present mostly as a kind of resilience or vulnerability rather than as a directive force.

Let me try to articulate what an African view of this material might emphasize differently.

First, the question "Is it Africa's turn?" itself frames the continent as awaiting permission from external circumstances. But the most important changes in Africa over the past two decades have not been external. They have been internal and political. They have to do with the slow, painful construction of democratic legitimacy, the rise of independent African media, the emergence of a generation of educated professionals who refuse to accept the corrupt politics their parents endured, the gradual expansion of women's participation in public life, the maturation of post-liberation states in southern Africa. These are not gifts from China or America. They are achievements of African citizens, often won at significant personal cost.

Miguel touches on this, particularly when he discusses Kenya's "new generation of civil-society leaders, journalists, and anti-corruption campaigners." But the framing of his essay still places these African actors as second-order causes operating within structures determined by Chinese demand and Western aid policy. I think the causation runs more substantially in the other direction than he allows: African political changes are creating conditions in which external economic engagement can produce broader benefits, not vice versa.

Second, Miguel's near-equation of Kenya with the continent is understandable given his deep field experience there, but it distorts. Africa is not a country. The trajectories of Botswana, Mauritius, Senegal, Ghana, and Tanzania are dramatically different from those of Sudan, Zimbabwe, and Equatorial Guinea, and they are different from each other as well. Mauritius has built a diversified middle-income economy with strong democratic institutions and rule of law; it bears almost no resemblance to the Kenya Miguel describes. Botswana has avoided armed conflict and built quite a competent state. Ghana has now experienced two peaceful transfers of power between major parties. The pan-African framing, helpful for some purposes, can obscure the specific institutional and political achievements of particular countries.

Third, I want to push back on the lazy use of "ethnic conflict" as an explanatory category. Miguel is careful in his Kenya discussion, noting that the Kalenjin-Kikuyu clashes were "most likely manufactured by the president himself." This is the right way to understand most African ethnic violence: not as the spontaneous eruption of primordial hatreds, but as the deliberate political construction of ethnic difference by elites pursuing power. When Western readers hear "ethnic clashes in Africa," they should think not of ancient tribal enmity but of cynical politicians manipulating identity for power. The Rwanda genocide was meticulously planned by an extremist faction of the state. The Côte d'Ivoire conflict was deliberately provoked by Henri Konan Bédié's invocation of *ivoirité*. The Kenyan post-election violence followed campaigns of ethnic mobilization by senior politicians. These are crimes of political elites, not collective tribal sins.

Fourth, on aid: I largely agree with Miguel's skepticism, but I would put the case more strongly. The international aid industry has become a parallel state apparatus in many African countries—an apparatus that absorbs talented African professionals into NGO salaries, distorts local labor markets, substitutes external priorities for democratic deliberation, and creates accountability relationships that point toward donor capitals rather than toward African citizens. The Paris Declaration on Aid Effectiveness gestures toward solving these problems, but the underlying structure persists. African countries will not develop sustainably while critical public services are funded by foreign donors who can pull the plug, hire the best people away from local government, and dictate the policy framework. The most damaging aid is not the aid that gets stolen; it is the aid that works on its own terms and thereby suppresses the development of accountable domestic institutions.

Fifth, I think Miguel's hopeful note about post-conflict resilience—citing the Blattman and Bellows-Miguel research—is worth taking seriously, but it should not be over-extended. Yes, communities that have suffered violence sometimes display remarkable civic engagement afterward. But they also display long-term trauma, distrust, and reduced human capital that will weigh on their societies for generations. Resilience is not a substitute for peace.

Finally: the question of climate change. Here Miguel is exactly right that Africa faces potentially catastrophic harm from a problem it did almost nothing to create. This is the great moral question of the international system in our time, and the conversation about Africa's "turn" should be inseparable from the conversation about climate justice. African countries should be aggressive in international negotiations, demanding both adaptation finance and meaningful emissions reductions from rich and rising powers alike. African governments should also be investing now in agricultural research, water infrastructure, and resilient crops, regardless of what the international system delivers.

Is it Africa's turn? I would rephrase the question: Africa's people are taking their turn, against considerable headwinds, and they need the world to get out of the way more than they need its rescue.

**Pay Attention to the Demographic Wave**

Edward Miguel's essay is wide-ranging and judicious, but it underweights what may be the single most important variable shaping Africa's next half-century: demography. Sub-Saharan Africa is in the early stages of a demographic transformation that will either deliver enormous economic gains—an African demographic dividend on the scale of East Asia's—or trap the continent in a catastrophic mismatch between people and opportunities. The optimistic and pessimistic versions of Africa's future hinge on this question more than on commodity prices, aid flows, or Chinese investment.

The numbers are striking. Sub-Saharan Africa's population stands at about 800 million today; UN medium projections put it at roughly 1.8 billion by 2050, and possibly higher. Africa's total fertility rate remains around 5 children per woman—well above India's 2.7 and China's 1.7. Africa will be the demographic story of the twenty-first century. The continent will become more populous, much younger relative to the rest of the world, and increasingly urban. By mid-century, one in four humans will be African.

This demographic momentum is both opportunity and threat. The opportunity comes from what economists call the "demographic dividend": when fertility falls and the working-age share of population rises, an economy enjoys a one-time boost as more workers support fewer dependents. East Asia's miracle is now widely understood to have been roughly one-third demographic dividend, one-third investment, and one-third productivity. China would not be the China we see today without its dramatic fertility decline from 6 children per woman in 1965 to under 2 today.

For Africa to capture a similar dividend, two things have to happen. First, fertility must fall, and substantially. There is good news here: African fertility has begun to decline in many countries, with sharper declines in Kenya, Ghana, Senegal, and South Africa. But progress is uneven. Niger's fertility rate is still over 7. And without sustained fertility decline, the working-age share won't rise enough to deliver the dividend.

Second, the young workers being born now need to enter productive employment. This is where Miguel's observation about the failure of manufacturing transformation becomes truly worrying. East Asia's demographic dividend was captured because its young workers were absorbed into export-oriented manufacturing. They moved from low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity factory work. If Africa's young workers cannot find similar opportunities—because Chinese competition has foreclosed labor-intensive manufacturing, because African infrastructure is too weak, because African institutions are too unreliable for foreign investors—then the demographic transition will produce huge cohorts of educated, urbanized young people with nowhere to go.

This is the recipe for political crisis. Demographers and political scientists have long noted that "youth bulges"—large cohorts of young men with limited economic prospects—are strongly associated with civil conflict, urban unrest, and extremist mobilization. The Middle East and North Africa offer a cautionary tale: years of demographic pressure combined with stagnant economies and authoritarian politics have produced a tinderbox.

Africa's urbanization is closely tied to the demographic question. Sub-Saharan Africa is urbanizing faster than any other region, but unlike East Asia's urbanization—driven by labor demand from coastal manufacturing—African urbanization is largely "push" rather than "pull": rural people are being driven into cities by land pressure, environmental stress, and limited rural opportunities, rather than being drawn by urban jobs. The result is cities like Lagos, Kinshasa, and Nairobi growing at extraordinary rates without the manufacturing employment base to support their populations. The slums of these megacities now house tens of millions of people in conditions of extreme deprivation.

Miguel's framing—Kenya's optimistic urban growth, internet cafés, new ATMs—captures the upper end of this story. The middle class is real, and it is growing. But for every Nairobi office worker buying a Japanese car, there are far more residents of Kibera or Mathare living without sanitation. The political volatility Miguel observed in 2007-2008 Kenya was rooted partly in this divide. The post-election violence drew significant participation from urban youth with no economic stake in stability.

What does this analysis imply for policy? Several things, in my view.

First, family planning deserves vastly more attention and resources than it currently receives. The 1990s saw a global retreat from family planning, partly because of the ICPD Cairo consensus's reframing of population issues, partly because of U.S. conservative opposition. But voluntary family planning is one of the highest-return interventions known. It reduces maternal and infant mortality, expands women's economic opportunities, and—at scale—creates the conditions for demographic dividend.

Second, education quality must be the central focus, not just enrollment. Africa has made enormous gains in primary enrollment over the past two decades, partly thanks to the MDG focus. But the educational quality is often abysmal. Children sit in classrooms with 80 students and one barely-trained teacher; many leave school functionally illiterate. The young cohorts entering the labor market need genuine skills, not just certificates.

Third, the conversation about manufacturing must address what kind of manufacturing is still possible for African countries given Chinese competition. Some economists have argued for "industrialization through services"—skipping manufacturing and moving directly into IT services, business process outsourcing, and similar activities. This is partly happening (Kenya's mobile innovation, Mauritius's financial services), but it cannot absorb the labor force in the way that manufacturing once did.

Miguel asks if it is Africa's turn. The demographic question makes this less a question about commodity prices or aid policy and more a question about whether Africa can build economies capable of absorbing the most rapidly growing labor force in human history. If yes, the continent's future may exceed even the optimists' projections. If not, the next thirty years may make the lost decades of the 1980s and 1990s look mild.

**Botswana Is Not a Template, It's a Mystery**

Edward Miguel ends his essay by holding up Botswana as a model—a country whose Drought Relief Program shows what is possible when governments take care of their citizens, and whose decades of stability and growth other African countries should emulate. I admire Botswana too, but I want to caution against drawing the wrong lessons from this exceptional case. Botswana is not really a template for African development. It is more like a puzzle that economists and political scientists have spent decades trying to solve, and the answers point in directions that should make us more humble about engineering development outcomes elsewhere.

The basic facts of the Botswana case are remarkable. At independence in 1966, Botswana was one of the poorest countries on earth, with about a dozen kilometers of paved road, almost no industry, and an economy dependent on cattle and migrant labor to South African mines. Today it is upper-middle-income, with the highest average growth rate in the world over the past four decades. It has held competitive elections continuously since independence, suffered no military coups, and maintained one of the lowest corruption rankings in Africa.

The puzzle is that Botswana's circumstances at independence looked like a recipe for disaster. It was extremely poor, ethnically diverse (Tswana groups plus several minorities), surrounded by hostile or unstable neighbors (apartheid South Africa, white-minority Rhodesia, Portuguese Angola and Mozambique), and—shortly after independence—the recipient of enormous diamond rents that should, by every theory of the resource curse, have destroyed its institutions. Yet none of this happened.

Why? The work of economists like Daron Acemoglu, Simon Johnson, and James Robinson points to several factors. First, Botswana inherited relatively strong pre-colonial Tswana institutions, particularly the *kgotla*—a traditional consultative assembly that established norms of accountability between rulers and ruled. Colonial rule (under indirect British administration of the Bechuanaland Protectorate) was relatively light-touch and did not disrupt these institutions as severely as colonialism did elsewhere. Second, Botswana's founding leaders—particularly Seretse Khama—were unusually committed to building inclusive political institutions and constraining their own authority. Third, the timing of diamond discovery (after independence) and the negotiating strategy with De Beers preserved national control over the resource revenues. Fourth, ethnic politics, while present, was managed rather than weaponized.

The honest summary is that Botswana's success rests on a combination of pre-colonial institutional inheritance, exceptional founding leadership, lucky timing, and some still-not-fully-understood political culture. These are not factors that can be straightforwardly replicated. You cannot import the *kgotla* tradition. You cannot legislate Seretse Khama into existence. You cannot retroactively give other countries Botswana's particular colonial trajectory.

This is why Miguel's framing of the Drought Relief Program as a transferable policy lesson is, I think, slightly misleading. The DRP works in Botswana because the broader institutional context allows it to work. The program reaches its intended beneficiaries because the civil service is professional and reasonably uncorrupt. It is sustained because the political system rewards governments that deliver services. It is fiscally feasible because diamond revenues, well-managed, fund it. Take the DRP and drop it into Zimbabwe or Sudan, and it would not produce the same outcomes—not because the program design is wrong, but because the institutional environment is different.

This is the deeper lesson I take from comparative African experience: institutions matter, and institutions are largely endogenous to long histories and political bargains that cannot be designed from outside. The development economics literature has been converging on this view for some time—the institutional turn associated with Douglass North, Acemoglu, Robinson, and many others has emphasized that the rules of the game matter far more than the specific policies played within them. But this insight cuts against the natural impulse of policy-oriented essays to recommend specific programs that other countries should adopt.

What does this mean for Miguel's broader argument? Several things, I think.

First, we should be more skeptical of the idea that good policy can substitute for good institutions. Many of the proposed solutions in Miguel's essay—the Rapid Conflict Prevention Support program, drought insurance, targeted aid—depend on having states capable of administering them honestly and effectively. In states without such capacity, the programs will be diverted, captured, or rendered useless.

Second, we should be more attentive to the political conditions that produce reform from within. The most encouraging African stories of recent decades—the democratic transitions in Ghana and Senegal, the institutional rebuilding in Rwanda (despite its authoritarian character), the consolidation of multiparty politics in Tanzania—have been driven primarily by internal political processes, not external interventions. The role of outsiders is best understood as supporting reform constituencies that already exist within these societies, rather than imposing reform from above.

Third, we should be humble about prediction. The Botswana case shows that small countries with good institutional luck can achieve far more than circumstances would predict. The Zimbabwe case (a country that in 1980 was among Africa's most promising) shows that institutional collapse can occur even from a favorable starting point. The variance in African outcomes is large, and our ability to predict which countries will do well over a 20-year horizon is limited. Miguel's "Africa's turn" framing perhaps oversells our ability to identify the continent-wide trajectory.

I want to be clear: I am not making a counsel of despair. The institutional perspective actually offers grounds for cautious optimism, because it suggests that the slow accumulation of institutional capacity in many African countries—better courts, more professional civil services, more independent media, more competitive elections—is the real foundation for sustained progress. These changes are happening, slowly, in many countries. They cannot be rushed or imposed, but they can be supported. And over the long run, they may matter more than commodity prices, Chinese investment, or anything else Miguel discusses.

**The Climate Threat Should Reorder Everything**

Edward Miguel devotes a section of his essay to climate change, and his treatment is sensible and informed. But I want to argue that climate change is not just one consideration among many in thinking about Africa's future—it is the master variable that should reframe almost every other question Miguel discusses, and the international community's failure to grapple with this is the most consequential injustice in contemporary global affairs.

Start with the basic asymmetry. Africa contributes a trivial share of global greenhouse gas emissions—roughly 4 percent of cumulative historical emissions, compared with North America and Europe at over 50 percent combined. Yet projections from essentially every credible climate model suggest that Africa will suffer disproportionately from climate change. The reasons are physical (much of the continent is already hot and dry, with limited margins for further warming), economic (agriculture is a much larger share of GDP and employment, and agriculture is uniquely vulnerable to temperature and precipitation changes), and institutional (adaptive capacity depends on resources that poor countries lack).

The numbers Miguel cites for the Sahel—potential temperature increases of 6.3°F and rainfall decreases of 24 percent over the century—are catastrophic if they materialize. Sahelian agriculture is already at the edge of viability with current rainfall variability. Reducing average rainfall by a quarter while increasing evapotranspiration through higher temperatures would push much of the region past the threshold of agricultural sustainability. Tens of millions of people, perhaps hundreds of millions, would face displacement.

But the Sahel is only the most extreme case. Across Africa, climate models project significant disruption to agricultural systems that hundreds of millions depend on. Maize yields—a staple across much of southern and eastern Africa—are projected to decline substantially as temperatures exceed optimal growing ranges. Coastal erosion will threaten major cities including Lagos, Dakar, and Maputo. Glacial retreat on Kilimanjaro and the Rwenzoris is reducing the dry-season water supplies that downstream communities depend on. Increased intensity of both droughts and floods is already being observed.

The implication for Miguel's optimistic narrative is sobering. The economic gains he documents—road improvements, cell phone penetration, expanded trade—are real but modest. They can be wiped out, in a single country or across a region, by climate-driven shocks that the affected societies have neither the resources nor the institutional capacity to absorb. A single severe drought can push fragile states like Niger or Chad into humanitarian crisis. A series of bad agricultural years can collapse political settlements that depend on patronage flows from a productive economy. The Sahelian projection, if accurate, doesn't just slow African development; it potentially reverses centuries of human settlement in the affected zones.

This reality should reorder our thinking in several ways.

First, the question of aid effectiveness needs to be rethought. Miguel's skepticism about aid as a tool for stimulating growth is well-founded, but climate adaptation aid is a different category of expenditure. It is not principally about stimulating GDP growth; it is about preventing collapse. Investments in drought-resistant crop varieties, water infrastructure, irrigation systems, early-warning systems, climate-resilient agricultural practices, and migration support are likely to have very high humanitarian returns even if their macroeconomic effects are modest. The international community is currently spending a small fraction of what climate adaptation in vulnerable regions will require.

Second, the framing of climate change as a "future" problem is wrong, particularly for Africa. The Sahelian drying trend has been observed for several decades already. Recent increases in extreme weather events, glacial retreat, and seasonal disruption are happening now. Climate impacts on African food security, water availability, and conflict are not hypothetical future concerns but present-tense realities. Miguel's RCPS proposal—aid triggered by rainfall and commodity-price shocks—is essentially an implicit acknowledgment of this, though he doesn't quite frame it that way.

Third, the moral arithmetic of international climate negotiations needs to be made explicit. The countries that have generated the vast majority of cumulative emissions are wealthy and largely temperate; the countries that will bear the heaviest costs are poor and largely tropical. This is not an unfortunate accident; it is a systematic transfer of harm from rich to poor that operates through the atmospheric commons. The standard frameworks for thinking about international assistance—charity, generosity, enlightened self-interest—do not adequately capture what is going on here. Climate-vulnerable countries have a justice-based claim on the resources of historic emitters, not a charitable claim.

Fourth, China's role becomes particularly fraught in this analysis. Miguel notes that China became the world's biggest greenhouse gas emitter in 2007. China's emissions growth is driving a substantial share of the climate trajectory that will harm Africa. The same Chinese economic boom that is paying for higher commodity prices benefiting African exporters is also generating emissions that will damage African food systems for generations. From an African perspective, China is simultaneously a customer and a polluter, an investor and a harm-imposer. The terms of African engagement with China should include, at minimum, robust African voice in international climate negotiations and Chinese commitments to climate adaptation finance.

Fifth, the international community's failure to act decisively on emissions is a betrayal of Africa specifically. The current trajectory of global climate negotiations—the post-Kyoto frameworks, the limited progress in Bali in 2007—reflects a refusal by major emitters (the United States most prominently) to accept the scale of action required. African countries have done almost nothing to cause this problem. They will pay enormous costs for it. The political economy of international climate policy is, viewed from this angle, deeply unjust.

Miguel asks if it is Africa's turn. I would say: it might be, if the climate doesn't betray the continent first. And whether it does is being decided now, in policy choices made far away from Africa, by people who will not personally pay the costs of getting it wrong.

**The Trouble with Big Narratives**

Edward Miguel's essay is asking a single big question: Is it Africa's turn? I want to suggest that this is the wrong question, framed at the wrong level of abstraction, and that the persistent temptation to ask such continent-wide questions has done genuine intellectual and policy harm over decades of Western engagement with Africa.

Consider what is contained within "Africa." The continent comprises 54 countries (depending on how you count) with a combined population approaching a billion, spanning territory from the Mediterranean to the Cape, encompassing the world's largest desert and its second-largest rainforest, hosting thousands of languages and dozens of major religious traditions, including states as different as Mauritius (per capita income around $10,000, robust democracy) and Somalia (no functioning central government for nearly two decades), Botswana (decades of stability) and Democratic Republic of Congo (the deadliest conflict since World War II), Senegal (peaceful electoral transitions) and Equatorial Guinea (one of the most repressive regimes on earth).

To ask "Is it Africa's turn?" is roughly equivalent to asking, in 1900, "Is it Eurasia's turn?" The question elides differences so vast that it can produce no useful answer. Sweden in 1900 was nothing like Tsarist Russia, which was nothing like Qing China, which was nothing like Meiji Japan. We would not group these countries together for analytical purposes, and we should not group their African counterparts together either.

Miguel knows this, and he periodically gestures toward differentiation. But the gravitational pull of the big-narrative frame keeps pulling him back. He talks about "Africa's recent gains in both political freedom and economic growth," "Africa's recovery," "Africa's first four decades of independence." This habit—not unique to Miguel, indeed pervasive in Western writing about the continent—has real costs.

The first cost is analytical. By averaging across 54 countries, we obscure what we most need to understand: why some countries are doing well and others poorly, what specific factors drive specific outcomes in specific contexts. The relevant comparisons for understanding Ghana's progress are not "Africa-wide trends" but other West African coastal states, or other resource-dependent democratizing economies. Lumping Ghana with Sudan and Somalia in pan-African averages tells us almost nothing about Ghana.

The second cost is policy-relevant. Aid and policy interventions are typically designed for specific countries or regions, and they should be tailored to those contexts. A discussion at the level of "Africa" generates recommendations at that level—the RCPS program, broad textile market access, generic anti-corruption efforts—when what is actually needed is fine-grained, country-specific engagement attentive to local political conditions, institutional capacities, and economic structures.

The third cost is moral and political. The pan-African frame allows Western readers to engage with African issues from a comfortable distance, treating the continent as a single object of pity, hope, or analysis. It discourages the harder work of learning about specific countries, specific histories, specific political dynamics. It reinforces the colonial-era habit of seeing Africa as homogeneous, primitive, awaiting rescue or development. African intellectuals have been pushing back against this framing for at least a century, with limited success.

Let me illustrate with one of Miguel's examples. He uses Kenya extensively, treating it as somewhat representative of broader trends. But Kenya's recent trajectory is shaped by specific factors that don't generalize well: its colonial history of white settlement and the resulting land question in the Rift Valley; its post-independence ethnic settlement under Kenyatta that privileged the Kikuyu; the corrosion of institutions under Moi's long rule; the specific ethnic geometry of the 2007 election; the particular role of the Kalenjin militias in Rift Valley violence. To understand what happened in Kenya in early 2008, you need to know Kenya in detail. The "African" framing actively obstructs this understanding.

What would a better framing look like? I would suggest several alternatives, each more useful than "Is it Africa's turn?"

We might ask sub-regional questions. Is West Africa's coastal belt—Ghana, Senegal, Côte d'Ivoire pre-conflict, Nigeria's south—building durable democratic institutions? What explains the divergence between similar Sahelian countries (Mali pre-coup vs Niger vs Burkina Faso vs Chad)? How is southern Africa's post-liberation political settlement evolving twenty years after the end of apartheid? These questions admit of more tractable answers.

We might ask functional questions. Among resource-rich African states, what explains the divergence between Botswana and Angola? Among landlocked African states, which have managed to integrate productively with neighbors? Among states with severe HIV/AIDS burdens, which have mounted effective responses? Among democratizing states, which have built institutions robust enough to survive contested elections?

We might ask country-specific questions, with the depth those require. What is happening in Ethiopia under Meles Zenawi's developmental authoritarianism? Can Nigeria translate oil rents into broad-based development under Yar'Adua? What does the Mbeki-Zuma transition mean for South Africa? Edward Miguel presents a wealth of evidence suggesting that Africa has turned a corner, that the terrible decades of war, famine, and despair may finally be ending, and that the international community should support Africa's nascent transformation. He also documents fragilities—the Kenya crisis, extractive political economies, climate change—that could derail progress. Both readings are defensible, and I do not propose to adjudicate between them.

Instead, I want to offer a more fundamental challenge to the essay's framing. The question "Is It Africa's Turn?" treats African development as a discrete event that can be answered yes or no. But development is not an event; it is a process. Africa will not wake up one day having "turned" and suddenly become developed. The trajectory will be uneven, marked by advances and reversals, successes and failures, as different countries and regions experience different outcomes. Some African countries will achieve sustained growth and democratization; others will remain trapped in conflict and poverty. The aggregate continental story will be built from these diverse national trajectories, not from a single continental narrative.

This point matters for how we think about policy. If development is a discrete event, then the international community might reasonably concentrate resources during a critical window of opportunity, hoping to tip Africa into sustained growth. But if development is a process, then the appropriate strategy is to support long-term capacity building without expecting immediate transformation. This means sustained engagement over decades rather than bursts of attention during crises or boom periods. It means building African institutional capacity rather than substituting external management. It means accepting that progress will be measured in generations rather than years.

The historical parallels Miguel invokes support this processual understanding. Latin America's nation-building took most of the nineteenth century. East Asia's development required three decades of sustained policy effort. The United States required eighty years and a brutal civil war to become a genuine nation-state capable of projecting economic and military power. These precedents counsel patience, not premature celebration or premature despair.

What does this mean for international engagement with Africa? First, it means sustaining commitment through both good times and bad. The Kenya crisis is discouraging, but it should not cause donors and investors to abandon Africa any more than successive crises in Latin America should have caused abandonment of that region. Setbacks are part of the process; what matters is that engagement continues. Second, it means focusing on building African capacity rather than achieving short-term results. The Botswana drought insurance program works not because it is a clever technical fix but because Botswana has the institutional capacity to implement it effectively. Building that capacity elsewhere requires long-term investment in governance, education, and health. Third, it means respecting African agency and avoiding the temptation to impose external solutions. African countries must ultimately own their development trajectories; external actors can support but not determine those trajectories.

I am cautiously optimistic that Africa is making genuine progress. The evidence Miguel marshals—economic growth, democratic transitions, improved infrastructure, declining conflicts in some regions—is real and should not be dismissed. But I am also realistic about the challenges ahead. Extractive political economies remain entrenched in many countries. Democratic institutions are shallow and fragile. Climate change threatens the most vulnerable regions. Commodity price volatility could reverse recent gains. The process of African development will be long, difficult, and marked by setbacks.

The international community should engage with Africa for the long term, supporting African efforts without expecting immediate transformation. This means maintaining aid commitments through booms and busts, keeping markets open for African exports, addressing climate change that threatens African agriculture, and engaging constructively with both African governments and civil societies. It means recognizing that development is a process, not an event, and that the measure of success is not whether Africa turns this year or next, but whether African societies are building the foundations for sustained progress over the long run. That is the appropriate stance from May 2008, and it remains the appropriate stance today.
