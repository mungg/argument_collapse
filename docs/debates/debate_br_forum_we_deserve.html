Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Evgeny Morozov's essay is the most ambitious attempt I have read to break out of the stale "realist vs. refusenik" debate around AI. His diagnosis—that both camps reify the existing artifact—is correct and welcome. So is his political conclusion: that "democratic AI" requires actual democracy rather than transparency dashboards. But the path he takes to that conclusion relies on a historical counterfactual and a conceptual binary that are both shakier than the prose suggests, and the weakness matters because it determines what kind of work the "Humanity Lobby" is supposed to do.

Start with the counterfactual. Morozov wants us to imagine an AI not "corroded" by the Cold War, one that could have grown out of cybernetics, pragmatism, and "ecological reason" instead of bureaucratic instrumentalism. But cybernetics was not the road not taken; it was the road taken first. Norbert Wiener literally invented modern feedback theory while building antiaircraft predictors for the U.S. military during World War II. The Macy Conferences were saturated with defense money. Warren McCulloch's neural net papers were partly funded by the Rockefeller Foundation's wartime priorities. If symbolic AI in the McCarthy mold was tethered to DARPA, so was its cybernetic cousin. The notion that AI's teleological fixation is an artifact of Dartmouth 1956 rather than of the broader twentieth-century marriage between computation and state power romanticizes a prehistory that was already deeply entangled with the Efficiency Lobby Morozov rightly indicts. The "more democratic" AI he wants did not lose a fair fight in 1956; it was never really on the ballot anywhere.

A similar problem haunts his hero examples. Project Cybersyn is by now nearly canonical in left-tech circles, and its symbolic power is real. But read Stafford Beer's actual Viable System Model and you find something startlingly close to what Morozov calls bureaucratic instrumental reason: nested control loops, variety attenuators, "algedonic" alarms designed to keep an industrial system on a predefined trajectory. Cybersyn's operations room was a managerial cockpit. The fact that the managers were supposed to be workers rather than capitalists is a political achievement worth defending, but it does not change the underlying telos. Likewise, Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation" was meant to let citizens choose among modeled futures—a profoundly important democratic move, but still a method for goal selection, not the open-ended "messing about" Hawkins championed. The Latin American experiments are evidence for Morozov's political claim (alternative institutional homes for computing exist) but they undercut his philosophical one (that ecological reason is opposed to teleology).

The opposition itself wobbles when pressed. Storm's flâneur, picking up the stone, is described as goal-less—and yet in the very next sentence the spear "project" originates. What the parable actually shows is something subtler than non-teleology: it shows that ends emerge from interaction with means rather than preceding them, and that human attention is plastic enough to reclassify an object mid-stride. This is a critique of fixed-goal optimization, not of goal-directed action as such. Dewey, whom Morozov invokes in his closing paragraph, made exactly this point about means-ends continuity, and so did the later pragmatists Donald Schön and Lucy Suchman, whose "situated action" research at Xerox PARC arguably gave us the most influential internal critique HCI ever produced. Morozov's "ecological reason" is not the opposite of instrumental reason; it is instrumental reason rescued from its own caricature. That is a more modest claim than the essay makes, but a more defensible one.

This matters because Morozov's strongest argument is not the philosophical genealogy but the political economy. The reason my interactions with GPT-4 feel like "arbitrage within a predefined, profit-oriented grid" is not, primarily, that AI inherited the wrong metaphysics from Herbert Simon. It is that the compute is owned by five companies, the training data was scraped under enclosure conditions, and the products are tuned by RLHF toward a customer-service register that smooths every interaction into a sale. Generative models, considered as artifacts, are in fact remarkably eolithic—open-ended, repurposable, terrible at fixed goals, prone to suggestive hallucination. Anyone who has watched a teenager use an LLM as a Dungeon Master, or a poet riff against one for hours, knows this. The teleological flattening happens at the product layer (the chat box, the agent framework, the API meter) and at the ownership layer, not at the level of the underlying statistical machinery. Morozov half-concedes this when he notes that LLMs "resemble the flexibility of the market more than the rigidity of bureaucracy," but he then re-attaches the problem to the deep history of AI rather than to its current owners.

If the diagnosis is mostly about ownership, then the remedy converges with what Morozov already gestures at in his last pages: public compute, training corpora treated as commons, model weights released under licenses that forbid closed downstream use, participatory governance over fine-tuning. These are not exotic proposals. EleutherAI, BigScience's BLOOM, Hugging Face's openness norms, the EU's nascent public-interest AI infrastructure—each is a small, contested experiment in the direction he wants. They are not refuseniks and not realists; they are people doing the unglamorous institutional work of building a Humanity Lobby out of compute clusters and licenses. Morozov's essay would have been strengthened by naming them and assessing their failures honestly, rather than reaching for Storm and Veblen.

Still, the larger provocation lands. We have spent two years arguing whether ChatGPT is a stochastic parrot or a proto-AGI when the prior question—whose problems is it allowed to pose, and whose answers count as success—remains untouched. Morozov is right that the answer is political before it is technical. The pragmatists he ends with would have insisted the two cannot be separated. That is the thread worth pulling.

Evgeny Morozov’s sweeping indictment of artificial intelligence as the captive offspring of Cold War teleology and the Efficiency Lobby is bracing, and his call to reclaim a more playful, open-ended “ecological reason” is a welcome provocation. He rightly insists that the history of computing could have been otherwise, and that the reduction of intelligence to problem-solving has cramped our technological imagination. Yet his utopian counterproposal, rooted in Hans Otto Storm’s eolithism and the tragic Latin American experiments of the early 1970s, risks trading one form of romanticism for another. By framing the choice as one between the Efficiency Lobby and a yet-to-be-built Humanity Lobby, Morozov underestimates the messy, contested, and already partly democratic terrain on which AI actually operates today. A more sober assessment suggests that the tools he dismisses as market-driven may, in fact, be capable of nurturing the very ecological reason he prizes—provided we build governance structures that redirect them toward public ends, rather than waiting for a radical rupture.

Morozov’s historical reconstruction is compelling. He shows convincingly that early AI’s fixation on goal-oriented problem-solving was not an inevitable feature of machine intelligence but a contingent product of funding streams aligned with military and bureaucratic imperatives. The Dartmouth Workshop’s circumvention of broader scientific accountability, the sidelining of cybernetics’ biological openness, and Herbert Simon’s seamless transition from public administration to cognitive simulation all lend weight to the charge that AI was built to serve the organization man. The contrast with Storm’s eolithism—the idea that intelligence can arise from idle curiosity, from stumbling upon a stone and letting its possibilities suggest themselves—is a powerful heuristic. It reminds us that the capacity to reframe, to play, to dwell in ambiguity is central to human creativity, and it exposes the poverty of an AI paradigm that treats us as nothing more than fleshy thermostats.

Yet the essay overstates the purity of this alternative and, in doing so, underplays the extent to which contemporary generative AI already operates in an eolithic mode. Morozov acknowledges that his language-learning app was built by acting like Storm’s flâneur, and he grants that large language models, powered by statistical patterns rather than fixed rules, can function as an “experimental workshop” for tinkering. But he then recoils, arguing that the corporate enclosure of these tools and their carbon footprints turn this play into a privatized, individualized affair—a truncated liberation at best. This is where the analysis falters. If the core insight of eolithism is that ends emerge through an interactive, open-ended engagement with materials and contexts, then the fact that millions of people are using ChatGPT, Stable Diffusion, and other generative tools to explore, remix, and repurpose in ways never intended by their makers is precisely a mass expression of ecological reason. The “neoliberal subject” is not a monolithic figure; the teenager in Mumbai prompting an AI to generate a fusion of classical ragas and lo-fi beats, the community organizer in São Paulo using a chatbot to draft grant proposals in multiple dialects, the retired autoworker in Ohio generating images that blend union iconography with surreal landscapes—all are engaged in forms of vernacular experimentation that resist the Efficiency Lobby’s instrumental grid. The fact that these activities currently route through proprietary APIs is a political challenge to be met with regulation, public options, and collective bargaining, not a reason to deem the entire apparatus irredeemable.

Morozov is on firmer ground when he invokes the Latin American experiments as evidence that information technology can be harnessed to pose problems collectively rather than merely solve them. Cybersyn, the Bariloche Foundation, and Oscar Varsavsky’s “numerical experimentation” offer genuine inspiration for how computing might facilitate deliberation about alternative futures. But his account flattens the present by suggesting that only a similarly grand, state-led project can break the grip of the Efficiency Lobby. In reality, the intervening decades have produced a rich ecosystem of civic tech, participatory budgeting platforms, and digital commons that, while often small in scale, operationalize the very “problem posing” he champions. The use of AI to analyze and visualize participatory budgeting proposals in cities from Paris to Seoul, or the deployment of open-source language models to help citizens interrogate legislative drafts, are direct inheritors of Varsavsky’s vision. These initiatives don’t require a revolutionary government; they thrive on the interplay of civil society pressure, municipal innovation, and the growing availability of open-weight models that can be fine-tuned for deliberation rather than consumption. The EU’s AI Act, finalized in 2024, and the Biden administration’s executive order on AI safety, while imperfect, signal that the regulatory toolkit for taming the Efficiency Lobby is expanding. The real “Humanity Lobby” is already taking shape in the form of coalitions like the Mozilla Foundation, the AI Now Institute, and the thousands of developers contributing to public-interest AI projects.

What’s missing in Morozov’s framework is a theory of transition. His essay oscillates between the dystopian present and a utopian future, with the Latin American examples serving as poignant but ultimately failed waystations. He rightly notes that eolithism, stripped of politics, degenerates into consumerist “interactivity” or Nicholas Negroponte’s MIT Media Lab—a space of corporate-funded play. But the lesson of that degeneration is not to demand a total break with existing institutions; it’s to build counter-institutions that can sustain the eolithic impulse. Publicly funded AI research centers, data trusts that give citizens collective bargaining power over their digital traces, and municipal AI utilities are all within the realm of the politically plausible, even within neoliberal states. They represent an incremental but real delinking from the Efficiency Lobby, and they can be scaled.

Ultimately, Morozov’s call to cultivate a Deweyan “Weltanschauung” grounded in ecological reason is valuable, but it must not become an alibi for rejecting the imperfect tools at hand. The digital sphere is already a contested stonefield, and the flâneurs are at work. The task is not to dream of a pristine workshop but to fight for the terms under which this collective tinkering can flourish—ensuring that the stonefield is not fully enclosed, that the tools are not only for those who can pay, and that the playful artifacts we create can be shared, repurposed, and built into new forms of common life. That fight will be waged in regulatory proceedings, in open-source communities, and in the streets, not only in the retrospective imagination of a road not taken.

Evgeny Morozov’s diagnosis of the current artificial intelligence landscape captures a vital and often overlooked truth: the trajectory of AI has never been historically inevitable. It is, instead, the product of specific institutional pressures, largely shaped by what he aptly terms the “Efficiency Lobby” of Cold War military, corporate, and bureaucratic interests. By framing the debate as a deadlock between pragmatic “realists” who merely wish to tweak the current market-driven models and radical “refuseniks” who reject the technology as poisoned at the root, Morozov clears the ground for a necessary alternative. His proposal to shift our focus from goal-oriented “instrumental reason” to open-ended, exploratory “ecological reason” is an alluring philosophical pivot. Yet, in attempting to rescue AI from the strictures of neoliberalism, Morozov establishes a false dichotomy. Instrumental reason is not the enemy of ecological reason; it is its necessary infrastructure. The problem with modern AI is not that it is goal-oriented, but rather whose goals it has been engineered to serve.

To illustrate his vision of ecological reason, Morozov invokes Hans Otto Storm’s concept of “eolithism”—the prehistoric wanderer who stumbles upon a stone and playfully imagines it as a spearhead. This mode of craftsmanship is characterized by idle curiosity and a lack of predetermined goals. It is a beautiful metaphor for human creativity, and Morozov is right to see traces of this “messing about” in the way users currently interact with generative AI. When we prompt a large language model, we often do not know exactly what we are looking for; we explore, we iterate, we play.

But a stone is a naturally occurring object; a multi-billion-parameter neural network is not. We must not confuse the user’s experience of a technology with the conditions of its production. The sprawling, open-ended sandbox of ChatGPT feels like an eolithic playground precisely because an astronomical amount of hyper-targeted, instrumental reason was marshaled to build it. The neural architectures, the massive datasets, the complex reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), and the vast data centers required to run them were all ruthlessly optimized toward a highly specific, teleological metric: minimizing loss in predicting the next token. You cannot have the eolithic wanderer in a digital environment without the Veblenian engineer first constructing the landscape.

This tension is visible in Morozov’s own historical examples. He points to Salvador Allende’s Chile and Project Cybersyn as a beacon of how technology might be used for alternative, democratic ends. But Project Cybersyn was not a triumph of aimless, ecological meandering. It was the ultimate expression of instrumental reason applied to socialist, rather than capitalist, ends. Stafford Beer, the British cybernetician who designed the system, was deeply concerned with teleology, control, and efficiency. The goal was to optimize national production, allocate resources effectively, and manage supply chains in real time to survive economic strangulation by hostile powers. The workers in Cybersyn’s Operations Room were not engaged in idle curiosity; they were solving urgent, concrete problems of national survival. The lesson of Cybersyn is not that we must abandon efficiency; it is that efficiency can be democratized and harnessed by the public rather than captured by private capital.

By romanticizing “play” and positioning “problem posing” as superior to “problem solving,” we risk disempowering the very democratic movements Morozov wishes to champion. If the “Humanity Lobby” rejects instrumental reason in favor of treating AI like a societal Rorschach test, it will lose to the Efficiency Lobby every time. The most pressing challenges facing democratic societies today—coordinating the transition away from fossil fuels, managing complex public health logistics, and redistributing wealth in an age of staggering inequality—are not abstract exercises in ecological reason. They are concrete crises that require massive, targeted, and highly efficient problem-solving capabilities. If AI can help us model climate outcomes or optimize public transit grids, we should not shy away from using it as an instrument just because the concept of instrumentation was forged in the Cold War.

Morozov is absolutely correct that the current ecosystem of generative AI operates as an enclosure of the digital commons. When OpenAI charges rent—and burns immense amounts of carbon—to generate a simple string of text, it imposes an artificial scarcity driven by the imperatives of venture capital. We are indeed being treated as isolated, entrepreneurial consumers rather than citizens. But the solution to this privatization is not to rethink the fundamental nature of intelligence. The solution is to democratize the ownership and the objectives of the models themselves.

We do not need to abandon the telos; we need to democratize it. Achieving this requires a robust political project aimed at building public-sector AI infrastructure. Imagine a digital equivalent to the Tennessee Valley Authority or a modernized Federal Art Project: open-source, energy-efficient models trained on public data, governed by public institutions, and deployed to solve public problems. Building such an infrastructure will require immense engineering discipline, clear goals, and, yes, instrumental reason. It will require us to decide what we want to build and then optimize our tools to build it.

The AI we deserve will not be stumbled upon by a carefree flâneur in a digital stonefield. It must be deliberately, meticulously constructed. We can absolutely decouple artificial intelligence from the profit-maximizing dictates of the neoliberal market and the militaristic origins of the Cold War. But doing so means reclaiming the power of problem-solving for the public good, recognizing that true ecological freedom is a luxury that only a masterfully engineered, democratic infrastructure can afford.

Evgeny Morozov is right to insist that “democratic AI” cannot mean merely cheaper access to today’s commercial models, a few more transparency reports, or a nicer interface on the same extractive infrastructure. The useful provocation of his essay is to ask not only who controls AI, but what forms of intelligence our institutions have decided to value. If AI is built to serve employers, militaries, advertisers, and platforms, it will predictably encode their picture of the human being: worker, target, consumer, risk score, prompt generator. A different AI politics has to begin by rejecting that anthropology.

But I worry that Morozov’s contrast between instrumental and ecological reason risks making the enemy too abstract. Goal-directed problem solving is not in itself the problem. The problem is who sets the goals, who bears the costs, and which goals are ruled out before democratic argument begins. A public health department trying to identify lead exposure, a union trying to understand wage theft, a tenant organization mapping evictions, or a climate agency modeling flood risk all need instrumental reason. They need databases, forecasts, classifications, and tools that make some ambiguity manageable. The danger is not that such tools pursue ends. The danger is that the ends are smuggled in by capital, empire, or bureaucracy and then presented as neutral optimization.

That distinction matters because democracy cannot live on open-endedness alone. Democratic publics do need spaces for “messing about,” for discovering problems rather than merely solving those handed down from above. Yet they also need the power to decide, allocate, prohibit, build, and repair. Participatory budgeting, which Morozov rightly invokes, works not because it escapes instrumental reason but because it subordinates instrumental tools to public contestation. Residents imagine possibilities, but then budgets must add up, timelines must be estimated, and tradeoffs must be faced. The emancipatory element lies in the institutional arrangement, not in the absence of goals.

The same is true of generative AI. Its most interesting democratic uses are not simply personal acts of bricolage, though those can be delightful. They are collective uses that change who gets to participate in complex domains. A community group can use language models to summarize zoning documents, draft public comments, translate materials, or compare policy options. A disability rights organization can use speech, text, and image tools to make meetings more accessible. Public defenders can use carefully governed systems to sift discovery. Teachers can use AI to generate multiple explanations of a concept, not to replace pedagogy but to widen the repertoire available to students. These uses are not revolutionary by themselves, and they carry risks of error, surveillance, and dependency. But they show that the relevant question is less whether AI embodies ecological reason than whether democratic institutions can domesticate instrumental systems for ecological ends.

That will not happen through consumer choice. Here Morozov’s analogy between today’s AI and the market is powerful. The major AI systems are trained on vast appropriations of culture, labor, and data; run on expensive chips and energy-intensive data centers; and delivered through platforms whose incentives favor dependency, personalization, and enclosure. Even when the interface feels playful, the business model pushes toward metering every act of curiosity. The user appears sovereign at the prompt box, but the surrounding infrastructure is privately owned, legally opaque, and environmentally consequential.

The policy response therefore has to be infrastructural. Public-interest AI should not mean a government chatbot bolted onto existing agencies. It should mean public compute capacity, public datasets governed by democratic rules, procurement standards that require auditability and contestability, and funding for noncommercial models oriented toward education, science, accessibility, journalism, and local government. Universities, libraries, public broadcasters, labor unions, and municipalities should have a role in shaping these systems. So should the workers whose writing, labeling, moderation, and maintenance make AI possible. If the training data is social, the governance should be social too.

There are partial precedents. The European Union’s AI Act, whatever its weaknesses, at least recognizes that some uses of AI are more dangerous than others and that “innovation” cannot be the only public value. The U.S. executive order on AI and the NIST risk management framework similarly reflect a growing recognition that voluntary corporate ethics is inadequate. But risk regulation is not enough. We also need affirmative capacity: institutions able to build and sustain alternatives. Otherwise democracy is reduced to saying yes or no to systems designed elsewhere.

Open source is part of this picture, but not a magic answer. Open models can reduce dependence on a few firms and enable scrutiny, adaptation, and local control. They can also spread capabilities for fraud, surveillance, or automated propaganda, and they do not solve the problem of compute concentration. A democratic AI agenda should therefore distinguish openness from publicness. Some components should be open; some sensitive systems should be tightly governed; and some uses should be banned outright. The common thread should be accountable power, not a single licensing philosophy.

Morozov’s deeper point is that AI critique must recover imagination. I agree. Yet imagination needs institutions that can hold it. The lesson of Cybersyn and the Latin American planning experiments is not simply that computers can help citizens explore alternative futures. It is that such exploration required a political project willing to challenge ownership, dependency, and the inherited terms of modernization. Today, a comparable project would have to confront platform monopolies, intellectual property regimes, data extraction, semiconductor geopolitics, and the carbon footprint of computation. It would also have to defend mundane democratic capacities—schools, libraries, local journalism, public administration—without which citizens cannot deliberate meaningfully about technical futures at all.

The AI we deserve, then, is not necessarily an AI purged of teleology. It is an AI whose purposes are publicly argued over, revisable, and constrained by rights; whose infrastructures are treated as common capacities rather than speculative assets; and whose design begins from a richer account of persons than productivity metrics can capture. We should preserve the eolithic moment: the surprise of finding a new use, the play of curiosity, the refusal to let efficiency exhaust intelligence. But we should also be willing to build the workshop, write the rules, fund the maintenance, and decide together when a tool should not be built. Democracy is not the opposite of purpose. It is the struggle over purposes made public.

Morozov's essay is intellectually ambitious in ways that are both admirable and frustrating. He is right to insist that debates about AI and democracy have been impoverished by their refusal to consider alternatives to the developmental path AI actually took. The question of what computing might have become without Cold War institutional pressures is genuinely worth asking. But his proposed answer—ecological reason, grounded in eolithism and animated by a Humanities Lobby unconstrained by the Efficiency Lobby—ultimately dissolves the political problem he correctly identifies into a philosophical one, and in doing so, sidesteps the harder questions.

The historical alternatives Morozov invokes are less reassuring than he suggests. Project Cybersyn in Allende's Chile collapsed within two years, undone by a combination of technical limitations, political opposition, and ultimately military coup. The Bariloche Foundation's contest to The Limits to Growth remains an interesting intellectual exercise but produced no durable institutional model. Varsavsky's "normative planning" never scaled beyond a small circle of Latin American scientists. Morozov presents these as evidence that alternatives were possible and promising; they might equally be read as evidence of how quickly such projects are absorbed, delegitimized, or destroyed when they threaten existing power structures. The question is not whether eolithic AI is imaginable but whether it is politically viable—and the historical record he cites does not inspire confidence.

More troubling is his treatment of what "ecological reason" would require. He acknowledges that this form of intelligence cannot be formalized, yet proposes technology as the means to enhance its exercise by humans. This is a significant logical move: if ecological reason resists formalization precisely because it thrives on nuance, context, and the ever-shifting meanings Morozov describes, then what kind of technological intervention could enhance it without inevitably distorting it? His own analysis of the Coordinator suggests he knows the answer: any technological system will embed the assumptions of those who build it. The Coordinator amplified instrumental reason because it was designed by people embedded in organizations optimized for that mode of rationality. An AI designed to amplify ecological reason would face the same problem at a higher level of abstraction.

Morozov is right that the realists and refuseniks both reify AI, but his alternative risks a different reification: treating "ecological reason" as a stable concept that can serve as a horizon for evaluation and a guide for policy. The philosophers he draws on—Heidegger, Storm, Veblen—offer rich descriptions of non-instrumental experience, but they were not in the business of designing institutions or technologies. Turning their insights into a research paradigm risks the same solutionism Morozov rightly critiques in Papert's "one laptop per child" initiative, which began with eolithic aspirations and ended as a technological fix stripped of politics.

What is missing from this account is not another philosophy of intelligence but a more precise diagnosis of why AI developed as it did and what levers might actually move it in different directions. Morozov gestures toward this when he notes that John McCarthy deliberately bypassed broader scientific consultation to secure DARPA funding. The concentration of AI development in a handful of companies today is a continuation of this pattern, not a departure from it. The question for democratic governance is not how to inspire a new research agenda grounded in ecological reason but how to contest the political economy that produces AI in the first place.

This requires engaging with questions Morozov keeps at arm's length: Who benefits from current AI systems? What regulatory and ownership structures might shift power toward workers, communities, and public institutions? What forms of democratic control over AI development are actually achievable given existing political constraints? His invocation of "radical political project" at the essay's end sounds promising but remains unspecified. The Latin American experiments he admires did not fail because they lacked eolithic philosophy; they failed because they lacked the political power to sustain alternatives against entrenched opposition.

Dewey's pragmatism, which Morozov mentions in conclusion, points toward a different orientation: intelligence as a social and practical activity, developed through collective problem-solving rather than individual cognition. But Dewey also understood that such intelligence requires institutional support—schools, communities, and publics capable ofhabitually engaging in the kind of inquiry he championed. The task is not to develop AI systems that approximate ecological reason but to build the social conditions under which human intelligence of all kinds can flourish, including the kind that resists technological capture. Morozov's essay illuminates why this is difficult. It is less clear on why it might be possible.

Morozov's Eolith and the Missing Workers

Evgeny Morozov has written the most intellectually ambitious piece on AI politics I have read this year, and I want to begin by saying what is too rarely said in these debates: he is largely right. The realist/refusenik dichotomy he sketches really does dominate the AI discourse, and both positions really do reify "AI" as a singular thing that one must accept or reject wholesale. His genealogy of teleological reasoning—from the General Problem Solver through the Coordinator to ChatGPT—is illuminating, and his recovery of Hans Otto Storm's eolithism is a genuine contribution. So is the reminder that the dream of computing as a tool for citizen deliberation was not always a fringe fantasy: Cybersyn, Bariloche, Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation" all happened, and they were defeated by tanks and structural adjustment loans, not by any technical impossibility.

And yet. For all his political sharpness, Morozov's essay has a curious lacuna at its center: the people who would actually do the work of constructing an "ecological reason" infrastructure are almost entirely absent. The Stormian flâneur strolls alone through the stonefield. Allende's workers appear briefly as managers in the Operations Room before fading back into the historical record. The "Humanity Lobby" is invoked as something that ought to exist but is never given social flesh. This is not a small omission. It is the omission that has haunted every left technology critique since the New Left, and it explains why Morozov's conclusion—"democratic AI requires actual democracy"—lands as a slogan rather than a strategy.

Consider what is actually happening in 2024 around AI labor. The Writers Guild of America just won, in their 2023 strike, the first major labor contract in any industry that explicitly constrains generative AI use. SAG-AFTRA followed. The Authors Guild is suing OpenAI. Kenyan workers who labeled toxic content for ChatGPT at $2 an hour have organized and gone public. Artists are mounting legal challenges to training-data scraping. None of these struggles fit cleanly into either the realist or refusenik camps Morozov describes, and none of them get a mention in his essay. They are precisely the kind of "problem-posing" politics he says we need—and they are happening, right now, without waiting for a Storm revival.

The point is not that Morozov ignores labor out of malice. The point is that his theoretical frame, focused on the cognitive history of "instrumental reason" and its alternatives, tends to locate politics in the realm of paradigm and Weltanschauung rather than in the realm of organized social conflict. When he writes that "we should turn ecological reason into a full-fledged research paradigm," the verb "we" is doing extraordinary work. Who is the "we" here? Critical theorists? Sympathetic computer scientists? Foundations? The state? Morozov knows perfectly well, having spent two decades arguing this point, that paradigms do not change because intellectuals propose better ones. They change because constituencies with material interests force them to change.

This matters because the eolithic moment Morozov celebrates—the flâneur stumbling on the stone, the language-app builder noticing a feature—is not actually politically neutral. It is, as he half-acknowledges in his self-criticism, a leisure-class activity. Storm's wanderer is, in Storm's own words, "fed, contented, thinking preferably about nothing at all." The capacity for idle curiosity is materially produced. It requires that someone else has done the cooking, the cleaning, the labeling of training data. Veblen knew this; that is what The Theory of the Leisure Class is about. An ecological reason worth having is one that universalizes the material conditions for idle curiosity, not one that merely valorizes the cognitive style of those who already possess it.

Joan Tronto, Nancy Fraser, Silvia Federici—none appear in Morozov's pantheon, which leans heavily on male pragmatists and male AI critics. This is not a complaint about representation; it is a complaint about analytic resources. The most sophisticated existing literature on non-instrumental reason is care-ethics literature, and it has the additional virtue of being attached to actually-existing political movements (care workers' unions, domestic workers' organizing, the social-reproduction-strike traditions of Latin American feminism).

Wonderful. But the energy and compute costs of state-of-the-art models are enormous—a single training run for a frontier model now exceeds $100 million, and inference costs are non-trivial. Either the public alternative subsidizes this at vast taxpayer expense, or it accepts smaller, less capable models, or it embraces a fundamentally different research program. This is the question that any serious "ecological reason" research program would have to answer first: what are the technical artifacts that embody this paradigm? Cybersyn had its Cyberstride and CHECO; Varsavsky had his simulation models. What does the 2024 equivalent look like? Without a concrete technical proposal, the essay risks being what Morozov himself has elsewhere criticized: a humanistic gesture toward "alternatives" that exists primarily in the conditional mood.

Morozov's essay is valuable precisely because it refuses the false choice between accommodation and refusal that has stifled AI discourse since ChatGPT's release. The eolith is a useful figure; the genealogy is illuminating; the Latin American examples are essential. But to convert this excellent essay into a political project, we need to ask what Morozov does not quite ask: which constituencies have an interest in ecological reason, what organizational forms could carry that interest into struggle, and which existing fights—over training data, over labor displacement, over public-sector procurement, over compute infrastructure—offer footholds for the larger transformation he envisions. The answers will not come from philosophy. They will come from organizers who treat Morozov's essay as a useful provocation rather than a finished program. I count myself among them.

In Defense of the Realists

Evgeny Morozov's essay performs a clever rhetorical maneuver: he sets up two camps—realists and refuseniks—pronounces them both deficient, and then offers his own utopian third way. The problem is that the "realist" position he describes barely resembles the work of the people actually doing realist AI policy. By treating realism as a position of resigned accommodation, Morozov is able to position himself as the only one thinking seriously about democracy. This is unfair to a serious tradition, and I want to defend it.

When Morozov characterizes realists as people who think "AI is here to stay, that its value depends on how we use it, and that it is, at minimum, worthy of serious political oversight," he is describing a stance so minimal that it amounts to a tautology. But the actual realist project—as practiced by researchers at AI Now, Data & Society, the Distributed AI Research Institute, the Ada Lovelace Institute, in policy work around the EU AI Act, in NIST's risk management framework, in the work of FTC chair Lina Khan on algorithmic harms—is far more substantive than this. It involves detailed empirical study of how specific AI systems harm specific people, the painstaking construction of audit methodologies, careful legal craftsmanship around antitrust and consumer protection, and slow institutional bargaining over standards and procurement. None of this is captured by Morozov's caricature, and the work matters.

Consider the EU AI Act, finalized this year. It is imperfect, like any regulatory document, but it represents the most serious democratic intervention into AI governance in any major jurisdiction. It bans certain practices outright (social scoring, real-time biometric identification in public spaces with limited exceptions), imposes risk-tiered obligations, requires transparency for general-purpose models above certain compute thresholds, and creates enforcement mechanisms. It is the product of three years of contested political negotiation, civil-society advocacy, technical input, and democratic deliberation across 27 member states. This is exactly the "actual democracy" Morozov says is required, and it produced concrete constraints on the technology. Yet because the EU AI Act presupposes that AI exists and tries to govern it, rather than asking what alternative AI might have looked like in a counterfactual world, Morozov's framework can register it only as a deficient compromise.

The deeper problem with Morozov's utopian counterfactual is that it provides no traction on the present. Yes, it would be lovely if we could "turn back the clock and shield computer scientists from the corrosive influence of the Cold War." We cannot. The Cold War happened. Symbolic AI failed, deep learning succeeded, the transformer architecture works, scaling laws are real, and seven companies have spent hundreds of billions of dollars building infrastructure that exists. The relevant political question is not "what AI would we have if history had been different" but "what do we do with the AI we have." This is the question realists are trying to answer, and Morozov's dismissal of their efforts as "narrow" misses how much hard work is actually involved in narrow questions.

Morozov's Latin American examples make this clearer, not less clear. Cybersyn is genuinely fascinating—I would urge anyone interested in alternative computing histories to read Eden Medina's wonderful book on it—but we must be honest about what it was. It was a partially-built system, designed for a small economy, that never reached full operation before being destroyed by a coup. We have no evidence that it would have scaled, no evidence that workers would in fact have used the Operations Room productively, and no evidence that the underlying cybernetic models (heavily indebted to Stafford Beer's idiosyncratic theories) would have functioned as advertised. To treat Cybersyn as a model for contemporary AI policy is to engage in a kind of historical wish-fulfillment that confuses suggestive ruins with proven blueprints. Similarly, the Bariloche Foundation's world model was an intellectually serious response to Limits to Growth, but it was a counter-model in a methodological argument, not the foundation of an alternative society.

The realist position takes seriously that we have to start from where we are. Where we are includes: highly capable foundation models in a small number of corporate hands; concentrated compute infrastructure with significant geopolitical implications; emergent labor displacement in some sectors; documented harms in others (recidivism prediction, hiring algorithms, content moderation); and a public that is broadly favorable toward AI applications while harboring specific anxieties. Effective democratic intervention in this terrain requires the unsexy work of standards, audits, liability regimes, antitrust enforcement, sector-specific rules, procurement requirements, and so on. Morozov gestures at this work only to dismiss it as insufficient.

He is, of course, right that it is insufficient by itself. No serious realist thinks otherwise. The hope that emerges from realist work is that incremental constraints accumulate into structural change—that bans on certain uses, transparency requirements, antitrust action, public-option proposals, and labor protections together shift the terrain. This is how every successful regulatory project in modern history has worked, from food safety to financial regulation to environmental protection. None of them began with a clean break from "instrumental reason"; all of them involved contested bargaining over actual industries with actual products.

There is also something philosophically off about Morozov's eolithic alternative. He says ecological reason "resists automation" because it thrives on "nuance and difference." Fine. But then what exactly are we asking AI to do? If ecological reason is by definition non-formalizable, then the question of whether AI embodies or undermines it is somewhat beside the point. The most we can ask is that AI systems be one tool among many in environments that also cultivate non-instrumental cognition. But that is exactly what realists already advocate when they push for AI to augment rather than replace human judgment in high-stakes domains. Morozov's framing is rhetorically grander than the realist framing without being practically different.

Finally, the realist position has the virtue of being honest about constraints. Building a "public, solidarity-based, socialized infrastructural alternative" to OpenAI would require, at minimum, tens of billions of dollars in compute, a research workforce currently competing with $500K Silicon Valley salaries, and political support that does not exist in the United States and barely exists in Europe. The realist asks: what can we actually achieve with the political coalitions and budgets we have? The utopian gestures at what we might have if everything were different. Both modes have their place, but in a moment when AI is being deployed in healthcare, education, and policing right now, the realist mode is doing the work that matters.

I share Morozov's worry about corporate capture. I share his admiration for the Latin American computing tradition. I want a more democratic AI politics too. But the path runs through the boring institutional work he disdains, not around it.

The Teleology Wasn't the Problem

Evgeny Morozov's essay diagnoses the original sin of AI as its "teleological" orientation—its commitment to goal-pursuit, problem-solving, and instrumental reason—and locates the corrective in Hans Otto Storm's "eolithism" and a recovered pragmatist tradition of "ecological reason." It is an elegant intellectual move, but as someone who has spent two decades working on machine learning, I think it gets the technical history almost exactly backwards. Teleology is not what went wrong with AI. The contemporary problem is precisely that frontier AI systems have become alarmingly non-teleological, generative rather than goal-directed, and that the social institutions surrounding them have not adapted to this fact.

Consider Morozov's own example: the General Problem Solver. Yes, Newell, Simon, and Shaw built a system that did means-ends analysis on formal problems. But GPS was a failure as a general technology. It did not survive contact with real domains. The line of work Morozov treats as foundational—rules-based, expert-system, symbolic AI—collapsed in the late 1980s precisely because the world refused to behave teleologically. The neural network paradigm that replaced it is not, contra Morozov, just "teleology continued by other means." It is structurally different. A large language model is not trying to solve a problem in any meaningful sense. It is performing next-token prediction over a distribution of human-generated text. This is a profoundly different operation from means-ends analysis, and the differences matter.

Morozov does seem to half-recognize this when he writes that today's AI "hallucinates its way to an answer" and that LLMs "resemble the flexibility of the market more than the rigidity of bureaucracy." But he immediately recodes this as a continuation of the same instrumental-reason project, just dressed in postmodern clothes. This is an analytical mistake. Hallucination is a symptom of non-teleological operation. The model has no telos; it has only a learned distribution. When we then bolt teleological scaffolding on top—through reinforcement learning from human feedback, through prompting, through agent frameworks—we are explicitly trying to make these systems more goal-directed, because their native operation is too unmoored from goals to be useful. This is the opposite of the picture Morozov paints.

Why does this matter? Because Morozov's prescription—that AI should "amplify ecological reason" rather than "embody instrumental reason"—turns out to be an apt description of what frontier generative AI already does, and the result is not the democratic flowering he hopes for. His own example of his language-learning app makes the point against him. He stumbled, flâneur-like, on a feature; he linked services together in unexpected ways; he treated ChatGPT as an "experimental workshop"; he produced something idiosyncratic and unplanned. By his own account, this is eolithic bricolage. The fact that he is dissatisfied with this experience—that he finds it "individualistic," "alienated," "ecologically costly"—suggests that the problem is not insufficient eolithism but something else entirely: the political economy of the platforms, the energy footprint of the compute, the inability to share or pool the labor, the proprietary control over the weights.

These are real problems. But notice that they are not problems about teleology versus eolithism. They are problems about ownership, infrastructure, externalities, and labor. Morozov's framework conflates a political-economic critique (which I largely share) with a cognitive-historical critique (which I think is mistaken). The former does not depend on the latter.

There is also a technical-historical question Morozov elides. The most important precursor to deep learning is not the Cold War operations researcher's means-ends analysis but the connectionist tradition that AI's symbolic establishment marginalized for decades. Rosenblatt's Perceptron was attacked in Minsky and Papert's 1969 book on grounds that, in retrospect, were over-stated; backpropagation languished; LeCun, Hinton, and Bengio worked in relative obscurity for years before the 2012 ImageNet result. The line of research that produced ChatGPT was emphatically not the favored child of the Efficiency Lobby; it was a heterodox bet by researchers who were often dismissed by the symbolic AI mainstream and who attracted serious industrial investment only after empirical results forced the issue. To paint contemporary deep learning as the smooth continuation of a 1950s teleological project is to flatten a much more interesting and contested history.

What about Morozov's pragmatist alternative? I am sympathetic to the move toward Dewey—his theory of inquiry, his rejection of means-end dualism, his account of habit and experience are all rich resources. But Dewey is not a deus ex machina. Pragmatist ideas have in fact circulated within AI research for decades. The embodied cognition movement, the situated robotics tradition associated with Rodney Brooks (whose famous 1991 paper "Intelligence Without Representation" is practically a Deweyan manifesto), reinforcement learning's emphasis on agent-environment coupling, even the recent enthusiasm for "world models" in robotics—all these draw, often explicitly, on pragmatist and ecological-psychological sources. James Gibson's notion of "affordances" (itself a sibling of Storm's eolithism) is foundational to modern HCI. Morozov writes as though the AI community has been deaf to non-Cartesian alternatives; in fact, it has been engaging them, sometimes well and sometimes badly, for thirty years.

The genuine puzzle is why none of this has produced the democratic technology Morozov wants. The answer, I would submit, has very little to do with the philosophy of cognition and very much to do with the political economy of compute. Modern frontier AI requires vast capital expenditure on data centers, energy, and chips. The companies that can afford this are few. Government investment in alternatives has been chronically underfunded. There is no public-option foundation model because no public has been organized to demand one. None of this would change if every AI researcher tomorrow read Dewey instead of Heidegger. It would change if the political coalition for public technology infrastructure were strengthened—which is, ultimately, the same point I take Morozov to want to land on, but which his cognitive-historical framing obscures.

So I want to thank Morozov for an unusually rich essay while resisting his diagnosis. The eolithic flâneur and the goal-directed problem-solver are not the deep stakes of contemporary AI politics. The deep stakes are who owns the compute, who labels the data, who pays the externalities, and who decides what gets built. These are political questions, not cognitive ones. We do not need a new philosophy of intelligence. We need a new political coalition for technology. The Cold War metaphors are pretty, but they may be leading us away from the fight that is actually in front of us.

Heidegger Was the Wrong Target

I want to register a philosophical disagreement with Morozov that is more than terminological. He proposes that the AI tradition would have benefited from John Dewey instead of Martin Heidegger as its critic, and he reads Heidegger's hammer analysis, via Dreyfus, as failing to break with the "teleological ethos" of goal-directed reason. This is, I think, a serious misreading of Heidegger and, by extension, a misreading of what was actually at stake in Dreyfus's critique of AI.

Heidegger's analysis of equipment (Zeug) in Being and Time is not, as Morozov suggests, fundamentally an analysis of how we hammer nails efficiently. The famous passages about the hammer are part of a much larger project to expose the inadequacy of the "present-at-hand" (vorhanden) understanding of being—the understanding that treats entities as discrete objects with determinate properties, available for theoretical contemplation or technical manipulation. Heidegger argues that this picture is derivative; primary engagement with the world is "ready-to-hand" (zuhanden), embedded in webs of significance and reference (Verweisungszusammenhänge) that cannot be reduced to explicit rules. When the hammer breaks, what becomes visible is not just a failed instrumental relation but the entire referential whole that had been silently sustaining the activity.

This is a much more radical critique of instrumental reason than Morozov credits. Heidegger is not saying that we sometimes act for goals and sometimes get bored; he is saying that the very framework of "agent + goal + means" is a derivative philosophical construction that misses what is most fundamental about human existence. The famous boredom analysis in The Fundamental Concepts of Metaphysics goes further still, arguing that profound boredom (tiefe Langeweile) reveals the groundlessness of all our purposive projects. This is precisely a critique of teleology, not (as Morozov implies) a critique that fails to make it.

Why, then, did Dreyfus's reception of Heidegger produce, in Winograd's Coordinator, a tool that remained captured by instrumental reason? Not because Heidegger lacked the resources for a deeper critique, but because Dreyfus selectively translated those resources for an American philosophical audience that wanted concrete arguments against specific AI programs. Dreyfus's job was to explain why expert systems would fail at chess, medical diagnosis, and natural language understanding—and he did that job magnificently, drawing on Heidegger's account of skilled coping. But the larger metaphysical critique of teleology was bracketed for tactical reasons, not because Heidegger had not made it.

Morozov's preferred alternative, John Dewey, is in many ways less hostile to instrumental reason than Heidegger is, not more. Dewey's pragmatism is famously a philosophy of inquiry, problem-solving, and adaptive intelligence. Dewey criticizes the means-end dualism, yes, but his criticism is that means and ends are continuous, not that ends should be displaced from the picture. The Deweyan agent is constantly engaged in problem-solving; what Dewey rejects is the rigid separation of contemplation from action and the false separation of fact from value. This is closer to the spirit of contemporary AI's "agent" frameworks than Morozov seems to realize. Dewey would not have built ChatGPT, but he might well have built Winograd's Coordinator, with some modifications.

Heidegger, by contrast, is genuinely unfriendly to the entire project of computational intelligence. His later writings on technology (Die Frage nach der Technik) argue that the essence of modern technology is "Ge-stell" (enframing), a mode of revealing that discloses everything as standing-reserve (Bestand)—as resource available for manipulation. This is not a complaint about specific technologies; it is a complaint about the metaphysical orientation in which technologies show up as solutions to problems. From this perspective, both ChatGPT and Cybersyn would be expressions of the same enframing, differing only in their ownership structures.

I raise this not to score philosophical points but because it matters for Morozov's political project. He wants to recover an "ecological reason" that resists instrumentalization while still being "technologized"—still operating through computational tools. Heidegger would have been suspicious of this very ambition. He would have asked whether the gesture of building tools to "amplify" non-instrumental intelligence does not itself capture that intelligence in the instrumental frame, treating it as something to be enhanced and optimized. The eolithic flâneur, on Heidegger's account, would lose his eolithic character the moment he started using ChatGPT as an "experimental workshop." Morozov's appropriation of Storm risks doing exactly what he accuses Negroponte's MIT Media Lab of doing: repackaging playfulness as "interactivity" and selling it back to us in a more sophisticated form.

If we want a genuinely non-instrumental engagement with computing, we may need to be more austere than Morozov is willing to be. We might need to ask whether some applications should not be technologized at all, whether some forms of human cognition are degraded rather than enhanced by computational assistance. Sherry Turkle's recent work on conversational AI and the erosion of human empathy points in this direction. So does Carissa Véliz's work on privacy as a precondition for selfhood. So does the long tradition of conservative humanism that Morozov, with his pragmatist commitments, tends to dismiss.

There is also a question about what kind of cognitive style is actually required for democratic citizenship. Morozov's "ecological reason" is appealing in the abstract, but democracy also requires the capacity for sustained argument, evidence-weighing, and collective decision under uncertainty—all of which are highly teleological activities. The boredom-stricken flâneur is a poor citizen; he is in fact a figure of withdrawal from public engagement, closer to Heidegger's Black Forest hut than to the Athenian agora. We should not romanticize this. The Latin American examples Morozov admires—Cybersyn, the Bariloche models—are notable precisely for their commitment to deliberate, structured, goal-oriented planning, not for their eolithic improvisation. Allende's project was about workers managing industries effectively; "messing about" would not have built socialism in Chile, and it will not build democratic AI now.

So I find myself in the curious position of being more sympathetic to the philosophical critique Morozov gestures toward than to the alternative he offers. The deep problem with AI is not that it embodies instrumental reason; it is that it embodies a particular metaphysical orientation in which everything must show up as available, calculable, and optimizable. Resisting this orientation is much harder than reading more Dewey. It may require modes of life that look unproductive, untechnologized, and irrelevant to the questions of the day. It may, in the end, require some refusals that even Morozov is not prepared to make. The "AI we deserve" might, on careful examination, be considerably less AI than we currently have.

The View from the Global South

I want to take seriously Evgeny Morozov's invocation of the Latin American computing tradition—Cybersyn, Bariloche, Varsavsky—but also to complicate his story. As someone who works at the intersection of technology and development policy, and who has spent time in both São Paulo and Buenos Aires reading these archives, I find Morozov's recovery of this tradition welcome but partial. There are things he misses, and there are things in the Global South AI conversation in 2024 that his framework does not yet see.

First, the recovery itself. Morozov is right that the early 1970s saw a remarkable flourishing of heterodox computing in Latin America, and that it has been systematically forgotten in the dominant histories of the field. Eden Medina's work on Cybersyn, Hugo Salinas Price on Bariloche, recent scholarship on Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation" and the Latin American World Model—these have begun to recover what was lost when military coups, structural adjustment, and the rise of Silicon Valley combined to make the U.S. model of computing seem inevitable. Morozov is also right to insist that these traditions were not merely about technology but about a politics: the politics of de-linking from the Global North's developmentalist agenda, which used "modernization" as ideological cover for asymmetric integration into Northern markets.

But there are two important things Morozov does not say, and a third he gets wrong.

What he does not say, first, is that these projects were profoundly statist. Cybersyn was a project of the Allende government, designed to manage nationalized industries; the Bariloche model was the product of a research institute backed by Argentine and international development funding; Varsavsky operated at the intersection of universities and state planning agencies. The "ecological reason" Morozov celebrates was, in these cases, an instrument of national-developmental sovereignty exercised through state planning. It is not at all clear that the same possibilities exist in 2024, when state capacity has been hollowed out across most of the Global South by four decades of neoliberal restructuring, debt service, and capital flight. A serious revival of the Latin American computing tradition would have to begin with a revival of state capacity, which means tax sovereignty, which means confronting the international financial architecture. None of this is impossible, but it is much further upstream than the question of AI.

What Morozov also does not say is that the Global South of 2024 is not the Global South of 1972. China is not the Soviet Union; it is a technological peer of the United States, exporting frontier AI models, surveillance systems, and infrastructural capacity to much of the world. Latin American, African, and Southeast Asian governments today face not one Efficiency Lobby but two, sometimes three, all competing for influence and offering different bundles of computing infrastructure, financing, and political conditions. The Argentina of Milei is buying into U.S. AI offerings; Brazil under Lula is trying to construct an autonomous position; Kenya is hosting both U.S. content-labeling operations and Chinese surveillance infrastructure. The "delinking" Morozov invokes from the Bariloche tradition is much harder to do when there are multiple cores to delink from and when the technologies in question are so capital-intensive that even mid-sized economies cannot independently develop them.

The thing Morozov gets wrong, I think, is the sociology of the eolithic moment. He treats Cybersyn as an embodiment of "ecological reason" in technological form, contrasting it with the bureaucratic instrumentalism of Cold War U.S. AI. But Cybersyn was, in its own way, intensely teleological. Stafford Beer's Viable System Model, which underpinned the project, was a deeply cybernetic framework focused on managerial control, feedback loops, and goal-attainment at multiple scales of an organization. The Operations Room was designed for executive decision-making, not for eolithic wandering. Workers were envisioned as managers—that is, as agents pursuing defined goals with informational support. To recast Cybersyn as a vehicle for Stormian flânerie is to romanticize it. What made Cybersyn valuable was not its escape from instrumental reason but its democratization of instrumental reason: it tried to put the tools of goal-oriented planning in the hands of workers rather than capitalists or technocrats. That is a different and, I would argue, more politically tractable goal than Morozov's ecological-reason program.

This matters because there is, right now in 2024, an active debate in Global South policy circles about "sovereign AI" and "public compute." Brazil has announced a major investment in domestic AI infrastructure. India is developing its IndiaAI mission. The African Union has begun discussing a continental AI strategy. UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI has been adopted by member states. The G77 has begun raising AI in UN forums. These are concrete political projects with real budgets and real institutional carriers, and they are precisely about wresting some degree of control over the technology from the Northern oligopoly. The Care Work That Computing Forgot

Evgeny Morozov's essay is, among other things, an extended argument that AI's foundational mistake was to model intelligence on "the fully committed, extrinsically motivated, emotionally detached office worker—a species of William Whyte's 'organization man.'" Reading this passage, I thought: yes, exactly, and what an extraordinary half-acknowledgment of a critique that has been made, for decades, with more precision and political force, by feminist scholars whom Morozov does not cite.

The phrase "organization man" should already alert us to something. Whyte's organization man is not just any worker; he is specifically the white-collar male whose work happens at the office while his wife performs the unpaid labor of social reproduction at home. The 1950s bureaucratic ideal that gave rise to early AI was made possible by the gendered division of labor that rendered care work invisible. The intelligence that AI tried to formalize was the intelligence of the boss; the intelligence that AI ignored was the intelligence of the secretary, the wife, the mother, the nurse, the teacher. This is not a marginal point. It is the central point about what AI took as its object of imitation.

Feminist philosophers of technology have been making this argument for forty years. Lucy Suchman's Plans and Situated Actions (1987) demonstrated, with detailed empirical study of human-machine interaction, that the planning model underlying classical AI radically misrepresented how human action actually proceeds—and that the misrepresentation tracked a gendered division of cognitive labor. Donna Haraway's "Cyborg Manifesto" (1985) refused both the technophobic and technophilic options and insisted on a feminist engagement with the partial, situated, embodied character of all knowledge production. Joan Tronto's work on care ethics has shown how a politics that takes care seriously requires a different account of attention, responsibility, and competence than the instrumental-reason model permits. Maria Puig de la Bellacasa's Matters of Care extends this to scientific and technological practice. None of these thinkers appears in Morozov's pantheon, which leans heavily on male pragmatists, male AI critics, and male early-twentieth-century engineers.

This is not, again, a representation complaint. It is an analytic complaint, because the feminist literature has resources Morozov's essay needs and lacks.

Consider Morozov's central concept, "ecological reason." He defines it as a "view of intelligence that stresses both indeterminacy and the interactive relationship between ourselves and our environments." This is essentially the definition of "situated knowledge" that has been the lingua franca of feminist science studies since the 1980s. Haraway's situated knowledges, Sandra Harding's standpoint theory, Karen Barad's agential realism, Suchman's situated action—all of these are sophisticated, well-developed accounts of exactly the cognitive style Morozov rebrands as "ecological." That tradition has worked out, in considerable detail, how such cognition relates to embodiment, to power, to scientific practice, and to political accountability. It has also done so in conversation with actually-existing social movements rather than as a thought experiment about counterfactual histories.

Consider also the eolithic flâneur. Morozov's image is appealing but it is also, conspicuously, a male solitary stroller in a stonefield. He is "fed, contented, thinking preferably about nothing at all." The question that any feminist reader is going to ask immediately is: who fed him? Who is at home with the children while he wanders? Whose labor produced the conditions of his idle curiosity? Storm's prehistoric example sidesteps this because it imagines a Stone Age individual outside any social reproduction system, but Morozov's contemporary analogue—him building a language app while "casually browsing the web"—does not have this excuse. The capacity for eolithic exploration is materially produced, and historically it has been disproportionately produced for men by women's invisible labor.

This is not just a complaint about Morozov's privilege. It is a substantive point about what a non-instrumental AI would look like. If we wanted to design computing infrastructure that supported "ecological reason," we would have to begin by asking which forms of cognition we want to support, and the answer cannot simply be the cognition of the leisured wanderer. It would have to include the distributed, multi-tasking, interruption-saturated cognition of the parent caring for small children; the relational cognition of the caregiver attending to a person with dementia; the attentive cognition of the teacher tracking twenty-five children at once; the negotiating cognition of the community health worker. These are forms of intelligence that contemporary AI is spectacularly bad at modeling and that policy discussions of AI conspicuously fail to center.

Notice what this would change about Morozov's prescriptions. He proposes "public, solidarity-based, and socialized infrastructural alternatives" to commercial AI. Wonderful. But what specifically should they do? If we take the feminist critique seriously, the answer cannot be "let the eolithic flâneur build whatever he wants." It has to be something more like: what does the home health aide need? What does the elementary school teacher need? What does the social worker need? What does the parent navigating special education bureaucracy need? Most of these workers (and they are mostly women, disproportionately women of color, often immigrants) have been entirely absent from the conversation about what AI should be. They are the people most likely to have AI imposed on them as a labor-discipline tool, and least likely to have a hand in designing it.

There is, gratifyingly, a small but growing literature on this. The Distributed AI Research Institute, founded by Timnit Gebru, has done important work on AI's relationship to labor, race, and gender. Data & Society has documented the conditions of ghost work performed largely by women in the Global South. The Care Collective's manifesto and Premilla Nadasen's work on care worker organizing connect care politics to technology politics. The recent literature on algorithmic management—Veena Dubal's work on gig workers, Karen Levy's work on truckers, Annette Bernhardt's work on warehouses—shows how AI is being deployed as a tool to intensify the disciplining of low-wage labor, and how this is reshaping the conditions of work for millions of (largely women, largely non-white) workers. This is the frontline of AI politics in 2024, and it is barely visible in Morozov's framework.

To be fair, Morozov is not hostile to any of this. I suspect he would readily agree that the feminist literature complements his project. But agreement is not the same as integration. The essay as written treats the central problem of AI as the cognitive-philosophical question of teleology versus eolithism, and the political problem as the institutional question of public versus private. It does not treat the gender division of labor as a central analytic category, and as a result it cannot quite see the labor force that is most affected by AI deployment, nor can it draw on the political traditions—care worker organizing, feminist mutual aid, the social reproduction strikes—that are most likely to produce a durable counter-coalition to the Efficiency Lobby.

A more capacious "Humanity Lobby" would put care workers and care thinkers at its center. It would treat the question "intelligent for what?" as inseparable from the question "intelligent for whom?" It would understand that the alternative to instrumental reason is not idle curiosity but attentive responsibility—a much harder, much more political ideal. And it would recognize that an AI worth having is one designed by, for, and with the people who have historically been written out of the definition of intelligence itself.

Against Cybersyn Nostalgia

Evgeny Morozov's essay is so well-constructed, so erudite, and so politically attractive that it is easy to overlook a question that ought to be obvious: did any of his preferred historical examples actually work? The Allende-era Cybersyn project, the Bariloche Foundation's world model, Oscar Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation," the MIT Environmental Ecology Lab, Negroponte's early Architecture Machine, Papert's One Laptop Per Child—Morozov parades these before us as embodiments of the alternative computing path. But every single one of them failed. Some failed because of coups and reactionary politics, yes. Others failed for internal reasons that Morozov does not examine. The accumulated failure rate ought to chasten the utopian invocations, and I want to spend this essay asking why it does not.

Cybersyn, the centerpiece of Morozov's positive program, deserves the closest scrutiny. The system was built over barely two years (1971-1973), never operated at full scale, and existed as much in Stafford Beer's diagrams as in actual functioning hardware. Its central technology, the Cyberstride statistical analysis system, was a fairly conventional Bayesian filter for time-series data; its famous Operations Room was a single mock-up with non-interactive screens. Could it have worked at scale? We don't know. The Pinochet coup ended the experiment before it could be tested. But the available evidence, as documented in Eden Medina's careful book Cybernetic Revolutionaries, suggests that Cybersyn was running into serious problems even before September 1973: telex coverage was incomplete, data was unreliable, workers in the nationalized factories were ambivalent about participation, and the management theory underlying the whole apparatus—Beer's Viable System Model—was a controversial and somewhat eccentric framework even among cyberneticians.

Morozov treats Cybersyn as a proof of concept for democratic computing. It is no such thing. It is, at best, a fragmentary trace of a possibility that was never realized. To build political programs on this trace is to engage in the kind of nostalgia that has consistently impoverished the left's relationship with technology. The Bariloche model is a similar case: a one-time research output, intellectually interesting, with limited practical influence even within Latin American planning circles. Varsavsky's "numerical experimentation," for all its conceptual elegance, did not generate the alternative development trajectories he hoped for. The MIT Environmental Ecology Lab dissolved without leaving institutional residue. The Architecture Machine Group became the Media Lab and was promptly colonized by exactly the corporate interests Morozov decries. One Laptop Per Child distributed computers to children in developing countries with disappointing pedagogical outcomes and has been the subject of substantial critical literature.

What unites these failures? Several things, I would suggest, and none of them are reducible to the bad influence of the Cold War.

First, all of these projects underestimated the difficulty of scaling participatory computing systems. A small team can build a clever prototype; a society cannot easily run on it. The information requirements, the training requirements, the maintenance requirements, the institutional alignment requirements all compound as you move from a workshop to a national system. This is true regardless of whether the underlying philosophy is "instrumental" or "ecological."

Second, they tended to assume that the political question (who controls the technology, for what purposes) was the hard part, and that the technical and organizational questions would resolve themselves once the politics was right. The historical record suggests the opposite: even well-resourced and politically aligned projects struggle enormously with the mundane work of making complex sociotechnical systems function. The Soviet OGAS project, the French Plan Calcul, the Cuban informatics initiatives, the Indian National Informatics Centre—each had moments of promise and each ran into the wall of organizational complexity. To pretend that an alternative political settlement would have produced a working alternative AI is to repeat a mistake the left has been making since the 1960s.

Third—and this is where I most sharply disagree with Morozov—these projects often produced worse outcomes for the people they claimed to serve. Top-down "participatory" technology, designed by intellectuals with idiosyncratic philosophical commitments and rolled out across populations who were not consulted, has a poor track record. One Laptop Per Child is a case study: brilliant designers, sincere intentions, terrible implementation, mixed-to-negative pedagogical results, substantial waste. The Soviet planners who tried to use computing for economic management produced shortages and misallocations comparable to those they were trying to solve. The lesson is not that public computing is impossible; it is that the gap between vision and execution is enormous and is usually filled by hubris.

Now, Morozov is too sophisticated a writer to be ignorant of any of this. He acknowledges, briefly, that the MIT Environmental Ecology Lab and One Laptop Per Child both failed politically. But he treats these failures as departures from the eolithic ideal rather than as evidence of its impracticability. The pattern is suspicious: every success is attributed to ecological reason, every failure is attributed to instrumental reason creeping back in. This is unfalsifiable, and it allows the utopian program to remain perpetually pristine while concrete alternatives accumulate disappointments.

There is also a deeper political problem. Morozov's essay reads, to me, as a piece of intellectual consolation for the contemporary left, which has watched in dismay as AI has been built by Silicon Valley with virtually no organized democratic resistance. The essay offers, in lieu of a strategy, a counterfactual—a beautiful "what if"—that allows readers to feel that the current state of AI is not the inevitable expression of capitalism but a contingent historical mistake. This is comforting. It is also possibly false. The current state of AI may in fact be the substantially inevitable expression of computing under capitalism, and confronting that possibility honestly is the precondition for any politically effective response.

What might a less nostalgic position look like? I would propose: take the actually-existing AI seriously, on its own terms, as a major industrial technology with massive externalities and concentrated ownership; develop the kind of policy and regulatory infrastructure that other industrial technologies have eventually been subjected to (food safety, finance, pharmaceuticals); strengthen labor protections against displacement; insist on environmental accountability for compute infrastructure; build worker power inside and adjacent to AI companies; and stop pretending that we have, in the historical archive, a worked-out alternative computing tradition that simply needs to be revived. We don't. We have fragments, suggestive failures, and a lot of work to do.

I share Morozov's belief that AI as currently developed is bad for democracy. I share his hope that an alternative is possible. I do not share his suggestion that Hans Otto Storm, Salvador Allende, and the MIT hippies have already given us the blueprint. They haven't. The blueprint, if it comes, will come from political struggle in the present, conducted by people who are clear-eyed about both the technology and the historical record. Romantic nostalgia is not strategy. The Allende reference, in particular, ought to come with a warning label about the consequences of underestimating one's opponents.

This is what daily work with these tools is like. Anyone who has spent serious time with GPT-4 or Claude knows the particular pleasure of the unexpected connection, the rapid prototype, the "huh, that worked" moment. Morozov is right that this is a genuinely different mode of work than the rigid, rules-based interaction with classical software. He has named something real.

The second thing I want to say is that the political-economic constraints he identifies are also real, and they shape my work in ways that are easier to feel than to articulate. The fact that I am paying OpenAI per token does change what I build. There are queries I don't run because they cost too much. There are experiments I don't try because the inference budget would be unjustifiable. There are features I don't ship because I cannot guarantee they won't hallucinate in costly ways. The "subscription model" is not an incidental feature of the technology; it shapes the design space of what gets built. A non-commercial alternative—local models running on my laptop, or shared community infrastructure—would produce different products. I have built small things with Llama and Mistral models on my own hardware, and the experience is genuinely different. Cheaper experimentation, more willingness to throw things away, more eolithic in Morozov's sense.

There is genuine open-source AI work happening, but the compute economics mean that the frontier of capability is set by a small number of well-funded labs. Even the most committed open-source contributors are using infrastructure—pre-trained weights, evaluation benchmarks, datasets—that came out of corporate or quasi-corporate efforts. This is not a moral indictment; it is a structural fact. A "public AI" that wants to be at the frontier of capability would need budgets and talent comparable to those at OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. That is a political project of enormous scale, and I do not see it coming from the kind of philosophical reorientation Morozov proposes.

The third thing is about what these systems actually are, technically. Morozov writes that LLMs "resemble the flexibility of the market more than the rigidity of bureaucracy" and that "their outputs are driven by changing data, not fixed protocols." This is approximately right but obscures something important. A trained model is, once trained, a fixed artifact. The "market-like" flexibility Morozov describes is really the flexibility of the prompting layer, not of the model itself. Even leaving the foundation models in corporate hands, there is enormous room for non-commercial, public-interest, or community-controlled work at the prompting and tooling layer. This is happening, in scattered ways, through projects like Hugging Face's open ecosystem, through academic NLP groups, through civic-tech communities. It is not glamorous, but it is real.

The fourth thing I want to say is that Morozov's eolithic ideal, while phenomenologically accurate to a part of AI-assisted work, misses a part that matters more for outcomes. A lot of what makes generative AI actually useful, in production settings, is teleological in exactly the way Morozov criticizes. We define an evaluation set, we measure performance against it, we iterate on prompts and scaffolding until performance improves. This is means-ends analysis on a model. Without it, the "experimentation" Morozov describes produces a lot of fun demos and few reliable tools. The genuine engineering work of making AI useful is, currently, much closer to Newell and Simon than to Storm's flâneur. I do not think this is a problem; I think it is what useful engineering has always looked like.

What follows? I think the right way to read Morozov, from a builder's perspective, is as proposing a corrective rather than a replacement. The dominant culture of AI development is over-indexed on benchmarks, on user-engagement metrics, on shipping features quickly to capture market share. It is under-indexed on the kind of open-ended exploration that produces genuinely new capabilities and on the kind of public-interest deliberation that decides what to build in the first place. Building in more eolithic time and space—both at the individual level and at the institutional level—would probably make the technology better, in both technical and social senses. Public-option AI infrastructure would expand the design space. Better tools for non-commercial experimentation would democratize who gets to build.

But the framing of "instrumental versus ecological reason" feels, from the inside of the work, less like a fundamental dichotomy and more like a choice of emphasis within a single activity that necessarily involves both. The hard questions are not philosophical but practical: how do we fund public compute? how do we structure access? how do we evaluate models for public-interest applications? how do we build the technical talent pipeline outside the FAANG salary regime? These are the questions builders care about, and I would love to read Morozov's next essay engaging them in detail.

In the meantime, I will say that his essay has changed how I think about my own work in at least one specific way. I have started, in the last few weeks, to spend an hour or two each week building things with no defined goal—just exploring what the models can do, with no product roadmap in mind. It is the most productive hour of my week, by every metric I care about. Whether that vindicates Morozov's eolithism or just describes good engineering practice, I leave to readers to decide.

Morozov relies heavily on Storm’s imagery of the prehistoric wanderer who picks up a stone and, in a moment of idle curiosity, envisions it as a spearhead. He contrasts this with “design,” which begins with a fixed goal. He argues that ChatGPT acts as a sort of experimental workshop for this eolithic process, allowing users to “mess about” without a predefined telos. But this analogy collapses upon close inspection. The stone in Storm’s example is a naturally occurring object. An LLM is a hyper-engineered, mathematically rigid artifact containing billions of parameters optimized through gradient descent.

When a user sits down at ChatGPT, they are not a prehistoric flâneur strolling through a field. They are a pilot stepping into the cockpit of a massively complex semantic engine. Interacting with an LLM effectively requires a highly intentional, structured form of communication known as "prompt engineering." To get a generative AI to produce anything of sustained value—whether it is a functional language-learning app or a coherent block of code—requires rigorous, iterative, goal-oriented refinement. The user must constrain the model’s latent space, correcting its hallucinations and guiding its outputs. This is not purposeless play; it is the very essence of instrumental reason. It is design.

By labeling the interaction with AI as “eolithic,” Morozov conflates the *versatility* of a tool with a lack of purpose. A Swiss Army Knife can be used for many things, but one does not use it aimlessly. Generative AI is a general-purpose technology, but its utility is entirely dependent on human teleology. If we abandon problem-solving and goal orientation, as Morozov’s “ecological reason” seems to suggest, we are left with nothing more than the digital equivalent of a kaleidoscope—a mesmerizing, hallucinatory toy that produces endless variations of text and image without any anchor to reality.

Furthermore, Morozov’s dismissal of “instrumental reason” as a relic of Cold War militarism and neoliberal efficiency ignores the critical necessity of problem-solving in the 21st century. We are not living in a prehistoric idyll where we can afford to think “preferably about nothing at all.” We are facing urgent, existential problems: climate change, pandemics, logistical bottlenecks, and extreme wealth inequality. Overcoming these challenges will require massive, coordinated, highly efficient interventions.

We need AI to optimize energy grids, accelerate the discovery of new materials for solar cells, model complex epidemiological scenarios, and manage global supply chains to reduce waste. These are deeply teleological tasks. They require exactly the kind of rigorous, goal-oriented “design” that Morozov critiques. If the “Humanity Lobby” adopts eolithism as its guiding philosophy, it will relegate itself to producing interactive art and participatory budgeting dashboards while the Efficiency Lobby continues to build the infrastructure that actually runs the world.

Morozov’s invocation of Salvador Allende’s Project Cybersyn is ironic in this context. He praises Cybersyn as an example of posing problems politically rather than technologically. Yet, Cybersyn was a masterpiece of instrumental reason. It was designed to solve a very specific, life-or-death problem for the Chilean economy: how to manage national production and bypass reactionary strikes in real-time. It was not built for eolithic play; it was built for survival. The tragedy of Cybersyn is not that it lacked “ecological reason,” but that it lacked the computational power and political time to fully execute its teleological goals.

The solution to the democratic deficit in AI is not to abandon problem-solving for a romanticized notion of play. The solution is to democratize the power to define the problems. The issue with modern AI is not that it is efficient; the issue is that it is efficient at maximizing engagement and generating profit for a handful of tech monopolies, rather than being efficient at solving problems for the public good.

We do need a radical political project to reclaim AI. But that project must embrace, rather than reject, the power of engineering and design. We must demand public funding for AI research directed at climate mitigation, public health, and sustainable logistics. We need democratic oversight of datasets and algorithms to ensure they serve public needs rather than corporate surveillance. The “AI we deserve” is not an eolithic stone to play with; it is a meticulously designed engine to help us build a survivable future. We cannot afford to be flâneurs; we must be architects.

Evgeny Morozov’s most valuable move is to refuse the cramped choice between resignation and refusal. The dominant debate about AI too often oscillates between “adopt it, regulate it, and compete” and “reject it as a poisoned artifact of empire and capital.” Morozov asks a harder question: what kinds of intelligence have been excluded by the institutional history of computing, and what would it mean to build technologies that nourish those forms rather than merely automate goal pursuit? The phrase “ecological reason” is useful because it names something many people have felt in their own encounters with generative AI. The technology is neither simply a calculator nor simply a fake person. It is often most interesting when used not to complete a predefined task but to widen the space of possible tasks. Ask it for a list of ways to organize a neighborhood meeting, a set of metaphors for explaining a medical diagnosis, a translation that preserves tone rather than literal structure, or a set of alternative framings for a local budget fight, and it can help people see paths they had not imagined. Its strongest democratic promise lies less in “solving” politics than in helping citizens pose problems more richly.

But Morozov is right that this promise is truncated by the institutional home generative AI has been given. The most advanced systems are not public workshops. They are rented appliances. The user enters alone, pays per query or through a subscription, and receives a personalized output from a proprietary infrastructure whose costs are largely hidden. The model of intelligence here is not the town meeting, the library, the classroom, the union hall, or the public laboratory. It is the platform marketplace. Even when the interaction feels playful, it is shaped by metering, surveillance, data extraction, and dependency.

If we take Morozov’s argument seriously, the policy response cannot stop at transparency mandates or procurement rules. Those are necessary but insufficient. We need public institutions capable of making AI into civic infrastructure. The analogy should not be “a government ChatGPT,” if that means merely a public-sector chatbot answering tax questions. The better analogy is the public library: a staffed, trusted, locally embedded institution that gives people access not only to information but to tools, training, community, and a measure of autonomy from private gatekeepers.

Imagine a network of public AI workshops housed in libraries, universities, community colleges, municipal offices, and labor education centers. Their purpose would not be to maximize “AI adoption.” It would be to let people experiment with computational tools in relation to their own projects: tenants analyzing lease patterns, teachers adapting materials for multilingual classrooms, small farmers comparing climate adaptation strategies, disability advocates designing accessible public information, local historians building community archives, patients preparing better questions for doctors, workers scrutinizing scheduling algorithms. Such workshops would combine technical support with democratic facilitation. The point would be not only to produce outputs but to cultivate judgment.

This matters because ecological reason is not a private mental faculty. It is social. Morozov’s Stone Age flâneur is a useful provocation, but democratic intelligence rarely emerges from solitary wandering alone. It emerges when people bring partial experiences into contact: a nurse knows one thing about a hospital workflow, a patient another, a janitor another, a billing specialist another. AI can help visualize, summarize, translate, simulate, and reframe those perspectives. But only institutions can gather them, legitimate them, and turn them into collective capacity.

That is why the examples from Chile and Argentina are so important. Their lesson is not that Project Cybersyn should be recreated with modern dashboards, nor that planning is inherently emancipatory. It is that information technology can be placed inside political projects that ask citizens and workers what futures they want. In contemporary terms, the democratic question is not simply whether an algorithm is explainable. It is whether the people affected by a system can contest the goals it serves.

For instance, consider health care. A realist AI agenda says: use models to summarize patient records, reduce physician paperwork, detect risk, and improve efficiency. That may be worthwhile. But a democratic ecological agenda asks different questions. Why are records so unreadable in the first place? Which burdens are being shifted onto patients? What kinds of knowledge are excluded from clinical encounters? Could AI help patients compare treatment paths, understand uncertainty, document symptoms, and participate more fully in decisions? Could it help nurses and care workers identify systemic failures rather than merely accelerate throughput? The same technology can either deepen managerial control or expand participatory knowledge.

The same distinction applies in education. The narrow question is whether students will cheat. The broader question is whether schools will use AI to intensify assessment culture or to create more time for exploration. A school district could deploy AI to generate worksheets, grade essays faster, and monitor students more closely. Or it could use AI to help students compare explanations, interrogate sources, stage debates, translate family histories, prototype projects, and reflect on how tools shape thought. The latter requires teachers with time, autonomy, and training. It cannot be purchased as a subscription add-on.

The hard part is funding and governance. Public AI cannot mean handing public money to the same firms under friendlier branding. It must include public ownership or stewardship of crucial layers: datasets, evaluation regimes, compute capacity, open models where appropriate, and domain-specific tools. There are already hints of such possibilities in open-source AI, national research clouds, university consortia, and nonprofit model development. But without a political coalition, these fragments will remain subordinate to corporate infrastructure.

A “Humanity Lobby,” to use Morozov’s phrase, would need to include teachers, librarians, unions, patient groups, artists, disability advocates, local governments, environmental organizations, and public-interest technologists. Its demand should be simple: if AI is becoming a general-purpose infrastructure, then the public must have institutions capable of shaping it for public ends. Not merely auditing it after deployment. Not merely begging companies to behave. Shaping it.

This also means accepting some limits. Ecological reason is incompatible with the fantasy of frictionless personalization at infinite scale. A democratic AI system might deliberately encourage reuse, sharing, and slower deliberation. It might tell a user: “Someone in your community already built a similar resource; would you like to adapt it?” It might privilege local knowledge over generic optimization. It might measure success by collective learning rather than engagement, productivity, or revenue. Such design choices will look inefficient from the standpoint of the market. That is precisely the point.

Morozov’s essay risks sounding abstract because “ecological reason” is a large philosophical banner. But its implications are concrete. Put AI in libraries, not only apps. Fund civic compute, not only corporate clouds. Treat datasets as public and communal resources where possible, not merely proprietary assets. Evaluate systems by whether they expand participation and judgment, not only by benchmark performance. Give workers and citizens authority over the goals embedded in automation.

The AI we deserve will not arrive from better prompts or kinder CEOs. It will require institutions that let people use computation to discover, debate, and revise their ends together. That is a democratic project before it is a technical one. Morozov is right: the question is not whether machines can be intelligent in our place. It is whether we can become intelligent enough, collectively, to build machines that do not make us smaller.

Evgeny Morozov offers a welcome antidote to the dreary realism of much AI debate. He is right that artificial intelligence has inherited institutional habits from military, corporate, and bureaucratic settings, and right that “democratizing AI” cannot mean merely adding transparency to systems whose purposes remain untouched. Democracy is not opposed to instrumentality. It depends on it. A city that wants to reduce childhood asthma, a government that wants to deliver unemployment benefits on time, a hospital that wants to avoid medication errors, a union that wants to enforce a contract, a climate agency that wants to cut emissions: all are engaged in goal-directed activity. These goals are not inherently oppressive because they are goals. They are democratically legitimate when they are publicly debated, revisable, and pursued through institutions answerable to those affected.

Morozov’s critique of AI’s teleological origins captures something real about the Cold War research environment. Early AI did grow in proximity to defense agencies and managerial dreams of command and control. The General Problem Solver is a telling artifact: it presumes formalized problems, stable environments, and specified ends. But it does not follow that goal-directed computation is merely bureaucratic rationality in technical form. Weather forecasting, epidemiological modeling, accessible speech recognition, traffic safety systems, protein folding prediction, and assistive technologies for disabled people are also goal-directed. The problem is not that they pursue ends. The problem arises when ends are imposed without democratic authorization or when optimization blinds institutions to values that cannot be captured in the metric.

That distinction matters because a democracy without competent administration is not more humane. It is merely more frustrating, unequal, and vulnerable to demagogues. The recent history of public life is full of examples. People lose faith in democratic government when public systems cannot deliver passports, benefits, housing permits, disability services, or disaster aid. Bureaucracy can be dehumanizing, but arbitrary undercapacity is dehumanizing too. If AI can help a public agency answer questions in multiple languages, identify backlogs, flag inconsistent decisions, or make forms easier to complete, that is not a betrayal of ecological reason. It is a democratic good.

Morozov rightly warns against the Efficiency Lobby. Yet efficiency is not always the enemy of dignity. For a poor person spending hours navigating welfare paperwork, an immigrant trying to understand a legal notice, or a nurse drowning in documentation, efficiency may mean time restored to life. The left should be careful not to concede “efficiency” to managers and markets. There is a democratic efficiency: the reduction of needless burdens, the simplification of access to rights, the conservation of public resources for public purposes.

The strongest part of Morozov’s essay is his insistence that AI tools should help citizens pose problems, not merely solve official ones. Participatory budgeting, public modeling, and simulations of alternative futures are exactly the kinds of uses that deserve attention. But these activities too require structure, deadlines, constraints, and decisions. Citizens cannot deliberate forever in a stonefield of possibilities. At some point a budget must be passed, a flood barrier built, a curriculum chosen, a strike authorized, a treatment plan selected. Democratic politics is not the suspension of telos; it is the collective struggle over teloi.

Much creativity does arise when people encounter materials without a fixed plan. But democratic institutions cannot be organized primarily around wandering. They must create conditions in which wandering can inform action. A public library, a classroom, an arts program, a makerspace, or a citizen assembly may protect open-ended exploration, but each also has rules, budgets, facilitators, and purposes. The question is not whether we can escape instrumental reason. It is whether we can subordinate instrumental reason to reflective judgment.

This is also where the analogy between generative AI and the market becomes too sweeping. Morozov says that today’s AI resembles markets: a universal method for satisfying diverse demands, governed by private platforms and metered access. There is truth in this, especially in the API economy. But LLMs are not markets in any deep institutional sense. They do not coordinate independent producers through prices; they generate representations from trained statistical patterns. Their defects—hallucination, opacity, bias, dependence on concentrated compute—are not simply market defects. Nor will decommodification alone solve them. A public model can hallucinate. An open model can produce abuse. A nonprofit system can encode dominant assumptions. A state-run system can surveil.

That is why the language of “the Humanity Lobby” needs institutional specificity. Who governs public AI? Elected officials? Expert agencies? Citizen boards? Courts? Professional associations? Worker councils? All of the above? Different domains require different arrangements. A public AI tool used in criminal justice should be subject to stricter prohibitions than one used in art education. A medical model needs clinical validation. A tool for legislative drafting needs transparency and archival accountability. A model for citizens exploring climate scenarios needs plural assumptions and contestable parameters. Democracy is not a single institutional form; it is an ecology of checks, rights, capacities, and forums.

Morozov’s own examples point toward this more pluralistic conclusion. Project Cybersyn was not merely eolithic experimentation. It was a system built to manage nationalized industries under conditions of political emergency. It had democratic aspirations, but also technocratic risks. The Bariloche Foundation’s alternative modeling was valuable because it contested the assumptions of Northern modernization and environmental discourse. But its value lay in goal-laden argument: whose development, whose sacrifices, whose needs, whose future? These projects did not transcend instrumentality. They politicized it.

The same should be our aim with AI. We should not ask technology to deliver a post-instrumental society. We should build institutions that make the purposes of technology contestable. In practice, that means several things. First, high-stakes AI systems should be governed by domain-specific law, not voluntary ethics. Second, public agencies should have in-house technical capacity so they are not dependent on vendors. Third, workers affected by AI deployment should have bargaining rights over its use. Fourth, public-interest research should be funded to build tools that serve neglected needs. Fifth, citizens should be able to challenge automated decisions and demand human review.

These measures may sound less utopian than ecological reason. But they are not merely “policy tweaks.” They are ways of relocating power. A democratic society should indeed invest in technologies that support exploration, creativity, and public imagination. It should also use technology to make trains run, benefits arrive, and hospitals function. The danger is not instrumental reason as such. The danger is unaccountable instrumentality: optimization without politics, efficiency without rights, automation without voice.

The AI we deserve should leave room for messing about. It should help students, artists, organizers, and citizens discover possibilities they did not know how to ask for. But it should also help us accomplish hard collective tasks. Clean water, decarbonization, elder care, housing, public health, and legal access are not neoliberal distractions. They are democratic ends. We need not choose between the flâneur and the administrator. A mature democratic politics must make space for both: the person who notices a new use for the stone, and the community that decides what to build with it.

The missing figure in Evgeny Morozov’s essay is the worker who is told that AI will make her “more productive.” Morozov gives us the bureaucrat, the office worker, the flâneur, the language learner, the citizen, the Chilean planner, and the eolithic junkman. But the central battlefield over generative AI in 2024 is the workplace. That is where the Efficiency Lobby is strongest, where the language of augmentation most quickly becomes speedup, and where democracy will either be made concrete or exposed as a slogan.

The philosophical distinction between instrumental and ecological reason is helpful, but for workers the problem is less abstract. AI is being introduced by employers who own the systems, define the metrics, capture the gains, and discipline the people whose labor supplies the data. A call-center worker finds scripts generated and monitored by AI. A warehouse worker is scheduled by predictive software. A journalist sees drafts, summaries, and headlines automated while staffing shrinks. A software engineer is told to use code assistants to ship faster. A nurse gets an AI documentation tool that may reduce typing but may also expand the volume of expected charting. A teacher receives AI grading and lesson-planning products while class sizes remain too large. In each case, the question is not only what kind of intelligence AI embodies. It is who controls the labor process.

This is why “democratic AI” must begin with workplace democracy. Transparency, open source, public models, and ecological experimentation are all important, but they will not protect people if employers retain unilateral authority to deploy AI. Workers need bargaining rights over adoption, monitoring, evaluation, data collection, staffing effects, and productivity expectations. Without that, AI will become another chapter in a long history of technological change whose benefits are socialized upward and whose harms are individualized downward.

Morozov’s history of AI as an offspring of bureaucratic and military rationality maps neatly onto labor history. Scientific management also promised to replace messy human judgment with optimized procedure. Time-and-motion studies, assembly-line discipline, standardized office routines, algorithmic scheduling, and platform management all share a dream: management sees; labor is seen. Generative AI adds a new twist. It does not simply measure work. It can absorb, imitate, and recombine the products of work. The writer’s archive, the illustrator’s style, the programmer’s repository, the translator’s phrasing, the support agent’s chat logs, the doctor’s notes, the teacher’s materials—all become raw material for systems that may later be used to cheapen the labor that produced them.

This is not merely a copyright issue, though copyright matters. It is a political economy issue. Training data is accumulated social labor. The fact that much of it was publicly visible on the internet does not make it free in any moral sense. Nor does the fact that models produce “new” outputs settle the distributive question. Industrial capitalism has always depended on converting social cooperation into private assets. Generative AI is an unusually vivid version of that process: a machine trained on collective expression returns as a subscription service sold back to the people whose culture made it possible.

Morozov’s proposed “Humanity Lobby” should therefore include unions at its center. Not as one constituency among many, but as the institutions best positioned to contest AI where it is deployed. A democratic AI agenda that does not empower workers will become a consumer-rights program: better notices, safer products, perhaps some public alternatives, while the workplace remains authoritarian. But most adults encounter AI not as autonomous citizens choosing tools in a marketplace. They encounter it as employees instructed to comply.

What would a labor-centered AI democracy require? First, notice and disclosure. Employers should be required to inform workers when AI systems are used to monitor, evaluate, schedule, recommend discipline, assign tasks, set pay, or generate work product. Secret algorithmic management is incompatible with basic dignity.

Second, bargaining. AI deployment should be a mandatory subject of collective bargaining. Workers should have the right to negotiate over whether a system is introduced, what data it uses, how outputs are reviewed, and how productivity gains are shared. If an AI tool saves time, that time should not automatically become higher quotas or layoffs. It could become shorter hours, better staffing, training, or improved service quality.

Third, audit rights. Workers and their representatives should have access to independent technical expertise. It is not enough for a vendor to claim that a system is accurate or unbiased. Workers need the ability to inspect error rates, appeal decisions, test disparate impacts, and challenge the assumptions embedded in metrics. This is especially important because AI systems often turn qualitative work into quantitative proxies. A teacher’s care, a nurse’s judgment, a social worker’s patience, a customer service agent’s emotional labor—these can be damaged by systems that measure speed or script adherence.

Fourth, data rights. Workplace data should not be treated as the employer’s unlimited property. Workers generate data through their actions, communications, movements, and outputs. They should have rights over collection, retention, secondary use, and model training. A company should not be able to use years of employee performance data to build automation tools without negotiation and compensation.

Fifth, public support for worker-led technology. Here Morozov’s ecological reason becomes especially powerful. Imagine unions, cooperatives, professional associations, and public universities jointly developing AI tools designed not to intensify labor but to support worker judgment. Nurses could build systems that flag unsafe staffing patterns. Teachers could create shared curricular assistants governed by educators rather than ed-tech vendors. Public defenders could use AI to manage discovery without surrendering client confidentiality to corporate platforms. Construction workers could use computer vision and language tools to identify safety hazards, with data controlled by joint labor-management committees. The same underlying techniques can serve surveillance or solidarity depending on governance.

This approach also changes how we think about “augmentation.” In corporate rhetoric, augmentation usually means that one worker can do more. In democratic labor politics, augmentation should mean that workers have more control, skill, and collective power. A tool that helps a novice employee learn from experienced colleagues may be augmenting. A tool that captures expert know-how and uses it to deskill the job is not. A system that reduces drudgery while preserving staffing may be welcome. A system that eliminates entry-level positions and hollows out career ladders is not. The distinction cannot be made by engineers alone. It must be made through organized worker voice.

Morozov is right to emphasize that today’s AI embodies a market ethos. But workplaces remind us that markets are often experienced as command. The employment contract gives bosses powers that would look intolerable in civic life: surveillance, discipline, unilateral restructuring, control over tools, and ownership of output. If AI democratization focuses only on public-facing systems while ignoring this private government, it will leave the Efficiency Lobby largely intact.

The recent wave of labor activism in Hollywood is instructive. Writers and actors did not merely ask whether generative AI was creative or whether models had consciousness. They asked whether studios could use AI to appropriate likenesses, generate scripts, undermine residuals, and weaken bargaining power. Their answer was collective negotiation. That should be the model across sectors. The point is not to ban every use of AI. It is to ensure that workers have enforceable power over its terms.

A labor-centered response also avoids two temptations. The first is technological fatalism: AI is coming, adapt or die. The second is artisanal nostalgia: preserve every existing job exactly as it is. Workers have often embraced technologies that reduce danger, drudgery, and monotony. But they have also learned that “innovation” without power becomes exploitation. The question is not whether tasks change. It is whether people share in deciding how they change.

Morozov asks us to imagine an AI not beholden to the Efficiency Lobby. We should answer: start where that lobby issues orders. Start at work. Give workers rights over AI. Fund worker-governed tools. Treat training data as social labor. Convert productivity gains into public and worker benefits. Build institutions where those affected by automation can say no, yes, or yes on different terms.

The AI we deserve is not a chatbot with better values pasted on top. It is a reorganization of power around the people whose knowledge, language, care, and creativity make the technology possible. If ecological reason means honoring the intelligence embedded in lived practice, then the first democratic demand is obvious: let workers govern the machines built from their work.

Evgeny Morozov’s essay is at its best when it recalls that technological paths are contingent. Artificial intelligence did not have to grow from the priorities of Cold War funders, bureaucratic rationalizers, and corporate platforms. It could have been otherwise. But his account underplays one of the most important ways it is already becoming otherwise: the unruly, imperfect, global open-source AI ecosystem.

To be sure, open source is no magic solvent for power. Open models can be captured by corporations, used for surveillance, exploited by malicious actors, or maintained by underpaid volunteers. They can reproduce the same data injustices and cultural biases as closed systems. Yet if Morozov is searching for eolithic possibility—playful repurposing, vernacular adaptation, unexpected recombination—then open-source AI is not a side issue. It is one of the few existing spaces where that possibility is visible at scale.

The past two years have shown that generative AI is not confined to the largest proprietary labs. Meta’s Llama releases, Mistral’s models, Stability AI’s earlier image tools, EleutherAI’s research culture, Hugging Face’s model repositories, countless fine-tunes, quantizations, adapters, local inference projects, and domain-specific experiments have created a lively ecology of tinkering. People run smaller models on laptops. Communities fine-tune systems for minority languages, role-playing games, legal research, coding help, accessibility tools, and local archives. Researchers inspect weights, compare methods, and build alternatives. Hobbyists stitch together speech recognition, text generation, image models, retrieval systems, and home automation in ways no product manager would have approved.

This looks very much like Morozov’s stonefield. A model released for one purpose becomes a translation aid, a writing partner, a game master, a tutor, a dataset cleaner, a search interface, a tool for blind users, a companion for language preservation, or a component in an art installation. The users are not merely consumers. They are modifiers. They do not just prompt; they fork, fine-tune, compress, evaluate, and share. The results are uneven, often fragile, sometimes irresponsible. But eolithic craft was never tidy.

Closed AI systems, by contrast, are anti-eolithic even when they feel flexible. They invite experimentation at the surface while forbidding deeper alteration. You may ask the model anything allowed by policy, but you cannot examine its training mix, change its weights, run it without permission, adapt it fully to your community, or preserve it if the company changes terms. The interface simulates openness; the infrastructure remains closed. In that sense Morozov’s language-learning app is a perfect example: creative at the user level, dependent at the system level.

Open-source AI changes the dependency relation. It allows schools, cities, libraries, small firms, cooperatives, researchers, and communities to build systems without sending every query to a corporate API. It makes possible local deployment where privacy matters. It permits adaptation to low-resource languages and specialized domains that are unprofitable for major firms. It enables public audits and red-team evaluations beyond what companies choose to disclose. It gives students and citizens a chance to understand AI as a material practice, not a magical service.

This is not an argument for abolishing all restrictions. Some model capabilities raise real safety concerns. Open image and voice tools can facilitate fraud, nonconsensual sexual imagery, and political manipulation. Open language models can assist spam, cyber abuse, or biological misinformation. The open-source community sometimes treats these worries too lightly, as if any limit were corporate propaganda. But the answer cannot be to centralize all advanced AI behind a few company gates. That path creates its own dangers: monopoly, state capture, opaque censorship, dependency, and the permanent infantilization of users.

A democratic AI politics should distinguish between openness as ideology and openness as institutional design. We need layered openness. For many models, open weights, open training recipes, open evaluations, and permissive local use should be encouraged. For higher-risk systems, access may need staged release, researcher review, licensing conditions, or compute thresholds. Public-interest auditing should be mandatory for both closed and open systems. But the default democratic instinct should be that people deserve the ability to inspect and modify general-purpose infrastructure, not merely rent it.

Morozov is right that the market ethos can colonize open source. Major firms release open models for strategic reasons: to commoditize complements, recruit developers, shape standards, or undercut rivals. Open repositories often run on corporate cloud infrastructure. Volunteer labor can become free research and development for capital. Still, this ambivalence is not unique to AI. The internet itself, Linux, Python, Wikipedia, and many scientific software communities have long existed in contested territory between commons and commerce. Their answer has not been purity. It has been governance: licenses, foundations, norms, public funding, and institutional alliances.

That is where Morozov’s “Humanity Lobby” can meet the open-source world. Instead of treating open AI as a libertarian hobby, public institutions should help turn it into a commons. Governments and universities could fund open models for public-interest domains: health information, legal access, education, climate adaptation, local government services, and language preservation. Libraries could host community model labs. Public broadcasters and museums could contribute curated datasets under democratic terms. Labor unions and professional associations could develop worker-governed tools. Cities could share open AI components rather than each purchasing proprietary systems from vendors. International consortia could support models for languages and regions ignored by Silicon Valley.

The key is not only open code or open weights, but open capacity. Compute is the bottleneck that can turn formal openness into practical dependency. If only large corporations can train or serve useful models, then “open” becomes ornamental. Public compute infrastructure—a research cloud, nonprofit inference services, shared university clusters, municipal or regional AI facilities—should be part of any democratic AI agenda. So should technical education. A community cannot govern tools it cannot understand.

Open source also disciplines the philosophical debate. Morozov contrasts instrumental and ecological reason, but in actual hacker practice the two constantly intermingle. Someone begins with a goal—make a model run on a cheap laptop—then discovers new uses. Someone fine-tunes for a narrow task, then the community repurposes the result. Someone builds a tool for efficiency, then artists misuse it beautifully. This is not the absence of goals; it is goal plasticity. The telos changes through contact with materials and other people. Open systems are valuable because they preserve that plasticity.

Closed platforms reduce plasticity. They decide which forms of misuse are permitted and which are forbidden; which integrations are supported; which prices make experimentation possible; which communities are worth serving. They also erase history. A model may be updated overnight, breaking workflows or removing behaviors users valued. Open models can be archived, compared, studied, and maintained. That continuity matters for democratic culture.

The strongest objection is that open-source AI empowers bad actors. It does. But so does literacy, encryption, chemistry, drones, and the internet. Democratic societies do not respond by confining all powerful knowledge to licensed monopolies. They build norms, laws, enforcement mechanisms, and counter-capacities. Moreover, closed systems are hardly safe by default. They can be abused through APIs, integrated into surveillance products, or quietly aligned with state and corporate interests. Morozov’s essay uses “ecological reason” to describe a form of intelligence grounded in context, indeterminacy, play, and mutual adjustment with the world. It is a suggestive phrase. The first test of any democratic AI agenda is whether it confronts the material ecology of computation: energy, water, minerals, land, e-waste, and the unequal geography of environmental harm.

Generative AI has made the physicality of the digital harder to ignore. Training frontier models requires massive data centers packed with specialized chips. Running them for millions of users requires continuing electricity and cooling. The exact numbers are often opaque, and companies have incentives to emphasize efficiency gains while hiding total growth. But the broad trend is clear enough: AI is accelerating demand for compute at precisely the moment when societies must decarbonize grids, conserve water, and allocate scarce infrastructure wisely. The fact that a single query may consume only a modest amount of energy compared with other activities does not settle the matter. At scale, and under a business model that encourages endless generation, even marginal costs become collective burdens.

Morozov’s language-learning app is charming, and his self-critique is important. Do we need every learner generating bespoke stories and audio anew, each through paid APIs, when shared resources could serve many people? This is not a small design question. It points to the absurdity of a computational economy organized around individualized abundance. We are building systems that can generate infinite custom text, images, code, voices, and video. But the planet does not have infinite energy, water, or mineral capacity. A democracy must ask not only “Can AI do this?” but “Is this use worth its material cost, compared with other uses?”

That question is largely absent from the dominant AI race. Companies compete to make models bigger, faster, more multimodal, and more deeply integrated into everyday workflows. Investors reward growth. Cloud providers build data centers. Governments fear falling behind China or losing industrial advantage. Users are encouraged to treat generation as frictionless. The result is a familiar pattern: private actors capture convenience and revenue while environmental costs are diffused across communities, grids, watersheds, and future generations.

The geography matters. Data centers are built somewhere. They draw on local electricity systems and water supplies. Chip supply chains require mining, fabrication, shipping, and disposal. The burdens do not fall evenly. Communities already exposed to industrial pollution, water stress, or weak labor protections are more likely to bear hidden costs. The “cloud” is not in the sky; it is in places where land is cheap, regulation is negotiable, and political power may be limited. A democratic AI politics must make those places visible.

Morozov contrasts the Efficiency Lobby with a Humanity Lobby. Environmental justice demands that the Humanity Lobby include the people living next to the infrastructure of AI and those extracting its materials. It is not enough to democratize access to models if the costs of access are imposed on others. Nor is it enough to build public AI systems if they reproduce the same energy-intensive habits as private platforms. A public bad is still a bad.

Companies developing and deploying large models should disclose not only training emissions but inference costs, data center locations in meaningful terms, water impacts, and mitigation strategies. Public agencies should not procure AI systems without environmental reporting.

If a database lookup can answer a question, it is wasteful to summon a giant language model. Ecological democracy would ask: what is the smallest, least resource-intensive system that meets a legitimate need? A society capable of ecological reason must discriminate between needs and manufactured demand.

If AI is becoming critical infrastructure, its physical footprint must be governed like infrastructure, not waved through as innovation.

The Stone Age wanderer’s stonefield did not require a hyperscale data center. Generative AI does. The metaphor of the workshop must be joined to the politics of the mine, the power plant, the river, and the landfill. Otherwise ecological reason becomes a privileged experience purchased through ecological unreason elsewhere.

Other uses are socially trivial or actively harmful. The market will not reliably distinguish between them because it recognizes willingness to pay, not public value. That is why Morozov’s call for a political project is indispensable.

The AI we deserve must be low-carbon, water-conscious, repairable, reusable, and governed by communities as well as users. It must encourage shared infrastructures rather than endless individualized generation. It must treat compute as something to be justified, not merely consumed. Morozov’s critique of goal-directed AI should push educators to ask uncomfortable questions. Have we built classrooms around the very instrumental reason we now fear AI will automate? When learning is reduced to task completion, students sensibly seek efficient completion. If the goal is a grade, and the grade rewards an output detached from process, then AI-assisted shortcuts are not an alien invasion. They are an adaptation to the logic students have been given.

Students need to develop memory, fluency, argument, attention, and the ability to sit with difficulty. Outsourcing every draft to a chatbot would impoverish them. Morozov’s invocation of David Hawkins’s “messing about” is especially relevant. AI can either short-circuit this exploration by supplying premature answers or enrich it by multiplying materials for inquiry.

Instead of asking for generic essays, teachers can ask students to connect readings to local observations, class discussions, personal field notes, or evolving drafts. Speculation about superintelligence can distract from present harms: labor exploitation, bias, surveillance, fraud, concentration, copyright, environmental costs, and the hollowing of public institutions. Machine learning already shapes targeting, surveillance, intelligence analysis, drone autonomy, cyber operations, border enforcement, and policing. Generative AI adds new capacities: automated persuasion, synthetic media, code generation, translation, document analysis, and simulation. Even when systems are unreliable, they can be useful enough to accelerate harmful activity. A model does not need to be conscious or generally intelligent to help a malicious actor write phishing emails, generate disinformation variants, summarize stolen data, or assist in vulnerability discovery. Morozov’s historical account of AI’s Cold War entanglements should make us more alert to these dangers, not less. If military and bureaucratic imperatives shaped AI from the beginning, then democratic AI requires more than cultivating eolithic workshops. It requires binding constraints on state and corporate uses of AI in coercive domains. The Efficiency Lobby is troubling when it optimizes office work. Some uses should be banned or sharply restricted: real-time biometric mass surveillance in public spaces, social scoring, fully autonomous lethal targeting without meaningful human control, predictive policing systems that reproduce discriminatory enforcement, AI systems that deny essential benefits without due process, and nonconsensual sexual deepfakes. It values imagination but does not confuse imagination with governance.

The historical examples Morozov invokes—Cybersyn, the Bariloche Foundation—are instructive for what they tell us about the limitations of planning rather than the possibilities of alternative AI. A genuinely eolithic approach to AI would need to be distributed, experimental, and responsive to local contexts rather than designed from above by technocratic visionaries, however well-intentioned.

Morozov's real target seems to be the concentration of AI development in a few private companies. On this point, we should agree. Several concrete governance mechanisms merit serious attention. First, algorithmic impact assessments—similar to environmental impact assessments—could require developers to evaluate how their systems affect civil rights, democratic participation, and social equity before deployment. Second, mandatory disclosure of training data, model architecture, and evaluation procedures would enable external auditing and public debate about AI systems' strengths and limitations. Third, stronger liability frameworks would hold developers and deployers responsible for foreseeable harms, creating incentives for safety and fairness that market incentives currently undermine.

High-stakes decisions affecting human welfare—employment, housing, criminal justice, healthcare—should be subject to stringent requirements regardless of whether the system is technically classified as "high-risk" under a predetermined taxonomy.

Morozov's emphasis on democratic participation is valuable but requires operationalization. What would it mean for citizens to genuinely participate in AI governance? Practical governance can proceed by trial and error, with rules being strengthened as we learn what works. We cannot afford to wait for a complete theory of intelligence before regulating the systems shaping human lives today.

The case for public AI rests on multiple grounds. First, many valuable AI applications—climate modeling, public health surveillance, educational resources—generate diffuse benefits that private markets underprovide because the benefits cannot be easily captured by investors. Second, AI systems making decisions about public goods and common resources should be accountable to the public rather than private shareholders. Indigenous communities developing AI tools for language preservation, ecological monitoring, and governance are not seeking to optimize instrumental rationality but to maintain and transmit cultural knowledge in changing conditions. A genuinely global perspective on AI would need to engage seriously with the diversity of intelligence traditions that exist worldwide. The Western focus on individual cognition that Morozov critiques—correctly—has a counterpart in many non-Western traditions that conceptualize intelligence differently, as distributed, collective, or embedded in relationships with non-human entities. Morozov's invocation of Heidegger's concept of boredom as an antidote to AI's teleological ethos is intriguing but philosophically problematic. For Heidegger, boredom is not merely a psychological state but a fundamental attunement that reveals the character of Being itself. In boredom, we are neither absorbed in our projects nor distracted by objects, but open to the world as a whole. This is a deeply anti-humanist position: the goal is not to enhance human capabilities but to enable Being to manifest itself through human existence.

Morozov seems to want something different: an AI that serves human flourishing by enabling modes of engagement that go beyond instrumental rationality. This is closer to Dewey's pragmatism than to Heidegger's phenomenology. For Dewey, intelligence is not a cognitive capacity possessed by individuals but an ongoing process of transaction between organisms and environments. What Morozov calls "ecological reason" is not unrelated to Dewey's understanding of embodied intelligence as a continuous process of adjustment and readjustment.

The choice between Heidegger and Dewey matters for how we think about AI. Heidegger's approach suggests that authentic relationship to technology requires withdrawal from the instrumental posture entirely—a kind of release from willing that is difficult to square with the practical engagement democratic citizenship requires. Dewey's approach suggests that we can develop habits and institutions that enable more intelligent engagement with technology without requiring metaphysical transformation.

Morozov notes that AI critics who turned to Heidegger failed to develop useful alternatives, while the pragmatist tradition is largely ignored by both AI developers and critics. This is an important observation, but it deserves more development. Why did Heidegger become the reference point for AI critique while Dewey remained marginal? One possibility is that Heidegger's critique is more dramatic and easier to deploy as a rhetorical weapon, while Dewey's pragmatism requires sustained engagement with practical problems that critics often find less appealing than grand philosophical pronouncements.

The consequences of this choice are significant. Heideggerian critiques of AI tend toward a kind of resigned skepticism—technology is dangerous, we are already in its grip, authenticity requires withdrawal. Pragmatist engagement with AI, by contrast, can be more hopeful, asking what we can do to make AI more useful for human purposes and how we can learn from experience to improve our designs. Morozov's essay wavers between these two approaches, sometimes gesturing toward Dewey's practical engagement and sometimes sliding into Heideggerian resignation.

A pragmatist approach to AI would start from concrete problems—how do we ensure that AI systems are fair, transparent, and accountable?—rather than grand metaphysical questions about the nature of intelligence. This does not mean abandoning philosophical reflection, but rather reconceiving it as an ongoing dialogue with practice rather than a foundation from which to deduce correct action. Morozov's alternative history is valuable precisely because it shows that current arrangements are contingent and could have been different. This is not efficient by corporate metrics, but it is highly effective by learning metrics. The failure of one laptop per child was not that it provided laptops to children but that it provided a standardized technological solution to what is fundamentally a social and pedagogical problem. Technology alone cannot create the conditions for learning; it must be embedded in relationships and practices that give it meaning.

He argues that we need a radical political project to direct technology toward human flourishing, but he also sometimes suggests that the right technology design could enable such flourishing even without the political transformation. These claims pull in opposite directions. If technology design alone were sufficient, the many attempts to develop humane computing technologies would have succeeded by now. If political transformation is necessary, then technology is at best a tool for movements that must do the harder work of changing power relations.

The educational context Morozov discusses is instructive. The promise of AI to personalize learning sounds appealing until we recognize that personalization often means optimizing for measurable outcomes rather than enabling genuine exploration. A technology that tailors content to student gaps as identified by algorithmic assessment is still operating within an instrumental framework; it has just made that framework more efficient.

What would genuinely eolithic AI in education look like? Perhaps it would involve AI systems that help students and teachers discover possibilities they had not anticipated, rather than filling gaps identified by diagnostic algorithms. Perhaps it would involve AI that preserves ambiguity rather than resolving it, helping students tolerate uncertainty rather than rushing to conclusions. Perhaps it would involve AI that connects learning to community contexts, enabling students to pursue questions that matter to them and their communities rather than predetermined curriculum standards.

We must build the social and institutional conditions that would make those tools meaningful. This means engaging in the unglamorous work of education reform: supporting teachers, reducing class sizes, providing resources, and resisting the accountability regimes that reduce education to measurement.
