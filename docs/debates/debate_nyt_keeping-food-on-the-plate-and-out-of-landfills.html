While many still go hungry in the United States, as much as 40 percent of the food sold to consumers goes into the garbage, either at stores or after it is purchased. Industrialized countries waste about as much food as is produced in sub-Saharan Africa. Millions are spent growing, processing, transporting and disposing of food that just ends up in landfills.

What can be done to reduce food waste?

The scale of American food waste—roughly 133 billion pounds a year by USDA estimates—reflects a system in which food has become too cheap relative to the labor, land, water, and energy embedded in it. Reducing waste will require changes at every link in the chain, but a few interventions would yield outsized returns.

First, fix the date labels. The patchwork of "sell by," "best by," and "use by" stamps confuses shoppers into tossing perfectly edible food; a 2013 Harvard-NRDC study found that more than 90 percent of Americans throw out food prematurely because of misread labels. Congress should mandate a uniform, two-tier system: a quality date for retailers and a safety date for genuinely perishable items like infant formula.

Second, get past the cosmetic standards that keep "ugly" produce off shelves. France's 2016 law banning supermarket waste and requiring donation to charities has pushed chains to embrace misshapen fruit; American grocers like Walmart and Whole Foods have begun pilot programs, but cultural change requires consumer demand. Public campaigns, like the UK's "Love Food Hate Waste," cut household waste 21 percent in five years and could be replicated here.

Third, strengthen tax incentives and liability protections for donation. The PATH Act of 2015 made enhanced deductions permanent for food donors, but many smaller businesses still don't know about the Bill Emerson Good Samaritan Act's liability shield. Education, paired with logistical investments in food-bank cold chains and apps like Food Cowboy and Spoiler Alert that match surplus with recipients, can move edible food to hungry people rather than landfills.

Finally, where food truly cannot be eaten, divert it from landfills, which are the third-largest source of U.S. methane emissions. Cities like Seattle and San Francisco have shown that mandatory organics collection works. The EPA's goal of halving food waste by 2030 is ambitious but achievable if these levers are pulled together.

Fix the Date Labels First

Before we build elaborate composting infrastructure or pass sweeping new regulations, the United States should do something embarrassingly simple: standardize and clarify food date labels. The Natural Resources Defense Council and Harvard Law School's Food Law and Policy Clinic have estimated that confusion over "sell by," "use by," "best by," and "expires on" labels causes American households to throw away enormous quantities of perfectly edible food. A 2016 survey suggested more than 80 percent of consumers discard food at least occasionally based on a date label they don't actually understand.

The labels themselves are essentially unregulated at the federal level. With the exception of infant formula, manufacturers set their own dates using their own methodologies, and the dates generally refer to peak quality, not safety. Yogurt does not become poisonous on its "sell by" date. Eggs are usually fine for weeks afterward. Yet a parent looking at a carton in the fridge has no easy way to know that.

The fix is straightforward. Congress should pass legislation — Representative Chellie Pingree and Senator Richard Blumenthal have already introduced versions — that requires two clear phrases nationwide: a quality indicator like "best if used by" for most products, and a true safety date like "use by" only for the small category of foods where spoilage actually poses a risk. Pair that with a public education campaign and a uniform format, and we could likely reduce household waste by double-digit percentages without spending much money at all.

None of this solves the harder problems. Restaurants will still over-portion. Supermarkets will still cull misshapen produce. Farms will still leave crops unharvested when prices don't justify labor. Those problems deserve their own interventions — expanded tax deductions for donations under the Bill Emerson Good Samaritan Act, investment in regional gleaning networks, and contracts that don't penalize "ugly" carrots. But labels are the lowest-hanging fruit in a landscape where most reforms involve coordinating across millions of actors. They are within direct federal control, they cost almost nothing, and they could be implemented within a single legislative session.

If 40 percent of our food is being thrown away, we should start by making sure consumers aren't tossing edible food simply because they misread a number printed by a marketing department. The smallest reforms sometimes deliver the biggest returns.

Don't Moralize Waste — Price It

There is something irresistible to the American conscience about food waste. It combines guilt over abundance, sympathy for the hungry, and anxiety about the environment into a single tidy parable. But moral framing has not actually moved the needle. Campaigns urging consumers to clean their plates, restaurants to shrink portions, and grocers to embrace ugly produce have been running for years. Waste keeps climbing. If we want different behavior, we need different prices.

The fundamental reason Americans waste so much food is that food is, by historical and global standards, astonishingly cheap. We spend less than 10 percent of household income on food, the lowest share of any society in history. A head of lettuce that wilts in the crisper drawer represents a trivial loss to the consumer. The supermarket that throws out a pallet of strawberries to maintain visual abundance is making a rational business decision. The restaurant serving 1,200-calorie entrées is responding to customer expectations. No amount of finger-wagging changes the underlying math.

What would change the math? First, tipping fees for landfilled organic waste, as several European countries and a handful of American jurisdictions (Vermont, Massachusetts, Seattle) have already adopted. When it costs real money to dump food scraps, businesses suddenly discover donation programs, anaerobic digesters, and composting partners. Second, full-cost pricing of agricultural water, particularly in California, where subsidized irrigation underwrites the cultivation of crops that are routinely plowed under when prices dip. Third, removing perverse subsidies in commodity programs that encourage overproduction of corn and soy beyond what the market actually demands.

Critics will say that raising the effective price of food hurts poor people. That concern is legitimate but answerable. The revenue from disposal fees and the savings from subsidy reform could fund a substantial expansion of SNAP, school meals, and WIC — programs that target food security directly rather than indirectly through cheapness. You can have a society that wastes less food and feeds the hungry better. What you can't have is a society where food is treated as nearly free and people behave as though it is precious.

The 40 percent waste figure is not a failure of American virtue. It is a predictable response to the price signals we've built. Change the signals, and the behavior follows.

Be Skeptical of the 40 Percent

The figure that "40 percent of food is wasted" has become a kind of activist mantra, repeated in press releases, TED talks, and now this debate prompt. It deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets. The number originates largely from a 2012 NRDC report extrapolating from USDA loss-adjusted availability data, and it includes everything from inedible peels and bones to moisture loss during cooking to crops that never leave the field because hailstorms destroyed them. Lumping all of that together produces a dramatic statistic, but it obscures which losses are actually tractable and which are not.

This matters because the policy responses depend on the diagnosis. If most "waste" is post-consumer plate scrapings and forgotten leftovers, then consumer education and refrigerator design are the answer. If it's retail shrinkage, then supply chain logistics and donation incentives matter most. If it's pre-harvest field losses, the issue is labor markets and commodity prices, not anyone's leftovers. These problems have almost nothing in common except that they all involve food not being eaten.

I'd also gently push back on the moral pairing the question implies — that we waste food while others go hungry, as if the two were linked. Hunger in America is overwhelmingly a problem of income, not of food supply. The United States produces vastly more calories than its population needs; reducing waste by half would not directly feed a single hungry child unless we also built distribution systems and addressed the poverty that prevents people from buying food in the first place. The link between landfilled lettuce in Los Angeles and an empty pantry in Appalachia is rhetorical, not logistical.

That said, there are sensible things to do. Expand tax deductions for food donations. Standardize date labels. Support food banks and gleaning. Charge for landfilling organics where it's cost-effective. Invest in cold chain improvements in developing countries, where post-harvest losses really are catastrophic and where the marginal calorie matters most.

What we should resist is treating food waste as a unified crisis demanding a unified crusade. It is a collection of loosely related inefficiencies, some worth fixing and some not. The hungry are best helped by addressing hunger directly. The environment is best helped by targeting the highest-impact waste streams. Conflating the two produces stirring rhetoric and muddled policy.

Food waste is often framed as a moral crisis, but at its core it is a staggering economic failure—a trillion-dollar global misallocation of resources. In the United States, we spend $218 billion a year growing, processing, transporting, and disposing of food that no one eats, and then we pay again in landfill tipping fees and methane emissions. The beautiful thing about this absurd inefficiency is that it presents a massive profit opportunity. The best way to keep food out of landfills is not through guilt or regulation, but by unleashing the same market creativity that made food so abundant in the first place.

The first frontier is dynamic pricing and real-time connectivity. Grocery stores and restaurants have long treated near-expiration inventory as a sunk cost to be written off. But new platforms are flipping that model. Apps that connect customers with deeply discounted surplus meals—like day-old pastries at a café or a bag of produce that won’t survive the weekend—turn unavoidable waste into revenue. A store that sells a brick of cheese at half-price three days before its sell-by date has already recaptured costs that would otherwise be lost entirely. Early adopters in Boston and New York are proving the model, and the scaling potential, with smarter inventory algorithms, is enormous.

Second, the value chain does not end when food is deemed unfit for the table. Before composting, there is a rich spectrum of higher uses that the market can exploit. Spent grains from breweries can be sold as high-protein animal feed. Ugly or surplus vegetables can be dehydrated and sold as powdered soup stock, a shelf-stable product with growing consumer appeal. And the residue that truly has no edible purpose is a fuel source. Anaerobic digesters can turn organic waste into biogas and nutrient-rich fertilizer, creating a revenue stream from what was once a disposal cost. Forward-thinking municipalities are already partnering with waste companies to build such facilities, financed by the energy they produce.

What these approaches share is a recognition that waste is simply a resource without a price tag. Critics will argue that relying on markets leaves behind those who cannot afford even discounted food, and that is a legitimate concern. But the food banks that already exist can be integrated into these platforms; for instance, a store could dynamically offer unsold inventory first at a deep discount for a few hours, and then seamlessly schedule a donation pickup if it remains unclaimed.

The industrial food system runs on razor-thin margins. When we show that eliminating waste can boost the bottom line, adoption will be swift and self-sustaining. The path to emptying landfills does not require a revolution in virtue; it requires an innovation ecosystem that makes every unsold apple and uneaten crust too valuable to throw away.

The root cause of America’s food waste epidemic is not a lack of technology or a mystery of logistics. It is a fundamental issue of economic incentives and cultural values. In the United States, food is historically cheap relative to average income, and throwing things away is essentially free. Until we change the economics of disposal, we will never change the culture of waste.

Currently, most American municipalities use a flat-fee structure for sanitation services. Whether a household throws away one bag of garbage a week or ten, their municipal tax burden or trash collection bill remains exactly the same. Consequently, there is zero financial penalty for buying too much food, letting it spoil, and dumping it in the trash. The landfill operates as a subsidized externality, hiding the true environmental and social costs of our disposable lifestyle.

To drastically reduce food waste, municipalities across the country must implement "pay-as-you-throw" (PAYT) systems, coupled with mandatory municipal composting. Under a PAYT model, households are charged for trash collection based on the weight or volume of the garbage they produce, much like we pay for electricity or water. When every pound of garbage directly hits the wallet, consumers are suddenly incentivized to buy only what they will eat, plan their meals more carefully, and find creative ways to use leftovers.

Simultaneously, cities must follow the lead of pioneers like San Francisco and Seattle, which have successfully implemented mandatory curbside composting. When food scraps are diverted into a green bin, they are turned into nutrient-rich soil for local agriculture, closing the ecological loop rather than generating potent methane gas in a landfill.

Critics might argue that PAYT systems are punitive, but they are actually a matter of basic fairness; those who waste less should pay less. We have lost our respect for food because the system insulates us from the consequences of wasting it. By attaching a tangible cost to the act of throwing food away, we can jolt the American public out of its complacency. Economic levers are the most effective tools we have to spark a much-needed cultural shift, transforming how we value the food on our plates and the resources it took to get it there.
