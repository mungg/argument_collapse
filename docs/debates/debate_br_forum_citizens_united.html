Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Lessig's diagnosis of "institutional corruption" is one of the most useful conceptual moves in recent constitutional argument, and his critique of Justice Kennedy's reasoning in *Citizens United* lands hard. But I want to push on two places where I think the lead essay either softens its argument or leaves a load-bearing premise underdeveloped: the analogy to *Lochner*, and the implicit faith that the alternatives Lessig proposes — Fair Elections, Ackerman-Ayres vouchers — would actually re-anchor the dependency he describes.

Start with *Lochner*. Lessig deploys it brilliantly as a rhetorical brand: he wants to label Kennedy's casual factual assertion (that "the appearance of influence or access… will not cause the electorate to lose faith in our democracy") as the same sort of unsupported empirical fiat that animated the bakers' case. The charge is fair as far as it goes. Kennedy does indeed assert as a constitutional matter what is in fact a contested empirical claim, and the Pew numbers Lessig cites point the other way. But the *Lochner* analogy cuts against Lessig in a way he doesn't fully reckon with. The standard post-1937 lesson — the one every sitting Justice endorses — is that courts should *defer* to legislative fact-finding. Apply that lesson straightforwardly here, and Congress's judgment that independent corporate expenditures corrupt the political process ought to win. Fine. But the reason First Amendment doctrine doesn't simply replicate the rational-basis test is precisely that we *don't* want legislatures (especially incumbent-protecting legislatures) writing the rules about who gets to criticize them and when. The whole point of heightened scrutiny in speech cases is to be suspicious of legislative motive. Lessig acknowledges this in a sentence, but the acknowledgment papers over a deeper problem: if courts must defer to Congress on what counts as "corruption," then Congress can expand "corruption" indefinitely — including, plausibly, to inconvenient speakers like Citizens United itself, a small ideological nonprofit, not Exxon. The 90-minute oral argument in which the Solicitor General's office conceded that BCRA's logic could reach books was not a hypothetical; it was the case. A constitutional regime in which legislatures get to define the corruption that justifies silencing speech needs more guardrails than "the appearance is bad."

The way out of this bind, I think, is exactly the doctrinal move Lessig gestures toward but doesn't fully build: distinguish *restrictions on speech* from *structures that reshape the economy of influence*. Kennedy's opinion would be far less troubling if his "narrow tailoring" gestures were taken seriously — that is, if the Court were saying not "Congress can do nothing" but "Congress must address dependency corruption through systems that empower more speakers rather than silencing some." That is what public financing, vouchers, and small-donor matching actually do. They are remedies for institutional corruption that don't require the Court to swallow the disturbing implication that the state may suppress political speech because legislators have decided, on their own behalf, that the speech makes them look bad. Lessig knows this — his closing turn to the Fair Elections Now Act and the Ackerman-Ayres vouchers admits as much — but he frames these as second-best options forced on us by Kennedy's *Lochnerism*. I would argue they are actually the *better* path even if Kennedy were entirely wrong, because they target the dependency itself rather than the symptom.

This brings me to my second worry: would these alternatives really sever the corrupting dependency? Lessig's mother-and-alcohol analogy is vivid, but it elides a fact that matters. Even if congressional campaigns were fully publicly financed tomorrow, the dependency Lessig describes would not vanish, because much of the "economy of influence" runs through channels that public financing leaves untouched: the revolving door into lobbying and consulting, post-career sinecures, the informational dependence on industry-supplied expertise, the cultural absorption of members into a Washington social class. Jack Abramoff's empire didn't run primarily on campaign checks; it ran on golf trips, jobs for staffers, and the architecture of access. Public financing addresses one stream of the river. It does not address the delta. To his credit, Lessig is laying the conceptual groundwork — institutional corruption is precisely meant to capture this broader economy — but the policy ask at the end of the essay is narrower than the diagnosis.

A related point: the iceberg theory Lessig borrows from Chamon and Kaplan is suggestive but is being asked to do a lot of work. The empirical literature on whether independent expenditures actually move legislator behavior, as opposed to correlating with pre-existing alignment, is genuinely contested. If the threat-of-expenditure mechanism is as powerful as Lessig suggests, we should already be seeing dramatic effects from the *Buckley*-era latitude individuals have enjoyed for thirty-five years. We may be — but the case has to be made, not assumed, especially since Lessig elsewhere insists (rightly) that the Court should not assume facts.

None of this should obscure the central achievement of the essay. Lessig has named something real. "Dependency corruption" describes what most Americans intuit when they look at sugar tariffs and used-car carve-outs, and Kennedy's refusal to take that intuition seriously is the genuine intellectual scandal of *Citizens United*. The question is whether the cure runs through the courts or around them. I suspect the latter — and I suspect Lessig, on reflection, does too.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of a democracy adrift is compelling, and his distinction between venal corruption and what he calls “dependency corruption” sharpens a real discontent. Many Americans do feel that Washington answers to funders, not voters. Yet even if we accept that a systemic ailment exists, the cure he urges—allowing Congress to restrict political speech to combat institutional corruption—poses dangers to the First Amendment that his essay too readily dismisses.

The core difficulty lies in the concept of “dependency corruption” itself. Lessig defines it as a dependency that conflicts with an institution’s intended design, here a dependency on contributors instead of on “the People alone.” But in a representative democracy, nearly every decision can be framed as cultivating a conflicting dependency. Interest groups, activist movements, media organizations, and yes, corporations, all seek to influence lawmakers. If the appearance of such influence, or a perceived “economy of influence,” can justify banning speech, the exception swallows the rule. The government could argue that any sustained effort to sway legislation—from union door-knocking to an environmental group’s ad blitz—generates a dependency that “weakens public trust” and thus warrants restriction. The First Amendment exists precisely because majorities in Congress cannot be trusted to distinguish benign persuasion from corrupting entanglements when the speech they dislike is the one they want to silence.

Lessig insists he is not arguing for the suppression of ordinary advocacy, only for the power to limit expenditures that create an improper dependency. But the line is illusory. Once we move beyond the clear, demonstrable quid pro quo—the bribe, the explicit exchange of cash for official act—we enter a world of speculation. Did a senator vote for milk subsidies because of campaign contributions from dairy cooperatives, or because preserving family farms fits her policy vision? Tracing the motive is impossible without intruding into the legislative mind. The Supreme Court’s refusal to expand the definition of corruption beyond bribery was not, as Lessig charges, “First Amendment Lochnerism.” It was a recognition that loosening that definition would invite the very sort of speech suppression the First Amendment was designed to prevent. The Austin Court’s “anti-distortion” rationale—that the state could block corporate speech to prevent “immense aggregations of wealth” from warping debate—was precisely such an invitation, and overturning it restored a principle essential to robust disagreement: the government has no business equalizing the volume of different speakers.

Lessig’s central factual claim—that the appearance of influence will cause the electorate to lose faith in democracy—may well be true. But it does not follow that the government can silence speech to prop up public confidence. Many things cause loss of faith: negative attack ads, broken campaign promises, polarized cable news. We would not tolerate a congressional ban on any of them in the name of institutional legitimacy. If polls show that citizens are cynical when they see large independent expenditures, the appropriate response is more speech to counter that cynicism, not an enforced muting of the spenders. Disclosure, which the Citizens United majority upheld, is the constitutionally sound mechanism: it arms voters with information while leaving the speaker free. If Lessig is right that iceberg threats are invisible, the remedy is to strengthen and modernize disclosure rules, not to ban icebergs altogether.

Lessig’s proposed alternatives—voluntary small-dollar matching and democracy vouchers—are more promising because they operate by adding voices rather than subtracting them. But they need not require rejecting Citizens United. They can coexist with corporate independent spending. The real gap is not between funders and voters, but between the scale of political speech and the public’s appetite for engaging with it. Strengthening the capacity of ordinary voters through vouchers may narrow that gap, but it does so by expanding the universe of speech, not by shrinking it.

None of this denies that Congress suffers from a trust deficit or that money tilts the terrain. But the framers’ solution to faction was not to choke off its expression. It was to multiply it, to let rival interests check one another under the same sky. Citizens United, for all its flaws, respects that architecture. A theory of dependency corruption that invites Congress to police which influences are “proper” would rebuild the edifice on a much weaker foundation.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “institutional corruption” offers a remarkably lucid vocabulary for the unease that most Americans feel when they look at Washington today. By separating the venal, *quid pro quo* bribery of a Rod Blagojevich from the systemic, legal dependencies that shape congressional behavior, Lessig accurately captures why a body composed largely of well-intentioned public servants routinely produces policies that the public actively dislikes. The dependency on funders has indeed eclipsed the intended dependency on voters.

Yet, while Lessig’s diagnosis of the disease is flawless, his critique of the Supreme Court’s refusal to permit speech restrictions as the cure is fundamentally flawed. Lessig laments that the Court in *Citizens United* rejected “dependency corruption” as a justification for banning independent corporate political expenditures. He argues that if Congress can ban bribery, it should be able to ban systemic dependency. But this leaps over a massive constitutional chasm, conflating the regulation of a transaction with the regulation of political speech.

If the Supreme Court were to adopt Lessig’s broad definition of institutional corruption as a compelling state interest sufficient to override the First Amendment, the cure would quickly prove worse than the disease. Allowing Congress to restrict political speech on the grounds that certain speakers create an "improper dependency" hands incumbents a tailor-made tool to silence their most well-funded critics. The First Amendment sets an intentionally high bar—*quid pro quo* corruption—because any broader definition, such as the “distorting effects” rationale from the now-overruled *Austin* decision, inevitably allows the government to decide whose voices are legitimate and whose are improperly loud.

Lessig attempts to frame Justice Kennedy’s majority opinion as an exercise in “First Amendment Lochnerism,” accusing the Court of substituting its own factual assumptions about public trust for those of the legislature. But this analogy misrepresents the architecture of constitutional law. *Lochner* involved the Court inventing unenumerated economic rights to strike down labor regulations under a basic rational-basis standard. *Citizens United*, by contrast, deals with the explicitly enumerated right to free speech, which necessarily triggers strict scrutiny. When a legislature bans political speech—even speech funded by a corporate treasury—the burden is entirely on the government to prove that the ban is narrowly tailored to a compelling interest.

Kennedy did not need to prove that the public would retain faith in democracy; rather, the government failed to prove that banning independent political documentaries was the only constitutional way to save that faith. Strict scrutiny places a heavy thumb on the scale of free expression. If we abandon that standard to defer to congressional fact-finding on what constitutes “corruption,” we invite majorities to perpetually rewrite the rules of political discourse to their own advantage.

Lessig is certainly correct about the "iceberg theory"—the credible threat of a massive independent expenditure by a corporation or union will absolutely influence candidate behavior. The implicit threat of a million-dollar ad campaign against a climate-change bill is a powerful deterrent. But in a free society, threatening to vigorously and publicly criticize a legislator’s record is not a loophole; it is the essence of democratic accountability, even if we find the wealth behind the megaphone distasteful. We cannot make it illegal to threaten to criticize a politician.

Where Lessig and I find absolute agreement is in his concluding prescription. Because the First Amendment rightly prevents us from artificially lowering the ceiling of political speech, our only constitutional recourse is to raise the floor. The structural alternative to censorship is subsidization.

If we wish to break the dependency on wealthy, out-of-district funders, we must create a counter-economy of influence that makes candidates dependent on the voters they actually represent. Lessig points to the Fair Elections Now Act and the Ackerman-Ayres democracy voucher system. These are not mere fallback positions; they are the optimal solutions. A system that matches small-dollar donations or grants voters democracy vouchers changes the incentive structure of campaigning without requiring a single censorious ban on independent expenditures. It forces politicians to spend their time courting thousands of constituents rather than a handful of corporate executives.

As we navigate this first election cycle after *Citizens United*, the surge of outside spending is undeniably alarming. But the path out of this crisis does not run through a reversal of First Amendment protections or a nostalgic return to the patchwork regulations of McCain-Feingold. It requires us to stop trying to silence the few and start financing the voices of the many. Lessig’s "dependency corruption" is the defining political crisis of our time, but we must cure it with more democracy, not less speech.

Lawrence Lessig is right to insist that the central problem in American campaign finance is not bags of cash changing hands. The more serious problem is the daily, lawful dependence of elected officials on a tiny class of funders whose priorities are not identical with those of the public. Any account of corruption that begins and ends with bribery will miss most of what citizens actually experience as corrupt: the phone calls returned, the meetings granted, the amendments quietly inserted, the issues never brought to a vote. A Congress may be composed mostly of honorable people and still operate within a dishonorable structure.

But I am less persuaded that Citizens United is the best place to locate the constitutional cure for that disease. The decision is troubling, and Justice Kennedy’s confidence that independent expenditures cannot corrupt is too breezy. Still, the First Amendment problem is not an afterthought. When government prohibits a corporation, union, or nonprofit association from spending money to distribute a film about a presidential candidate, it is regulating political advocacy at the core of democratic debate. That should make even campaign-finance reformers uneasy.

The awkward fact in Citizens United is that the plaintiff was not General Motors or ExxonMobil but a nonprofit ideological corporation that wanted to distribute a documentary attacking Hillary Clinton. The statute did not merely regulate contributions to candidates; it prohibited certain election-season communications funded from a corporate treasury. To say that Citizens United could have spoken through a PAC is not a complete answer. PACs are burdensome, regulated vehicles, and the right to speak often includes the right not to speak through a government-designed substitute. If a conservative nonprofit can be silenced near an election, so can the Sierra Club, the ACLU, the NRA, or a small incorporated publisher of political documentaries.

Lessig’s answer is to distinguish suppression of speech from regulation aimed at “dependency corruption.” That distinction is morally powerful, but constitutionally slippery. If Congress may restrict independent political spending because it produces public distrust or creates a dependency on funders, then the question becomes: which speakers may Congress silence, and when? Corporations? Unions? Wealthy individuals? Media corporations? Advocacy nonprofits funded by wealthy donors? A great many speakers participate in “economies of influence.” The danger of a broad anti-corruption rationale is that it can become a license for incumbents to decide which concentrations of political power are too “distorting” to be heard.

That is why the Court’s narrowness, though unsatisfying, has a democratic logic. Quid pro quo corruption is not the only morally relevant corruption, but it is a relatively administrable constitutional boundary. Bribery and coordinated contributions can be regulated because they are tied directly to candidates and officeholders. Independent advocacy is different, not because it is harmless, but because prohibiting it requires the state to ration political persuasion. Once we move from “this payment bought that official act” to “this spending creates an unhealthy political dependency,” courts must judge the legitimacy of political influence itself. That judgment is hard to make without favoring some speakers over others.

Lessig’s Lochner analogy therefore cuts both ways. He is right that the Court should not simply announce, without evidence, that access and influence will not damage public faith in democracy. The assertion is implausible. Americans plainly believe that money matters too much in politics, and Congress’s miserable approval ratings cannot be separated from the perception that organized money receives privileged treatment. But Lochner also warns against judges constitutionalizing their own theory of proper political economy. If the Court should not impose laissez-faire economics on legislatures, neither should it too readily bless legislative attempts to equalize political influence by suppressing disfavored speakers.

The deeper problem is that both sides are tempted by an impossible aspiration: a politics cleansed of unequal influence. Representative government has always involved organized interests, wealthy patrons, partisan newspapers, labor unions, churches, trade associations, and ideological movements. Madison did not think factions could be eliminated; he thought their effects could be controlled through institutional design. The question is not whether some citizens will have more influence than others. They will. The question is whether our institutions can prevent that inequality from hardening into dependence.

On that point Lessig’s proposed remedies are far more promising than his constitutional critique. Small-donor public financing, matching funds, and democracy vouchers attack dependency without banning speech. They change the incentives of candidates rather than silence independent speakers. If members of Congress could raise competitive sums by appealing to large numbers of ordinary citizens, they would spend less time cultivating lobbyists, PACs, and wealthy contributors. The New York City matching-funds model, whatever its imperfections, suggests that public matching can diversify the donor pool and make small contributions politically meaningful. A national system along those lines would do more to restore trust than another round of prohibitions vulnerable to judicial invalidation.

Disclosure also deserves more defense than Lessig gives it. He is right that disclosure cannot reveal every threat, wink, or implied promise. The “iceberg” problem is real: a corporation or union need not spend millions if candidates know it credibly could. But secrecy makes the problem far worse. Voters, journalists, shareholders, and competitors should be able to know who is financing political messages. Disclosure will not eliminate dependency corruption, but it can discipline it. The Court in Citizens United upheld disclosure requirements by a lopsided vote, and reformers should exploit that opening aggressively.

There are also corporate-governance reforms that do not require banning speech. If corporations are to spend treasury funds in elections, shareholders should have meaningful notice and perhaps approval rights. Political spending by managers may not reflect the views of investors, employees, or consumers. Requiring internal accountability would respect the First Amendment interest in political association while reducing the agency problem created when corporate officers deploy other people’s money for political ends.

Finally, Congress can tighten rules on coordination. The formal line between independent and coordinated spending is too easy to manipulate. Lessig is convincing when he describes a world of nods and winks. If “independent” expenditures are functionally planned with campaigns, they should be treated as contributions and limited accordingly. That is not censorship; it is enforcement of the very distinction on which Citizens United relies.

Lessig’s most important contribution is to shift attention from villainy to architecture. We do not need to believe that members of Congress are crooks to see that their dependence on funders deforms representation. Yet the remedy should be architectural as well. Build alternative funding channels. Require sunlight. Police coordination. Empower shareholders and members of associations. Reduce the amount of time candidates spend dialing for dollars.

Citizens United may make reform harder, and the Court’s complacency about corruption is unwarranted. But a democracy worth defending must be cautious about empowering Congress to prohibit political advocacy, even advocacy financed by corporations. The better path is not to make some speakers quieter. It is to make representatives less dependent on the few who can afford to speak the loudest.

Lawrence Lessig's framework of "institutional corruption" offers a compelling lens through which to understand the democratic failures that Citizens United has exacerbated. His central insight—that the dependency of legislators on campaign funders rather than voters constitutes a form of corruption distinct from and arguably more damaging than individual bribery—is one that deserves serious attention from anyone concerned about democratic legitimacy. Yet while I find much to agree with in his analysis, I think his proposed remedies, however appealing in theory, face significant practical obstacles that his essay underestimates, and his critique of the Court, while often justified, occasionally overreaches.

Lessig is surely right that the framers intended Congress to be dependent upon the People alone, and that our current system of private campaign finance has created a second, conflicting dependency. The statistics he cites about out-of-state donations to candidates are striking and suggest a fundamental disconnect between those who fund campaigns and those who elect representatives. When 67 percent of a Massachusetts senator's contributions come from outside the state, and 73 percent of his opponent's funding follows the same pattern, the claim that representatives are responsive to their constituents becomes difficult to sustain. This is institutional corruption in the truest sense: not the bad acts of evil individuals, but the systematic distortion of democratic accountability.

Where I find myself more skeptical is in Lessig's proposed solutions. The democracy voucher system he describes with Ackerman and Ayres is elegant in its logic—if every citizen can allocate a small amount to candidates, and candidates can receive money only from citizens in their districts, then the dependency on voters and the dependency on contributors might indeed converge. But this proposal faces a daunting political hurdle that Lessig does not adequately address: its enactment would require dismantling the very system that benefits those with the power to prevent it. Incumbent legislators, who benefit disproportionately from the current regime of private fundraising, would have to vote themselves out of their primary source of competitive advantage. The history of campaign finance reform suggests that we should be humble about the prospects for such transformational change.

The Fair Elections Now Act, with its matching small-dollar contributions, faces somewhat less daunting obstacles, but still confronts the basic problem of getting incumbents to embrace a system that might disadvantage them. Lessig notes that sponsors estimate it could raise more money for campaigns than the current system, but this estimate requires faith that small-dollar donors would materialize in sufficient numbers to replace bundled large contributions. The record of public financing regimes at the state level is mixed at best—some have shown genuine promise, but others have struggled to attract participants.

I also want to push back somewhat on Lessig's treatment of the Court's decision in Citizens United. His critique of Justice Kennedy's majority opinion is often incisive, particularly his observation that Kennedy's dismissal of the appearance-of-corruption concern lacks any empirical grounding. The Lochnerism charge is apt in a narrow sense: just as the Lochner Court substituted its own judgment about economic regulation for that of legislatures, the Citizens United Court substituted its intuitions about the effects of independent expenditures for any serious engagement with evidence. Lessig is right that courts should not be entitled to a "free pass" when invalidating legislation.

But Lessig goes too far when he suggests that the solution is simply to recognize "dependency corruption" as a constitutionally cognizable category that justifies speech restrictions. The difficulty is that any such recognition requires courts to draw lines that may be no less contestable than the lines they refused to draw in Citizens United. What counts as an "improper dependency"? If large independent expenditures create such dependencies, does that mean the Court was wrong to protect them, or merely that Congress could regulate them? The distinction matters because Lessig's own analysis acknowledges that independent expenditures are "not benign in every case"—they may matter when campaigns are aware of them and when tacit coordination is possible. But acknowledging that they sometimes matter does not tell us where to draw the line, and courts would have to draw it.

This is not a small difficulty. The history of campaign finance regulation is littered with well-intentioned laws that courts struck down, sometimes correctly and sometimes not, but almost always in ways that reflected deep disagreement about the proper relationship between money and speech. Lessig wants us to believe that if we just adopt his framework of institutional corruption, we can solve these problems. I am not so sure. The framework is valuable as a diagnostic tool—it helps us see what has gone wrong—but it does not itself provide a therapeutic prescription.

What does seem clear to me, and what Lessig's essay establishes beyond reasonable dispute, is that the current system is unsustainable. Public trust in Congress at historic lows, combined with the Citizens United decision removing limits on corporate independent expenditures, has created conditions that may produce the "great cynicism" about democratic governance that Lessig rightly warns against. Whether the answer is democracy vouchers, small-dollar matching, or something else entirely, some structural reform is necessary if we are to restore even a plausible fiction of representative government. Lessig has given us better language to describe the problem. Converting that description into durable reform will require political will that has so far been conspicuously absent.

**The First Amendment Is Not a Loophole**

Lawrence Lessig has done the country a service by giving a name—"institutional corruption"—to something many of us feel about Congress in our bones. But naming a worry is not the same as identifying a constitutional violation, and the burden of his essay is to convince us that the Court in *Citizens United* missed something so obvious that Justice Kennedy's reasoning amounts to a kind of "First Amendment Lochnerism." I think the analogy is upside down. The danger of Lochnerism was that the Court, untethered from any clear constitutional command, struck down a duly enacted statute by inventing a freedom (liberty of contract) that the text did not contain. The First Amendment, by contrast, is text. It is a clear constitutional command. When the Court enforces that command against a federal statute that explicitly criminalizes the screening of a political film during election season, it is not engaged in Lochnerism. It is doing its job.

Lessig's complaint, stripped of its rhetorical scaffolding, comes down to this: he believes Congress should have broader latitude to regulate political expenditures because expenditures create "dependencies" that distract legislators from their constituents. The problem is that "dependency corruption," as he defines it, is so capacious that almost any influence on a legislator could qualify. Constituent letters create a dependency. Interest-group endorsements create a dependency. Editorial board endorsements create a dependency. Polling data creates a dependency. So do friendships, party loyalties, ideological tribes, and the personal ambitions of staffers who hope someday to run for office themselves. If Congress can regulate speech in the name of preventing the legislator from feeling pulled by anything other than "the people alone," it can regulate practically everything. The Court was right to insist that the only kind of corruption that justifies suppressing political speech is the narrow kind—quid pro quo—because any broader definition collapses into the legislature's unbounded authority to police the influences on itself. And legislative self-policing of speech is precisely what the First Amendment exists to prevent.

Consider Lessig's hypothetical of Exxon "letting it be known" that it will spend up to a million dollars in any district to defeat candidates who accept climate science. This is presented as a frightening illustration of post–*Citizens United* corruption. But what is it, really? It is Exxon announcing in advance what kind of political speech it intends to engage in, on what subject, in what jurisdictions. The right of a corporation, an advocacy group, a union, or a wealthy individual to announce in advance the contours of its political advocacy is the very heart of what the First Amendment protects. The fact that politicians may modify their behavior in anticipation of being criticized in advertising is not corruption; it is democracy functioning more or less as it is supposed to. Voters need information about candidates, and the campaign-finance regime that Lessig wants would suppress vast amounts of that information at exactly the moment voters most want it—the sixty days before an election.

Lessig is rhetorically careful, but the implications of his theory are not careful at all. He writes that he does not want to "extirpate human nature," only to design institutions that avoid "conflicting dependencies." Yet the only institutional design he proposes that genuinely fits his theory—a system of "democracy vouchers" or Fair Elections Now matching funds—works by displacing private speech with publicly subsidized speech. That may be a fine policy idea. I have nothing against it, taken on its own terms. What I object to is the use of "institutional corruption" as a rationale for the additional step of forbidding private citizens, associations, and yes, corporations, from spending their own money to talk about candidates. The voucher system can be enacted tomorrow without overturning *Citizens United*. Lessig's beef with the Court is not really that it foreclosed reform; it is that it foreclosed *prohibition*.

The "iceberg theory" of Chamon and Kaplan, on which Lessig leans heavily, deserves more skepticism than he gives it. The theory says that the threat of independent expenditures may matter more than realized expenditures. Maybe. But the same is true of any potential political weapon—the threat of a primary challenge, the threat of an unflattering newspaper exposé, the threat of an interest group's negative endorsement, the threat of a celebrity's televised ridicule. We do not regulate any of these things, because we recognize that the chilling effect of pre-emptive regulation would be far worse than the supposed harm. The First Amendment does not say "Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech, except when the speech is too effective."

Nor is Lessig fair to Justice Kennedy on the question of empirical proof. Kennedy was not asserting as a brute fact that the appearance of influence will never affect public faith. He was making a constitutional claim about which side bears the burden of justification. When a statute criminalizes core political speech, the government must show a compelling interest, narrowly tailored. It is *not* the job of the Court to gather sociological evidence about whether the public's mood will sour because a corporation ran an attack ad. The presumption runs the other way: speech is permitted unless the government can prove a specific, cognizable harm. To complain that Kennedy did not marshal social-science data is to misunderstand which party bears the burden in First Amendment doctrine.

There is a final point worth saying plainly. Lessig invokes the Great Recession and global warming as evidence of the catastrophic costs of "institutional corruption" and contrasts these with the petty wrongdoing of Blagojevich and Cunningham. The rhetorical move is powerful, but the causation is asserted rather than shown. Did campaign contributions cause the financial crisis, or did a bipartisan consensus around deregulation, low interest rates, and homeownership policy do that work? Did campaign contributions block climate legislation, or did genuine public ambivalence about energy prices and economic dislocation? When reformers blame money for outcomes they dislike, they often elide the inconvenient fact that the policies in question were *popular*, or at least not unpopular enough to overcome legislative inertia. Treating every policy disappointment as proof of corruption is itself a form of bad faith with democracy.

I share Lessig's worry that Americans have lost faith in Congress. I do not share his confidence that the cure is to give Congress more power to regulate the political speech of those who criticize it. That cure would, I fear, be worse than the disease.

**Lessig Is Right About the Disease, Too Cautious About the Cure**

Lawrence Lessig's essay is the most lucid statement available of why *Citizens United* is a constitutional disaster, and his concept of "dependency corruption" deserves to enter the standard vocabulary of campaign-finance debate. But for all its analytic precision, the essay pulls its punches at exactly the moment when bolder thinking is needed. Lessig writes as if the central problem is that Justice Kennedy misread the First Amendment, and as if the appropriate response is to push Congress toward voucher systems and Fair Elections matching funds. These are good ideas. They are nowhere near sufficient.

Start with what Lessig gets exactly right. The framers' explicit design for the House was a "dependence on the people"—a phrase Lessig draws from Federalist 57 and that he could have multiplied tenfold from the founding-era record. Dependence is not corruption; *misdirected* dependence is. When 67 percent of John Kerry's senatorial money came from outside Massachusetts, his electoral dependence on Massachusetts voters was already attenuated by his fundraising dependence on a national donor class. Multiply that by 535 members and you have a Congress whose center of gravity has drifted away from the geography it represents. Lessig's "economy of influence" frame captures this drift better than the older language of "bribery" ever could.

But here is where Lessig holds back. He treats the problem as a defect in legal doctrine that could be corrected by a future Supreme Court willing to recognize a broader definition of corruption. That is a comforting thought for a law professor. It also misreads the political economy of the situation. The Roberts Court is not going to overturn *Citizens United* in the foreseeable future, and the political coalition that produced the five-justice majority is not going anywhere. Worse, the donor class that benefits from *Citizens United*—and that benefited from the pre-*Citizens United* regime almost as much—has every incentive to make sure that future judicial appointments preserve the new dispensation. Lessig's hope that Kennedy's opinion "is not likely to survive very long" is, I'm afraid, wishful.

If the doctrinal route is blocked, what remains? The voucher and matching-fund proposals Lessig endorses are a beginning, but they share a quiet flaw: they assume that flooding the system with small-donor money will swamp the influence of big money. The math does not really work. The Ackerman-Ayres voucher scheme would, at $50 per voter, generate roughly $7 billion per cycle—a lot. But corporate political spending plus high-net-worth individual spending is already in that range, and after *Citizens United* it will rise. Independent expenditures by corporations and Super-PAC-like vehicles will not be displaced by public financing; they will operate alongside it, in many cases dominating the airwaves in the weeks before elections, when public-financed candidates have already spent their war chests.

The real lesson of *Citizens United*, then, is that we cannot regulate our way out of dependency corruption through campaign finance alone. We need structural reform of how Congress itself operates. Some options Lessig does not seriously discuss:

First, ban congressional fundraising during legislative sessions, as several states do for their legislators. If a Senate Banking Committee member cannot accept money from financial-services PACs while financial-reform legislation is on the calendar, the most blatant form of dependency is at least disrupted in time. This rule does not implicate *Citizens United* at all, because it regulates the conduct of legislators, not the speech of donors.

Second, lengthen House terms to four years, with half elected every two. The two-year House cycle, which the framers thought would tether representatives to the people, now tethers them to a permanent fundraising treadmill. The average House member spends, by various estimates, between a quarter and a half of working hours dialing for dollars. A four-year cycle would, ironically, restore something closer to the framers' vision by reducing the *frequency* of dependence on funders.

Third, dramatically tighten the revolving door. The lobbying industry now functions as a deferred-compensation system for former members and senior staff. The promise of a million-dollar K Street job is itself a form of dependency corruption that operates entirely outside the campaign-finance system Lessig analyzes. A long, strictly enforced cooling-off period would do more to break the dependency than another round of contribution limits.

Fourth, fund congressional staff and research capacity at levels comparable to the executive branch. One reason Congress is so dependent on lobbyists is that lobbyists provide the analytical work that members' offices cannot afford to do themselves. Starve the legislative branch of expertise and you guarantee that expertise will be supplied by interested parties. This too is a dependency, and one entirely curable by appropriation.

Lessig's framework, in other words, points toward a much more ambitious reconstruction of legislative dependence than the campaign-finance fixes he ultimately recommends. By treating *Citizens United* as the central trauma, he risks limiting his audience's imagination to the narrow channel of contribution limits and public financing. The Court's decision is bad, but the dependency corruption it exacerbates was already pervasive under the pre-2010 regime. McCain-Feingold did not save us from it. BCRA was, in many respects, a system for routing the same money through different channels. We need to think bigger.

One last point on rhetoric. Lessig is, as always, careful to insist that members of Congress are "decent souls" and that the corruption he describes is institutional rather than personal. This is generous and probably accurate. It is also strategically self-defeating. The reform coalition that produced the Pendleton Act, the direct election of senators, and the campaign-finance reforms of the 1970s did not succeed by reassuring the public that politicians were nice people captured by structures. It succeeded by making people angry. Lessig's institutional framing risks letting individual members off the hook for choices they make every day—choices to hold high-dollar fundraisers with industries they regulate, choices to spend their evenings at K Street townhouses rather than constituent meetings. These are not impersonal forces. They are decisions. A reform movement worth having will name them as such.

*Citizens United* is the title, but it is not the whole story. Lessig has given us the right diagnosis. We need a more aggressive treatment plan than he is willing to prescribe.

**The Framers Did Not Authorize the Speech Police**

Lawrence Lessig's invocation of the framers is rhetorically masterful and, on examination, mostly anachronistic. He builds his case for a broad regulatory power over political speech on the foundation of a single phrase—"dependent upon the People alone"—as if Madison and Hamilton would have nodded approvingly at the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act. They would not have. They would have been astonished and probably appalled.

Begin with what the framers actually did about money in politics: nothing. There is no provision in the Constitution regulating campaign expenditures, contributions, or political advertising, because in the eighteenth-century imagination these activities were precisely the activities that the First Amendment, and the broader culture of republican liberty, were meant to protect. Federalist 57, which Lessig quotes for the proposition that members of the House should feel "an habitual recollection of their dependence on the people," is concerned with the *structural* features of representation—frequent elections, broad suffrage, the absence of property qualifications—not with regulating the content or financing of political communication. To leap from "dependence on the people" to a congressional power to suppress corporate political films during election season is to leap across two centuries of constitutional development.

Indeed, the dependencies the framers actually feared, which Lessig himself catalogs with some charm, were dependencies on *foreign* powers and on the executive—on monarchs who might buy off legislators through patronage, on hereditary aristocracies that might capture republican institutions through wealth and pedigree. The Constitution's emoluments clauses, its prohibitions on titles of nobility, and its separation of legislative and executive power all aim at these specific dangers. None of these provisions is plausibly extended to cover voluntary contributions from American citizens to American campaigns. Lessig wishes "the framers had said 'princes, foreign or domestic.'" Well, they didn't. And the reason they didn't is that they understood the difference between a political system corrupted by entrenched aristocratic power and a political system in which free citizens organize, persuade, and yes, fund advocacy on the issues they care about.

Lessig's framework runs into a deeper problem when it tries to specify which "dependencies" count as corrupting. He concedes that a representative's dependence on voters is appropriate—it is the very point of representative democracy. He claims that a representative's dependence on contributors is corrupting, because contributors are not the same set as voters. But what about the dependencies in between? A senator from Idaho is more "dependent" on potato farmers than on tech workers because potato farmers vote in larger numbers in Idaho. Is that corruption? A representative from a swing district is more "dependent" on the median voter than on her party's base. Corruption? A national party leader is "dependent" on donors in New York and California to fund races in marginal districts elsewhere. Corruption? Once you start labeling structural features of pluralist politics as "dependency corruption," you have given the regulator a license to declare any disfavored pattern of influence illegitimate. That is not a constitutional theory. It is an invitation to incumbent self-dealing.

Notice, too, the specific outcome Lessig wants regulators to be able to achieve. He wants Congress to be able to forbid Citizens United, a nonprofit corporation organized by ordinary citizens, from showing a film critical of Hillary Clinton in the run-up to a primary. Read that sentence twice. The most basic activity of self-government—organizing with fellow citizens to criticize a candidate for high office—was, under the McCain-Feingold regime that Lessig defends, a federal crime carrying potential prison time. Lessig is not wrong that political speech can have lopsided effects depending on who has more money to spend. But the response that the First Amendment authorizes to that fact is more speech, not government licensing of political films. Justice Kennedy's opinion got the basic structure right. The default is liberty. The exception is narrow.

The Lochner analogy that Lessig deploys deserves a closer look, because it cuts in the opposite direction from the one he intends. *Lochner* was wrong because it invented an unenumerated right—liberty of contract—and used that right to strike down ordinary economic regulation. The First Amendment is the precise opposite of an unenumerated right. It is the most explicit, textually rooted, historically grounded protection in the Bill of Rights. When the Court strikes down a federal speech restriction, it is enforcing the text of the Constitution, not inventing a doctrine to override it. Lessig's complaint that Kennedy made "factual speculations" rather than gathering social-science data inverts the proper allocation of burden. The First Amendment does not say "Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech, unless social scientists report that the public would tolerate the abridgment." It says no law. The presumption runs against the regulator. That is not Lochnerism; that is constitutionalism.

There is also something quietly elitist about the entire "institutional corruption" framework, despite Lessig's populist gloss. The framework assumes that ordinary voters cannot evaluate political advertising, cannot discount it for source bias, cannot decide for themselves whether a candidate who took money from Wall Street is therefore unworthy of their vote. The voucher schemes Lessig endorses essentially say: the political conversation will be sanitized in advance by a public-financing apparatus, so that voters do not have to do the difficult work of discriminating among speakers. But this is a strange model of democratic citizenship. The framers' "dependence on the people" presupposed that the people would be active judges of political claims, exposed to all manner of partisan propaganda and capable of sifting through it. The remedy for bad political speech is good political speech, not federal prohibitions enforced by the FEC.

None of this is to deny that money in politics is a real concern, or that disclosure requirements and anti-bribery laws are constitutional and wise. It is to deny that the framers' design authorizes the sweeping regulatory power Lessig wants the Court to recognize. *Citizens United* did not destroy American democracy. It restored a piece of the First Amendment that BCRA had unwisely curtailed. If Congress is corrupted, the remedy lies with Congress and the voters—not with a Supreme Court newly empowered to police the boundaries of permissible political expression.

**The Empirical Case Against Lessig**

Lawrence Lessig's essay is built on a chain of empirical claims that he treats as obvious and that political scientists have spent the last forty years systematically failing to confirm. This is not a small problem for his argument. If money does not in fact buy congressional votes to anything like the degree the public believes, then "dependency corruption" is less a description of how Congress works than of how voters imagine Congress works—a distinction that matters a great deal for what kind of reform is appropriate.

Consider the literature. Stephen Ansolabehere, John de Figueiredo, and James Snyder published a now-famous paper in 2003 ("Why Is There So Little Money in U.S. Politics?") asking precisely the question that haunts the campaign-finance debate: if money is so powerful, why is there so little of it? Total political spending in the United States is, on a per-capita basis, modest compared to commercial advertising for breakfast cereal. If contributions reliably moved policy in the directions Lessig describes, rational corporate actors would invest vastly more than they do. The fact that they don't suggests that contributions are doing something other than buying votes—most likely, buying access and influencing the marginal allocation of legislator attention rather than reversing legislator preferences.

The most careful studies of roll-call voting find that contributions explain very little variance in legislators' votes once you control for party, ideology, and constituency. Members of Congress vote, by and large, the way their districts want them to vote, in line with their own ideological commitments. The exceptions are real but narrow: low-salience issues with concentrated interests on one side and diffuse interests on the other—the sugar tariffs and milk subsidies Lessig rightly invokes. But on the big-ticket items he gestures toward—the Great Recession, global warming—the story is much more about ideology, partisanship, and genuine public ambivalence than about purchased votes.

Take climate legislation. The Waxman-Markey cap-and-trade bill failed in the Senate in 2010 not primarily because the fossil-fuel industry bought senators, but because senators from coal-producing states, manufacturing states, and energy-export states represented constituents who feared higher energy costs. Manchin, Rockefeller, Landrieu, and others would have voted as they did with or without industry contributions. Calling that "dependency corruption" papers over a real disagreement among Americans about whether and how to price carbon. Lessig wants to attribute policy outcomes he dislikes to corruption, because corruption explanations have a satisfying moral structure. But a more honest accounting recognizes that the median American voter is significantly less progressive than the readership of *Boston Review*, and that legislative outcomes often reflect this.

The same point applies to the financial crisis. Did campaign contributions from Wall Street prevent better regulation of the financial sector? Or did a broad bipartisan ideological consensus, embraced by Democratic and Republican administrations alike, in favor of homeownership, deregulation, and financial innovation produce the policy environment that led to the crisis? Larry Summers and Robert Rubin did not push deregulation because they were dependent on Wall Street contributions. They pushed deregulation because they genuinely believed it would produce growth. They were wrong, but they were not bought. Attributing every policy failure to money obscures the harder fact that smart, well-intentioned people, listening to their constituents and their best understanding of the evidence, often make bad decisions.

This is where Lessig's analogy of the alcoholic mother becomes telling. He uses it to suggest that good intentions are not enough when a conflicting dependency intrudes. Fair enough. But the analogy also has another lesson: the diagnosis of alcoholism requires evidence of the actual behavioral effects of the substance. We do not diagnose alcoholism from the mere presence of alcohol in the household. We diagnose it from observable patterns of impaired judgment. By analogy, the diagnosis of dependency corruption requires evidence of impaired legislative judgment—evidence, that is, that legislators are voting against their constituents' interests because of money. The political-science literature does not provide much of that evidence on the issues that matter most.

What the literature *does* support is Lessig's point about public *perception*. Americans do believe that money buys results in Congress. Trust in Congress is at historic lows. These are facts. But the appropriate response to a public-perception problem is not necessarily the same as the response to a behavioral-corruption problem. If the issue is that voters wrongly believe money is corrupting Congress to a greater degree than it actually is, then reforms focused on transparency, civic education, and procedural visibility might do more good than expenditure caps. Conversely, if voters' perceptions are accurate and the political-science literature is missing something, then we need to identify the mechanism by which money operates before we can design effective remedies. Lessig's framework, by treating perception and behavior as basically interchangeable, obscures this critical distinction.

The "iceberg theory" Lessig invokes deserves a similar skepticism. The Chamon-Kaplan idea is intriguing—that the threat of independent expenditures matters more than realized expenditures—but as of 2010 it is theoretical rather than empirically established. We do not have systematic evidence that legislators actually respond to such threats in the way the theory predicts. There are good reasons to doubt that they do: most threats are not credible, most legislators have strong ideological priors that are not for sale at any price, and most independent expenditures, when they occur, are remarkably ineffective at moving voters who have already formed opinions about incumbents.

None of this is to deny that the campaign-finance system has problems. Fundraising consumes far too much of legislators' time. The donor class is not demographically representative. Lobbyists have privileged access. These are real concerns and worth addressing. But the case for the most expansive Lessig-style reforms—voucher systems, expenditure prohibitions, sweeping public financing—rests on an empirical claim about the magnitude of corruption that the evidence does not robustly support.

A modest reform agenda grounded in what we actually know: better disclosure, tighter revolving-door rules, restrictions on fundraising during legislative sessions, modest public financing. A reform agenda grounded in the conviction that money has hollowed out American democracy: ambitious, gratifying, and probably overshooting the actual problem.

**Lessig's Reformism Is Too Small for the Crisis He Describes**

Lawrence Lessig is one of the most thoughtful liberal critics of the American campaign-finance system, and his concept of dependency corruption is a genuine contribution. But the essay he has written for *Boston Review* exemplifies a deeper limitation of the legal-academic reform tradition: it cannot bring itself to name the structural forces that actually produce the corruption it identifies. The result is a diagnosis far more radical than the cure Lessig proposes, and a politics that drifts steadily toward technocratic Band-Aids while the underlying disorder metastasizes.

What is the real problem? It is not, in the first instance, that the Supreme Court got *Citizens United* wrong. It is that an enormously powerful concentration of corporate wealth has, over the last four decades, captured the political institutions of the United States—legislative, executive, judicial, and regulatory—and oriented them around the interests of capital rather than labor, the planet, or the public. Campaign finance is one channel of this capture. It is far from the only one. Lobbying, regulatory revolving doors, the think-tank ecosystem, corporate media ownership, the intellectual hegemony of neoclassical economics in universities and policymaking, the disciplining force of capital flight on small open economies—all of these operate together as a system. *Citizens United* did not create this system. It removed one of the few legal restraints on its most visible manifestation.

Lessig's framework cannot quite see this because it insists on treating corruption as an institutional drift from "intended" purposes, as if there were a pristine original design from which Congress has unfortunately deviated. But the framers' Congress was itself a tool of merchant capital, slaveholders, and propertied elites. The "dependence on the people" Lessig invokes was a dependence on a sharply restricted electorate of white male property owners. The history of American democracy is not a history of decline from founding purity; it is a history of struggle by excluded groups—workers, women, Black Americans, immigrants—to wrest substantive representation from a system designed to deny it to them. *Citizens United* is one episode in the long counterrevolution against that struggle, not a unique departure from a previously sound design.

This matters because it determines what kind of reform might actually work. Lessig's preferred remedies—Ackerman-Ayres democracy vouchers, Fair Elections Now matching funds—are clever, well-designed, and almost certainly inadequate. The voucher system would inject several billion dollars of small-donor money into campaigns. Independent expenditures by corporations and wealthy individuals would continue to dwarf this in the high-stakes races where money matters most. Even if voucher money funded primary challenges and grassroots candidates, those candidates would still face hostile coverage in corporate-owned media, opposition from a well-funded think-tank ecosystem, and the structural pressures of governing in an economy that punishes deviation from elite policy preferences. The voucher system addresses the symptom (uneven distribution of contributions) without touching the underlying inequality of resources that gives some Americans far more political power than others.

A more honest reform agenda would have to grapple with the fact that political equality is impossible without substantial economic equality. This was understood by an earlier generation of American progressives—Brandeis, La Follette, even some Republicans of the trust-busting era—who recognized that concentrated wealth and democratic self-government cannot coexist. Their response was structural: antitrust, the income tax, labor rights, securities regulation, public utilities. These were not, primarily, campaign-finance reforms. They were attempts to break the economic power that translates into political power. The campaign-finance reform tradition, by contrast, has too often functioned as a substitute for structural economic reform, allowing reformers to talk about corruption without talking about capitalism.

Lessig's own framing illustrates the limitation. He writes that the institutional corruption he describes has "likely done more damage to the United States in the last decade than all of the quid pro quo corruption in American history combined." Strong words. He cites the Great Recession and global warming. But the Great Recession was not caused by campaign contributions. It was caused by financial deregulation pursued by Democrats and Republicans alike, embedded in a global financial architecture, justified by an economic ideology that has dominated American policymaking since the late 1970s, and made possible by the destruction of countervailing power—organized labor, in particular—over the same period. Climate inaction has similar structural roots: the political weakness of environmental constituencies relative to fossil-fuel-producing regions, the dependency of the American economy on cheap energy, the global collective-action problem. Campaign contributions are downstream of these factors, not upstream. Reforming campaign finance without reforming the underlying political economy is like reforming the symptoms while leaving the disease in place.

There is also a question about the political theory underlying Lessig's argument. He cites Federalist 57 with apparent approval, and he treats the framers' design as authoritative. But the framers' design produced the very system Lessig now decries. Frequent elections, single-member districts, an underfunded legislative branch dependent on outside expertise, weak parties, separation of powers that requires extensive coalition-building—all of these structural features create the entry points through which money operates. A serious reform of American democracy would have to consider proportional representation, public financing of media, sectoral collective bargaining, antitrust enforcement to break up corporate giants, and probably constitutional amendment to limit private wealth in politics directly. None of these are in Lessig's essay.

I do not mean to be ungenerous. Lessig is right that *Citizens United* is bad, that dependency corruption is a useful concept, and that some reform of campaign finance is better than none. The matching-funds and voucher proposals are worth pursuing. But we should not pretend that they are sufficient to the crisis he himself describes. A democracy in which fossil-fuel interests can block climate action, financial interests can capture regulation, and the median voter's preferences are systematically ignored on issue after issue is not a democracy with a campaign-finance problem. It is a democracy with a political-economic-power problem. The campaign-finance frame, by localizing the diagnosis, ends up minimizing the remedy.

If Lessig's essay marks the limit of what a major American legal academic is willing to say in 2010, that itself is a measure of how constrained our political imagination has become. The corruption is deeper than he is willing to name. The reform required is correspondingly bigger.

**Lessig's "Lochnerism" Charge Cuts Both Ways**

Lawrence Lessig has built much of his critique of *Citizens United* around the accusation that Justice Kennedy is engaged in a form of "First Amendment Lochnerism"—that is, substituting the Court's own factual intuitions for the considered judgment of the legislature. The accusation is rhetorically devastating, because Lochnerism is the constitutional sin that no contemporary justice will admit to. But the accusation is also doctrinally confused, and the more carefully one examines it the more it boomerangs against Lessig's own argument.

What was wrong with *Lochner*? Two things. First, the Court invented an unenumerated right—liberty of contract—and used it to strike down economic regulation. Second, the Court refused to defer to legislative findings about the relationship between the regulation and its asserted purpose. Lessig focuses almost exclusively on the second feature: the Court's willingness to second-guess the New York legislature's empirical judgment about whether long bakery hours were a health hazard. He then analogizes this to Kennedy's willingness to second-guess Congress's empirical judgment about whether independent corporate expenditures undermine democratic faith.

But this analogy depends on suppressing the first feature of *Lochner*: the absence of an enumerated constitutional right. The First Amendment is enumerated. It is textually explicit. It is one of the most carefully crafted limitations on legislative power in the Constitution. When the Court strikes down a federal speech restriction, it is not inventing a doctrine like liberty of contract. It is enforcing a textual command. The proper analogy to *Lochner* would be a case in which the Court invented some unwritten right to corporate political activity and used it to strike down BCRA. That is not what *Citizens United* did. *Citizens United* enforced the actual text of the First Amendment.

This matters because the burden of justification works differently in the two contexts. Under post-*Lochner* doctrine, economic regulation enjoys a strong presumption of constitutionality; the legislature does not need to prove its empirical case, and rational-basis review accepts almost any plausible factual story. Under First Amendment doctrine, the presumption runs the other way. Restrictions on core political speech receive strict scrutiny: the government must demonstrate a compelling interest and narrow tailoring. This is not Lochnerism; it is the standard hierarchy of constitutional rights, in which textually enumerated rights protected by strict scrutiny enjoy stronger judicial protection than unenumerated economic interests subject to rational-basis review.

Lessig knows this, of course. He concedes that "free speech deserves a much more vigorous defense against improper legislation than the liberty of contract." But having made the concession, he proceeds as if the concession does not matter—as if Kennedy were obligated to gather empirical evidence the way Lochner-era courts unjustifiably did. The argument cannot have it both ways. Either First Amendment scrutiny is stricter than economic scrutiny (in which case the legislature bears the burden of empirical proof, and Kennedy was within his rights to find that burden unmet), or the two are similar (in which case Lessig's broader defense of free-speech protections is undermined).

There is a deeper doctrinal point here. The First Amendment is not just an individual right; it is a structural feature of American constitutionalism. It exists in significant part because the framers and the early republic understood that legislatures have a built-in conflict of interest when it comes to regulating political speech. Legislators have every incentive to suppress speech they find threatening, inconvenient, or critical. The legislators who passed BCRA were the very same incumbents whose interests were served by limiting third-party criticism of incumbents in the 60 days before an election. This is not a paranoid theory; it was openly acknowledged at the time. Treating BCRA as a neutral exercise of legislative wisdom, in the way Lessig urges, ignores the obvious self-interest of the regulators.

This structural concern is precisely what justifies aggressive judicial scrutiny of speech regulations. The Court does not defer to legislative findings about the harms of political speech for the same reason it does not let legislators sit in judgment of their own elections: the conflict of interest is too acute. Justice Kennedy's refusal to take Congress's word for the corrosive effects of independent expenditures is not Lochnerism. It is the appropriate skepticism that the First Amendment requires when the regulator and the regulated are, in effect, the same entity.

Now consider the substantive doctrinal innovation Lessig wants the Court to embrace: a broad concept of "dependency corruption" that would justify regulating speech to prevent legislators from being influenced by the wrong kinds of constituents. This concept is doctrinally unmanageable. Every legislator is influenced by countless constituencies—voters, donors, party leaders, media, interest groups, ideological allies, family, friends, hometown loyalties. "Dependency" is everywhere in political life. To allow the legislature to regulate speech in order to control which dependencies its members may form is to hand the legislature a roving warrant to police political communication.

Lessig tries to limit this by tying dependency corruption to the framers' specific concept of "dependence on the people." But the framers' dependence-on-the-people language was not a constraint on permissible legislative influences; it was a structural feature of representative design (frequent elections, broad suffrage). Repurposing it as a doctrinal limit on private speech is anachronistic. The framers did not contemplate, and almost certainly would not have endorsed, a federal power to suppress political films during election season in the name of preserving legislators' purity of focus.

What Lessig really wants, I think, is for the Court to recognize a much broader range of governmental interests as compelling enough to override the First Amendment. He frames this as a matter of factual humility—let the legislature decide. But the more candid framing is doctrinal expansion: convert the narrow anti-quid-pro-quo interest into a sweeping anti-influence interest, and watch the scope of permissible speech regulation expand accordingly.

This is, in its own way, a Lochnerian move—an attempt to remake First Amendment doctrine around an extra-textual conception of democratic health, rather than the actual text and history of the speech clause. The Court was right to resist it. The defense of free political speech is not perfectible, but it is the closest thing American constitutionalism has to a non-negotiable commitment. Lessig has not given us a reason to negotiate it away.

**Lessig Diagnoses the Symptom and Prescribes More of the Disease**

There is a great deal in Lawrence Lessig's essay that ordinary Americans, including those of us who do not call ourselves liberals, would recognize. Yes, Congress is broken. Yes, lobbyists own too much of Washington. Yes, the institution is captured by interests that the people did not vote for and cannot easily dislodge. The Tea Party movement that has erupted across the country over the last eighteen months is in significant part a reaction to exactly the institutional decay Lessig describes. But the prescription that follows from his diagnosis—a vast expansion of federal regulatory power over political speech, coupled with new public-financing schemes that route billions of taxpayer dollars to political campaigns—will make the disease worse, not better.

Begin with a point Lessig glances over. He treats *Citizens United* as a catastrophic worsening of an already-bad system. But the system was bad in significant part because of the regulatory regime Lessig wants to extend. The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act did not clean up American politics. It pushed political activity into ever more obscure channels—527 organizations, issue-advocacy nonprofits, sham PACs, and the various creative workarounds that wealthy actors have always found when faced with restrictions on their political activity. The bipartisan name on the law was misleading; BCRA was a triumph of incumbent self-protection. It made it harder for outsiders to challenge sitting members of Congress in the weeks before elections, when challenges most matter. The Tea Party candidates who have won primaries against established Republicans in 2010 owe their opportunity, in part, to *Citizens United*'s loosening of the restrictions that previously protected incumbents.

The corrupted-democracy frame thus becomes a way of avoiding the harder work of building genuine majoritarian support. Independent expenditures would continue. Wealthy donors would continue to organize. The donor class would adapt, as it always has. And it would resist the temptation to ban speech in the name of cleansing democracy, because the cure of speech bans has, throughout American history, been wielded most effectively by the very establishments that need challenging.

Lessig is right about the disease. The diagnosis of dependency corruption captures something real. The remedy for a captured Congress is not to give Congress more power to regulate its critics. It is to reduce the stakes of capture by limiting what Congress can do. That is a lesson the framers understood, and one we would do well to remember.

**The Disclosure Path Lessig Underestimates**

In the middle of his essay, Lawrence Lessig dismisses disclosure as an inadequate remedy for the kind of corruption *Citizens United* enables. The Court, he writes, was "ignorant" about how money actually moves through campaigns when it suggested that mandatory disclosure could address concerns about large independent expenditures. He then deploys the Chamon-Kaplan "iceberg theory" to argue that the visible portion of spending vastly understates the influence at work, because credible threats of future spending operate alongside actual spending.

I want to take Lessig's iceberg theory seriously and argue that it points toward a different conclusion than the one he draws. The right response to *Citizens United* is not to keep fighting a constitutional battle over expenditure limits—a battle Lessig himself concedes is unlikely to be won at the Supreme Court for years—but to pursue an aggressive disclosure regime that confronts the iceberg directly, exposing not only realized expenditures but also commitments, threats, and coordination patterns.

Lessig's frustration with disclosure is understandable but, I think, premature. He observes that under existing disclosure rules, a $2,000 contribution coupled with a $8,000 threat would only show up as a $2,000 reported amount, leaving the threat invisible. True. But this is not an inherent limitation of disclosure as a regulatory strategy; it is a limitation of the *current* disclosure rules, which were designed in a pre-*Citizens United* world and have not been updated to track the new mechanics of independent expenditure. Congress has substantial latitude to expand disclosure obligations, and the *Citizens United* opinion itself—by an 8-1 vote—explicitly endorsed disclosure as a constitutionally permissible response to concerns about corporate spending. Only Justice Thomas dissented. This is a remarkable consensus, and reformers should be using it.

What would aggressive disclosure look like? Several elements:

First, real-time disclosure of all independent expenditures, with strict penalties for delay. Currently, much independent spending is reported in batches after the fact, often after the relevant election. A 24-hour electronic reporting requirement, with automated publication on a public website, would make the spending visible while it matters.

Second, disclosure of the corporate decision-makers behind political expenditures. When a corporation decides to spend $1 million on a political campaign, the shareholders and the public should know which officers and directors approved the spending and on what grounds. The Securities and Exchange Commission has substantial authority to require such disclosure as a matter of corporate governance, independent of campaign-finance law. A coalition of institutional investors—pension funds, mutual funds, endowments—has been pressing for exactly this kind of disclosure since 2003, with growing success.

Third, disclosure of donor identities behind shell organizations. The most pernicious feature of the post-*Citizens United* landscape is not corporate spending per se but the proliferation of nonprofit organizations that can accept unlimited donations without disclosing their donors and then spend those donations on independent expenditures. Congress can and should require that any organization spending above a threshold on political advertising disclose the actual sources of its funding. The DISCLOSE Act, currently under consideration, attempts something like this, though imperfectly.

Fourth—and here is where the iceberg theory becomes relevant—Congress could require disclosure not only of spending but of *commitments to spend*. If Exxon announces (as in Lessig's hypothetical) that it intends to spend up to $1 million in any district to support climate skeptics, that announcement could itself be made a reportable event. Disclosure of contingent commitments would surface the threats that, on Chamon and Kaplan's theory, do much of the actual influencing.

Fifth, more vigorous enforcement of the "coordination" rules. Independent expenditures are constitutionally protected because they are independent. The moment they cease to be independent—the moment coordination occurs—they convert into contributions and may be regulated accordingly. The current coordination rules are riddled with loopholes (the so-called "firewall" requirements are easily satisfied in form while being violated in substance), and the Federal Election Commission has been institutionally paralyzed by partisan deadlock for years. A serious reform agenda would empower an independent enforcement body to investigate and prosecute coordination violations aggressively.

Note what this disclosure-centric agenda accomplishes. It does not require overturning *Citizens United*. It does not require congressional fact-finding that the Supreme Court might ignore. It does not require new doctrinal innovations around "dependency corruption" that may take decades to win judicial recognition. It works with the grain of the *Citizens United* majority's own reasoning, which embraced disclosure as the constitutionally appropriate remedy. It can be enacted by statute, implemented quickly, and refined over time as new evasion strategies emerge.

It also has a political-economy advantage Lessig undervalues. Expenditure caps create a permanent incentive for evasion—every actor with substantial resources will look for ways around the cap, and the regulator will be in a perpetual game of whack-a-mole. Disclosure, by contrast, can be made comprehensive in ways that caps cannot. There is no equivalent to "shell expenditures"; once you require disclosure of all spending and all commitments above a threshold, the entire iceberg becomes visible. Voters then can do what voters are supposed to do: decide whom to trust and whom to discount based on full information about who is talking to them and at whose expense.

Lessig might respond that voters cannot or will not process disclosure information effectively—that an information dump does not actually change behavior. There is some evidence for this skepticism. But there is also evidence, from a variety of contexts, that disclosure does affect behavior at the margins, particularly when the disclosed information is salient, easy to access, and timely. Compare the impact of nutrition labels on food packaging, conflict-of-interest disclosures in medical journals, or pollution disclosure under the Toxics Release Inventory. None of these regimes is perfect. All of them have produced measurable changes in behavior by the regulated parties. Political expenditure disclosure, done well, could do the same.

Lessig's voucher and matching-fund proposals are not bad ideas, but they will be politically difficult to enact, they will face their own constitutional challenges in the current Court, and they will not eliminate the iceberg problem he himself identifies. Disclosure is the politically achievable, constitutionally robust, and incrementally improvable path. It is the path the *Citizens United* majority itself opened. Reformers should walk through it rather than continue knocking on the door that the Court has closed.

The Framers might have envisioned a purely local dependence, but the Constitution they wrote also created a national republic in which members of Congress are to serve the nation’s interests, not only those of their districts.

The time members spend fundraising is a genuine drain, and the need to raise ever-larger sums may deter promising candidates who lack personal wealth.
