The Republican leaders of the House and Senate may squirm a bit, though, when he addresses a joint session of Congress. After decades of a conservative papal vision, the pope’s call to fight inequality and climate change has inspired many progressives, but infuriated conservatives. Is he focusing on issues at the center of Catholic teaching, or has he abandoned core Catholic beliefs to promote a liberal agenda?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The framing of Pope Francis as either a faithful steward of Catholic teaching or a smuggler of liberal politics into the Vatican misses what is actually radical about him. He is genuinely radical—but in the older, etymological sense of returning to roots. The novelty is largely a matter of emphasis, tone, and pastoral style, not doctrine.

On the substance of Catholic social teaching, almost nothing Francis has said is new. The denunciation of unfettered capitalism in *Evangelii Gaudium* echoes Leo XIII's *Rerum Novarum* (1891), Pius XI's *Quadragesimo Anno*, and John Paul II's *Centesimus Annus*. The environmental theology of *Laudato Si'* draws explicitly on Benedict XVI, who was nicknamed the "Green Pope" for installing solar panels at the Vatican and warning about ecological degradation. The preferential option for the poor, the critique of an "economy that kills," and the insistence on the dignity of migrants are not Francis's inventions—they are mainline Catholic doctrine that American conservatives have long preferred to ignore while emphasizing the Church's positions on abortion and marriage.

What is new is the reordering of priorities and the warmth of the delivery. When Francis says "Who am I to judge?" about a gay priest seeking God, he has not changed the Catechism; he has changed the music. When he washes the feet of Muslim prisoners or embraces a disfigured man in St. Peter's Square, he is performing a theology of encounter that John Paul II articulated but rarely embodied in quite this idiom. The annulment reforms announced this month streamline procedure without altering the indissolubility of marriage. Even his most provocative gestures on Communion for the divorced, awaited at next month's Synod, are likely to be pastoral accommodations rather than doctrinal reversals.

Where conservatives have a legitimate complaint is that Francis has decisively shifted the political valence of the papacy. By foregrounding inequality, climate, and migration—issues where Catholic teaching aligns with the American left—and downplaying culture-war battles, he has made it harder for the Republican coalition to claim the Church as an ally. That is a real change, but it is a change in which battles the Church chooses to fight publicly, not in what it believes.

So: radical in style, in pastoral imagination, in geopolitical positioning—yes. Radical in doctrine—no. The discomfort he provokes among American conservatives is less a sign of his heterodoxy than of their selective hearing during the previous two pontificates.

Pope Francis’s impending U.S. visit ignites a debate: Is he a radical? To conservatives who fret over his economic critiques and climate advocacy, and to progressives who embrace his social justice, the answer seems an unambiguous yes. But this framing misses the point. What appears radical is a profound re-centering of Catholic teaching on its foundations.

His 2013 *Evangelii Gaudium* on inequality is ancient tradition. Condemning an economy that “kills” echoes Old Testament prophets and Church Fathers like St. John Chrysostom, who equated neglecting the poor with theft. Modern popes from Leo XIII to Benedict XVI navigated a middle path between individualism and collectivism. Francis’s visceral language reiterates the core critique: that the love of money over human dignity is idolatry, a standard Catholic doctrine. He hasn’t abandoned belief; he’s insisting on its uncomfortable economic implications. He refuses to divorce faith from the marketplace, continuing a tradition upheld by John Paul II and Benedict XVI.

His *Laudato Si’* extends stewardship. Critics deride it as a leftist brief, but the document is rooted in a sacramental vision and deep solidarity with the poor who suffer first from pollution. The call to care for our “common home” weaves St. Francis of Assisi’s poetry with the tradition’s commitment to natural law and the common good. Far from a political manifesto, it is a deeply theological work. It is inherently pro-life, defending all creation against a throwaway culture.

His itinerary—meeting undocumented immigrants, homeless people, and prisoners—underscores this. It is not a political prop but the corporal works of mercy given flesh, the consistent ethic of life protecting the vulnerable from conception to death. This seamless garment approach, championed by Cardinal Bernardin, is central to the pro-life mission. By visiting those on death row or facing deportation, the pope isn’t touring a platform; he’s simply being a pastor.

The true radicalism lies not in doctrinal novelty but in his refusal to let the Gospel’s social demands be tamed by any partisan agenda. He makes both hosts and party leaders squirm because his message challenges the moral blindness of every ideology. He is not shaping a liberal platform; he is inviting a fallen world to a demanding, ancient love. He is the Vicar of Christ, not a politician. That such a call feels radical in 2015 says more about our lost bearings than about any revolution in Rome.

To ask whether Pope Francis is promoting a "liberal agenda" is to fundamentally misunderstand the nature of the papacy and the breadth of Catholic Social Teaching. By applying the narrow, binary lens of modern American politics to a two-millennia-old global institution, both progressives and conservatives misinterpret the pope’s pastoral focus as a partisan platform. Pope Francis is indeed radical, but his radicalism stems from a return to the Gospel's roots, not a departure from core Catholic beliefs.

When the pope visits the United States and meets with prisoners, undocumented immigrants, and the homeless, he is not abandoning Catholic doctrine; he is embodying it. His recent encyclical, *Laudato Si'*, framed climate change as a moral imperative that disproportionately affects the global poor. Similarly, his apostolic exhortation *Evangelii Gaudium* issued a blistering critique of unfettered capitalism, warning that "such an economy kills." To American fiscal conservatives, this rhetoric sounds like socialism. Yet, the Church has never endorsed absolute, unregulated capitalism. Since Pope Leo XIII’s *Rerum Novarum* in 1891, the Vatican has consistently defended workers' rights, the dignity of the poor, and the common good over pure profit.

Furthermore, while Pope Francis’s tone is notably more urgent and pastoral than his predecessors, the substance of his message represents continuity. Pope John Paul II frequently condemned unbridled consumerism, and Pope Benedict XVI was famously dubbed the "green pope" for his strong environmental advocacy. Francis has simply moved these long-standing tenets of Catholic teaching from the footnotes to the front page.

Those who accuse the pope of abandoning core beliefs ignore his unwavering commitment to orthodox Catholic theology. He has firmly maintained the Church's opposition to abortion, reiterated the traditional definition of marriage, and kept the door closed on the ordination of women. American progressives who cheer his economic and environmental stances often find themselves frustrated by his strict adherence to traditional social doctrines. He refuses to fit neatly into either a Democratic or Republican platform.

Ultimately, Pope Francis is challenging the selective cafeteria Catholicism that so often plagues American politics, where politicians on both the left and the right champion only the teachings that align with their secular ideologies. His agenda is not liberal or conservative; it is simply Catholic. If his insistence on prioritizing the marginalized, protecting the environment, and critiquing the excesses of wealth feels radical, it is only because the original message of the Gospel is inherently radical.

The question "how radical is Pope Francis?" is the wrong question, because it imports American political categories into a setting where they do not belong. A better question: what has actually changed under this pontificate, and what has not?

What has not changed is doctrine. Francis has not altered Church teaching on abortion, contraception, the male priesthood, marriage, or the Real Presence. He has reaffirmed each. Anyone expecting a doctrinal revolution from the Synod on the Family is likely to be disappointed; the deliberations have been heated but the formal teaching authority of the Church is not a democratic legislature, and Francis has shown no inclination to rewrite the catechism.

What has changed is the Church's posture toward the world. Three shifts are worth naming.

First, a shift in priorities. Francis has chosen to lead with mercy, poverty, and care for creation rather than with the culture-war flashpoints that dominated the John Paul II and Benedict XVI eras in the American imagination. He has not contradicted his predecessors; he has rebalanced the megaphone. This is a legitimate prerogative of any pope and reflects his pastoral reading of where the world's wounds most need binding.

Second, a shift in audience. Francis is the first non-European pope of the modern era, and his frame of reference is the global South — favelas, refugees, dispossessed indigenous communities. To him, an American debate about marginal tax rates looks different than it does from Washington. When he criticizes "an economy that kills," he is thinking of sweatshop workers in Bangladesh and farmers displaced by extractive industry, not of Bernie Sanders's platform.

Third, a shift in tone. The famous "who am I to judge?" line did not change moral teaching. It did change who feels welcome to walk through the door. That is a pastoral revolution, not a doctrinal one, and it is precisely the sort of move a Jesuit trained in the spiritual exercises of Ignatius would make.

So is he radical? In the literal sense of returning to roots — radix — yes. He is trying to recover the Gospel's preferential option for the poor and the Franciscan vision of creation. In the sense Americans usually mean — overturning fundamentals — no. Conservatives who feel betrayed and progressives who feel vindicated are reading the same Rorschach blot. The pope addressing Congress next week will defy both camps, because he answers to neither. He answers to a tradition older and stranger than American politics.
