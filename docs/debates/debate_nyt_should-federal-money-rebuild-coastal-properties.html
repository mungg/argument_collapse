Should Federal Money Rebuild Coastal Properties?

The New York Times recently noted that “tens of billions of tax dollars have been spent on subsidizing coastal reconstruction in the aftermath of storms.” Critics of this spending say that it props up a a costly and deadly habit: rebuilding in places that are becoming uninhabitable.

Should the federal government stop helping to rebuild properties in areas vulnerable to natural disasters? Would it make sense for the federal and local governments to buy such land, to prevent future loss of homes and lives?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

In the wake of Sandy, sympathy understandably pulls toward helping homeowners rebuild what they lost. But sympathy makes for bad coastal policy. For decades, the federal government — chiefly through the National Flood Insurance Program, beach-renourishment grants, and post-disaster aid — has been quietly subsidizing the riskiest real estate in America. The Biggert-Waters reforms passed this summer acknowledge what economists like Robert Meyer and Howard Kunreuther have argued for years: premiums have been priced well below actuarial risk, and the subsidy is capitalized into beachfront property values. Taxpayers in Kansas are, in effect, underwriting second homes on the Jersey Shore.

The case for ending automatic rebuilding subsidies is strongest where sea-level rise and erosion make repeated losses near-certain. Paying to rebuild a house on a barrier island that has already flooded three times is not disaster relief; it is a recurring transfer payment with a body count attached. The federal government should phase in risk-based premiums, deny flood insurance renewal on properties that suffer repetitive severe losses, and condition disaster aid on rebuilding to stricter elevation and setback standards — or not rebuilding at all.

Buyouts are the humane complement. Programs like New York's and New Jersey's Blue Acres, and FEMA's Hazard Mitigation Grant Program, have shown that voluntary purchases at pre-storm value, followed by permanent conversion to open space, can be cheaper than the next rescue. The land then buffers inland communities from surge.

None of this requires abandoning the coast wholesale. Dense urban shorelines like Lower Manhattan warrant hard engineering. But scattered beachfront cottages on eroding sand are a different matter, and federal checks should stop chasing them into the sea.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Federal disaster aid should not be a blank check to put the same buildings back in the same dangerous places. After Katrina, Ike and now Sandy, it is clear that the issue is not simply bad luck. Sea levels are rising, coastal development has expanded, wetlands and dunes have been destroyed, and federal policy has often hidden the true cost of living at the water’s edge.

That does not mean Washington should abandon storm victims. Emergency rescue, temporary housing, debris removal and help for low-income families are legitimate national obligations. Many people in vulnerable coastal areas are not wealthy owners of beach houses; they are renters, retirees, fishermen and working families whose neighborhoods long predate today’s risk maps. A sudden cutoff would be cruel and politically impossible.

But rebuilding aid should be conditional. Federal money should help communities become safer, not merely restore yesterday’s exposure. Homes that are rebuilt should meet higher elevation and construction standards. Infrastructure money should favor protective natural systems — wetlands, dunes, barrier islands — and relocation of essential facilities out of flood zones. The National Flood Insurance Program should charge rates that increasingly reflect real risk, with means-tested help for people who cannot afford an abrupt increase, rather than broad subsidies that reward repeated loss.

Buyouts should be a central tool, especially for properties that flood again and again. Voluntary purchases at fair pre-storm value can give families a way out, create open space that absorbs future storm surge, and save taxpayers money over time. Local governments often resist because they lose tax base, so federal and state programs should help towns plan for retreat rather than punish them for accepting reality.

The hard truth is that not every coastline can be defended forever. Federal policy should draw a line between helping people recover and financing the illusion that risk has not changed.

The federal government should stop writing blank checks to rebuild in flood zones. The arithmetic is brutal: the National Flood Insurance Program is roughly $18 billion in debt to the Treasury, mostly thanks to Katrina, and Sandy will push that figure higher still. We have built an elaborate machine that taxes Iowans and Coloradans to subsidize beachfront homes in the Hamptons and Ocean City. That isn't disaster relief; it's a regressive transfer dressed up as compassion.

The deeper problem is moral hazard. When Washington guarantees that ruined homes will be rebuilt on the same vulnerable sand, owners have no incentive to relocate, and local governments have every incentive to permit dense development right up to the dune line. Private insurers long ago refused to cover much of this risk at affordable rates. That market signal was telling us something important, and we drowned it out with federal subsidy.

A sensible reform has three parts. First, phase the NFIP toward actuarially honest premiums, as the Biggert-Waters Act passed this summer began to do. Homeowners who choose to live in harm's way should pay the true cost of that choice. Second, federal disaster aid after a storm should come with a condition: rebuild to stricter elevation and construction standards, or take a buyout. Third, fund a serious voluntary buyout program for the most repetitively flooded properties—the roughly 1 percent of insured properties that generate around 30 percent of claims.

Buyouts are not a punishment; they are a kindness, and they are cheaper than the alternative. New York's Governor Cuomo has already floated the idea for Staten Island neighborhoods inundated by Sandy. Converting that land to wetlands and dunes provides storm protection for the communities behind it. With sea levels rising and storms apparently intensifying, the question is not whether some coastal land will return to the sea but whether we will manage that retreat intelligently or keep paying, again and again, to pretend otherwise.

Before we congratulate ourselves for the tough-minded idea of abandoning the coast, we ought to look at who actually lives there. The image of federal flood insurance as a subsidy for Kennedys in Hyannis Port is a useful caricature, but it isn't the whole picture. The Rockaways, Staten Island's South Shore, Gerritsen Beach, the bayous of Plaquemines Parish, the working waterfronts of New Jersey—these are largely middle- and working-class neighborhoods. Telling those families now that they're on their own is not just politically impossible; it's unfair. They played by the rules we wrote. If we want to change the rules, we should pay the transition costs ourselves rather than impose them retroactively on people whose only mistake was trusting the government's implicit promise.

That argues for generous buyouts, not for cutting people loose. A federally funded program that offers pre-storm market value for homes in the most vulnerable areas, paired with relocation assistance, is humane and probably cost-effective over a few decades. It also avoids the political trap of seeming to punish disaster victims.

It does not, however, argue for continuing to subsidize new construction or wealthy second-home owners. Here the line should be sharp. New buildings in mapped high-hazard zones should receive no federal insurance, no federal mortgage backing, and no federal rebuilding aid. Existing primary residences should be eligible for buyouts or for insurance at premiums that gradually rise toward actuarial reality, with means-tested assistance for lower-income owners.

The coast is not a monolith and neither are its residents. A policy that distinguishes between a fisherman's bungalow and a Hamptons mansion, between honoring old commitments and underwriting new folly, is the one most likely to actually move people out of harm's way.

The debate over coastal rebuilding tends to be framed as a fiscal question, but the real driver is climate. We are arguing about whether to keep bailing water out of a boat whose hull is steadily sinking. Sea levels along the mid-Atlantic have risen roughly a foot over the past century, and the rate is accelerating. Warmer oceans feed more energetic storms. Sandy was not a freak; it was a preview.

In that context, the conventional disaster cycle—storm, federal aid, rebuild in place, wait for next storm—is no longer a defensible use of public money. It is, in effect, a policy of denial subsidized at taxpayer expense. Every rebuilt house in a chronically vulnerable area locks in future losses, future emergency deployments, future deaths. Worse, it crowds out investments we actually need: hardened infrastructure, restored wetlands and barrier islands, storm surge barriers for dense urban cores like Lower Manhattan, and managed retreat from the most exposed shorelines.

I would go further than most reformers. The federal government should publish, within five years, hazard maps reflecting projected mid-century sea level and storm risk, not just historical floodplains. Properties in the highest-risk zones should be ineligible for federally backed mortgages or insurance for new construction, period. Existing owners should be offered buyouts on a rolling, voluntary basis, with the offers becoming less generous over time to encourage earlier acceptance. Local governments that continue to permit dense development in these zones should lose access to federal disaster aid altogether.

This sounds harsh, but the alternative is harsher: a slow-motion catastrophe in which we spend hundreds of billions over coming decades defending the indefensible, and still lose the land and many of the people on it. Acknowledging that some places cannot be saved is not defeatism. It is the prerequisite for saving the places—and the public resources—that actually can be.

The cleanest way to think about this is to ask who is making the decision and who is bearing the cost. Right now, those are different people. A homeowner decides to build on a barrier island. A local zoning board decides to approve a subdivision in a marsh. A state legislature decides to keep the lights green for coastal development. And then, when the inevitable storm hits, the federal taxpayer writes the check—through flood insurance subsidies, through FEMA, through highway repairs, through SBA loans, through Army Corps projects. This is a textbook externality problem, and like most externality problems it produces too much of the activity being subsidized.

The solution is to realign incentives. First, charge true risk-based premiums for federal flood insurance, with subsidies, if any, narrowly targeted at low-income primary residents rather than vacation homes. Biggert-Waters moved in this direction; it should be implemented vigorously despite the predictable howling from coastal congressional delegations. Second, make federal disaster aid contingent on local land use reform. A town that keeps issuing permits in V-zones should not get a blank check when those permits become rubble. Third, shift a portion of federal disaster spending from rebuilding to buyouts and mitigation, which have far higher long-run returns.

Note what this approach is not. It is not a federal eviction notice. People who want to live on the coast at their own expense are welcome to do so; freedom includes the freedom to take risks. What it ends is the arrangement in which those risks are socialized while the amenity values—the ocean views, the rental income, the appreciated land values during the good years—remain private.

A federal buyout program is a useful tool, particularly for chronically flooded neighborhoods and for creating natural buffers. But the more fundamental reform is upstream: stop subsidizing the choice to build in harm's way, and a lot of the rebuilding problem will solve itself over the coming generation.

In the United States, zoning laws, building codes, and development permits are almost exclusively controlled by local and state governments.
