To Tip or Not to Tip

“Following the custom in Japan, Sushi Yasuda’s service staff are fully compensated by their salary. Therefore gratuities are not accepted. Thank you,” says a bill at the Midtown Manhattan Japanese restaurant, which decided to do away with tipping this month.

Instead of leaving a gratuity at the end of the meal, Sushi Yasuda customers are now expected to pay a higher all-around bill, which includes a service fee.

Should other restaurants and bars follow suit?

Yes, and the case for abolishing tipping in American restaurants is stronger than nostalgia for the ritual suggests.

Start with what tipping actually does. Research by Michael Lynn at Cornell has shown, repeatedly, that tip size correlates only weakly with service quality. It correlates much more robustly with the server's attractiveness, the customer's mood, whether the server touched the diner or drew a smiley face on the check, and—troublingly—with race: black servers earn smaller tips than white servers for equivalent service. A compensation system that rewards demographic luck more than competence is not a meritocratic marvel; it is a custom we've rationalized after the fact.

Tipping also offloads the employer's payroll obligations onto customers, obscures the true price of a meal, and produces wildly volatile income for workers, especially those in the back of the house, who are legally barred from most tip pools. Including service in menu prices, as Sushi Yasuda and most of Europe and Japan do, lets restaurants pay cooks and dishwashers more equitably and lets diners compare prices honestly.

The usual objection—that service will collapse without the tip's whip—is hard to credit when one considers that service in Tokyo, Paris, and Sydney is generally excellent. Restaurants compete for customers; managers can still discipline and reward staff. Sushi Yasuda is not making a quirky gesture; it is pointing at a better default.

The no-tipping model gaining traction at establishments like Sushi Yasuda deserves serious consideration, though widespread adoption faces real obstacles. Tipping culture in America is less about rewarding excellence than perpetuating a system riddled with inconsistency and bias. Studies repeatedly demonstrate that tips correlate more strongly with a server's appearance, race, and gender than with the quality of service rendered. Customers should not be put in the uncomfortable position of determining fair compensation for labor they cannot properly evaluate.

That said, the transition requires honest accounting of what workers actually earn. Many servers, particularly in high-end establishments, make substantially more through tips than they would through fixed salaries. A restaurant eliminating tips must raise prices significantly to recruit and retain capable staff—otherwise the change merely redistributes income from workers to owners. The most successful experiments, like those announced around this time by Union Square Hospitality Group, pair no-tipping policies with increased wages and show that diners will accept higher menu prices when the model is clearly explained.

The deeper issue is dignity. Compelling customers to navigate guilt, social pressure, and uncertainty about appropriate tip percentages creates anxiety and resentment. Service workers should not depend on the generosity—or caprice—of strangers. A salary-based model treats their work as professional labor deserving steady compensation, which may actually elevate service standards by removing the transactional taint from the server-customer relationship.

Whether the American dining public is ready for this shift remains the practical question. Cultural habits are stubborn, and competitive pressures may discourage adoption. But for restaurants willing to commit fully and communicate clearly, the no-tipping model offers genuine benefits for workers and customers alike.

The 15-to-20-percent gratuity is not really optional, not really tied to service quality, and not really fair to the people it ostensibly rewards. It is a ritual we perform to preserve the fiction that restaurant prices are lower than they actually are.

The standard objection is that tipping incentivizes good service. But every other developed country manages excellent restaurant service without it — Tokyo, Paris, and Rome are not known for surly waiters. Service quality is driven by training, management, and professional pride, not by the dangling carrot of a few extra dollars.

Tipping is a relic that survives because each individual restaurant fears being the first to abandon it. Before we cheer Sushi Yasuda's experiment as the future of American dining, let's slow down. Tipping is annoying, sometimes unfair, and occasionally embarrassing — but it works better than its critics admit, and the alternatives have their own problems.

Start with the servers themselves. Ask waiters in fine-dining restaurants in New York whether they want to be put on straight salary, and the answer is overwhelmingly no. A good server at a busy Manhattan restaurant can earn $60,000 to $100,000 a year on tips, much of it lightly taxed. Replace that with a flat hourly wage plus service charge distributed by management, and most of that upside disappears. The "abolish tipping" movement is largely driven by owners and academics, not by the people who actually take home the cash.

Then there's the customer's leverage. The tip is one of the few mechanisms by which an ordinary diner can register displeasure in real time. Yes, the correlation between tip and service quality is weaker than we'd like to believe — but it isn't zero, and the social expectation that good service earns a good tip shapes behavior even when individual tips don't.

Service charges, meanwhile, are not the panacea they appear. In many European countries that include service, tipping has crept back in anyway, because customers still want to reward good waiters. Service charges are also often pocketed by ownership rather than distributed to staff, which is why some jurisdictions regulate them tightly. And a 20 percent all-in price hike on the menu may scare off customers who happily would have tipped 20 percent at the end.

Sushi Yasuda is a special case: a high-end restaurant invoking a specifically Japanese cultural norm to justify the change. That's fine. But generalizing from one sushi bar to the whole industry ignores both what American servers want and how American diners behave. Let a thousand pricing experiments bloom — but don't pretend the answer is obvious.

The interesting thing about the Sushi Yasuda story isn't whether tipping is good or bad. It's that we are even having the conversation. But high-end establishments, which already attract diners willing to spend, are well positioned to lead.

The deeper shift Sushi Yasuda represents is rhetorical: it treats waiters and sushi chefs as skilled professionals on stable salaries, not as performers begging for applause. That dignity matters. There's a quietly radical element to Sushi Yasuda's move that the debate has largely missed: it's not just about service, it's about who counts as part of the restaurant.

Under the standard tipping model, the dining room and the kitchen live in different economies. Front-of-house staff — servers, bartenders, sometimes bussers — share in customer generosity. Back-of-house staff — line cooks, prep cooks, dishwashers — do not. Federal law in most states actually forbids sharing tips with cooks. The result is a structural inequality, often compounded by race and immigration status, that has nothing to do with skill or contribution. A skilled line cook running a hot station for ten hours may earn less than the server who carries her food to the table.

Folding gratuities into the price and then paying everyone from the same pot allows a restaurant to compensate kitchen workers more fairly. That's especially important at sushi restaurants, where the chefs are literally performing for the customer — the idea that the person handing you the plate gets tipped while the person crafting the nigiri does not is absurd. Sushi Yasuda's no-tipping policy is partly a way to honor the chef, who in Japanese tradition is the center of the meal.

If we want to debate whether American restaurants should follow, we should debate it on these terms: do we want a wage structure that rewards the whole team, or one that lavishes attention on whoever is closest to the customer's wallet?

There are reasons to keep tipping in some contexts — casual restaurants, bars where bartenders genuinely earn their keep through hustle and personality. But for any restaurant that takes its cooking seriously, the no-tipping model is worth a hard look. It's not about copying Japan. It's about deciding whose work we value, and being honest about it on the menu.

The tipping debate is usually framed as a binary — keep it or kill it — but the more useful question is what tipping is actually for, and whether something else could serve those purposes better in different settings.

Tipping does at least four distinct jobs in American dining. It supplements low base wages so owners can advertise lower menu prices. It offers customers a quality-control mechanism, however imperfect. It functions as a social ritual marking the end of a meal. And it provides servers with tax-advantaged, high-variance income that many of them prefer. Any reform has to grapple with all four.

Sushi Yasuda's all-inclusive pricing addresses the first job neatly and probably improves on the second (managers can train and discipline staff without ceding control to customer whim). It eliminates the third, which some customers will miss and others won't. And it likely reduces the fourth, depending on how the service charge is distributed.

For a high-end sushi restaurant, that tradeoff makes sense. The clientele is sophisticated, the staff is highly trained, the service style is formal, and the cultural reference point (Japan) gives the policy coherence. It also probably helps the restaurant retain skilled sushi chefs by sharing revenue across the whole staff.

For a neighborhood diner or a sports bar, the calculation is different. The service model is more transactional, the wage variance is part of the appeal for many workers, and customers expect to see lower prices on the menu. Mandating Sushi Yasuda's approach across the industry would flatten distinctions that the market is actually pretty good at managing.

So: should other restaurants follow? Some, yes — especially fine-dining establishments where service charges already exist in everything but name, and where back-of-house equity matters. Most, probably not yet. The best outcome is a more varied landscape, in which diners can choose, and the assumption that 20 percent at the end is the only way to pay for dinner finally loses its grip.

Critics will argue that tipping incentivizes excellent service, but this assumes customers are qualified judges of kitchen operations, which they rarely are. Quality control should rest with management, not with diners who know nothing about food preparation times or kitchen complexity. A flat service fee included in prices allows restaurants to reward high performers through proper performance reviews, not arbitrary pocket change.

The restaurant industry has resisted meaningful reform for too long. Other establishments should recognize that Sushi Yasuda's model isn't radical—it's simply more honest about what dining actually costs.

Sushi Yasuda's no-tip policy may work for a high-end sushi establishment serving a specific clientele, but applying this model broadly would be a disaster for the American restaurant industry and the workers it employs.

The uncomfortable truth is that most restaurants operate on razor-thin margins. If mandatory service charges replace tips, either menu prices skyrocket—pricing out middle-class diners—or restaurants absorb costs they can't afford, leading to layoffs and closures. The economics simply don't work for most establishments.

More critically, removing tipping dismantles a system that, whatever its flaws, actually empowers servers to earn significantly more than minimum wage. In cities like New York, experienced servers at busy restaurants can earn $50,000 or more annually through tips. Under a fixed wage model, even if employers technically raise base pay to compensate, the bureaucracy of collective bargaining and corporate pricing structures would likely result in lower take-home pay for workers.

There's also something valuable about the direct connection between service quality and compensation. When a server knows their income depends on customer satisfaction, they have genuine incentive to excel. A flat service fee, by contrast, pays the attentive server the same as the inattentive one.

Finally, tipping respects consumer sovereignty. Americans generally prefer having control over how much they pay for service. Removing this choice imposes a one-size-fits-all model that treats adults like children who can't be trusted to make appropriate judgments about quality.

Let Sushi Yasuda serve its upscale clientele as it sees fit. But the broader restaurant industry should recognize that tipping, for all its quirks, serves important economic and social functions that aren't easily replaced.

The debate over tipping versus inclusive pricing misses the real issue: what matters is whether restaurant workers receive fair, stable compensation. The mechanism matters less than the outcome.

In theory, eliminating tipping and raising base wages sounds progressive. Workers gain predictable income; customers pay transparent prices; discrimination based on customer bias diminishes. These are genuine benefits.

But we shouldn't romanticize the transition. Many restaurant owners, particularly independent establishments outside major cities, cannot simply raise prices and maintain customer traffic. The reality is that customers in 2013 are price-sensitive, and significant menu increases would push them toward cheaper alternatives. The result? Reduced dining-out overall, fewer restaurant jobs, and economic contraction in an industry that employs millions.

A more pragmatic approach might involve hybrid models: restaurants could implement service charges while communicating clearly about how those charges benefit workers, rather than hiding behind optional gratuities. Some establishments have experimented with service charges that go entirely to staff, distributed through transparent systems. This preserves some flexibility while reducing the most egregious aspects of tip-based discrimination.

We should also acknowledge that tipping serves important signaling functions. When a customer tips well, it communicates genuine satisfaction; when they tip poorly, that also communicates something. Managers can observe these patterns and address problems. A flat service fee eliminates these valuable feedback mechanisms.

Rather than demanding universal adoption of Sushi Yasuda's model, we should encourage innovation and experimentation while respecting that different restaurants serve different markets. The goal is worker welfare, not ideological purity about compensation structures.

I spent ten years as a server in restaurants ranging from dive bars to fine dining establishments, and I can tell you: most servers don't want to eliminate tipping.

The restaurant industry's wage structure is genuinely broken, but tipping isn't the villain. Poverty-level server wages mandated by the tipped minimum wage are the problem. Currently, federal law allows employers to pay servers as little as $2.13 per hour, counting on tips to reach minimum wage. This creates a system where workers bear enormous financial risk and where employer accountability disappears.

The solution isn't eliminating tips—it's raising the tipped minimum wage to a living standard and enforcing it. If every server automatically earned $12 or $15 per hour regardless of shift quality, we could eliminate the most exploitative aspects of the current system while preserving the earning potential that makes restaurant work worthwhile for experienced servers.

Here's what I observed repeatedly: good servers at busy restaurants earned substantially more than kitchen staff or managers. This reflects the market reality that good service is genuinely valuable. Eliminating tips doesn't liberate servers—it strips them of a compensation mechanism that often worked to their advantage.

As for discrimination in tipping, yes, it exists. But eliminating tips doesn't eliminate discrimination—it just changes who does the discriminating. Now it's managers making hiring and scheduling decisions, and research shows managers can be just as biased as customers.

The real villains are restaurant owners who exploit the tipped minimum wage, not the tipping system itself. We should demand fair wages and enforcement, not a wholesale restructuring that might benefit some workers while harming others.

Cultural context matters enormously in this debate, and Americans should examine their assumptions more carefully.

Tipping in America emerged from post-Civil War labor practices specifically designed to avoid paying freed slaves fairly. The tradition was explicitly racist in origin, with restaurant owners arguing that formerly enslaved people would work harder if their compensation depended on white customers' goodwill. We inherit this system not because it's efficient or fair, but because of historical momentum and employer convenience.

Meanwhile, Japan—where Sushi Yasuda's owners clearly drew inspiration—has a fundamentally different cultural approach to service. Japanese restaurant workers typically receive comprehensive training, career-track employment, and social respect for their craft. Service is understood as professional duty, not performance for optional compensation. This is the correct model.

For American diners who resist change, consider: would you tip your doctor? Your accountant? Your child's teacher? We don't tip professionals in other industries because we understand their compensation should be fair and predictable, negotiated between employer and employee rather than crowdsourced from customers. Why should restaurant work be different?

The Sushi Yasuda model represents cultural maturity. It treats service staff as professionals deserving of stable employment rather than supplicants dependent on customer generosity. Other restaurants should follow this lead not because economics force them to, but because it's the ethical thing to do.

Will the transition be easy? No. Will some customers resist? Certainly. But cultural practices can and should change when they rest on questionable foundations. Tipping is neither efficient nor dignified; it's simply familiar. And familiarity alone is not a defense.

The Sushi Yasuda announcement is clever marketing disguised as policy reform. Let's be honest: an exclusive Manhattan sushi restaurant catering to wealthy customers can experiment with no-tip policies because its clientele has money to spend and novelty to appreciate. This tells us almost nothing about what would work for the Olive Garden, the local Chinese buffet, or the neighborhood diner that most Americans actually frequent.

Real restaurant reform requires addressing real economic realities. The National Restaurant Association estimates that nearly half of American restaurant workers receive tips, and the industry employs approximately 12 million people. Any policy change affecting this workforce must account for the vast diversity of establishments, from food trucks to five-star establishments, from rural diners to urban chains.

What would actually help? Mandatory minimum wage floors for tipped workers with no tip credit. Aggressive enforcement against employers who steal tips or fail to pay legally required minimums. Portable benefits that follow workers across employers. These concrete measures address worker welfare without requiring expensive menu restructurings that smaller establishments cannot absorb.

The fetishization of single-model solutions—whether all-tipping or no-tipping—distracts from the complicated policy work that would actually help restaurant workers. We should be suspicious of simple answers to complex economic problems, regardless of how progressive they sound.

Sushi Yasuda can do whatever it wants. But let's not pretend that a luxury restaurant's staffing experiment provides a blueprint for an industry employing one in ten Americans. Real reform requires granular, enforceable, widely applicable policies—not gestures that look good in a New York Times Room for Debate feature.
