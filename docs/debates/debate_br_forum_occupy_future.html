Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The editors are right to frame Occupy as part of a long American tradition of measuring institutions against principles. That tradition runs from the abolitionists to the suffragists to the civil rights movement, and it has always depended on a particular rhetorical move: insisting that the country honor the commitments it has already publicly avowed. Occupy belongs in this lineage. But the framing in the lead essay, while generous, may understate how much harder this iteration of the work will be — because the gap that has opened between our principles and our practices is not only material but constitutional, in the small-c sense of how our common life is structured.

Consider what we now know about the distribution of income. The Congressional Budget Office's October 2011 report found that, between 1979 and 2007, after-tax household income for the top 1 percent grew by 275 percent, while the middle three quintiles grew by under 40 percent and the bottom quintile by just 18. The work of Piketty and Saez places the top 1 percent's share of national income back at levels not seen since the late 1920s. Median household income has barely budged for a decade and has fallen during the recovery. Roughly one in six Americans is in poverty by the new Supplemental Poverty Measure; long-term unemployment is at post-war highs; nearly four million homes have been lost to foreclosure since 2007. These are not the byproducts of a temporarily off-kilter economy. They are the contours of a different economy than the one most Americans believe they live in.

If that were the whole problem, the lead essay's optimism would be well placed. Reform movements have repeatedly closed material gaps when they could mobilize political voice. What is distinctive — and dangerous — about this moment is that the channels through which voice ordinarily travels are themselves being warped by the same inequality. Citizens United, decided less than two years ago, did not invent the role of money in politics, but it removed a major guardrail at precisely the moment when concentrated wealth had reached historic levels. The result is visible in the early shape of the 2012 cycle: the rise of super PACs, the willingness of single donors to sustain entire presidential campaigns, the bipartisan deference to financial-sector preferences in everything from Dodd-Frank's implementation to the foreclosure settlement negotiations. Larry Bartels and Martin Gilens have already shown in their work that, on contested questions, the policy preferences of low-income Americans bear almost no statistical relationship to what Congress actually does. The principle of equal political voice is not merely unrealized; it is being actively eroded.

This is why I think Occupy's most important contribution so far has been diagnostic rather than programmatic. The slogan "We are the 99%" did something that years of careful policy writing could not: it gave ordinary people a vocabulary for naming a structural fact about American life. Before September, "inequality" was a wonk's word. It is now a kitchen-table word. Pew, Gallup, and the major networks have all registered a sharp uptick in public concern about the gap between rich and poor in the weeks since Zuccotti Park filled. That is a real accomplishment, and one that the clearing of the encampments on November 15 has not undone.

But diagnosis is not cure, and here I want to push gently against the lead essay. The civil rights movement that the editors invoke did not succeed only by exposing the disjuncture between principle and practice. It succeeded because it coupled moral witness to a concrete legislative agenda: desegregation, the Voting Rights Act, fair housing. The suffragists wanted the vote. The abolitionists wanted emancipation. Occupy has, so far, deliberately resisted that kind of specificity, partly out of a justified fear of being co-opted, partly out of an admirable commitment to horizontal deliberation. The risk is that a movement which refuses to name what it wants leaves the naming to others — to sympathetic politicians who will translate its energy into the narrow vocabulary of their existing agendas, or to opponents who will fill the vacuum with caricature.

The questions the editors pose at the end of their statement — whether we are entering a new reform moment, whether we can build an open and successful movement — are therefore not rhetorical. They depend on choices yet to be made. A reform agenda adequate to the diagnosis would have to address campaign finance, the tax treatment of capital, the governance of finance itself, the bargaining power of workers, and the conditions of access to higher education and health care. None of these is beyond the reach of ordinary democratic politics. But reaching them will require Occupy, or some successor formation, to translate moral clarity into legislative specificity — and to do so before the encampments fade from memory and the country's attention drifts elsewhere.

The Occupy movement has succeeded in one crucial respect: it has forced the question of economic inequality back into the national conversation after decades of neglect. The lead essay rightly frames this moment as a test of whether our democratic institutions can live up to our professed ideals of equal opportunity and political voice. But the historical record suggests that bridging the gap between rhetoric and reality requires more than a diagnosis; it demands a credible strategy for institutional reform, and here the movement’s path remains uncertain.

The analogy to past reform eras is instructive but incomplete. The abolition of slavery, women’s suffrage, and the civil rights movement were each propelled by sustained organizing, clear moral imperatives, and concrete legislative goals. Occupy, by contrast, has coalesced around a broad critique—the corrupting influence of concentrated wealth—without yet articulating a coherent set of demands. This is not a fatal flaw; movements often begin with inchoate outrage. But the leap from encampments to durable change will require translating the language of “the 99 percent” into specific proposals that can mobilize a broad coalition beyond the parks and into the voting booth.

The obstacles are formidable. Extreme inequality is not an accident; it is the product of decades of policy choices—tax cuts favoring capital gains over wages, financial deregulation, the erosion of collective bargaining, and the Supreme Court’s *Citizens United* decision, which further opened the floodgates of corporate money in politics. These structures are mutually reinforcing: economic power buys political influence, which in turn rigs the rules to entrench economic power. Breaking that cycle demands not just protest but electoral organizing and legislative tenacity, work that is grindingly slow and often unglamorous.

There is also a tension within the movement’s own ethos. Occupy’s commitment to horizontal, consensus-based decision-making has been a source of its radical democratic energy, but it also makes it difficult to negotiate with established power or to make the strategic compromises that political success often requires. The civil rights movement had its SNCC and its NAACP, its direct-action wing and its lobbyists. A healthy ecosystem of dissent needs both the prophetic voice and the pragmatic operator. So far, Occupy has largely been the former.

Skeptics warn that without a clear platform, the energy will dissipate. Supporters counter that changing the terms of debate is itself a victory. Both may be right. The Tea Party demonstrated that a loosely organized insurgency can reshape a political party’s priorities, but only by relentlessly focusing on elections and primary challenges. Occupy’s leaders have so far shown little appetite for that kind of engagement.

Still, the movement has already changed the weather. Terms like “income inequality” and “the one percent” now dominate op-ed pages and campaign speeches. The question is whether this rhetorical shift can be converted into the kind of structural reform that, as the lead essay notes, has periodically renewed American democracy. History suggests the arc of reform is long and depends on building durable institutions that outlast the moment of moral outrage. Occupy has lit a spark; now it must decide what to set aflame.

The lead essay rightly identifies the Occupy movement as a profound moral reckoning, one that places our soaring economic inequality at the center of the American democratic project. By framing the current crisis as a disjuncture between our egalitarian principles and our lived reality, the editors capture exactly why the occupation of Zuccotti Park and public spaces across the country has resonated so deeply. The brilliant simplicity of the "We are the 99%" slogan has succeeded where years of academic papers and policy briefs have failed: it has laid bare the reality that our social contract is fundamentally broken.

The "new developments" threatening our cherished principles are indeed glaring. In the wake of the 2008 financial crisis, the American public watched as the architects of the collapse were bailed out with taxpayer funds, while millions of ordinary citizens faced mass foreclosures, stagnant wages, and persistent unemployment. When paired with the Supreme Court’s recent *Citizens United* decision, which opened the floodgates for unlimited corporate spending in elections, it is impossible to ignore the growing consensus that political voice is now strictly tethered to wealth. We are living in a moment where the basic premise that everyone has a right to be heard is being systematically undermined by the outsized influence of a financial elite.

However, while the comparison to the abolition of slavery, women’s suffrage, and the civil rights movement is inspiring, it also reveals the greatest challenge facing Occupy today. Those historical precedents ultimately succeeded because they managed to translate sweeping moral outrage into highly specific, actionable legal remedies—the Thirteenth Amendment, the Nineteenth Amendment, and the Civil Rights and Voting Rights Acts. The systemic inequality protested today is much more diffuse. It is the accumulated result of decades of complex tax policy, financial deregulation, deindustrialization, and the weakening of organized labor.

Can an open, horizontally organized, and deliberately leaderless movement successfully reverse these entrenched structural trends? That remains the vital, unanswered question. Occupy has demonstrated immense power in its refusal to issue a conventional list of demands, using its radical democratic processes—like the general assembly and the human microphone—to model the society it wishes to see. But changing the national conversation from debt and austerity to wealth distribution is only the first phase.

If Occupy is to permanently realign our institutions with our ideals, as the lead essay hopes, it will eventually need to bridge the gap between street-level democratic engagement and the levers of institutional power. It must find a way to direct its formidable grassroots energy toward concrete policy reversals—whether that means financial transaction taxes, rigorous campaign finance reform, or renewed protections for collective bargaining. We are undeniably at a historical crossroads, but realizing our most cherished principles will require turning the occupation of our public squares into the transformation of our public policy.

Occupy’s most important achievement so far is not a policy platform but a shift in moral vocabulary. For years Americans have been told to understand insecurity as a personal failure: too much borrowing, too little education, insufficient flexibility. Occupy has insisted that private troubles are also public facts. When wages stagnate while productivity rises, when families lose homes after banks are rescued, when students mortgage their futures for credentials that no longer guarantee stability, the issue is not simply character. It is power.

The lead essay is right to place this moment in the long American argument between principle and practice. The United States has always joined democratic language to undemocratic arrangements: slavery beside liberty, disfranchisement beside popular sovereignty, Jim Crow beside equal protection. Today’s contradiction is less formal but still profound. Most citizens possess the vote, yet policy is shaped in an environment of concentrated wealth, professional lobbying, revolving-door regulation, and campaigns increasingly dependent on large donors. After Citizens United, this imbalance is likely to grow worse. Political equality cannot survive as a civic ideal if economic inequality gives some citizens a megaphone and others a whisper.

The evidence of divergence is no longer easy to dismiss. The recent Congressional Budget Office report found that from 1979 to 2007 after-tax income for the top 1 percent rose 275 percent, far outpacing everyone else. At the same time union density has fallen, secure pensions have disappeared, and risk has shifted from institutions to households. The financial crisis exposed the asymmetry starkly: gains were private, losses socialized. No wonder “We are the 99 percent” resonated. It is a crude slogan in one sense—there are real differences within the 99 percent—but it names a political reality: the rules have been written to favor those already best positioned to influence them.

Still, moral clarity is not enough. If Occupy is to become more than an alarm bell, it must answer a strategic question: how can a movement committed to radical democracy exercise power in institutions that reward hierarchy, message discipline, and bargaining? The encampments have modeled participation, mutual aid, and deliberation. Those are not trivial accomplishments. But general assemblies alone cannot renegotiate mortgages, reform campaign finance, restore labor rights, or change tax policy. Movements need pressure from outside and allies inside; they need disruptive energy and legislative imagination.

This does not mean Occupy should immediately become a conventional interest group with a ten-point plan. Its breadth is part of its strength. Premature narrowing could reduce a systemic critique to a menu of technocratic fixes. But a movement can preserve openness while naming targets: foreclosure relief, a financial transactions tax, public financing of elections, tougher bank regulation, progressive taxation, job creation, protection for collective bargaining, and affordable higher education. Such demands would not exhaust Occupy’s democratic promise, but they would test whether elected officials are willing to govern for the many rather than the few.

The future of Occupy will depend on whether it can connect indignation to organization. American reform has rarely come from politeness alone. Abolitionists, suffragists, labor organizers, and civil-rights activists all made respectable society uncomfortable before they changed it. They also built durable institutions, cultivated leaders, and forced choices. Occupy has opened a necessary conversation about who rules, who benefits, and what democracy requires. The next task is to make that conversation impossible for power to ignore.

The Occupy movement has performed a valuable service by putting inequality back on the national agenda. For decades, scholars, activists, and policymakers had documented the growing gap between the very rich and everyone else, but this research rarely penetrated mainstream political discourse. The 99 Percent versus the 1 Percent framing has changed that, bringing questions of economic concentration and political influence to the center of public debate. This alone makes the movement significant, regardless of what comes next.

But the lead essay raises harder questions that deserve more rigorous attention. The authors ask whether we are experiencing a disjuncture between our principles and our institutions severe enough to warrant comparison with slavery, the denial of women's suffrage, or segregation. I believe the answer is yes, though the diagnosis requires care.

The evidence for extreme inequality is robust and was well-established by November 2011. The work of economists like Thomas Piketty and Emmanuel Saez had demonstrated that the income share of the top 1 percent had returned to levels not seen since the 1920s. The financial crisis and subsequent recession had deepened this concentration: while millions faced foreclosure and unemployment, financial institutions received bailouts and executive compensation continued at historic heights. The political consequences were equally troubling. The Supreme Court's Citizens United decision in 2010 had opened new channels for corporate money in politics, and the broader patterns of campaign finance suggested that economic power translated directly into political power. The notion that everyone has an opportunity to get ahead or a meaningful chance to be heard was increasingly difficult to defend.

The more difficult question is whether a movement like Occupy can translate this recognition into lasting change. History suggests that movements for economic justice face particular challenges. They must build coalitions across racial, geographic, and cultural lines. They must navigate the gap between symbolic demands and specific policy proposals. And they must contend with powerful interests that benefit from the status quo.

The Occupy movement's strengths have included its rejection of traditional hierarchical structures, its capacity to generate solidarity among participants, and its ability to command media attention. These are not trivial accomplishments. But the movement has faced genuine questions about sustainability and strategy. The camps required enormous resources to maintain. Evictions in Oakland, New York, and elsewhere demonstrated the physical vulnerability of the occupation model. And the movement's internal debates about demands, decision-making processes, and relationships to existing political institutions reflected real tensions that any democratic movement must confront.

The authors ask whether we can build an open, democratic, and successful movement to realize our ideals. This is the right question, but it requires more than optimism to answer. Success will depend on whether the energy generated by Occupy connects to the patient work of coalition-building, policy development, and electoral engagement that has historically produced reform. The civil rights movement, the women's suffrage movement, and the labor movement all required decades of organizing beyond moments of mass mobilization.

What is clear by November 2011 is that the problems Occupy identified are real and that many Americans recognize them. Whether the movement can help forge the institutional innovations needed to address inequality remains to be seen. The principles articulated in the lead essay—opportunity, voice, meaningful participation—are worth defending. The harder question is which strategies will actually advance them.

**The Numbers Behind the Slogan**

The Boston Review's framing essay asks whether we're entering a moment when the gap between our principles and our institutions has been "cast into especially sharp relief." For anyone who has been paying attention to the economic data over the past three decades, the answer is plainly yes—and Occupy deserves credit for finally forcing that data into political conversation.

Consider what Emmanuel Saez and Thomas Piketty's work on income concentration has shown. The share of national income accruing to the top one percent of American households has roughly doubled since the late 1970s, from around ten percent to over twenty percent. The top 0.1 percent has done even better. Meanwhile, real median wages have essentially stagnated since 1973, even as productivity has continued to climb. The Congressional Budget Office's October 2011 report, "Trends in the Distribution of Household Income Between 1979 and 2007," confirmed the picture from a different angle: after-tax income for the top one percent grew by 275 percent over that period; for the middle three quintiles, by under 40 percent.

These are not abstractions. They translate into concrete differences in life expectancy, educational attainment, civic participation, and—crucially—political voice. Martin Gilens's research on policy responsiveness suggests that when the preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the middle class and the poor, it is overwhelmingly the affluent whose preferences prevail in legislation. This is the empirical core of the "we are the 99%" slogan, and it is what makes the slogan more than a rhetorical flourish.

I would push the Boston Review's lead essay to be more specific, though. The language of "principles" and "practices" is appealing but also slightly evasive. The American story is not only one of principles slowly catching up to practice; it is also one of organized economic interests actively rewriting the rules to their advantage. The decline of unionization (from roughly a third of private-sector workers in the 1950s to under seven percent today), the deregulation of finance culminating in the repeal of Glass-Steagall, the shift to a tax code that treats carried interest as capital gains, the explosion of corporate money in elections following *Citizens United*—none of these are accidents of cultural drift. They are the result of decades of concerted effort by well-funded actors.

If Occupy is to translate moral energy into durable change, it needs to be at least as concrete about its diagnosis as about its grievances. The good news is that the policy menu is not mysterious. Restore meaningful progressivity to the tax code. Tax financial transactions. Break up institutions that are too big to fail. Reverse *Citizens United* by constitutional amendment if necessary. Strengthen the right to organize. Expand the Earned Income Tax Credit. Invest in infrastructure and public higher education at a scale appropriate to a wealthy nation. None of this is utopian; most of it was mainstream American policy within living memory.

The objection one hears constantly—that Occupy "has no demands"—is partly disingenuous (the demands are not hard to infer) and partly an artifact of the movement's deliberate horizontality. But I think it is also a real weakness. Social movements that have changed America—abolition, women's suffrage, the labor movement, civil rights—combined moral witness with specific legislative and institutional targets. The March on Washington was for "Jobs and Freedom," with concrete demands attached. The suffragists wanted the Nineteenth Amendment, not a vague reordering of gender relations. Occupy will eventually have to make analogous choices about what, exactly, it is asking the political system to do.

This is not a counsel of compromise so much as a counsel of leverage. Public attention is a scarce resource, and movements that fail to convert attention into institutional outcomes tend to dissipate. The Tea Party, whatever one thinks of its politics, understood this; it ran candidates, took over primaries, and reshaped a major political party in the span of two election cycles. Occupy has more diffuse goals and more skepticism about electoral politics, but the underlying logic is the same.

The most encouraging thing about the moment is that the diagnostic conversation has finally shifted. A year ago, the dominant frame in Washington was deficit hysteria; the threat to American prosperity was supposedly Social Security and Medicare, not concentrated wealth or unemployment. Today, polling shows that majorities across the political spectrum think inequality is too high and that the wealthy do not pay their fair share. That is a genuine political opening.

Whether the opening becomes a turning point depends on whether the energy in Zuccotti Park and a thousand smaller encampments can find institutional expression. The Boston Review's series will be valuable if it helps think through how. The principles are not really in dispute. The question is whether we have the political will and organizational creativity to make them operative again.

**A Skeptic's Note**

I want to register a dissenting view to the celebratory tone of the Boston Review's introduction to its Occupy series. I am not unsympathetic to many of the concerns animating the protests—I think crony capitalism is a real problem, the financial sector wields too much influence, and student debt has reached levels that distort young people's life choices. But the framing on offer here, and from the Occupy movement more broadly, conflates several distinct issues and substitutes mood for analysis.

The lead essay invokes the abolition of slavery, women's suffrage, and the civil rights movement as analogues. This is an extraordinary comparison. Those movements were directed at specific, identifiable injustices: human beings owned as property; citizens denied the vote because of their sex; citizens segregated, lynched, and disenfranchised because of their race. The wrongs were concrete; the remedies were concrete; the moral case was overwhelming.

What is the analogous concrete wrong here? "Extreme inequality" is named, but inequality is not, in itself, a wrong. It is a description of a distribution. Whether a particular distribution is unjust depends on how it arose and what it does to people. A country in which Steve Jobs becomes a billionaire by building products hundreds of millions of people voluntarily buy is not in the same moral universe as a country in which a hereditary aristocracy extracts rents from a peasantry. Occupy's rhetoric flattens this distinction.

There is, to be fair, a defensible version of the inequality concern that focuses on the mechanisms by which wealth was accumulated rather than on the bare fact of wealth concentration. Hedge fund managers paying lower effective tax rates than their secretaries because of the carried-interest loophole; bankers whose institutions were rescued by taxpayers continuing to draw bonuses; regulatory capture across multiple agencies; the revolving door between Treasury and Wall Street—these are real grievances with real remedies. But Occupy does not generally distinguish between earned and unearned wealth, between productive entrepreneurs and rent-extracting insiders. The "1%" is treated as a single undifferentiated class, which is both empirically misleading and morally lazy.

I am also skeptical of the implicit theory of democracy on display. The Boston Review essay celebrates the movement's potential to "re-democratize American politics." But Occupy's most visible organizational innovations—the general assembly, the people's mic, consensus decision-making—are not models of democracy in any sense recognizable from American constitutional tradition. They are exercises in small-group deliberation among self-selected participants. The vast majority of the American "99%" has not joined an encampment, does not attend general assemblies, and has not authorized anyone to speak for them. To claim to represent 99 percent of the population on the basis of a few thousand participants in dozens of cities is a rhetorical move, not a democratic credential.

Real democratic legitimacy in America comes from elections—messy, money-soaked, frustrating elections, but elections all the same. If Occupy's participants believe American politics has been captured by economic elites, the appropriate response is to organize, recruit candidates, register voters, and contest primaries. The Tea Party, which I have my own disagreements with, did exactly this. Within two years it transformed a major party. Occupy's apparent disdain for electoral politics is, I think, a confession of weakness dressed up as principle.

I also want to push back on the historical narrative the lead essay assumes. America is described as a country whose history has been "driven by tension" between principles and practices, with progress coming from moments of dramatic reform. This is part of the story, but it leaves out the parts where reformist enthusiasm went badly wrong—Prohibition, the eugenics movement, various populist enthusiasms that scapegoated immigrants or financiers. Mass movements are not self-validating. They can be right; they can also be wrong. Skepticism about whether a particular movement is in fact in the lineage of abolition and suffrage is not bad faith; it is intellectual honesty.

Finally, a note on the substantive economics. The most pressing problem in the American economy at this moment is not inequality but unemployment. Roughly fourteen million Americans are out of work; millions more are underemployed or have left the labor force entirely. The macroeconomic challenge is a demand shortfall and a balance-sheet recession, neither of which is meaningfully addressed by the symbolic politics of occupying parks. Many of Occupy's demands—debt forgiveness, expanded social programs—would help on the margins, but the most important interventions (monetary stimulus, fiscal stimulus, mortgage restructuring at scale) are not really what the movement is about.

I do not want to be entirely dismissive. Public attention is genuinely on the right questions for the first time in years, and that is partly Occupy's doing. But attention is not analysis, and protest is not policy. The Boston Review would do its readers a greater service by interrogating the movement's premises than by celebrating its mood.

**Where Is Labor?**

There is one striking absence in most commentary on Occupy Wall Street, and the Boston Review's introductory essay reproduces it: the labor movement. The history of American egalitarianism is not primarily a history of cultural awakening or moral persuasion. It is a history of organized workers building institutions powerful enough to bargain with capital and to pressure the state. Any "Occupy the Future" series that does not put labor at the center of its analysis is going to miss the main lesson of the twentieth century.

Consider the chronology. The Gilded Age—the period most often compared to our own—was ended not by enlightenment among elites but by the rise of mass industrial unionism, the populist and progressive movements, and ultimately the New Deal coalition. The compression of incomes that produced the broadly shared prosperity of the postwar decades, what economists call the Great Compression, was sustained by a unionization rate of roughly one in three private-sector workers, a federal minimum wage with real purchasing power, and a tax code that imposed top marginal rates above 70 percent. None of this happened by accident, and none of it was the result of moral suasion alone. It was the product of organized power.

The unwinding of that order beginning in the 1970s tracks closely with the decline of organized labor. Private-sector union density today stands at around 6.9 percent—lower than at any point since before the Wagner Act. As unions have shrunk, the wage share of national income has fallen, the political power of working-class voters has dwindled, and the policy menu in Washington has narrowed to options acceptable to professional and managerial elites. This is not a coincidence. It is causation.

So when I look at Occupy, I see a movement with the right diagnosis and uncertain strategy. The protests have drawn welcome attention to inequality and financial power, but the encampments are largely composed of students, recent graduates, and downwardly mobile professionals. The connection to working-class organizations has been real—several unions have provided logistical and moral support, and there have been important moments of convergence, like the November 17 actions—but it has not yet been transformative. Occupy needs labor more than labor needs Occupy.

What would a deeper integration look like? It would mean putting the right to organize at the center of the policy agenda. The Employee Free Choice Act, which would have made union recognition meaningfully accessible, died quietly in 2010 and has not been revived. Card check, first-contract arbitration, and stronger penalties for employer interference in organizing campaigns are not glamorous demands, but they are the structural reforms most likely to durably shift bargaining power back toward workers.

It would mean defending public-sector unions, which are now the bulk of organized labor and which have been under direct attack from state legislatures—most visibly in Wisconsin earlier this year, where the movement to recall Scott Walker has been an important precursor to Occupy in terms of mobilizing populist energy. The Wisconsin Capitol occupation last winter was, in many ways, the template Occupy is now scaling up.

It would mean engaging with the contemporary realities of work. The American workforce is increasingly composed of low-wage service workers, contingent workers, immigrants, and people piecing together livings from multiple part-time gigs. Traditional industrial unionism does not reach these workers well. New organizational forms—worker centers, sectoral bargaining, alt-labor groups like Working America and the Restaurant Opportunities Center, the domestic workers movement that won historic legislation in New York—are pioneering ways to organize people who fall outside the Wagner Act framework. These efforts deserve far more attention than they get.

It would also mean reckoning with the global dimensions of the inequality problem. Capital is mobile in ways labor cannot easily match, and the offshoring of manufacturing has put a ceiling on wage demands in tradable sectors. Any serious agenda has to think about trade policy, currency manipulation, and international labor standards—issues that do not fit neatly on a placard but that shape the bargaining environment workers actually face.

I will close with a worry. American liberalism in the post-1968 period has often privileged cultural politics over economic politics, and movements led by educated young people have a tendency to drift in that direction even when they begin with economic grievances. Occupy must resist this. The conversation has to stay focused on jobs, wages, debt, and power—not because cultural questions are unimportant but because economic inequality is the engine driving political inequality, and political inequality is what makes every other reform harder.

The Boston Review's series will be most useful if it asks, repeatedly and concretely, how Occupy can become a vehicle for reviving organized economic power among ordinary Americans. The principles invoked by the lead essay are admirable. But principles do not enforce themselves. Power does.

**Right Diagnosis, Wrong Doctor**

I find myself in the curious position of agreeing with much of what Occupy Wall Street says about American institutions while disagreeing with virtually everything it proposes about what to do. The Boston Review's lead essay frames the contemporary moment as one in which institutions have drifted from principles. That is correct. But the diagnosis is incomplete, and the implied prescription—more democratic activism aimed at expanding government—gets the direction of the problem backward.

Begin with the diagnosis the protesters and I share. The American economy has, over the past several decades, become increasingly characterized by cronyism. Politically connected firms and industries extract favorable treatment from the state. The financial sector, in particular, operates under an implicit government guarantee: heads I win, tails the taxpayer loses. When Bear Stearns and AIG and the auto companies failed, their creditors and shareholders were made substantially whole through public action. The bailouts of 2008 and 2009 transferred enormous resources to the politically connected, with little accountability and no meaningful structural reform of the institutions involved. Dodd-Frank has, if anything, entrenched the too-big-to-fail problem by formalizing the largest banks' status as systemically important.

This is a real grievance, and Occupy is right to name it. The injustice is not that some people are rich; it is that some people are rich because the rules have been rigged in their favor. A free society should be entirely comfortable with the first and entirely intolerant of the second.

Where I part company with the movement is on what follows from the diagnosis. The Occupy frame, and the Boston Review's framing of it, treats government as the natural ally of the 99% against the 1%. This gets things almost exactly backward. The 1% is rich and powerful in significant part because government has grown into an enormous discretionary instrument that can be used to redirect resources to favored constituencies. The larger the state, the more valuable it becomes to capture, and the more wealth will be invested in capturing it. This is not a partisan observation; it is a structural one, and it explains why the financial sector has thrived under both Republican and Democratic administrations, why agricultural subsidies survive every reform effort, why the tax code is metastasizing with carve-outs, and why regulators consistently come to identify with the industries they regulate.

The libertarian remedy is to shrink the prize. Reduce the state's discretionary power to allocate economic resources, and you reduce the return on lobbying, regulatory capture, and political donations. Replace targeted subsidies with neutral rules. End the implicit guarantee for large financial institutions by letting them fail and breaking them up if necessary to make failure tolerable. Remove tax preferences and lower rates across the board. Open up occupational licensing regimes that protect incumbents at the expense of new entrants and consumers. Reform a patent system that has become a tool for entrenchment rather than innovation.

The Occupy movement's preferred remedies move in the opposite direction. More regulation, more progressive taxation, more government intervention in markets, more discretionary spending—all of which will be administered by the same political class whose corruption the protesters are protesting. The expectation seems to be that, having identified the capture problem, we can simply hand more authority to the captured institutions and trust them to act differently this time. I do not find this plausible.

There is a deeper philosophical point. The lead essay invokes "everyone, not just the rich" as the beneficiaries of opportunity. I agree. But opportunity, in the morally robust sense, is the freedom to act on one's own initiative without requiring permission from authorities. The expansive state that the activist left wants to build is, by its nature, a state that requires permissions—licenses, certifications, approvals, conditions on aid. The poor and the marginal are precisely the people least equipped to navigate such regimes, and they are the people most harmed when economic activity is channeled through bureaucratic discretion.

I would also dissent from the Boston Review's casual invocation of abolition, suffrage, and civil rights as Occupy's lineage. Those movements were about extending the formal protections of liberal democracy—property, vote, due process—to people who had been excluded from them. They were, in the deepest sense, libertarian achievements: the removal of state-enforced disabilities. Conflating that legacy with a project to expand state economic discretion is historically tendentious.

None of this is meant to dismiss the genuine pain and disorientation that the financial crisis and its aftermath have inflicted. Millions of Americans were harmed by the housing bubble and bust, by underwater mortgages, by long-term unemployment, by college debt incurred against expectations of employment that did not materialize. These are real injuries. They deserve real responses. But the response should focus on dismantling the structures that produced the injury—the bubble-blowing monetary policy, the federally subsidized housing finance system, the higher education debt machine, the regulatory state that protects incumbents—not on expanding them.

Occupy has the right enemy. It is choosing the wrong weapons.

**A Cautionary Note from Civil Rights History**

The Boston Review's lead essay places Occupy in a lineage with abolition, women's suffrage, and the civil rights movement. As a historian of the last of these, I want to offer both encouragement and caution about that comparison.

The encouragement first. American social movements have repeatedly forced the country to confront the gap between its founding language—equality, liberty, consent of the governed—and the realities experienced by significant portions of its population. The movements that succeeded did so by combining moral clarity with organizational sophistication, and by sustaining their efforts over years and decades, not weeks and months. Occupy is showing the beginnings of moral clarity. Whether it will develop the other capacities remains to be seen.

The caution is this: nothing about the civil rights movement was as spontaneous, leaderless, or improvisational as it sometimes appears in retrospect. The Montgomery bus boycott, often remembered as a moment when an ordinary woman refused to move, was in fact carefully planned by an experienced network of activists who had been preparing for the right test case for years. Rosa Parks was a trained organizer and the secretary of the Montgomery NAACP. E. D. Nixon had been working on bus desegregation since the 1940s. Martin Luther King Jr. was selected as the boycott's spokesperson in part because, as a newcomer, he had not yet accumulated enemies. The boycott itself required a sophisticated alternative transportation system, sustained over more than a year, requiring fundraising, dispatching, mechanical maintenance, and constant negotiation.

Behind the public moments stood institutions. The black churches provided meeting space, communication networks, financial infrastructure, and moral authority. The NAACP provided legal expertise. The Highlander Folk School trained activists. The Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, the Congress of Racial Equality each brought distinct strategic capacities. Even when these organizations disagreed sharply—and they did, often bitterly—they constituted an ecology of mutually reinforcing institutions that could absorb setbacks, sustain morale, train new participants, and translate moral energy into legislative and judicial outcomes.

I emphasize this institutional reality because contemporary social movements, including Occupy, often define themselves in opposition to formal organization. The general assemblies, the consensus process, the refusal to designate leaders—these are presented as principled commitments to horizontal democracy. They have undeniable virtues: they make participation accessible, they distribute moral risk, they resist co-optation by ambitious individuals. But they also have costs. Movements without designated leaders cannot negotiate. Movements without institutional memory cannot learn from defeat. Movements without sustained organizational capacity cannot outlast the news cycle.

The civil rights movement was not afraid of leaders, and it was not afraid of institutions. It produced King, Bayard Rustin, Ella Baker, Fannie Lou Hamer, John Lewis, Diane Nash, James Lawson, Septima Clark, and many others. Each of them played distinct roles within a movement that had factions, debates, hierarchies, and chains of command. The movement also produced specific legislative demands—the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965—and organized methodically to achieve them, including by maintaining relationships with sympathetic figures inside the Democratic Party and the federal executive.

A second caution. The civil rights movement was successful in significant part because it was disciplined about violence, and disciplined about its public image. Nonviolence was not merely a tactic; it was a strategic commitment that required intensive training. SNCC's workshops drilled participants in how to absorb blows without responding, how to protect one another, how to maintain dignity under provocation. The result was a series of confrontations—Birmingham, Selma—in which the contrast between disciplined protesters and brutal repression created the moral conditions for federal intervention.

Occupy has not yet been tested in quite this way. There has been confrontation with police, sometimes brutal, and to its credit the movement has largely maintained nonviolent discipline. But it has not invested in the kind of systematic training that sustained the civil rights movement, and there are visible factions—particularly around tactics of property destruction in some West Coast actions—that could easily compromise the movement's broader appeal. Sustaining nonviolent discipline at scale is not automatic. It requires teaching, modeling, and sometimes the exclusion of those who refuse to abide by it.

Third, the civil rights movement was a long project. The Montgomery boycott was in 1955–56. The Civil Rights Act was in 1964, nine years later. The Voting Rights Act was in 1965. Real implementation of school desegregation, fair housing, and voting rights protections stretched well into the 1970s and beyond. The activists who started the movement could not know whether their efforts would yield results in five years or fifty. They committed anyway, building careers and lives around the work.

Occupy is two months old. It would be premature to render any verdict, positive or negative. What I would urge, from the vantage of the earlier movement, is patience, institution-building, and a willingness to learn from movements that came before. The principles invoked by the Boston Review's lead are correct. The hard work is in the institutions.

**Tahrir, Madrid, Zuccotti**

One of the most striking things about Occupy Wall Street is how American commentators have discussed it almost entirely in American terms. The Boston Review's lead essay is no exception: it situates Occupy within the American tradition of abolition, suffrage, and civil rights. That tradition matters, but it cannot account for what is actually happening on the ground in lower Manhattan and a thousand other places. Occupy is a node in a global wave, and any serious analysis has to begin there.

Consider the timeline. In December 2010, Mohamed Bouazizi set himself on fire in Tunisia. By January, the Tunisian government had fallen. By February, Hosni Mubarak had been forced from power in Egypt after the occupation of Tahrir Square. Bahrain, Yemen, Syria, Libya followed in various forms. In May, the indignados of the 15-M movement occupied Madrid's Puerta del Sol, taking direct inspiration from Tahrir and explicitly objecting to a political system they described as corrupted by financial interests. Athens has been in near-continuous protest since 2010 over austerity and the role of European financial institutions. By summer, the camps had spread across Europe, including major actions in Israel against housing costs. In September, Adbusters' call to occupy Wall Street drew on this transnational repertoire and explicitly invoked Tahrir.

To treat Occupy as an isolated American phenomenon is to miss what is most distinctive about it: the synchronous emergence, across very different political contexts, of a politics of square occupation, horizontal assembly, refusal of representational politics, and identification of finance and political corruption as the central enemies. This is not a coincidence. It reflects shared material conditions—the global financial crisis, the imposition of austerity on populations who did not cause the crisis, the ongoing transfer of resources from public to private hands under the guise of crisis management—and shared cultural infrastructure—social media, transnational youth networks, a generation educated for futures their economies cannot provide.

The American conversation, including the Boston Review's framing, sometimes treats this global context as background. I would argue it should be foreground. Several implications follow.

First, the diagnosis cannot be purely about American inequality. The countries where Occupy-like movements have flourished include some with much less internal inequality than the United States (Spain, Greece) and some with profoundly different political traditions (Egypt). What they share is not a domestic Gini coefficient but a particular relationship between national publics and a transnational financial order. Bondholders demand austerity; governments comply; populations are told that there is no alternative. The "1%" in this frame is not just the American rich; it is a global creditor class whose interests are protected by an international architecture of trade agreements, central bank policies, and conditionality.

Second, the strategic options available to American activists are different from those available in Tunisia or Egypt. The Tahrir occupation succeeded in toppling a dictator because the regime had limited democratic legitimacy and because the military ultimately declined to defend it. The American political system, however captured, is not Mubarak's Egypt. Occupation alone will not produce comparable institutional change, and pretending otherwise sets the movement up for disappointment. The American context requires the harder, slower work of contesting elections, building organizations, and changing laws within a system whose formal legitimacy is broadly accepted.

Third, the global frame should make American activists more humble and more curious. The Spanish indignados have been at this longer than Americans have, and they have generated sophisticated debates about the relationship between square occupations and political party formation. The Egyptian revolutionaries have learned painful lessons about what happens after the dictator falls and the organized forces—the military, the Muslim Brotherhood—reassert themselves. The Greek experience is instructive about how quickly mass mobilization can be drained by repeated cycles of austerity and broken promises. American activists should be reading and talking to their counterparts elsewhere, not reinventing wheels.

Fourth, there is a real opportunity in the global frame to build solidarities that have been mostly absent from American left politics. The trade-union movement has long resisted such solidarities, sometimes for understandable defensive reasons. But a politics that recognizes the international financial architecture as the common adversary opens possibilities for cooperation across borders that did not exist when the framework was purely about American jobs versus foreign jobs.

I do not want to overstate the analogy. American protesters are not facing live ammunition. The stakes for Egyptian or Syrian activists are incomparably higher than for those of us sleeping in Zuccotti. But the political imagination is shared, the tactics are shared, the analysis of who runs the world is increasingly shared. The Boston Review's series will be valuable if it sometimes lifts its eyes from the American context to see what the rest of the world has been doing for the past year. Occupy is part of something larger than itself. Understanding that something is the first step toward making it more effective.

**The Money Problem**

If I had to identify the single institutional failure that most clearly drives the gap between American principles and American practice—the gap the Boston Review's lead essay rightly identifies—it would be the role of money in our political system. Almost every other grievance Occupy has raised, from financial deregulation to tax inequity to the housing crisis to student debt, traces back in significant measure to the fact that organized money exerts disproportionate influence over American policymaking. Without addressing this, no other reform agenda is sustainable.

The empirical case is overwhelming. Political scientists have spent the last decade documenting what political donors and lobbyists have known forever: policy outcomes in the United States are far more responsive to the preferences of affluent citizens and organized interests than to the preferences of ordinary citizens. Larry Bartels's *Unequal Democracy*, Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson's *Winner-Take-All Politics*, Martin Gilens's work on policy responsiveness, Kay Lehman Schlozman and collaborators on inequality of political voice—the literature converges on a clear finding. When the preferences of upper-income Americans diverge from those of middle- and lower-income Americans, Congress votes with the upper-income preferences. This is true across both parties and across most issue domains.

How did we get here? Two trajectories converge. The first is the steady increase in the cost of political campaigns, driven by media expenses, professional consulting, and the lengthening of the campaign season. A House race that cost $87,000 on average in 1976 now costs well over $1.4 million for an incumbent. Senate races routinely cost tens of millions. To finance these campaigns, members of Congress now spend a startling portion of their working hours on the phone with donors. The structural pressure to attend to the concerns of high-dollar contributors is built into the daily schedule of legislators.

The second is the legal architecture surrounding political money, which the Supreme Court has progressively dismantled. *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) established the doctrine that money is speech, limiting Congress's ability to cap independent expenditures. *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) extended this logic to corporations and unions, holding that independent political expenditures by these entities cannot be limited. *SpeechNow v. FEC* (2010), decided in the wake of *Citizens United*, gave rise to Super PACs, which can accept unlimited contributions from any source as long as they do not formally coordinate with campaigns. The 2012 election cycle, now underway, is the first to fully feel the effects of this architecture, and the spending projections are unprecedented.

The result is a system in which the formal equality of citizens—one person, one vote—coexists with an extraordinary inequality in the political voice that money can purchase. The Boston Review essay invokes the principle that "everyone, not just the rich, should have a right to be heard when our country makes decisions." Under the current rules, this principle is not even approximately satisfied.

What is to be done? Several reform agendas are worth taking seriously.

The most direct response is a constitutional amendment to overturn *Citizens United* and clarify that Congress and states have authority to regulate political spending. This is a long project—amendments are difficult by design—but it is not hopeless. Public support for overturning *Citizens United* polls high across party lines. Several state legislatures have already passed resolutions calling for an amendment. Movements like Move to Amend are building the infrastructure for a sustained campaign.

Short of an amendment, public financing of elections offers the most promising path. Programs like the one in Connecticut, or the matching funds system in New York City, demonstrate that small-donor democracy is possible at scale. The Fair Elections Now Act, which has been introduced repeatedly in Congress, would extend a similar model to federal elections. Such systems do not eliminate private money, but they reduce candidates' dependence on it and open the door to candidates who lack access to wealthy networks.

Disclosure is another front. The DISCLOSE Act, which would require organizations engaging in political spending to identify their donors, has been blocked repeatedly but remains a key short-term reform. Voters cannot meaningfully evaluate political speech when they do not know who is funding it.

Reforming lobbying is harder but essential. The revolving door between Congress, executive agencies, and lobbying firms creates structural incentives for regulatory capture. Stronger restrictions on post-employment lobbying, more transparency about lobbying contacts, and meaningful funding for legislative staff capacity (so that legislators do not need to rely on lobbyists for substantive expertise) would all help.

I would urge Occupy and the Boston Review's series to keep this set of issues at the center. Inequality of wealth and inequality of voice are mutually reinforcing. You cannot effectively address the first without addressing the second, and the second is more tractable in the short run because it admits of specific procedural reforms that can be pursued through ordinary politics. Get the money out, or at least begin to, and the other agendas become realistic. Leave it in, and they will continue to founder.

Occupy's slogan is "we are the 99%." Right now, the political system does not respond to us as if we are. Changing that is job one.

**On the Trouble with "Principles"**

I want to take issue with the rhetorical frame of the Boston Review's lead essay rather than with any particular policy claim. The essay invites us to think about Occupy as an effort to bring "institutions into better alignment with" American "principles." This is a familiar move in American political discourse—perhaps the most familiar move—and it has both strengths and significant weaknesses worth examining.

The strength is rhetorical and tactical. Framing reform as faithful to the country's founding principles disarms the charge of radicalism and conscripts national mythology in favor of change. Frederick Douglass did this brilliantly in "What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?", indicting slavery in the name of the Declaration of Independence. King did it in "Letter from Birmingham Jail" and the "I Have a Dream" speech, demanding that America cash the promissory note its founders had signed. The move works because it allows reformers to claim continuity with the most sacred elements of national identity while pressing for transformation.

But the move has costs that we should be honest about, especially if we are going to use it ourselves.

First, the "principles" frame tends to be vague enough to accommodate almost any politics. The lead essay says Americans believe that "everyone, not just the rich, should have the opportunity to get ahead." This is a sentiment available to libertarians, social democrats, and authoritarian populists alike, depending on what "opportunity" turns out to mean operationally. The agreement at the level of principle does political work precisely by deferring the harder disagreements about institutional specifics. When the disagreements come, the principle alone will not resolve them, and we should not pretend it can.

Second, the appeal to founding principles flattens American history in ways that obscure how contested those principles always were. The Declaration spoke of equality while its author owned human beings. The Constitution's framers were deeply divided about democracy, and the document they produced was in significant part designed to constrain popular government. The franchise extension narrative passes over the violent suppression of Reconstruction, the disenfranchisement of black Southerners for most of the twentieth century, the long exclusion of Asian Americans, the continuing disenfranchisement of people convicted of felonies. "We" did not extend the franchise as a single national act of moral progress; portions of the country were violently dragged into compliance, and the work remains incomplete.

Treating American history as a story of principles slowly working themselves out can produce a kind of complacency. If the arc of history bends toward justice on its own, our task is just to nudge it along. But the arc bends toward justice, when it does, because specific people organize, fight, lose, regroup, and sometimes win. Many efforts fail. Many gains are reversed. The civil rights of the Reconstruction era were comprehensively dismantled within a generation; the labor protections of the New Deal have been substantially eroded over the past forty years. Progress is neither inevitable nor cumulative.

Third, the principles frame can make it harder to grapple honestly with the ways American institutions are not deviations from American principles but expressions of them. American capitalism was not an accident; it was a project, supported by figures from Hamilton onward, who understood the country as a commercial republic. The protections of property rights enshrined in the Constitution have been used since the early republic to limit redistributive politics. The Senate, the electoral college, judicial review—these are not malfunctions of American democracy; they are constitutive features of it, designed in part to prevent the kinds of majoritarian economic redistribution that Occupy would, on the most generous reading, ultimately favor.

A more honest framing might be: we are arguing for a different set of principles than the ones currently dominant, or for a different interpretation of contested ones. We are not just asking America to be true to itself. We are asking it to become something it has not been before—more egalitarian, more democratic, more responsive to the experiences of working people. There is no shame in this. The abolitionists were doing it. The suffragists were doing it. The labor movement was doing it. None of them were simply asking America to honor preexisting commitments; they were transforming what those commitments meant.

Fourth—and this is what I most want to press—the principles frame tends to obscure the question of power. Politics is not primarily about clarifying which principles we hold; it is about determining whose interests get served when interests conflict. Occupy's most important contribution to American political discourse is naming a structure of interests: the 1% versus the 99%. This is not a principles claim; it is a power claim. It says that a particular configuration of wealth and influence is producing outcomes that serve a small minority at the expense of a large majority. The remedy is not just better principles but redistributed power.

When the Boston Review series proceeds, I hope it will be willing to leave the language of principles behind at times and engage directly with the language of interests and power. Otherwise the conversation risks becoming an exercise in moral self-congratulation, where everyone agrees in principle and nothing actually changes.

**Channel the Energy**

I want to offer a friendly but somewhat impatient response to the Boston Review's framing essay. I share its sympathies. I think Occupy has been an enormously valuable intervention in American political discourse, and I think the questions the editors pose are the right questions. But I have a worry, which I will state bluntly: movements that do not translate into electoral and legislative outcomes within a reasonable window of time tend to dissipate, and the window for Occupy is closing faster than its participants seem to realize.

A bit of context. I have spent most of my adult life working in and around Democratic politics, including on several campaigns and at advocacy organizations. From that vantage, the past three years have been a wrenching experience. The Obama administration came into office with the largest Democratic majorities since the 1970s, in the context of the deepest economic crisis since the 1930s, with a popular mandate that ought to have permitted ambitious reform. What we got, in policy terms, was the Recovery Act (too small, but real), the Affordable Care Act (a genuine if compromised achievement), and Dodd-Frank (deeply inadequate to the financial sector's structural problems). The midterms then produced a House Republican majority elected in part by mobilizing populist anger that, in a more functional political ecology, would have flowed toward the administration rather than against it.

That populist energy did not flow leftward, in 2009 and 2010, in significant part because there was no organized left to receive it. The Tea Party, well-funded by Koch-aligned donors and amplified by Fox News, captured the energy first. Occupy, two years later, represents a belated effort to do on the left what the Tea Party did on the right. The question is whether it will be more than belated—whether it will succeed in shaping the 2012 election cycle and the policy environment that follows it.

To do so, the movement has to make some choices that, so far, it has resisted making.

First, it needs to articulate demands. I understand the principled objections to doing so. Demands can be co-opted; specific asks can be granted symbolically while the underlying structure is preserved; the diversity of the movement makes consensus on any specific platform difficult. All true. But demands are also how movements connect to the legislative process and how they signal to potential allies what they actually want. The civil rights movement had demands—a Civil Rights Act, a Voting Rights Act, federal protection of voter registration drives, ending of school segregation. The labor movement had demands—the right to organize, minimum wages, the eight-hour day, unemployment insurance. Demands focus political conflict in productive ways.

What might Occupy's demands look like? I would suggest a relatively compact set: restoration of Glass-Steagall or equivalent structural separation in banking; a financial transactions tax; closure of the carried-interest loophole; restoration of pre-2001 top marginal rates and elimination of the preferential rate on capital gains; meaningful relief on student debt and underwater mortgages; a constitutional amendment to permit regulation of campaign expenditures; public financing of federal elections. None of this is utopian. All of it is broadly popular. Most of it has been within mainstream American policy debate within the past generation.

Second, the movement needs to engage with electoral politics, however reluctantly. This does not mean endorsing the Democratic Party, which has been a complicit partner in many of the developments the movement opposes. But it does mean recognizing that legislation is made by elected officials, that elected officials are chosen in elections, and that staying outside the electoral process means leaving its outcomes to others. Specific tactics: identifying primary challengers to incumbents most beholden to financial interests; running insurgent candidates in winnable districts; mobilizing turnout in the 2012 cycle around financial reform and inequality themes; engaging with state-level legislative agendas where some of the most important policymaking now happens.

Third, the movement needs durable organization. The encampments cannot last forever, and as winter sets in, several have already been cleared by local authorities. What remains, beyond the physical occupations? If the answer is "the inspiration," that is not enough. The movement needs working groups that survive the camps, websites and communication infrastructure that persist, funding mechanisms that can support full-time organizers, training programs for new activists, and—yes—designated spokespeople who can engage with media and legislators on behalf of the broader movement. Horizontalism is appealing as an ethos, but successful movements have always combined participatory practices with durable infrastructure.

Fourth, the movement needs to think about coalitions. Labor unions have been the most visible institutional allies, and that relationship should be deepened.
