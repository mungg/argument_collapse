Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Sandel's distinction between the fairness and corruption objections is genuinely useful, and his core empirical claim—that markets are not morally inert—deserves the prominence he gives it. The Swiss nuclear-waste study and Titmuss's blood-donation work do seem to show that introducing prices into certain settings can shift the meaning of the transaction itself, sometimes perversely. But I want to press on two places where the argument runs faster than the evidence warrants.

First, the muscle-versus-commodity metaphor is doing too much work. Sandel is right that Arrow and Summers offer no real defense of the claim that altruism is depleted by use, and right that Aristotelian habituation is a serious alternative. But "virtues grow with exercise" is also a metaphor, and an under-specified one. Which virtues, exercised how, in which institutional settings? Parents who give freely to their children do not thereby become more generous to strangers; nationalist solidarity does not obviously generalize to cosmopolitan concern. There may be specific moral capacities that strengthen with practice and others that are domain-bounded or even subject to compassion fatigue. Without finer-grained claims, Sandel's metaphor risks being only the inverse of Arrow's—rhetorically more attractive, but no better grounded.

Second, the corruption argument needs an account of when commodification actually corrupts, and Sandel's examples slide between cases of quite different structure. The bought wedding toast is corrupted because it depends on deception: revealed, it disappoints precisely because the recipient assumed it expressed something it did not. That is a story about authenticity and false signals, not about money as such. The Swiss villagers' case is different: there, the offer of cash reframed a civic question as a market question, and the meaning of saying yes changed. The legacy-admissions case is different still—an institutional integrity problem about whose mission a university serves. Lumping these under "crowding out" obscures the distinct mechanisms, and therefore the distinct remedies. Some corruptions can be cured by transparency, others by norm enforcement, others only by refusing the market altogether.

This matters because, without that finer taxonomy, the corruption argument can be deployed against almost any market expansion a critic happens to dislike. Sandel rightly insists we deliberate about the meaning of goods, but deliberation requires criteria. Why does paying plasma donors seem tolerable to most when paying whole-blood donors seemed not to? Why do we accept paid childcare but recoil at paid adoption? Presumably because the goods at stake—what we take parenthood, citizenship, friendship to mean—differ in identifiable ways that a fuller theory should articulate.

None of this undoes Sandel's central provocation. The presumption that markets leave goods unchanged is indeed a faith, not a finding. But replacing it with a counter-faith in moral muscles is not yet the moral inquiry he rightly demands.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Michael Sandel’s meditation on the moral limits of markets is a timely and elegant provocation, but it risks overstating the case. His central claim—that market values “crowd out” non-market norms—rests on a series of carefully chosen vignettes that often conflate the crass commercialization of a good with its more thoughtful integration into market frameworks. While I share his unease about the creep of naked cash exchanges into intimate life, I worry that his argument slides too quickly from insightful critique to a blanket suspicion of markets that is neither wholly supported by evidence nor attentive to the ways commercial arrangements can coexist with, and even sustain, moral commitments.

Consider the wedding toast example. Sandel is surely right that discovering your best man bought a speech online would taint the moment. But is the problem the market, or the deception? If the best man explained that he lacked the gift of eloquence but wanted to honor you properly, and thus commissioned a speech that he then personally delivered with sincerity, would it still feel corrupt? Many of us already outsource expressions of care—buying a thoughtfully chosen card, hiring a musician for a celebration, or even employing a therapist to help us become better friends. The moral content of the act resides not in the refusal of all market mediation, but in the motives and transparency surrounding it. By focusing on the extreme case of a hidden purchase, Sandel stacks the deck against any commercial involvement in social life.

The nuclear waste siting study from Switzerland is more troubling for his thesis. The offer of cash did indeed reduce acceptance, and residents’ indignation at being “bribed” suggests a genuine crowding-out of civic spirit. But the interpretation is fragile. It may not be that the money itself corrupted the moral calculus; rather, the framing of the payment as an individual bribe undercut the collective deliberation that had already taken place. Had the compensation been designed as a community fund voted on through a public process, it might have enhanced the sense of shared sacrifice rather than displacing it. The lesson is not that markets always corrupt, but that the institutional design and narrative around payments matter immensely. A windfall payment to individual households felt like a buyout; a community chest for local schools or environmental monitoring might have felt like recognition of a burden nobly borne.

Sandel also leans heavily on Richard Titmuss’s classic comparison of blood donation systems, but by 2012 we have decades more experience. The United States maintains a mixed system—some paid plasma, heavily volunteer whole blood—without evidence that the paid plasma market has eroded the willingness to donate altruistically. In fact, a robust commercial plasma industry coexists with a strong culture of voluntary blood donation, suggesting that moral motivations are not a single, depletable resource. Separating the two spheres can even reinforce altruism by keeping it sheltered from scarcity pressures. The “muscle” analogy Sandel invokes is appealing but not ironclad; virtues can also be sustained by carving out protected domains where they are deliberately exercised, while markets handle the routine provision that might otherwise strain those virtues.

Ultimately, Sandel’s corruption argument serves best as a warning against thoughtless commodification, not as a general indictment of markets in morally freighted domains. Markets, like any social institution, can be designed with care for the values at stake. The real task is not to draw a bright line where markets must end, but to engage in the messy, democratic work of shaping market arrangements that respect, rather than dissolve, the norms we cherish.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Michael J. Sandel offers a compelling and eloquent diagnosis of a modern anxiety: the creeping encroachment of market values into spheres of life where they do not belong. His distinction between the "fairness objection" and the "corruption objection" provides a highly useful framework for understanding why we instinctively recoil at the idea of buying a friend or auctioning off college admissions. However, while Sandel rightly warns that commodification can degrade intimate and civic goods, his critique of economic reasoning ultimately misinterprets the structural role of markets in a complex society, particularly regarding the limits of human altruism.

Sandel takes particular aim at the economic assertion, articulated by Kenneth Arrow and Lawrence Summers, that altruism is a scarce resource that must be conserved. He counters with an appealing metaphor: altruism and civic virtue are not finite commodities like fossil fuels, but rather like muscles that grow stronger with exercise.

Yet, even if we accept the muscle metaphor, biological reality dictates that muscles suffer from fatigue and are inherently limited in their capacity. Evolutionary psychology and sociology strongly suggest that human empathy and altruism are bounded. We are highly capable of exercising the "muscle" of altruism toward our family, friends, and immediate community. But we struggle profoundly to extend that same selfless devotion to millions of unseen strangers. Economists do not necessarily believe that exercising virtue depletes a literal reservoir of it in the brain; rather, they recognize that altruism is fundamentally limited in its scope. Markets are indispensable because they allow us to coordinate, cooperate, and distribute resources globally without requiring us to summon a deep, muscular love for people we will never meet.

Consider Sandel’s citation of the Swiss nuclear waste study. It is true that introducing financial incentives can sometimes "crowd out" civic duty in local, cohesive communities. But how often can we rely on the pure civic sacrifice of a small village to solve national or global infrastructure problems? While civic virtue is a noble mechanism for a town of 2,100 people, it is rarely sufficient for the staggering logistical demands of a modern global economy.

Sandel also leans heavily on Richard Titmuss’s 1970 study of blood donation to argue that commercialization represses altruism. Yet, if we look at the broader landscape of medical science, the limits of the "gift relationship" become glaringly apparent. We do not rely on altruistic volunteerism to research, manufacture, and distribute pharmaceuticals, MRI machines, or surgical equipment. If we demanded that the entire medical supply chain run on the moral purity of the gift economy, we would face catastrophic shortages. The market mechanism, while lacking the warm moral glow of an unbought gift, reliably delivers life-saving goods because it aligns self-interest with public need.

Sandel is absolutely right that we must aggressively protect our intimate spheres from the cold logic of utility maximization. To monetize a wedding toast or a heartfelt gift is to strip it of its meaning. But as we debate the moral limits of markets, we must not romanticize our capacity for universal benevolence. Market mechanisms are not inherently corrosive simply because they bypass virtue; they are a necessary adaptation to the limits of our moral endurance, enabling mass human flourishing where altruism alone would inevitably fall short.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Michael Sandel is right to insist that markets are not morally inert. The most useful part of his argument is his attack on the lazy assumption that adding money to a practice merely adds an incentive, leaving everything else unchanged. We know from ordinary life that this is false. A dinner invitation, a wedding toast, a favor from a neighbor, even an apology can be transformed by payment. Economists themselves have helped show this: the well-known study of Israeli day-care centers found that fining parents for late pickup increased lateness, apparently because a moral obligation was recast as a price. Sandel’s warning is therefore not anti-economics. It is better economics, and better moral philosophy.

But Sandel sometimes writes as if “the market” carries one simple meaning wherever it appears: calculation, self-interest, and corruption. That is too quick. Money can crowd out moral norms, but it can also support them. A nurse’s wage does not necessarily corrupt care; a teacher’s salary does not make teaching less of a vocation; a paid minister, musician, or therapist may still act from commitment as well as compensation. The question is not whether money is present, but what role it plays and what social meaning surrounds it. Payment can define a relationship, or it can make possible the time, training, and stability that allow nonmarket values to flourish.

This distinction matters most in hard cases such as organs, blood, and care work. Titmuss’s comparison of British and American blood systems remains a powerful challenge to market triumphalism. Yet even here the lesson is not simply “payment bad, donation good.” Blood safety depends on testing, screening, and institutions as well as donor motivation. Plasma markets operate differently from whole-blood donation. And organ transplantation presents an especially tragic tradeoff. In the United States today, tens of thousands wait for kidneys, and thousands die each year before receiving one. To say that selling a kidney may degrade the body is morally serious. But so is allowing preventable death while black markets and transplant tourism exploit the desperate in far worse conditions.

A regulated system of compensation for kidney donors might still be wrong. But it should not be dismissed by analogy to bought friendship. We could imagine a public agency, not an open auction, offering fixed compensation, medical counseling, waiting periods, lifelong health coverage, and allocation by medical need rather than ability to pay. Such a system would not treat kidneys exactly like used cars. It would try to combine reciprocity with limits, gratitude with protection, and supply with justice. Perhaps even that would corrupt the meaning of bodily integrity. But the argument has to compare real alternatives, not purity against markets in the abstract.

Sandel’s best point is that democratic citizens should debate the purposes of goods and institutions. Universities should ask whether admissions preferences for donors betray learning. Communities should ask whether cash payments for waste sites buy consent rather than earn it. Schools should ask whether paying children for grades teaches curiosity or merely compliance. But democratic debate should also recognize pluralism. Citizens disagree, reasonably, about whether a practice is degraded by money. In such cases, prohibition can impose one moral meaning by law, while also producing scarcity, hypocrisy, or underground exchange.

The real task is not to keep markets everywhere out, nor to invite them everywhere in. It is to design boundaries. Some things should not be sold at all: votes, verdicts, persons, public offices. Some may be paid for only under strict rules: medical research participation, military service, reproductive labor, organ donation if we choose that path. Some are not corrupted by payment but by bad institutions. Markets do crowd out morals when we let price become the whole language of value. But morals can also guide, discipline, and humanize markets—if we refuse both market fundamentalism and moral nostalgia.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Sandel is right that markets are not mere neutral mechanisms, and his account of the corruption objection is the most persuasive part of the essay. When money enters certain domains—whether wedding toasts, organ exchange, or admissions at elite universities—it does not merely supplement existing norms but can displace them. The Swiss nuclear waste study is particularly valuable, showing that a financial offer to accept a local burden actually reduced civic cooperation rather than increasing it. Sandel reads this correctly: the offer was perceived as an attempt to purchase consent, which bypassed the deliberative relationship that might have produced genuine acceptance. This is a serious cost, and it is one that standard economic analysis ignores when it focuses only on price effects.

Where I want to push back is on the sweeping quality of the argument. Sandel suggests that markets are systematically crowding out non-market norms across a wide range of social practices. But the crowding-out effect he describes depends heavily on context, and not all cases fit the pattern equally well. The nuclear waste scenario works as it does because civic acceptance of a shared risk is constitutively a matter of collective deliberation. Offering money substitutes a market transaction for a democratic one, and the two are incommensurable. But this is a special case. Many market transactions do not replace deliberative relationships; they replace nothing at all, or they replace inefficient or coercive non-market arrangements. The question is not whether markets can crowd out norms worth preserving but whether they do so inevitably, as Sandel's framing sometimes implies.

On the debate between Titmuss and Arrow, Sandel's critique of the economistic conception of virtue is philosophically compelling but sociologically incomplete. The metaphor of virtue as a muscle that grows stronger with use is appealing, but it does not settle the empirical question of whether altruism is depleted or replenished under particular institutional conditions. There is evidence for both directions, and Sandel offers little to adjudicate between them. He is right that Arrow's view of virtue as a scarce resource to be conserved is strange, but the alternative—that virtue reliably expands with practice—is equally underargued.

The most serious limitation of Sandel's essay is the thinness of his account of fairness. He explicitly sets the fairness objection aside to focus on corruption, but these arguments are not easily separated. Many of the cases he discusses—organ sales, sweatshop labor, admissions—involve both corruption and severe background inequality, and attending to corruption alone can obscure how structural inequality shapes what counts as a voluntary market choice. Sandel's essay is a valuable contribution to thinking about market limits, but its framework works best when applied to honorific and relational goods, and it requires supplementing when it meets the harder cases where economic desperation and moral corruption run together.

**The Economist's Reply: Sandel Mistakes Sociology for Argument**

Michael Sandel's essay is graceful and accessible, but it papers over a serious analytical problem with rhetorical questions and well-chosen anecdotes. The core move is this: Sandel observes that introducing money into certain transactions sometimes changes how people feel about them, and concludes that markets "corrupt" or "crowd out" non-market values. But noticing that an attitude shifts is not the same as showing that the shift is bad. Sandel owes us an argument, and he largely declines to give one.

Take the Swiss nuclear waste case, which Sandel treats as a damning indictment of market reasoning. The villagers were willing to accept the dump out of civic duty, but withdrew consent when compensation was offered. Sandel reads this as evidence that money "crowded out" a noble public-spiritedness. But there is an equally plausible reading: the offer of payment provided the villagers with new information. A government that previously framed the issue as a shared sacrifice was now suggesting—through the very act of offering compensation—that the burden was substantial enough to require purchase. The villagers may have reasonably updated their estimate of the risks, or concluded that if the state was willing to pay, they should bargain harder. The Frey and Oberholzer-Gee finding is real and interesting, but it does not by itself license Sandel's moralized interpretation.

Or take the wedding toast. Sandel asks whether you would feel differently if your best man had paid for the words he delivered. Most of us would. But "most of us would feel funny about it" is the beginning of moral inquiry, not its end. We also feel funny when our parents hire a tutor rather than teaching us themselves, when politicians read speeches written by staff, when doctors consult UpToDate rather than relying on memory. The fact that we have intuitions about authenticity does not mean those intuitions should govern public policy or should be elevated into a general theory of what markets corrupt.

The deeper problem is that Sandel never specifies what counts as the "constitutive norms" of a practice. He gestures at friendship, at higher education, at civic life, and asserts that markets degrade them. But practices evolve. The "constitutive norms" of marriage once excluded romantic love, included dowries, and treated wives as property. The norms of higher education once excluded women, Jews, and Catholics. Were the reformers who changed these institutions "corrupting" them? Sandel's framework offers no way to distinguish a corruption from a beneficial reform, because it treats whatever norms happen to be in place as morally privileged.

This is conservative in the technical sense: it grants standing to incumbent practices simply because they exist. And the consequences are not trivial. Sandel waves at the kidney market without quite endorsing or opposing it, but if his framework means anything, it means we should ban paid organ donation. The result is that thousands of people die each year on waiting lists while their potential donors—people who would freely choose to sell a kidney for life-changing sums—are forbidden from doing so. To say that markets in organs are "degrading" while the actual people involved suffer and die requires more than aesthetic discomfort. It requires an argument that the alleged corruption outweighs the very concrete harms of prohibition. Sandel doesn't make that argument.

I will grant Sandel one substantive point. Kenneth Arrow and Lawrence Summers do go too far when they treat altruism as a strictly finite resource. The empirical evidence on whether moral motivations are depleted or strengthened by exercise is genuinely mixed. Sandel is right that the "altruism as commodity" metaphor is doing too much work in the economic literature. But the response is not to swing fully to Aristotle and assume virtues grow with practice in every domain. The honest answer is that we don't know, and the answer probably varies by context. In some settings, payment crowds out intrinsic motivation; in others (think of paying nurses), it sustains the work that allows virtue to operate.

The proper attitude toward markets is empirical and case-by-case. Where introducing prices destroys information, undermines reciprocity, or genuinely changes the meaning of an exchange in ways that participants themselves would regret on reflection, restrict them. Where prohibition merely satisfies the moral aesthetics of professors at the cost of real welfare for poor people, lift the restriction. Sandel's essay confuses the first kind of case with the second by treating discomfort as evidence. Discomfort is data; it is not yet a verdict.

**The Missing Politics of Commodification**

Sandel's essay performs a valuable public service by reopening a question that mainstream economics had successfully ruled out of bounds: what should and should not be for sale? For a generation, "everything has a price" has been treated less as a hypothesis than as a precondition for serious policy analysis. Sandel pushes back, and he is right to do so. But his treatment, for all its elegance, suffers from a curious depoliticization. By framing the debate as a contest between "the corruption objection" and "the fairness objection," and then explicitly siding with the former, Sandel removes from view the actual political economy that has made commodification such an urgent issue in our time.

Consider the timing. Sandel is writing in 2012, four years after a financial crisis triggered by the wholesale conversion of mortgages—previously embedded in long-term relationships between bankers and borrowers—into tradable securities. He is writing during a wave of austerity in Europe, in the middle of debates about the privatization of public education in the United States, amid the explosive growth of for-profit colleges preying on veterans and the poor. The question of "where markets belong" is not, at this moment, a philosophical puzzle about wedding toasts. It is a political question about which actors get to enclose which commons.

Sandel mentions these stakes only obliquely. His chosen examples—the bought toast, the cash gift, the legacy admission—are all individual-level transactions. They invite the reader to consult her intuitions about etiquette and intimacy. They do not invite her to ask who benefits when public utilities become private monopolies, or when the security of old age becomes a portfolio of equities to be managed by financial intermediaries. The corruption frame, taken alone, makes commodification look like a problem of cultural decline rather than a problem of power.

This matters because Sandel's own examples often turn out, on inspection, to be about power as much as about meaning. Take legacy admissions. Sandel admits that the fairness and corruption objections both apply but says his focus is corruption. Yet what is actually objectionable about a Harvard donor purchasing a seat for his mediocre son? Is it the metaphysical degradation of the diploma, or is it that the children of the rich are buying their way past the children of the poor, locking in hereditary advantage in a society that calls itself meritocratic? The corruption framing makes this sound like a sin against the Platonic Form of the University. The fairness framing makes it sound like what it actually is: class war, conducted by means of the admissions office.

Sandel might reply that fairness arguments are well-canvassed and that his contribution lies in articulating the under-appreciated corruption argument. Fair enough. But by setting them aside, he risks endorsing a politics of taste in which what matters most about market encroachment is how it makes thoughtful people feel. The genuinely radical question—who is doing the encroaching, and at whose expense—gets muted.

There is also a class dimension to Sandel's examples that deserves notice. The wedding-toast purchaser, the gift-card recipient, the parent buying legacy admission—these are problems of the comfortable. The actual frontier of commodification in 2012 looks quite different: payday lending in poor neighborhoods, the sale of plasma by the desperate, the privatization of water systems in Latin American cities, gestational surrogacy in India. In each of these, the corruption objection and the fairness objection are not really separable. What corrupts the practice is precisely that it is offered as a choice to people who cannot meaningfully refuse it.

This is why Titmuss remains a more politically incisive thinker than Sandel acknowledges. Titmuss did not just argue that paid blood was an aesthetic degradation of a gift relationship. He showed that paid blood systems systematically extract from the poor for the benefit of the affluent, and that they produce contaminated supplies precisely because the financial incentive selects for desperate donors with disease risks. The corruption was inseparable from the inequality.

Sandel's call for democratic deliberation about market boundaries is welcome. But deliberation requires that we name the actors and interests at stake, not just the norms in jeopardy. To ask "how we want to live together" is the right question. The wrong move is to answer it as if the obstacle were merely confused thinking among economists, rather than organized power among those whose fortunes depend on selling what should not be sold. Markets do not crowd out morals on their own. They are pushed.

**In Defense of Cash, Gifts, and the Limits of Sandel's Intuitions**

Michael Sandel is a brilliant lecturer and a humane critic of contemporary capitalism, and there is much in his essay to commend. But I want to defend something he treats with gentle condescension: the practice of giving cash, the economist's instinct that signaling matters, and more broadly the suspicion that many of our intuitions about "corruption" reflect parochial cultural prejudices rather than universal moral truths.

Start with cash gifts. Sandel quotes Waldfogel's "Scroogenomics" with the air of someone exhibiting a curiosity, and dismisses Mankiw's signaling account as "strangely wooden." But the case for cash is stronger than Sandel allows, and the disdain for it is not nearly as universal as he implies. In China, the red envelope of cash is the central gift exchange of the most important holiday of the year. In Jewish tradition, bar mitzvah gifts of money in multiples of eighteen carry rich symbolic meaning. In Korean and Japanese weddings, guests bring cash in carefully calibrated amounts. None of these cultures believes that cash "diminishes" the relationship. The Western middle-class taboo against cash gifts is a recent and local convention, not a deliverance of moral reason.

This matters because Sandel's whole argumentative method depends on intuitions about "constitutive norms." A practice has its own internal logic, he tells us, and money corrupts that logic. But which practice, and whose logic? Sandel takes for granted that a wedding toast must be the spontaneous outpouring of a close friend's heart. Yet professional speechwriters have ghostwritten orations for centuries. Cicero wrote speeches for clients. Hired eulogists were standard in the Roman world. Many wedding traditions across cultures have featured professional praise-singers, paid griots, or trained performers. The notion that authenticity requires that the speaker compose his own words is a Romantic-era convention dressed up as timeless moral truth.

Once we notice how culturally contingent Sandel's intuitions are, the corruption argument starts to look less like moral philosophy and more like a sophisticated defense of a particular bourgeois sensibility. Sandel never grapples seriously with the possibility that the people whose intuitions he is consulting—educated, affluent, mostly Western readers of Boston Review and Harvard students—are not a reliable jury. They are, in fact, exactly the demographic most likely to share a particular set of aesthetic preferences about gift-giving, about higher education, about the marketplace of speech.

Now consider the signaling account that Sandel mocks. He says love is "a way of being with and responding to another person," not "private information that one party reports to the other." This is a false dichotomy. Of course love is more than information transfer. But it also involves communication, and communication involves the credible conveyance of states that the other party cannot directly observe. When my partner spends time choosing a gift she knows I'll like, she is doing something that demonstrates attention—and the demonstration matters precisely because it would be costly to fake. This is exactly Mankiw's point, and it is not wooden but illuminating. It explains why we want gifts that took thought rather than gifts that took only money: not because money is metaphysically polluted, but because money is too easy. The cost has to be the right kind of cost.

This signaling perspective also explains why monetary gifts work fine in contexts where the relationship is well-established and the signal is unnecessary—between grandparents and grandchildren, say, or in cultures with fixed conventions about wedding cash. The signal of effort is needed precisely when the relationship is being courted, tested, or affirmed in front of others. Mankiw's wooden account turns out to track the data better than Sandel's expressive one.

Where does this leave us? I want to be clear: I do not think markets belong everywhere. Sandel is right that there are domains—organ sales, perhaps; certainly the auctioning of political offices and judicial verdicts—where commodification corrodes something important. But the line cannot be drawn by polling the intuitions of well-educated Westerners and treating the result as moral data. It has to be drawn by asking what specific harms commodification produces, what specific goods are served by prohibition, and whose preferences and discomforts are being privileged in the answer.

Sandel's essay invites us to renew our public moral discourse. I agree. But part of that renewal should involve interrogating our own moralized intuitions about money with at least as much skepticism as we apply to the economist's. Cash is not tacky. Hired words are not lies. And the suspicion of markets, however congenial to my own politics, deserves better defenders than aesthetes who mistake their refinement for ethics.

Michael Sandel has placed his finger on a deep unease many of us feel in contemporary life: the steady conversion of relationships, honors, and civic duties into commodities. His argument that markets “crowd out” non-market norms is compelling, but I want to push it further. The corrosion he identifies is not only a matter of diminished personal virtues or corrupted social practices; it poses a direct threat to democratic self-government. When market thinking colonizes every sphere, it changes the way citizens understand their role in a shared political project, and that, in turn, hollows out the public realm.

Sandel points to the Swiss waste-siting study as evidence that monetary offers can displace civic spirit. That example is telling, but its full significance becomes clearer when we connect it to a broader trend: the increasing reliance on market-like mechanisms to distribute public goods and make collective decisions. School vouchers, pollution permits, congestion pricing, pay-for-performance in public services—these may seem like sensible efficiency measures, but they train us to see ourselves as consumers of government rather than as authors of our common life. If every interaction with the state is a transaction, then the virtues of deliberation, compromise, and shared sacrifice atrophy. We stop asking “What do we owe one another?” and start asking “What’s in it for me?”

Sandel’s corruption objection, which focuses on the degradation of goods and practices, can thus be extended to the practice of citizenship itself. Democracy, like friendship or academic integrity, is not a mere preference-satisfaction machine. It depends on norms of mutual respect, a willingness to be persuaded, and an orientation toward the common good. When market values dominate, these norms are displaced by instrumental reasoning. Political campaigns become advertising wars; public discourse becomes a clash of interest groups; and voters come to view the ballot box as a shopping cart.
