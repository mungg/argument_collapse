Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Kate Soper's essay performs an important service simply by refusing the standard environmentalist posture of grim sacrifice. For decades green politics has been hobbled by an implicit Augustinian script — we have sinned through consumption, and must do penance through restraint — which has handed corporations and their political clients an easy rhetorical victory. Soper is right that the actually existing affluent lifestyle is in many respects miserable: time-starved, sedentary, anxiety-saturated, mediated by surveillance advertising and stitched together by Amazon Prime. To name the consumer treadmill itself as the killjoy, rather than its critics, is a clarifying move. And the empirical record on hedonic adaptation, the Easterlin paradox, and the documented gulf between hours worked and reported well-being lends her case more support than skeptics usually concede.

Yet for an argument so insistent on its own realism, "Alternative Hedonism" is curiously evasive about politics. Soper anchors her case in what, borrowing from Raymond Williams, she calls a "structure of feeling" — a nascent intuition that something has gone wrong. But Williams used that phrase to describe sensibilities legible in literature and cultural production; Soper deploys it to do heavy lifting as a political constituency. The evidence offered for this latent post-consumerism is thin: some growth in Degrowth and Next System circles, the popularity of certain NGOs, and pandemic-era shifts whose durability is unclear. Meanwhile, the same period has produced an enormous backlash against fuel taxes (the gilets jaunes), surging SUV sales, a post-lockdown revenge-travel boom, and electoral rewards for politicians promising cheaper petrol. If a structure of feeling exists, it is at present vastly outweighed by a structure of preference that points the opposite direction. Soper acknowledges this in passing — "the absence to date of any serious consumer revolt" — but does not let it discipline her optimism.

The agency question compounds the problem. Soper writes that "concerned consumers in more affluent societies must lead the way." But the affluent consumer is precisely the figure whose tastes are most thoroughly produced by the system in question, and whose carbon footprint is most catastrophic. Asking the top decile — responsible, as Soper notes, for over half of global emissions between 1990 and 2015 — to serve as the moral vanguard of degrowth is not obviously more plausible than asking financiers to lead the charge against finance. The class that does the most flying, the most driving, and the most second-home buying is unlikely to discover, through gentle cultural persuasion, that it secretly prefers allotments and cycle rickshaws. Some will, of course; the question is whether enough will, fast enough, and without the kind of coercive policy instruments Soper mostly leaves unnamed.

Jayati Ghosh's interjection in the essay — that peasants across much of the world would find the idea of reducing the workweek laughable — deserves more weight than Soper gives it. Her reply, that growth is needed "in the short term" in the poorest nations, gestures at the issue without engaging it. Roughly three billion people still lack access to clean cooking fuels; hundreds of millions remain without reliable electricity. The infrastructure required to bring them to a decent standard of living is energy- and material-intensive, and "regenerative and distributive" framings, however attractive in Kate Raworth's idiom, do not by themselves resolve the arithmetic. There is also a deeper conceptual difficulty: artisanal slow work, foregoing fast travel, walking instead of driving, growing one's own food — these are recognizable as pleasures partly because they have become rare and optional for affluent Westerners. For the smallholder in Bihar or the domestic worker in Manila, "slow working" is not a baroque utopia but a description of grinding life. The aestheticization of pre-industrial labor has a long and uncomfortable lineage in Northern thought, from Ruskin onward, and Soper does not quite avoid its gravitational pull.

There is also a missing mechanism. The shorter workweek and the universal basic income, presented almost as adjuncts in Soper's exposition, are in fact the load-bearing walls of her vision: without them, the cultural shift she describes is unreachable, since most people lack the disposable time and security to consume differently. But UBI plus a four-day week, on anything like a globally meaningful scale, requires confronting the structure of capital ownership, the tax base, and the international monetary order in ways that go well beyond a "structure of feeling." Soper endorses the Debt for Climate campaign, which is to her credit, but the political program implied — debt cancellation, reparations, decommodification of large sectors, planned reduction of throughput — is closer to ecosocialism than to a hedonic reorientation of consumer desire. The cultural project and the political project are not the same, and conflating them risks letting the easier one substitute for the harder.

None of this means Soper is wrong; it means she is offering less than the situation requires. Her contribution is to refuse the false choice between affluence-as-we-know-it and ascetic sacrifice, and to insist that a better life is materially and psychologically available within ecological limits. That is a necessary corrective to the dominant framing. But the path from cultural critique to political transformation runs through institutions and conflicts that "Alternative Hedonism" mostly leaves in shadow. Without a more concrete account of who fights whom, with what tools, on whose behalf, the alternative hedonist risks becoming what the consumerist already is: a consumer of agreeable imagery, browsing utopias in the comfortable knowledge that the present will continue.

Kate Soper’s call for an “alternative hedonism” arrives at a moment of profound unease. After a pandemic that briefly suspended the routines of consumption, and as war in Europe sends energy prices spiraling, the question of what constitutes a good life is no longer merely philosophical. Soper’s diagnosis that consumerist affluence frequently fails to deliver on its promise of happiness is a welcome antidote to the fatalism that treats ever-escalating material throughput as the only horizon of political possibility. Too often, even environmentalists frame necessary changes as sacrifices rather than as openings to more fulfilling ways of living. By insisting that a post-growth future could be richer in genuine pleasure—in slower travel, convivial streets, craft work, and time for relationships—Soper challenges the monopoly that advertising holds over the imagination of desire. Yet for all its rhetorical appeal, her vision rests on a fragile political and economic foundation, one that underestimates how deeply the present order structures people’s capacities to choose differently, and how unequally the so-called pleasures of “simpler” living are distributed across class and geography.

The central difficulty is that Soper’s alternative hedonism presumes a latent “structure of feeling” that is already widespread in affluent societies—an inchoate yearning for something other than the work-and-spend treadmill. But the empirical evidence for such a shift remains thin. While surveys show growing anxiety about climate change, they do not show a corresponding willingness to embrace lower-consumption lifestyles, especially among those whose hold on comfort is already precarious. In the United States and much of Europe, inflation in essentials such as food, housing, and fuel is intensifying economic insecurity. For working-class families, the “convenience of Walmart and Amazon” is not an ironic pleasure to be relinquished in favor of artisanal slow living, but a genuine lifeline that stretches tight budgets. Telling such households that they might enjoy walking to the shops or mending clothes more than they realize sounds, at best, like the counsel of those insulated from scarcity. Soper is right that consumerism is itself a regime that coerces everyone, even the poor, into its logic. But the escape from that regime requires material resources—time, education, social networks, and a cushion of wealth—that are unevenly distributed. Without a massive redistribution of both income and power, calls for reduced consumption will continue to sound like austerity for the many while a mobile elite curates its low-carbon pleasures.

Soper’s treatment of the global dimension compounds this problem. She acknowledges that the poorest nations need temporary growth and that the rich world owes a climate debt, yet she moves quickly to portray indigenous and traditional modes of production as the real “progressive” models. This romanticization risks dismissing the legitimate aspirations of billions of people who seek precisely the modern infrastructure—reliable electricity, refrigeration, decent housing, motorized transport—that has transformed life expectancy and opportunity in the Global North. As the climate economist Kate Raworth and others have emphasized, the goal is not to halt development but to compress the resource throughput associated with it, which means that richer countries must drastically shrink their material footprint while allowing poorer countries the ecological space to expand theirs. Soper’s alternative hedonism remains oddly silent on the technological and industrial transformations that might make such a convergence possible. She notes the promise of “smartest, greenest technologies” in passing but is far more interested in the revival of artisanal production and the pleasures of non-market exchange. Yet without a massive, state-led deployment of renewables, electrification, and circular industrial systems, the shift she advocates will never reach the scale necessary to matter for the climate. By downplaying the productive side of the equation, she cedes too much ground to those who argue that degrowth means a return to premodern privation.

Politically, Soper’s vision is strangely weightless. She gestures toward a universal basic income, a shorter working week, and a revised educational system, but offers no account of the power struggles required to realize any of these. The fossil fuel industry, automakers, agribusiness, and the advertising complex will not voluntarily dismantle the consumerist order because a few more people discover the joys of cycling. The post-pandemic rush to restore “normal” consumption, the explosion of private car use as people avoided public transit, and governments’ panicked subsidies for gasoline suggest that the system’s default response to crisis is to double down on the very practices Soper wishes to supplant. A structure of feeling, however appealing as a cultural diagnosis, does not organize workers, win elections, or break corporate monopolies. The degrowth movement has so far remained marginal precisely because it has not built a durable mass constituency. Soper’s essay, for all its eloquence, exemplifies the movement’s tendency to substitute moral appeal for strategic thinking about power.

Perhaps most troubling is the implicit assumption that the pleasures of slower living are self-evidently superior once people are “freed” from the consumerist spell. But that claim demands interrogation. The thrill of speed, the convenience of a car, the fantasy of fashion, and the novelty of distant travel are not simply false needs implanted by manipulative marketers; they answer to genuine human desires for autonomy, variety, and distinction. A politics of alternative hedonism that denies this, or that dismisses such pleasures as merely the products of a hedonic treadmill, risks coming across as puritanical after all. It is one thing to point out that more stuff does not correlate neatly with greater life satisfaction; it is another to prescribe which forms of pleasure deserve a place in the good life. Soper insists she does not want to moralize, but her essay is full of judgments about what is genuinely fulfilling (walking, gardening, conversation) and what is hollow (shopping, fast travel, gadgets). The danger is that this becomes yet another register of cultural distinction, a way for the ecologically concerned middle class to signal its virtue while leaving the structures of inequality intact.

This is not to reject the core of Soper’s project. She is right that the climate crisis demands a new politics of prosperity, one that de-links well-being from endless material accumulation. She is right, too, that many people in affluent societies are exhausted, lonely, and starved for time, and that some of the remedies she proposes—walkable cities, a shorter workweek, public spaces for conviviality—would improve lives across the board. But for her alternative hedonism to become more than a literary exercise, it must be embedded in a robust political economy. That means taking on the concentrations of wealth and corporate power that lock us into high-carbon pathways, not simply hoping that a “structure of feeling” will swell into a democratic majority. It means building coalitions between trade unions, climate activists, and tenants’ movements that can fight for public luxury and shared infrastructure, rather than leaving people to find their private satisfactions in the interstices of a still-dominant capitalism. And it means articulating a vision of the good life that is not afraid to promise abundance—of clean energy, of care, of leisure, of connection—not just for the few who can already afford to step off the treadmill, but for everyone. Without such a politics, the pleasures Soper celebrates will remain, at best, a consolation for the enlightened few while the many are told to tighten their belts for the planet.

Kate Soper’s “For an Alternative Hedonism” offers a seductive critique of our contemporary economic treadmill. By framing the transition away from carbon-intensive capitalism not as a sacrifice but as a liberation into a better, richer life, she neatly sidesteps the politically toxic language of austerity. There is undeniable truth in her diagnosis: our hyper-commodified culture breeds anxiety, isolation, and ecological ruin. However, while her diagnosis of spiritual and environmental exhaustion is acute, her prescription leans heavily on a romanticized vision of pre-industrial rhythms that cannot sustain a modern global population. By prioritizing “artisanal” production and degrowth over sustainable abundance, Soper’s alternative hedonism risks becoming a boutique lifestyle for the privileged rather than a viable political economy for the masses.

The core vulnerability in Soper’s vision lies in its material mechanics. She advocates for the reintroduction of traditional artisanal modes of production and slower, more localized economies, dismissing profit-driven efficiency. But efficiency in production is not merely a capitalist trick to maximize profit; it is the mechanism by which basic goods are made universally accessible. Artisanal production is inherently labor-intensive and low-yield. If we abandon economies of scale in agriculture, textiles, and basic manufacturing, the cost of everyday necessities will inevitably skyrocket. In a world of eight billion people, returning to closed-loop craft production would not usher in an era of egalitarian leisure; it would recreate a society where only a fraction of the population can afford bespoke, sustainably crafted goods, while the rest are forced into backbreaking labor just to survive.

This structural reality fatally undermines Soper’s accompanying social proposals. She envisions a three- or four-day workweek paired with a Universal Basic Income (UBI), allowing citizens to pursue the humanities, civic engagement, and local conviviality. But a robust UBI and a drastically reduced labor force require a massive economic surplus. How does a society generate the immense material wealth needed to support a non-working populace if it has dismantled its most efficient industrial supply chains in favor of slow, artisanal craft? Historically, the reduction of human working hours and the expansion of leisure were made possible precisely by the technological leaps and mass-production models Soper critiques. We cannot simultaneously abandon high-yield industrial efficiency and expect to maintain the surplus that makes widespread leisure possible.

Rather than retreating into a localized, slower economy, a truly emancipatory green politics must be rooted in abundance. As we face the cascading crises of 2022, the solution to the climate emergency is not to demand that people artificially restrict their horizons, but to rapidly build out the infrastructure for clean, limitless energy. A future powered by massive investments in solar, wind, and next-generation nuclear power can decouple economic prosperity from carbon emissions. This "green abundance" framework acknowledges that technological development and high-capacity production are not the enemy; fossil fuels are. By electrifying our grid and automating mundane labor, we can actually achieve the free time Soper desires without imposing a regime of material scarcity.

Soper is entirely correct that we need a new definition of the “good life,” one that values time, community, and the natural world over the endless accumulation of disposable plastics. But building that world requires more than a shift in our “structure of feeling.” It requires immense technological and industrial coordination to provide housing, healthcare, transit, and food for billions of people sustainably. Alternative hedonism, with its focus on slow travel and mending old clothes, offers a beautiful aesthetic for those who already have their basic material needs met. Yet, as a structural response to global climate change and inequality, it falls short. True liberation will not be found in slowing down to craft our own goods, but in deploying human ingenuity to create an ecologically sustainable abundance that everyone can share.

Kate Soper is right to insist that climate politics has too often spoken in the register of guilt, sacrifice, and emergency while leaving the imagination of pleasure to advertisers. A movement that asks people only to renounce will lose to one that promises ease, status, novelty, and belonging—even when those promises are counterfeit. The strongest part of her case is therefore not the familiar indictment of consumer capitalism, but the more subversive claim that much of what passes for affluence is already a bad bargain: long commutes, anxious status competition, processed food eaten in haste, children unable to roam, elderly people isolated, streets surrendered to cars, homes filled with disposable goods, and work organized less around usefulness than around profitability.

That argument deserves to be central to climate politics. The question is whether “alternative hedonism” can become more than a moral sensibility or lifestyle critique. To do so, it must be translated into institutions that make low-carbon pleasures ordinary, cheap, and secure. Otherwise it risks becoming another burden placed on individuals who are already time-poor, cash-poor, and infrastructure-poor.

Take travel. It is true that car culture has imposed enormous costs: air pollution, traffic deaths, noise, land consumption, and the erosion of public space. In the United States, tens of thousands die in road crashes each year; globally, outdoor air pollution kills millions. Many people do not experience driving as freedom so much as compulsion. But they are compelled because housing, work, schools, shops, and health care have been built around the car. Telling them that walking, cycling, trains, and buses are more pleasurable will ring hollow if the bus comes once an hour, the bike lane disappears at the intersection, rents near jobs are unaffordable, and the train is slower and more expensive than a flight.

The same is true of consumption. Mending, sharing, borrowing, gardening, cooking, and buying secondhand can be deeply satisfying. Yet they require time, space, skills, and social trust. A parent working two jobs may buy fast food not because she has been hypnotized by capitalism but because the supermarket is far away, the kitchen is inadequate, the children are hungry now, and exhaustion is real. The alternative pleasure must be made materially available: shorter working hours, better wages, public kitchens and libraries of things, safe parks, local shops, repair rights, durable products, and housing that allows community rather than merely warehousing people.

This is where Soper’s essay would benefit from a sharper distinction between private consumption and public abundance. Some forms of consumption plainly need to contract in rich countries: frequent flying, oversized vehicles, fast fashion, meat-heavy diets, disposable electronics, and the planned obsolescence that turns the earth into a supply chain at one end and a dump at the other. But other forms of provision need to expand dramatically: renewable electricity, insulation, heat pumps, public transit, care services, parks, schools, clinics, clean water systems, and cultural institutions. The goal should not be generalized lessness. It should be less waste, less domination by markets, less positional competition—and more shared capacity.

This distinction matters politically. “Degrowth” is often heard, fairly or not, as a demand that ordinary people accept diminished prospects. In 2022, with inflation high, energy prices surging after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, and many households anxious about heating bills, that language is especially vulnerable. A compelling climate politics must be able to say: your life can become more secure, not more precarious; your home warmer, not colder; your food better, not scarcer; your neighborhood quieter and safer, not merely poorer. The promise is not hair-shirt environmentalism but public luxury and private sufficiency.

Soper’s emphasis on shorter working time is especially promising. The pandemic revealed both the fragility of work-centered identity and the cruelty of a labor market that labels some workers “essential” while paying them poorly. Experiments with shorter workweeks, including the widely discussed Icelandic trials, suggest that reduced hours can maintain productivity while improving well-being. But shorter hours cannot simply be appended to an unequal economy. If salaries fall with hours, the affluent gain leisure while the poor gain hardship. If care work remains feminized and undervalued, “free time” for some will mean more unpaid labor for others. A politics of time must therefore include living wages, stronger unions, universal child care, paid leave, and redistribution of care between genders as well as between households and the state.

Nor should alternative hedonism romanticize slowness in every domain. Speed and convenience are not always capitalist deceptions. For disabled people, older people, rural residents, caregivers, and those with medical needs, technologies that save labor can be liberating. A washing machine, a mobility scooter, telemedicine, or a fast train may enlarge life rather than impoverish it. The problem is not technology as such, but technologies designed to maximize extraction, surveillance, dependency, and turnover. A low-carbon society will need extraordinary technical competence: clean grids, storage, efficient buildings, green steel and cement, public health systems resilient to heat, and agriculture adapted to a changing climate. The relevant contrast is not artisanal virtue versus modern technique, but democratic usefulness versus profit-driven churn.

Soper is also right that the left has often been more comfortable arguing about distribution than about desire. But desire is politically formed. Advertising, credit, zoning, workplace discipline, school schedules, and tax policy all teach people what to want. If SUVs are subsidized by road design, if flights are cheap while rail is neglected, if fresh food is costly while processed calories are everywhere, if status is attached to newness and disposability, then consumer “choice” is already structured. The task is not to scold desire but to change the world in which desire is educated.

That requires conflict. Fossil fuel companies, airlines, automakers, agribusiness, fast-fashion firms, digital advertising platforms, and real estate interests will not be charmed into alternative hedonism. They will defend assets, habits, and political influence. A postconsumerist politics must therefore be willing to regulate, tax, ban, build, and redistribute. It should restrict advertising to children, mandate repairability, phase out internal combustion engines, invest in rail and buses, retrofit buildings, protect workers in declining industries, and cancel unjust debts that keep poorer countries locked into extractive development. Pleasure needs planning.

Soper’s great contribution is to remind us that climate politics cannot live by carbon accounting alone. Parts per million, emissions pathways, and temperature targets are indispensable, but they do not tell people what mornings will feel like, what streets will sound like, what childhood might recover, or what freedom could mean after consumerism. The low-carbon future will be won only if it is felt not merely as necessity but as release.

Still, the path to that future runs less through individual renunciation than through collective provision. People will choose the train when the train is excellent. They will cook when they have time, money, and decent kitchens. They will buy less when goods are durable, repairable, and not tied to humiliation or status panic. They will welcome car-free streets when those streets are safe, lively, and accessible. Alternative hedonism is most persuasive when it becomes a politics of institutions: not simply asking people to desire differently, but giving them good reasons to do so.

Soper's essay articulates with considerable force a critique that has long circulated in heterodox economic and environmental thought but rarely receives such systematic treatment in mainstream political discourse. Her central insight—that the consumerist lifestyle promoted by advanced capitalism is neither as satisfying nor as sustainable as its boosters claim—deserves serious engagement. Yet several tensions in her argument warrant further examination, and some of her optimistic conclusions about political possibility seem premature given what we know about the structural obstacles to the transformation she envisions.

One of the essay's most valuable contributions is its insistence that environmental reform cannot be conceived merely as sacrifice. The greens-as-killjoys trope has long hampered serious climate politics, and Soper is right to invert it: the hyperconnected, overworked, anxiety-producing "affluence" of contemporary capitalism is itself joyless in ways that go beyond the obvious inequalities. Her analysis of hedonic adaptation—the psychological mechanism by which acquisitive pleasures fail to deliver lasting satisfaction—provides a welcome counterweight to the assumption that "more" automatically means "better." The research she cites, while not definitive, coheres with a broader body of evidence suggesting that subjective well-being correlates more strongly with social connection and leisure time than with income growth beyond a certain threshold.

Yet this insight raises a difficult question Soper does not fully address: if the pleasures of consumer culture are so hollow, why do they retain such grip on the popular imagination? She acknowledges that advertising and marketing play an enormous role in sustaining desire for status goods, but this concession only deepens the puzzle. If billions must be spent annually to ensure that consumers continue wanting what the market provides, this suggests either that human desire is more malleable than her argument assumes, or that something genuine in human psychology finds the consumerist package appealing despite its well-documented disappointments. Soper's invocation of Raymond Williams's "structure of feeling" is suggestive here, but she understates how diffuse and contradictory that structure remains. The yearning for "something else" she detects is real, but so is the deep attachment many people retain to the possibility—or memory—of material abundance.

This brings me to a more fundamental concern: the essay's optimism about political transformation seems somewhat disconnected from the oligarchic character of contemporary democracies. Soper writes hopefully about a "global green renaissance" led by "concerned consumers in more affluent societies," but this framing overlooks how thoroughly corporate power shapes the conditions under which consumer choices are made. The surveillance capitalism she mentions is not merely an invasive presence but a sophisticated apparatus for channeling desire in commercially profitable directions. Similarly, the political influence of fossil fuel interests and their allies in government has proven remarkably resilient despite overwhelming scientific consensus on climate change. As of September 2022, the United States Congress had just passed significant climate legislation, but its passage required extraordinary political effort and compromises that considerably weakened its provisions. The gap between what climate science demands and what democratic politics can deliver remains vast.

The essay also sidesteps the genuinely difficult question of global distributive justice. Soper argues that nations whose consumption exceeds planetary carrying capacity should not be held up as aspirational models, and she is correct that the historical emissions of wealthy countries make their moral position precarious. But she gives insufficient attention to the aspirations of billions of people in middle-income and developing nations who reasonably desire standards of living that affluent nations currently enjoy. The suggestion that these populations should embrace post-consumerist values, while understandable from a planetary perspective, raises questions about who bears the costs of transition and how their consent might be secured. Robert Pollin's objection, cited in the essay, points to a real gap: "short term" is doing considerable work in Soper's argument without clear definition.

Finally, I want to note an absence that seems striking given the essay's date. By September 2022, the COVID-19 pandemic had already produced extensive commentary on how crises might catalyze social transformation. Some of this literature was optimistic, suggesting that the disruptions of 2020-2021 demonstrated the possibility of rapid, large-scale economic and social change. Other analysts were more skeptical, noting that the pandemic had in many cases accelerated concentration of wealth and corporate power rather than democratizing it. Soper invokes the pandemic briefly, suggesting it may have begun to fracture the consensus around consumerist living, but does not engage with the considerable evidence that this hope remains unfulfilled. The "structure of feeling" she detects is real, but its political manifestations remain weak and fragmented.

None of these objections refutes Soper's core claim that dissociating well-being from resource-intensive consumption is both necessary and potentially desirable. The alternative hedonism she outlines offers a vision worth taking seriously, and her insistence that environmental politics must make pleasure its ally rather than apologizing for limiting it is strategically important. But translating this vision into feasible policy requires reckoning honestly with the power of those who benefit from the current arrangement and the cultural depth of consumerist attachments. Soper has done the important work of articulating what a post-growth future might look like; the harder task of building coalitions capable of realizing it remains largely ahead of us.

**The Pleasure Gap: Why Alternative Hedonism Needs a Theory of Power**

Kate Soper's "alternative hedonism" is one of the more bracing reframings of green politics on offer. For decades, environmentalists have ceded the territory of desire to their opponents, accepting the role of scold while corporations played the role of pleasure-giver. Soper rightly insists this is a strategic and philosophical error. A postconsumerist politics will get nowhere if it sells itself as renunciation. It must instead make the case that the affluent lifestyle we are being asked to surrender is itself a thin and exhausting substitute for things we have already lost — time, conviviality, walkable streets, unhurried craft, edible food, breathable air, children who can play outside. On that ground, Soper is persuasive and the essay deserves a wide hearing.

But I want to press hard on a weakness she half-acknowledges and then waves through. The "we" of alternative hedonism is doing enormous work in this essay, and it is not at all clear that the working-class people Soper invokes would recognize themselves inside it.

Consider the central proposals: a three- or four-day workweek; a universal basic income; the substitution of artisanal "slow working" for industrial throughput; vacations closer to home; allotment gardening; second-hand shops and repair cafés; train travel through European countryside. Each of these is desirable in itself. But the cultural texture of the package is unmistakably that of a particular fraction of the European professional middle class — people with the cultural capital to enjoy artisanal bread-making, the housing security to keep an allotment, the disposable hours to mend rather than replace, and the educational background that makes "non-instrumental" learning a pleasure rather than a humiliating reminder of school failures. Soper says she is "sensitive" to the objection that consumerism's pleasures have been unevenly distributed. But sensitivity is not yet an argument.

The honest version of the objection is this: for a great many working-class people in affluent societies, the consumerist offer was not a substitute for some richer life they were tricked into abandoning. It was a real, hard-won gain over conditions of genuine scarcity, drudgery, and hierarchy. Cheap clothing, cheap flights, cheap food, cheap furniture — these things matter to people whose grandparents owned two shirts. A weeklong package holiday to Spain is not, for the warehouse worker who finally got one, a degraded counterfeit of a Grand Tour. It is the first time anyone in the family went anywhere. To tell such a person that her pleasures are shallow, manipulated, and ecologically unaffordable, and that what she really wants is to bike through a colonnaded cycle path to a flea market, is to risk a politics of contempt regardless of how warmly it is phrased.

This is not a rhetorical worry. It is precisely how the populist right has built its coalition over the past decade: by telling working-class voters that environmentalists and cosmopolitan progressives look down on their cars, their meat, their flights, their stuff. The gilets jaunes did not erupt over a carbon tax because French workers love fossil fuels. They erupted because the carbon tax landed on commuters who had no alternative to driving, while the Parisian professional class — already living the alternative-hedonist life of cargo bikes and farmers' markets — felt virtuous. Soper's framework will reproduce this pattern unless it is built atop a much sharper analysis of who pays for the transition and who currently captures its benefits.

Here is where I think the essay's deepest gap lies. Soper writes as though the obstacle to alternative hedonism is mainly cultural — a "structure of feeling" not yet crystallized, advertising's monopoly over imagination, the absence of utopian blueprints. But the obstacle is structural and it has a name: the distribution of power between capital and labor. The reason workers cannot adopt the four-day week is not that they have been brainwashed into the work ethic. It is that wages have stagnated for forty years while housing, healthcare, education, and childcare costs have exploded. The reason they buy from Amazon is not that they secretly prefer warehouses to high streets; it is that they have neither the time nor the money to do otherwise. The reason they fly Ryanair is that rail in most of the world has been deliberately starved or privatized. These are outcomes of political defeats — the breaking of unions, the financialization of housing, the offshoring of production, the capture of regulators — and they will not be reversed by inviting people to imagine more enchanting train rides.

Soper gestures toward the structural side when she mentions universal basic income and debt cancellation. But these are mentioned as accompaniments to the cultural shift rather than as its precondition. I would invert the order. Without a redistribution of time and money, alternative hedonism is a lifestyle available only to those who already have both. The four-day week becomes a perk for knowledge workers in Reykjavik; the artisanal bakery becomes a £6 sourdough loaf; the repair café becomes a charming neighborhood feature in the part of the city where the bus drivers can no longer afford to live. Meanwhile the people doing the actual work of social reproduction — cleaning, caring, delivering, stocking — work longer hours than ever to subsidize the gentle pace of others.

What would a more class-conscious version of Soper's argument look like? It would begin not with consumer desire but with the working day. It would treat the reduction of working time as the central demand around which everything else organizes, because shorter hours simultaneously reduce production, redistribute employment, and create the conditions under which slower forms of consumption become physically possible. It would link this demand to wage floors high enough that fewer hours actually pay the rent, which means confronting the rentier economy directly. It would treat public provision — transit, housing, childcare, healthcare, parks, libraries — as the material substrate of the good life, because these are the goods that decommodify pleasure and make it accessible regardless of income. And it would be far more honest that this program requires breaking the political power of specific actors: fossil capital, the asset-management complex, the platform monopolies. Alternative hedonism cannot be a politics of invitation only. At some point it must also be a politics of confrontation.

There is one further point. Soper insists, rightly, that we cannot let "us" off the hook by blaming "them" — that affluent consumers' appetites are also driving the crisis. True. But the moral weight of this point is unevenly distributed. The richest 10 percent globally — to which Soper refers — includes essentially the entire professional class of the rich countries, including, almost certainly, most readers of this forum. The richest 1 percent are responsible for roughly twice the emissions of the poorest half of humanity combined. If alternative hedonism is to be taken seriously as an ethical demand, it should weigh much more heavily on the top of the income distribution than on the bottom. The frequent-flying executive, the second-home owner, the SUV commuter — these are the people whose consumption is genuinely a choice and whose pleasures are genuinely fungible with the cyclable city and the four-day week. The cleaner on the night bus is not the moral problem.

Soper's vision is attractive precisely because it imagines pleasure rather than sacrifice. But pleasure, like work, is something we already distribute very unequally, and any politics that wants to redistribute it will have to do more than appeal to a structure of feeling. It will have to fight.

**The Time We Don't Have: Alternative Hedonism as Feminist Political Economy**

Kate Soper's lead essay is the best short statement I have read of why the climate transition cannot succeed as a politics of austerity. Her core move — refusing the assumption that consumerist affluence is the standard against which postcarbon life will look diminished — opens a door that environmental politics has kept stubbornly closed. I want to push that door wider by reading alternative hedonism through a frame Soper touches on but does not develop: feminist political economy and the politics of time.

Begin with what Soper calls the "work-dominated, time-scarce, junk-ridden 'affluence'" of contemporary life. The phrase is precise. The defining experience of the affluent worker today is not abundance but harriedness. We outsource cooking because we have no time to cook; we outsource childcare because we have no time to parent; we outsource cleaning, laundry, eldercare, dog-walking, even friendship (in the form of paid therapy) because we have no time for any of it. The market then sells us back, at a markup, the relationships and capacities our work has stolen from us. Hochschild called this the "commercialization of intimate life" twenty years ago, and it has only deepened. The fast-food meal, the gig-delivered groceries, the Amazon impulse purchase at 11 p.m. — these are not expressions of decadent desire. They are coping mechanisms of people whose discretionary time has been confiscated by waged labor and the unwaged labor of social reproduction that piles on top of it.

Once you see this, Soper's argument acquires a sharper edge. The pleasures she catalogues — cooking from scratch, mending clothes, growing vegetables, walking children to school, lingering in the street, learning an instrument — are not whims. They are the activities of social reproduction and self-cultivation that capitalism has progressively stripped out of life and either commodified or eliminated. They are, overwhelmingly, activities historically performed by women, undervalued precisely because they were unwaged, and now invisible enough that we no longer recognize their loss as loss. To recover them as pleasures is to reverse one of the longest movements of capitalist modernity: the conversion of self-provisioning into commodity dependence.

This is why I think the feminist dimension of alternative hedonism is not optional or supplementary. It is the conceptual key. The thing that has to be redistributed is not, in the first instance, stuff. It is time — and not time as an abstract quantity but time understood through the lens of who currently has it, who currently spends it on whom, and what activities have been priced out of the lives of the time-poor.

Consider the four-day week, the central practical demand of Soper's program. In a conventional framing, it is a labor demand: workers should get more leisure. But this framing already absorbs the patriarchal assumption that what happens outside paid work is "leisure" — golf, hobbies, rest. For most adults, especially most women, the hours outside paid work are not leisure but a second shift of cooking, cleaning, caring, scheduling, emotional labor, school logistics, and the management of household supply chains. A four-day week distributed equally between partners, combined with serious public investment in childcare and eldercare and with cultural permission for men to actually do the care work, would do more for the lived condition of women than any number of representation campaigns. It would also, incidentally, make Soper's other proposals possible, because the slower pleasures she invokes — cooking, walking, conviviality — all presuppose discretionary time that is presently monopolized by paid and unpaid labor.

Lida Maxwell, quoted in the lead essay, warns that uncritical celebrations of "desire" risk reinscribing the white, heteronormative subject of consumer society. She is right, and the warning bites. The historical archive of anti-consumerism contains more than its share of moralism aimed at women's pleasures specifically: the fashion-conscious woman as the symbol of frivolity, the suburban housewife as the avatar of mass culture, shopping itself as a feminized vice. Soper is too sophisticated to fall into this trap explicitly, but the trap is set by the genre, and her essay does not fully disarm it. An alternative hedonism worth having will have to specify that the pleasures of fashion, ornament, sexual expression, and self-fashioning are not the enemy. The enemy is the system that makes those pleasures available only through the planet-destroying churn of fast fashion and the body-shaming surveillance of social-media advertising.

There is a constructive path here. The slow-fashion movement, the rise of dressmaking and mending communities, the resurgence of swap economies and "shrub" libraries — these are not the death of style but its democratization. Conviviality and ornament are perfectly compatible. The Black feminist tradition, in particular, has long insisted that pleasure, adornment, and beauty are not bourgeois indulgences but resources of survival and resistance, and any postconsumerist politics that cannot make room for that insistence will fail.

This leads me to my second point of expansion. Soper's structure of feeling is rooted, I think, in a register that is still recognizably European, white, and a bit melancholic — the lost world of unhurried streets and artisanal bakers. There is nothing wrong with that register, but it is not the only one available. Across the Global South, and within diasporic communities in the North, alternative hedonisms already exist in robust, lived form. The extended family meal that goes on for hours; the open courtyard cooled by neighbors' chatter; the markets that double as social centers; the cooperative childcare of multigenerational households; the ritual calendar of festivals and saints' days — these are not nostalgic fragments but ongoing practices, eroded in many places by exactly the work-and-spend dynamic Soper criticizes. A genuinely global alternative hedonism would treat these as central exhibits, not honorable mentions.

Nemonte Nenquimo's words, which Soper quotes, point in this direction. The Waorani critique of extractivist civilization is not just about ecology; it is about the spiritual poverty of a culture that cannot sit still. Indigenous and feminist epistemologies converge on a striking point: the most exhausted thing in the affluent world is the affluent worker, and the most enviable resource in many "underdeveloped" places is precisely the social fabric that the affluent world has burned for fuel.

What follows for politics? Three things. First, the demand for time — shorter hours, public holidays, paid leave, predictable schedules — should be foregrounded as the climate demand, because nothing else in Soper's program is possible without it. Second, the infrastructure of social reproduction — childcare, eldercare, healthcare, schools, parks, libraries, transit — should be understood as climate infrastructure, because it is what permits low-carbon, high-conviviality life. Third, alternative hedonism should explicitly disavow any romance of the simple life that erases gendered labor; the goal is not to send anyone back to the kitchen but to make the kitchen, the street, and the workplace each less totalizing than they have become.

Soper has given us a frame strong enough to carry these expansions. The task now is to fill it in with the texture of lives actually lived — and to insist that the politics of pleasure is, before anything else, a politics of time.

**Against Alternative Hedonism: The Case for Abundance**

Kate Soper has written an elegant essay, and like many elegant essays it is wrong in instructive ways. The wrongness is not in the diagnosis of consumer culture — much of which is accurate — but in the prescription, which combines romanticism about the past, pessimism about technology, and an unearned confidence that other people share her tastes. I want to mount a direct case against alternative hedonism, on grounds that are themselves green and themselves left.

Start with the empirics. Soper writes as though the link between consumption and ecological damage were straightforward, such that less consumption necessarily means less damage. This is the central premise of the degrowth literature she draws on. But it has been increasingly difficult to defend as an empirical claim about the rich countries over the past fifteen years. The United Kingdom's per capita carbon emissions today are lower than they were in 1860, while GDP per capita is many times higher. The United States has reduced its emissions by about 20 percent from the 2005 peak even as the economy has grown substantially. Sweden, Denmark, Germany, and France have all decoupled growth from emissions in absolute terms over the past two decades. The cause is not the moral awakening of consumers. It is the collapsing cost of solar, wind, batteries, and LEDs, plus the displacement of coal by gas and renewables in electricity generation. Technology, not asceticism, is what bent the curves.

This matters because the decarbonization challenge ahead is roughly a problem of completing this substitution: electrifying transport, heat, and industry, and powering the electricity with low-carbon sources. None of this requires anyone to fly less, eat less meat, or buy fewer T-shirts, although those choices are fine for those who want to make them. The variable that swamps all behavioral variables is the carbon intensity of the energy system. And the carbon intensity of the energy system is determined by technology and capital deployment, not by consumer mood.

Soper is dismissive of electric vehicles — they still involve plastics, congestion, accidents — and of green growth generally. This dismissal will not survive contact with the arithmetic of the energy transition. There is no plausible path to a stable climate that does not involve a massive expansion of clean electricity generation, an enormous build-out of transmission, an industrial mobilization to electrify steel and cement, and yes, hundreds of millions of electric vehicles, heat pumps, and induction stoves. Each of these requires more mining, more manufacturing, more shipping, more capital expenditure — more growth, in any meaningful accounting. The alternative is not Soper's pastoral of bicycles and allotments. It is a planet that fails to decarbonize and the poor of the world condemned to do without.

This brings me to the second problem, which is global. Soper concedes that growth is needed in the "short term" to provide for basic needs in the poorest countries, but the concession is grudging and unspecified. Robert Pollin, whose pointed objection Soper acknowledges, is right that this is the weakest moment in her essay. The world has roughly four billion people living on less than ten dollars a day. Bringing them to even modest material decency — adequate calories, durable shelter, reliable electricity, basic appliances, education, healthcare — will require an enormous expansion of energy use, cement, steel, and manufactured goods. There is no degrowth path that delivers this. To say "the rich countries shrink and the poor countries grow until convergence" sounds neat, but the convergence point itself sits well above current rich-country energy use if the poor countries are to enjoy anything like a dignified material life. Either we electrify abundance for everyone, or we accept permanent stratification of the human prospect. Soper cannot have it both ways.

Third, the romanticism. Soper invokes artisanal production, slow craft, traditional methods, walking, idling, allotments. These are pleasant things, and I do not begrudge anyone who enjoys them. But the historical record on pre-industrial production is unambiguous: it was backbreaking, inefficient, and short-lived. The peasants Jayati Ghosh evokes do not find the idea of slow craft enchanting. They find it the condition they would like, urgently, to escape. The same is true of artisanal manufacture more generally — it was abandoned not because of capitalist plotting but because industrial methods were vastly more productive, freeing human labor for other purposes. To recommend slow methods as a general program is to recommend a much poorer world, in which the productivity gains that have fed, housed, and lengthened human lives are partially undone. Soper would protest that she does not mean a wholesale return — only a selective revaluation. But the moment one specifies which goods will be artisanal (clothing? furniture? food? medicine? semiconductors?), the costs become apparent and the program shrinks toward the bourgeois aesthetic Soper otherwise wants to escape.

Fourth, the empirics on happiness. Soper cites the familiar finding that beyond some threshold, more income does not correlate strongly with self-reported well-being. She is responsible enough to flag the methodological problems, but she does not draw the right inference. The right inference is that across-the-board reductions in material consumption are likely to produce sharp losses in well-being among the people for whom income still matters most — the global poor and the working class of rich countries — while the gains accrue only to those already above the satiation threshold. This is not an attractive distributional profile for a putatively progressive program.

What, then, is the alternative? I would call it abundance liberalism, or, in a more left register, eco-modernism. Its premises are: (1) human flourishing requires material abundance, including energy abundance, and we should not be embarrassed by this; (2) the climate problem is fundamentally a problem of clean technology deployment, and is solvable with policies that make clean energy cheap and dirty energy expensive; (3) the binding constraints on the transition are not consumer desire but planning failures, NIMBYism, regulatory sclerosis, and capital misallocation; (4) where genuine luxuries (frequent flying, beef, large detached houses) are environmentally costly, the answer is to make them expensive via carbon pricing, not to moralize about them; (5) housing density, public transit, walkable urbanism, and shorter working hours are all good, but they are compatible with — indeed require — economic dynamism.

Will Rinehart, quoted in the lead, gets at part of this when he says Soper does not go far enough and that abundance is the only framework that actually delivers her ostensible goals. He is right. A society in which clean energy is so cheap that everyone has enough, in which housing is built where people want to live, in which intercity rail is fast and frequent because we invested in it, in which the working week is shorter because productivity is higher — that society achieves more of what Soper wants than alternative hedonism ever could, and it does so without asking anyone to renounce pleasures they have only just gotten access to.

Alternative hedonism is, in the end, a politics for people who already have enough. The rest of the world needs more.

**The View from the South: On Soper's Missing Specifics**

I write from a part of the world where the questions Kate Soper raises look quite different than they do from a London or Boston study. Her essay is thoughtful and parts of it I agree with. But it suffers from a peculiar abstraction that allows the rich-country reader to feel that the obligations of the climate transition fall mainly on shared cultural attitudes — the structure of feeling, the romance of consumerism, the appetite for stuff — rather than on the specific countries and classes whose specific actions have created and continue to create the crisis. Jayati Ghosh's brief intervention in the forum gets at this, and I want to develop it.

Soper acknowledges that growth is necessary in "the short term" for the "poorest nations" to meet basic needs. Robert Pollin asks the obvious question: how short is short, and which nations count? Soper does not say. This is not pedantry. It is the central question. Nearly all the world's population lives in countries that are not yet at rich-country levels of material well-being. India's per capita energy use is one-tenth that of the United States. Sub-Saharan Africa's electricity consumption per capita is lower than New York City's refrigeration load. Indonesia, Nigeria, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Egypt, the Philippines, Vietnam — none of these will be told, with a straight face, that they should now embrace alternative hedonism instead of the development that has eluded them. Yet without a clear account of who grows and who shrinks, the rhetoric of degrowth defaults to a global posture that is at best ambiguous and at worst objectively conservative — preserving current global hierarchies under a green coloration.

Let me put it sharply. If "we" need to consume less, who is "we"? The American household at the eightieth percentile of the world income distribution? The Indian household at the same eightieth percentile, which has very different material conditions? The Indian household at the fortieth percentile, which lacks reliable electricity? Without disaggregation, the call to consume less becomes a generalized pressure that, in the actually existing international order, will be applied most easily where state capacity is weakest and bargaining position worst — namely, the South. Rich countries will continue, as they have, to consume what they consume; poor countries will be told their development must be different from the development the rich countries enjoyed.

We have seen this script before. It is the script of climate negotiations since Rio. Common but differentiated responsibilities, in theory; in practice, the steady erosion of any differentiation, until poor countries are expected to leapfrog directly into low-carbon development without the financing, technology transfer, or fiscal space to do so. Meanwhile, the historical responsibility of the rich for the bulk of cumulative emissions goes unrequited, and "loss and damage" remains a phrase rather than a fund.

Soper to her credit mentions the Debt for Climate campaign and quotes Monbiot on reparations and debt cancellation. This is the strongest moment in her essay, and I would have liked it to be the spine rather than a paragraph. The single most important thing that affluent societies can do for the global climate is not to take up alternative hedonism. It is to deliver, at scale, three things they have so far refused to deliver: financing for clean development at concessional terms; transfer of technology free from intellectual property barriers; and compensation for loss and damage already inflicted. Without these, no amount of cultural revaluation in the North will alter the trajectory of global emissions, because the next century's emissions will be dominated by the energy choices of countries that have not yet built their core infrastructure.

Now consider Soper's specific proposals from a Southern vantage. A three- or four-day workweek? Most workers in my country are in the informal sector, where there is no week, only the days you eat and the days you do not. A universal basic income? An excellent idea in principle, but financed how, in a country whose tax base is a fraction of the OECD norm and whose fiscal space has been further compressed by debt service to Northern creditors? Slow artisanal production? My country has spent decades trying to industrialize against the gravitational pull of cheap imports from China, and the idea that we should now embrace deindustrialization as a virtue would be received as a bad joke. Closer-to-home vacations? Tourism is the third largest source of foreign exchange for much of the Global South, and the Northern traveler who decides to take a train through Wales instead of flying to Mombasa is, in the aggregate, a fiscal threat to East African economies. Each of these proposals has different valence depending on where one stands.

I do not raise these to dismiss alternative hedonism. I raise them to insist that the politics of pleasure has to be a politics of specific obligations across specific borders, not a generalized cultural mood. The honest version of Soper's argument, addressed to its actual audience, would say: rich-country consumers, your absolute level of consumption is roughly four to ten times what is globally sustainable; you must shrink it; here is how to make the shrinking pleasurable; and the resources released must flow, as transfers and as opened ecological space, to the poor of the world. That is a defensible program. The undifferentiated call to "consume differently" is not.

There is a further point about indigenous and traditional knowledge that I want to make carefully, because I am wary of how it has been used. Soper invokes Nemonte Nenquimo and indigenous critiques of extractivism, and rightly. But there is a risk, well known to those of us who have watched Northern environmentalism over decades, of romanticizing the lifeways of peoples whose actual political demands include things like land title, infrastructure, schools, clinics, and the right to make their own development choices — including, sometimes, choices that Northern environmentalists would not endorse. The Waorani are not living artisanally because they have chosen alternative hedonism. They are defending a territory against extractive capitalism that wants to destroy it. Solidarity with that struggle does not consist in importing its aesthetics into the Northern lifestyle press. It consists in dismantling the financial, military, and corporate machinery that enables the extraction in the first place. That machinery has addresses; most of them are in the North.

Finally, on the question of pleasure itself. Soper's essay is grounded in a recognizable European post-1968 tradition that sees affluent life as spiritually impoverished and seeks remedies in slowness, conviviality, and craft. This tradition has produced some of the best cultural criticism of the past half-century. But it is not the only tradition that thinks about pleasure and the good life, and a global alternative hedonism would need to engage seriously with Buen Vivir, with Gandhian sarvodaya, with various African traditions of communal sufficiency, and with the long Asian debates on simplicity and excess. Each of these is more than a footnote. Each has substantive things to say that the European tradition alone does not.

Soper opens a door. The view from this side of it is wider than she suggests, and the obligations sit much more heavily on her side than on mine.

**Structure of Feeling, Structure of Power: On the Limits of Cultural Persuasion**

Kate Soper's essay turns on a phrase borrowed from Raymond Williams: "structure of feeling." She locates her hope in a popular, still-inarticulate intuition that something has gone wrong with affluent life and must change. Alternative hedonism, in her telling, is the political articulation that this nascent feeling has been waiting for. I want to take seriously both the diagnosis and the wager — and to suggest that the wager underestimates what Williams himself understood about how structures of feeling become structures of politics.

Williams was, importantly, a materialist. When he wrote of structures of feeling, he was attempting to describe the lived texture of ideology as it forms and reforms in response to underlying changes in productive life. He did not think that feelings made history on their own, nor that articulating an emergent feeling would be sufficient to transform a social order. On the contrary, his life's work was an effort to understand how cultural change tracks, sometimes lags, sometimes outruns the slow grinding shifts in the means of production and the relations of class. A Williams-inflected reading of our present would ask not only whether the structure of feeling Soper detects is real (I think it is) but what material conditions are producing it and what conditions would be required for it to consolidate into a political force.

So let me try that reading. What conditions are currently producing the disaffection Soper diagnoses? Several, I think, are visible. Wage stagnation across the rich world has decoupled rising labor productivity from rising household income, leading to a generation of workers who are producing more and getting less. Housing costs have absorbed an ever-larger share of disposable income, foreclosing the wealth-accumulation paths their parents took for granted. Working hours have proven stickier downward than productivity gains predicted, and the spread of digital communications has effectively eliminated the boundary between work and rest. Care responsibilities have intensified as state support has retreated. Climate anxiety, especially among the young, has shaded existential dread into the texture of everyday choices. The pandemic produced, for a brief moment, an enforced slowness that allowed millions of people to register what their normal lives actually felt like.

These conditions are real and they are generating real disaffection. The Great Resignation, the resurgence of union organizing in unexpected sectors (Amazon, Starbucks, academia), the rise of antiwork discourse online, the popularity of degrowth and post-growth thinking in places they could not have appeared twenty years ago — these are symptoms of the structure of feeling Soper invokes. So far, so good.

But here is where I think the essay's wager becomes vulnerable. Structures of feeling can resolve in many political directions, not all of them progressive. The same disaffection with affluent modernity that produces alternative hedonism also produces religious revival, ethnonationalism, conspiracy thinking, the longing for strongmen who promise to restore meaning by force. Across Europe and the United States, the most successful political projects of the past decade have been ones that named the malaise Soper names — alienation, hollow consumerism, lost community, vanishing localities, dignity-of-work nostalgia — and channeled it into reactionary directions. Trumpism, Brexit, the European far right, Bolsonarismo, even certain currents in post-liberal Catholic intellectualism, all sell themselves as cures for the same disease alternative hedonism diagnoses. They are, in Williamsian terms, competing articulations of the same structure of feeling.

This is not a reason to abandon Soper's project. It is a reason to be much more candid about what would have to be done to ensure that the emergent disaffection consolidates as her kind of postconsumerism rather than as some darker formation. And here the essay is, frankly, thin. Soper offers a vision and a set of proposals, but very little institutional theory of how the vision becomes the policy. Who builds the coalition? Through what organizations? Against what opposition? With what theory of state power, electoral strategy, or international cooperation?

The essay's implicit answer seems to be: by writing essays like this one, by persuading "concerned consumers in more affluent societies" to "lead the way," by trusting that the structure of feeling will find its own political expression. This is, I think, a category error. Structures of feeling do not find their own expression. They are organized into expression by political projects with leaders, institutions, money, media, and willingness to fight. Reagan and Thatcher did not arrive because individual consumers had quietly developed neoliberal feelings; they arrived because a forty-year project of think tanks, journals, business federations, and political entrepreneurs built the institutional machinery to turn discontent into a specific political form. The green new dealers have begun to do this on the left, but the work is far less advanced.

Let me also press a more philosophical point. Soper is admirably honest about the tension between subjective and objective accounts of well-being, and she does not pretend to resolve it. But the tension matters more than she allows. Alternative hedonism is, in its rhetoric, a politics of voluntary persuasion — it asks people to want different things — but it cannot in practice avoid moments of paternalism. The three-day week is not a matter of taste; it requires legal change that overrides employer preferences and, in some sectors, employee preferences too.
