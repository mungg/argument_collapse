Should Opioid Training for Doctors Be Mandatory?

As police continue to investigate the role prescription narcotics may have played in the death of the musician Prince, a group of advisers to the Food and Drug Administration recommended mandatory training for doctors who prescribe these powerful painkillers. The hope is that this initiative will help stem what some are calling an epidemic of overdose deaths involving opioids. The F.D.A. is not required to follow advice such as this, though it often does.

While some doctors are supportive of the plan, the American Medical Association has fought the proposal for several years now. Should doctors be forced to undergo this training?

Mandatory training is a modest, overdue response to a crisis the medical profession helped create. The CDC's 2016 guidelines made clear what the data have shown for years: prescribing practices, not just illicit drug use, are central to the roughly 28,000 opioid-related overdose deaths recorded in 2014. When physicians routinely send patients home with month-long supplies of oxycodone after wisdom-tooth extractions, the problem is not a few bad actors but a knowledge and culture gap across the profession.

The AMA's objection — that mandates are paternalistic and burdensome — would carry more weight if voluntary education had worked. It hasn't. The FDA's existing Risk Evaluation and Mitigation Strategy for extended-release opioids has been voluntary since 2012, and uptake has been disappointing, with only a fraction of targeted prescribers completing it. Meanwhile, surveys consistently show that medical schools devote only a handful of hours to pain management and addiction, and primary care doctors — who write most opioid prescriptions — report feeling underprepared.

A few hours of required CME, tied to DEA registration, is not a heavy lift. Physicians already complete mandatory training on topics from HIPAA to child abuse reporting. Given that prescribers function as gatekeepers to the most dangerous legal drugs in America, requiring them to demonstrate basic competence in safe prescribing is the bare minimum.

The death of Prince, with prescription opioids reportedly found at the scene, has thrown a stark spotlight on a crisis that has been building for over a decade. An FDA advisory panel’s recent recommendation to mandate opioid-prescriber training is not bureaucratic overreach—it is a belated but necessary step to address an epidemic that claims more than 14,000 American lives each year from prescription opioid overdoses alone. While the American Medical Association has resisted such mandates, the scale of this public health disaster demands that we stop treating education as optional.

The core problem is well-documented: a vast mismatch between the aggressive prescribing of opioids and the education doctors receive about them. Studies have shown that many physicians feel inadequately trained in pain management and addiction risk. In 2011, a national survey found that fewer than half of medical schools included dedicated opioid-prescribing education. A 2015 study by the National Safety Council revealed that 99% of primary care physicians prescribed opioids for longer than the three-day period recommended by some state guidelines, and nearly a quarter did not recognize that opioids are an inappropriate first-line treatment for chronic non-cancer pain. This is not a matter of bad faith but of a systemic knowledge gap that voluntary efforts have failed to close.

The CDC’s new opioid-prescribing guidelines, issued in March 2016, underline what responsible prescribing looks like: using non-opioid therapies first, prescribing the lowest effective dose for the shortest duration, and monitoring patients closely. Yet guidelines alone are passive documents. A mandatory training program transforms these principles into active clinical competence. It would ensure that every prescriber understands how to screen for substance use disorder, recognize when a patient is at risk for respiratory depression, and counsel patients on safe storage and disposal. These are not advanced specialties; they are basic safety skills akin to the mandatory training already required in areas like infection control or pediatric vaccination protocols.

The AMA’s objections—that mandatory training intrudes on physician autonomy and imposes a regulatory burden—ring hollow against 165,000 prescription-opioid overdose deaths since 1999. The organization has publicly recognized the epidemic, yet its lobbying against concrete action undermines its stated commitment. Furthermore, training need not be onerous: online modules, integrated into existing continuing medical education requirements, could be completed in a few hours. A handful of states, including Massachusetts and New York, have already shown that mandatory education is feasible without crippling medical practice.

Prescription opioids often serve as a gateway to heroin, with four in five new heroin users reporting that they started with pain pills. The crisis is fueled not by street corners but by prescription pads. Requiring doctors to learn what the evidence clearly shows—that opioids are rarely the right choice for chronic pain, and that their risks often outweigh their benefits—is not a punishment. It is a lifeline for the patients who trust them.

Prescribing opioids is a powerful medical act with potentially life-altering consequences. Mandatory training is a modest requirement compared with the harm caused by uninformed prescribing. It will not solve the epidemic by itself, but it is a necessary part of a broader response.

Before reflexively endorsing mandatory training, we should ask a harder question: is there good evidence that it will actually reduce overdose deaths? The honest answer is that we don't know. The FDA's voluntary REMS program for extended-release opioids has been running since 2012, and rigorous evaluations of its impact on prescribing behavior and patient outcomes are thin. Layering on a federal mandate without that evidence base risks substituting the appearance of action for action itself.

Consider what mandatory training typically looks like in practice. A physician logs into an online module, clicks through slides, answers a multiple-choice quiz, and prints a certificate. Whether that experience changes prescribing decisions a year later at 4 p.m. on a Friday, with a demanding patient in the room, is an empirical question—and the literature on continuing medical education generally finds that passive, didactic formats produce modest behavior change at best.

There are also better-targeted tools available. Prescription drug monitoring programs (PDMPs), which let physicians see a patient's controlled-substance history at the point of care, have produced measurable reductions in doctor-shopping and high-risk prescribing in states like New York and Kentucky. Expanding access to naloxone, broadening medication-assisted treatment with buprenorphine, and reforming pain-management metrics in hospital reimbursement schemes (the infamous HCAHPS pain questions) all have stronger evidence behind them than mandatory CME.

None of this means training is worthless. The CDC's March 2016 guidelines are genuinely useful, and many physicians would benefit from working through them carefully. But "useful" and "should be federally mandated for every prescriber in the country" are different propositions. A national mandate creates uniform compliance burdens while doing little to distinguish the overprescribing pain clinic from the conscientious internist.

If policymakers want to require something, they should require participation in PDMPs, not seat time in a webinar. They should fund residency-level curriculum reform, where habits are actually formed. And they should be honest that the opioid crisis is now being driven increasingly by illicit fentanyl and heroin—problems no amount of physician education will solve. Mandatory training is the easy answer. The harder work lies elsewhere.

The debate over mandatory opioid training tends to be framed as a fight between public health and physician autonomy. That framing misses what is really at stake: professional accountability. Medicine is a self-regulating profession that has been granted enormous social trust, including the legal authority to dispense Schedule II narcotics. When the profession demonstrably fails to police itself, it forfeits the presumption that government should stay out.

And the profession has failed. From the late 1990s onward, American doctors quadrupled opioid prescribing. They did so partly in response to aggressive pharmaceutical marketing, partly in response to well-meaning but misguided campaigns to treat pain as the "fifth vital sign," and partly because medical school curricula devoted shockingly little time to pain management or addiction medicine. A 2011 survey found that the average U.S. medical school provided fewer than ten hours of instruction on pain over four years. Veterinary students received more.

Given that record, the AMA's posture—opposing mandatory training while issuing voluntary guidance—reads as guild protectionism dressed up as principle. The organization is essentially arguing that doctors should be trusted to fix on their own a problem they helped create. That is not a credible position when 28,000 Americans are dying each year.

I would go further than the FDA advisers. Training should be mandatory, periodic, and tied to DEA registration—the license that permits opioid prescribing in the first place. If you want the privilege of writing Schedule II prescriptions, you should demonstrate current competence in safe prescribing, addiction screening, and patient counseling. This is no different from periodic recertification in other high-stakes domains.

Will training alone end the epidemic? Of course not. The crisis now has its own momentum, with heroin and illicit fentanyl filling demand as pill mills are shut down. But the pipeline of new patients introduced to opioids in clinical settings remains real, and prescribers are the gatekeepers. Asking them to be educated gatekeepers is the most modest possible reform.

The AMA should drop its opposition and lead. If it doesn't, the FDA—and Congress—will rightly impose what the profession refused to embrace.

I'm a primary care physician, and I want to push back on the rush to make opioid training a federal mandate. Not because training is bad—it isn't—but because mandates of this kind tend to produce predictable unintended consequences that the policy conversation is ignoring.

The first consequence is overcorrection. Already, in the wake of CDC guidelines and DEA enforcement actions, colleagues of mine are simply refusing to prescribe opioids at all. Chronic pain patients who have been stable for years on moderate doses are being abruptly tapered or discharged. Cancer survivors, sickle-cell patients, and people with severe neuropathic pain are reporting that they cannot find physicians willing to treat them. A national mandate signals, in effect, that opioid prescribing is presumptively suspect. Many busy clinicians will respond rationally by exiting the practice altogether, leaving the most vulnerable patients to suffer or to seek relief on the street.

The second consequence is regulatory creep. Once the FDA establishes a precedent of mandating training for a drug class, the model will be extended. Will we soon require mandatory training for benzodiazepines? Stimulants? Anticoagulants, which kill more people than opioids in some age groups? Each addition is defensible in isolation; collectively, they transform the practice of medicine into an exercise in compliance.

The third issue is targeting. The overprescribers driving the crisis are not, by and large, thoughtful internists who would benefit from a refresher course. They are a relatively small number of high-volume prescribers operating in identifiable patterns that state PDMPs and DEA data can flag. A precision approach—auditing outliers, revoking licenses, prosecuting pill mills—would do far more good than blanketing 700,000 DEA registrants with mandatory CME.

I support better education on pain and addiction. I support it being built into residency training, into board recertification, and into the routine work of professional societies. What I do not support is a federal mandate that treats every prescriber as a potential abuser, that further deters appropriate pain treatment, and that substitutes symbolic policy for the harder work of going after the actual bad actors. The road to a worse problem is paved with good mandates.

Physician training requirements for controlled substances are not novel—they exist in various forms across healthcare systems, andopioid prescribing warrants similar attention given the documented harms.

Critics who frame this as interference with medical practice miss the point. Medicine has never been a purely private matter between doctor and patient. Public health considerations have always shaped medical regulation, from vaccination requirements to prescribing restrictions. The opioid crisis represents a clear case where collective action is necessary.

The implementation should be straightforward: brief, targeted modules covering risk assessment,识别 of problematic prescribing patterns, and patient counseling strategies. This knowledge already exists—what's lacking is universal dissemination.

What's particularly compelling is the argument from fairness. Many physicians received minimal training on pain management and addiction during medical school and residency. Those who have sought additional education have done so voluntarily, often at their own expense. Mandatory training would ensure all prescribers meet baseline standards, creating a more equitable professional environment while protecting patients.

The AMA's opposition is disappointing. Organized medicine should be leading efforts to address this crisis, not defending the status quo. Physicians took an oath to do no harm—mandatory training helps fulfill that promise.

I urge the FDA to move forward decisively.
