Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Brandon Terry's essay performs an indispensable act of intellectual retrieval. The King he excavates—the political theorist of fear and ideology, the architect of coercive nonviolence, the defender of basic income and full employment, the recruiter of Chicago gang members into nonviolent training—is barely recognizable from the figure summoned each January to chide protesters for impoliteness. Terry is right that this is no minor distortion. When Cass Sunstein can describe King as "backward looking and even conservative," when the bust in the Oval Office gazes upon presidents authorizing drone strikes, something has gone wrong not only with our memory but with our capacity to think politically. Terry's essay is a needed corrective, and his diagnosis of how racial pessimism, on one side, and reflexive call-out culture, on the other, foreclose King's more demanding mode of analysis seems to me largely correct.

Yet precisely because Terry is so persuasive on what King thought, I want to press him on a problem he gestures at but does not quite resolve: the institutional and historical conditions that made King's politics legible and effective—conditions that, as Terry himself admits, are largely gone. The question "MLK now" is not only a question of recovering ideas; it is a question of whether the political technology King refined can be reassembled from the parts available to us.

Consider what made the Birmingham campaign work. It required, first, a dense institutional infrastructure—above all the black church, which provided not only theology but buildings, money, communications networks, choirs, mass meetings, and a moral language thick enough to discipline a cadre of ordinary people into accepting beatings without retaliation. Terry acknowledges that this institution is in decline, weakened by prosperity theology, scandal, and the migration of younger black Americans away from religious life. But he treats this mostly as a problem for King's "embrace among the unchurched." It is more than that. It is a problem about whether mass nonviolent direct action of the kind King theorized is materially possible without an equivalent. The Movement for Black Lives is admirable, but it has not generated, and may not be able to generate, a sustained Birmingham- or Selma-scale logistical capacity. The post-2014 cycle of protest reveals how much of what we call a "movement" is now thinly institutionalized, dependent on social media virality, and consequently brittle.

Second, King's politics required a particular external audience. Terry rightly notes that "the idea that Americans will be ashamed in the eyes of the world…now seems quaint." But this concession should ripple further through his argument than he allows. King's "world-historical" dramaturgy worked because Cold War competition gave Jim Crow propaganda value to Moscow and embarrassed Washington into action. Today the relevant geopolitical audience is largely indifferent or itself authoritarian; the State Department lectures no one on civil rights with credibility. If the staged spectacle of unearned suffering depended on a watching world that could be shamed, what does coercive nonviolence look like once shame is no longer a lever? Terry's appeal to internationalizing the movement around climate, weapons, and inequality is intuitive, but it skips past the harder fact that the symbolic field on which King operated has been substantially demolished.

Third, and most pointedly: Terry raises Malcolm X's challenge—that King's nonviolence drew its force from the implicit threat of violent rebellion from below—and concedes its bite without quite answering it. He notes that cameras left Missouri when the threat of violence subsided, and that the Moral Mondays protests, models of nonviolent discipline, never broke through nationally. This is a serious admission. It suggests that what Terry calls King's "coercive" nonviolence may have been a particular historical achievement, dependent on the visible proximity of an alternative (Watts, Newark, Detroit) that made King the "responsible" interlocutor establishment elites could not refuse. Strip that dialectic away and you may not have a purer nonviolence; you may simply have ignorable nonviolence. Terry's faith that King's strategy of "throwing racist ideology off balance" remains generative deserves more defense against the possibility that the strategy was, in part, parasitic on conditions we cannot or should not wish to reproduce.

None of this is a refutation of Terry; it is a request that the recovery project go further into the question of preconditions. King thought systematically about the cultivation of virtues, about the morphology of protest, about the political economy of automation. He did so because he believed political action required the disciplined construction of capacities, not their assumption. The "MLK now" Terry wants is faithful to King in spirit only if it asks—with the same realism Terry rightly praises—what institutions, what audiences, what threats, and what moral languages must be built or rebuilt to make King-style action effective. Otherwise the recovery risks becoming what Terry warns against: another quotation, this time more sophisticated and left-coded, but still detached from the material work that gave the original its weight.

On Terry's larger claim, however, I am convinced. King's thought, taken whole, is exactly the kind of public philosophy the current moment lacks. The first task is to read him; the second, harder one, is to figure out which of his conditions we still possess.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Brandon M. Terry’s erudite and morally urgent essay offers a vital corrective to the flattened, sainted version of Martin Luther King, Jr. that dominates public memory. By recovering the radical King—the diagnostician of racism’s affective, sociopolitical, and cognitive dimensions; the theorist of mass nonviolent coercion; the critic of capitalism who saw civil disobedience as essential to economic justice—Terry gives us a thinker far more unsettling than the apostle of colorblind consensus. Yet precisely because this recovery is so important, we must press on the tensions that remain in his account. To honestly engage King’s legacy on the fiftieth anniversary of his death is to ask not only what his ideas were, but also what lives they might have in a political landscape that has shifted dramatically since the 1960s. On that score, Terry’s retrieval risks overestimating the portability of King’s synthesis, particularly its ethical and strategic architecture, into our present moment.

Terry is right that King’s three-part analysis of racism—irrational fear, congenital deformity of the nation’s ideology, and cognitive failures that deny the oppressed’s full humanity—avoids the dilemmas of both liberal colorblindness and totalizing despair. But in defending this nuanced view, Terry dismisses the “racial pessimists” who see anti-blackness as near-immutable. His worry is that such pessimism leads to political paralysis or a retreat into fugitive gestures. Yet the Trump era makes it harder to confidently distinguish between justified alarm and defeatism. By September 2018, we have witnessed a presidency that openly encourages white nationalist resentment, draws a moral equivalence between neo-Nazis and counter-protesters, and stokes the very “irrational fears” King diagnosed. The synergy King perceived between fear, ideology, and stereotyping was once counteracted by a shared ideal—however hypocritical—of democratic principles. That ideal now seems shredded. In such a climate, the hope that mass nonviolent spectacle can “dissolve the stereotype of the grinning, submissive Uncle Tom” may be poorly matched to a world where black protestors are routinely cast as dangerous, ungrateful, and criminal. Terry concedes this shift in his final pages, but he does not fully grapple with its implications. If the racist worldview no longer predicts passivity but rather foreordains violent aggression, then the redemptive surprise of nonviolent discipline may lose much of its power to “unsettle racist expectations.” The spectacle risks being absorbed immediately into a preexisting narrative of threat.

This connects to a deeper challenge that Terry acknowledges but does not resolve: the place of violence and coercion in King’s praxis. He notes Malcolm X’s argument that King’s success depended on the implicit threat of rebellion from below, and he asks what kind of “nonviolence” this amounts to. The question remains acute. In the lead-up to Ferguson, the cameras trained on the streets precisely when the possibility of violent confrontation seemed highest, and they pulled back when that threat subsided. Meanwhile, the resolutely peaceful Moral Mondays movement, which Terry mentions, attracted far less national attention and failed to translate its moral seriousness into the kind of coercive leverage King thought necessary. This is not to celebrate violent tactics but to question whether King’s mature insight—that direct action must create a crisis “so crisis-packed that it will inevitably open the door to negotiation”—can today be achieved without responses that push beyond the boundaries of nonviolence as King defined it. The Movement for Black Lives, despite Terry’s noting its affinity for nonviolent direct action, has largely refused to condemn acts of property destruction or to police protestor anger in the way King’s organizational discipline required. This may reflect not a failure of nerve but a strategic reading of a media ecosystem that rewards disruption and conflict more than dignified suffering. If so, then King’s nonviolent ethic, rooted in his Christian metaphysics of agape and redemptive sacrifice, may be one of the parts of his legacy least ready for transplantation into a secular, fragmented public sphere.

Terry’s handling of gender and sexism highlights another axis of this difficulty. He forthrightly concedes that King’s thought suffered from “severe logical and moral failures” regarding gender, and he calls for adding sexism to King’s triple evils. This is a necessary amendment, but it understates the structural problem. King’s vision of leadership was deeply patriarchal, grounded in the black church’s hierarchical authority and a charismatic model that concentrated moral and organizational power in a single male figure. The “magnificent universal quality” of nonviolent armies that he celebrated did not eliminate the exclusions that kept women from the inner circles of decision-making. Today, the most vibrant antiracist movements—Black Lives Matter, the Women’s March, the campaigns against sexual harassment—are explicitly feminist, intersectional, and often leaderless or distributed in structure. To retrieve King’s legacy in a way that speaks to these movements requires more than appending sexism as a fourth evil; it demands a fundamental rethinking of the organizational form and the conception of virtue that King championed. When Terry claims that King’s “politics of character” is not respectability politics but an ethical commitment to living well, he still operates within a framework that can easily slide into supplicating norms. The virtues of humility, magnanimity, and thrift may nourish personal dignity, but in a movement context they can also be mobilized to discipline marginalized people into palatability. That King himself supported youngsters who “threw off their middle-class values” suggests his own thinking was not entirely settled on this point.

None of this is to diminish the force of Terry’s central achievement. He has given us a King who is a profound interlocutor, not a hollow icon. Yet the very difficulty of the present moment—an ascendant global authoritarianism, a climate crisis, a collapse of faith in democratic institutions—calls for a radicalism that may have to go beyond King’s own boundaries. King’s internationalism, his insistence on an ethical shift away from scarcity, his call for a global beloved community: these still ring with prophetic power. But the tools he gave us—disciplined nonviolent armies, the redemptive love of agape, a faith that suffering will humanize the oppressor—may be artifacts of a particular historical conjuncture that no longer obtains. Engaging King honestly means refusing to treat his thought as a finished blueprint. It means asking, with the “divine dissatisfaction” he admired in Du Bois, whether some of the most cherished commitments of his mature thought must themselves be thrown off, just as the middle-class values were, in the pursuit of justice. That is the challenge Terry’s essay invites, and it is one that his generous and acute reading makes it impossible to avoid.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Brandon M. Terry’s essay performs a vital rescue mission, reclaiming Martin Luther King, Jr. from the sanitized, mythological monument he has become in the American popular imagination. Terry correctly diagnoses the "costs of canonization," illustrating how conservative gaslighting and liberal complacency have stripped King of his radicalism, turning a fierce critic of American empire and economic exploitation into a defanged mascot for a mythical colorblind consensus. By stripping away this accumulated mythology, Terry reveals a King who is not merely an inspirational orator, but a rigorous, systematic political philosopher whose ideas remain urgently necessary for our current political crises.

Terry’s breakdown of King’s tripartite theory of racism—affective, sociopolitical, and cognitive—is particularly illuminating for our moment in 2018. By recognizing that racism is fueled by irrational fears and entrenched in the nation's "congenital deformity," King avoided the trap of viewing racial injustice as a mere failure of individual morals or a lack of education. As Terry astutely observes, when we lose this nuanced, multifaceted understanding of racism, we fall into unproductive contemporary traps. We either reduce anti-racism to the corporate superficiality of "diversity training" and "consciousness-raising," or we swing to the opposite extreme of totalizing racial pessimism.

Terry’s critique of this latter camp—pointing to thinkers like Ta-Nehisi Coates and Calvin Warren—is one of the essay's strongest interventions. If white supremacy is treated as a permanent "force of nature" or a totalizing physical law, then political action is reduced to mere aesthetic defiance or cynical nihilism. King’s systemic focus, conversely, maintained that racism interacts with contingent, shifting forces like political economy, automation, and class differentiation. By recognizing racism as a formidable but ultimately malleable historical construct, King preserved the intellectual foundation for radical hope and structural change.

This systemic understanding is precisely what drove King to embrace the coercive power of mass civil disobedience. We frequently remember the King of the "I Have a Dream" speech, but Terry draws our attention to the King of the Birmingham jail and the Chicago slums. Direct action, in King’s mature thought, was not simply a moral appeal to the conscience of the majority, nor was it strictly limited to breaking unjust laws. It was a mechanism to create a crisis—a deliberate structural disruption designed to force negotiation and make the invisible suffering of the marginalized impossible to ignore. Furthermore, Terry rightly emphasizes King’s insistence that this coercive disobedience be deployed against economic injustice, a point that resonates deeply today as automation and vast wealth inequality continue to hollow out the American working class.

Yet, this brings us to the most provocative tension in Terry’s essay: the relationship between coercion and love. King insisted that disruptive mass action must be bound by *agape* and a strict adherence to nonviolence. In our era, where respectability politics are rightly viewed with suspicion by young activists, King’s moral demands can easily be misinterpreted. Terry brilliantly defends King by drawing a distinction between a "politics of respectability" (supplicating to white norms to prove one's fitness for citizenship) and a "politics of character." King’s emphasis on humility, generosity, and nonviolence was not about pleasing the oppressor, but about securing the dignity, self-respect, and ethical flourishing of the oppressed themselves.

However, we must ask whether demanding this "politics of character" places an uneven and perhaps unsustainable burden on marginalized people in 2018. Asking the oppressed to bear the weight of both structural disruption and profound moral elevation—to maintain *agape* toward a society that wields police violence and state terror against them—remains an incredibly tall order. Amidst the resurgence of overt white nationalism, emboldened by the highest political offices, the anger of younger activists in the Movement for Black Lives is not merely a failure of discipline; it is a highly rational response to seemingly intractable hostility.

Terry acknowledges this, noting that King did not deny righteous anger but sought to redirect it away from corrosive hatred. But in a decentralized, hyper-mediated digital age where algorithms reward outrage and balkanize our media consumption, channeling mass anger into disciplined, loving confrontation is fundamentally harder than it was during the mid-century civil rights movement. The institutional anchors that sustained King’s vision, particularly the Black church, are weakened, and the theatrical power of the street march has been diluted by routine and militarized police management.

To his immense credit, Terry is intellectually honest in identifying King's blind spots, recognizing that we cannot simply transplant King’s model into the present without modification. Terry rightly insists that we must amend King’s "triple evils" to include a fourth: sexism. King's reliance on patriarchal, charismatic leadership and his essentialist views on gender are glaring weaknesses. It is precisely in these areas that contemporary organizers have successfully evolved beyond King’s model, building decentralized, queer-inclusive, and women-led coalitions that reflect a more expansive vision of liberation.

Ultimately, Terry’s essay succeeds because it refuses to treat Martin Luther King, Jr. as a flawless idol. Instead, it invites us to engage him as a profound interlocutor. King’s legacy is not a static roadmap but a philosophical framework—one that demands we continuously and experimentally rethink the morphology of protest. By embracing the friction between King’s demand for radical economic coercion and his unwavering commitment to ethical love, we can find the conceptual tools necessary to resist the authoritarianism and nihilism of our own time.

Brandon Terry is right that the most damaging misremembering of King is not simple ignorance but overfamiliarity. The national King holiday, the schoolroom “I Have a Dream,” the ritual invocation of “content of their character” have made King available to almost everyone and accountable to almost no one. Conservatives can conscript him into colorblindness; liberals can admire him as a saint of civility; radicals can dismiss him as the mascot of a spent integrationism. All three responses evade the harder King: the critic of capitalism, militarism, and white supremacy; the organizer willing to manufacture crisis; the democratic moralist who believed that hatred corrodes the hater without thereby excusing the injustice that produces rage.

But if we are to retrieve King for the present, we should be careful not to turn a richer King into another kind of icon. The temptation is not only to domesticate him, but also to make him answer every contemporary question. Terry’s King is a powerful interlocutor because he holds together what our politics often tears apart: structure and agency, anger and discipline, coercion and love, race and political economy. Yet the very breadth of this synthesis should remind us that King’s achievement was not merely intellectual or ethical. It was institutional. His virtues were housed in organizations, churches, training sessions, unions, newspapers, local networks, and national campaigns. Without those social forms, “nonviolence” risks becoming either a personality trait demanded of the oppressed or a theatrical tactic performed for cameras that no longer shame the powerful.

This matters especially for Terry’s discussion of love and hatred. King’s ethic of agape remains indispensable, not because victims owe sentimental affection to their oppressors, but because democratic politics requires a way to fight enemies without making permanent enmity the ground of collective life. In the age of Donald Trump, Charlottesville, family separation, police killings, and a public sphere organized around provocation, the discipline of refusing hatred is not meekness. It is a form of political self-defense. Hatred simplifies the world at precisely the moment when movements need subtle judgment: whom to persuade, whom to isolate, when to disrupt, when to negotiate, when to forgive, and when forgiveness would be a lie.

Still, exhortations to love can become cruel when detached from power. King did not ask black people simply to be morally beautiful in the face of degradation. He asked them to join organizations capable of converting suffering into leverage. The Montgomery bus boycott, Birmingham, Selma, Chicago, and the Poor People’s Campaign were not exercises in moral witness alone. They targeted transit systems, merchants, courts, police departments, federal officials, landlords, employers, and the conscience of an international public. They made injustice costly. That is why the phrase “nonviolent coercion,” which Terry emphasizes, is so important. King’s love was not a substitute for power; it was a discipline for wielding power without reproducing domination.

The contemporary left has often understood the need for disruption, but it has struggled to build durable forms that can survive beyond moments of outrage. Black Lives Matter forced the country to confront police violence and the disposability of black life; the Fight for $15 made low-wage work a matter of public justice; immigrant-rights activists have dramatized the violence of deportation; the teachers’ strikes of 2018 have shown that labor militancy can still command broad sympathy when it speaks in the language of common goods. Yet these movements also reveal the difficulty of sustaining democratic organization in a fragmented media environment. A viral video can ignite protest, but it cannot by itself train leaders, settle strategy, build trust, or negotiate victory.

Here King’s example points less to a single tactic than to a political ecology. He depended on institutions that were themselves morally compromised: patriarchal churches, cautious civil rights organizations, male charismatic leadership, Cold War liberal alliances, and labor unions that were often racially exclusionary. Terry is right to insist that sexism must be named as a fourth evil, not treated as a footnote. But the lesson is not that movements can wait for pure institutions. It is that democratic struggle requires building and reforming institutions at the same time. The black church could sustain courage and reproduce hierarchy; unions could fight exploitation and exclude black workers; electoral politics could open doors and discipline radical demands. King’s genius was not purity. It was the capacity to work amid contradiction without surrendering the horizon of justice.

That horizon also demands greater attention to the state. King’s late radicalism was not anti-statist. His calls for guaranteed income, full employment, fair housing, demilitarization, and massive anti-poverty spending presumed democratic control over public resources. This is one reason the easy contrast between protest and electoral politics fails. Direct action can create crisis, but crisis must be translated into law, budgets, administrative power, and enforceable rights. The voter suppression battles of our own moment, the weakening of unions after Janus, and the capture of courts by conservative legal movements all show that movements cannot disdain institutions of governance. If they do, their opponents will not.

King’s relevance in 2018, then, lies not in providing a usable saint but in posing a demanding question: what forms of collective life can make disciplined, disruptive, multiracial democracy possible now? The answer cannot be nostalgia for the midcentury church, nor confidence that social media will spontaneously assemble “the people,” nor a politics of denunciation that mistakes moral clarity for power. It will require organizations that can absorb anger without being ruled by it, practice solidarity across real differences, contest elections without becoming captive to parties, and use disruption to force material concessions.

Terry’s essay helps restore King’s radicalism. The next step is to restore his realism. King believed that America could change, but he never believed it would change because it was asked politely. He believed it would change only when organized people made injustice impossible to ignore and harder to maintain. That remains the task.

The essay that precedes this one performs a valuable service. Brandon M. Terry is right that Martin Luther King Jr. has been calcified into a symbol of consensus and conciliation, and right that this falsifies his actual intellectual and political legacy. His careful parsing of King's threefold analysis of racism—its affective, sociopolitical, and cognitive dimensions—offers genuine insight into how King understood the problem he was combating. And his insistence that King's theory of civil disobedience was less about moral exception and more about coercive disruption deserves wider recognition. These are real contributions to public understanding.

Yet something in the essay's governing impulse gives me pause. Terry frames his project as one of "recovery" and "retrieval"—of excavating King from beneath the debris of sentimental commemoration so that contemporary activists might benefit from his example. This framing, while understandable, carries its own ideological freight. The language of retrieval presupposes that what we need from the past is usable models, exemplary figures whose authority can be marshaled for present purposes. It positions the responder as a kind of curator, restoring a canonical figure to working condition.

There is a paternalism built into this gesture, even when it is animated by genuine radical sympathy. When Terry writes that King "calls on us to think and argue publicly about the crises of our present," he presents King's thought as a resource waiting to be tapped. But the movements Terry implicitly wants to serve—Black Lives Matter, the Movement for Black Lives, the various coalitions mobilizing around economic justice—are not suffering from a deficit of intellectual exemplars. They are suffering from concrete material conditions: decades of deindustrialization, the hollowing of labor organization, the architecture of carceral control, the relentless privatization of public life. What would it mean to take King's challenge seriously not as a thinker to be retrieved but as a provocation to be surpassed?

Terry himself gestures toward this possibility when he notes King's blindness to gendered dimensions of authority within the civil rights movement, or when he acknowledges that the black church—a crucial institutional substrate of King's thought—is "profoundly weakened" in contemporary conditions. These admissions are significant, yet the essay does not fully follow their logic. If King's thought is inseparable from institutions and traditions that have been substantially eroded, what does it mean to retrieve him for an era that has inherited his critique without his organizational infrastructure?

I am also uncertain about Terry's treatment of King's Christian ethics, which he defends against "suspicion" in a secular age. Terry is right to resist the reduction of King's ethics to "respectability politics," and his distinction between the two senses of respectability—one supplicatory, one concerned with the social bases of virtue—is illuminating. But the deeper question is whether agape, as King theorized it, can be translated into secular terms without losing its critical purchase. Terry suggests it can, pointing to philosophers like Tommie Shelby who have sought to recast King's commitments in Rawlsian language. Perhaps. But there is a risk in this translation that Terry does not adequately explore: the risk that what looks like King's ethics, once translated into procedural terms, becomes another version of liberal moralism—the duty to reason fairly with those who harm us, stripped of the transformative vision that made King's practice something more than moral suasion.

Terry's most original argument concerns nonviolence's capacity to destabilize racist ideology by defying its predictions. The racist worldview, he notes, expects humiliated subjects to return humiliation. Nonviolent resistance disrupts this loop, creating what King called "new possibilities for living together." This is genuinely insightful, and it helps explain why King valued nonviolence not merely as tactical calculation but as a kind of political epistemology. Yet Terry does not fully reckon with the structural conditions that once made this disruption possible. Mass nonviolent action worked in the 1960s partly because it was broadcast through a relatively consolidated media landscape that could not easily look away. Today's media environment is fragmented, and the spectacle of suffering that once shamed national conscience can be algorithmically contained within niche audiences. King could assume that if he created sufficient crisis, the world would have to watch. That assumption no longer holds.

None of this diminishes the importance of Terry's core argument: that King deserves to be engaged as a thinker, not just quoted as an icon. That argument is correct and necessary. My concern is that it does not go far enough. The question is not only whether we retrieve King more faithfully but whether the very gesture of retrieval, however well-executed, addresses the deeper problem Terry identifies. King challenged his era to confront its complicity in violence and poverty. He called for nothing less than the transformation of the basic structure of society. The Pessimist's Patience: On What Terry Cannot Recover

Brandon Terry's essay is the most generous and the most demanding portrait of Martin Luther King, Jr., I have read in some time. It rescues King from the mausoleum of consensus liberalism and offers him back to us as a working political philosopher—one whose ideas about racism, civil disobedience, and ethical virtue might still organize a serious left politics. I want to honor that achievement while pressing on the place in Terry's argument that seems to me most strained: his impatience with what he calls "racial pessimism," and his too-quick dismissal of figures like Calvin Warren and (in a different register) Ta-Nehisi Coates.

Terry's complaint is roughly this: pessimism inflates racism into a "force of nature," forecloses hope, and reduces black politics to "fugitive gestures of dissent and denunciation." This is a real tendency, and it deserves the critique. But Terry's framing risks the inverse mistake. By making hope a kind of cognitive duty—"the value in pulling racism out of its obscurity… lies in the confidence that it can be changed"—he can sound as though the moral cost of despair is so heavy that despair is forbidden, regardless of the evidence. That is not King's position, properly understood, and it should not be ours.

Consider what King himself was lamenting in 1967 and 1968. The Civil Rights Act and the Voting Rights Act were on the books; the moral spectacle of Birmingham and Selma had already been performed; the legislative high tide had broken. And yet the riots were burning, Vietnam was metastasizing, the Chicago campaign had been turned back by the most ferocious white mob King ever encountered, and the Poor People's Campaign was struggling for breath. King's late writings are shot through with a tone that, if any of us wrote it today, Terry might call pessimist. "I have almost reached the regrettable conclusion that the Negro's great stumbling block in his stride toward freedom is not the White Citizens Counciler or the Ku Klux Klanner, but the white moderate." "We have moved from the era of civil rights to the era of human rights." "The dream I had in Washington has, in many points, turned into a nightmare." This is not the language of confident progress. It is the language of a man who has begun to suspect that the country he loves may be structurally incapable of the justice it advertises, and who is choosing, in spite of that suspicion, to keep working.

That choice—to keep working in spite of—is what Terry wants to defend, and it is what I want to defend too. But it is not the same as the cognitive optimism Terry sometimes seems to require. The pessimist's question is whether the long history of American racial domination warrants empirical confidence that mass nonviolent action can dislodge it. To that question, fifty years after King's assassination, the answer is genuinely contested. The Voting Rights Act has been gutted by Shelby County. The racial wealth gap is roughly what it was in 1968. Mass incarceration has built a carceral state of a magnitude unimaginable to King's generation. The Movement for Black Lives, which has done extraordinary organizing since Ferguson, has generated more police body cameras than convictions. To insist on hope in the face of this record is a moral choice; it cannot be smuggled in as a theoretical premise.

Terry's strongest move against pessimism is the charge that it makes everything "intrinsically heroic"—that it lets black life under late capitalism off the hook of political judgment. There is something to this. When self-care, endurance, and even nihilism all become forms of resistance, the concept of resistance has lost its content. But the inverse danger, which Terry underweights, is that the King of his recovery becomes a figure who is always available to scold black activists for tactical excess—for "humiliating the opponent," for failing to "win his friendship and understanding," for the slippage of anger into hatred. The Carnegie Hall King who praised Du Bois's communism, who refused to be embarrassed by the word "radical," who in his last year was talking about restructuring the whole of American society—this King is harder to instrumentalize in that way. But the King of agape, of "unearned suffering is redemptive," of "love your enemies," remains very easy to weaponize against the actual movements doing the work. Terry knows this; he warns explicitly against "critics of the left" who exhort reconciliation without insisting on justice. But the warning is brief, while the chapter on the corrosiveness of anger is long.

Here is where I think the pessimists have something Terry needs. Warren's argument is not just that hope is foolish. It is that the political grammar of "appeal"—the spectacle of black suffering offered to a white public capable of being shamed into recognition—has been exhausted as a tactic, because the white public it presupposed no longer exists, if it ever did. Terry actually concedes much of this in his closing pages: cameras have proliferated, suffering images have saturated, "the idea that Americans will be ashamed in the eyes of the world… now seems quaint." But if the audience for redemptive sacrifice has dispersed, then King's coercive realism—the Birmingham realism, the realism that knew Kennedy moved only when the streets burned—becomes more important than his agape, not less. Malcolm's accusation in "Message to the Grass Roots" was not, in fact, refuted by King; it was structurally vindicated by him. The civil rights bill came after the bombing of King's brother's house, not after the Lincoln Memorial speech.

This is the place where I would push Terry hardest. He treats Malcolm's challenge as a "still challenging contention" and then moves on. But it is more than challenging; it may be decisive for our present. If nonviolent direct action works in part because of the implicit, ambient possibility of violent rebellion, then the ethics of "love thy enemy" cannot do the political work King and Terry assign to it. Love is, at best, the discipline that prevents the implicit threat from becoming an explicit catastrophe; it is not the engine of change. To say this is not to license violence. It is to say that the moral economy of American protest has always been more brutal than King's homilies allow, and that any honest recovery of King has to sit with that.

What I want from Terry, then, is less defensiveness toward the pessimists and more willingness to absorb their lesson. The lesson is not that hope is foolish. It is that hope, if it is to be more than sentiment, has to be earned against a long record of disappointment, and that the political work of earning it cannot rely on the same affective machinery—shame, conversion, redemptive suffering—that produced earlier disappointments. Terry's King, the one who organizes Chicago gangs and demands a guaranteed income and calls America the greatest purveyor of violence in the world, is the King who took that lesson most seriously. That King is, in the technical sense, a pessimist about America and a hopeful actor within it. The distinction matters. Without it, "MLK Now" risks becoming another, subtler form of the canonization Terry sets out to refuse: a King available to chasten black anger, smooth out the rough edges of black radicalism, and absolve a liberal readership of the harder question of what, exactly, it is now willing to lose.

In Defense of the Consensus King

Brandon Terry's essay is learned, generous, and almost certainly wrong about the King we ought to teach our children. Terry wants to free King from "canonization" and return him to us as a radical political thinker, comparable to Du Bois, whose challenges to American capitalism, militarism, and complacency we have buried under marble and quotation. The intellectual King he recovers is real. But Terry's claim that the popular King—the King of consensus, of "I Have a Dream," of the Memorial on the Mall—is a distortion that "leads our political and moral judgment astray" mistakes the function of a public icon for the function of a scholarly subject. The consensus King is not a falsehood. He is an achievement, and it is worth defending him against his own admirers.

Begin with what Terry concedes. King was a Christian minister who quoted Jefferson and Lincoln; who framed the civil rights struggle as a demand that America "live out the true meaning of its creed"; who repeatedly invoked the Declaration and the Constitution as moral resources; who insisted that the movement's coercive force be channeled through love and nonviolence; who believed in interracial brotherhood as a regulative ideal, not a rhetorical pose. Cass Sunstein's characterization of the movement as "backward looking and even conservative" is not a smear. It is an accurate description of the deepest stratum of King's public argument: that America's founding promises, properly read, condemn American practice, and that the work of the movement is to redeem the promise rather than discard it.

Terry concedes this when he describes King's "unapologetic identification" with the "democratic spirit" that has "always faced" American racism. That is the consensus King. The point Terry then wants to make—that King was also a critic of militarism, capitalism, and municipal segregation—does not undermine the consensus reading. It complicates it. King was a radical reformer working within an essentially liberal democratic tradition. He was not a revolutionary. He believed in elections, in constitutional amendment, in the possibility of moral conversion of one's opponents. He worked with presidents. He was willing to discipline his own movement to keep faith with the broader public. These are not the marks of a thinker who thought, with Calvin Warren, that American democracy was a horizon "that will never come."

What Terry calls "canonization" is in fact a public memory that has done extraordinary moral work. The King on the Mall has been used to discredit white nationalism, to expand the moral imagination of generations of schoolchildren, to give Americans a shared figure across regional and racial divides at a moment when shared figures are vanishing. That King has done more to delegitimize racism in this country than any other single rhetorical resource of the last fifty years. To dismiss this as "gaslighting" because it has also been used by conservatives in colorblind constitutional arguments is to confuse a downstream tactical complaint with the upstream achievement. The fact that some conservatives quote "content of their character" against affirmative action does not mean the line itself was a strategic concession to racism. It means it was a sentence powerful enough that even its opponents wanted to claim it.

Terry's preferred King—the King of guaranteed income, of automation as a labor crisis, of Vietnam as imperial atrocity, of "the greatest purveyor of violence in the world"—is the King of 1966 to 1968. That King was lonely, embattled, and politically failing. The Chicago campaign was a defeat. The Poor People's Campaign was struggling before his death and collapsed after it. His Riverside Church speech on Vietnam alienated the White House, much of the civil rights establishment, and the editorial pages of the country's major newspapers. The historical evidence is that the late, radical King could not build the coalition the early, integrationist King had built, and that his strategic intuitions in those last years—however morally admirable—did not deliver legislative or institutional wins comparable to 1964 and 1965. This is not a knock on him. The work of the prophet is not always the work of the politician. But it does suggest that Terry's revisionism is asking us to elevate the King who was losing over the King who was winning, and to do so for reasons that have more to do with our present discontents than with King's own assessment of what worked.

There is also a question of public theology that Terry treats too quickly. He worries that King's Christianity is now an "impediment" to "the unchurched" and wonders whether King's ethics can be "rethought in other institutions and traditions." This is the wrong worry. King's authority in American public life rests on his being a Christian minister speaking in a recognizably scriptural register to a country that, however imperfectly, still understands itself through that register. The Birmingham letter is unintelligible without Augustine and Aquinas. The Mountaintop speech is unintelligible without Moses. To strip King of his theology in order to make him available to a secular left is to keep his rhetoric while losing what made the rhetoric land. It will not work, and the attempt to make it work has the unpleasant side effect of treating the actual faith of the actual man as an embarrassment to be managed.

This brings me to what I take to be Terry's most useful insight, around which I would build a different essay than he does. Terry distinguishes two senses of "respectability politics": the strategic concern with comportment as a tool of persuasion, and the deeper ethical concern with the cultivation of virtues necessary for a good human life. He rightly notes that King held the second view robustly: humility, compassion, courage, magnanimity, thrift. Terry treats this as a residual element of King's thought that contemporary critics misunderstand. I would put it more strongly. This is the heart of King, not a residual. The reason King's example continues to matter—and why he can be commemorated by Americans who do not share his policy preferences—is that he embodied a public character of a kind that has become exceedingly rare. The dignity, the discipline, the refusal of cheap hatred, the willingness to suffer arrest with composure: these are not decorative. They are the substance of why he became, and remains, the figure he is.

The consensus King, then, is not a sanitized King. He is a King whose particular greatness consisted in the integration of radical demand with civic virtue. He could be uncompromising about Vietnam and gracious to Lyndon Johnson; he could lead boycotts and refuse to humiliate the boycotted; he could be arrested forty times and never act with contempt for the arresting officer. This integration—not the radicalism alone, not the gradualism alone—is what generations of Americans have rightly intuited when they place him on the Mall. Terry's recovery of the radical King is a useful scholarly intervention. It should not become an argument for replacing the public King with a different, sharper, less reconciling figure. The public King is closer to the truth of the man than Terry will admit, and the country still needs him.

The Fourth Evil Is Not an Appendix

Brandon Terry deserves credit for what most King scholarship still avoids: acknowledging, in plain language, that "any retrieval of King's legacy has to amend his triple evils to include a fourth: sexism." That sentence appears, in Terry's essay, almost as a parenthesis, in a paragraph that also gestures at King's "essentialist views on gender and normative views on the family" and their "severe logical and moral failures." Terry, who has co-authored important work on this with Shatema Threadcraft, certainly knows the scale of what he is conceding. But the form of the concession—as a brief admission embedded in a long defense—reveals a deeper problem in the essay's structure. The fourth evil is not an appendix. If you take it seriously, it reshapes the other three. And once it does, the picture of King that emerges is less the "profound interlocutor" Terry wants us to engage and more an intellectual whose categories require thorough reconstruction before they can do much work for us today.

Begin with the analysis of racism. Terry praises King's three-part model: irrational fear, sociopolitical structure, cognitive and empathetic failure. Each component is illuminating. But each was developed by a thinker who, in practice and in print, treated black women as the supporting cast of black liberation. The "irrational fears" King catalogues are fears coded in the public imagination as white male fears—loss of standing, contamination, retaliation—and the political object of those fears is also coded male: the black man who might vote, work, marry across the color line, retaliate. King's analysis is not wrong about this, but it is partial. The fears that drive the policing of black women—fears of black maternal "deviance," of welfare "queens," of black women's sexuality as a threat to white reproductive norms—operate by a different logic, and they have been at least as politically consequential in shaping American racial policy from Moynihan to the welfare reform debates Terry knows well. A theory of racism that does not metabolize gender at its analytic core will keep missing them.

The sociopolitical component fares no better. King's account of "congenital deformity" and the "democratic spirit" that opposes it is a story of public political contest: voting, legislation, civil disobedience, public discourse. It is a story whose actors are the kinds of people who appear in public—preachers, lawyers, soldiers, students, marchers. It is not equipped to see the sites where racial domination was, and is, reproduced through the everyday governance of households, bodies, and reproduction: the convict-leasing of black women, the sterilization campaigns of the mid-century, the domestic-worker exclusions from the New Deal labor regime, the welfare surveillance that turned black motherhood into a managed status. These are not addenda. They are the infrastructure of American racial capitalism. To leave them out of one's foundational analysis is to define racism in a way that systematically privileges the experiences of black men who could imagine themselves as protagonists of a public political drama.

The third component, the cognitive one, is in some ways the most damaging to leave ungendered. King's powerful argument that mass disciplined protest "dissolved the stereotype of the grinning, submissive Uncle Tom" and forced America to see black people as "active organs of change" is true—and it is true about a stereotype of black masculinity. The stereotypes that fixed black women were different: the Mammy, the Jezebel, the matriarch. None of them was dissolved by the march on Washington. Some have been intensified by the very dynamics of black male public heroism that the civil rights movement generated. When King's wife was made to play, even in his own household, the role of the supportive helpmeet to the great man, that was not a private failure. It was an expression of an ideological structure that the movement reproduced precisely as it was confronting its racial counterpart.

Once you see this, the theory of civil disobedience that Terry praises also looks different. Terry quotes King's celebration of nonviolent direct action as having a "magnificent universal quality" that transcends "gender, age, physical disability, education, and wealth." This is true in principle and was demonstrably false in practice. Ella Baker had to fight King and the SCLC leadership to be heard. Septima Clark's citizenship schools, which built the actual organizational base of the movement in much of the South, were undervalued in the SCLC budget while King's appearances were not. Dorothy Cotton's role in the Citizenship Education Program is barely remembered. The "magnificent universality" of nonviolent protest was, in the King-led wing of the movement, organized through profoundly hierarchical structures of charismatic male authority, often grounded in the gender norms of the black Baptist church. Terry mentions this. He does not let it touch the theory.

The ethics fares similarly. Terry's most lyrical pages defend King's emphasis on love, magnanimity, humility, and the disciplined channeling of anger. These virtues are not gendered in their content. But they have been gendered in their distribution. The demand that the oppressed not slip into "corrosive" anger, that they refuse to "humiliate the opponent," that they maintain ethical composure under conditions of degradation—this demand has been pressed hardest, in American public life, against black women. The "angry black woman" is not a stereotype invented yesterday. It has been deployed against figures from Ida B. Wells to Shirley Chisholm to the founders of the Movement for Black Lives. To recover, in 2018, an ethics that warns above all against the slippage of anger into hatred is to recover a posture that, however philosophically defensible in the abstract, is bound to be applied unequally in practice. Terry knows this; he wants the demand to "cut in two directions" and not to license complacency. But the cut never quite seems to fall the same way for both sexes.

I do not want to argue, against Terry, that King should be abandoned. The Movement for Black Lives' founders—three black women, two of them queer—have not abandoned him, even when they have chosen Audre Lorde or Assata Shakur as more proximate ancestors. They have done something more interesting: they have built an organizing practice that is decentralized, anti-charismatic, explicitly feminist, and explicitly indebted to King's tactical vocabulary. That is what taking King seriously while taking the fourth evil seriously actually looks like. It is not the addition of a sentence about sexism to a fundamentally untouched theoretical edifice. It is the willingness to rebuild the edifice when the additional weight reveals which beams were never load-bearing in the first place.

Terry ends his essay by saying the spirit of King is most alive when we "embrace the challenges" of new ideas, new sacrifices, new movements. I agree. The challenge his own essay underweights is the one his own footnote acknowledges: that the most generative inheritors of King are working in a register he himself did not learn to speak. To honor him is to keep going where he could not.

The Economic King and the Limits of Recognition

Brandon Terry's essay is at its most powerful when it places King's economic radicalism at the center of the picture. The figure who took on Vietnam and the Poor People's Campaign in his last year, who endorsed a guaranteed income and a federal job guarantee, who saw automation as a coming crisis for labor as a whole rather than for black workers alone, who organized in Chicago's slums against ghettoization as a problem of housing markets and not just hearts—this King has been almost entirely erased from public memory, and Terry's essay is among the better recent attempts to put him back. What I want to do is push that part of Terry's argument further than he himself takes it, because I think the economic King is not just one strand of the legacy. He is the King most relevant to our moment, and recovering him requires a sharper break with certain habits of contemporary racial discourse than Terry is willing to make.

The opening chapters of Why We Can't Wait and Where Do We Go from Here read, in 2018, as eerily prescient. King argues that without a serious confrontation with structural economic change—automation, capital mobility, the decline of stable industrial employment—civil rights victories would prove hollow. He noticed before almost anyone else that legal desegregation, even at its most successful, would deliver only formal access to institutions that were already being hollowed out by economic forces operating beneath them. A black worker who wins the right to apply for a job in a unionized auto plant has won something real, but he has won less if the plant is being moved to Tennessee, then to Mexico, then automated out of existence altogether. King saw this. He was not always right about the timing or the policy response, but he was right about the trajectory. Fifty years on, the wages of non-supervisory workers are roughly where they were in 1973 in real terms; private-sector union density is below seven percent; and the wealth gap between black and white households remains, on every available measure, unchanged or worse than at the time of his death.

This is the King who matters now, and Terry knows it. But Terry's essay is also embedded in a contemporary conversation in which the dominant frame for racial injustice is recognition—the unmasking of unconscious bias, the diagnosis of white supremacy as a totalizing cultural phenomenon, the demand for representation, the policing of cultural appropriation, the proliferation of diversity trainings. Terry is appropriately critical of these moves at the level of theory; he warns against treating "everything—from Hollywood awards and university syllabi to police violence and mass shootings—as all part of a white supremacist totality." But the warning is delivered en passant. The bulk of his essay is devoted to elaborating King's theory of racism in ways that contemporary recognition politics will recognize as congenial—the affective dimension, the ideological dimension, the cognitive dimension—without ever quite landing the harder point that King's mature politics treated all of these as subordinate to, or at least inextricable from, a confrontation with class.

Consider King's most famous late move: the decision to launch a Poor People's Campaign whose constituency was explicitly multiracial. This was not a tactical concession to coalition building. It was a theoretical claim about what justice now required. King had concluded that the next stage of the struggle could not be framed as a black struggle. Not because black-specific injustices had been redressed—they manifestly had not—but because the structural transformations he saw coming would create a population of dispossessed Americans, white and black and brown, whose interests were objectively aligned, and whose alignment was being prevented by exactly the racial ideologies the civil rights movement had been fighting. To divide the dispossessed along the color line was, for King, the central political achievement of American capital, and any movement that could not contest that division would lose.

This argument has a sharp implication that contemporary racial discourse tends to refuse. If King was right, then a politics that treats whiteness as itself the enemy, that frames every social conflict as an instance of white supremacy, that makes the unmasking of white psychological pathology its central rhetorical task—such a politics, however emotionally satisfying, is doing the work of capital. It maintains the division King was trying to break. It generates white reaction without generating multiracial coalition. It produces a public sphere in which racial grievance and counter-grievance circulate endlessly while the underlying questions of who owns what, who works for whom, and on what terms, remain untouched.

Terry will not say this, and I think his reticence is the major lacuna in an otherwise admirable essay. He criticizes the inflation of "racism" as an explanatory category. He notes that black disadvantage is the product of multiple interacting phenomena, including "technology, political economy, and cultural patterns." But these acknowledgements are made in the language of analytic precision, not in the language of political strategy. He never quite says what King said directly: that black politics had to be embedded in a broader class politics or it would not deliver black liberation, and that the chief obstacle to such embedding was not white workers' racism alone but black activists' own occasional refusal of class as the relevant category.

The Chicago campaign is the clearest case. King went to Chicago to fight ghettoization—a project that required, simultaneously, an attack on racist housing policy and an attack on the broader political economy of urban property. He sought to organize gang members, as Terry notes, but he also sought to organize white ethnic neighborhoods, with mixed and often miserable results. The lesson he drew from Chicago was not that white northerners were irredeemable racists. It was that the political economy of American cities had been engineered to make cross-racial working-class coalitions almost impossible, and that breaking that engineering would take a movement far larger and more disciplined than anything the civil rights coalition had so far produced. That is the political project the Poor People's Campaign was meant to inaugurate.

Where does this leave us? The King Terry wants to recover is a thinker whose theory of racism is genuinely powerful but whose strategic conclusion was that racism cannot be defeated on its own terms. It has to be defeated through a politics that recomposes the working majority of the country across the color line. Such a politics is harder, less ideologically satisfying, and slower than the alternatives currently on offer. It requires admitting that white workers are not the enemy. It requires admitting that black workers, white workers, Latino workers, and Asian workers have material interests in common that exceed their cultural differences. It requires the kind of patient, institution-building, coalitional labor that does not produce dramatic moments for social media. It is, in short, the politics of the Poor People's Campaign.

That campaign is the part of King's legacy least picked up by his heirs. Terry gestures at it; he should have made it the center. Until we do, we will keep mistaking our discomforts for our injuries, and our injuries for the whole of our condition.

Can Agape Survive Without God? On the Theological Stakes of Terry's King

Toward the end of his rich essay, Brandon Terry pauses over what he calls the "knotty metaphysics of natural law and Christian ethics" that grounds King's case for nonviolent direct action. He concedes that, "in our more secular and pluralist era," these roots "may invite suspicion," and he wonders whether King's vision can be "suitably rethought in other institutions and traditions." The wondering is brief. Terry then proceeds, in the body of his essay, to translate King's ethical vocabulary—agape, redemptive suffering, the beloved community, the virtues of magnanimity and humility—into a register that is largely secular, philosophical, and Rawlsian. I want to argue that this translation, however well-intentioned, cannot succeed; that the most important things King has to teach us cannot be detached from the theology that produced them; and that any serious recovery of King for our moment has to confront, rather than finesse, what the loss of his religious world does to his politics.

Begin with agape. Terry follows King in distinguishing agape from erotic and romantic love and in defining it as the disposition that treats even political enemies as moral equals whose welfare we must consider. So described, agape can sound like a philosophical doctrine—a kind of universalist Kantianism, a maximally generous reading of the moral law. But that is not what agape is in King's actual usage. Agape, for King, is a participation in the love of God for human beings. It is grounded in the conviction that every person bears the image of God, that this image is not extinguishable by any sin or oppression, and that the appropriate human response to the image is a love that mirrors God's. Strip the theology away and you have a sentiment without a foundation. You can say, with the secular philosopher, that we ought to treat each person as an end in herself. You cannot say, in the same way, that we are commanded to love our enemies, that this love is possible, and that practicing it draws us into a redemptive process whose outcome is guaranteed by something beyond our political failures. The latter set of claims is what gives King's nonviolence its psychological power and its strategic patience. The former set, by itself, is consistent with despair.

The same point applies to redemptive suffering. Kenneth Clark's complaint to King—that it is "too much" to expect victims of cruelty to love their tormentors—reads, in a secular frame, as devastating. And in fact within secular ethics it is devastating. There is no philosophical resource that can convert the suffering of the oppressed into a meaningful contribution to the moral education of the oppressor. The suffering is just suffering. The torment is just torment. To volunteer for more of it, in the hope that it will work some change in the hearts of one's enemies, is, in a purely secular frame, masochism. Terry is honest enough to acknowledge this: "given the ample historical record of unrequited love, unredeemed sacrifice, and failed integration, it is easily argued that such sacrifices are not just beyond the duties of the disadvantaged but indeed masochistic." But he gestures past the worry rather than answering it. The answer King would give—that suffering accepted in love participates in the suffering of Christ and is taken up into the divine work of reconciliation—is not available to most contemporary readers, and Terry knows it. What he does not quite admit is that without that answer, the case for accepting suffering loses much of its force, and what remains is a tactical argument about how unearned suffering affects the consciences of bystanders. That argument was already weakening in King's lifetime, as Malcolm noted. In an age of saturation imagery and political polarization, it has weakened further. Terry says as much in his closing pages. He does not draw the implication: that without the theology, the practice of redemptive nonviolence is on borrowed time.

The point is not, of course, that we should all become Christians or that nonviolence cannot be defended at all on non-theological grounds. There are serious secular cases for nonviolent direct action—Gene Sharp's, for example—that rest on careful empirical analyses of when nonviolent campaigns succeed and why. Those cases are valuable. But they are not King's case. They cannot generate the demand to love one's enemy. They cannot vindicate the practice of suffering as such. They cannot ground the unconditional commitment to the dignity of the oppressor that distinguishes King's politics from a purely instrumental nonviolence. A secularized King is a King reduced to strategy, and a King reduced to strategy is a King whose strategy is in trouble.

Terry's deepest move in the essay is to offer, in place of King's metaphysics, an ethics of virtue: humility, compassion, generosity, courage, thrift, magnanimity. He is right that this language has been unjustly dismissed as "respectability politics" by readers who do not know Higginbotham. He is right that the cultivation of these virtues is essential to the flourishing of the activist and to the moral seriousness of any movement. But he is wrong, I think, to suppose that virtue ethics can be detached from a thicker account of human flourishing without losing its grip. Aristotle's virtues are intelligible only within the polis whose ends they serve. King's virtues are intelligible only within the beloved community whose realization they prepare us for. Without the eschatology—without the conviction that the arc of the moral universe is long but bends toward justice, because God is bending it—the practice of these virtues becomes a personal discipline, not a politics. It can sustain individuals. It cannot organize movements.

There is also a related institutional point, which Terry raises and then declines to develop. The black church was, for King and his generation, not merely a source of moral language. It was the organizational infrastructure of the movement. It provided meeting space, money, networks, training, song, and—decisively—a public sphere insulated from the white gaze, where black political life could be planned. The church is now, as Terry concedes, "a profoundly weakened institution." No comparable institution has emerged to replace it. The Movement for Black Lives is largely organized through social media and through nonprofits. These are powerful coordinating tools, but they cannot do what the church did, because they are not communities that meet weekly, hold one another accountable across decades, and educate children into a shared moral world. The depth of King's commitments was sustained by depth of community, and the depth of his community was sustained by the church. Take the church away and you do not get a secular movement of equivalent strength. You get something thinner and more brittle, however much it can mobilize in the short term.

What follows from all this? Not, I think, that we should abandon King. The contrary. It is that we should be honest, more honest than Terry quite manages to be, about what we are losing when we try to make him available to a post-Christian readership. The essay's treatment of King's ethical thought is likewise suggestive but incomplete. Terry emphasizes King's insistence on virtues like compassion, humility, and generosity, but he does not explore how these virtues might be cultivated in contemporary political contexts. The church-based communities that nurtured King's ethical vision have been weakened, as Terry acknowledges, by prosperity theology, political clientelism, and corruption. What institutional forms might replace them? Secular left movements have thus far failed to provide adequate alternatives, substituting ideology for ethics and mobilization for moral formation.

These gaps do not negate the essay's value. They indicate, rather, how much work remains to be done if King's legacy is to be genuinely recovered rather than merely invoked. The recovery requires not just accurate historical scholarship but creative adaptation—imagining how King's insights might speak to conditions he never faced, how his ethical vision might be cultivated without the institutional supports he enjoyed, and how his political strategies might be transformed for an era of fragmented media, weakened labor, and globalized capital.

I want to challenge the framework that underlies Terry's essay, not because I dispute the need to recover King's radicalism but because I think the very concept of recovery is misleading. The effort to retrieve King's "unadulterated" ideas treats them as a hidden treasure that can be unearthed and deployed, but this metaphor obscures more than it reveals. Ideas do not exist in isolation from the social contexts that give them meaning, and King's ideas were shaped by circumstances that cannot be replicated.

This is not an argument against studying King seriously. It is an argument against a certain kind of intellectual nostalgia that hopes to import King's conclusions wholesale into contemporary debates. The specific policy proposals King endorsed—basic income guarantees, full-employment programs, federal干预 in housing markets—were products of a particular moment in American political economy, when Keynesian assumptions dominated economic thought and the institutional capacity for such interventions still existed. This shift in emphasis from content to context has practical implications. Terry's essay focuses on King's ideas about racism, nonviolent action, and ethics, but it does not adequately address the question of how King's qualities might be cultivated in new leaders. Leadership development is one of the most neglected aspects of contemporary social movement theory; we analyze strategy and tactics but rarely ask how individuals develop the moral authority and political judgment that King exemplified.

Some will object that such questions are elitist—that they privilege a certain conception of leadership that King himself would have rejected. But King was clearly a leader of unusual moral and intellectual stature, and any movement that hopes to achieve lasting change must produce leaders of comparable quality. This does not mean finding King's successor; it means understanding the developmental pathways that shaped King's capacities and creating conditions under which similar capacities might emerge in new generations.

The contemporary political environment presents distinctive challenges for such leadership development. The 24-hour news cycle and social media create incentives for instant celebrity and shallow engagement; the fragmentation of civil society weakens the institutional supports for long-term moral formation; and the polarization of political discourse makes it difficult to build the kind of broad coalitions that King's leadership made possible. These obstacles are not insurmountable, but recognizing them is a prerequisite for addressing them.

Terry's essay would be strengthened by a more sustained reflection on these institutional questions. The essay's threefold schema is useful as a map of King's intellectual concerns, but it does not provide a guide for action. We need not only to understand King's ideas but to imagine how they might generate new institutional forms suited to contemporary conditions. This is a creative task that cannot be accomplished by historical scholarship alone, but historical scholarship—including the kind Terry performs—provides essential resources for the creative work ahead.

Terry's essay rightly laments the canonization of King and calls for engagement with his thought as a "profound interlocutor." But I want to push this argument further by suggesting that King's true significance lies not in his specific analyses or strategies but in his methodological approach to political judgment. King was not primarily a theorist but a practitioner who developed his ideas through engagement with concrete circumstances, and this experimental, adaptive quality of his thought is what contemporary activists most need to recover.

Political judgment—the capacity to discern what action is appropriate in given circumstances—is a virtue that cannot be reduced to principles or rules. It requires practical wisdom, which can only be developed through experience and reflection. King exemplified this virtue in his willingness to adapt strategy to circumstance, to learn from failures, and to revise his analysis as conditions changed. The consistency of his core commitments (nonviolent resistance, interracial solidarity, economic justice) coexisted with continuous adaptation in tactics and rhetoric.

This experimental quality of King's thought is visible throughout his career. Early in Montgomery, he emphasized the moral power of passive resistance to awaken the conscience of white moderates. By Birmingham, he had come to appreciate the coercive dimension of nonviolent direct action—the way mass protest could create crises that compelled negotiation. By Chicago, he was organizing in northern urban contexts quite different from southern rural ones, and his analysis shifted accordingly. This evolution was not inconsistency but intellectual growth driven by practical engagement.

Contemporary movements would benefit from adopting a similar experimental approach. Too often, activists become attached to particular tactics (social media mobilization, direct action, electoral engagement) or particular theories (structural racism, intersectionality, class-first analysis) as if these represented permanent insights rather than contextual adaptations. The history of social movement strategy offers abundant examples of tactics that succeeded in one context and failed in another, and any serious approach to political action must reckon with this variability.

Terry's essay offers King's analysis of racism as superior to alternative frameworks, but this framing risks becoming another fixed doctrine. King himself would have resisted such canonical status; he was engaged in ongoing inquiry, not the possession of final truth. The essay's threefold schema should be understood not as a completed theoretical structure but as a starting point for further inquiry, a set of questions to be asked rather than answers to be defended.

This approach has implications for how we understand King's legacy. King did not leave a manual for activists to follow but an example of how to think about political problems. His value lies not in the specific content of his positions but in the quality of judgment he brought to bear on difficult questions. How should activists balance the moral imperative to resist injustice against the strategic imperative to build lasting power? How can anger be channeled into effective action without being corrupted by hatred? What institutional forms might sustain moral commitment across generations?

These are the questions King faced, and they remain unanswered. Terry's essay helps us see that King took them seriously, but it cannot resolve them for us. The recovery of King's thought is not an end in itself but a resource for ongoing political and ethical reflection. In this sense, Terry's intervention is valuable precisely because it points beyond itself—toward the kind of serious public deliberation about justice and power that King exemplified and that democratic life requires.
