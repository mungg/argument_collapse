A recording of Donald Trump bragging in 2005 about his ability to sexually assault women because of his star power outraged many voters and led dozens of Republican politicians to renounce him. But some have pointed out that, paradoxically, the scandal was a kind of favor in that it helped reveal how entrenched sexism truly is in society, even when so many dismiss it as a minor problem or a thing of the past.

How does the reaction to Trump’s offensive comments reflect on progress for women?

The leaked 2005 tape, and the cascade of reactions to it, offer a genuinely mixed report card on how far women have come. On one hand, the speed and breadth of the backlash is itself a marker of progress. Thirty years ago, a powerful man boasting about grabbing women would have been laughed off as locker-room banter by nearly everyone in earshot — and would likely never have surfaced as a political problem at all. That dozens of Republican officeholders, including Paul Ryan and John McCain, felt compelled to publicly disavow their own nominee three weeks before an election tells us that the cultural cost of being seen as condoning sexual assault has risen sharply. So does the fact that women across the political spectrum responded with the #NotOkay hashtag, sharing first assaults in numbers that crashed feeds for days.

On the other hand, the very need for that outpouring is the more sobering revelation. The stories women told weren't historical artifacts; they were recent, ordinary, and overwhelmingly unreported. Trump's words struck a nerve precisely because so many women recognized the entitlement he described from their own lives — at work, on the subway, at fifteen. And the political reaction, for all its volume, has been strikingly conditional. Many Republicans who "unendorsed" Trump did so by invoking their daughters and wives, framing the offense as an affront to female relatives rather than to women as autonomous people. Others have already begun creeping back. Polls this week show Trump still commanding the support of a majority of white men and a substantial share of white women, suggesting that even bragging about assault is, for many voters, outweighed by partisanship or grievance.

So the episode reveals progress as asymmetric: norms have shifted faster than behavior, and rhetoric faster than power. Women now have language, platforms, and at least some institutional allies to name predation when they see it. What they still lack is the assurance that naming it will reliably cost the predator anything.

The release of the 2005 recording in which Donald Trump boasted about groping women without consent immediately ignited a firestorm, revealing a fault line in American attitudes toward gender and power. The reaction to his comments illuminates the uneven, often contradictory nature of progress for women. More troublingly, the scandal activated familiar defenses: the impulse to minimize harm by framing the words as mere talk, not actions, or to shift focus onto the women who might have been targeted. This reflects an entrenched cultural script that grants powerful men the benefit of the doubt while scrutinizing the credibility and motives of women.

The paradox of the moment is that it laid bare both heightened expectations and their fragility. That Trump’s candidacy survived such a revelation demonstrates that for a significant swath of society, sexism remains a normal, even acceptable, price of doing business. The fact that the tape was from 2005—an era hardly ancient—underscores how recently such predatory talk could be exchanged among elites with assumed impunity. The progress for women is real but precarious: we have arrived at a point where a candidate’s sexist remarks spark a nationwide crisis, yet we still live in a country where they do not automatically end a political career. The reaction, in sum, is a mirror reflecting a society that has learned to name misogyny but has not yet resolved to defeat it.

Let's be careful before we declare the Trump tape a teachable moment about American sexism. It may be exactly the opposite: a reminder of how narrow our definition of unacceptable behavior has become, and how quickly we retreat to symbolic outrage instead of structural reform.

It was not his comments about menstruation, or about breastfeeding, or his rating of women on a ten-point scale on national radio for years. It was not the lawsuits, the nondisclosure agreements, the reports of how Miss USA contestants were treated. It was a tape — vivid, vulgar, and crucially, audible. The reaction tells us less about a maturing public conscience than about the media's appetite for a clip that plays well on a loop.

After the election, win or lose, the temptation will be to say: we faced the worst, we condemned it, the system worked. That conclusion would be a disaster. The pay gap will not close because Billy Bush lost his job. Women in low-wage service jobs, who experience harassment at staggering rates and have no HR department to call, will not be helped by a news cycle. The women who clean Trump's hotels will go to work on Monday regardless.

The most revealing thing about the reaction to Trump's 2005 remarks is not the reaction itself, but its asymmetry across political lines — and what that asymmetry tells us about whether "progress for women" is a shared national project or a partisan one.

Look at the numbers. After the tape, Trump's support among Democratic women, who were never going to vote for him, dropped from near zero to near zero. Among independent women, it fell sharply. Among Republican women, it dipped but held, and a striking share — by some polls a majority — said the comments would not change their vote. Meanwhile, prominent conservative women like Kellyanne Conway defended him on Sunday shows, while Democratic men condemned him in terms indistinguishable from Democratic women.

What this suggests is uncomfortable for the standard narrative on both sides. The standard liberal narrative says the tape proved that sexism is "entrenched" — true, but it elides the fact that the entrenchment is unevenly distributed and increasingly correlated with party. The standard conservative narrative says liberals are weaponizing a private remark — but the remark describes a crime, and pretending otherwise requires a willful redefinition of words.

The honest reading is that we are watching the gender politics of the country sort itself, the way the racial politics of the country sorted in the 1960s. A generation ago, attitudes about women's roles cut across both parties; today they are becoming a defining partisan cleavage. The reaction to Trump is the clearest illustration yet. He has driven college-educated women — historically a swing group — toward the Democratic column in numbers that may prove durable. He has simultaneously consolidated support among voters, including some women, who experience modern gender norms as a kind of cultural imposition and view Trump's vulgarity as honesty about how men "really" are.

If this realignment holds, "progress for women" will increasingly mean different things to different Americans. To one side, it will mean reproductive autonomy, workplace protections, and a culture in which men like Trump are disqualified from public life. To the other, it will mean preserving traditional family structures and resisting what they see as a feminist overreach embodied in figures like Hillary Clinton. Both sides will claim to be advancing women's interests. Both will be telling the truth as they see it.

This is the deeper revelation of the Trump tape: not that sexism is entrenched — we knew that — but that the consensus required to dislodge it does not exist. The bipartisan revulsion of the first 48 hours was largely a media artifact; underneath, the country split along familiar lines and stayed there.

Progress on this issue will therefore not look like a national awakening. It will look like a slow, contested, election-by-election negotiation in which one coalition keeps pushing and the other keeps resisting. The tape did not change that dynamic. It clarified it.

It suggests that progress for women, as defined by the commentariat class, may not be progress as experienced by those outside that class. It also suggests that the political left's confidence in its own moral superiority on questions of gender may rest on foundations more fragile than assumed. When a significant portion of the electorate responds to charges of sexism by questioning the motives of the accusers, something besides ignorance or malice is at work.

The reaction was not, as some commentators suggested, simply a revival of "deplorability" in new form. It was a demonstration that the cultural revolution in attitudes toward women, led so confidently by metropolitan opinion, had not penetrated all of American life equally. Whether one welcomes or laments that fact is a separate question from whether it is real. The reaction to Trump's tape made it real.

The most striking feature of the reaction to Donald Trump's 2005 recording was not the condemnation but its scope—and, crucially, its limits. Mainstream Republicans, party officials, and conservative media figures rushed to express disgust. But the condemnation carried its own revelation: it was largely confined to words, to "locker room talk," to rhetoric rather than conduct. The question of what Trump's statements signified about his actual behavior remained, remarkably, unaddressed in most official responses.

This matters because progress for women depends not on what we say but on how we act when confronted with misconduct. The recording described specific actions—groping, kissing without consent, using celebrity status to gain physical access to women's bodies. The response treated these descriptions as mere hyperbole, jokes in poor taste, the verbal equivalent of harmless machismo. This calibration—outrage at words, indifference to actions—hardly represents a commitment to women's bodily autonomy.

If the reaction tells us anything about progress, it tells us that we have made more progress in condemning certain kinds of speech than in preventing certain kinds of behavior. The norms governing what men may say publicly have tightened considerably. The norms governing what men may do privately have proven far more resistant to change. A man who speaks as Trump spoke in 2005 is condemned; a man who acts on such impulses—if the allegations against Trump are accurate—may simply be another businessman, another Hollywood figure, another powerful man whose conduct remained concealed.

The women who took to social media in the days following the tape's release, sharing their own experiences of harassment and assault, were performing a different kind of work. They were drawing the connection between language and conduct that official condemnation elided. Their response suggested that real progress for women will require not merely changed speech codes but changed power relations—consequences for misconduct, accountability for those with the power to harm, and recognition that the gap between what powerful men say and what they do is itself the problem.
