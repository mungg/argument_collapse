Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Atheism, strictly speaking, is no more a religion than abstaining from chess is a hobby. It is a position on a single question — whether any gods exist — and that's all the word, taken at its etymological root, can honestly be made to carry. A person who answers "no" shares that answer with Stalinists, Ayn Randians, Theravada Buddhists (on some readings), Richard Dawkins, and a great many people who simply never gave the matter much thought. No creed, no liturgy, no community follows from the bare denial.

But the question gains traction because atheism in public life is rarely just the bare denial. The "new atheism" of Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris and Dennett comes with characteristic texts, in-group vocabulary, conferences, moral commitments (often a robust liberal humanism), and a recognizable enemies list. Alain de Botton's "Religion for Atheists" and now Sanderson Jones's Sunday Assembly in London push the analogy further: songs, sermons, fellowship, a weekly gathering to feel uplifted among like-minded strangers. If you grew up Anglican and walked into one of these meetings, the family resemblance would be hard to miss.

That resemblance, though, is to the social scaffolding of religion, not to religion itself. Durkheim taught us long ago that churches do many things besides making metaphysical claims — they bind communities, mark time, console the grieving, school the young. Susan Jacoby's point about tragedy is precisely that secular people need those functions too, and have been ill-served by a movement that often confined itself to debunking. Building atheist congregations is an admission that the New Atheist polemic, by itself, leaves people cold in a hospital waiting room.

So the honest answer is twofold. As a proposition, atheism is not a religion; calling it one is usually a rhetorical move to neutralize it ("you have faith too!") and it should be resisted. As a movement and a way of life, organized atheism is increasingly borrowing religion's forms, and there is nothing embarrassing about that — Gutting is right that unbelievers owe themselves reasons, and Jacoby is right that they owe themselves rituals. Whether the result deserves the name "church" matters less than whether it actually consoles, educates, and binds.

To ask whether atheism is a religion is to stumble over a definitional threshold that is more than semantic. Atheism, in its simplest and most widely accepted sense, is the absence of belief in a god or gods. Religion, by contrast, almost universally involves some form of belief in supernatural reality, a transcendent object of worship, and a framework of sacred narrative and ritual. By that standard, atheism fails the first and most essential test: it posits no deity, no divine order, no revealed truth. It is not a religion in any substantive sense. But the question persists because the visible culture of atheism in 2013 — from the rise of “atheist churches” in Britain to the arguments of public intellectuals like Susan Jacoby and Gary Gutting — increasingly borrows the outward forms and community functions of religious life, blurring the line for casual observers.

The Sunday Assembly, a London gathering launched this very month, illustrates the confusion. Its founders describe it as part atheist church, part secular celebration, with songs, inspirational talks, and a collective focus on living better. Here are people assembling for purpose and fellowship, rituals that mimic a worship service. Yet the content is entirely secular: no prayer, no supernatural claims, no sacred text. It is a social technology borrowed from religion, not a new faith. One might as well call a book club a religion because it meets regularly and discusses existential themes. The packaging does not determine the substance.

Jacoby, in her recent writings, argues that moments of tragedy remind us “of what atheism has to offer” — presumably, a stoic humanism that finds solace in shared fragility rather than divine plan. Gutting adds that atheists, like religious believers, ought to articulate reasons for their worldview, acknowledging that unbelief is a positive epistemological stance, not a mere void. Both are right to stress that atheism can be morally and intellectually robust. But neither claim transforms a philosophical position into a religion. A worldview that provides meaning and ethical guidance does not automatically become a religion; otherwise, Stoicism, Marxism, and even devoted fandom would qualify.

The deeper question — can atheism replace religion? — probes sociology, not theology. Religion historically supplies community, moral formation, rites of passage, and existential consolation. Atheism can replicate some of these by creating voluntary associations and humanist ceremonies, but it cannot replicate the numinous experience or the binding authority of a divine command. Whether it can satisfy the underlying human needs that religion addresses is an empirical question still unfolding. The rapid growth of nonbelief in Britain suggests that for many, the answer may be yes; for others, the hunger for transcendence endures. Atheism, then, is not a religion but a potential competitor in the marketplace of meaning — a distinction that keeps the debate both live and necessary.

To call atheism a religion is to commit a profound category error. At its core, atheism is simply the absence of belief in deities; it dictates no dogma, requires no rituals, ordains no clerics, and reveres no holy texts. As the old adage goes, if atheism is a religion, then not collecting stamps is a hobby. We are social creatures who crave meaning, ethical frameworks, and solidarity. For centuries, traditional faith has bundled these deeply human needs with supernatural claims. As the "nones"—those unaffiliated with organized religion—rapidly become one of the fastest-growing demographics in both the United States and Britain, they are proving that this bundle can be unspooled. People can gather, reflect, support one another, and mourn without appealing to divine intervention.

Atheism only tells you what a person does not believe; it leaves a blank slate. It is humanism, science, and secular philosophy that provide the positive values to live by. Atheism itself will never be a religion, but the vibrant, community-oriented secularism emerging today proves it is more than capable of replacing one.

Atheism is not a religion in the ordinary sense, but it can become religious in some of its social forms. The distinction matters. To say there is no God, or that one lacks belief in God, is not the same kind of thing as saying the Nicene Creed, keeping kosher, praying five times a day or venerating ancestors. Atheism has no necessary scripture, clergy, ritual calendar, doctrine of salvation or commandment to gather weekly. It is a negative position on one question: whether gods exist.

But that does not mean atheists have no worldview, no moral commitments and no need to explain themselves. Susan Jacoby is right that in moments of tragedy atheism may offer something: not providence, perhaps, but honesty about randomness, solidarity without metaphysical consolation, and a refusal to say that suffering is part of a divine plan. Gary Gutting is also right that atheists should articulate their reasons. If a believer may be asked why faith is rational, an atheist may be asked why disbelief is rational — or, more precisely, why the available arguments and evidence do not justify belief.

The “atheist church” phenomenon in Britain shows both the attraction and the limit of the comparison. People do not go to church only because they believe propositions about God. They go for music, moral exhortation, child-rearing, mutual aid, rites of passage, memory, belonging and a sense that life should be lifted above shopping and television. A secular gathering with songs, reflection and community service can provide some of that. In a country where Anglicanism often functions more as heritage than conviction, it is not surprising that people would want the architecture of church without the theology.

Still, atheism alone cannot replace religion, because religion has always been more than an answer to the God question. Atheism can clear ground; it cannot by itself build a full moral or communal life. Humanism, liberalism, socialism, scientific naturalism, existentialism or civic republicanism may furnish richer accounts of meaning and obligation. An atheist may draw on any of these, just as a believer may draw on traditions outside formal doctrine.

Nor should critics label atheism a religion merely to score a legal or rhetorical point. If every comprehensive conviction is a religion, then democracy, capitalism and environmentalism are religions too, and the word loses usefulness. Better to say this: atheism is not a religion, but atheists are human beings, and human beings reliably make communities, rituals and moral languages. The interesting question is not whether atheism secretly is church, but whether secular people can create institutions that satisfy human needs without pretending to possess sacred certainty.

By traditional definitions, religion typically encompasses belief in supernatural forces, organized rituals, and moral frameworks grounded in transcendent authority. Atheism, understood simply as disbelief in gods, meets none of these criteria. Whether that makes it a religion depends less on theism than on what secular communities choose to build.

Can atheism replace religion? That is the more interesting half of the prompt, and the answer is: only partially, and only for some people.

Religion does many things at once. It makes metaphysical claims about the universe. It provides ritual structure around birth, marriage, death. It offers community. It transmits moral teaching across generations. It consoles the suffering. It awes the worshipper. Atheism, strictly speaking, addresses only the first function — and only by denying it.

For the metaphysical job, atheism is sufficient: if you find the arguments for God's existence unpersuasive, you don't need a replacement framework any more than you need a replacement for astrology once you stop believing in horoscopes. But for the other functions — community, ritual, consolation, moral formation, transcendence — atheism by itself offers nothing. This is the gap that institutions like the new London atheist church are trying to fill.

Whether they can succeed is an open question. Religions evolved their rituals over millennia, and they draw their power partly from the conviction that they connect us to something real and eternal. A Sunday assembly that consciously mimics church liturgy while insisting it's all just metaphor risks feeling thin — a karaoke version of the real thing. On the other hand, secular Jews have long maintained robust traditions of community and ritual without strong supernatural belief, so it isn't impossible.

What seems most likely is that "replacement" will look less like a single atheist church and more like a portfolio. People will get community from neighborhoods, hobbies, and civic groups; consolation from therapy, friends, and philosophy; awe from nature, art, and science; moral formation from family and secular institutions. Religion bundled these functions; secular life will unbundle them.

That isn't quite the same as atheism replacing religion. It's modern life replacing religion, with atheism as one intellectual component among many. Whether that arrangement satisfies the human longings religion has historically served — or whether something will rush in to fill the void, perhaps in less benign forms — remains the central cultural question of our time.
