Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Hacker and Pierson have done valuable work cataloging the surprising scale of Biden-era Democratic ambition, and they are right to push back against the lazy consensus that Democrats abandoned economics for "wokeness." The empirical record of 2021—the size of the proposals, the breadth of their distributive aims, the willingness to embrace industrial policy and active antitrust—genuinely does cut against Piketty's Brahmin Left thesis as a description of the U.S. case. But several features of their account understate the constraints the new Democratic coalition imposes, and the chasm between what was proposed and what was achieved deserves more interpretive weight than they give it.

Start with the substance of what actually passed. The Inflation Reduction Act, CHIPS, and the bipartisan infrastructure law are genuine accomplishments, but they are not, in the main, redistributive programs. They are productivist, supply-side investments aimed at firms and regions—tax credits for manufacturers, grants for semiconductor fabs, money for ports and bridges. Their distributional effects flow indirectly, through job creation and wage premiums tied to local content and labor standards. By contrast, the parts of Build Back Better that would have directly transferred income downward—the expanded Child Tax Credit, paid leave, child care subsidies, the care economy build-out—are precisely the parts that died. This is not a quibble. The pattern of what survived and what didn't tells us something important: the U-shaped coalition can underwrite massive public investment in production, but it could not underwrite an entitlement-style transformation of family policy, even with COVID as cover. Manchin and Sinema are convenient scapegoats, but their willingness to greenlight industrial subsidies while killing the CTC extension reveals the actual operative cleavage.

This matters because Hacker and Pierson's bet—that Democrats can "build power through policy"—rests on visible material improvements changing voters' minds. Their own evidence already complicates the story: the CTC expansion, which cut child poverty by 40 percent, generated no detectable political payoff before it lapsed. As we sit here in early 2024, unemployment is below 4 percent, real wages have recovered, the IRA is steering hundreds of billions into red-state factory construction, and Biden's approval on the economy is mired in the high 30s. If pocketbook politics worked the way Hacker and Pierson suggest it should, this would be the moment we'd see it. Instead, voters tell pollsters the economy is terrible, and Trump leads in the swing states the IRA was explicitly designed to court. Either the policy-to-votes mechanism is much weaker than the authors hope, or—more likely—the media and identity dynamics they gesture toward in their final pages are not a residual problem but a dominant force in how voters perceive the party.

The authors' treatment of housing and zoning is the most telling silence in the essay. They acknowledge that opportunity hoarding is "the greatest threat to the continued success of high-growth urban hubs" and that affluent metro Democrats fiercely protect exclusionary zoning even while professing progressive values. They then explain how the national party "organizes this out" of politics by treating it as a local matter. Fair enough as description—but this is the load-bearing wall of the entire coalition. The housing affordability crisis is the single most acute material grievance facing the Democrats' downscale metro voters, and it is caused, in significant part, by the political behavior of their upscale metro voters. Any honest accounting of the U-shaped coalition's limits has to reckon with the fact that the coalition holds together precisely because it does not attempt to redistribute the asset that matters most to its affluent wing. Call this what you like, but it is not the overcoming of Piketty's diagnosis. It is a variation on it.

A related complication is that the U-shape itself may be less stable than the essay assumes. Polling through 2022 and 2023 has shown meaningful erosion of Democratic support among Hispanic and, to a lesser degree, Black working-class voters—precisely the lower arm of the U. If this trend continues into November, the Democrats will look less like a cross-class metro party and more like a college-educated metro party with declining minority working-class adhesion. That is a much closer fit to the Brahmin Left model than Hacker and Pierson allow. And it suggests that the cultural and identity dimensions of party image, which they tend to treat as misperceptions ginned up by hostile media, may be doing real work in how working-class voters of all races sort themselves.

None of this means the 2021 agenda was unimportant or that we should return to declension narratives about Democratic capitulation. Hacker and Pierson are right that something genuine shifted, and right that progressive organization, COVID, and the Sanders-Biden unity process made it possible. But the lesson of 2021–22 may be less encouraging than they suggest: the party can pass ambitious legislation, but it cannot yet convert legislation into either durable transfers or durable political loyalty. Until Democrats are willing to take on the local arrangements that produce inequality within their own coalition—zoning chief among them—the Blue Divide will keep narrowing what counts as politically possible, even when the party is bolder than its critics suppose. The "near miss" of Build Back Better may, on closer inspection, have been a near miss of a particular kind: bold on production, timid on the asset and family structures that actually shape life chances in metro America.

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson offer a compelling account of the Democratic Party’s surprising leftward lurch on economic policy, insisting that a carefully curated pocketbook agenda has allowed the party to manage its increasingly bifurcated coalition. As they note, the 2021–22 window came remarkably close to producing the most redistributive legislation since the Great Society. Yet their analysis, while rich, tends to understate the enduring fragility of this arrangement and the structural forces that may yet tear it apart. As the 2024 election season heats up, the tensions they catalogue look less like manageable trade-offs than active fault lines.

The first difficulty is that the “near miss” narrative itself papers over just how thoroughly the density tax and institutional veto points constrained the final outcome. Yes, congressional Democrats overwhelmingly backed Build Back Better, but the pivotal votes of Joe Manchin and Kyrsten Sinema were not idiosyncratic accidents; they reflected durable electoral incentives in states that the party must repeatedly win to hold power. Even if Democrats managed to expand their Senate majority in 2024, the chamber’s extreme malapportionment guarantees that other senators from deep-red states will wield outsized influence. The $400,000 tax pledge, which Hacker and Pierson describe as a shrewd way to shield all but the top 2 percent from higher levies, further limits the fiscal tools available for universal social insurance. It is hard to see how the party can permanently fund large-scale family and care policies without eventually asking more from upper-middle-class professionals—precisely the suburban voters who are most sensitive to tax hikes. For now, the gap is bridged by rhetoric and deficit spending, but that strategy cannot hold indefinitely, particularly as interest costs on the national debt mount.

The essay’s treatment of cultural and racial divisions also deserves more skepticism. Hacker and Pierson argue that Democratic elites have successfully organized divisive social issues “out” of national politics, foregrounding economic proposals that carry implicit racial justice benefits. But the 2024 campaign is already demonstrating how difficult it is to keep cultural conflict at bay. The southern border has become a political emergency for blue-city mayors, and Republican attacks linking immigration to crime and overwhelmed social services are finding new purchase even among some working-class voters of color. Abortion, which the authors mention only briefly in connection with the Dobbs decision, has erupted as a central mobilizing force, yet it also reveals the cultural schisms within the metropolitan coalition: highly educated secular liberals are driving the party’s maximalist rhetoric, while many moderate and religious voters—including Latino Catholics and Black Protestants—hold more ambivalent views. The balance-of-power suburbs that Democrats have won are filled not only with graduates of elite universities but also with parents who may recoil at school culture wars or progressive criminal justice reforms. If Donald Trump and his allies succeed in making the 2024 election a referendum on “wokeness” rather than on kitchen-table economics, the Blue Divide could quickly become a chasm.

Housing and the opportunity hoarding that the essay acknowledges but then largely sets aside represent perhaps the deepest unresolved threat. Hacker and Pierson note that restrictive zoning and local control are “widely perceived to be local responsibilities,” enabling national Democrats to avoid direct confrontation. But that perception is fraying as housing costs become a crisis for renters and young families across the metropolitan core. The party’s younger, less affluent voters—especially in cities—are increasingly furious at the exclusionary policies of affluent suburbs, many of which are represented by Democrats. Climate policies that aim to densify neighborhoods or expand transit will inevitably run into local resistance. If the national party continues to tiptoe around these issues, it risks demobilizing the very base it needs to turn out in urban centers while offering suburbanites little reason to accept even modest incursions on their property values. The Biden administration’s tentative steps toward incentivizing zoning reform have been too small to test the coalition’s resilience; a serious push would likely provoke the backlash that Hacker and Pierson portray as avoidable.

Perhaps the most sobering element of their argument is the concession that, so far, there is little evidence that ambitious economic policies have built durable political power. The expanded child tax credit expired without a significant electoral reward, and even a strong labor market and infrastructure spending have not lifted President Biden’s approval ratings out of dangerously low territory. Hacker and Pierson hint that polarization and the right-wing media ecosystem may simply be too powerful for policy success to cut through. If that is true, the entire strategic logic—that a redistributive economic agenda can hold together the Democratic coalition while clawing back some white working-class support—rests on a shaky empirical foundation. In the absence of clear political returns, the party’s moderate wing, already nervous about spending and crime, may reassert itself after 2024, especially if Republicans seize on debt and inflation.

The New Blue Divide is real, and Hacker and Pierson are right to push back against the lazy caricature that Democrats have abandoned economics for boutique cultural causes. But the preconditions that allowed the party to advance such a bold agenda in 2021—a once-in-a-century pandemic, a brief respite from deficit fearmongering, and the unified horror at January 6—are already fading. What remains is a coalition held together more by opposition to an authoritarian GOP than by a shared positive vision, and a set of institutional obstacles that will never drift away on their own. The project of building a durable cross-class, multiracial majority will require confronting, not just managing, the Blue Divide’s deepest cleavages. The 2024 election may not test this fully, but the years after almost certainly will.

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson offer a compelling corrective to the conventional wisdom that the Democratic Party has abandoned economic redistribution in favor of cultural elitism. As they rightly point out, the Biden administration's legislative ambitions—from the American Rescue Plan to the infrastructure and climate bills—represent a staggering investment in industrial policy and the working class. Yet, while Hacker and Pierson elegantly map the "U-shaped" coalition of affluent suburbanites and working-class urbanites, they are far too optimistic about the durability of the compromises required to hold it together. The structural contradictions of this new blue divide cannot simply be engineered away by clever legislative packaging.

The fundamental weakness in Hacker and Pierson’s analysis is their treatment of macroeconomic constraints. They suggest that the Democratic Party can sustain its expansive, multi-trillion-dollar social agenda without alienating affluent suburbanites by simply passing the bill to corporations and the top two percent of earners. But the post-pandemic economic reality has brutally exposed the limits of this "tax only the rich" framework. Pumping vast sums of money into the economy without broad-based taxation to absorb excess demand is a recipe for inflation. The authors briefly note Joe Manchin’s inflation concerns as the obstacle to the broader Build Back Better agenda, but they treat this as a mere political hurdle rather than a fundamental economic constraint of their coalition. Affluent suburbanites may be socially liberal, but their tolerance for progressive economics evaporates when it threatens their purchasing power or retirement portfolios. By refusing to ask the upper-middle class to contribute proportionally through broad-based taxes—a hallmark of the European social democracies the American left frequently idealizes—the Democratic Party has placed an artificial and highly inflationary ceiling on its redistributive capacity.

Furthermore, Hacker and Pierson offer an overly generous reading of the Democrats’ approach to suburban "opportunity hoarding." They acknowledge that affluent blue-leaning suburbanites use restrictive zoning to hoard resources and inflate housing costs, which directly harms the working-class voters at the other end of the U-shaped coalition. However, they dismiss this as a "localized" issue that national Democrats can conveniently sidestep to maintain coalition harmony. This is a profound miscalculation of what drives working-class economic anxiety. Housing costs are not a peripheral, local concern; they are the single largest line item in the working-class budget and the primary driver of geographic inequality. A national economic agenda that aggressively subsidizes green energy and domestic manufacturing, while implicitly protecting the exclusionary zoning practices of its wealthiest voters, is fundamentally incoherent. National Democrats cannot claim to be building a working-class economy while turning a blind eye to the very mechanisms that price the working class out of high-opportunity metropolitan areas.

Finally, Hacker and Pierson place too much faith in the political science maxim that party elites can simply organize cultural issues "out" of politics by keeping the focus on pocketbook economics. This assumes a vacuum that the opposition is more than happy to fill. The rightward drift of non-college-educated voters—including a slow but highly alarming erosion of support among Hispanic and Black working-class men over the last several election cycles—is not happening because these voters oppose the child tax credit or industrial policy. It is happening because cultural and social polarization is real, and economic delivery is not a panacea for profound cultural alienation. The Republican Party has proven highly adept at organizing those cultural issues back "in," forcing Democrats onto defensive terrain that their economic messaging frequently struggles to overcome.

Hacker and Pierson are entirely correct that the Biden-era Democratic Party has successfully navigated a perilous institutional obstacle course to deliver a surprisingly robust economic agenda. But the "New Blue Divide" remains a highly unstable fault line. A political coalition that refuses to tax its upper-middle-class base, protects their exclusionary property wealth, and hopes economic stimulus will paper over cultural defection is not a sustainable model for long-term democratic resilience. Eventually, to genuinely serve its working-class base, the Democratic Party will have to ask its affluent suburbanites to make actual sacrifices.

Hacker and Pierson are right to insist on a fact too often obscured by both conservative caricature and left melancholy: the Democratic Party of 2021 was not the Democratic Party of 1993. Whatever one thinks of Joe Biden’s political instincts, the agenda his administration and congressional allies put forward marked a real break with the era of deficit fetishism, trade fatalism, welfare retrenchment, and deference to concentrated corporate power. The expanded child tax credit, care economy investments, green industrial policy, antitrust revival, pro-union appointments, and willingness to use public procurement and subsidies to shape markets were not rhetorical flourishes. They were evidence that a large part of the party had learned something from the failures of the Clinton and Obama periods.

But the story is less reassuring than the lead essay suggests. The “new blue divide” has not been overcome; it has been managed, deferred, and in some cases disguised. Democrats have found a form of economic activism that can coexist with affluent metropolitan liberalism, but that coexistence depends on avoiding the hardest questions: who pays, who builds, who gets access to high-opportunity places, and whose consumption patterns must change. The result is a party that has become more ambitious about public spending and industrial policy, but remains uncertain about whether it can confront the forms of private power most embedded in its own coalition.

The easiest part of the new Democratic economics is taxing people richer than most Democratic voters. Biden’s $400,000 pledge was politically shrewd, and Hacker and Pierson are right that American inequality makes it possible to raise serious money from corporations and the very wealthy. But this also reveals the limits of the coalition. The United States cannot build a durable social-democratic state solely on the promise that only someone else will pay for it. Universal social insurance systems in other rich democracies are not financed only by billionaires. They rest on broad tax bases, visible benefits, and a political bargain in which middle- and upper-middle-class citizens contribute because they also receive. Democrats have embraced the benefits side of that bargain but remain afraid of the contribution side.

That fear is understandable. Many of the voters who have moved toward Democrats in the Trump era are socially liberal, college-educated, and alarmed by authoritarianism, but they are not necessarily egalitarian in their day-to-day economic interests. They may support climate investment, abortion rights, gun regulation, and higher taxes on billionaires. They may even support unions in the abstract. Yet they also benefit from high home values, selective schools, exclusionary zoning, professional licensing, and metropolitan labor markets stratified by education. A politics that challenges those advantages will encounter resistance not from caricatured plutocrats alone, but from the upper-middle-class backbone of blue America.

Housing is the clearest case. Hacker and Pierson rightly identify “opportunity hoarding” as a dangerous unresolved conflict, but it deserves even more centrality. If Democrats cannot make expensive metropolitan regions build abundant housing, many of their other commitments will be undermined. Wage gains will be eaten by rents. Climate goals will be harder to reach as people are pushed into longer commutes. Racial and class segregation will persist behind progressive yard signs. Young families will leave high-productivity regions or delay having children. The party can pass subsidies for child care, transit, and clean energy, but if blue jurisdictions cannot permit apartments near jobs and trains, the governing model will look hypocritical and ineffective.

This is why the distinction between national and local politics cannot remain a convenient escape hatch. It is true that zoning is formally local. But national parties shape priorities, rhetoric, incentives, and intergovernmental bargaining. Democrats have become increasingly comfortable criticizing red-state attacks on democracy and civil rights; they should become equally comfortable criticizing blue-state scarcity politics. Governors and state legislatures in places such as California, Massachusetts, New York, Washington, and Colorado matter at least as much to the future of egalitarian metropolitan politics as senators from West Virginia or Arizona. A party of metro America has to govern metro America better.

The lead essay also underplays the geopolitical and macroeconomic forces behind Biden’s turn. The new industrial policy was not only the product of progressive pressure or lessons from the Great Recession. It also reflected a bipartisan reassessment of China, supply chains, and national resilience. COVID-19 exposed the fragility of global production networks; Russia’s invasion of Ukraine reinforced the strategic importance of energy and manufacturing capacity; China’s rise made laissez-faire assumptions about trade and technology look naïve across much of Washington. The CHIPS and Science Act, battery and semiconductor investments, domestic-content rules, and “foreign entity of concern” restrictions cannot be understood merely as redistribution. They are part of a national-development strategy shaped by security competition.

That matters politically because it helps explain why some forms of Democratic economic activism have advanced while others have failed. Industrial subsidies can be sold as jobs, climate policy, and national security at once. They create concentrated beneficiaries: firms, unions, regions, and local officials. Care work, paid leave, and the child tax credit are in some respects more directly redistributive and more transformative of family life, but they lack the same coalition of powerful producers. They also challenge gendered assumptions about private responsibility more directly. It is not an accident that the climate-and-manufacturing parts of Bidenism survived in the Inflation Reduction Act while much of the care agenda did not.

The inflation shock further complicates the “near miss” interpretation. Manchin used inflation as a rationale for shrinking Build Back Better, and one can argue he exaggerated the connection. Still, the politics of 2021–22 cannot be separated from rising prices. Voters experienced the economy less through legislative ambition than through groceries, gas, rent, and interest rates. A party that wants to restore faith in activist government must show not only that it can spend, but that it can deliver capacity, abundance, and stability. Permitting reform, grid expansion, administrative competence, and anti-bottleneck governance are not technocratic side issues. They are central to whether the new economic agenda can survive contact with public impatience.

The most promising element of Biden-era Democratic economics is its move from compensation to production: not just helping people after markets fail, but shaping markets so they generate better jobs, cleaner energy, and broader opportunity. Yet production politics has a discipline that Democrats have not fully accepted. It requires building things even when incumbents object; choosing among competing environmental, labor, speed, and cost goals; and admitting that process-heavy liberalism can empower veto points as surely as the Senate does. If every transmission line, apartment building, factory, rail project, and clean-energy installation must run a gauntlet of litigation and local obstruction, industrial policy will disappoint.

So the lead essay is right against the cynics: the Democratic Party has changed, and the change is consequential. But the next test is not whether Democrats can write a more ambitious platform. They have already done that. The test is whether they can turn a coalition united by anti-Trump alarm and broad support for taxing the very rich into a governing majority willing to confront scarcity, local exclusion, administrative sclerosis, and the privileges of its own winners.

A durable pro-democracy coalition cannot be built on negative polarization alone. Nor can it be built only on programs that almost pass. It will require visible improvements in ordinary life: cheaper housing, reliable child care, faster clean-energy deployment, better wages for non-college workers, and public institutions that work without heroic navigation. The new Democratic economics has opened that possibility. Whether the party can make good on it depends on moving from carefully curated redistribution to a more confrontational politics of democratic abundance.

The lead essay by Hacker and Pierson is a compelling account of the Democratic Party's recent economic pivot, and it offers a sharp rebuttal to the view that Democrats have become captive to affluent suburbanites indifferent to redistribution. Their evidence that Biden's 2021 agenda was the most ambitious since the 1960s is hard to dispute, and their analysis of the "density divide" illuminates how geography has reshaped American party politics. Yet the essay's optimism about the durability of this shift and the cohesion of the U-shaped coalition requires scrutiny. Beneath the surface of bold policy proposals lies a more sobering story of institutional constraint, strategic miscalculation, and a coalition held together as much by opposition to Republicans as by shared commitment to economic justice.

The authors frame the failure of Build Back Better as a "near miss," suggesting that with slightly better luck or a stronger hand in Congress, the agenda might have succeeded. This framing is understandable but misleading. The Biden administration's experience was not an anomaly but the latest in a long series of Democratic efforts to enact bold economic policy—efforts that have consistently been frustrated by the same structural barriers. The filibuster, Senate malapportionment, gerrymandering, and the Electoral College are not temporary obstacles; they are features of an effectively anti-majoritarian Constitution that systematically advantages geographically dispersed Republicans. The "near miss" narrative implies that these constraints are surmountable with the right political winds, but the evidence from the past half-century suggests otherwise. Unless Democrats can consistently win 60 Senate seats or eliminate the filibuster (neither of which is likely in the near term), ambitious economic agendas will continue to face veto points that are nearly impossible to clear. The essay acknowledges these constraints but does not adequately grapple with their permanence.

The essay also understates the fragility of the U-shaped coalition it describes. The authors argue that shared geography and common interests in the economic health of metro areas bind affluent suburbanites and struggling urban voters together. This may be true in the abstract, but the actual policies required to address economic inequality often expose real tensions. Expanding the child tax credit, investing in affordable housing, or raising taxes on corporations and the wealthy all have distributional consequences that could activate affluent voters' concerns about their own pocketbooks. The essay notes that Biden's $400,000 tax pledge has allowed Democrats to avoid these tensions for now, but this is a band-aid rather than a cure. Eventually, the revenue demands of an expansive welfare state will require contributions from higher-income households, and the coalition's resilience under that pressure remains untested. The failure of the expanded child tax credit to boost Democratic popularity—despite its dramatic reduction of child poverty—suggests that economic policy alone does not cement partisan attachments in an era of negative polarization. Voters may support generous programs in the abstract but fail to reward the party that delivers them if the broader narrative is dominated by cultural grievance.

The authors' claim that Democrats have successfully avoided racialized messaging by embedding social justice into economic proposals is strategically astute but politically limited. While it is true that the 2021 agenda included provisions designed to benefit communities of color disproportionately, and while the party has largely avoided direct confrontation on issues like immigration or criminal justice, the Republican messaging apparatus has been highly effective at framing Democratic economics as culturally threatening. The persistent "woke" label, amplified by right-wing and mainstream media alike, has colored public perception of Democratic policy even when the policy itself is explicitly economic. The essay notes this risk but does not adequately account for how difficult it is to control narrative in a fragmented media environment. The party's economic messaging competes not only with Republican attacks but with the endless churn of social media and cable news, which reward cultural conflict over programmatic debate. In this environment, the party's careful avoidance of racialized rhetoric may matter less than the essays' optimism suggests.

Finally, the essay's celebration of the party's shift risks obscuring a deeper pattern: the Democratic Party has oscillated between progressive and centrist phases throughout its history. The New Deal, the Great Society, the Clinton triangulation, and now Biden's agenda represent not a permanent realignment but a series of responses to electoral and ideological pressures. The progressive wing has gained influence, but it remains checkable by institutional barriers and the party's own institutional centrists. Without a sustained investment in the organizational infrastructure of progressive politics—stronger unions, more robust civic institutions, electoral reform—the "transformation" described by Hacker and Pierson may prove as fragile as previous leftward turns. The essay's own evidence points in this direction: the party has not reversed the decline of organized labor, has not substantially improved its standing among white working-class voters, and has not built a lasting political coalition based on economic self-interest rather than anti-Republican sentiment.

Hacker and Pierson are right that the 2021-22 Democratic agenda represented a genuine departure from the Brahmin left narrative, and they are right to insist that the story of the Democratic Party cannot be reduced to cultural liberalism. But their optimism about the durability of this shift and the cohesion of the new coalition understates the formidable obstacles—constitutional, institutional, and political—that stand in the way of translating bold proposals into lasting policy. The Bait and the Switch Were Always Built In

Hacker and Pierson tell a story of near-triumph: a bold, redistributive agenda that almost made it through, foiled at the last minute by Joe Manchin and the density tax. Their account is meticulous and, in many respects, correct. But the framing — "remarkable just how close the agenda came to succeeding" — gets the politics backward. Build Back Better did not narrowly miss. It was structurally engineered to miss, and the structural engineers were Democrats themselves.

Consider the basic architecture of the 2021 effort. The largest, most ambitious legislation Democrats have proposed in fifty years was constructed entirely under the constraint of budget reconciliation, with no serious attempt to abolish or even meaningfully reform the filibuster. The Child Tax Credit expansion was made temporary at the outset, not because that was politically necessary but because permanence would have required compromises Democratic leaders would not make. Universal pre-K, the care economy investments, paid leave — every one of these "transformative" programs was sunset early to fit a ten-year budget window. The PRO Act, the only piece of legislation that would have shifted the underlying balance of class power, was never seriously fought for. Medicare-for-All was treated, even rhetorically, as outside the realm of polite conversation.

When Hacker and Pierson write that "the overwhelming majority of the Democratic caucus" supported a bold agenda, they conflate roll-call votes with political will. A caucus that knows a bill will not survive Manchin can vote for anything. The question is what leadership fights for when the stakes are real. And the record there is clear: Chuck Schumer did not threaten Manchin; the White House negotiated against itself for months; the progressive caucus was talked down from withholding infrastructure votes that were its only leverage. This was not a tragedy of institutions. It was a series of choices by people who preferred a half-loaf within the existing donor and consultant ecosystem to a confrontation that might have produced a full loaf or a fight worth losing.

Hacker and Pierson acknowledge the awkward fact that "few if any of [the Democrats'] initiatives seem to have built power through policy." They puzzle over this, then offer some hopeful conjectures: maybe time will help, maybe organizations will get stronger, maybe the media environment makes it impossible. What they will not say is the obvious thing. You cannot build durable working-class power through means-tested, time-limited transfers administered through the tax code, designed in the first instance to be invisible to recipients and unobjectionable to suburban professionals. The CTC was a beautiful policy and an inert political instrument. People received deposits in their bank accounts and did not know whom to thank. When Manchin killed the extension, there was no mobilization, because no mobilization had ever been built. That isn't a tragic accident; it's the consequence of designing the program for the comfort of the Democrats' upscale wing rather than the political activation of its downscale one.

This is what Heather Gautney was pointing at with the phrase "bait and switch," and Hacker and Pierson's response is essentially to insist that the bait was sincerely offered. Perhaps it was. But sincerity of intention is beside the point when the political form of an agenda systematically undermines its political durability. Universal programs build constituencies. Means-tested ones build resentment, paperwork, and quiet attrition. Programs run through state bureaucracies and visible institutions — Social Security, Medicare — anchor partisan loyalty across generations. Programs delivered through Treasury deposits or insurance subsidies vanish into the background hum of household finance.

There is a deeper problem with the Hacker-Pierson account that becomes visible once you stop accepting their framing of the U-shaped coalition as a fact of nature. The "Brahmin left" they want to distinguish from Piketty's version is still, in important respects, Piketty's left. The wealthiest counties in America did not vote for Biden because they had been won over to redistribution. They voted for Biden because Trump made the Republican brand intolerable for educated professionals while leaving the underlying material interests of those professionals — low capital gains taxes, asset-friendly monetary policy, residential zoning, selective school admissions, the carried interest loophole — substantially untouched. None of these were on the BBB table. The agenda's actual revenue side — partial repeal of the 2017 tax cuts, modest corporate minimum taxes — left the structural mechanisms of upward redistribution intact. This is not an accident. It is what the U-shaped coalition will tolerate, and the authors are right about that. They are simply wrong to call the result "bold."

What would a genuinely bold agenda look like? It would attack opportunity hoarding directly: federal preemption of exclusionary zoning, conditioned on housing and transportation dollars; an end to the mortgage interest deduction beyond modest caps; full taxation of capital gains at death; a wealth tax that the Brahmin left would actually feel. It would rebuild labor power through card check and sectoral bargaining, not through subsidies that nudge unionization at the margins. It would attack the price of housing, education, and health care — the three goods whose inflation makes professional-class anxiety politically explosive — rather than subsidizing demand within unreformed markets. Hacker and Pierson concede most of this in passing, especially on zoning, and then explain why national Democrats cannot do any of it. They are describing the limits of the coalition while telling us how transformative the coalition has become.

There is something almost touching about the authors' insistence that the story should not be lost in the noise. They want us to recognize the genuine break with Clintonism, the seriousness of industrial policy, the realness of Lina Khan. I grant all of this. The party is to the left of where it was in 2010, and that matters. But "to the left of Larry Summers in 2009" is a low bar, and the test of a political movement is not how it compares to its own worst recent self but whether it can produce durable change in the distribution of power. By that test, the Biden era has so far been a near miss on the policy side and a clear miss on the political-economic side. The donor class has not been displaced. Labor density continues to fall. Housing has become more, not less, unaffordable. The professional-managerial class remains the party's center of gravity, and its preferences continue to shape what is thinkable.

The honest version of the Hacker-Pierson story would be this: Democrats have rediscovered Keynesianism and industrial policy under the pressure of a pandemic, a wave of progressive activism, and competition with China. They have done so in a way carefully calibrated not to threaten the underlying class structure of their own coalition. That is genuinely better than Rubinomics. It is not a transformation, and treating it as one risks letting the party's leadership coast on the gap between its rhetoric and the lives of the people it claims to serve. The next opportunity, when it comes, should be greeted with more pressure and less gratitude.

The Missing Country in the Story Is China

Hacker and Pierson have produced a careful and largely persuasive account of the Democratic Party's recent leftward turn on economics, anchored almost entirely in domestic coalitional dynamics: the rise of the progressive wing, the influence of think tanks like the Roosevelt Institute, the activism that followed George Floyd, the shock of COVID. These are real factors. But there is a startling omission at the heart of their analysis, one that becomes more conspicuous the longer one reads. The word "China" does not appear. The word "Russia" does not appear. The phrase "supply chain" does not appear. The international context within which the Biden administration's industrial policy was actually conceived and sold is simply absent.

This matters because the most distinctive features of Bidenomics — the CHIPS Act, the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic-content requirements, the export controls on advanced semiconductors, the willingness to subsidize manufacturing on a scale not seen in generations — were not primarily products of the Sanders-Warren wing of the party. They were products of a bipartisan elite consensus that the United States had become dangerously dependent on Chinese manufacturing and that great-power competition required reshoring strategic industries. Jake Sullivan's "foreign policy for the middle class" was the conceptual hinge: a recognition that the geopolitical and the distributional could be welded together, with industrial subsidies serving both ends.

Read the actual political speeches surrounding the passage of CHIPS and the IRA. Read the floor statements of the Republicans who voted for CHIPS. The framing is overwhelmingly about Beijing, not about inequality. Senators who would not vote a dime for child care found tens of billions for semiconductor fabs because Taiwan looks fragile and TSMC sits at the choke point of the global economy. This is industrial policy, yes, and it has redistributive consequences, yes. But its political logic does not run through the U-shaped coalition Hacker and Pierson describe. It runs through Lockheed Martin, Intel, Micron, and the Pentagon.

Once you see this, several puzzles in the Hacker-Pierson account begin to resolve. Why did Democrats manage to pass the most ambitious industrial legislation in decades while failing to pass paid family leave and the Child Tax Credit extension? Not because Manchin was capricious. Because industrial policy aligned with a bipartisan national-security consensus and a powerful set of corporate interests, while social policy did not. Why did the party's affluent suburban wing tolerate massive subsidies to manufacturing? Because those subsidies enrich firms in which the professional-managerial class holds stock and provide a respectable patriotic frame for spending that would otherwise look like industrial favoritism. Why did the bipartisan elements of the Biden agenda survive while the partisan ones did not? Because the bipartisan elements were the ones with a China rationale.

This is not a small point. It changes the political economy of what we are seeing. Hacker and Pierson present a story in which a left-leaning party, pressured from below by progressives and activists, embraced a redistributive agenda and ran into the wall of the Senate. The actual story is more layered: a national-security-driven shift in elite consensus opened space for a partial redistributive agenda that progressives then tried to ride. When the geopolitical justification ran out — when there was no Beijing-flavored reason to make the CTC permanent — the agenda collapsed. The progressive wing did not have the autonomous power to carry the rest. It had power only insofar as its preferences overlapped with the China hawks' priorities.

There is a parallel here to a much older story. The American welfare state was largely built under the impetus of two world wars and a cold war. The GI Bill, the interstate highway system, the National Defense Education Act, even substantial parts of the postwar housing finance system, were sold as national-security imperatives. Sweden and Britain did not need this framing; the United States, with its more individualist political culture and its powerful business class, did. The Biden agenda continues this pattern. Where it could be framed as competition with China — chips, batteries, electric vehicles, critical minerals, rare earths — it advanced. Where it could not — care work, child poverty, paid leave — it stalled.

This frame also illuminates the international dimension of the leftward turn that Hacker and Pierson treat as exceptional. They note that other center-left parties have lost working-class voters and gained the highly educated. They might also have noted that those same parties — the German SPD, the French Socialists insofar as they still exist, even British Labour under Starmer — are also moving toward industrial policy, energy security, and reshoring. The synchronization is not coincidence. It is the response of advanced-economy political systems to the breakdown of the post-Cold War globalization consensus and the recognition that strategic dependence on geopolitical rivals is a vulnerability. Bidenomics is the American instantiation of a wider pattern.

The omission of China also leads Hacker and Pierson to misjudge the durability of the shift. They suggest, hopefully, that with a slightly stronger congressional hand, the 2021 agenda could be revived. But the durable parts of that agenda were durable because they had a national-security backstop. The vulnerable parts were vulnerable because they did not. Unless the Democratic coalition can find a way to give care policy, child poverty, and housing the same geopolitical valence as semiconductors — which is genuinely difficult, though demographic decline and labor force participation arguments offer some purchase — the next window will see the same pattern: industrial spending advances, social spending stalls.

There is a critical political lesson here for the left. If you want to expand the welfare state in the United States, you almost certainly have to find a way to frame your preferred policies in terms that the national-security state can endorse. This is uncomfortable. It involves alliances with people whose other commitments — to defense spending, to confrontation with China, to military deployments — many progressives find odious. But the historical record is clear: in the American context, the welfare state grows when it can plausibly claim to be making the country stronger against rivals, and stalls when it cannot. The Hacker-Pierson account treats the international as a kind of stage backdrop. It is in fact the engine of much of what they describe.

I do not mean to dismiss the domestic story they tell. The Sanders campaigns mattered. The Roosevelt Institute mattered. Sabeel Rahman and Lina Khan mattered. The energy of post-Floyd organizing mattered. These were necessary conditions. But they were not sufficient, and treating them as the whole story produces an account that cannot explain why some parts of the 2021 agenda survived and others did not, why the synchronization with other rich democracies is so striking, or why the next administration — Democratic or Republican — will continue to do industrial policy regardless of which coalition wins. The new blue divide cannot be understood without the new Cold War.

The Cultural Insularity Problem Does Not Go Away by Being Ignored

Hacker and Pierson's argument has a clever architecture. The standard critique — that Democrats have become a party of college-educated cultural progressives detached from the economic concerns of working-class voters — is dismissed as a mischaracterization. The party's real agenda, they insist, has been overwhelmingly economic. Look at the legislation. Look at the tweets. Look at Build Back Better. The cultural insularity story is a creation of right-wing media noise and confused journalism, not of the party itself.

I find this unconvincing for several reasons, and I want to take it seriously because Hacker and Pierson are serious analysts and the question is genuinely important. Where Have All the Democrats Gone?, the Judis and Teixeira book they criticize in passing, is not a reactionary lament. It is an account of how a party can lose voters by being seen to hold them in contempt, and how the things a party fails to say can matter as much as the things it says.

Begin with the methodology. Hacker and Pierson and their coauthors looked at the Twitter feeds of congressional Democrats between 2015 and 2023 and found that economic content dominated. Fine. But to whom does congressional Twitter speak? Mostly to journalists, activists, and other politically engaged elites. It is not a useful proxy for the messages that reach the median white voter without a college degree in Erie County or Maricopa County or Macomb County. Those messages come through cable news, local broadcast, Facebook clips, TikTok algorithms, and the slow drip of viral moments. In those channels, the dominant images of the Democratic Party are not the Child Tax Credit. They are college presidents stumbling over questions about antisemitism, school boards arguing over pronouns, district attorneys declining to prosecute, professors saying intemperate things about Israel, corporate diversity statements, and the brief but vivid "defund the police" moment.

It does not matter that Joe Biden never said "defund the police." It does not matter that Kamala Harris does not actually want to abolish ICE. It does not matter that Chuck Schumer's tweets are about infrastructure. The party is perceived through its loudest and most visible adjacent figures, and the loudest and most visible adjacent figures are the ones who get clicks, which means the ones who say things on cultural questions that are alienating to voters who do not share a college-educated metropolitan worldview. Hacker and Pierson half-acknowledge this when they write that "GOP leaders and right-wing commentators relentlessly pillory the Democrats as obsessed with identity and culture," but they treat this as an external imposition rather than something the party has any agency over.

It is something the party has agency over. When a Democratic president gives a speech about manufacturing, his cabinet secretaries can choose to repeat that message or they can choose to spend their available political capital on other priorities. When activist groups affiliated with the Democratic coalition speak in language that is highly legible to graduate-educated metropolitans and highly illegible to everyone else, leadership can push back on that language or it can defer to it. The choices the party makes about what to celebrate, what to ignore, and what to gently push aside reveal something important about its center of gravity. The Hacker-Pierson account treats this dimension as essentially solved — they note that immigration and criminal justice reform were "put on the legislative backburner" as if this were a strategic masterstroke rather than a tell. A party confident in its connection to working-class voters does not need to hide its position on immigration. It defends a position those voters can accept.

The polling data through 2023 should give Hacker and Pierson pause. Biden is underwater on the economy despite an objectively decent macroeconomic performance. His approval among Hispanic voters has slipped substantially since 2020, and among Black voters under thirty-five it has slipped dramatically. Working-class voters of color — the supposed beneficiaries of the Democrats' redistributive turn — are not flocking to a party that has just delivered them historic levels of spending. Why? The Hacker-Pierson account would predict that they should be, or at least that they should be moving in that direction. They are not. Something is interfering with the transmission of the economic message, and "the media is loud" is not a sufficient explanation, because the media has always been loud.

The interference, I would argue, is cultural distance. The Democratic Party has become, in the perception of many of its erstwhile supporters, a party whose central concerns are those of college-educated professionals: climate, racial equity formalisms, gender identity, foreign policy posturing on Israel and Ukraine, abortion as a values statement rather than a concrete policy. Many of these are good and important commitments. But they are not what most working-class voters, white or non-white, lie awake at night thinking about. And when a party's visible affect signals that those are the things it really cares about, no amount of CTC delivery will close the gap.

Consider an experiment that the Hacker-Pierson framework cannot easily run. Suppose Biden had spent 2021 and 2022 publicly fighting with progressive cultural activists. Suppose he had cracked down hard on the border, given a major speech distancing himself from "defund," signed a federal parental rights bill, and used the bully pulpit to push back on the more excessive forms of campus and corporate progressivism. He would, by Hacker and Pierson's measure, look more "moderate" — and yet his ability to deliver the economic agenda might have been substantially enhanced, because the cultural distance that prevents the economic message from landing would have been narrowed. The Clinton template, hated by today's progressives, understood this. Sister Souljah was not an accident.

I do not think Hacker and Pierson are wrong that the party has tacked left economically. They are right about that, and the description is valuable. But they conclude from this that the cultural critique is misplaced, and that does not follow. A party can tack left economically and still be culturally insular, and the cultural insularity can prevent the economic tack from being electorally rewarded. The relatively poor political return on the Biden agenda is consistent with exactly this diagnosis.

What follows? Not the abandonment of the economic agenda — that should continue. But a willingness, currently absent, to discipline the cultural impulses of the party's most online and least representative wing. Until the Democratic Party can credibly signal to working-class voters that it shares their basic moral universe — that it does not regard their views on crime, immigration, schools, and patriotism as embarrassments to be managed — it will continue to underperform its policy offering. Hacker and Pierson have written a fine description of a party that has its economic policy partly right and its political posture significantly wrong. The latter does not fix itself by being denied.

What Democrats Cannot Touch Tells You What They Are

There is a moment in Hacker and Pierson's essay that, once you notice it, reorganizes the whole argument. They acknowledge that residential zoning, exclusionary local land use, and what they call "opportunity hoarding" are the deepest source of inequality in metropolitan America. They concede that affluent suburban Democrats are "highly protective of these arrangements even when they profess economically and racially progressive values." And then they pivot to the observation that "the localized, low-profile character of these policies facilitates the national party's efforts to keep them far from its core agenda."

This sentence is doing enormous work. It is doing more work than the authors seem to want to acknowledge. Read carefully: the national Democratic Party is choosing to leave undisturbed the single largest mechanism by which wealth, opportunity, and racial advantage are reproduced in the United States — because to do otherwise would split its coalition. The U-shaped coalition the authors celebrate is held together by an unspoken pact in which the affluent leg gets a quiet veto over the policies that most directly threaten its position, while the lower leg gets the visible programs that do not touch the underlying allocation of land, schools, and neighborhoods.

This is not a minor caveat. It is the central feature of the coalition, and it determines what "bold economic agenda" can actually mean. Biden's plans, ambitious as they were on paper, did not include federal preemption of exclusionary zoning. They did not include conditioning transportation or housing dollars on local upzoning. They did not include reforms to the property-tax-financed school system that allows wealthy suburbs to functionally hoard educational resources. They did not touch the mortgage interest deduction in any serious way, or the step-up in basis at death, or the carried interest loophole, or the preferential treatment of capital gains. Hacker and Pierson are correct that the agenda would have done good things for poor families. They underplay how carefully it avoided doing anything that would have meaningfully reduced the wealth of the affluent suburban families on whose votes the coalition depends.

This is what a "Brahmin left" actually is. It is not a left that refuses redistribution. It is a left that practices redistribution in forms that pose no threat to the assets, neighborhoods, schools, and credentialing systems through which the upper-middle class secures its advantages. Spending on infrastructure, climate, manufacturing, child care, and health care can all be financed by taxes on corporations and the very rich. The top 2 percent — Wall Street, the actual capital owners — pays the bill. The top 20 percent — the professional class that constitutes the upscale leg of the U — gets handed redistributive credit without ever being asked to give up the things they actually care about: their houses, their school districts, their professional licensure cartels, their college admissions advantages.

This is a much more cynical reading than Hacker and Pierson's, and I want to be clear that I do not think it requires bad faith from anyone involved. It is a coalitional equilibrium, and equilibria do not require malice. But it has consequences. The most important consequence is that, even on its own terms, the agenda Hacker and Pierson admire cannot solve the problems it identifies. You cannot reduce metropolitan inequality without changing land use. You cannot make housing affordable without breaking the political grip of incumbent homeowners. You cannot equalize educational opportunity while leaving the school district boundary map intact. You cannot reduce wealth inequality while leaving the structural advantages of the top decile untouched and taxing only the top 2 percent.

The housing crisis is the clearest test case. American metropolitan rents and prices have decoupled from incomes over the past three decades, driven primarily by local restrictions on supply. This is not contested. It is true in San Francisco and Los Angeles and Boston and New York and Seattle and Washington. These are the wealthiest, most productive, most Democratic metros in the country. The Democratic Party's local affiliates have, with rare exceptions, been responsible for the regulatory architecture that produces the shortage. There is now a YIMBY movement within the party trying to change this. The Biden administration has gestured at the issue — the federal "housing supply action plan" of 2022, some carrots for local zoning reform. But the gestures are precisely calibrated to avoid actually overriding local autonomy, because to do so would shatter the coalition.

Hacker and Pierson's response is essentially that this is a tragedy, but a manageable one, because national politics can keep the issue out of the spotlight. I would argue the opposite. The shortage is no longer manageable. Housing costs are the dominant material reality of the lives of younger metropolitan voters, including those toward the upscale end of the U-curve. They are the reason millennials feel poorer than their parents despite higher nominal incomes. They are a major driver of fertility decline, of the inability of young families to live near their parents, of the political alienation of the under-forty cohort that is increasingly visible in the data. A party that cannot speak to this issue because its coalition forbids it is a party with a strategic vulnerability that will not stay hidden.

The same point applies to occupational licensing, to the legal cartelization of medicine and law and dentistry, to the protectionist credentialing systems that allow professionals to capture rents. These are bipartisan failures, but they are particularly damning for a center-left party that claims to care about inequality. They are also untouchable for exactly the same reason zoning is untouchable: the coalition will not bear it.

What this means for the Hacker-Pierson thesis is something stronger than a friendly amendment. The "bold economic agenda" they celebrate is bold along precisely those dimensions where the U-shaped coalition has no internal conflict — namely, taxing the very rich and corporations to fund transfers and investments. It is timid, indeed silent, along every dimension where the coalition has a real internal conflict — namely, the protected position of the upper-middle class in housing, education, and labor markets. To call this a transformation overstates what has happened. To call it Piketty's Brahmin left disguised in industrial-policy clothing understates it only modestly.

A genuinely transformative Democratic agenda would begin where Hacker and Pierson stop: at the local arrangements that produce metropolitan inequality. It would dare to split its own coalition along the lines that actually matter. Until it does, "bigger, bolder, broader" will mean bigger transfers from the very rich to the very poor, with the middle of the U-curve receiving symbolic redistribution while keeping its actual privileges intact. That is better than nothing. It is not the new New Deal.

The Strategy of Sidestepping Race Has a Shelf Life

I want to focus on one move in Hacker and Pierson's argument that I think is both true as description and worrying as a long-term prediction: the claim that the Democratic Party has dealt with the racial dimension of its coalition by building racial equity into broad economic programs while keeping explicitly racial rhetoric and explicitly racial policy off the front pages. They credit this strategy with allowing the party to hold together its multiracial, cross-class base. They are right that this is what has been attempted. I am much less convinced that it has worked, or that it can continue to work.

The strategy has two parts. First, design economic programs whose universal benefits flow disproportionately to households of color because those households are disproportionately at the bottom of the distribution. The Child Tax Credit expansion is the paradigm: not a race-targeted program, but one whose effect on Black and Hispanic child poverty was outsized. Second, keep the racially explicit elements of the party's agenda — immigration reform, criminal justice reform, reparations conversations, voting rights framed in racial terms — at a lower volume than the universal economic program. Hacker and Pierson are admirably frank about this: party leadership "put the two most racialized priorities" on the back burner.

This is a coherent strategy. It is also the strategy of every successful liberal politician of the past half century who has wanted to build a multiracial majority, from Bill Clinton to Barack Obama. Obama, in particular, was a master of universalist framing with racially progressive substance. The question is whether the conditions that allowed this strategy to work for Clinton and Obama still obtain, and I think there are good reasons to doubt that they do.

The first problem is that the party's activist base no longer accepts the strategy. The period from 2014 to 2020 saw a sustained challenge from within the Democratic coalition to the universalist framing. Black Lives Matter, the racial reckoning of 2020, the proliferation of explicitly race-conscious language in nonprofits, universities, and major corporations, the emergence of "equity" as a goal distinct from equality — these have changed what the party's most engaged supporters expect and demand. A Democratic president who in 2024 tried to talk the way Obama talked in 2008 about race would face a revolt from his own base. He would be accused of erasure, of throwing Black voters under the bus, of capitulating to white anxiety. The space for universalist framing has narrowed even as the strategic case for it has, if anything, strengthened.

The second problem is that the conservative media environment will not allow the strategy to operate quietly. Hacker and Pierson note that Republican leaders "relentlessly broadcast the image of a 'woke' Democratic party," and they treat this as an unfortunate external constraint. But it is a determinative one. The strategy of building race into economic programs while keeping race out of rhetoric depends on the public not hearing about the racial framing. In an environment where every internal Democratic document, every Justice Department memo, every State Department job posting, every corporate DEI training tied loosely to the administration is being mined and broadcast by an attentive opposition media, the racial dimension cannot be kept off the front pages. It is constantly on the front pages, just not by the party's choosing.

The third problem is that the strategy is producing measurable defections from precisely the voters it is meant to retain. Hispanic voters, who were assumed by many Democratic strategists to be a reliable component of the coalition, have moved noticeably toward the Republicans since 2016. The shifts among Hispanic men in Florida, Texas, and the Rio Grande Valley are particularly striking. Black voters under forty-five show some erosion as well, though less dramatic. If the universal-economics-with-racial-undertones strategy were working, we would expect the opposite: economic delivery should be cementing minority loyalty. It is not.

Why not? I would argue that the strategy assumes a kind of Black and Hispanic voter who exists less and less. It assumes voters whose primary political identity is racial and who respond favorably to a party they understand as quietly working in their interest. But Hispanic and Black voters are not monolithic, and many of them — especially men, especially younger voters, especially those without college degrees — have political identities that are religious, regional, occupational, or simply individual, and that react against a Democratic Party they perceive as obsessed with racial categorization. The very racial framing that the party's progressive activists have insisted on has produced a backlash within the coalition the strategy was designed to hold together.

There is also a deeper issue. The Democratic coalition Hacker and Pierson describe — affluent white professionals plus working-class voters of color — is increasingly held together not by shared interests but by shared opposition to Trump and the Republican Party in its current form. The negative polarization the authors mention is real and is more or less the only thing holding the U-shape in place. Negative polarization is fragile. It depends on the other party's willingness to remain frightening. The moment a Republican emerges who is less personally repulsive while pursuing similar policies — and such Republicans will eventually emerge — the negative-polarization glue weakens, and the underlying contradictions of the Democratic coalition become much more salient.

What follows from this analysis? I do not think the answer is that Democrats should retreat from racial equity. But I do think the strategy of sidestepping requires more discipline than the party is currently capable of exercising, and that discipline has to come from the top. Biden has been, on balance, a model of restraint here. His vice president less so. The activist groups that surround the party, less so still. The Justice Department, the Department of Education, and a range of regulatory agencies have produced documents and rules whose framings undercut the universal message Biden himself tries to deliver. The result is a party whose presidential rhetoric is calibrated to the universalist Clinton-Obama model but whose institutional voice is calibrated to the post-2020 activist model. Voters hear both.

Hacker and Pierson's strategic claim — that Democrats have managed their racial coalition by foregrounding economics — describes the intention better than the reality. The intention is real. The execution is contested. And the political return is, at best, ambiguous. A more honest version of their argument would acknowledge that the party's racial strategy is unstable, that it is partly working at the presidential level and largely failing at the institutional and activist level, and that the failure is showing up in the data. The new blue coalition is not a finished accomplishment. It is a daily, fragile balancing act, and one of the things that could collapse it is the very racial politics the party has tried, with mixed success, to organize out.

In Defense of the Manchin View

It has become fashionable, in the precincts where Hacker and Pierson write and where I sometimes write, to treat Joe Manchin as a villain — the man who stood between America and the largest expansion of the welfare state in two generations. Hacker and Pierson are too sophisticated to put it in those terms, but the structure of their argument carries the same moral charge. The 2021 agenda was a near miss. Manchin and Sinema killed it. The density tax conspired against the metropolitan party. Better luck next time.

I want to offer a contrary reading. Manchin was substantially right on the merits, and the Democratic Party's failure to engage seriously with what he was telling it was a significant strategic error whose consequences are still being felt. The Hacker-Pierson essay, by treating Manchin as an obstacle rather than as a serious interlocutor with a defensible position, misses the most important political economy story of the Biden years.

Start with what Manchin actually said. He said, in early 2022, that the country was facing an inflation problem, that injecting an additional $2 trillion of demand into an already-stretched economy would make it worse, and that programs designed to be temporary should not be made permanent through accounting fictions. Each of these claims was empirically defensible at the time. The administration's own economists were divided. Larry Summers, much maligned in the Hacker-Pierson account, had been warning since early 2021 that the American Rescue Plan was too large for the output gap and would generate inflation. By the time the Build Back Better debate was underway, his predictions had been broadly vindicated. CPI was running above 7 percent. Real wages were falling. The political effects on the Democratic majority were severe.

Hacker and Pierson treat all of this as essentially background noise — they mention inflation only as the justification Manchin invoked, with a faint implication that the justification was opportunistic. But inflation was the central fact of the 2021-2022 economy and is the central fact of the political climate facing Democrats in 2024. It is the reason Biden's approval on the economy is poor despite low unemployment. It is the reason voters of all races and classes report dissatisfaction with their economic situation. And it is, plausibly, partly the product of policy choices that the authors of the essay defend.

Consider the counterfactual. Had the full BBB passed in something like its initial form, with all the demand-side stimulus front-loaded, what would the inflation rate have been in 2022 and 2023? It is hard to say precisely, but the direction is clear. The labor market was already tight. Supply chains were already strained. Additional fiscal stimulus would have pushed against capacity constraints and produced more inflation, not less. The Inflation Reduction Act was rebranded specifically because the political team understood that the public was no longer in the mood for further demand stimulus, and the resulting bill was deliberately less stimulative than what it replaced. The compromise that emerged was, in important respects, the bill that should have been pursued from the beginning.

The deeper point Manchin was making — and that the Democratic political establishment was unable to hear — is that the political economy of an inflationary environment is fundamentally different from the political economy of the secular stagnation era. From roughly 2010 to 2020, the binding macroeconomic constraint was deficient demand. The standard progressive case for more spending was strong. After 2021, the binding constraint shifted to supply, and the standard progressive case had to shift with it. Some progressive economists understood this — there was useful work on "supply-side progressivism" from people like Ezra Klein, the Niskanen Center, and Roosevelt itself. But the party's political machinery did not adjust. It continued to push demand-side programs in an environment that no longer rewarded them.

Hacker and Pierson hint at this when they note that "few if any of [the Democrats'] initiatives seem to have built power through policy." But they treat this as a puzzle to be explained by media noise or insufficient organizing. The more parsimonious explanation is that voters experienced the totality of the Biden agenda as part of an inflationary episode that hurt them, and they apportioned credit and blame accordingly. The CTC extension delivered checks; inflation simultaneously eroded the purchasing power of those checks and of every other income source. The net experience was not gratitude. It was anxiety.

There is a related point about the size and shape of the agenda. A more disciplined Democratic Party, taking Manchin's concerns seriously rather than treating him as an obstacle, would have designed the 2021 package differently. It would have made the popular elements — the CTC expansion, the ACA subsidy enhancement, child care subsidies — permanent, while paring back the elements that were either inflationary or politically marginal. It would have prioritized the climate provisions, which were genuinely transformative and had a long-tail rather than near-term demand effect. It would have funded fewer ambitions more durably. Instead, the party tried to do everything at once, with sunsets engineered to make the price tag look smaller, and ended up with neither permanence nor magnitude.

The Hacker-Pierson essay's framing — bold agenda, narrow miss, structural obstacles — obscures the real lesson. The lesson is that the Democratic Party, having spent the entire Obama era debating whether the stimulus was big enough, swung to the opposite error in 2021 and has had to live with the political consequences. Manchin was the only senior Democrat with sufficient independence to say so. He was vilified for saying so. He was eventually proven roughly correct.

This has implications for what "next time" should look like. Hacker and Pierson urge that the 2021 agenda be ready to revive when the political window reopens. I would urge instead that the next agenda be substantially redesigned. Make fewer things permanent, but actually permanent. Choose carefully which constituencies you want to build. Pay attention to the macroeconomic environment and adjust accordingly. Stop treating moderation as betrayal. The Brahmin left moderates Hacker and Pierson disparage — the Clinton Democrats, the Obama Democrats, the Manchins of the current party — were not wrong about everything. They were right that a center-left party in a polarized country with a tilted institutional playing field has to be disciplined about both the substance and the politics of what it promises. The Biden agenda was insufficiently disciplined on both fronts, and the price has been a presidency that, despite real accomplishments, struggles to defend its record to the voters who supposedly benefited from it.

That is not a story of structural obstacles. It is a story of strategic choices that could have been made differently.

You Cannot Build Working-Class Power Without Building Workers' Organizations

Hacker and Pierson note, almost as an aside, that "few if any of [the Democrats'] initiatives seem to have built power through policy. Even the very large temporary expansion of the child tax credit, for example, seems to have had little impact." This admission deserves more weight than they give it. It is, in fact, the central political fact about the Biden agenda, and it points to what is missing from the entire Hacker-Pierson account: a serious treatment of the labor movement.

The phrase "build power through policy" is associated with Jacob Hacker himself, and it carries a specific meaning. The idea is that well-designed government programs can reshape political coalitions by creating constituencies with stakes in their preservation. Social Security built a constituency for itself. The GI Bill built one. Medicare built one. The Affordable Care Act, after a long struggle, is now beginning to build one. The theory is sound. But it depends on policy doing something quite specific: not just transferring resources, but creating organized groups capable of acting politically in defense of the transfer.

This is where the 2021 agenda fell short, and where the failure was not primarily about Manchin or the filibuster. The Biden agenda contained almost nothing that would have rebuilt organized labor as a political force. The PRO Act, which would have substantially reformed labor law to make union organizing easier, was endorsed in principle but never seriously fought for. Sectoral bargaining did not appear. The agenda's labor provisions were largely additive rather than structural: requirements that subsidized projects use union labor, support for unionization in the care economy, prevailing-wage provisions on infrastructure spending. These are good things, but they do not change the underlying balance of organizational power between workers and employers.

The contrast with the New Deal is instructive. The New Deal built a political coalition that lasted forty years not primarily because of Social Security or unemployment insurance — though those mattered — but because the Wagner Act and the subsequent burst of CIO organizing created a mass labor movement that became the organizational backbone of the Democratic Party. From 1935 to roughly 1975, union density of around 25 to 33 percent of the workforce meant that millions of workers experienced their daily economic lives through institutions that were tied, directly and indirectly, to the Democratic Party. They received union newsletters. They attended union meetings. They were canvassed by union shop stewards. They were turned out by union political operations.

The decline of union density to its current 10 percent — and 6 percent in the private sector — is the single most important fact about American political economy in the past fifty years. It is the precondition for everything Hacker and Pierson describe: the rightward drift of the median Democrat, the rise of the donor class as the party's effective financial base, the cultural dominance of college-educated professionals in party institutions, and the inability of policy benefits to translate into durable political loyalty among working-class voters. You cannot have a working-class political party without working-class organizations, and the Democratic Party has not had robust working-class organizations for two generations.

The CTC story illustrates this perfectly. Hacker and Pierson note that the expansion cut child poverty by 40 percent and yet "had little impact" politically. They are puzzled. They should not be. The CTC was delivered as a Treasury deposit. There was no organization through which recipients experienced the benefit. No one knocked on doors in the spring of 2022 to tell working-class families that the deposits would stop if Manchin won and would continue if he lost. No union local mobilized its members around the issue. No fraternal organization, no church network, no neighborhood association made the CTC into something with social meaning beyond the bank statement. The benefit was atomized. It vanished as quietly as it arrived.

Compare this with how Social Security functions. Social Security is not just a transfer program. It is a transfer program with an enormous organizational ecosystem around it — AARP, the alliance of seniors' groups, the local senior centers, the financial planning industry that incorporates Social Security into retirement advice. Seniors know what Social Security is, who delivers it, who threatens it, and how to mobilize politically to defend it. The CTC had none of this scaffolding. It was a beautiful policy with no political body.

What Hacker and Pierson's essay needs, and largely lacks, is a theory of how labor movement decay constrains everything else they describe. The U-shaped coalition they analyze is in part an artifact of union collapse. In a world with 30 percent union density, the working-class part of the U would be politically organized and would constitute the party's center of gravity. The college-educated professionals would still be there, but they would be a minority faction within a labor-led party, not the dominant institutional voice. The fact that the party today is dominated by its college-educated wing is not a natural feature of postindustrial politics. It is the consequence of a specific policy failure — the inability or unwillingness of the Democratic Party, over forty years, to defend the legal infrastructure that allowed workers to organize.

This has consequences for the Hacker-Pierson conclusion as well. They suggest that "perhaps change will only come with greater direct investments in building a stronger network of supportive organizations, including labor unions." This is right, but it is offered as a passing speculation rather than as the central conclusion. It should be the central conclusion. A Democratic Party that wants to build durable power has to make labor law reform its single highest priority, ahead of any individual transfer program, because without it the transfer programs will not generate the political constituencies needed to make them permanent.

This is a hard argument to advance within the contemporary Democratic Party, because labor law reform is unglamorous, technical, and intensely opposed by exactly the corporate interests that fund the party. It is not popular with affluent suburbanites in the way that climate spending is. It does not generate viral moments in the way that cultural debates do. But it is the precondition for everything else. The 2021 agenda, judged by what it would have left behind organizationally, was an opportunity missed. The next opportunity must not be missed in the same way.

There is also a positive sign Hacker and Pierson largely ignore: the resurgence of labor organizing in the past few years. Starbucks workers, Amazon workers at JFK8, UAW workers winning a remarkable strike against the Big Three in 2023, the broader uptick in NLRB elections and approval of unions in polling. This is happening despite a hostile legal environment, not because of one. Imagine what it could be in a friendly legal environment. The next Democratic majority, if there is one, should be measured primarily by whether it changes that legal environment. Everything else is downstream.

Until they do, the policy debates Hacker and Pierson care about will continue to play out under conditions that systematically defeat democratic majorities.

The authors are honest about this in places. They acknowledge the density tax. They acknowledge that Democrats have won seven of eight presidential popular votes while losing six of nine Supreme Court seats. This is a misallocation of analytic attention. Consider what the institutional asymmetries mean for the policy story. Even if every contestable claim Hacker and Pierson make about the leftward shift of Democratic economics is correct, that shift operates within a system in which fifty-five percent of the voters routinely produce something less than fifty percent of the legislative power. A center-left agenda that commands majority support is structurally unable to enact itself. The 2021 near miss the authors describe was not an aberration. It is the modal outcome of an antimajoritarian system facing a center-left majority.

What follows? In my view, the most important strategic question for the Democratic Party is no longer "what should our policy agenda be" but "how can we change the rules of the game so that policy agendas, ours or anyone else's, can actually be enacted when they have majority support." This is a procedural question, and procedural questions are politically difficult because they are abstract and remote from voters' daily concerns. But the alternative — continuing to write detailed policy programs that the institutional system is designed to block — has reached the limits of what it can accomplish.

The procedural agenda is reasonably well understood. It includes filibuster reform; admission of D.C. as a state and serious consideration of Puerto Rican statehood; expansion or restructuring of the federal judiciary; a national popular vote interstate compact or constitutional amendment for the presidency; voting rights legislation along the lines of the Freedom to Vote Act; reforms to the redistricting process; reforms to campaign finance. None of these are policy in the conventional sense. All of them would change the structural environment within which policy is made. And none of them are politically easy, because the institutional Republican Party correctly understands that its survival as a competitive force depends on the maintenance of these antimajoritarian rules.

Hacker and Pierson are not unaware of this agenda. But they treat it as background. The essay's emphasis is on what Democrats want to do, not on what would have to change for them to be able to do it. This is the same imbalance one finds in much liberal policy commentary, and it is, I think, partly responsible for a kind of strategic exhaustion. We are encouraged to celebrate near-miss policy victories rather than to focus on the institutional architecture that produced the near miss.

There is also a deeper issue, which is that the antimajoritarian features of the system are interacting with the radicalization of the Republican Party to produce something genuinely dangerous. The January 6 reference in the essay's opening paragraph is real and grave. The authors note that "the ability of the United States to resist the threat of democratic backsliding seems to rest almost entirely on the electoral success of the Democratic Party." This is true. If American democracy depends on a single party winning electoral majorities under rules that systematically disadvantage that party, the long-run probability of democratic failure is high. We are essentially asking the Democrats to hit a moving target that gets harder to hit each cycle. They will not succeed forever.

The institutional reform agenda is the only path out of this trap. A democracy that can routinely translate majorities into governing power is more stable than one that cannot. A judiciary not dominated by minority-appointed justices is more legitimate. A Senate that does not give Wyoming the same weight as California is more representative. These changes are not partisan in the simple sense, even though one party would lose power under them. They are structural preconditions for the functioning of constitutional democracy in a country that has become too unequal and too polarized for its eighteenth-century rules.

I would also press Hacker and Pierson on a related point. Their account of the Democratic Party's policy evolution emphasizes endogenous factors: the rise of the progressive wing, the influence of think tanks, the COVID shock. But these factors operate within an institutional environment that selects for certain kinds of politicians and certain kinds of policy entrepreneurship. A party that knows it will face filibusters and gerrymandered House maps and an unfriendly Court will produce a different set of leaders than a party that knows it can enact what it wins. The institutional environment shapes the policy environment, and not just at the margins.

There is one consolation in the Hacker-Pierson essay that I want to credit. They are right that the Democratic Party has been more ambitious in the Biden era than its critics on the left have acknowledged. They are right that the Brahmin-left framing misses something important about the current coalition. They are right that the work of preparing for the next opportunity has to happen now. I would simply add: the most important work is not policy design. It is institutional reform. The next Democratic majority, if it gets one, should spend its political capital on the filibuster, on D.C. statehood, on voting rights, on the courts, before it spends any of it on policy. Without those changes, the policy near miss will repeat itself indefinitely, and each near miss will be celebrated by essays like this one as a moral victory while the system continues to grind toward minority rule.

Hacker and Pierson have written the policy essay that the moment calls for. The essay the moment more urgently requires is the institutional one. I hope they write it next.

What would a more honest Democratic racial politics look like? It would acknowledge that economic growth alone does not remedy racial inequality, that historical policies created advantages that persist today, and that race-conscious remedies remain necessary despite their political costs. The authors document Democratic boldness on economics while implicitly conceding Democratic timidity on race. That timidity is not a strategic choice to be celebrated; it is a constraint to be overcome.

The lead essay makes an important contribution to comparative political analysis by situating American Democrats within a global pattern. The "Brahmin left" thesis—borrowed from Thomas Piketty—argues that left-of-center parties across wealthy democracies have shifted toward educated professionals while losing working-class voters. The authors argue that Democrats have bucked this trend by combining Brahmin appeal with redistributive ambition.

This argument is suggestive but requires closer examination. The comparison to European left parties is illuminating, but the institutional contexts differ in crucial ways. European center-left parties typically operate under proportional representation systems where coalition governance is normal and ideologically diverse coalitions are structural necessities rather than strategic choices. The German Social Democrats or British Labour can form governments with explicit programmatic commitments to redistribution because their electoral systems translate votes into seats proportionally and their coalition partners share broadly similar orientations.

American Democrats operate under radically different constraints. The first-past-the-post system punishes small-party movements, but it also prevents the kind of programmatic bargaining that characterizes coalition government in Europe. The Democratic Party must be simultaneously a coalition of interest groups, a electoral vehicle, and a governing institution—a combination that creates internal tensions European parties manage through coalition agreements with separate parties.

The authors recognize these constraints but treat them as obstacles to be overcome rather than features of the system to be worked around. They note that "the density tax... increases pressure for economic moderation" and that Democrats "badly need upscale suburban votes." But they do not seriously engage with the possibility that the Democratic Party's structural position requires fundamental institutional reform rather than strategic adjustment.

Consider what a serious response to the density tax would look like. It would include advocacy for statehood for DC and Puerto Rico, which would add two reliably Democratic senators immediately. It would include pressure for electoral reform, including potentially multi-member districts or ranked-choice voting in House elections. It would include campaign finance reform to reduce the importance of donor concentration in high-density areas. None of these appear as priorities in the authors' analysis. Instead, they offer a strategy of managing constraints within existing institutions.

This is understandable politically but intellectually limiting. The authors have provided an excellent analysis of Democratic strategy within current constraints. They have not asked whether those constraints should be treated as permanent.

The most striking claim in the lead essay is that "Democrats are now much less clearly the party of the 'have nots.'" The authors support this claim with impressive data: Democrats now represent 24 of the 25 wealthiest congressional districts, win counties producing 71 percent of GDP, and dominate the 24 most economically productive metro areas. This is a genuine transformation worth taking seriously.

But what does it mean for democracy? The authors implicitly assume that Democratic success is democratic success—that a party governing for the affluent while maintaining support among the poor is better than the alternative. This assumption deserves scrutiny.

Consider the policy implications of Democratic reliance on affluent suburban voters. These voters are, by definition, beneficiaries of the existing economic order. They have mortgages to protect, retirement accounts to preserve, and neighborhood arrangements to maintain. Their policy preferences may align with Democratic redistribution in the abstract, but they also include resistance to housing construction that might lower property values, opposition to tax increases that might affect their marginal rates, and ambivalence about redistribution that might be perceived as benefiting "others" more than themselves.

The authors acknowledge these tensions when they discuss opportunity hoarding and local control. But they treat these as problems to be managed rather than contradictions to be resolved. The party's ability to "organize issues out" of politics is presented as a strategic success; it might equally be described as a failure of democratic accountability. When affluent suburban interests are protected by political design rather than democratic deliberation, the result is quiet plutocracy dressed in progressive clothing.

The authors also understate the degree to which affluent Democratic voters may be motivated by cultural rather than economic factors. College-educated suburbanites may support Democratic economic policies because they believe in redistribution, but they may also support Democrats as a cultural signal—as the party of tolerance, professionalism, and cosmopolitanism opposed to the cultural backwardness of Republicans. If the latter motivation is stronger, then the coalition's commitment to redistribution is more fragile than the authors assume.

This matters for 2024 and beyond. If affluent suburban support for Democrats is primarily cultural rather than economic, then a Republican Party that moderates on cultural issues—or a Democratic Party that is perceived to have gone too far on redistribution—could peel off these voters rapidly. The economic commitments the authors celebrate may be epiphenomenal, contingent on cultural alignment that cannot be assumed to persist.

I want to engage with the lead essay's treatment of what might be called the "electoral linkage" question: does policy success translate into electoral success? The authors acknowledge that "there is so far little evidence that the Democrats' efforts to expand their coalition through ambitious policy have yielded significant political results." This admission is significant and deserves more attention than the authors give it.

The historical record on policy-electorate linkages is mixed. The Affordable Care Act, as the authors note, became more popular over time. But this is the exception; more often, policy successes fail to translate into electoral support. Obama's stimulus prevented a depression but was criticized as inadequate. Trump's trade wars failed to deliver promised manufacturing revival but did not prevent his reelection. George W. Bush's Medicare prescription drug benefit did not save Republicans in 2006 or 2008.

Why does policy success fail to translate? Several mechanisms are plausible. First, voters may not attribute policy outcomes to the governing party if the economy is complex or if opposing media emphasize other causes. Second, policy benefits are often diffuse and invisible—the absence of a worse outcome is hard to notice—while policy costs are often concentrated and salient. Third, the electoral cycle may not align with policy effects; voters may reward or punish incumbents based on current conditions rather than past policy.

For Democrats, these dynamics create a structural problem. Their ambitious agenda required years of legislative struggle that dominated news coverage and generated constant criticism. The benefits—reduced child poverty, expanded healthcare access, climate investment—would materialize slowly and diffusely. The costs—higher taxes on corporations, regulatory burden on business, perceived inflation—would be immediate and visible. This asymmetry may have made the policy agenda politically counterproductive even if it was substantively sound.

The authors seem to recognize this dynamic when they note that "the media landscape is now so noisy that the ability of a party to build electoral support through economic governance is severely limited." But they do not follow this recognition to its conclusion. If the media environment prevents policy success from translating into electoral support, then the strategy of governing for policy achievements is insufficient for political survival. Democrats need something else—perhaps a more robust political communication infrastructure, perhaps a willingness to make controversial claims about their accomplishments, perhaps a different balance between policy ambition and political messaging.

The authors have documented Democratic policy boldness. They have not explained why that boldness should succeed politically. That explanation awaits future work.

The lead essay is primarily a defense of Democratic Party strategy, and as such it performs the ideological work of legitimating that strategy. This is not a criticism—it is an observation about genre. Forum essays are typically contributions to ongoing political debates, and contributors typically take positions. But readers should understand that the perspective offered is a particular one: from within the coalition the authors analyze, looking outward at electoral and policy possibilities.

Consider what a critique from the left might look like. The authors celebrate Democratic boldness on economics but do not ask whether that boldness was sufficient. The American Jobs Plan called for $2 trillion in infrastructure spending over eight years—approximately 1 percent of GDP per year. The Green New Deal, which inspired elements of Biden's approach, called for $10-15 trillion over ten years. By the standards of the crisis Democrats claimed to be addressing—climate change, infrastructure decay, care economy collapse—the Biden agenda was modest, not bold.

Similarly, the authors treat the shift in Democratic economic policy as a break with the past, but that break was partial and reversible. Clinton's deficit reduction and welfare reform remained uncontested. The 2017 tax cuts were slated for partial rather than full repeal. Financial regulation remained weaker than pre-2008 levels. The party that the authors describe as pursuing "the most extensive package of economic benefits for working- and middle-class families the Democrats had proposed since at least the 1960s" was simultaneously a party that had not reversed any major rightward shift of the previous four decades.

A left critique would also question the coalitional analysis. The authors argue that Democrats successfully managed tensions between affluent suburbanites and poorer urban voters. But one could argue that affluent suburban interests have not merely been accommodated—they have been prioritized. The failure to address zoning reform, the retention of state and local tax deductions that disproportionately benefit high-tax affluent jurisdictions, the modest proposals on housing subsidy reform: these suggest that when push came to shove, Democratic protection of affluent voter interests trumped redistribution to the poor.

None of this means the authors are wrong about the direction of Democratic change. The party has clearly shifted left on economics, and this shift is worth documenting and analyzing. But it is worth analyzing critically, with attention to what the authors do not say as well as what they do. The New Blue Divide is a real phenomenon. Whether it represents genuine transformation or rhetorical adjustment remains, as the authors themselves admit, to be seen.
