Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Nancy Hirschmann has named something real. Anyone who has taught undergraduates in the last decade has met Susan and Anthony, and anyone who has watched the discourse around "opt-out" mothers, intensive parenting, and the helicopter hover knows that a certain kind of full-time motherhood has curdled into something that looks less like care than like dynastic management. The question is whether care feminism is responsible for this, and whether the remedy is, as Hirschmann suggests, for women to "care a little less" and become "stronger in individualist terms." On both counts I am unpersuaded — not because Hirschmann is wrong about the symptoms, but because her diagnosis collapses distinctions that matter and her prescription reprivatizes a problem that requires structural answers.

Start with the anecdotes. Carla Wagner's parents and Anthony's mother are not displaying care; they are displaying what Annette Lareau, in *Unequal Childhoods* (2003), calls "concerted cultivation" run amok — an upper-middle-class project of clearing obstacles from a child's path so that class advantage can be reproduced. The mother who berates a professor for failing her plagiarizing son is not loving him too much; she is treating him as a portfolio asset and the professor as an impediment. Joan Tronto, Sara Ruddick, Virginia Held — the care theorists Hirschmann has in mind — explicitly distinguished care from this kind of tribalism. Ruddick's "preservative love" is bound to a practice of "training for sociability," not to clearing your child's record with the dean. Calling Anthony's mother's behavior a "perversion of care" is one move; saying it shows what care theory enables is quite another. Hirschmann concedes that none of these mothers read the theory. The harder question is whether they would have recognized themselves in it if they had.

This matters because the conflation does political work in the essay. When Hirschmann's students sneer at welfare mothers while romanticizing stay-at-home motherhood for themselves, the problem isn't that care theory gave them the vocabulary. The problem is that an older American ideology — the family wage, the deserving versus undeserving poor, the privatized household — was never displaced. Care feminism in its strongest versions (Eva Feder Kittay's *Love's Labor*, 1999; the welfare-rights tradition Hirschmann briefly invokes) argued for *universal* recognition of dependency work, precisely against the moralized double standard her students reproduce. If those students are using "care" to launder class contempt, the answer is to teach them the rest of the tradition, not to abandon it.

The empirical premise of the essay also deserves scrutiny. Hirschmann leans on Lisa Belkin's 2003 "Opt-Out Revolution" as if it described a real demographic shift. By 2010 we have good reasons to doubt that. Heather Boushey's 2005 analysis for the Center for Economic and Policy Research found no statistically significant "opt-out" effect among college-educated mothers once the broader recession-era labor market was controlled for. Pamela Stone's *Opting Out?* (2007) — based on interviews with the very women Belkin profiled — found that "choice" was largely a face-saving narrative imposed on women who had been pushed out by inflexible workplaces and husbands who refused to share the second shift. The students in Hirschmann's class are responding less to a feminist theory than to a *Times* trend piece that misread its own evidence. That changes the politics. If the issue isn't a wave of empowered women choosing domesticity but a structural squeeze that makes paid work untenable for mothers, then exhorting women to "insist upon change themselves, in their individual relationships" is exactly the wrong response.

This is where I most resist Hirschmann's turn toward Linda Hirshman. The argument that women should retain economic clout in order to bargain with their husbands has surface appeal, but it presumes that the bargaining table is the relevant site of contest. It is not — or rather, it is only one site, and the least promising one. As Hirschmann herself acknowledges, men with access to "family-friendly" policies don't use them, because the workplace penalizes them; women take maternity leave because the workplace expects them to. The "motherhood penalty" Mary Blair-Loy and others document is not a problem any individual couple can solve through better negotiation. It is a problem of how jobs, careers, and benefits are designed. Telling women to bargain harder while leaving those designs in place is asking them to win a game whose rules guarantee they will lose.

The better-supported alternative, by 2010, comes from Scandinavia. Iceland's 2000 parental-leave reform reserved three months of paid leave exclusively for fathers — use it or lose it. Sweden's "daddy month," introduced in 1995 and extended in 2002, did the same. Both produced dramatic increases in fathers' actual uptake of leave, with downstream effects on the division of housework and on mothers' wage trajectories. Gøsta Esping-Andersen's work on "de-familialization" makes the broader case: only when caring work is partially socialized (through public childcare, eldercare, and non-transferable paternity leave) does the household become a site where genuine renegotiation of gender roles is possible. Hirschmann is right that Cuba's 1975 Family Code didn't work; but a paper edict against an entire economic structure is not the same as the patient redesign of incentives.

This points to what I think care feminism actually got right and what it got wrong. It was right to insist that dependency work is valuable, that justice cannot be theorized as if all citizens were independent adults, and that care should be recognized publicly. It was wrong — or at least incomplete — to leave the gendered allocation of that work to private negotiation. Hirschmann ends by saying "it is women's power, not care itself, that will enable gender equality." I would put it differently: it is the *unbundling* of care from womanhood that will enable gender equality, and that unbundling cannot be accomplished by women individually choosing to care less. It requires structures — non-transferable paternity leave, universal childcare, workplaces that penalize the absent father rather than the present mother — that make it materially irrational for men to keep free-riding.

The mothers Hirschmann opens with do not need to care less. They need to care about more than their own children — about the sixteen-year-old Carla Wagner killed, about the welfare mothers her students despise. That is what care theory, at its best, has always demanded. The task is to keep that demand and add to it a politics that can actually make men show up.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Hirschmann’s provocative essay is a welcome intervention, but her argument mistakes symptoms for causes. She rightly worries that idealizing care has not freed women from the double day, but her solution—that women must “care less” and gain economic power—risks abandoning the very insights care feminism contributed. The problem is not that care theory is flawed; it is that a society organized around market values and male privilege has perverted it.

Her parade of horribles—mothers who lie for children, daughters who skip finals for sorority rush—describes elite entitlement, not the practice of care. The mother who berates a professor for her son’s plagiarism is enacting a culture of privilege that long predates care feminism. Hirschmann concedes these parents never read the theory, yet she blames it for being misused. That is like blaming a cookbook for a bad meal prepared by someone who ignored the recipe.

The four implications she draws from treating care as a public good are startling but largely straw women. Public oversight of care already exists—welfare conditionality, child protective services—but it falls hardest on poor mothers. Treating care as a public good need not mean micromanaging parenting; it can mean, as the welfare rights movement argued, a guaranteed income for all caregivers, without tying it to measurable outcomes. Her suggestion that we might jail mothers of drug dealers or tax them for the love they get is a parody, not a logical corollary. Feminists have long distinguished between compensating care as a social contribution and turning mothers into state contractors.

Hirschmann’s most troubling move is to blame women’s choices for the persistence of the sexual division of labor. She argues that stay-at-home mothers reinforce patriarchy and that the tax code’s marriage penalty reflects their preferences. But this reverses causality. The tax system was designed in the 1930s and 1940s to privilege a male breadwinner model, and it persists because powerful interests benefit, not because opt-out women lobby for it. Women who stay home are responding rationally to labor markets that penalize mothers, a care infrastructure that is grossly inadequate, and a culture that shames mothers who “outsource” childrearing. Blaming them for not fighting harder echoes the very individualism Hirschmann rejects.

Her call for women to gain economic leverage is not new; it is a return to a liberal feminism that care theory originally sought to transcend. But as Arlie Hochschild has shown for decades, women’s paychecks do not magically produce egalitarian households. Working-class and minority women have always worked for wages, yet patriarchy endures. Rather than urging women to withdraw care, we should demand policies that truly value it—universal childcare, paid parental leave, a shorter workweek—policies that would enable men to participate without stigma. Hirschmann is right that care feminism must confront its romanticizations, but the path forward is not less care, but a collective struggle to make care a shared responsibility.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Nancy J. Hirschmann accurately diagnoses a frustrating reality of contemporary gender politics: the stalled revolution in the home. Despite decades of feminist theorizing and activism, women still perform the vast majority of unpaid care work, a dynamic that profoundly limits their economic and political power. Hirschmann is also right to point out that the language of "care" is too often co-opted by elite women to justify a retreat into traditional domesticity, sheltering themselves and their privileged children from the broader demands of civic engagement.

Yet, while her diagnosis of this "opting out" phenomenon is sharp, Hirschmann’s proposed solution—that women must amass individual economic clout to force men into doing their fair share—fundamentally misdiagnoses the root of the problem. By urging women to "care a little less" and embrace individualism to gain bargaining power, Hirschmann inadvertently asks feminism to capitulate to the very patriarchal, capitalist structures it originally sought to transform.

First, it is crucial to recognize the deep class bias inherent in Hirschmann’s critique. Her argument relies heavily on anecdotes about sorority members, future investment bankers, and Lisa Belkin’s much-debated 2003 "Opt-Out Revolution." But the women in Belkin’s article, much like Hirschmann’s students, represent a highly privileged fraction of society. For the vast majority of American women, "opting out" of the workforce is simply not an economic possibility. Working-class and middle-class women are already performing the paid labor Hirschmann prescribes, yet they remain saddled with Arlie Hochschild’s famous "second shift." Their participation in the wage economy has not magically granted them the leverage to equalize the domestic sphere. If economic clout were the sole prerequisite for equality at home, the millions of women who currently serve as primary breadwinners would not still be doing the majority of the laundry and childcare.

Furthermore, Hirschmann’s strategy privatizes the struggle for gender equality, placing the immense burden of dismantling patriarchy squarely onto the shoulders of individual women. She suggests that because public policies like maternity leave have failed to change male behavior, women must take matters into their own hands by wielding their salaries as cudgels in marital negotiations. This transforms the feminist political project into a series of exhausting, individualized domestic disputes. It is profoundly unfair to demand that women not only navigate workplaces inherently hostile to caregivers but also return home to fight a relentless war of attrition with their partners. Men's refusal to perform care work is a systemic failure, bolstered by a society that penalizes male caregiving. It cannot be solved simply by women becoming tougher negotiators in the living room.

Most concerning is Hirschmann’s retreat to the language of market individualism. She argues that women must become stronger in "individualist terms" and that our vision of care should resemble "tough love." But care feminism, born from thinkers like Carol Gilligan, emerged precisely because the male-dominated sphere of market individualism is an impoverished model for human flourishing. To abandon the effort to elevate care as a public good just because it is difficult is to accept that the only work that matters is wage labor. If we concede that economic clout is the only valid currency for social power, we abandon the millions of necessary, life-sustaining acts of care that cannot—and should not—be monetized or leveraged.

Hirschmann is correct that public policies have not yet succeeded in forcing men to change. However, the answer is not to abandon the demand for structural support, but to radically expand it. We must advocate for policies that do not merely accommodate women’s caregiving, but actively incentivize men’s, such as the "use-it-or-lose-it" paternity leaves being pioneered in parts of Europe.

Feminism should not settle for a world where women achieve equality only by imitating the unencumbered male worker of the industrial age. We do not need women to care less; we need a society that cares more. Forcing women to trade the profound, albeit currently burdensome, work of care for the cold leverage of a paycheck is not a victory for equality. It is a surrender to a system that was built without women in mind.

Nancy Hirschmann is right to worry about the sentimentalization of care. A feminism that treats mothering as naturally ennobling, politically innocent, or automatically democratic has forgotten too much: the privatized family has always been a school not only of love, but also of hierarchy, entitlement, racism, and obedience. The mother who lies to a professor for her daughter, the parent who shields a drunk driver from consequence, the family that treats rules as obstacles for other people—these are not aberrations outside the domain of care. They are among care’s familiar temptations. Particular love can enlarge moral imagination, but it can also shrink it to the size of “my child.”

Still, I resist Hirschmann’s conclusion that the central remedy is for women to “care a little less,” if that means that individual women must refuse full-time mothering in order to force men to change. That proposal risks making women once again the shock absorbers of a broken system. Women are told first to absorb the costs of care because children need it, then to absorb the costs of refusing care because equality needs it. Either way, the burden of transformation falls on women’s private decisions.

The trouble is not care as such, nor even the claim that care is a public good. The trouble is that care remains organized as a private dependency relation, with women expected to provide it invisibly and men permitted to treat it as optional. Hirschmann sees this clearly. But her skepticism about state and employer reforms seems too sweeping. It is true that “family-friendly” policies often fail when they are formally gender-neutral but socially gendered: men do not take leave, women do, and employers penalize mothers while rewarding fathers. But that is not an argument against policy. It is an argument against weak policy.

A leave policy that can be transferred from father to mother will often become maternal leave. A workplace culture that praises the always-available employee will punish the worker who cares. A tax structure that treats the second earner as an add-on will reinforce wives’ economic dependence. These are not reasons to retreat to bargaining at the kitchen table. They are reasons to design institutions that change the bargaining position before the couple ever gets to the table: individual taxation, affordable childcare, shorter standard workweeks, paid leave with nontransferable portions for each parent, and protections strong enough to make caregiving by men ordinary rather than heroic.

Hirschmann is right that law cannot send “housework police” into homes. But law has never needed to regulate every act directly in order to shape norms. Public schooling did not eliminate class inequality, but it changed the meaning of childhood and citizenship. Social Security did not abolish dependence in old age, but it made elderly poverty less inevitable. Anti-discrimination law did not end racism or sexism, but it altered what institutions could openly demand. Likewise, care policy cannot by itself make men loving, competent caregivers. But it can make the male breadwinner/female caregiver model less economically rational and less culturally automatic.

I am also unconvinced by the suggestion that if care is publicly supported, the state is thereby entitled to judge mothers by their children’s outcomes. We support many public goods without demanding impossible guarantees. We fund schools though not every student becomes a good citizen. We subsidize mortgage interest though not every homeowner strengthens a community. We provide police and courts though they do not eliminate crime. Public support need not mean that mothers are paid only if they produce morally admirable adults. It can mean that a decent society recognizes dependency as a universal human condition and refuses to make those who meet dependent needs poor, isolated, or subordinate.

Here Hirschmann’s distinction between a citizenship-based entitlement and a “social value” wage is important. I would follow the first path more firmly than she does. Caregivers need support not because each child can be proved to yield a measurable civic return, but because children, the disabled, the sick, and the elderly require care, and because the people who provide it should not be punished with poverty or civic marginality. The case for public support should rest less on mothers’ moral productivity and more on social justice: dependency work is necessary, unequally distributed, and often exploited.

Hirschmann’s essay is strongest when it exposes class hypocrisy. Her students idealize stay-at-home motherhood for themselves while condemning poor mothers for not working. That double standard has been central to American welfare politics. Middle-class mothers are praised for intensive devotion; poor mothers are disciplined for dependence. Affluent children need enrichment; poor children need their mothers’ labor-market discipline. Any feminism worthy of the name must reject this divide. But the answer cannot be to universalize suspicion of mothers. It must be to challenge the economic and racial arrangements that make some women’s care honorable and others’ care pathological.

The deepest question is what kind of persons families are preparing children to become. Hirschmann rightly invokes Wollstonecraft’s “jostlings of equality”: children need institutions beyond the family where they encounter peers as equals, not merely parents as advocates. But this is an argument for robust public life—good schools, integrated neighborhoods, youth programs, civic obligations—not simply for mothers’ labor-force attachment. Paid work can foster independence, but it does not automatically produce democratic character. The investment banker who works eighty hours a week is not necessarily a better citizen than the mother who volunteers at a shelter or organizes a school board campaign.

Women need power. On that Hirschmann is exactly right. But power will not come only from women caring less. It will come from making care less feminine, less private, less economically ruinous, and less easily confused with indulgence. Feminism should not celebrate the self-sacrificing mother, but neither should it shame the caregiver. Its demand should be more radical: everyone gives care, everyone receives care, and no one is made subordinate because of it.

The lead essay's central claim—that the feminist celebration of care has inadvertently reinforced women's disempowerment—is provocative and warrants serious engagement. Nancy J. Hirschmann’s argument that "opting out" perpetuates gender inequality and fails to transform the sexual division of labor is compelling, especially in light of data showing that even family-friendly policies do not shift men’s participation in caregiving. However, the essay’s conclusion—that women must "care a little less" and gain individualist power—risks underestimating the structural dimensions of oppression and oversimplifying the relationship between choice, care, and equality.

Hirschmann rightly identifies a paradox at the heart of care feminism: by emphasizing the social value of mothering, feminists may have inadvertently legitimized a role that keeps women economically dependent and politically marginalized. The evidence she cites—for instance, that married women still do two to three times more childcare and housework than men—is difficult to refute. Moreover, her observation that women who "opt out" often forfeit positions of leadership is salient, given that their absence from public life weakens the critical mass needed to challenge gender hierarchies. The essay’s critique of tax policies that penalize dual-income families and its analysis of the "motherhood penalty" in employment further illustrate how systemic inequalities constrain women’s choices.

Yet the essay’s prescription—women must "care a little less" and become stronger in "individualist terms"—presents a solution that, while pragmatic, may not address the root causes of gendered care labor. Hirschmann acknowledges that coercion is often subtle, embedded in culture and policy rather than overt. If women are to insist on change within individual relationships, they must do so in a context where men have little incentive to relinquish power. The essay’s reliance on women’s economic leverage assumes that such leverage will translate into relational change, but this may not account for the stubborn persistence of patriarchal norms. As Hirschmann herself notes, even in Cuba, where formal equality was mandated by law, the sexual division of labor remained largely unchanged. This suggests that individual bargaining within families is insufficient without broader cultural and institutional transformation.

Furthermore, the essay’s framing of "choice" understates the extent to which choices are shaped by structural constraints. While Hirschmann nods to Ann Cudd’s argument that individual choices can perpetuate oppression, she stops short of fully grappling with the implications. If women’s "opting out" is a collective action problem—where each individual’s rational choice reinforces a system that harms all women—then the solution cannot rest solely on individual women making different choices. The essay would benefit from engaging more deeply with the concept of structural coercion and the ways in which care feminism might be refined to address it.

Hirschmann’s critique of state intervention is also nuanced but potentially overly pessimistic. She argues that the state has been an unreliable ally for women’s equality, pointing to welfare reform and family-friendly policies that fail to change the division of labor. However, this overlooks successes such as Title IX, anti-discrimination laws, and policies that have expanded women’s access to education and employment. The state, while imperfect, remains a site of contestation where feminist advocacy can yield tangible gains. Dismissing state intervention altogether may cede ground to forces that reinforce gender inequality.

The essay’s most valuable insight is its insistence that care feminism must reckon with the power dynamics it has inadvertently supported. The vision of care as an ethic of connection and responsibility is not inherently flawed, but it must be accompanied by strategies that challenge the material conditions under which care is performed. This means advocating for policies that make men’s participation in caregiving not just possible but normative—parental leave that is explicitly gendered to encourage men’s uptake, for instance, or public investment in affordable childcare that reduces the burden on mothers. It also means continuing to demand workplace reforms that do not penalize mothers, while recognizing that such reforms alone will not dismantle gendered expectations.

In sum, Hirschmann’s essay raises important challenges for care feminism, but its prescription risks placing the burden of change disproportionately on women. A more robust feminist response would insist on structural transformations—in law, policy, and culture—that redistribute care labor equitably. Until men are not just allowed but expected to care, women will continue to bear a burden that "caring a little less" cannot alleviate.

**The Anecdote Is Not the Argument**

Nancy Hirschmann's essay opens with three vivid stories: the tequila-drunk teen driver headed for Paris, the sorority rusher trading on a fake memorial service, the plagiarizing senior whose mother claims he "isn't a cheater." These are striking. They are also, I want to insist, doing far more rhetorical work than they should be allowed to do. Before we follow Hirschmann to her sweeping conclusions about care feminism, the sexual division of labor, and the duty of women to become more individualist, we ought to pause over the evidentiary base.

What do these anecdotes actually show? They show three privileged, badly-behaved young people whose parents enable them. They do not show that full-time mothering produced this enabling. We are not told whether Carla Wagner's mother stayed home or worked; we are not told whether Susan's mother is a high-powered attorney who phoned in the lie between meetings; we are not told that Anthony's mother quit her job to raise him. In fact, the failure pattern Hirschmann describes—wealthy parents covering for their children's misconduct—is at least as well documented among dual-career professional families as among full-time homemakers. The "helicopter parent" of the 2000s discourse is often a working mother on her BlackBerry, calling the dean between conference calls.

This matters because Hirschmann uses the anecdotes to establish that "full-time mothering" produces moral failure, which then becomes the wedge for her broader claim that women's withdrawal from paid labor is socially destructive. But the anecdotes establish nothing of the kind. They establish that affluent American parenting is often morally compromised by an obsessive investment in the child's worldly success. That is a critique of American class culture, not of mothering as such, and certainly not of care feminism, which Hirschmann concedes none of these mothers had ever read.

I worry about a second slippage too. Hirschmann moves seamlessly from "many wealthy stay-at-home mothers do not raise public-spirited citizens" to "stay-at-home mothering does not serve responsibility to self, others, and community." But these are different claims. The first is about a particular class culture in which competitive child-cultivation has replaced civic life for both parents. The second indicts a much larger and more varied population—including the immigrant grandmother raising her grandchildren, the rural mother of four whose husband works two jobs, the lesbian co-parent who left her job because daycare cost more than her salary, the African-American mother who homeschools because her local public school has failed black boys for three generations. These women are also "stay-at-home mothers." Are they, too, opting out of the fight for equality?

Hirschmann's classroom is, by her own admission, the source of her sample. Her students are women at an elite university who plan to marry men who will support them while they raise one or two children. This is a small and historically peculiar slice of American life. To diagnose the failures of care feminism on the basis of how this slice rationalizes its anticipated choices is to confuse a sociology of elite reproduction with a critique of a theoretical tradition.

There is a deeper problem. The essay is structured around bad behavior—the lying student, the cheating senior, the drunk driver—but the political theory of care has never claimed that care, performed by whomever, produces good citizens automatically. Gilligan's claim was descriptive and normative about a moral orientation, not predictive about parenting outcomes. Sara Ruddick's "maternal thinking" identified practices and virtues, not guarantees. Joan Tronto situated care within political structures of power and inequality precisely to resist the romantic reading Hirschmann attributes to "us." Hirschmann's critique works only if we accept that care feminism promised a kind of moral transmission belt: caring mother in, public-spirited citizen out. No serious care theorist made that promise. Hirschmann is rebutting a strawman version of the tradition she helped build—and then drawing prescriptive conclusions for actual women's lives on the basis of the rebuttal.

I share many of her policy worries. The marriage penalty for dual earners is real. The "motherhood penalty" Blair-Loy and others document is real. The failure of family-friendly policies to actually redistribute domestic labor is real. The capture of "choice" rhetoric by a small group of affluent women, amplified into the bogus "opt-out revolution" by Lisa Belkin and her imitators, is real. But the response should be a clearer-eyed sociology of how class, race, and labor markets actually shape women's lives—not a return to the prescriptive framework of Linda Hirshman, who at the end of the day tells most women to behave more like a small subset of professional-managerial women.

Hirschmann ends by calling for women to "care a little less." I want to ask: which women? The single mother in Mississippi caring for her diabetic mother and three children is not the woman whose excessive care is sustaining patriarchy. The hedge-fund wife planning her son's college application strategy from his kindergarten is not engaged in care at all in any morally weighted sense; she is engaged in dynastic positioning. Lumping these women together under the banner of "full-time mothering" obscures everything that matters about the structural problem.

Care feminism's failures, where they exist, are failures of how the tradition has been popularized, not failures of its core insight: that the unpaid sustaining labor of human life is socially valuable, intellectually serious, and politically constitutive. We should hold onto that insight. We should also build better tools for distinguishing the moral practice of care from the class-coded simulacrum of "good parenting" that produces the Carlas and Anthonys of Hirschmann's anecdotes. Those tools will not come from urging women to care less. They will come from a sharper analytic separation between care as a practice and child-cultivation as a project of class reproduction—which is the actual sin on display in the opening stories.

I am grateful to Hirschmann for raising these worries; she is right that something has gone wrong with how care talk circulates. But the diagnosis needs to start from a more honest reading of what care feminism actually said, and a more representative sample of who full-time mothers actually are.

**The Class Blindness of Both Sides**

Nancy Hirschmann is worried about her students. They are women at an elite university, mostly white, mostly affluent, who tell her they intend to marry well, stay home, and raise one or two children whom they will polish for the next round of elite reproduction. She finds that their conception of motherhood is morally smug, indifferent to other women, and that they invoke care feminism—her own tradition—to justify it. She is right to be worried. But her essay's response to this problem inherits the very class blindness that produces the problem in the first place.

I am the daughter of a woman who cleaned houses for a living. My mother did not "stay home"; she went to other people's homes to clean up after the children of women who stayed home. When she came back at night, she cooked, did laundry, helped me with my homework, and slept. By Hirschmann's framework, she was a working mother and so on the right side of the feminist ledger. By any honest accounting, she was also doing two unpaid shifts and one paid shift of care work, much of which subsidized the comfortable child-rearing of women like Hirschmann's students.

The reason Hirschmann's essay frustrates me is that it stages the debate inside a household of two adults—one a male earner, the other a wife choosing whether to "opt out"—and then asks how to redistribute labor within that household. But this household is not the typical American household. It is the elite, two-parent, single-earner-with-supporting-spouse household that Edward McCaffery's tax analysis foregrounds. The vast machinery of actual care work in America runs through nannies, housecleaners, home health aides, daycare workers, and the disproportionately immigrant and minority women who do this labor for low wages and few benefits. None of these women appear in Hirschmann's essay. They are not her students; they are not her students' mothers; they are not the women whose alleged "choice" to stay home is reinforcing patriarchy. They are the ones whose underpaid labor allows her students to imagine they can have both elite careers and intensive mothering—just by hiring it out, as Joan Williams, Bridget Anderson, and Pierrette Hondagneu-Sotelo have documented.

This omission is not incidental. Hirschmann's diagnosis—women care too much, and they must care less so men will care more—presupposes that the alternative to caring is paid professional labor. For a woman with options, this framing makes sense. For a woman without them, "care less" is not advice; it is unintelligible. Care less by whose lights, and toward whom? My mother could not care less about me; she could only care while exhausted.

When Hirschmann does discuss working-class women, it is mostly to note her students' contemptuous treatment of welfare mothers. She is right that this contempt is appalling. But the analysis then moves on without integrating welfare mothers into the substantive theory. The argument concludes with a Hirshmanite prescription about elite women refusing to leave the workforce. This prescription has nothing to say to a single mother whose paid work is grueling, low-wage, and offers nothing remotely like the leverage Hirschmann imagines paid work confers. It has nothing to say to a married couple in which both adults already work full-time at low wages and still cannot afford childcare. It has nothing to say about the structural fact that for many American families the "second shift" is performed not by a stay-at-home spouse but by grandparents, neighbors, older siblings, or simply nobody, with children left alone.

I want to push further. The "opt-out revolution" that Belkin diagnosed and that Hirschmann here critiques is, even by the most generous read of the data, a phenomenon affecting an extremely small slice of American women. Most women who leave the labor force do not do so to manage a Park Slope brownstone; they do so because they have been laid off, because childcare exceeds their wage, because a family member became ill, because the local labor market collapsed, or because their employer refused to accommodate a pregnancy. To treat the elite "opting out" narrative as the defining problem of women's labor in 2010 is to mistake an op-ed phenomenon for a labor-market reality.

What would a class-aware response look like? It would foreground the conditions of paid care workers, who are themselves mothers, and whose labor is the load-bearing wall under American gender arrangements. It would demand a universal, well-funded childcare system on the European model, rather than fixating on whether elite women should stay home. It would treat health care, sick leave, and a living wage as the actual preconditions of any meaningful "choice" about family form. It would ask whether the supposed superiority of paid work in conferring "leverage" survives contact with a Walmart shift. It would not ask, plaintively, whether feminism can convince Anthony's mother to care about civic virtue; it would ask whether the social wage Anthony's mother and millions of others rely on—Social Security, Medicare, public schools, public universities—can be defended against a generation of policy attacks.

I do not think Hirschmann would object to any of this in principle. But the essay's frame keeps drifting back to the elite household and its dilemmas, and then proposing prescriptions for women in general. This is the recurring pattern of a certain strand of academic feminism: a diagnosis derived from an unrepresentative sample, generalized into a universal counsel.

The most honest sentence in Hirschmann's essay is the admission that her students, encountering descriptions of welfare mothers, "draw on the idea of care to shore up class divisions and selfish interest." Yes. But care theory's complicity in this is less the issue than the broader fact that academic feminism has spent decades worrying about elite women's career trajectories while the women who clean up after them have remained largely invisible in the theory. Until that changes, "women must care less" will keep meaning, in practice, that elite women must hire other women to care more.

**In Defense of Choice—Even Choices I Wouldn't Make**

Nancy Hirschmann's essay frustrated me, and I want to be honest about why. I am a feminist. I work full-time. I would not be a stay-at-home mother for any sum of money. I find a great deal of the contemporary "intensive mothering" culture preposterous and stifling. And yet I do not think Hirschmann's argument, which essentially amounts to "individual women's reproductive and domestic choices are politically suspect and ought to be different," can be reconciled with the feminism I believe in.

The argument has a clear structure. (1) Women's "choice" to stay home is not really free, because gender norms and tax incentives push them. (2) Whatever its causes, the aggregate effect of such choices is to reinforce patriarchy. (3) Therefore individual women have an obligation—or at least are objects of feminist concern, in a way men are not—to refuse this choice and stay in the paid workforce.

Each step has a problem. The first step is true but proves too much. Of course women's choices are constructed under conditions of inequality. So are men's. So are every choice anyone makes in every domain. The choice to be a doctor rather than a nurse is shaped by gender; so is the choice to be a nurse rather than a doctor; so is the choice to be neither. If "constructed under conditions of inequality" is sufficient to make a choice politically suspect, then no choice is ever clean and the category does no work. The interesting question is not whether choices are constructed but whether they are responsive to reasons, whether the agent endorses them on reflection, whether the agent could have chosen otherwise without unreasonable costs. Many stay-at-home mothers can pass these tests. Some cannot, but neither can many career women.

The second step—that the aggregate effect of women's home labor is to reinforce patriarchy—I accept as partly true. Yes, the unequal sexual division of labor is a structural problem, and yes, individual decisions sum into structures. But this kind of "aggregation" critique is dangerous in feminist hands because it makes the female body the site of correction. Hirschmann cites Ann Cudd's argument that women have a duty to refuse "feminine" roles to dismantle patriarchy. The trouble with this view is that it places the political obligation on the people who already bear the costs of the unequal structure. We do not ordinarily think the wage-laborer has a duty to refuse low-wage work to overthrow capitalism; we think capital ought to be regulated. Why does the woman bear the duty to refuse motherhood-as-role in order to overthrow patriarchy? Why does the burden of structural change land on her shoulders, her body, her relationships?

The third step is where the argument runs into open contradiction with its stated values. Hirschmann wants gender equality, autonomy, and a richer conception of women's flourishing. She also wants women to make different choices than they currently make. When women's actual choices fail to track her prescriptions, she does not conclude that her prescriptions might be off; she concludes that the women are insufficiently feminist. This is, I think, what choice feminism's defenders have always been pointing to as a problem with prescriptive feminism: it tells women that real freedom would consist of choosing what feminists want them to choose. That is not freedom. It is a different master.

Let me try a different framing. Suppose we take seriously the possibility that some women genuinely prefer intensive mothering, find it rewarding, and would rank a few years of full-time childcare above any career achievement available to them. Suppose, also, that they understand the economic risks—reduced lifetime earnings, vulnerability in divorce, Social Security implications—and accept them in exchange for time with their children. Is there any sense in which such a woman is acting wrongly? I cannot find one that survives examination. She is doing something many men also wish they could do but have been culturally forbidden from doing. She is doing something the historical record suggests was important to many human lives across cultures. She is doing something that her own family genuinely benefits from. To call her a collaborator in patriarchy seems to confuse the structural fact that women predominate in this role with the moral status of any particular woman occupying it.

What feminism owes such women is not censure but protection: real legal protections in marriage and divorce; Social Security credits independent of spousal earnings; health insurance not tied to employment; and recognition that her years of unpaid work created real value the law should reflect. These are the policy goals Hirschmann herself endorses, and then she turns around and treats the women they would protect as part of the problem.

I share Hirschmann's frustration with her students. The smug certitude that other women on welfare should work while they themselves will not is contemptible, and a serious feminist pedagogy should challenge it. But the response is to teach them to recognize their own class privilege and their disregard for the women who will likely be cleaning their homes—not to teach them that motherhood is a politically lower vocation than investment banking.

The deepest mistake in Hirschmann's essay is the implicit hierarchy: paid work is the site of agency, leverage, and citizenship; care work is the site of disempowerment and false consciousness. This hierarchy is exactly the patriarchal hierarchy feminism set out to dismantle. The early care feminists understood this clearly. Their argument was not that women should keep doing care work; it was that care work is real work, full of meaning and skill, that should be honored and that men should share. Hirschmann's argument inverts this, agreeing with patriarchy that care work is low-status work, and recommending that women should escape it.

I would rather we kept the original insight. The labor of raising children is one of the most consequential things a person can do. It deserves time, support, and structural protection. Some women will choose to do it primarily; some men will, increasingly; many couples will share it. The job of feminism is to make any of these arrangements possible, dignified, and reversible—not to lecture women about which to choose.

**Hirschmann Doesn't Go Far Enough**

I want to register a friendly amendment to Nancy Hirschmann's essay: it doesn't go far enough. She has finally said out loud what many of us who came up through second-wave feminism have been muttering for years—that the celebration of care, the romance of mothering, and the chic of "opting out" have served as ideological cover for women's wholesale retreat from public life. She is right. But she keeps softening her conclusion with hedges. Linda Hirshman, whom Hirschmann cites approvingly, was clearer: if you're a woman with talent, education, and resources, and you spend your life as the unpaid domestic appendage of a husband, you are betraying both yourself and the women who will come after you.

Let me put the hard version of the argument plainly. Educated women in the United States in 2010 have a society-wide responsibility. That society made enormous investments in them: public schools, scholarships, university subsidies, anti-discrimination law, the painfully won infrastructure of Title IX. To take all of that and pour it into a single household, polishing two children for the Ivy League, is not a private choice. It is a withdrawal of public resources from the public, justified by an ideology of motherhood that conveniently aligns with husband's preferences and patriarchal expectations. The choice may feel personal. Its consequences are not.

Hirschmann is too gentle on her students. When women in an elite feminist theory course tell their professor that they will be stay-at-home mothers because mothering is "the most important job anyone could do," they are not engaging in feminist analysis. They are reciting an ideology that women in every generation have been told by men who benefited from it. The fact that they have learned to call it "care" rather than "duty" or "calling" is a marketing change, not a substantive one. The job description hasn't moved. The husband still gets the career, the income, the pension, the social standing, the friendships from work, and the option to leave. The wife still gets the children, the laundry, the dependency, and the option to be left.

This is why I find Hirschmann's gestures toward sympathy with care theory unsatisfying. She wants to keep the moral architecture of care—the idea that connection, attention to particulars, responsiveness to need are valuable orientations—while ditching the prescriptive consequence that women are the ones who provide them. But the moral architecture and the gendered division of labor are not so easily pried apart. Care theory was, from its inception, deeply ambivalent on this point. Gilligan said women speak in a different voice. Ruddick said maternal practice generates a particular form of thinking. Noddings centered the mother-child relation as paradigm. These framings were intellectually fertile and politically catastrophic. They told a generation of women that what they were already doing was philosophically important. They did not tell men to do it. The structure of the discourse made men outsiders to care; it made men's continued absence from caregiving compatible with their respect for care.

That is why the practical results have been so dismal. Hirschmann cites the numbers: women still doing two to three times the housework. Family-friendly policies used by men to bank early career advantage. Maternity leaves used as opportunities to write off the careers of women who take them. Tax codes that punish the second earner. These are not failures of implementation. They are the structural endpoints of an ideology that says care is socially valuable but assigns it to women in practice.

The way out is not more public recognition of care. It is the opposite. Public policy should aggressively encourage the equal sharing of paid and unpaid work between adults in every household. Tax the second earner equally, not more, by treating each adult as a separate filer. Mandate paternity leave that is use-it-or-lose-it, on the Scandinavian model—non-transferable, well-paid, of substantial duration. Remove the spousal benefits in Social Security that subsidize the one-earner household. Make divorce settlements presumptively equal in the absence of agreement. Treat childcare as essential public infrastructure, like roads. And, yes, end the cultural mythology that the highest calling of an educated woman is the cultivation of her own offspring.

I expect to hear that this is harsh, that it disrespects women's choices, that it sounds like Linda Hirshman, who was vilified for saying women shouldn't have children if they planned to mother them full-time. So be it. Vilification is what happens to feminists who refuse to make peace with the new gloss on the old arrangement. Each generation has had its accommodationists: those who said women should stay home but read more books while doing it; those who said women should work but only part-time; those who said women should "balance" everything; those who say now that women should "lean in" sometimes and "opt out" other times. Each accommodation has kept the basic structure in place.

Where I would push past Hirschmann is on the question of children themselves. She still treats childbearing as a settled good and worries only about how the labor is divided. I would ask the harder question: is the social expectation that women will have children, and that two-child families are the natural form of life, itself part of the structure of oppression? Many educated women do not want children. Many more would not want them if they were not constantly told they should. The "should I work?" debate has crowded out the "should I bear?" debate. Maybe educated women's caring "less"—as Hirschmann puts it—should include caring less about whether to have children at all.

Hirschmann ends with the suggestion that care should become more "tough love" and less empathic giving. I agree. But the tough love should start with feminists themselves, and with the temptation to keep dressing up the old division of labor in the new vocabulary of choice. Either feminism means a serious challenge to the gendered organization of social life, or it doesn't. Hirschmann is right that we have spent too long pretending it can mean both.

I am a man, a father of two young children, and I have read Nancy Hirschmann's essay with some discomfort. My wife and I both work full-time. Let me start with the obstacles, which Hirschmann mentions only glancingly. Arlie Hochschild's work, which she cites, established that men using family-friendly workplace policies are penalized in ways comparable to mothers, and often more severely. The man who takes paternity leave is regarded as not serious about his career. The man who leaves a meeting because his child is sick is "not committed." The man who works part-time to care for his family is, in the eyes of many employers and colleagues, simply not a man. These are real penalties, and they keep many fathers who would like to do more from doing more. Hirschmann notes the phenomenon and moves on. It is a plan to immiserate two adults simultaneously, on the theory that this immiseration will somehow shame the labor market into changing. I see no evidence the labor market is reachable by this route.

The cultural side is harder. The script for fatherhood in the United States has loosened a great deal in my lifetime—my own father did almost no caregiving and felt no shame about that, whereas I cannot imagine modeling the same for my children. But the script is still narrow. Male caregivers report being treated with mild suspicion in playgrounds and pediatricians' offices. The "Mr. Mom" tradition of comic incompetence persists in advertising. Many of the men I know who are heavily involved fathers describe a curious dual judgment: women admire them; men, especially older men, regard them with quiet condescension. None of this is a tragedy. It is, however, the texture of cultural change, and the texture is rougher than Hirschmann's account suggests.

What I want to say to feminist readers, including Hirschmann, is that the men your project depends on are mostly invisible in your prose. The men in Hirschmann's essay are the husbands of Susan and Anthony (absent from the anecdotes entirely), the unspecified husbands of the elite stay-at-home mothers, the abstract figures whose share of housework is 7-10 hours per week, the colleagues banking early tenure. This omission has political consequences. The strategy Hirschmann ultimately recommends—women becoming more individualist, gaining economic clout, leveraging that clout to force men to do more care work—treats men as obstacles to be coerced by economic pressure. It does not treat men as potential partners with their own interest in this change. But many men, in my generation and the one behind me, do have such an interest. We want to know our children. We want lives in which work is not the entirety of what we are. We want marriages of equals because the alternative is suffocating. The feminist project's success depends, in part, on enlisting us actively. And we are more likely to enlist when feminism speaks to us as people with stakes in the outcome, not only as recalcitrants who must be moved by wifely leverage.

The other reason I want a richer account of fatherhood here is that Hirschmann's main empirical claim—that the gender of the primary caretaker has "profound implications" for children's understanding of gender—cuts both ways. Yes, if children only ever see mothers caring and fathers earning, they will internalize that pattern. But the corollary is that when children see fathers caring, fathers cooking, fathers staying up with sick toddlers, this changes their internal map. My own children's expectations are different from mine. They know fathers do this work because they have watched their father do it. This is fair as a critique of overreliance on such policies. But I would not abandon them. In my own life, my employer's policies allowing flexible scheduling and parental leave have made my participation in caregiving possible. Without them, I would be a more absent father. Multiply that across millions of households and the policies matter. They are necessary but not sufficient, like most policies. The fact that they have not solved the problem alone is not an argument against them; it is an argument for combining them with the cultural changes Hirschmann calls for.

I close with a small request. The next generation of feminist writing about care and gender ought to take fatherhood seriously as a site of theory and practice—not just as the absent term in the equation. Men are not natural antagonists of feminism. Many of us are trying. The trying is harder than the public discourse acknowledges. And it is more important than ever, because Hirschmann is right about one thing above all: nothing will change until men do.

**Care Feminism Was Always Political: A Reply**

Nancy Hirschmann writes as if care feminism were a body of thought that promised political fruits—engaged citizens, gender equality, a more just society—and has failed to deliver them. Reading her essay, you might think Gilligan, Ruddick, Noddings, Held, Kittay, and Tronto all wrote prescriptive blueprints for social outcomes, and that those blueprints have empirically flopped. This is not the care feminism I know. The care feminism I know was, from the start, primarily a critique of moral philosophy and political theory, not a program for fixing parenting in America. Hirschmann's essay seems to me to mis-locate the tradition's claims and then hold it accountable for failing to do things it never set out to do.

Let me try to restate what care feminism was about. The dominant Western tradition of moral and political philosophy—Kant, Mill, Rawls, the social contract tradition more broadly—took as its paradigm of moral agency the independent adult rational chooser, contracting with other independent adult rational choosers. This paradigm systematically excluded a vast domain of human life: the labor of bearing, feeding, raising, and tending humans who are not (yet, or any longer) independent or rational. Care theorists pointed out that this exclusion was not innocent. It marked a particular gendered division of intellectual labor: men theorized public life while women's domestic labor was invisible, both empirically and conceptually. The political and moral theory we inherited was a theory by and for those who were not doing the care work that sustained them.

The care feminist response was to insist that the labor of caregiving—and the moral orientation it cultivates—belongs in the center of political theory, not at its periphery. This is a claim about what counts as ethics, about the inadequacy of contractarian models for explaining human social life, about the gendered structure of philosophy itself. It is not, primarily, a claim about how to organize twenty-first-century American households.

Hirschmann's critique therefore aims at something off-center. She wants to know whether care talk has changed the division of labor in actual marriages, whether it has produced more civic-minded children, whether it has freed women from the second shift. These are reasonable empirical questions. But they are also not the standards by which to evaluate a body of moral and political theory. We do not evaluate Rawls by asking whether American income distribution has become more egalitarian since A Theory of Justice; we evaluate him by asking whether his arguments are sound and whether his framework helps us think clearly about justice. Care feminism deserves the same treatment.

That said, I want to engage Hirschmann's substantive worries on their own ground. She has four specific concerns. Let me take each.

First, she worries that if care is a public good entitled to public support, then the public has the right to oversee the care. This concern was anticipated in the welfare debates of the 1990s and is real. But care theorists like Tronto and Kittay have explicitly addressed it. The argument was never that the state should commodify and supervise the labor of mothering. It was that the structural conditions of caregiving—income, health, time, infrastructure—should be supported as public goods, with the labor itself remaining within the autonomy of those performing it. Public daycare is supported by the state but parented by families. The distinction is operational. Hirschmann collapses it.

Second, she worries that parents with adequate resources still raise terrible citizens, and that more resources won't fix this. Granted. But care theorists did not claim that resources fix everything. The claim is that resources are necessary for the basic dignity of the caregiving relation. A mother with no income, no childcare, no health insurance, and no time will struggle to care well regardless of her moral inclinations. Removing those structural deprivations does not guarantee good citizens; it removes a major obstacle to good caregiving. This is a modest claim, not the maximal one Hirschmann attributes.

Third, she worries that parents derive non-transferable goods—love, intimacy, family vacations—from their children, which complicates the "social good" framing. This is true and theoretically interesting, but I do not see how it undermines care feminism. Most goods in human life are mixed: they have public dimensions and private benefits simultaneously. Teachers benefit from teaching even as they produce public benefits; the public's interest in their teaching is not vitiated by their private satisfaction. Why should mothers be different?

Fourth, she worries that care feminism has reinforced the gendered division of labor by celebrating women's caring work. Here she has the strongest point, and care theorists have been arguing about it among themselves for thirty years. The debate between, roughly, the Gilligan-Noddings tendency to root care in maternal experience and the Held-Tronto tendency to politicize care and degender it is alive and unresolved. Hirschmann reads care feminism as monolithic on this question, but it isn't. The degendering project is ongoing, contested, and central.

What I find most odd about Hirschmann's essay is its retreat to a strikingly liberal framework at the end. The diagnosis is that care feminism reinforced women's role; the prescription is that women should become "stronger in individualist terms by gaining and retaining economic clout and social status." This is, more or less, the prescription of liberal feminism circa 1975: get women into the labor market, lean on the economic leverage that produces, and the rest will follow. Care feminism arose precisely as a critique of this prescription, pointing out that it left the labor of human reproduction unaccounted for, devalued, and inevitably dumped on whoever was at the bottom of the labor market—poor women, immigrant women, women of color. Hirschmann's essay does not really answer that critique. It just sets it aside.

Care feminism's central claim—that the work of sustaining human life is morally and politically important, and that any theory of justice that ignores it is incomplete—remains true. The fact that this claim has been misappropriated by Hirschmann's smug undergraduates is not an argument against the claim. It is an argument for teaching them better.

**The Tax Code, the Workplace, and the Limits of Exhortation**

Nancy Hirschmann's essay is most persuasive when it gets concrete. She is right about Edward McCaffery's analysis of how the U.S. tax code penalizes second earners, particularly when their incomes approach their spouses'. She is right that maternity leave alone has not redistributed care work. She is right that men face professional stigma for using family-friendly policies, with the result that the policies redistribute leave but not labor. Where I think the essay falters is in its turn, at the close, toward exhortation: women should care less, women should refuse, women should demand. Exhortation is a thin instrument against structural problems, and the structural problems Hirschmann names are amenable to policy in ways her conclusion underplays.

Let me lay out a different set of conclusions from the same diagnosis.

First, the tax code. The marriage penalty on dual earners is not a force of nature; it is a 1948 policy decision (the joint return) updated incoherently across decades. The simplest fix is mandatory individual filing, as is the norm in most other OECD countries. Each adult files on her own income; the household is no longer the tax unit. The "single breadwinner subsidy" disappears, the second-earner penalty disappears, and the implicit incentive for one spouse to stay home dissolves. Sweden made this transition in 1971 and saw a meaningful and durable increase in married women's labor force participation. The U.S. could do the same. Hirschmann mentions McCaffery but does not actually call for this reform; her closing pages turn to individual women's bargaining. The tax code is a far blunter and more effective instrument.

Second, parental leave. The current U.S. regime—unpaid FMLA leave for twelve weeks, available to a minority of workers—is among the worst in the industrialized world. But the more important question, granted that we are pushing for paid leave, is the design. Hirschmann notes that "parental leave" (gender-neutral) has been gamed by men in academia to bank extra time for tenure. The lesson is not that gender-neutral leave is a mistake; it is that leave should be designed to actually be taken for caregiving, with policy guardrails. The Swedish "daddy month," now several months, makes a portion of paid leave non-transferable to the other parent. If the father doesn't take it, the family loses it. The result has been a dramatic increase in fathers' uptake of leave. Quebec adopted a similar policy in 2006 and saw male leave-taking jump from less than 30 percent to over 75 percent in a few years. This is the kind of policy that actually changes behavior. Exhorting wives to refuse household labor does not.

Third, childcare. The U.S. is a low-quality, high-cost outlier among rich democracies. The cost of full-time childcare in major metros now exceeds in-state public university tuition. This single fact pushes second earners out of the labor market more powerfully than any tax code or norm. France, with its écoles maternelles, and the Scandinavian states, with their universal childcare entitlements, have demonstrated that this is solvable. Universal, high-quality, publicly funded childcare from age one onward would do more for gender equality than any reform Hirschmann names. It would also do more for the welfare of children, especially low-income children, than the current patchwork.

Fourth, the workplace. Family-friendly policies fail when they are stigmatized. The stigmatization arises in part because they are perceived as accommodations for a minority, granted at the discretion of management. Make them universal and mandatory, and the stigma dissipates. France's 35-hour work week, Germany's robust works councils with say over working hours, Sweden's general right to part-time work for parents of young children—these structures shift the baseline from which everyone operates. They do not eliminate the motherhood penalty Blair-Loy documents, but they significantly reduce it.

Fifth—and this is where Hirschmann is most clearly right—none of the above is sufficient without a cultural change in men's expectations of their own lives. But cultural change is itself partly produced by policy. When fathers take leave, other fathers expect to take leave. When men leave the office at 5:30 because of paternity policies that require it, this becomes normal. What is it actually for? Children are born helpless. They remain dependent for many years. The developmental literature on this is vast and increasingly converging. The work of Allan Schore, Daniel Stern, and others has documented the neurobiological consequences of caregiver attunement in the first years of life. The work of John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth on attachment has been refined and extended by subsequent generations of researchers. The basic finding is that children need stable, responsive, primary attachment relationships, and that the quality of those relationships in early childhood has consequences across the lifespan.

None of this requires that the primary caregiver be the biological mother. None of this requires that the primary caregiver be a woman. None of this requires that one adult perform all of the caregiving. But all of this does require that someone, or some small set of someones, be reliably and attentively present to the child—and that this presence requires real time, in real hours, every day, for years.

Hirschmann's essay, focused as it is on the politics of adult labor, never quite confronts the time problem. The actual hours a young child needs are not infinitely flexible. This is the hard knot that the politics of work-and-family keeps trying to untie and cannot.

**The Problem with Pathologizing Choice**

There is a move in Nancy Hirschmann's essay that deserves explicit attention because I think it characterizes a recurring tendency in contemporary feminist thought. The move is this: when women's stated preferences and choices fail to align with the political prescriptions of a particular feminist argument, the choices are reinterpreted as products of false consciousness, structural coercion, or ideological capture, such that they can be set aside without genuine engagement. Hirschmann's students say they want to be stay-at-home mothers. Hirschmann concludes that their wants are constructed under conditions of inequality and therefore do not constitute reasons. The essay then proceeds to recommend a course of action contrary to their wants. The move is sophisticated, but it is also philosophically dangerous, and I want to push back on it.

Begin with the obvious. Of course preferences are shaped by social conditions. This is true of all preferences, everywhere, always. The question is what follows from this fact. If "shaped by social conditions" is sufficient to delegitimize a preference, then no preference is ever legitimate and the language of choice and autonomy is meaningless. We need a more discriminating standard. The relevant standard, on most accounts of autonomy in the contemporary philosophical literature, involves something like: the agent reflectively endorses the preference; the agent has access to alternatives; the agent has information relevant to the choice; the agent is not under duress. Many stay-at-home mothers can meet these criteria. Some cannot, and we should worry about them. But the wholesale move from "preferences are socially constructed" to "preferences are inauthentic" is a category error.

Hirschmann's essay flirts with this error repeatedly. The students' wish to be mothers is not engaged as a substantive position—as something that might have reasons behind it, including reasons Hirschmann should take seriously—but as a symptom of a deeper structural pathology. The argument never has to grapple with the possibility that intensive mothering might, for some women, be a considered and defensible life plan. This presupposition is interesting because it inverts the original feminist insistence on listening to women's experience. Carol Gilligan's project, which Hirschmann acknowledges as foundational to care feminism, was rooted in the methodological commitment to take seriously what women actually said about their moral lives, rather than assuming their voices were defective versions of male reasoning. Catharine MacKinnon's work, particularly on pornography, took the strong position: women's stated preferences for traditional roles, sexualized self-presentation, and so on are products of patriarchal conditioning so thorough that the preferences carry no normative weight. Martha Nussbaum's capability approach took a more moderate position: we should worry about "adaptive preferences" when women have internalized restrictions that limit their flourishing, but we should also be cautious about overriding stated preferences in the name of women's "real" interests. Drucilla Cornell and others have argued that any feminism that systematically discounts women's voices is internally contradictory.

I find myself in the Nussbaum camp on this. Adaptive preferences are real and politically serious. The Indian widow who reports satisfaction with a diet that has destroyed her health, in Nussbaum's famous example, is not exercising autonomy in a way we should respect at face value. We need a real account of when preferences should be questioned and when they should be respected. Hirschmann does not give us one. Her essay treats the preferences of her students as pre-emptively suspect, in a way that I think most of us would find troubling if applied to other domains.

Consider an analogy. Suppose a labor theorist concludes that workers in a particular industry should unionize for their own good, but those workers consistently refuse. The labor theorist could conclude that the workers' refusal reflects false consciousness, manipulated by employer propaganda, and that their preferences should therefore be discounted in the political analysis. This conclusion might be right. It might also be that the workers have considered reasons—about the particular history of their union, the particular conditions of their workplace, their particular life plans—that the theorist has not adequately engaged. The intellectually responsible move is to engage the workers' reasons, not to declare them unavailable to themselves.

Hirschmann's students may be wrong about their futures. They may be naïve about marriage. They may underestimate the costs they will pay. They may, in middle age, deeply regret choices made at twenty. All of this is possible. It would not justify treating their current articulated preferences as politically void. They are adults with reasons, and the feminist project's relationship to them should be one of argument, not preemptive dismissal.

The results have generally been bad—bad politically, in that the vanguards alienated their constituencies; bad ethically, in that they treated other adults as objects of correction rather than partners in shared deliberation. Feminism has had its own vanguardist moments and they have been among its least successful. The instinct to look at the next generation of women and conclude that they are insufficiently feminist, that their choices reveal their lack of understanding, that the movement must therefore intensify its prescriptions—this instinct should be examined carefully.

A more productive stance, I think, would be the following. Make the case for sharing care work openly and persuasively, in argument rather than in admonition. Identify the policy reforms that reduce the costs of equal sharing—tax reform, parental leave, childcare. Trust women, including young women, to reason about their own lives once the conditions of reasoning are improved. And resist the temptation to conclude that, when women decide differently from how feminist theorists would prefer, the women must be wrong rather than the theorists. Sometimes the women are wrong. Sometimes the theorists are. The work of distinguishing the two cases is the actual work of feminist thought, and it cannot be done by formula.

Fourth, she argues that women need to maintain economic power to achieve equality, and that this requires caring less about care.

Let me take these points in turn. On the first, Hirschmann is correct. The empirical record shows that care feminism has not produced the redistribution of care work that its proponents hoped for. This is a genuine failure, though it is not unique to care feminism; other feminisms have also failed to produce the changes they sought. The question is whether this failure is a reason to abandon care feminism or to modify it.

On the second point, I am less sure. The claim that care feminism has become ideological cover for the status quo conflates the theory with its reception. Care feminism was developed to illuminate the social importance of care work, not to justify women's retreat from public life. That some women have used it for this purpose does not mean the theory is wrong; it means the theory has been misappropriated, which is a different problem.

But Hirschmann has a response to this: if care feminism can be put to uses opposed to those for which it was intended, maybe something is wrong with the theory itself. This is a serious challenge, and I do not think it can be dismissed. A theory that predicts one outcome and produces another is either wrong or incomplete. Care feminism has predicted transformation and produced persistence; this suggests the theory needs revision.

The third point—about care as a public good—seems to me the most original and the most problematic. Hirschmann argues that if care has social value, then the public has a right to demand that care be performed adequately. This logic is impeccable, but it leads to conclusions that care feminists cannot accept: state oversight of parenting, intrusive surveillance of families, punitive treatment of mothers who fail to raise productive citizens.

I think Hirschmann is right that this is a logical consequence of the public-goods argument, but I do not think it is the only possible consequence. One could argue that care is a public good in the sense that society benefits from it, without accepting that the public therefore has the right to supervise how it is performed. This is the argument for public support without public oversight—something like the way we support the arts without telling artists what to create.

On the fourth point, Hirschmann's recommendation that women care less strikes me as both true and trivial. True, because women cannot fight for equality while simultaneously doing all the care work themselves; something has to give. Trivial, because it does not tell us what women should care less about, or how they should go about caring less without damaging their relationships with their children and families.

The deeper problem with the essay is that it assumes individual choices are the site of feminist struggle. Hirschmann writes about women choosing to stay home or go to work, about women caring more or less, about women maintaining or relinquishing economic power. But collective action—through unions, political parties, advocacy organizations—is barely mentioned. This is a curiously apolitical feminism, one that locates the problem in individual behavior rather than in the structures of power that shape that behavior.

I do not want to conclude that Hirschmann is wrong. Her critique of care feminism's outcomes is largely correct, and her insistence that women need power to achieve equality is important. But the solution she proposes—individual women caring less and demanding more—is insufficient. What is needed is collective power: women organized to demand the structural changes that would make equality possible. Without that collective dimension, individual women will continue to face impossible choices, and care feminism will continue to provide ideological cover for the status quo it intended to challenge.
