Let's not kid ourselves. Whatever Andi Dorfman's day job, "The Bachelorette" is the same confection it has been for nine prior seasons: rose ceremonies, helicopter dates, hot tubs in Provence, and a final episode engineered to produce a marriage proposal on national television. Swapping a former NBA dancer for an assistant district attorney does not transform the show's structural message, which is that the most consequential thing a woman can do — more consequential than prosecuting felonies in Fulton County — is select a husband from a stable of bachelors curated by a production company.

Consider what the format actually requires of Andi. She must put her career "on hold," as the promotional copy proudly announces, in order to participate. She must perform vulnerability on cue, weep photogenically, and pick a winner by a deadline set by ABC's sweeps calendar. The men compete; she is the prize disguised as the chooser. That inversion of the original "Bachelor" premise has always been the franchise's fig leaf — "see, the woman has the power!" — but the power is ceremonial. She cannot rewrite the rules, cannot decline to choose, cannot opt for a long engagement without producing a ratings crisis.

The fairy-tale machinery is, if anything, more retrograde than usual this season because of the contrast Andi herself sets up. A prosecutor is supposed to embody skepticism, cross-examination, suspicion of staged narratives. Yet here she is being courted in castles. The producers will surely lean on her legal background for kitschy effect — expect "objection!" jokes and a "case for love" voiceover — which only underscores how the show metabolizes a woman's professional identity into flavor text for the romance.

Calling Andi a "new kind of bachelorette" because she has a law degree mistakes biography for structure. Trish Regan was a CNBC anchor; Andrew Firestone was an heir; Emily Maynard was a single mother. Each season, the franchise advertises the lead's distinguishing trait, and each season the trait is sanded down by the format. Inspiring professional women would mean showing one whose career is not a sacrifice to be paused but the center of her life. ABC isn't in that business. It's in the engagement-ring business, and Andi is this year's spokesmodel.

The interesting question isn't whether Andi Dorfman represents progress or regression. It's why we keep asking television shows to be referenda on the status of women in the first place. "The Bachelorette" is a competition format borrowed from game shows and dressed up in tulle. Demanding that it inspire a generation of professional women is like demanding that "Deal or No Deal" inspire a generation of statisticians.

Still, since the question is on the table, here's a more useful frame than progress-versus-regression: what does the show actually do to the people inside it, and what does it model for the people watching?

Inside the frame, the structural facts are unflattering. Contestants sign contracts that give producers enormous editorial control. Long shoots with limited sleep and copious alcohol are standard. The lead chooses among men she has known for, at most, a few weeks of compressed, manipulated time. The engagement rate from the franchise is high; the marriage rate is famously low. None of these facts change because the lead is a lawyer.

Outside the frame, what viewers absorb is a script about romance: that love is a tournament, that the worthy partner reveals himself through dramatic gestures, that ambivalence is failure and certainty is virtue. These scripts are not neutral. They shape expectations that real relationships, with their messy timelines and unphotogenic compromises, cannot meet. A "new kind of bachelorette" with a J.D. doesn't rewrite that script; she just gives it a more flattering résumé.

The genuinely new thing in dating-show culture, if there is one, is not Andi but the audience. Reality TV viewers in 2014 are sophisticated. They read recaps, follow contestants on Twitter, parse the editing, know which producer is feeding which storyline. They consume "The Bachelorette" the way earlier generations consumed pro wrestling — as a kayfabe whose pleasures depend on knowing it's constructed. In that context, asking whether Andi is empowering or paternalistic may be the wrong genre of question altogether. She's a character in a long-running franchise. The franchise will outlast her, and the cultural meaning of her season will be made mostly by the people watching, not by her or by ABC.

Allison Yarrow's question deserves a direct answer: no, Andi Dorfman is not a new kind of bachelorette, but she may be a useful one.

She is not new because the show has featured accomplished women before. DeAnna Pappas was a real-estate agent, Jillian Harris an interior designer, Desiree Hartsock a bridal stylist, Emily Maynard a single mother managing a child's medical needs. Each was sold to viewers with a tidy professional descriptor that was promptly subordinated to her role as romantic protagonist. Andi's prosecutor credential will follow the same arc. The show's grammar — limousine arrivals, group dates, the Fantasy Suite, the proposal — does not bend to accommodate the lead's biography; the biography bends to the grammar.

But she may be useful, and here is where I part company with the more dismissive critics. Visibility matters even when the vehicle is imperfect. A young woman who watches a prime-time show and sees a prosecutor at the center of it — interrogating men's intentions, articulating what she wants, walking away from suitors who don't measure up — absorbs something, even amid the fairy-tale frosting. The image of a woman with subpoena power choosing among men who must impress her is not nothing. It is not a feminist manifesto, but mass culture rarely traffics in manifestos. It traffics in images, and images accumulate.

The honest critique of "The Bachelorette" is not that it features paternalistic stereotypes — the lead is, formally, the one with the power — but that it presents marriage as the singular life goal worth pausing everything for. That message is genuinely retrograde, and Andi's career-on-hold framing reinforces it. A truly new bachelorette would be one whose season acknowledged that finding a partner is one project among several, not the project that supersedes all others. ABC will not produce that show, because the existing one works.

So we get Andi: a woman whose actual life is more interesting than the show will let her be, performing in a format whose constraints she presumably understood when she signed on. Whether her season inspires professional women or merely flatters them with a more polished surrogate, I suspect, will depend less on what ABC airs than on how viewers choose to read it.
