Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Canonization is not a humanitarian award; it is the Catholic Church's declaration that a particular soul is in heaven and worthy of veneration. Once that is understood, much of the secular argument against Mother Teresa's sainthood loses its force. Christopher Hitchens's critique — that her hospices offered poor medical care, that she accepted donations from unsavory figures like Charles Keating, that she preached against contraception and abortion in places ravaged by poverty — is a critique of her ideology and her institutional choices, not of her personal sanctity. The Church has never claimed saints are competent hospital administrators or progressive social reformers.

What canonization requires is heroic virtue and, in most cases, verified miracles. By the ordinary standards the Church uses, Mother Teresa qualifies. She left a comfortable teaching post to live among Calcutta's destitute, built an order that operates in over a hundred countries, and, as her posthumously published letters revealed, persevered through decades of agonizing spiritual darkness without abandoning her vocation. That last detail, far from undermining her case, strengthens it: she continued to serve when she felt nothing in return.

One can reasonably wish she had pushed harder for better medicine or spoken less rigidly about reproductive ethics. But those are arguments with Catholicism itself. Within the Church's own framework — which is the only framework canonization answers to — Francis's decision is defensible and overdue.

The criticisms of her homes for the dying—reports of overcrowding, reused needles, and a scarcity of analgesics—are deeply troubling by modern medical standards. Mother Teresa famously viewed suffering not as a problem to be eliminated at all costs but as a participation in Christ’s passion, and her order’s charism was to soothe that suffering through love, not always cure it. The canonization process, with its requirement of two verified miracles, attests to her personal holiness, not a spotless organizational record. Sainthood does not negate the validity of debate about her methods; it points to a life wholly consumed by God and neighbor. On that measure, her legacy—of galvanizing thousands of women to serve the most abandoned—warrants the honor.

Pope Francis’s decision to approve sainthood for Mother Teresa aligns with her enduring global image as the ultimate champion of the destitute. For millions, she is already the saint of the gutters, a woman who abandoned comfort to embrace the poorest of the poor in Calcutta. However, elevating her to the Church’s highest altar requires us to look past deeply troubling aspects of her legacy, setting a dangerous precedent for how we view and execute charity.

The primary controversy surrounding Mother Teresa is not merely a matter of administrative oversight, but of fundamental philosophy. Medical professionals and researchers who visited the Missionaries of Charity facilities consistently documented severely substandard conditions. Patients were frequently denied basic hygiene, adequate pain management, and standard medical interventions. This was not entirely due to a lack of resources—her order accumulated millions of dollars—but rather a theological conviction that suffering brought the afflicted closer to Christ. Glorifying poverty and pain is fundamentally different from working to alleviate them.

Furthermore, her willingness to accept substantial donations from notorious dictators, such as Haiti’s Jean-Claude Duvalier, raises serious ethical questions about the compromises made in the name of her mission.

Canonization is not just an acknowledgment of good intentions; it is the presentation of a life as a model for moral emulation. By canonizing Mother Teresa, the Church risks endorsing a model of charity that romanticizes human suffering instead of actively curing it.

Teresa was neither the flawless humanitarian of hagiography nor the cynical operator critics describe. She was something more complicated — and perhaps more worthy of honest engagement than either extreme allows.

Canonization is an internal matter of the Catholic Church. When Rome elevates a person to sainthood, it is making a theological claim — that this individual lived a life of heroic virtue and now intercedes with God on behalf of the faithful. Whether one agrees with that claim depends largely on whether one accepts the Catholic worldview in the first place. By the Church's own criteria, Mother Teresa is an obvious choice. She took vows of poverty, founded a religious order that now operates in over 130 countries, served lepers and the dying in Calcutta when few others would, and inspired hundreds of thousands of vocations and donations to relieve human misery. Two miracles have been attributed to her intercession, satisfying the procedural requirement.

The critiques marshaled against her — most famously by Christopher Hitchens in "The Missionary Position," and more recently by Canadian researchers who examined her homes' medical practices — are serious and worth taking seriously. They point to inadequate pain management in her facilities, an ideology that romanticized suffering, the acceptance of donations from unsavory figures like Charles Keating, and her staunch opposition to contraception and abortion in a country choked by poverty. But these are criticisms of her theology and her management, not of her sincerity or sanctity as the Church understands it. The Catholic Church does not canonize efficient NGO administrators; it canonizes people it believes are united with Christ.

Non-Catholics are free to find the whole apparatus of sainthood strange, and free to evaluate Teresa by secular humanitarian standards on which she is genuinely contested. But asking whether the Church should canonize her is a bit like asking whether the Nobel committee should award a particular physicist: the institution has its own rules, and by those rules she qualifies. Francis's decision is unsurprising and, within Catholic logic, entirely defensible.

The question of whether Mother Teresa "should" be canonized is interesting mainly because it reveals how thoroughly the debate has slipped its theological moorings. Canonization, in Catholic teaching, is not an award for humanitarian excellence. It is the Church's solemn declaration that a particular soul is in heaven and may be publicly venerated. The criteria are heroic virtue and verified miracles, not GiveWell-style cost-effectiveness scores.

Once you understand that, much of the contemporary controversy looks like a category error. Critics complain that her hospices were not modern medical facilities — true, but neither were the leper colonies of Father Damien, who is also a saint. They complain that she accepted dirty money — but so did countless medieval founders of religious houses whose donors built cathedrals with the proceeds of conquest. They complain about her theology of suffering — but that theology, drawn from a long Christian tradition stretching back to Paul, is precisely the worldview the Church is endorsing when it canonizes her.

Where the critics have a real point is on a narrower question: does this particular canonization advance the Church's witness in the modern world? Here reasonable Catholics can disagree. Some will say Teresa's witness to the dignity of the discarded — the dying on the streets of Calcutta whom no one else would touch — is exactly what a wealthy, comfortable, secular West needs to see exalted. Others will worry that her ascetic embrace of poverty as spiritually meaningful papers over structural injustices the Church should be naming.

My own view is that Francis has made a defensible call. Teresa was flawed, occasionally badly so, and her published doubts about God's existence make her a more interesting saint than the hagiography suggests. But sanctity has never required perfection, only fidelity. By that measure, she qualifies.
