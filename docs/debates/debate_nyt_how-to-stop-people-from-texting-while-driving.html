How to Stop People From Texting While Driving

Most states have banned texting while driving, but distracted driving only seems to be getting worse. In New York, legislators introduced a bill that would allow police officers arriving at the scene of a crash to test drivers’ phones for evidence of recent activity with a device called the “Textalyzer” — the digital equivalent of the Breathalyzer.

Is this the best way to deal with public health consequences of texting and driving? What else can be done to stop this pervasive practice?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The "Textalyzer" proposal is creative, but it asks a piece of forensic technology to solve what is fundamentally a behavioral problem. Police can already subpoena phone records after a serious crash, and the difficulty isn't usually proving that a driver was tapping the screen — it's catching the millions of drivers who never crash but text constantly anyway. A device deployed only at crash scenes does nothing about the daily epidemic of drivers glancing down at lit screens on the highway. It also raises real Fourth Amendment questions: even if the Textalyzer is engineered to read only metadata, drivers and courts will rightly be suspicious about what a roadside scan of a personal device actually captures.

If we want to change the behavior itself, the lesson from drunk driving is instructive. What moved the needle on DUI was not any single gadget but a combination of stiff, predictable penalties; high-visibility enforcement; sustained public-education campaigns; and a generational shift in social norms that made driving drunk shameful. Texting needs the same treatment. Right now, fines for a first offense in most states are low enough to be a nuisance, not a deterrent, and enforcement is sporadic because the violation is hard to observe from a patrol car. Raising penalties, adding license points, and funding targeted patrols — including unmarked vehicles and elevated observation posts, which some states have tried successfully — would do more than any post-crash forensic tool.

Technology should be aimed upstream, at prevention rather than punishment. Apple, Google, and the carriers already have the capability to detect when a phone is moving at vehicular speed and to silence notifications or auto-reply that the user is driving. AT&T's "DriveMode" and similar apps exist but are opt-in and barely marketed. Regulators could push for "drive mode" to be the default setting, the way seatbelt chimes became standard. Insurance carriers, through usage-based programs like Progressive's Snapshot, can also reward phone-down behavior directly, which several are beginning to do.

Finally, we should not underestimate culture. Surveys consistently show that most drivers consider texting behind the wheel dangerous — and then admit doing it themselves. That cognitive dissonance is where public-health campaigns, driver-education curricula, and employer policies (commercial fleets have made real progress here) can work. The Textalyzer may eventually have a narrow role in serious-injury investigations, but treating it as the centerpiece of policy would repeat the mistake of looking for a technological shortcut around the harder work of changing how people drive.

The Textalyzer is a shiny technological solution to a profoundly human problem. While the impulse to treat texting behind the wheel with the same forensic rigor as drunk driving is understandable, the device threatens civil liberties without promising to change behavior. Worse, it distracts us from the messy, multifaceted approach that evidence suggests actually works.

First, the legal and practical hurdles are enormous. Unlike a Breathalyzer, which measures a physical state, a Textalyzer would scan the contents of a personal electronic device. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches, and the Supreme Court’s 2014 ruling in Riley v. California made clear that police need a warrant to search a cellphone. Advocates claim the Textalyzer only reveals whether the phone was in use, not the content of messages. But the distinction is fragile: even metadata about app usage is deeply revealing, and the device would have to bypass lock screens and encryption, raising serious questions about compelled self-incrimination. Furthermore, the technology is unproven; no field tests have demonstrated that it can reliably distinguish between hands-free voice commands, passenger use, or a driver glancing at a map versus actively texting. Handing an untested black box to police officers on accident scenes invites endless legal challenges while doing little to alter the pre-crash calculus of a distracted driver.

More fundamentally, the Textalyzer focuses solely on punishment after the fact. By 2016, we have decades of public health research showing that fear of legal consequences alone is a weak deterrent for habitual behaviors reinforced by powerful social and psychological rewards. Texting while driving is a compulsive act for many, driven by the dopamine hit of notification and the illusion of multitasking. A device that adds a small, distant probability of getting caught does not override the immediate pull of the ping.

What else can be done? The most promising interventions combine making the behavior harder with making it socially unacceptable. Automakers and app developers are already rolling out technological nudges: Apple’s recently announced Do Not Disturb While Driving mode and similar Android features can detect motion and silence notifications automatically, removing the temptation. Insurance companies are offering significant discounts for drivers who install telematics devices that monitor phone use, turning safety into a financial incentive. These approaches work upstream, preventing the distraction in the first place.

But technology alone won’t suffice. We need a sustained cultural shift akin to the one that made drunk driving a stigma rather than a norm. Slogans like “It Can Wait” have raised awareness, but they must be reinforced by peer pressure and vivid storytelling. States should mandate that driver’s education courses include visceral, graphic simulations of distracted driving crashes, as some already do. Parents need to model undistracted driving from the moment children sit in a car seat. And law enforcement should intensify high-visibility patrols that ticket texting drivers, not via a suspect post-crash scan, but through old-fashioned spotting of erratic driving — a method that, combined with publicized crackdowns, has been shown to reduce handheld phone use significantly.

The Textalyzer represents a desire for a quick fix, a silver bullet that bypasses the hard work of cultural change. But privacy concerns and the mismatch between after-the-fact detection and real-time prevention make it a dangerous distraction itself. The path to fewer shattered lives lies not in a roadside data probe, but in engineering our devices and social norms so that silence behind the wheel becomes automatic.

California*, the Supreme Court unanimously ruled that police officers generally need a warrant before searching the digital contents of a cell phone. Smartphones are not merely communication devices; they are the modern repositories of our most private lives, holding everything from financial records to intimate communications. Proponents of the Textalyzer argue that the device would only pull metadata—registering taps and swipes without reading the content of messages. But allowing law enforcement to plug a forensic extraction tool into a personal device without a warrant fundamentally undermines digital privacy. It is a slippery slope that civil liberties advocates rightly oppose.

If retroactive police surveillance is not the answer, what is? The solution lies in a combination of preventative technology and a profound cultural shift.

First, the technology industry must take greater responsibility for the crisis its devices have exacerbated. Smartphone manufacturers and cellular providers should build robust, default "driving modes" into their operating systems. While third-party apps currently exist to block incoming texts and disable keyboards when GPS detects highway speeds, these require users to actively opt in. To be effective, these speed-locking features must be standard, requiring drivers to explicitly opt out or confirm they are merely passengers. If technology created this distraction, technology can be engineered to mitigate it.

Second, we need a sustained cultural campaign analogous to the decades-long fight against drunk driving. Organizations like Mothers Against Drunk Driving (MADD) didn't just advocate for stricter laws; they made driving under the influence socially unacceptable. Today, texting while driving is still viewed by many as a minor, ubiquitous indiscretion rather than a lethal choice. High-visibility enforcement of existing laws, coupled with relentless public awareness campaigns, must aim to stigmatize the behavior.

We cannot arrest our way out of the distracted driving epidemic, nor should we sacrifice constitutional privacy protections to try. By shifting the burden to preventative technology and fostering a fierce social stigma, we can save lives before the crash ever occurs.

There is something faintly absurd about the Textalyzer debate, which has consumed so much oxygen on op-ed pages: we are arguing about a forensic gadget when we have not even committed, as a society, to taking distracted driving seriously in the first place.

Consider what we have actually done. Forty-six states have banned texting while driving. By the late 1980s, a combination of cultural stigma (MADD's work was extraordinary), license suspensions, mandatory minimums, sobriety checkpoints, and ignition interlock requirements transformed the landscape. Drunk-driving fatalities fell by roughly half over two decades. None of that happened by inventing a single clever gadget. It happened because society decided, at every level, that the behavior was unacceptable.

We have not done this with texting. Many Americans still believe — wrongly, the research is overwhelming — that they can text safely while driving. Parents text while their kids are in the back seat. Cops text in their cruisers. Politicians text from the back of black SUVs and then vote on distracted-driving bills. The behavior has not been stigmatized; it has been normalized.

Before we equip every police officer in New York with a device to scan crashed drivers' phones, let's ask a basic question that the Textalyzer's boosters seem to be skipping: does it actually work, and how do we know?

This is not a rhetorical question. The Textalyzer is being marketed by Cellebrite, an Israeli forensics firm best known for its work cracking phones for law enforcement and intelligence agencies. The company has been deliberately vague about exactly what the device captures, how it captures it, what its error rate is, and whether independent researchers have been allowed to audit its claims. Reporters who have asked basic questions have been told that the technology is proprietary. Sound familiar?

We have been here before. Field drug tests have produced thousands of false positives, leading to wrongful arrests for cotton candy, sugar, and bird droppings mistaken for cocaine. Breathalyzers themselves, often invoked as the gold standard, have repeatedly been found to produce unreliable readings due to calibration errors, software bugs, and operator mistakes; entire convictions in Massachusetts and New Jersey have been thrown out as a result. Forensic disciplines we once considered settled — bite mark analysis, hair comparison, even some fingerprint methods — have been criticized by the National Academy of Sciences as scientifically unsupported.

Now we are being asked to trust a proprietary phone-scanning device, deployed by officers under roadside conditions, used as evidence to suspend licenses and possibly impose criminal liability. What is the false-positive rate when a passenger was using the driver's phone? When a notification arrived but was not interacted with? When the phone was mounted as a hands-free GPS, perfectly legally? When iOS or Android logs are ambiguous about which app generated activity? These are not edge cases; they are the meat of any real-world enforcement program.

The proper sequence is: independent validation first, deployment second. The National Institute of Standards and Technology should be asked to evaluate any such device. Defense attorneys and academic computer scientists should have access to test it. Error rates should be published. Until then, "Textalyzer" is a marketing term, not a forensic tool.

There may well be a place for technologically assisted enforcement of distracted-driving laws. But the lesson of the past thirty years of forensic science is that we should be skeptical of any black box that claims to read the truth off our devices. Privacy concerns aside, the most basic question — is this thing accurate? — has not been answered.

I want to defend an unfashionable position: the texting-while-driving panic, while not unfounded, is being addressed in ways that will mostly fail because we keep refusing to acknowledge what it actually is. It is not, primarily, a law enforcement problem. It is an addiction problem.

The compulsion to check one's phone is engineered. The notification systems built into modern smartphones exploit the same intermittent-reinforcement mechanisms that make slot machines so effective: you don't know when the next interesting message will come, so you check constantly. Tristan Harris, formerly of Google, has written about this with some candor; the entire attention economy is structured around hijacking users' impulse control. It would be naive to expect a $150 fine to overcome that pull while someone is sitting bored at a red light, much less to expect a hypothetical future Textalyzer to do so.

This reframing matters because it changes which interventions make sense. Public health approaches to addiction have generally found that punishment is among the least effective tools. What works better: changing the environment so the addictive behavior is harder, changing the substance itself so it is less compulsive, providing substitutes, and using social norms to shift behavior over time.

Translated to our problem: the most effective interventions will come from the platforms. Driving-mode features that suppress notifications by default, not as opt-in. Apple and Google could agree tomorrow to ship this and save more lives than every state legislature combined. They have so far declined, partly because notification engagement is the lifeblood of their ecosystem. That is a corporate-responsibility question Congress should be asking pointedly.

Second-most effective: car-side interventions. Auto manufacturers integrating phone holsters, large hands-free displays that handle messaging through voice, and physical phone lockboxes on premium models. These exist piecemeal; they should be standard.

Third: norm change of the MADD variety. We need stories, faces, victims, public-service campaigns that genuinely stigmatize the behavior the way drunk driving was stigmatized. This works, but it takes decades.

The Textalyzer fits nowhere on this list. It is enforcement theater — a device that lets legislators say they are doing something while leaving the actual driver of the behavior, the compulsive engagement loop of modern phones, completely untouched. It will produce some convictions, some lawsuits, some Fourth Amendment cases, and very little behavioral change.

If we are serious about saving lives, we need to stop treating this as a problem of bad drivers and start treating it as a problem of a powerful technology that, in its current configuration, is killing thousands of people a year. Address the technology.

Over time, a culture would develop where phone use at the wheel carries reputational consequences, not just legal ones.

This would require infrastructure—certified devices, standardized protocols, privacy safeguards—that does not yet exist. The New York proposal is a first step, but it is too narrow. We need state laws that establish Textalyzer authority, federal guidelines that set standards, and industry participation that creates scalable solutions.

Is this a nanny state? Perhaps. But we accept the nanny state readily when it protects us from others' choices. A driver who texts endangers me, not just themselves. I have no ability to protect myself from their distraction; I can only trust that collective social pressure will change their behavior. The Textalyzer creates the conditions for that pressure to operate. Let us build them.
