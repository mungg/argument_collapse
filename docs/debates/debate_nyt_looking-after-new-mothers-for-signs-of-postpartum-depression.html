Looking After New Mothers for Signs of Postpartum Depression

A dozen states have laws that encourage some form of awareness and education about postpartum depression. In three of those states — New Jersey, Illinois and West Virginia — screening is required by law.

As a recent Times series points out, this form of maternal depression is “more common and varied than previously thought” and can result in tragedy.

Should screening for postpartum depression be mandatory? How can those suffering be assured of treatment?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Mandatory screening sounds like an easy win — a brief questionnaire like the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale takes only minutes — but the harder question is what follows a positive screen. Without a guaranteed pathway to treatment, screening risks becoming a box-checking exercise that flags suffering and then abandons it. New Jersey's 2006 law, the first of its kind, has shown that detection rates rise but that referrals frequently dead-end in waiting lists, uninsured patients, or providers unfamiliar with perinatal mental health.

That argues not against mandatory screening but for pairing it with infrastructure. Obstetricians and pediatricians — who, importantly, see mothers more often than psychiatrists do in the first postpartum year — need warm-handoff arrangements with mental health clinicians, lactation-aware prescribers, and home-visiting programs. The ACA's coverage of depression screening as a preventive service helps, but Medicaid, which pays for roughly half of U.S. births, varies wildly in postpartum coverage past 60 days, precisely when many cases emerge.

So yes, require screening — at the standard well-baby visits as well as the six-week checkup, since symptoms can surface late. But couple the mandate with reimbursement for follow-up, training requirements, and stigma-reducing public education. Detection without treatment is its own cruelty.

Mandatory screening for postpartum depression is one of those rare public health interventions that costs little, stigmatizes no one, and could prevent enormous suffering. We already require new mothers to undergo dozens of clinical assessments during pregnancy and delivery — blood pressure checks, gestational diabetes tests, Group B strep swabs. Yet the most common complication of childbirth, affecting an estimated one in seven women, often goes unmentioned at the six-week visit, if that visit happens at all.

The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale is a ten-question instrument that takes about five minutes to complete. New Jersey has required its use since 2006, after former First Lady Mary Jo Codey championed the legislation. The sky did not fall. Pediatricians and obstetricians integrated the questionnaire into routine visits, and women who would otherwise have suffered silently were identified and referred. The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists and the American Academy of Pediatrics both endorse routine screening precisely because clinical intuition alone misses too many cases.

The harder question — and the one critics of mandates rightly press — is what happens after a positive screen. A checkbox on a form is not treatment. In rural West Virginia or inner-city Newark, a referral may lead nowhere if there are no psychiatrists taking Medicaid, no childcare to attend appointments, no paid leave to recover. Screening without a treatment pipeline risks becoming a bureaucratic ritual that satisfies legislators while failing mothers.

This is an argument for doing more, not less. States that mandate screening should pair the requirement with reimbursement for behavioral health integration in OB and pediatric practices, telepsychiatry coverage, and home-visiting programs like the Nurse-Family Partnership, which has shown real success reaching isolated new mothers. The Affordable Care Act's parity provisions and Medicaid expansion give states new tools; they should use them.

The recent Times series detailing tragedies — mothers harming themselves or their infants — should remind us that postpartum depression is not a "baby blues" inconvenience but a medical emergency in waiting. Mandatory screening is the floor, not the ceiling. Combined with serious investment in follow-up care, it would save lives.

Before we rush to mandate one more thing on the obstetrician's checklist, we should ask a harder question: does mandatory screening actually help the women it targets, or does it mostly help legislators feel they have done something?

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force has been notably cautious about routine depression screening precisely because screening only improves outcomes when it is embedded in a system that can diagnose, treat, and follow up. The Edinburgh scale generates a fair number of false positives. A woman flagged as possibly depressed who then receives nothing — no warm handoff to a clinician, no medication management, no therapy — has been labeled, not helped. Worse, in our current legal climate, a documented positive screen could become evidence in a custody dispute or a child welfare investigation. Low-income women and women of color, who are already disproportionately surveilled by these systems, have rational reasons to fear honest answers on a state-mandated questionnaire.

Look at what mandates actually buy. Illinois requires that women be given information about postpartum depression; New Jersey requires screening but does not guarantee treatment. In neither state is there a robust mechanism to ensure that a depressed mother in a county with no psychiatrist accepting her insurance gets care. The mandate becomes a paper exercise, with liability flowing to providers who fail to document compliance, while the underlying shortage of perinatal mental health services goes unaddressed.

There is also something worth defending in the discretion of the clinical relationship. A trusted obstetrician or midwife who knows her patient may elicit more honest disclosure through conversation than through a form handed over at the front desk. Mandates tend to crowd out judgment with documentation.

A better approach: invest in universal access to perinatal mental health care, train pediatricians (who see new mothers far more often than obstetricians do in the first year) to recognize symptoms, expand home-visiting programs, and subsidize community-based peer support like Postpartum Support International's networks. Screen widely, yes — but as a clinical norm backed by real services, not as a statutory checkbox that risks punishing the women it claims to protect.

That is not the same as a thoughtful conversation between a clinician and a new mother, and the evidence that legislated screening improves outcomes (as opposed to screening rates) is surprisingly thin.

What actually moves the needle is professional norms backed by payment. When ACOG recommended screening and insurers began reimbursing for it, screening rates rose without anyone passing a law. When the AAP urged pediatricians to assess maternal mental health at well-child visits, many of us added it to our routine. These changes happened because the profession internalized them, not because a statute compelled them.

That said, I take the tragedies described in the Times series seriously, and I do not think the status quo is acceptable. The real failure is not at the screening step but at the treatment step. In my own referral network, the wait for a perinatal psychiatrist can stretch past three months. For Medicaid patients it can be longer. We have built a healthcare system in which a woman can be diagnosed with a treatable illness and still wait a season for help while her infant grows and her marriage frays.

The legislative energy spent on screening mandates would be better spent on loan forgiveness for perinatal mental health providers, telepsychiatry parity, and Medicaid coverage for the full year postpartum rather than the current 60 days. Detection is easy. It is treatment — accessible, affordable, timely treatment — that we have not yet been willing to pay for. That is where the law should focus.

A compassionate impulse does not always make good law. The push for mandatory postpartum depression screening, however well-intentioned, threatens to substitute bureaucratic compulsion for genuine support and carries risks that its advocates underestimate. We should champion robust, voluntary screening programs and universal access to care, but a legal mandate is a different and dangerous instrument.

First, consider the screening tools themselves. The Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, widely used and respected, is a self-report questionnaire that can be profoundly influenced by a mother’s immediate mood, cultural background, or even her fear of being judged unfit. False positives are inevitable. In a system where screening is mandatory, a flagged score can trigger a cascade of interventions — child protective services visits, psychiatric evaluations — that, for a woman already fragile, can feel like persecution rather than help. The history of mandated reporting in other health contexts is not reassuring; it often breeds distrust between patients and providers, deterring honest disclosure precisely when it is needed most.

Then there is the question of follow-up. As the Times series has highlighted, postpartum depression is varied and complex, yet our mental health infrastructure is patchwork. Passing a law requiring screening without simultaneously guaranteeing swift, affordable, and culturally competent treatment is legislative theater. It creates a cruel dynamic: a mother is identified as suffering, labeled, perhaps even publicly documented, but then placed on a six-month waiting list for a therapist or told her insurance won’t cover the recommended care. The distress of being seen but not helped can be worse than the invisibility.

A better approach builds on existing well-child visits, where pediatricians already see mothers repeatedly. We need mandatory insurance coverage for maternal mental health care, not mandatory screening of mothers. Fund community-based peer support networks, train midwives and doulas to recognize warning signs without the threat of state action, and make voluntary, destigmatized screening a universal expectation, not a legal obligation. A new mother’s relationship with her caregivers must be built on trust. The blunt instrument of a mandate can fracture that trust, leaving vulnerable women more isolated than before.
