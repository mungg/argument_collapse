Was Marx Right?

In the golden, post-war years of Western economic growth, the comfortable living standard of the working class and the economy’s overall stability made the best case for the value of capitalism and the fraudulence of Marx’s critical view of it. But in more recent years many of the forces that Marx said would lead to capitalism’s demise – the concentration and globalization of wealth, the permanence of unemployment, the lowering of wages – have become real, and troubling, once again.

The fall of communism discredited Marx’s political vision. But, as observers have wondered before, is his view of our economic future being validated?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Marx was a dreadful prophet of the political future and a remarkably acute diagnostician of the economic present. To ask whether he was "right" is to confuse those two things. The Soviet experiment proved that the dictatorship of the proletariat was a euphemism for the dictatorship of the party, and the Maoist disasters added their own seventy-million-corpse footnote. None of that, however, refutes the descriptive parts of *Capital*. It only refutes the prescriptive parts of *The Communist Manifesto*.

Start with what Marx actually argued about capitalism as a system. He claimed that competition would drive firms to substitute machinery for labor, that capital would concentrate in fewer and larger hands, that production would globalize ("the need of a constantly expanding market chases the bourgeoisie over the whole surface of the globe"), that crises of overproduction would recur, and that labor's share of output would tend to fall relative to capital's. Look around in 2014. The top 1 percent's share of U.S. income has climbed back to levels not seen since 1928. Median male wages have been flat or falling for four decades. The labor share of GDP, long treated by economists as a Kaldorian constant, has dropped measurably across the OECD since 2000. Five years after the collapse of Lehman Brothers, long-term unemployment in Europe and labor-force dropout in America look uncomfortably like Marx's "reserve army." If a Victorian polemicist sketched the world's economic skeleton 150 years ago and the X-ray today matches the sketch, that is worth noticing.

Where Marx was wrong matters too. He underestimated capitalism's capacity to raise absolute living standards even as it produced inequality; the British worker of 1880 did not face the immiseration Marx predicted, and neither does the Chinese worker of 2014, who has been pulled out of agrarian poverty by precisely the globalization Marx described. He underestimated the political response: the welfare state, progressive taxation, universal suffrage, antitrust law, and independent central banking all blunted the contradictions he thought would prove fatal. The labor theory of value is dead as economics, replaced by marginalism. And his teleology — the inevitable supersession of capitalism by socialism by communism — was metaphysics dressed up as science. History does not have a destination.

But notice what makes the question feel live again. The compromises that tamed capitalism between 1945 and 1975 — strong unions, capital controls, high marginal tax rates, a tight link between productivity and wages — have largely been dismantled. Piketty and Saez have shown that the post-war equalization was a historical anomaly inside a longer trend toward rentier concentration. Financialization has shifted income from wages to capital gains. Trade and automation have hollowed out the bargaining power of workers who used to be the median voter. In this environment Marx's vocabulary — alienation, commodification, the fetishism of finance, surplus extraction — describes things liberal economics struggles to name.

So: was Marx right? He was right that unregulated capitalism produces concentrations of wealth and power that menace both equality and democracy, and that those tendencies are structural rather than accidental. He was wrong about what to do about it. The honest answer in 2014 is that his diagnosis deserves rereading precisely because his cure has been so thoroughly discredited. We are not heading toward communism. We may, however, be heading back toward the nineteenth century, and that is the warning worth heeding.

The question of whether Marx was right has a cyclical quality. In periods of broad prosperity, he seems a dusty relic, but when capitalism convulses, his ghost returns. The current moment—emerging from the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression—is clearly one of the latter. But being “right” requires parsing which Marx we mean. There is the diagnostician of capitalism’s internal logic and the prophet of its revolutionary overthrow. The first has aged remarkably well; the second, not at all.

Marx’s core economic insight was that capitalism is not a static equilibrium machine but a dynamic system driven by contradictions that periodically erupt into crisis. He argued that competition forces capitalists to accumulate and reinvest, leading to the concentration and centralization of capital. Today, that process is undeniable. Over the past three decades, mergers, acquisitions, and the rise of global corporate behemoths have produced levels of industrial concentration that would have been unimaginable in the post-war era. In sectors from finance to technology, a handful of firms dominate, echoing Marx’s vision of a shrinking capitalist class presiding over ever-larger pools of capital.

He also predicted the immiseration of the working class—a theory often caricatured as a steady slide into absolute poverty. A more subtle reading, however, sees it as relative deprivation, a decline in labor’s share of income and a growing reserve army of the unemployed. Here, too, the recent evidence is striking. Since the 1970s, wages for the median worker in advanced economies have largely stagnated, even as productivity has climbed. The fruits of growth have flowed disproportionately to capital and the top sliver of earners. The globalization of production has created that reserve army on a planetary scale, disciplining labor with the credible threat of offshoring. The financial crisis made permanent the temporary joblessness of millions, especially the young, in what looks unsettlingly like the chronic surplus populations Marx described.

His theory of crisis, rooted in a falling rate of profit, remains deeply contested among economists. Yet the dynamic he identified—the tendency for periods of boom to generate speculative bubbles that then burst—has been on grim display. The Savings and Loan crisis of the 1980s, the Asian financial crisis, the dot-com bubble, and finally the subprime mortgage disaster all fit a pattern of financialization that Marx saw as a natural outgrowth of an economy searching for profitable outlets when productive investment yields diminishing returns. The 2008 meltdown, with its complex derivatives and systemic risk, was a crisis not despite capitalism’s sophistication but because of it, a very Marxian notion.

However, if his economic diagnosis has been partially validated, his political prognosis tells a different story. The upheaval Marx expected would be triggered by the immiseration he foresaw. But the fall of communism and the global retreat of the left have not been followed by a resurgence of revolutionary class consciousness. Instead, the response to the crisis has been fragmented and largely reactive: the Tea Party on the right, Occupy Wall Street on the left. Neither coalesced into a coherent political movement capable of challenging capital’s fundamental structures. Labor unions continue to decline in the West, and the “precariat” remains disorganized. The state, far from being an executive committee of the bourgeoisie in a simple sense, expanded massively to rescue the financial system, demonstrating a flexibility Marx underestimated. Capitalism’s survival instinct—its ability to use state power to socialize losses while privatizing gains—proved robust.

So, was Marx right? Yes, in that he was an unparalleled anatomist of capitalism’s endogenous dysfunctions. His description of a system driven by relentless accumulation, deepening inequality, and recurring crises reads less like prophecy and more like a sober analysis of today’s headlines. But this is a partial vindication. The revolutionary sequel never arrived, and the very concentration of wealth he outlined has, in the absence of mass opposition, translated into immense political influence, tilting policy further in capital’s favor. Marx grasped the disease but was wrong about the body’s inevitable revolt. That remains the open question.

Nearly a quarter-century after the fall of the Berlin Wall seemingly relegated Karl Marx to the ash heap of history, his ghost has returned to haunt the industrialized West. The prolonged hangover of the 2008 financial crisis, characterized by stagnant wages, structural unemployment, and a widening gulf between the ultra-rich and everyone else, has cast a harsh light on the intrinsic vulnerabilities of the free market. When we ask if Marx was right, we must distinguish between Marx the revolutionary prophet and Marx the economic diagnostician. While his utopian cures proved disastrous in the twentieth century, his diagnosis of capitalism’s underlying pathology remains astonishingly relevant today.

Marx understood, perhaps better than any classical economist, that capitalism is inherently unstable and driven by a relentless logic of accumulation. He predicted that the competitive pressures of the free market would inevitably lead to the concentration of wealth in fewer and fewer hands, creating monopolies and oligopolies. Looking at our contemporary corporate landscape—where a handful of colossal financial institutions and multinational conglomerates wield unprecedented power—it is difficult to argue with his foresight. Furthermore, Marx accurately anticipated the globalization of capital. He recognized that the bourgeoisie would be compelled to "nestle everywhere, settle everywhere," chasing cheaper labor and new markets across borders. Today, as global supply chains decouple corporate profits from the prosperity of Western workers, his 19th-century analysis reads like a contemporary headline.

The post-war "golden years" mentioned in the prompt are increasingly looking like a historical anomaly rather than the capitalist baseline. Following World War II, a unique combination of factors—the massive destruction of European capital, the bargaining power of strong labor unions, and the geopolitical necessity of offering an attractive alternative to the Soviet bloc—produced a brief era of shared prosperity and a robust middle class. However, since the late 1970s, as these constraints were dismantled, Western economies have reverted to their historical mean. The current era of rising inequality, where returns on capital consistently outstrip overall economic growth, resembles the Gilded Age of Marx’s own time. The modern proliferation of precarious, part-time labor also echoes his concept of the "reserve army of labor," which serves to keep wages perpetually depressed.

Yet, recognizing Marx's prescience does not require accepting his fatalism. Where Marx erred most profoundly was in his underestimation of capitalism’s extraordinary adaptability and the political agency of democratic societies. He believed that the escalating immiseration of the working class would inevitably trigger a proletarian revolution and the collapse of the system. Instead, capitalism demonstrated a remarkable capacity to save itself from its own excesses through political intervention. The New Deal, the creation of the welfare state, and progressive taxation were all mechanisms that successfully redistributed wealth and stabilized the system. Moreover, while wages stagnate in the West, the integration of global markets has undeniably lifted hundreds of millions of people out of absolute poverty in nations like China and India—a testament to capitalism’s productive capacity that Marx recognized but ultimately discounted.

Ultimately, Marx was fundamentally wrong about the destination, but he was profoundly right about the trajectory. We ignore his economic critiques at our own peril. If we wish to preserve the dynamism of the market while avoiding social rupture, we must acknowledge the structural flaws Marx identified and use democratic tools to correct them.

Marx was right about capitalism in the way a great pathologist may be right about a disease without knowing how the patient will live with it. He identified real tendencies: capitalism constantly revolutionizes production, spreads across borders, concentrates economic power, turns human relations into market relations and periodically generates crises that are not accidental but built into the system. Those insights look more relevant after the financial crisis of 2008 than they did during the confident decades after World War II.

The postwar settlement made Marx seem obsolete because it softened capitalism’s sharpest edges. Strong unions, high marginal tax rates, public investment, social insurance, regulated finance and rapid productivity growth allowed workers in the United States and Western Europe to share broadly in prosperity. That was not pure laissez-faire capitalism vindicating itself; it was capitalism disciplined by democratic politics, fear of communism, labor organization and the memory of depression and war. When those restraints weakened, some of the dynamics Marx emphasized returned more visibly: rising inequality, pressure on wages, financial speculation and insecurity even amid aggregate wealth.

Marx’s strongest claim was not simply that workers would be poor. It was that capitalism gives owners of capital structural power over those who must sell their labor. That remains recognizable. Global supply chains allow firms to seek cheaper labor and weaker regulation. Automation and outsourcing reduce bargaining power for many workers. Corporate profits and executive compensation have risen while median wages in the United States have stagnated for long stretches. Wealth is far more concentrated than income, and inherited advantage remains powerful. In this sense, Marx’s language of class conflict, though unfashionable, still describes something real: the distribution of gains is not determined by productivity alone but by power.

He was also prescient about globalization. The “bourgeoisie,” he wrote in the Communist Manifesto, must nestle everywhere, settle everywhere, establish connections everywhere. That sounds less like 1848 than like the world of container ships, multinational corporations, offshore finance and iPhones assembled through far-flung production networks. Capital is mobile; labor is much less so. This asymmetry disciplines governments as well as workers, since states are often told that taxes, regulations or labor protections must be trimmed to remain “competitive.”

But Marx was wrong in important ways. His expectation that capitalism would produce steadily worsening misery for workers in the advanced economies has not been borne out. Even after wage stagnation, ordinary people in rich countries have access to goods, medicine, education and longevity unimaginable in the 19th century. Globally, hundreds of millions have escaped extreme poverty in recent decades, especially in China and India, through market-oriented growth, however unequal and environmentally costly. Capitalism has shown an extraordinary capacity to innovate, adapt and absorb opposition.

Marx also underestimated politics. He treated the state too often as an instrument of class rule and not enough as a battlefield on which capitalism could be modified. Social democracy, labor law, unemployment insurance, public pensions, central banking and progressive taxation did not abolish capitalism, but they did change its outcomes. The lesson of the 20th century is not that markets automatically produce justice, but neither is it that their contradictions inevitably lead to socialist revolution. Institutions matter.

Most damaging to Marx’s political legacy is that regimes claiming his authority produced repression, scarcity and one-party rule. The collapse of Soviet communism did not refute every page of Marx’s economic analysis, but it did discredit the idea that abolishing private capital through a centralized state reliably liberates workers. Concentrated political power can be even more dangerous than concentrated economic power.

So was Marx right? He was right to insist that capitalism is not a natural or harmonious order, that it creates winners and losers, that crises recur, and that wealth tends to buy power. He was wrong to think he had discovered iron laws leading to capitalism’s collapse and proletarian emancipation. The better conclusion is not Marxism as prophecy, but Marx as warning. Capitalism can produce dynamism and broad prosperity only when constrained by democratic institutions, social protections and countervailing power. When it is left to its own logic, Marx becomes much harder to dismiss.

The 2008 financial crisis and its aftermath gave Marx's critics an uncomfortable moment. For years, the comfortable assumption had been that liberal capitalism had solved the problems Marx identified: the business cycle was tamed, poverty was declining, and the working class in developed nations enjoyed unprecedented material comfort. Then came the crash, the bailouts, and the grinding stagnation that followed—and suddenly the ghosts of Das Kapital seemed worth consulting again.

But the question of whether Marx was "right" requires us to be precise about which Marx we mean. The political prophecies—revolutionary uprisings in advanced industrial nations, the inevitable collapse of capitalism under its internal contradictions—clearly did not materialize as Marx imagined. Western capitalism proved far more adaptable than he anticipated, incorporating labor organization, social safety nets, and regulatory mechanisms that dampened some of the most exploitative tendencies he identified. The fall of actually-existing communist regimes in 1989 and 1991 seemed to settle the argument definitively in capitalism's favor.

Yet the economic diagnosis deserves renewed attention in our era. Marx identified real tendencies of capitalist systems: the tendency toward concentration of capital and the emergence of monopolistic or oligopolistic structures; the recurring pattern of financial speculation disconnected from productive investment; and the structural pressure to drive down wages and casualize labor to maintain profit rates. These tendencies have not disappeared—they have, if anything, intensified.

Consider the evidence available to us in 2014. The past three decades have witnessed extraordinary concentration of wealth: the top one percent of income earners capture an increasingly large share of national income across both developed and developing nations. Wage growth for ordinary workers has stagnated even as productivity climbed. The financial sector, rather than being contained, has grown to extraordinary proportions and demonstrated its capacity to destroy enormous amounts of actual wealth while extracting enormous bonuses. These are precisely the dynamics Marx analyzed—they did not originate with him, but he offered a theoretical framework for understanding why they recur.

The globalized economy Marx could only partially anticipate has amplified these tendencies rather than resolving them. Capital moves freely across borders while labor remains relatively immobile, fundamentally weakening workers' bargaining position. Corporations can playing nations against each other for the most favorable regulatory environment. The "permanence of unemployment" and underemployment the question mentions reflects not individual failures but structural conditions that market signals alone cannot correct.

None of this validates the full Marxian program or its political conclusions. But it does suggest that the comfortable narrative—that Marx's diagnosis was refuted by history—was premature. Capitalism has not collapsed, but it has not solved its internal contradictions either. It has periodically externalized them: exporting exploitation to the global periphery, financializing the economy to concentrate gains, and tolerating mass unemployment as a disciplinary mechanism. These are not the actions of a system in perfect health.

The question worth asking may not be whether Marx was right or wrong, but which of his insights remain useful for understanding our current condition.

Marx was right about the diagnosis and wrong about the cure, and we should be honest about both halves of that ledger. Reading the first volume of *Capital* today is a startling experience because so much of what Marx described — the relentless drive to lower wages, the reserve army of the unemployed, the tendency of capital to concentrate, the way technological change displaces labor faster than it absorbs it — reads like a commentary on the last decade rather than the 1860s. The financial crisis of 2008, the foreclosure wave, the bailouts that socialized losses while privatizing gains, the stagnation of median wages since roughly 1979 even as productivity has climbed — these are not phenomena that mainstream neoclassical economics anticipated or comfortably explains. They are phenomena Marx essentially predicted.

The most durable Marxian insight is that capital, left to itself, tends toward concentration. Thomas Piketty's forthcoming work on wealth inequality, and the long series of papers he and Emmanuel Saez have produced on top income shares, document this with a precision Marx could not have managed. The top one percent's share of national income in the United States is back to levels last seen in 1928. Global wealth is increasingly held by a tiny stratum of individuals and institutions whose holdings grow faster than the economies they sit atop. This is the Marxian story told in data.

What Marx got wrong, and wrong catastrophically, was the politics. The proletariat did not become a unified revolutionary subject; it fragmented along lines of nation, race, religion, sector, and skill. The dictatorship of the proletariat, where it was tried, became a dictatorship over the proletariat. Central planning could not solve the calculation problem Hayek and Mises identified. And capitalism proved more adaptive than Marx imagined — it absorbed the welfare state, the eight-hour day, social insurance, and antitrust law, and was strengthened rather than destroyed by these concessions.

That adaptiveness is precisely the point worth dwelling on. The post-war golden age the prompt mentions was not capitalism being itself; it was capitalism constrained — by strong unions, high marginal tax rates, capital controls under Bretton Woods, financial regulation under Glass-Steagall, and the political pressure of an actually existing communist alternative. When those constraints were dismantled beginning in the late 1970s, capitalism reverted to something much closer to the system Marx described. The "Marxian" tendencies we are now observing are not a refutation of the post-war experience but a confirmation that the post-war experience was the exception, achieved through deliberate political work.

So the relevant question is not "was Marx right?" in some all-or-nothing sense, but rather: which Marx? The Marx of revolutionary eschatology, the Marx who believed history had an iron destination in communism, has been thoroughly refuted by the twentieth century. But the Marx of political economy — the analyst of how capital accumulates, how labor is disciplined, how crises emerge from the contradictions of production — is having something of a vindication.

The practical implication is not that we should reread Lenin. It is that we should take seriously the possibility that without active political counterweights, capitalism does generate the pathologies Marx identified, and that managing those pathologies is the central political task of our time. That means rebuilding worker bargaining power, taxing capital appropriately, restraining finance, and being clear-eyed about the difference between markets (useful) and oligarchy (corrosive). Marx's diagnosis can guide us even if his prescription cannot. Ignoring the diagnosis because we reject the prescription is the kind of intellectual error that produces avoidable suffering.

The recurring fascination with Marx tells us more about the anxieties of intellectuals than about the actual trajectory of the global economy. Every recession since 1848 has produced a wave of "Marx was right after all" essays, and every subsequent recovery has quietly retired them. We are in the trough phase of that cycle now, and we should be cautious about confusing temporary disorientation with historical vindication.

Consider what has actually happened during the period in which capitalism was supposedly proving Marx correct. Since 1990, somewhere between 700 million and a billion people have been lifted out of extreme poverty, the largest and fastest reduction in human misery in recorded history. The agent of this transformation was not socialism but the integration of China, India, and much of Southeast Asia into global capitalist markets. Child mortality has collapsed. Literacy is near universal among the young. Life expectancy in the developing world has gained roughly two decades since Marx-revival sentiment was last fashionable in the 1960s.

This is the inconvenient fact for the "Marx was right" thesis. The global working class, taken as a whole, has never been better off. What has happened is that within rich countries — particularly the United States and the United Kingdom — the unskilled and semi-skilled workforce has lost ground, both absolutely in some cases and relatively in many more. This is a serious problem and deserves serious policy attention. But it is not what Marx predicted. Marx predicted immiseration of the proletariat as a class, globally, under the logic of capital accumulation. What we have instead is convergence: workers in poor countries getting richer faster than workers in rich countries, with global inequality between individuals likely declining for the first time since the industrial revolution.

The "permanence of unemployment" claim in the prompt is similarly overstated. European unemployment is genuinely troubling, but it largely reflects specific policy failures — the design of the euro, labor market rigidities, austerity timing — not the inner logic of capital. The United States, for all its problems, has unemployment under seven percent and falling. Marx's reserve army was supposed to be a structural necessity of capitalism, not a cyclical phenomenon that varies wildly across capitalist economies depending on their institutions.

What about concentration? Yes, top income shares have risen, especially in Anglo-American economies. But the composition of the top one percent has changed dramatically: it is now dominated by high-earning professionals — doctors, lawyers, finance specialists, technology executives — not by a rentier class living off inherited capital. Marx's story was about owners versus workers. The contemporary story is much more about education, skills, and superstar effects in winner-take-all markets. These require different remedies and a different theoretical framework.

The deeper problem with Marx is methodological. His system is built around a labor theory of value that no serious economist has accepted for over a century, and a teleological view of history that belongs more to Hegelian metaphysics than to social science. When contemporary writers say "Marx was right," they usually mean something much weaker: capitalism produces inequality, crises happen, the rich are politically powerful. These observations don't require Marx; they were made by Adam Smith and J.S. Mill, and they are handled more rigorously today by mainstream economists like Krugman, Stiglitz, and Piketty without any of the dialectical apparatus.

Capitalism has serious problems in 2014. They deserve concrete, technical, institutional responses — better regulation, more progressive taxation, stronger safety nets, investment in education and infrastructure. Wrapping these familiar reforms in the dramatic vocabulary of Marxism adds nothing but a frisson of radicalism for people who miss the twentieth century's certainties.

The question "Was Marx right?" is poorly posed, because Marx was many thinkers stitched into one beard. There was Marx the philosopher of alienation, Marx the political revolutionary, Marx the historical sociologist, and Marx the analytical economist. Each must be judged separately, and the answers diverge sharply.

Marx the philosopher of alienation — the young Marx of the 1844 manuscripts — looks remarkably prescient. His core claim was that under conditions of wage labor, work becomes something done not for its own intrinsic meaning but as an instrumental sale of one's life-energy to another, leaving the worker estranged from the product, from the act of production, from fellow workers, and ultimately from his own species-being. Look around the contemporary economy: the call-center scripts, the warehouse productivity monitors that time bathroom breaks, the gig-economy drivers managed by algorithm, the meaningless email rituals of the cubicle. The empirical literature on work satisfaction, burnout, and the rise of "bullshit jobs" suggests alienation is not a quaint nineteenth-century complaint but a defining experience of modern labor. On this dimension, Marx is alarmingly contemporary.

Marx the revolutionary — the author of the *Communist Manifesto* and the theorist of proletarian dictatorship — is the Marx history has most decisively repudiated. Not just because communist regimes were tyrannical, though they were, but because the political subject Marx invoked never coalesced. National identity proved stronger than class identity in 1914 and has remained so. The expected polarization into two great hostile camps gave way to a complex stratification of middle classes, professional classes, and service classes that Marx's binary categories cannot capture. Whatever future critique of capitalism emerges, it will not look like 1917.

Marx the historical sociologist deserves more credit than he usually gets. His insistence that modes of production shape ideology, culture, and law was a methodological revolution that has soaked into mainstream social science to the point where we no longer recognize its Marxian origins. When journalists explain political behavior by reference to economic interest, or historians explain cultural shifts by reference to material conditions, they are working in a paradigm Marx more or less invented. This is intellectual victory by absorption, the highest form of vindication.

Marx the economist is the most contested. The labor theory of value is dead; the transformation problem was never solved; the falling rate of profit has not in fact fallen in any straightforward way. But Marx's larger picture — that capitalism is dynamic, crisis-prone, globalizing, and tends to concentrate wealth — has held up startlingly well. The Schumpeterian themes of creative destruction were essentially borrowed from Marx. The contemporary work on rising inequality, on financialization, on the secular tendencies of capital, all engages a research program Marx initiated even when its practitioners reject his conclusions.

So: was Marx right? He was right that capitalism is the most revolutionary force in human history. He was right that it generates inequality and alienation as features, not bugs. He was right that economic structure shapes politics more than politics shapes economic structure. He was wrong that the proletariat would unite, that revolution was inevitable, that the dialectic pointed toward communism, and that planned economies could outperform markets. The verdict, then, is split. Marx is the most important diagnostician of capitalism we have, and one of the worst prescribers of its alternatives. The mistake is to take him as a package deal. He himself reportedly said, late in life, "I am not a Marxist." A century and a half on, neither should we be — but neither should we be free of him.

We should resist the seductive symmetry of the question. The phrasing — was Marx right? — invites us to treat the post-war boom and the post-2008 stagnation as natural states of capitalism that respectively refute and confirm a theoretical doctrine. But capitalism is not a natural object that reveals its essence over time. It is an institutional artifact whose behavior depends on the rules under which it operates. The rules changed, and so did the outcomes. Marx didn't predict the future; political choices made the future.

The post-war "golden age" was the product of an extraordinary and historically specific configuration: the destruction of European capital stocks in the war, the dollar-gold monetary system at Bretton Woods, capital controls that gave nation-states room to pursue full-employment policies, marginal tax rates above 70 percent in the United States, union density approaching 35 percent, an antitrust regime willing to break up firms, and a geopolitical contest with the Soviet Union that forced Western elites to deliver visible improvements in working-class life. Capitalism within these constraints produced shared growth.

Beginning in the 1970s, every one of those constraints was loosened or removed. Bretton Woods collapsed. Capital controls were dismantled. Top tax rates were cut roughly in half. Union density fell to about eleven percent in the United States. Antitrust became permissive. Financial regulation was rolled back, culminating in the repeal of Glass-Steagall in 1999. The Soviet alternative disappeared. And capitalism, freed of these constraints, produced rising inequality, stagnant wages, asset bubbles, and a catastrophic financial crisis.

This is not Marx being vindicated. This is policy being vindicated — specifically, the policies of the social-democratic and New Deal traditions. The lesson is not that capitalism inevitably tends toward the pathologies Marx described; the lesson is that capitalism *unconstrained* tends toward them, while capitalism *constrained* can deliver shared prosperity. This is a far more useful conclusion because it points to action. Marx offered no path forward except revolution, which we have very good reasons not to want. The social democrats offered a path forward consisting of taxes, regulations, unions, and public goods, which we have very good reasons to want and which we know works because it has worked.

The recent enthusiasm for Marx among intellectuals is therefore both understandable and somewhat self-defeating. Understandable because the gap between the actually existing economy and the textbook one has become embarrassingly large, and Marx is the most famous critic of the textbook. Self-defeating because invoking Marx places the critique in a frame — revolution, the abolition of private property, the dictatorship of the proletariat — that is politically toxic and historically discredited, when the actual reforms needed are quite modest and quite traditional.

Raise the top marginal tax rate. Tax capital gains as ordinary income. Strengthen collective bargaining rights. Break up the largest banks. Invest in public infrastructure and education. Build out the social safety net. None of this requires Marx. All of it was within the policy mainstream as recently as the Eisenhower administration. The fact that proposing it now sounds radical tells us how far the political center of gravity has shifted, not how correct Marx was.

So my answer is: Marx was right about what capitalism does when its tendencies run unchecked, but he was right by accident, because his theoretical framework is mostly wrong. The institutions of the mid-twentieth century knew better, and we have forgotten what they knew. The task is not to rediscover Marx; it is to rediscover Walter Reuther, Frances Perkins, and John Maynard Keynes.

There is a strange ritual that happens whenever capitalism stumbles: the commentariat asks whether Marx was right, debates it for a season, and then moves on without changing anything.
