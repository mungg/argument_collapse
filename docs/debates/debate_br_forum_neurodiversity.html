Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Robert Chapman has done something important here: they have named a real tension. Neurodiversity discourse has, over the past decade, drifted from a movement of self-advocacy into a vocabulary that human resources departments, defense contractors, and lifestyle brands now deploy without breaking a sweat. The "superpower" framing in particular has become a way of sorting neurodivergent people into the deserving (employable, profitable, photogenic) and the disposable (institutionalized, incarcerated, mute, multiply disabled). Any honest account of where the movement stands in 2024 has to reckon with this. Where I want to push back is on the proposed cure — a politics of "Neurodivergent Power" modeled on Black Power and oriented toward anticapitalist mass organization. I worry that this prescription mistakes the diagnosis.

Start with the analogy itself. The Black Power moment Chapman invokes had a coherent constituency: a population with shared historical experience, shared cultural inheritance, and, crucially, shared recognition of one another as a "we." Neurodivergence has none of this in the same way. The category is a coalition of administrative diagnoses — autism, ADHD, dyspraxia, dyslexia, Tourette's, sometimes schizophrenia and bipolar disorder, sometimes intellectual disability — assembled relatively recently and held together largely by the political work of saying they belong together. A nonspeaking autistic person in a group home, a hedge fund analyst with ADHD, and someone cycling through psychiatric wards during episodes of psychosis do not obviously share interests, let alone a standpoint. Chapman is aware of this; the essay's most compelling passages are about how Liberal Neurodiversity has cashed out its gains for the most palatable members of the coalition. But the proposed solution — collective consciousness, a revolutionary party, mass organizing — presumes the very solidaristic subject whose absence is the problem.

This matters because the strongest part of Chapman's critique is materialist, not cultural. The people dying in their fifties of untreated cardiovascular disease, the quarter of UK prisoners with ADHD, the autistic adults shut out of employment — their situation is not primarily a representational problem fixable by a different consciousness. It is a problem of housing, health care, deinstitutionalization, policing, and income. And here is the awkward fact: most of the concrete wins Chapman wants — free housing, decarceration, an end to psychiatric incarceration, real workplace accommodations — are wins that would be delivered by ordinary, recognizable, redistributive social-democratic politics. They do not require a distinctly neurodivergent revolutionary subject. They require the welfare state to function and to extend to populations it has historically abandoned.

I suspect Chapman would say: yes, but those gains will not come without organized pressure, and neurodivergent people need their own movement to ensure they are not last in line. Fair enough. But the historical lesson of the disability rights movement — including the parts of it that produced the ADA Chapman somewhat grudgingly credits — is that gains came through coalition with labor, with parents, with churches, with veterans, and through the patient instrumentalizing of liberal legal machinery. The 504 sit-ins were sustained by the Black Panthers delivering food, yes, but the demand was for the enforcement of an administrative rule. "Liberal" reformism, in other words, has done more for the materially worst-off disabled people than any revolutionary alternative on offer.

There is also a quieter worry. The essay's treatment of psychiatry leans toward the antipsychiatric pole that Chapman elsewhere distances themself from. The example of the worker with paranoid delusions who needs webcams covered is offered as evidence that "reasonable accommodation" is ideologically constrained. It is also, plausibly, an example of someone who is unwell and would benefit from treatment they may currently lack access to. Neurodiversity politics has always had to navigate this: the difference between an autistic person who does not want to be cured and a person in florid psychosis who is suffering is not merely ideological. Collapsing everything into "neuronormative domination" — including, in the essay's most strained moment, Aaron Bushnell's self-immolation reframed as a refusal of stifling normality — risks losing the ability to say that some neurodivergent experiences involve real suffering that deserves clinical response, not just political reinterpretation.

What I would keep from Chapman is the insistence that the movement stop measuring itself by how many autistic software engineers get hired at Microsoft and start measuring itself by the conditions of those in group homes, jails, and shelters. What I would resist is the conclusion that this requires transcending the rights framework rather than radically extending it. The ADA promised reasonable accommodation; it has not been delivered to most of the people it covers. "Neurodivergent Power" is a stirring slogan. "Enforce the law, fund the services, close the institutions, house everyone" is less stirring, and probably more emancipatory.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Robert Chapman’s essay arrives at a pivotal moment. The neurodiversity movement has indeed won tangible victories, yet the radical promise of challenging neuronormative society seems increasingly contained within a framework that leaves the most marginalized behind. Chapman’s call to move beyond “Liberal Neurodiversity” toward a politics of “Neurodivergent Power” is a bracing and necessary provocation. It forces us to ask whether rights-based reforms and pride celebrations can ever be more than a pressure-release valve for the very system that disables us. But while I share Chapman’s anticapitalist intuitions, I worry that the proposed shift—from piecemeal inclusion to revolutionary confrontation—risks jettisoning strategic tools before they have been fully wielded, and may inadvertently sideline those neurodivergent people whose immediate survival depends on the flimsy protections that liberalism offers.

Chapman is right that liberal reforms are easily co-opted. The “superpowers” narrative, once a subversive reclamation, now circulates comfortably in corporate diversity manuals, recasting neurodivergence as an untapped resource. Yet we should not underestimate how profoundly this narrative has also transformed self-understanding for countless individuals. Before they can challenge capitalism, many neurodivergent people first need to believe that their minds are not simply broken. The pride movement, for all its accommodationist tendencies, has made that possible. To dismiss it as mere “Liberal Neurodiversity” risks shaming the very people who are just beginning to recognize their own worth. A radical politics need not reject pride; it can politicize it, linking individual celebration to a collective critique of the conditions that make such celebration so fragile.

Moreover, the legal framework of disability rights—flawed and unevenly enforced as it is—remains a lifeline for those who cannot afford to wait for revolution. Chapman notes that “reasonable accommodations” are interpreted to suit neoliberal priorities, and this is undeniably true. But for a trans person with ADHD facing workplace discrimination, the ability to file an ADA complaint, however imperfect, can mean the difference between housing and homelessness. In many countries outside the Global North, even these thin protections do not exist. A movement that abandons the fight for legal recognition in favor of building an anticapitalist vanguard may fail to meet people where they are: struggling to survive in the here and now. The lesson of the Black Power movement, to which Chapman insightfully compares our moment, is instructive. That shift toward militancy came *after* the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act had been won—and even then, many activists continued to use legal strategies alongside more radical ones. The neurodiversity movement has not yet secured a comparable baseline of rights globally. For many, the liberal project is still incomplete, not exhausted.

Chapman’s proposal for “neurodiversity studies departments” also gives me pause. Universities are thoroughly neoliberal institutions, and the creation of academic fields often leads to professionalization and depoliticization. The same elite capture that Chapman diagnoses in the “neurodiversity industry” could easily replicate itself within the academy, where tenure and research funding incentivize conformity. Might it be more radical to invest in community-led knowledge production—autistic-led mutual aid groups, mad pride collectives, online networks of peer support—rather than petitioning for a seat at the seminar table? The desire to unearth suppressed histories and develop political theory is vital, but the site of that work matters. A department can become a silo; a living movement, embedded in prisons, shelters, and precarious workplaces, can remain accountable to those most affected.

I share Chapman’s commitment to intersectional, anti-imperialist solidarity. The link between neurodivergence and the machinery of war, incarceration, and economic extraction is undeniable, and the movement must name these connections. However, there is a tension between building a broad anti-capitalist coalition and maintaining the specificity of neurodivergent experience. When we say that both neurotypicals and neurodivergents are harmed by the same system, we risk flattening the distinct ways that neuronormativity operates: it is not just general exploitation but the pathologization and segregation of particular cognitive and emotional styles. Any revolutionary politics must hold both the commonality *and* the particularity, avoiding a class-reductionist narrative that subsumes disability into a generic proletarian struggle. The goal, as Chapman implies, is not simply to include neurodivergent people in the existing workforce but to fundamentally reshape the rhythms, expectations, and values of social life. That requires a politics that keeps cognitive difference at the center, not as an afterthought to economic analysis.

Finally, the question of organizational form deserves more scrutiny. Chapman gestures toward the need for a “revolutionary party,” citing Jodi Dean. But many neurodivergent people have good reason to be wary of hierarchical, vanguardist structures. The very cognitive traits that lead us to be diagnosed—difficulty with rapid social cueing, intolerance for bureaucratic ambiguity, burnout from sustained executive demands—can make traditional party discipline alienating or inaccessible. The mass organizing of the future may need to look more like the decentralized, mutual-aid networks that flourished during the pandemic, which often accommodated disability in creative ways, than like a Leninist party. Neurodivergent Power should not replicate the neuronormative assumptions of previous leftist organizations.

None of this is to reject Chapman’s core insight: liberal reformism is a dead end if we do not cultivate a radical imagination. The neurodiversity movement does need to build power, not just accommodations, and it must do so in solidarity with decolonial, abolitionist, and anti-war struggles. But the path from here to there might require a both/and approach—fighting fiercely for the rights on the books while simultaneously building the counter-institutions and collective consciousness that can outlast capitalism. The future of neurodiversity lies in a dialectical tension: holding the imperfect gains of the present alongside a vision for total transformation, and never sacrificing one for the other. Only then can we refuse to be mourned, not just as individuals with a “way of being,” but as a collective force with the power to remake the world that disables us.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Robert Chapman correctly identifies a profound limitation in the contemporary neurodiversity movement: the neoliberal co-optation of cognitive difference. The popular discourse of neurodivergent "superpowers," while perhaps a necessary cultural corrective to decades of medicalized shame and stigma, ultimately demands that neurodivergent individuals prove their worth through capitalist productivity. Chapman effectively demonstrates how this paradigm—what they term Liberal Neurodiversity—abandons those who cannot be easily assimilated into the modern workforce.

As Chapman notes, a framework that only accommodates an autistic person so long as they can code for a tech firm, or a person with ADHD so long as their hyper-focus can be harnessed for corporate strategy, offers no true liberation. It engages in "extractive abandonment," leaving behind those with complex support needs, those experiencing psychosis, and those languishing in the carceral system. Rights-based accommodations often hinge on the word "reasonable," which under our current economic order typically translates to "profitable" or, at the very least, "not disruptive to profit."

However, while Chapman’s diagnosis of the problem is acute, their proposed prescription—a pivot to "Neurodivergent Power" explicitly modeled on the revolutionary, anti-capitalist, and anti-imperialist platforms of the Black Panther Party—is fraught with strategic and conceptual risks. In attempting to merge neurodiversity with a totalizing revolutionary agenda, Chapman risks subordinating the immediate, specific material needs of disabled people to a broader ideological project that may never arrive.

Chapman suggests reconstructing the project of the revolutionary party to fight global capitalism, imperialism, and the prison-industrial complex, arguing that neuronormativity cannot be defeated without defeating the global economic order. While intersectionality accurately demonstrates how these systems overlap to disproportionately harm marginalized neurodivergent people, demanding the overthrow of capitalism as a prerequisite for neurodivergent liberation sets an impossibly high bar. If our movement requires the dismantling of the entire global economic system before a nonspeaking autistic adult can secure safe, permanent housing and adequate daily care, we are effectively telling the most vulnerable among us that they must wait for a distant, hypothetical utopia.

Furthermore, the analogy to the Black Power movement, while historically resonant, overlooks the unique nature of disability and cognitive divergence. Black liberation rightfully sought to dismantle a socially constructed racial hierarchy that had no basis in biology. Neurodivergence, however, is not solely a socially constructed minority identity oppressed by a normative majority; it frequently involves intrinsic, embodied realities—what disability scholars call impairment—that require ongoing, specialized, and often highly individualized care.

A revolutionary political vanguard cannot unionize away the need for psychiatric support for severe schizophrenia, nor can an anti-imperialist coalition inherently eliminate the sensory agony some autistic individuals experience in crowded, overstimulating environments. The material realities of neurodivergence necessitate highly specific, resource-intensive accommodations and medical supports. These are arguably more efficiently negotiated and secured through targeted structural reforms and robust state interventions than through a generalized revolutionary class struggle, which historically has not always prioritized the needs of the cognitively disabled. When a movement makes everything its target—from the war in Gaza to the existence of prisons—it risks diluting its capacity to effect concrete change for its primary constituency.

Rather than abandoning the hard-won gains of rights-based advocacy for a revolutionary vanguard, the movement should lean into a robust, materially grounded disability justice framework—one that sits between liberal reformism and Chapman's revolutionary abolitionism. This means demanding structural changes that decouple human survival from labor productivity without necessarily requiring a complete socialist revolution. Policies such as universal basic income, universal single-payer healthcare, the abolition of subminimum wages for disabled workers, and massive public investment in community-based care networks directly address the material deprivations Chapman highlights. These are radical, transformative demands, but they remain concrete, policy-oriented goals that can be fought for within our lifetimes.

Chapman is absolutely right that the neurodiversity movement must stop settling for superficial inclusion in a punishing system. Dimmed supermarket lights, corporate diversity initiatives, and television representations of autistic savants are woefully inadequate for addressing the school-to-prison pipeline or the shocking mortality rates of people with intellectual disabilities. The neurodiversity movement must indeed build political power and recognize the systemic nature of cognitive oppression.

But the alternative to corporate co-optation need not be a sweeping, totalizing revolution that absorbs neurodiversity into every global grievance. Instead, we can build a fierce, unapologetic movement centered directly on the material survival and flourishing of neurodivergent people. We can demand a society where worth is not tied to economic output, where care is a fundamental human right, and where the specific, embodied realities of cognitive disability are met with abundant resources rather than revolutionary rhetoric. True neurodivergent power lies in forcing the world to sustain us as we are, here and now.

Robert Chapman is right that neurodiversity has reached a point at which vocabulary can outrun transformation. A company can celebrate “different minds” while refusing flexible hours; a university can publish an inclusion statement while making disabled students fight semester after semester for the same accommodations; a school can praise autism acceptance while suspending the child whose distress is read as defiance. The language of neurodiversity is now familiar enough to be marketed, and that familiarity creates a real danger: the movement’s most palatable claims—creativity, innovation, resilience—are absorbed, while its harder demands are ignored.

But the future of neurodiversity should not be framed as a choice between “liberal” rights and revolutionary power. The people most likely to be harmed by that opposition are often the very people Chapman wants to center: those with significant intellectual disabilities, people in institutions, people who need daily support, people without income, people in prisons, people whose communication is not easily legible to professionals or activists. For them, rights are not mere liberal decoration. They can be the difference between a segregated classroom and a public education, between forced institutionalization and community living, between unemployment and supported work, between medical neglect and enforceable care standards.

The problem is not that rights-based politics has gone too far. It is that it has usually not gone nearly far enough, and that rights without material support become hollow. The Americans with Disabilities Act did not make disabled people equal, but it changed the terrain on which disabled people could fight. The same is true of special education law, Medicaid-funded home- and community-based services, supported decision-making reforms, and the Supreme Court’s Olmstead decision affirming the right to receive services in integrated settings. These are imperfect, uneven, bureaucratic, and often humiliating systems. Yet they are also tools people use to get out of institutions, keep housing, obtain communication devices, challenge exclusion, and survive.

A more radical neurodiversity politics should therefore be additive rather than dismissive. It should ask: what would it take to make rights real? The answer is not only consciousness-raising, though that matters. It is public money, trained support workers, accessible housing, universal health care, income supports that do not punish people for working or marrying, transportation, respite, peer-run crisis services, non-police emergency response, and schools designed around regulation and communication rather than compliance. These are not glamorous demands, and they do not fit easily into either corporate diversity language or romantic images of revolt. But they are the infrastructure of freedom.

Chapman’s strongest point is that the “superpower” version of neurodiversity is too narrow. It reassures employers that autism may produce good coders, ADHD may produce energetic entrepreneurs, and dyslexia may produce visionary CEOs. Sometimes such reframing helps individuals shed shame. But as a political story it fails because it makes acceptance contingent on usefulness. It leaves out the nonspeaking autistic adult who needs round-the-clock assistance, the psychotic person who does not become more employable through positive branding, the child whose learning disability is not secretly genius, the person with traumatic brain injury cycling between homelessness and jail. A movement that cannot speak honestly about dependency, pain, ambivalence, and support needs will reproduce the hierarchy it claims to oppose.

Still, in rejecting deficit narratives, neurodiversity advocates should be careful not to create a new orthodoxy in which treatment, diagnosis, or even the wish for relief is treated as betrayal. Sinclair’s insistence that autism is not separable from the person remains morally important. But many neurodivergent people experience aspects of their conditions as disabling in themselves, not only because society is hostile. Severe anxiety, self-injury, catatonia, seizures, insomnia, executive dysfunction, addiction, depression, mania, or sensory pain are not made irrelevant by the social model. A liberatory politics should defend people against coercive normalization while also defending access to wanted care. The line between support and suppression is not always simple, but the answer cannot be to deny either side of the truth.

This is where the analogy to Black Power is illuminating but limited. “Neurodivergent Power” can name collective refusal: refusal of eugenics, of segregated institutions, of poverty wages, of carceral responses to distress, of workplaces that burn people out and discard them. Yet neurodivergence is not one people in the way Black Americans have been made a people through a shared history of racial domination. It is a broad and internally varied category, spanning autism, ADHD, dyslexia, intellectual disability, Tourette’s, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, epilepsy, trauma-related conditions, and more, depending on usage. Some neurodivergent people are disabled; some do not identify that way. Some want cure for particular symptoms; others fear cure as erasure. Some can organize publicly; others need advocates, family members, or support staff to make participation possible. A politics built in their name must be unusually pluralist.

That pluralism also requires taking families and caregivers seriously without returning to parent-dominated paternalism. Early autism politics rightly challenged organizations that spoke about autistic people while excluding them. But many people with high support needs rely on circles of care, including parents, siblings, attendants, teachers, and direct support workers. Those relationships can be controlling or abusive; they can also be the condition of agency. The slogan “nothing about us without us” remains indispensable, but “us” must include people who communicate unconventionally and people whose preferences emerge through trusted relationships rather than formal meetings, manifestos, or social media.

Chapman is also right that capitalism disciplines minds and bodies through productivity. But neurodivergent liberation cannot wait for capitalism’s end, nor can it be reduced to capitalism alone. State socialist societies have also institutionalized disabled people, medicalized dissent, and valued citizens according to productivity. Families, schools, clinics, prisons, and bureaucracies can be cruel under many economic systems. The deeper target is not only the market but any social order that grants dignity conditionally: if you work, if you behave, if you speak, if you recover, if you become manageable.

The future of neurodiversity should combine the impatience of radical politics with the specificity of disability policy. It should fight eugenic research agendas and also fight Medicaid waiting lists. It should oppose police violence and also build crisis alternatives. It should criticize corporate appropriation and also help workers get accommodations tomorrow morning. It should build culture, scholarship, unions, and mutual aid, while defending the legal levers disabled people already possess.

Neurodiversity began as a refusal to mourn the existence of people whose minds diverge from the norm. Its next stage should be a refusal to ration belonging. Not only the brilliant, not only the employable, not only the articulate, not only the proudly identified, not only those whose differences can be redescribed as gifts. Everyone. That will require power, yes—but also care, law, money, patience, and institutions remade so that needing support is no longer treated as a failure to be human.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Chapman's essay offers a compelling diagnosis of what he calls Liberal Neurodiversity's limitations, and many of his critiques deserve serious engagement. The analysis of how neurodivergent "strengths" can be mined for corporate profit while the most marginalized remain abandoned is particularly incisive. His observation that accommodations often fail those with complex psychiatric needs—where "reasonable" is determined by profitability rather than genuine inclusion—highlights a structural tension that reformist approaches struggle to address. Yet I want to push back on several aspects of his proposed alternative while affirming much of his underlying critique.

The Black Power analogy is instructive but imperfect. Chapman correctly notes that both the civil rights movement and neurodiversity activism had to grapple with co-optation and the limits of legal reform. However, the Black Power movement was never a unified tendency—it encompassed everything from cultural nationalism to Marxist-Leninist organizing, and many of its most lasting contributions came through institutional development rather than revolutionary rupture. The Black Panthers, whom Chapman discusses, built survival programs, community health initiatives, and educational institutions that outlasted the movement's peak. This suggests that neurodivergent liberation might similarly require building durable infrastructure—from neurodiversity studies departments to cooperative economic models—rather than simply pivoting toward a revolutionary posture that may prove premature in conditions lacking mass revolutionary consciousness.

More troubling is Chapman's treatment of intersectionality. While he invokes the concept, his framework tends to subordinate other axes of oppression to a neurodivergent-centered analysis. He acknowledges that "neurotypicals are often little better off," but this understates how race, class, and disability interact in ways that cannot simply be folded into a neuronormativity-focused analysis. The school-to-prison pipeline he describes disproportionately affects Black and Indigenous neurodivergent people, and addressing it requires sustained alliance with racial justice movements rather than merely drawing parallels to their struggles. Similarly, his discussion of Global South contexts risks treating neurodivergence as a universal category while ignoring how disability and neurodivergence are shaped by colonialism, extractive economies, and neocolonial medical systems in ways that exceed the neuronormativity framework.

Chapman's concrete demands—freeing incarcerated neurodivergent people, ending homelessness, halting imperialist wars—are admirable, but their relationship to neurodiversity specifically remains unclear. These are demands that virtually all progressive movements share. If Neurodivergent Power is to be more than a rebranding of broader anticapitalist politics, it needs to articulate what is distinct about neuronormativity as a system of domination. The essay gestures toward this with its discussion of "normal subjectivity," but this concept remains underdeveloped. What specifically constitutes neuronormative domination as opposed to ableism, capitalism, or other intersecting systems? Without this analytical clarity, Neurodivergent Power risks becoming a coalition label rather than a distinct political framework.

Finally, Chapman's dismissal of "non-hierarchical social movements" and call for a "revolutionary party" deserves scrutiny. The failures he identifies in the 2010s protest wave are real, but the alternatives he suggests carry their own risks. Parties—even ostensibly revolutionary ones—are prone to bureaucratism, exclusion, and the subordination of marginalized constituencies to supposedly universal programs. The history of twentieth-century revolutionary movements offers as many cautionary tales asinspirational ones. A neurodivergent politics worth building would need to grapple with this history rather than simply echoing demands for party organization that have recurred across radical movements without guaranteeing liberation for disabled people.

None of this negates Chapman's core insight: that individual accommodation within capitalism cannot constitute liberation, and that the neurodiversity movement must develop more collective, structural responses to the systems that produce neuronormativity. The movement needs both the insurgent energy Chapman advocates and the patient institutional building that has characterized other successful liberation movements. What remains to be developed—and what the movement might yet produce—is an analysis precise enough to distinguish neuronormativity's specific mechanisms while building alliances broad enough to challenge them.

The Diagnosis Is Not the Politics

Robert Chapman has written a stirring manifesto, and like most manifestos, its strength lies in its conviction rather than its precision. Reading it as a clinician who has spent two decades working with people diagnosed with autism, ADHD, schizophrenia, and intellectual disability, I find myself agreeing with much of the critique of "Liberal Neurodiversity" while balking at nearly every move Chapman makes toward something more ambitious. The trouble begins not at the level of strategy but at the level of category. Chapman wants to braid together a vast number of conditions — autism, ADHD, dyspraxia, bipolar disorder, psychosis, intellectual disability, the aftermath of war trauma in Gaza — into a single political subject called the "neurodivergent." But the people who fall under this umbrella do not share a common experience, a common etiology, a common prognosis, or, most importantly, a common interest. Treating them as if they do is not a radical move; it is a rhetorical one, and it imports problems that the movement has not begun to address.

Consider the gulf between, say, a software engineer with a recent autism diagnosis who finds the framework illuminating, and a nonverbal adult with profound intellectual disability and self-injurious behavior whose family has spent decades trying to find supported living that does not amount to neglect. These two people are both, in Chapman's vocabulary, "neurodivergent." But the engineer wants the world to accept her as she is; the nonverbal adult, who cannot articulate wants in the way the discourse demands, needs intensive, expensive, often medical care that the neurodiversity movement has historically been ambivalent about and sometimes openly hostile to. When Chapman writes that autism "cannot be treated or cured without erasing something essential about autistic identity," he is repeating Jim Sinclair's claim as if it were settled. It is not settled. It is contested by many parents, by many clinicians, and — crucially — by many autistic people themselves, particularly those whose disability is severe enough that the romanticization of cognitive difference reads as cruel.

This is the first place where I think Chapman's essay misfires. He notes, correctly, that "we hear little or nothing of the superpowers of those of us with more complex forms of disablement." But he treats this as a failure of representation rather than as a diagnostic problem for the entire framework. If your political vocabulary works only for the most able among the disabled, perhaps the vocabulary is wrong. The superpowers discourse is not just a co-optation by capital; it is a logical extension of an identity politics that built itself around the experiences of articulate, often relatively privileged self-advocates and then extrapolated outward. The Spectrum 10k boycott Chapman cites approvingly is a case in point: a successful campaign organized by autistic adults that may or may not have served the interests of families desperate to understand why their child has co-occurring epilepsy, GI dysfunction, and an IQ below 40. The political subject "autistic community" can speak for the engineer; whether it can speak for the child is a real question, and pretending it is already answered does not help.

The second misfire concerns medicine. Chapman gestures at antipsychiatry without endorsing it, and he wants to keep the strategic benefits of disability rights legislation, which means he needs diagnoses to remain real. But his framework treats medical categories almost entirely as instruments — sometimes useful for accommodations, sometimes useful for solidarity, sometimes a site of co-optation. What is missing is any sense that these categories track something about the world, that some of them correspond to conditions that cause real suffering independent of social arrangements, and that treating those conditions can be the most liberating thing a person ever experiences. I have watched people stabilized on lithium return to lives they had given up on. I have watched ADHD medication transform what a college student could do in a week. To frame these interventions primarily as concessions to "neuronormativity" is to mistake the experience of many of the people the movement claims to represent. There is a reason that the relationship between psychiatric service users and the neurodiversity movement has always been uneasy. Many people in long-term contact with mental health services do not want to be told their psychosis is a "way of being" they should celebrate; they want it to stop.

This brings me to the third misfire, which is the political analogy itself. Chapman invokes Kwame Ture and the Black Panthers and proposes that what neurodivergent people need is "Neurodivergent Power." The analogy is supposed to do enormous work — to authorize a militant, anticapitalist, party-based politics — but it strains under inspection. Black Americans in 1966 shared a coherent social position produced by centuries of specific oppression; they shared churches, neighborhoods, music, family histories, and a clear external enemy in white supremacy. Neurodivergent people, as Chapman defines them, share a contested set of diagnostic labels that did not exist in their current form a generation ago and that vary enormously across cultures and contexts. To propose a vanguard party organized around such a category is to propose something genuinely without precedent, and the burden of explanation is heavier than Chapman acknowledges. Who speaks for whom? On what basis? With what claim to represent the interests of people whose interests are, in many cases, directly opposed?

I want to be clear about what I am not saying. I am not saying that the neurodiversity movement has failed, or that the systemic critiques Chapman raises are wrong. The disproportionate incarceration of people with ADHD and autism is a scandal. The early deaths of people with intellectual disabilities are a scandal. The conditions of psychiatric inpatient units in much of the world are a scandal. The use of disability accommodations as instruments of profit extraction in the tech sector is real. These things deserve sustained political attention, and the rights-based framework Chapman calls "Liberal Neurodiversity" is genuinely inadequate to many of them. But the answer is not to scale up the same categorical confusion into a revolutionary politics. The answer is to be more honest, not more ambitious — to acknowledge that different conditions require different responses, that medicine and social change are not opposed, that some people need treatment, others need accommodation, others need housing and income, and many need all four.

A politics that begins from those concrete needs, organized through existing movements for housing, prison abolition, labor, and public health, would do more for the people Chapman cares about than a new vanguard organized around a contested category. The reason "Liberal Neurodiversity" has won the victories it has won — and they are real victories — is that it stayed close to the concrete. The way forward is to stay closer still.

A Comrade's Doubts About Neurodivergent Power

Robert Chapman is asking the right question. After three decades, the neurodiversity movement has reached the limits of what rights-based reform can do, and what remains is a difficult conversation about how political identities tied to medical categories interact with the basic structures of capitalism. I share Chapman's anticapitalism, share their alarm about the "extractive abandonment" that defines neoliberal disability policy, and share their conviction that intersectional solidarity — with Palestinians, with prisoners, with the homeless — is non-negotiable. And yet I want to register some comradely doubts about the framework of "Neurodivergent Power" the essay proposes. The doubts are about strategy, about the political subject, and about the historical analogy that does so much of the argumentative work.

Start with the analogy. Chapman draws a parallel between the proposed shift from "Liberal Neurodiversity" to "Neurodivergent Power" and the shift from civil rights to Black Power in the 1960s. The parallel is rhetorically effective but historically unstable. Black Power did not arise as a strategic upgrade to civil rights; it arose from a community with deep institutions — Black churches, HBCUs, mutual aid traditions, an actual press, decades of labor organizing, the long memory of slavery and Reconstruction — that already constituted a people in something close to the political sense. The civil rights movement won what it won because of those institutions, and Black Power was an internal radicalization of a constituency that already existed as a constituency. The neurodiversity movement does not have an analogous base. Its institutions are mostly nonprofits founded in the last twenty years, its "community" is largely online, and its categories of identity are objects of contestation even within the movement itself. To call for a "revolutionary party" of the neurodivergent is to call for the construction of something that has no popular base waiting for it.

This matters because revolutionary politics, when it succeeds, does so by articulating the felt experience of a class or a community in a way that politicizes existing solidarities. It does not invent solidarities out of theoretical categories. And here we should be honest: the category "neurodivergent" is theoretical. It groups conditions that have almost nothing in common — autism, ADHD, dyslexia, bipolar disorder, Tourette's, sometimes anxiety and depression, sometimes intellectual disability, in some recent usages even C-PTSD — under the loose umbrella of being "outside neurological norms." The grouping is useful for some purposes, particularly for arguing that a wide variety of cognitive differences should be respected. It is also enormously useful to employers and consultants, which is part of Chapman's concern. But as a basis for a revolutionary subject, it is brittle. The person with profound intellectual disability, the high-functioning autistic engineer, and the schizophrenic homeless man do not share a common interest that can be politically mobilized; they share an academic category.

What this means in practice is that "Neurodivergent Power" risks reproducing the very elite capture Chapman warns against. The people in a position to articulate, lead, and represent such a movement are precisely the most articulate — and they are disproportionately white, college-educated, and located in the relatively privileged sectors of the neurodivergent population. This is not a knock on them; it is a structural feature of any identity politics organized around a heterogeneous category. The Black Panthers had this problem too, but they had a community base that pulled against it. A movement built primarily around the discursive identity "neurodivergent" has no such ballast.

I think Chapman's instincts are better than the framework. The concrete demands at the end of the essay — free the incarcerated, house the homeless, end imperialist war — are demands shared with the broader socialist left. They do not require a separate vanguard organized around neurodivergence; they require neurodivergent people to be present, visible, and powerful within socialist and abolitionist movements that already exist. The same goes for the workplace fights Chapman mentions. Unionizing against the pace and norms of the neuronormative workplace is unionizing, full stop. Many neurotypical workers also experience these conditions as crushing, which is why disability accommodations historically have been won most effectively by union shops with strong floors and seniority protections, not by individual rights-based claims. The path forward is to build labor power and use it for everyone — including neurodivergent workers — rather than to construct a parallel structure that mirrors capital's tendency to fragment workers along identity lines.

There is a deeper theoretical point here that I think Chapman's essay flirts with but does not quite confront. If, as Chapman suggests near the end, "neurodivergent people are not oppressed by neurotypicals; rather, both neurotypicals and neurodivergents are harmed by our market-oriented system" — then the framework of neurodivergent identity is doing a lot of work that the framework of class would do better. The reason "reasonable accommodation" gets interpreted to mean "does not interfere with profit" is that capital sets the terms. The reason intellectually disabled people die young is that public health is rationed by class. The reason the school-to-prison pipeline disproportionately captures neurodivergent kids of color is the carceral state, which is itself an instrument of racialized class control. These are problems of political economy, and identity-based mobilization can illuminate but not fundamentally challenge them. Class can.

I am not saying neurodivergence should be abandoned as a political category. I am saying it works best as a lens, a way of seeing how capitalism produces and discards certain forms of life, rather than as the organizational basis of its own movement. The strongest moments in Chapman's essay are precisely the analytic ones, where they show how the production of debility in Palestine generates profits for arms manufacturers, where they connect autistic affect to the policing of acceptable responses to atrocity, where they trace the logic of extractive abandonment through ADHD employment statistics. This is the work of theory, and it serves the work of organizing in a much wider field.

So my response to Chapman is: keep the diagnosis, keep the critique, but rejoin the broader socialist movement rather than building a parallel one. The future of neurodiversity is not a neurodivergent party. It is a labor movement, a housing movement, a tenants' movement, an abolition movement, and a socialist movement in which neurodivergent people show up as themselves and bring the analytic clarity their experience affords. That, I think, is what Black Power, at its best, actually did — it did not separate from the broader left; it pulled the broader left toward a sharper analysis of empire and capital. We can do the same, without inventing a constituency that does not yet exist.

In Defense of Reform

Robert Chapman's essay performs a familiar move on the left: take a reform tradition that has won meaningful victories, label it "liberal" in a tone that suggests embarrassment, and then propose a more radical successor that will accomplish what the reformers could not. The successor is sketched in broad strokes — a revolutionary party, mass organization, cultural institutions, neurodiversity studies departments, an "intercommunal" politics modeled on the Black Panthers — and the suggestion is that this is what the moment requires. I am unpersuaded. The "Liberal Neurodiversity" Chapman wants to move beyond is, on the evidence Chapman themselves marshals, the most successful disability politics in American and British history. Before we declare it inadequate, we ought to be sure that what replaces it would do better, and on this question the essay is, to put it mildly, vague.

Consider what Chapman concedes. Reasonable accommodations under the ADA have transformed access to workplaces, schools, transportation, and public space for millions of people. The Autistic Self Advocacy Network helped end the practice of paying autistic workers below minimum wage and helped ban the use of electric shocks at the Judge Rotenberg Center. Cultural representations have shifted from tragedy and freakery toward more humane depictions. Sensory-friendly retail hours and airport sensory rooms now exist. Workplace coaching programs help neurodivergent adults find and keep jobs. Pride-based identity work has improved mental health outcomes for many. Each of these is a real change in a real person's life. The case against "Liberal Neurodiversity" cannot be that it has failed, because it has not failed; it has won, repeatedly, against tremendous odds. The case has to be that its victories are insufficient. And here is where the argument gets thin.

Chapman's central indictment is that the reformist movement leaves the most marginalized behind. This is true. It is also true of every successful reform movement in history. The expansion of LGBT civil rights left poor trans women of color disproportionately exposed to violence. The Civil Rights Act did not end the school-to-prison pipeline. The welfare state did not abolish poverty. Reform movements, by their nature, move outward from those best positioned to claim rights. The right question to ask is whether the alternative would actually reach the people the reformist movement has failed to reach. Chapman's answer is that a revolutionary anticapitalist movement would do so. But the historical record on revolutionary anticapitalist movements and their treatment of disabled people is, to put it gently, mixed. The Soviet Union institutionalized psychiatric patients on a massive scale and used psychiatric diagnoses against dissidents. Maoist China was hardly a paradise for the mentally ill. Cuba, often invoked as a counterexample, has its own complicated record. There is no historical example I am aware of in which a revolutionary movement organized around anticapitalism actually liberated the most cognitively marginalized people. The reformist tradition Chapman dismisses is the tradition that has, in practice, produced what improvements exist.

Then there is the question of the historical analogy. Chapman's invocation of Kwame Ture and the Black Panthers is meant to inspire, but the Black Panther Party is a peculiar model to recommend. The Panthers did real and admirable community organizing, but they were also crushed within a decade, partly by state repression but partly by their own internal contradictions, factionalism, and inability to sustain a political base. Their "revolutionary party" model did not transform American capitalism. The civil rights movement, including the legislative achievements Chapman implicitly disparages by calling them mere "antidiscrimination legislation," did more to change the actual conditions of Black life in America than the Panthers did. This is not to denigrate the Panthers' contribution to political consciousness; it is to ask whether the strategic lesson Chapman draws — that we should imitate the Panthers rather than the civil rights movement — is supported by the historical record. It is not.

I also want to push back on the framing of co-optation. Chapman writes that capitalism is "adapting to neutralize the mass of people fed up with its stifling norms and exclusions, using superficial moves toward greater inclusion." This is true. It is also what happens when a movement wins. The "strengths-based" discourse is partly a marketing veneer, but it is also a real shift in how millions of employers and educators think about cognitive difference, and that shift has material consequences for ordinary neurodivergent people trying to find work, get through school, and live with dignity. The choice between purity and co-optation is not really a choice; any successful movement gets absorbed into the structures it sought to change, and the question is whether the absorption represents net progress. For neurodivergent workers who can now get accommodations they could not get a generation ago, the answer is yes, even if the discourse around their accommodations is sometimes silly.

Chapman gestures at concrete demands at the end of the essay — free the incarcerated, house the homeless, end imperialist war. These are admirable goals, but they are not specifically neurodivergent goals, and the argument for why neurodivergent identity should be the organizing principle for pursuing them is not made. Homeless people need housing whether or not they are neurodivergent. Prisoners need decriminalization and decarceration whether or not they are neurodivergent. The war in Gaza needs to end for many reasons, only one of which is the disability it produces. To organize these struggles under the banner of "Neurodivergent Power" is to add a layer of identity politics that may or may not strengthen the underlying coalitions. Chapman seems to want both — a specifically neurodivergent movement and a broad anticapitalist front — but it is not obvious that the two are compatible.

The honest assessment, I think, is this: the neurodiversity movement has been remarkably successful at the things it set out to do, and like every successful movement it has produced its own limits, contradictions, and disappointments. Those disappointments do not invalidate the project; they are the project's growing pains. The right response is not to abandon the rights-based, accommodations-focused, representation-improving work of the last thirty years, but to extend it — to push for stronger enforcement of existing rights, to organize the workplaces where neurodivergent workers are most precarious, to build the legal infrastructure that lets people actually claim the rights they have on paper, and to ally with disability rights organizations doing the unglamorous work of housing, transportation, and long-term care.

Calling this "Liberal Neurodiversity" and contrasting it unfavorably with a not-yet-existing revolutionary alternative is a rhetorical gesture, not a strategy. Reform has worked. More reform will work. The future of neurodiversity is the patient, unfashionable continuation of the very tradition Chapman wants us to move beyond.

What About My Son?

I read Robert Chapman's essay carefully, twice, because I wanted to find my son in it. My son is twenty-three. He is autistic, intellectually disabled, and has a seizure disorder. He does not speak. He communicates through a combination of gestures, an iPad with picture cards he uses inconsistently, and behaviors that the clinical literature would call "self-injurious" and that I have come to understand as a kind of language. He requires support to dress, to eat, to bathe, to use the bathroom, and to keep himself from running into traffic, which he does when he is overwhelmed, which is often. He will need this support for the rest of his life. His father and I, in our late fifties, lie awake at night thinking about what happens when we die.

I am telling you this because the neurodiversity movement, in its public-facing form and in its more radical formulations like Chapman's, is not for him, and the longer the movement avoids reckoning with this fact the more harm it does. Chapman acknowledges the problem, briefly. They note that "multiply marginalized neurodivergent people are all too often left behind," that media depictions celebrate detectives and doctors rather than people in institutional care, that the "superpowers" discourse never quite reaches people like my son. Then they move on. The proposed solution — Neurodivergent Power, a revolutionary party, cultural institutions, consciousness-raising, neurodiversity studies departments — does not engage with what would change for him. It is hard to see how a neurodiversity studies department would help my son. It is hard to see how a revolutionary party would help my son.

Here is what would help my son: better-funded community-based support services, with workers paid enough to make caregiving a sustainable career rather than a revolving door of underqualified strangers. Decent group homes inspected by people empowered to close the bad ones. Medical care from doctors who understand that nonverbal patients still feel pain and still need diagnostic workups when something changes. Schools that do not call the police on dysregulated children. A legal framework for supported decision-making that does not force families to choose between guardianship (which strips legal personhood) and abandonment (which leaves a vulnerable adult exposed). Respite care so that aging parents can sleep. A plan for what happens after we die. Research, yes, research — into the co-occurring medical conditions, the GI problems and the epilepsy and the sleep disorders, that make life harder for my son and the many others like him, and yes, research into the causes of autism, because the question of why so many children are now affected is a legitimate scientific question and not a eugenicist plot.

This last point is where I part most sharply with Chapman, and where I want to push hardest. Chapman praises the boycott of Spectrum 10k and frames its goal as preventing the eugenic selection of autistic fetuses. I understand the fear. I share some of it. But the result of that successful boycott is that we know less about autism than we might have, and the families and individuals who would have benefited from that knowledge — many of them families like mine, dealing with profound autism and its medical comorbidities — were not the ones leading the boycott. The autistic activists who organized it were overwhelmingly people with a very different kind of autism than my son's, and they spoke on his behalf without asking, because he cannot be asked. This is the recurring pattern. When people who can write op-eds speak for people who cannot, the people who can write op-eds get to define what counts as the community's interest. And the interest they define is consistently one that prioritizes the social model — autism is fine, the world is the problem — over the medical reality that for some autistic people, autism brings serious, sometimes life-threatening medical conditions that they would, if they could, want addressed.

I want to be clear that I am not opposed to neurodiversity as a concept. I am grateful for the work it has done in shifting public attitudes. I am grateful that my son's nephew, who is autistic but verbal and bright, is growing up in a world that does not see him as a tragedy. I am grateful for the changes in employment and education that benefit people on the milder end of what used to be called the spectrum. But the movement's success at this end has come at a cost to the other end. The vocabulary of "different not less," "spiky cognitive profiles," and "superpowers" has made it harder, not easier, to talk about profound autism. Funders have shifted resources toward strengths-based research. Service providers have rebranded around inclusion in ways that quietly exclude the people who need the most support. Families like mine have learned to keep quiet at autism advocacy events, because mentioning that your son cannot use the toilet without help is treated as a kind of betrayal.

Chapman's call for a more radical politics does not address this. If anything, it intensifies it. The proposal to integrate neurodivergent struggle into anti-imperialist, decolonial, abolitionist movements is exciting if you are the kind of neurodivergent person who can attend a meeting, read a theoretical text, and articulate a position. It is irrelevant if you are my son. The structures of power being targeted — capitalism, the carceral state, empire — are real and worth opposing, but the political subjectivity assumed by the proposal is one my son does not possess and will never possess. He is not going to join a party. He is not going to develop a consciousness in the sense Chapman means. What he is going to do, if things go well, is live a life of small pleasures and meaningful relationships in a supportive environment, and that supportive environment is built by exactly the unglamorous, social-service, rights-based work Chapman dismisses as "Liberal Neurodiversity."

I want a politics of neurodiversity that includes my son. Such a politics would have to give up some of what the current movement holds dear. It would have to acknowledge that some forms of cognitive difference are, in fact, disabilities in a strong sense, not just socially constructed barriers. It would have to make room for parents and caregivers as legitimate political actors, not as the enemies the early movement positioned them as. It would have to support the kind of careful, ethical medical research that families like mine desperately need. And it would have to be honest about the limits of self-advocacy when many of the people most affected cannot self-advocate.

These are not radical demands. They are minimal ones. They are the ones a movement that actually wanted to include everyone would have to take seriously. Chapman's essay, for all its sweep and ambition, does not.

Against the Neuro

Robert Chapman wants a more radical neurodiversity politics. I want to ask whether the neurodiversity framework is the right vehicle for radical politics at all. My worry is not that Chapman is too revolutionary; my worry is that the prefix "neuro-" smuggles in exactly the kind of biologized, individualizing assumptions that a genuinely emancipatory disability politics should be trying to displace.

The neurodiversity movement made its peace, early on, with the idea that the differences it sought to defend were brain-based. "Neurological pluralism," in Harvey Blume's phrase. The framework's strategic genius was to take a medicalized vocabulary — the same vocabulary that had been used to pathologize — and reclaim it in the spirit of disability pride. Autism is in the brain, yes, but so what? The brain that produces autism is no worse than the brain that produces neurotypicality. This move did real work, and I do not want to dismiss it. But it has consequences that Chapman's essay does not fully reckon with.

First, by anchoring identity in the brain, neurodiversity discourse imports the assumptions of contemporary neuroscience — a field that is, despite its prestige, in serious methodological crisis, with replication failures across major findings and ongoing debates about the validity of its core categories. We do not actually have reliable neural signatures for autism, ADHD, dyslexia, or most of the conditions the movement defends. The "neuro" in neurodivergent is doing rhetorical work that the underlying science does not yet support. This is a problem for the movement's credibility, but more importantly it is a problem for the movement's politics. When researchers eventually pin down whatever heterogeneous biological processes underlie these categories, will the political identities survive? Will autism even remain a single thing? The history of psychiatric classification suggests it will not. Categories that seem stable today look quaint in a generation.

Second, the "neuro" framing tends to locate the difference inside the individual, even as the movement insists that disability emerges from the interaction between individuals and environments. The contradiction is structural. To say "I am neurodivergent" is to make a claim about one's brain. To say "society is neuronormative" is to make a claim about the social. The movement wants both claims at once, but the rhetorical center of gravity tends to drift toward the individual, because brains belong to people. This is why the discourse has been so easily absorbed by corporate diversity programs: the unit of analysis is already the individual employee with their distinctive cognitive profile, which is exactly the unit capitalism wants to manage.

Chapman sees this drift and tries to correct it by adding a structural anticapitalist analysis on top. But the structural analysis is grafted onto a fundamentally individualist categorical scheme, and the grafting does not take. You can see the strain in the essay's argument about Gaza. Chapman wants to describe the production of disability in Palestine as a "mass-disabling and neurodivergifying event," using the vocabulary of neurodiversity to politicize a colonial atrocity. But the violence done to Palestinians is not really illuminated by the framework of neurological difference. It is illuminated by the framework of colonialism, settler violence, and political economy. Calling Palestinians "neurodivergent" because they have been traumatized is at best a category error and at worst a kind of imperial appropriation, where a Western diagnostic vocabulary is extended over a situation it did not arise to describe and cannot really illuminate.

There is a deeper alternative available, and it is one Chapman alludes to but does not embrace. The disability studies tradition — particularly its more materialist strands, drawing on work by Marta Russell, Liat Ben-Moshe, and others — has long argued that disability is produced by political economy, that the category "disabled" is a category of those whose bodies and minds do not fit the labor demands of capital. From this angle, the question is not whether neurodivergent people deserve recognition for their cognitive differences, but how capitalism produces and discards human variation. The political subject is not the neurodivergent individual but the disabled class, in something close to a Marxist sense: those expelled from or marginalized within the wage relation by the structure of work itself.

This framework has several advantages over the neurodiversity framework. It does not require us to wait for neuroscience to validate diagnostic categories. It does not split the disabled population into the neurodivergent and the physically disabled in a way that disadvantages coalition. It does not get co-opted as easily, because its central claim — that capital itself produces disability — is harder to absorb into corporate diversity programs. And it generates political demands that are continuous with the broader left: universal income, decommodified care, public housing, decarceration, a labor movement that fights for shorter hours and a slower pace.

Chapman, I suspect, would not disagree with most of this. The concrete demands at the end of their essay are essentially these. But they arrive at these demands through the wrong door. By preserving the neurodiversity frame and trying to radicalize it, Chapman ends up with a politics that is both ambitious and oddly narrow: ambitious in its rhetorical scope, narrow in its political subject. A genuinely radical disability politics would let go of the "neuro-" prefix entirely, or at least demote it from the organizing concept to one descriptive vocabulary among several. It would organize the disabled, broadly construed, around the political economy that disables them, and would treat the question of which diagnostic categories apply to whom as secondary.

This is not to deny the importance of cognitive difference, or the lived realities that autistic, ADHD, and other neurodivergent people share with one another. It is to say that those realities are not the foundation of a political program; they are reasons to participate in one. The foundation has to be elsewhere — in the analysis of how value, labor, and care are organized, and how human variation is sorted into categories of usefulness and uselessness by an economic system that does not care about any of us.

Chapman's essay is closer to this analysis than most. But it stops short. The future it imagines is a more radical neurodiversity. The future I want is one in which the prefix "neuro-" is one tool among many, and the organizing principle is the disabled class — a class of which neurodivergent people are part, but not the center.

The Inflation of Identity

There is a particular kind of essay that has become familiar over the last decade. It begins by celebrating the achievements of an identity-based movement, pivots into a critique of how those achievements have been "co-opted" by capital, and concludes by proposing that what is needed is a more radical, anticapitalist version of the same movement, modeled on the Black Panthers or some other touchstone of 1960s radicalism. Robert Chapman's essay is an accomplished example of the form. Read closely, however, the essay raises questions that go beyond its own argument and bear on the broader expansion of identity categories in contemporary politics. I want to raise some of those questions, in a spirit of friendly skepticism, because I think the neurodiversity discourse is at a turning point and the direction it goes from here matters.

The first question is about the breathtaking expansion of who counts as neurodivergent. Twenty years ago, "neurodiversity" was a term used by a small number of autistic activists. Today, it covers autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette's, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, OCD, sometimes depression and anxiety, sometimes the effects of trauma. In some popular formulations, anyone whose brain does not produce a perfectly neurotypical experience — and who knows what that even means — can claim the identity. Diagnostic rates for autism and ADHD have risen severalfold in a generation. TikTok, as Chapman notes, has become a major vector for self-diagnosis. Whether this expansion reflects genuine progress in recognizing previously hidden conditions, a generation more attuned to its own psychology, or something more like a diagnostic fashion, is an open question. Chapman treats it as straightforwardly good. I am not so sure.

The reason I am not so sure is that political identities have a way of becoming less politically powerful as they expand. The Black Power analogy Chapman invokes is instructive here, but not in the way Chapman intends. Black identity in 1966 was rooted in a specific, historically produced social position. The Black Panthers could organize around it because it had a determinate content. "Neurodivergent" does not have a determinate content. It is a discursive category that has expanded to cover a substantial fraction of the population — by some estimates, fifteen to twenty percent of people in wealthy countries, and rising — and it includes such heterogeneous experiences that any political program built around it has to either remain extremely abstract or quietly privilege one constituency within the umbrella over others. Chapman's program does both. The abstract calls for liberation are universal; the concrete examples — autistic activists protesting eugenics research, ADHD adults navigating workplaces, autistic teenagers in Israeli military intelligence — privilege a particular kind of articulate, relatively able neurodivergent person.

The second question is about the relationship between identity and pathology. The neurodiversity movement has always wanted to have it both ways: these conditions are identities, not pathologies, and yet they should also count as disabilities for purposes of legal protection. This combination has been strategically clever — it has secured both pride and accommodations — but it is intellectually unstable, and Chapman's essay shows the strain. Chapman wants psychosis to count as a form of neurodivergence whose accommodations should include things like covering up workplace webcams to address paranoid concerns about government surveillance. Set aside whether this is a reasonable accommodation; ask instead what is being claimed. It is being claimed that paranoid delusions are not symptoms of an illness to be treated but expressions of an identity to be accommodated. Many psychiatric service users would reject this characterization of their own experience. They want their delusions to stop, not to be accommodated. It asks the autistic person to prove their need and the employer to judge whether that need is "reasonable"—a term that, as Chapman notes, is defined by capitalist logic. What if we shifted the framework entirely?

Universal design offers one model. Rather than accommodations that require individual proof of need, universal design creates environments that work better for everyone without requiring disclosure or negotiation. Quiet office spaces, for example, benefit not just autistic people but anyone who struggles with open-plan noise. Flexible scheduling helps neurodivergent people but also parents, caregivers, and anyone with medical needs. If we pursued universal design aggressively, we might reduce the need for individual accommodation requests—which are themselves a site of discrimination—while creating workplaces that are more humane for all.

Similarly, the employment statistics Chapman cites—22 percent of autistic people in paid work in the UK, 60 percent higher likelihood of being fired for adults with ADHD—are not inevitable. They reflect policy choices. Stronger anti-discrimination enforcement, sectoral bargaining that protects all workers from arbitrary firing, and robust social support that allows people to survive periods of unemployment would dramatically change these numbers. The problem is not that rights-based reform can't work; it's that the rights we have are too weak and too poorly enforced.

Chapman's discussion of the Global South is important but could be sharpened. Many countries do have disability rights legislation, but they lack the enforcement mechanisms, judicial systems, and administrative capacity of wealthier nations. International development funding that conditions aid on disability inclusion, capacity-building for Disabled People's Organizations in the Global South, and technology transfer for accessibility would address some of these gaps without requiring a global socialist revolution.

I am not dismissing the need for deeper structural change. But I am suggesting that there is enormous unfinished work at the level of policy and institutional reform—and that abandoning this work for a revolutionary horizon serves nobody well. The movement needs people pushing for incremental improvements and people imagining radical alternatives. What it doesn't need is the premature abandonment of the former in favor of the latter.

I want to offer a different kind of critique of Chapman's essay: one that engages with its political economy but reaches different conclusions about the proper response.

Chapman is right that capitalism co-opts everything, including neurodiversity. The "strengths-based" approach that reframes autistic people as untapped corporate resources is a perfect example. But I want to push back on the implicit assumption that this co-optation is a reason to abandon identity-based politics in favor of a more explicitly anti-capitalist framework. In my view, Chapman's proposed alternative—collective "Neurodivergent Power"—is actually more vulnerable to co-optation than what he dismisses as Liberal Neurodiversity.

Consider: what does it mean to build "Neurodivergent Power"? Chapman gestures toward unions and mass organization, but his concrete examples lean heavily on the Cambridge Spectrum 10k boycott and the analogy to Black Power. These are identity-based mobilizations, not class-based ones. The boycott was organized by autistic activists around an issue specific to autism. The Black Panther analogy assumes that neurodivergent people constitute a coherent group with shared interests that can be organized politically. But if neurodivergence is as internally diverse as I suspect it is, on what basis do we build collective power?

Chapman's answer seems to be that both neurotypicals and neurodivergents are harmed by capitalism, so the movement should be anticapitalist first and neurodiversity-specific second. But this is precisely the move that has repeatedly failed identity-based movements. The assumption that class struggle is the master struggle that subsumes all others has historically led to the marginalization or erasure of identity-specific concerns. If we build a movement premised on the unity of all workers against capitalism, won't the neurodivergent elements of that movement be subordinated to the neurotypical majority?

Anarchist traditions offer a different model. Rather than building "Neurodivergent Power" as a unified political force, we might build a multiplicity of neurodivergent organizing spaces—autistic-led groups, ADHD support networks, mad pride collectives—that federate when common cause can be found but maintain autonomy otherwise. This would be less vulnerable to co-optation than a single unified movement because there is no single point of capture. It would be more responsive to internal diversity because each group could organize around its own needs and desires. And it would build power through mutual aid and community building rather than through the construction of a political bloc that then negotiates with capitalism.

Chapman's essay is compelling but risks reproducing the same essentializing logic he critiques in Liberal Neurodiversity. He identifies neurodivergence with a unified political subject when the reality is more complex. The path forward is not "Neurodivergent Power" but neurodivergent pluralities.

I want to step back from the political economy that dominates Chapman's essay and focus on something that feels undertheorized in both the neurodiversity movement and its critiques: the question of representation and culture.

Chapman notes that popular representations of neurodivergent people tend to feature "white, middle-class" autistics, often with extraordinary abilities. Autistic detectives, bipolar CIA agents, autistic doctors: these are the figures through which neurodivergence enters mainstream culture. And Chapman is right that this selective representation distorts our understanding of neurodivergence, erasing the experience of multiply marginalized neurodivergents who don't fit these narratives.

But I think the problem goes deeper than Chapman acknowledges. The neurodiversity movement's embrace of representation as a site of struggle reflects a broader turn toward culture and identity in left politics that is not without its problems. Changing who appears on television and in films is important—but it doesn't necessarily change material conditions. And when representation becomes the primary measure of progress, it can obscure the fact that behind every "positive portrayal" of a neurodivergent person is a real person whose day-to-day existence may be unchanged by whether their condition is represented positively or negatively.

More troublingly, the focus on representation can become a way for institutions to appear progressive without doing the harder work of structural change. Universities can offer neurodiversity scholarships, corporations can recruit neurodiverse talent, media can tell inspiring neurodivergent stories—and all of this can happen while the actual conditions of the most marginalized neurodivergents remain unchanged or worsen. Representation becomes a cover for inaction.

This is not to say that representation doesn't matter. It does. The ability to see oneself reflected in culture, to have one's experience validated rather than pathologized, has genuine psychological and political effects. But we should be clear-eyed about what representation can and cannot accomplish. It can shift attitudes and build solidarity; it cannot by itself change material conditions.

What would it mean to take representation seriously as a site of struggle while remaining clear about its limits? Perhaps it means demanding representation that centers multiply marginalized neurodivergents—not just white autistics in prestigious professions, but Black autistics navigating incarceration, poor people with ADHD struggling to access healthcare, multiply disabled people whose conditions don't make for inspiring narratives. Perhaps it means interrogating the "inspiration porn" dynamic that celebrates individual neurodivergent success stories while leaving the structures that produce marginalization intact. Perhaps it means building neurodivergent cultural institutions—publishing houses, film festivals, community spaces—that don't depend on mainstream validation.

Chapman's essay is an important contribution to neurodiversity politics. But I worry that even his more radical framework risks leaving culture as a secondary concern, a reflection of "deeper" material structures rather than a site of struggle in its own right. A politics adequate to neurodiversity in 2024 needs to think seriously about both.
