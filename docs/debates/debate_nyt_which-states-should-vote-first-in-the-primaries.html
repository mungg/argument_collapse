Which States Should Vote First in the Primaries?

The nation’s first votes for presidential candidates are in states that are largely white and disproportionately rural — Iowa, which just held its caucuses, and New Hampshire, which will hold its primary on Tuesday.

Are they representative enough to warrant the outsize power they hold each campaign season? Which other states deserve to vote first in the primaries?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Iowa and New Hampshire's privileged position is indefensible on representational grounds. Iowa is roughly 91 percent white, New Hampshire 94 percent; together they contain almost no Latino voters, very few Black voters, and few of the dense, multiethnic metros where most Americans actually live. Yet candidates spend a year shaping platforms around ethanol subsidies and small-town diner politics, and the press treats narrow Iowa pluralities — Santorum in 2012, Huckabee in 2008 — as oracles. The Democratic coalition in particular is roughly 40 percent nonwhite; letting two of the whitest states in the country winnow that field is a structural anachronism.

The remedy, though, isn't simply swapping in California or Texas. Big states reward only money and television, eliminating the retail politics that lets long-shot candidates be tested. A better system would pair a small, diverse state (Nevada or New Mexico) with a small, working-class one (perhaps South Carolina or Michigan's Upper Peninsula region), then rotate regional primaries thereafter so no state holds permanent veto power. Iowa and New Hampshire have earned their organizational expertise, but expertise isn't entitlement. A nominating calendar that begins where America actually looks like itself would produce nominees better matched to the November electorate.

The ritual is as familiar as it is undemocratic: every four years, presidential hopefuls spend a year trudging through Iowa’s county fairs and New Hampshire’s town halls, genuflecting before two of the nation’s least representative electorates. The question of who votes first is not merely procedural—it shapes policy priorities, winnows fields, and often anoints front-runners before most of the country has uttered a single preference. If the goal of a nominating process is to produce a candidate who reflects the party’s coalition and can govern a diverse republic, Iowa and New Hampshire should not be the permanent gatekeepers.

The demographic distortions are glaring. Both states are over 90 percent white, whereas the Democratic primary electorate in 2008 was roughly one-third nonwhite, and the nation itself is far more heterogeneous. Iowa’s caucuses, conducted in an overwhelmingly rural setting, overrepresent a sliver of the base: older, more ideological activists who reward candidates fluent in agricultural subsidies and retail politicking. New Hampshire’s primary, with its libertarian streak and lack of large urban centers, presents a similarly narrow lens. The issues that dominate early coverage—ethanol mandates, the “first in the nation” pieties—bear little resemblance to the concerns of metropolitan voters, African Americans, Latinos, or the working poor who form critical blocs in November.

A more defensible sequence would move states that more accurately mirror the party’s rank and file to the front of the calendar. For Democrats, Nevada and South Carolina already offer a corrective, but they arrive third and fourth, after momentum has hardened. Nevada, with its large Latino and union populations, urban dynamics in Las Vegas, and western libertarian-conservatism, is a microcosm of emerging electoral battlegrounds. South Carolina’s primary—where African Americans cast a majority of Democratic ballots—tests a candidate’s ability to speak to the party’s most loyal constituency. Front-loading these contests would force aspirants to build genuinely multiracial coalitions from the start, rather than relying on a head start among homogeneous electorates and hoping minority voters coalesce later.

Republicans, too, would benefit from a reshuffling. South Carolina’s socially conservative, military-heavy GOP electorate is far more representative of the national Republican base than Iowa’s caucus-goers, who have a history of elevating insurgents with niche appeal. A first contest that included Sun Belt states with growing diversification, like Arizona or Georgia, would pressure candidates to address immigration and economic mobility in ways that resonate beyond the Upper Midwest.

The deepest flaw is entrenchment itself. Any state that consistently goes first accumulates disproportionate power in perpetuity. A superior model would rotate the early window among several states eager for the responsibility—perhaps through a lottery system that groups states by region and demographic profile. This would prevent single-state industries from developing around the primary calendar and compel candidates to campaign broadly from day one. The current order is a historical accident, not a sacred design. It is time to retire it and let the full diversity of the country shape the choice of nominees.

Tomorrow's New Hampshire primary, following last week's closely watched Iowa caucuses, once again highlights a recurring flaw in our presidential nominating process: the disproportionate influence of two largely white, rural states. The United States in 2016 is a highly diverse, predominantly urban and suburban nation. It is true that Nevada and South Carolina currently follow Iowa and New Hampshire specifically to inject Latino and African American voices into the early process. The argument that Iowa and New Hampshire wield too much influence rests on a valid concern about demographic representation, but the critique oversimplifies what makes these states useful gatekeepers. The real question isn't whether these states mirror the nation's diversity—they clearly don't—but whether they test qualities that predict electoral success.

Retail politics rewards candidates who can actually connect with voters face-to-face. In small, retail-focused states, name recognition and money matter less than organizational depth and genuine persuasion skills. A candidate who can't build a coalition in Iowa, where voter turnout requires sustained personal commitment, likely lacks the organizational muscle to win in November. This isn't nothing; it's a meaningful filter.

That said, the current system needs reform. Giving four or five states permanent outsized influence creates distortions. Candidates tailor their messages to a narrow demographic, and voters in the vast majority of states participate in what amounts to coronation ceremonies after the race is effectively decided.

A regional rotation system would preserve the retail-politics benefit while improving representativeness. Dividing the country into regions—Northeast, Southeast, Midwest, West, Southwest—each holding early primaries on a rotating basis would expose candidates to diverse electoral environments. A candidate might face a union-heavy Midwestern primary, then a Sun Belt Hispanic voters primary, then a Northeastern suburban primary. This would better test a candidate's ability to build different coalitions.

South Carolina's recent addition demonstrated that the system can diversify. Adding Nevada, with its diverse union electorate, further helped. But incremental adjustments leave us with an awkward patchwork. A structured rotation would be more systematic and more fair.

The states voting first don't need to be perfectly representative. They need to test skills that translate to national victory. The current selection fails that test on representativeness without fully serving the practical function either. A regional rotation would better achieve both goals.

Iowa and New Hampshire's privileged position is a historical accident that has hardened into an indefensible status quo. Together these two states are about 91% and 94% non-Hispanic white, respectively, in a country that is roughly 62% non-Hispanic white. Their economies—corn and ethanol in Iowa, small manufacturing and retail politics in New Hampshire—bear little resemblance to the service-and-tech economies that employ most Americans. The strongest case for reform is democratic equality. A vote in the Iowa caucuses is worth, in attention and influence, perhaps fifty times a vote in California's June primary. That disparity has no principled justification. The defenders of the current order argue that small states force candidates to engage in "retail politics," shaking hands at diners rather than buying television ads. But retail politics in two demographically narrow states is not a virtue if it systematically filters out candidates who appeal to the actual Democratic and Republican coalitions.

New Hampshire even has a statute requiring its secretary of state to schedule the primary a week before any "similar election." Before we tear down Iowa and New Hampshire, it is worth asking what they actually do for the nominating process. The answer, I think, is more than critics admit.

Presidential primaries serve two functions. They aggregate voter preferences, and they test candidates. The first function is best served by a large, diverse electorate. The second is best served by a small, attentive one. You cannot vet twenty candidates in a national primary; voters in California or Texas would never meet most of them, and the contest would collapse into a name-recognition and money race. Iowa and New Hampshire, whatever their demographic deficiencies, force candidates to answer unscripted questions from voters who have heard the stump speech four times and are not impressed. Senators and governors who cannot do this get weeded out. That is a public service.

The demographic critique is real but overstated. Yes, both states are overwhelmingly white. But the early window now includes Nevada and South Carolina, which together include large Latino, African American, and union constituencies. By the time a candidate has run that gauntlet, they have been tested in front of a reasonably broad cross-section of the party coalition. Hillary Clinton's struggles with Black voters in 2008, and Bernie Sanders's current effort to broaden beyond a white liberal base, have been exposed precisely because South Carolina exists.

What about the claim that Iowa picks the nominee? It doesn't. Recent Iowa Republican winners include Mike Huckabee, Rick Santorum, and Ted Cruz—none of whom became the nominee. Iowa winnows; it does not anoint. New Hampshire has a better track record but has also blessed insurgents (Buchanan, McCain twice) who flamed out.

I would accept modest reform: rotating Iowa and New Hampshire's positions with two other small states from different regions every cycle, so the privilege is shared. But replacing them with a national primary or with Florida and California first would simply hand the nomination to whoever raises the most money the earliest. The current system, for all its flaws, allows underfunded candidates a fighting chance. Throw that away carelessly and we'll regret it.

The framing of this debate misses the real problem. The question is not which two or four states should go first, but why we let any two states dominate the calendar at all. The American primary system is a Rube Goldberg machine, jury-rigged over decades by parties trying to balance grassroots input, elite control, and state pride. There is no constitutional reason it has to look this way.

Consider a different design: a national primary day, perhaps in May, preceded by three months of nationally televised debates with rotating regional emphasis. Every voter would cast a ballot at the same time. The argument against this is that it would advantage well-known and well-funded candidates. Fair enough. So design around it: cap primary spending, provide candidates with a modest federal stipend or free broadcast time once they cross a petition threshold, and use ranked-choice voting so an obscure but broadly acceptable candidate can prevail over a polarizing plurality winner.

Or try the "American Plan" that political scientists have floated for years: ten two-week windows, each adding states with progressively larger populations, with the order randomized each cycle. Small states would still go first—giving the retail-politics virtue some breathing room—but no single small state would always go first.

Or use a rotating regional primary, with four regions and the leadoff region changing every four years.

Any of these is better than what we have, because any of these distributes attention, vetting, and influence more evenly. The current calendar is defended by people who benefit from it: the Iowa and New Hampshire political classes, the consultants who specialize in those states, the journalists who have built sources there, and the ethanol lobby. The interests it serves are not democratic interests.

The real obstacle is collective action. Both parties have to move together, or one state defects and the cascade resumes. But the RNC and DNC have coordinated on calendar rules before (the "window" rule, penalties for jumping the queue). They could do it again if their leadership decided that demographic representativeness and democratic equality matter more than tradition. So far, they have not.

Let's be blunt: the reason Iowa and New Hampshire go first is that they have written laws and party rules saying they go first, and the national parties have lacked the spine to override them. New Hampshire's statute literally requires its primary to precede any other state's. That is not a democratic principle; it is a legislative power play, enforced by the threat of a chaotic race to January if anyone tries to leapfrog.

The most interesting fact about the Iowa-and-New-Hampshire debate is how stable it is. Every four years, the same critique surfaces, the same defenses are mounted, the same conclusion is reached: nothing changes. This suggests that the explanation for the calendar is not principled but structural, and reformers who do not engage the structure will keep losing.

Three structural facts maintain the status quo. First, the parties are federations of state parties, and state parties guard their prerogatives jealously. Any reform that demotes Iowa or New Hampshire requires their state parties to accept demotion, which they will not. Second, the penalties for violating the calendar are imposed by national party committees on which the offending states are represented; enforcement is selective and political. Third, candidates have no incentive to challenge the system because the candidates running this cycle need Iowa and New Hampshire votes; the candidates who might benefit from reform are not yet candidates.

So what would actually change the calendar? Probably a crisis. If a candidate clearly disliked by the party establishment won both Iowa and New Hampshire and threatened to win the nomination on the strength of those two contests, the establishment might finally agree to dilute their influence. Or if demographic shifts within the parties made the disconnect between early states and the coalition so glaring that activist groups demanded change, the DNC in particular might act. The Latino vote share in the Democratic coalition is rising every cycle. At some point, the contradiction between that fact and an Iowa-and-New-Hampshire leadoff will become politically untenable.

In the meantime, the most achievable reform is incremental: expand the early window to include more demographically representative states, shorten the gap between contests so Iowa and New Hampshire have less time to dominate the narrative, and impose harsher delegate penalties on states that leapfrog. Full rotation is a worthy long-term goal but probably requires a precipitating event the parties have not yet had.

The debate over which states should go first is therefore less a debate about democratic principle than about when, and under what pressure, the parties will finally act on a principle they already accept.

Iowa and New Hampshire are often derided as pale, rural anachronisms that distort our presidential primaries. Yet this criticism misses the profound democratic value embedded in their first-in-the-nation status. These states do not serve as a demographic mirror of America; they serve as a crucible. The intimacy of their contests forces candidates to submit to relentless, face-to-face scrutiny in living rooms, diners, and town halls. There is no place to hide from an undecided voter who has shaken your hand three times and still has questions. This process tests not only a candidate’s message but also their character, stamina, and ability to build grassroots organization—skills that translate directly to a general election.

History is replete with underdogs who used this terrain to prove their mettle. Jimmy Carter, a one-term Georgia governor, camped out in Iowa and turned relentless retail politicking into a nomination. Barack Obama’s 2008 Iowa win signaled that a black candidate with a message of hope could build a multiracial coalition even in a predominantly white state. These were not flukes; they were stress tests that national polls could never replicate. The early contests reward authenticity and punish phoniness in ways that media-driven campaigns in larger states cannot.

Critics point to the lack of racial diversity, but representation is not the sole purpose of a primary calendar. They also function as information-gathering mechanisms, revealing which candidates can organize in different environments, appeal to different constituencies, and survive retail political combat. They provide visibility to underdogs who cannot compete on advertising spend alone. They force candidates to travel the country and learn about diverse communities, not just fundraise in New York and LA.

Different states accomplish these goals differently. Iowa and New Hampshire excel at filtering candidates on organizing ability and grassroots energy. Nevada adds crucial information about diverse electorates. South Carolina provides insight into Black voter engagement critical to Democratic success. A well-designed calendar would sequence these informational functions to maximize what we learn before the field narrows.

My proposal: a permanent five-state early window rotating quarterly. First: Nevada. Second: New Hampshire. Third: South Carolina. Fourth: Iowa. Fifth: a Midwest state—Wisconsin or Michigan—that adds industrial-economy diversity to the mix. Then the rest follow. Every four years, a different region moves up.

This approach respects what each state uniquely offers while preventing any single state's quirks from permanently distorting the process. It maintains small-state influence for retail organizing while adding genuinely diverse voices to the early window. Most importantly, it produces nominees who must demonstrate appeal across multiple American realities before securing their nomination—not just the ability to win a quirky caucus in a 91%-white state.
