Are Social Networks Just a Fad?

Social media have changed the way people interact: young women in India protect their marriageability by being discreet on Facebook, while in the U.S. family members often feel the sting of learning about estranged loved ones through a screen. These networks are not just personal, of course; they are also political. Thomas Friedman recently wrote that although Facebook and Twitter may have been able to ignite a revolt in Egypt, they have failed to supply the “brick and mortar” needed to sustain real change.

Are social media part of a long-term shift in how people interact, like the telephone? Or, especially after Facebook’s worrisome I.P.O., do these networks look like a fad that will become less attractive the longer it lasts?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The framing of the question — telephone or fad? — is a false binary that confuses a specific company's stock price with a deeper shift in human communication. Facebook's botched IPO tells us something about Morgan Stanley, Nasdaq's order systems, and the difficulty of pricing mobile advertising. It tells us almost nothing about whether people will keep using social networks.

Consider what has already happened. Roughly 900 million people use Facebook monthly. Twitter has become standard infrastructure for journalists, politicians, and emergency responders. LinkedIn has quietly rewired professional hiring. Weibo in China, VKontakte in Russia, Orkut's long run in Brazil — the pattern repeats across very different cultures and regulatory regimes. That is not the signature of a fad like Beanie Babies or even Second Life. It is the signature of a general-purpose communication technology finding its level.

The better historical analogy isn't the telephone specifically but the broader pattern: new media that lower the cost of staying in loose touch with many people at once never seem to get un-invented. Postcards, the telephone, email, SMS — each was once derided as trivial chatter, and each became permanent. Social networking does something none of those quite did: it makes weak ties persistent and searchable. That is a genuinely new affordance, and the early sociology (boyd, Ellison, Hampton, Rainie) suggests people value it.

What is faddish is any particular platform. Friendster fell, MySpace collapsed, and Facebook will eventually be displaced or transformed — perhaps by mobile-native services we're already seeing in Instagram and WhatsApp. The category is durable; the brands are not. Confusing the two is the real analytical mistake.

The rush to declare social networks a passing fad confuses platform volatility with a fundamental shift in human communication. Facebook’s rocky IPO and the inevitable churn of trendy apps obscure a deeper truth: the connective tissue these networks have built is unlikely to dissolve anytime soon. The telephone analogy is apt. Early skepticism about the device’s utility faded as it became an invisible, essential layer of daily life. Social media are following the same trajectory, embedding themselves into everything from dating to disaster response.

Consider the behavior already routinized by mid-2012. Hundreds of millions of people, across continents, now maintain ambient intimacy with broad circles of friends, family, and acquaintances. They learn of births, deaths, and breakups through a news feed—often before a private phone call. This is not a novelty seeking to be replaced; it is a new literacy. The fact that an Indian woman curates her Facebook persona to navigate traditional marriage markets, or that an American feels wounded by a public estrangement, proves the profound and permanent stakes these platforms now carry. They have become sites where social consequences are genuinely felt, not merely playgrounds for status games.

The political dimension reinforces this permanence. Thomas Friedman correctly notes that a tweeted revolution cannot build institutions, but he misidentifies the role of the tool. The telephone did not write legislation either, yet no one dismisses it as irrelevant to governance. Social networks have proven to be extraordinary instruments for rapid mobilization, bearing witness, and shattering state monopolies on information—a capacity that movements from Tahrir Square to Occupy Wall Street have permanently weaponized. That they cannot, alone, sustain a new government does not make them a fad. It confirms they are a powerful new layer of social infrastructure, one that will be as enduring as it is incomplete.

Equating Facebook’s notoriously rocky I.P.O. with the demise of social networking is a category error. Wall Street's valuation struggles have little to do with the fundamental utility of digital connection. Social media is not a fleeting cultural trend like the CB radio; it represents a structural shift in human communication, akin to the telephone. While individual platforms will inevitably rise and fall—as Friendster and Myspace already taught us—the underlying architecture of the social web is now permanently embedded in our daily lives.

This permanence is largely driven by the rapid adoption of the smartphone. We no longer sit at a desk to "log on" to the internet; we carry it in our pockets everywhere we go. Social networks have become the default infrastructure for sharing news, maintaining distant relationships, and organizing civic life. While Thomas Friedman is right that a hashtag cannot govern a country in the wake of the Arab Spring, or build the "brick and mortar" of lasting democratic institutions, criticizing social media for failing to do so misses its true function. The telephone didn't build institutions either, but it fundamentally altered how human beings coordinate.

As we move forward, the sheer novelty of "friending" and broadcasting status updates will undoubtedly wear off, just as the magic of making a long-distance phone call eventually did. But replacing a novelty with everyday utility is the exact opposite of a fad. Social networks will simply become an invisible, essential utility.

The question itself betrays a category error. "Social networks" are not a product like the Pet Rock or planking; they are a technological capability — the ability to maintain a persistent, searchable, many-to-many connection with the people in your life. That capability is not going anywhere, any more than email or text messaging is going anywhere.

What can absolutely be a fad is any particular company. Friendster was a fad. MySpace was, in retrospect, a fad. Facebook may well prove to be a fad in the corporate sense — its IPO last month suggests a company whose growth assumptions are wobblier than its underwriters claimed, and whose mobile strategy is conspicuously underdeveloped. Twitter could be eclipsed tomorrow by something we haven't heard of yet. Foursquare may not exist in five years.

But conflating the fate of Facebook the stock with the fate of social networking the behavior is a mistake people keep making. When Pets.com collapsed, pundits declared e-commerce a fad; Amazon would like a word. The platforms churn; the underlying human practice — sharing one's life with a chosen audience through a screen — only deepens.

The more interesting question is what kind of long-term shift we are looking at. The telephone analogy is apt in one respect: like the telephone, social media collapse distance and become invisible infrastructure. But the telephone was a one-to-one medium that left no trace. Social networks are one-to-many and permanent. That difference is what generates both the marriageability anxieties in India and the awkward family discoveries in the U.S. that the prompt mentions. Those are not bugs of a passing craze; they are the early adjustment pains of a society learning to live with a new medium of record. We will be adjusting for decades.

Facebook's IPO should give us pause, but not for the reasons the financial press fixated on. The botched debut, the insider selling, the sagging share price — these tell us something about Wall Street's appetite for hype, not about whether ordinary people will keep posting baby photos.

Still, I want to push back on the easy consensus that social media are obviously here to stay. Three reasons for skepticism.

First, the engagement numbers are softer than they look. Anyone who has watched a teenager knows that "being on Facebook" increasingly means lurking, not posting. The site is becoming a utility for keeping tabs on people, not the bustling town square the company sells to advertisers. Utilities are useful, but they are also boring, and boring services are vulnerable to displacement.

Second, the business model is fragile. Display advertising on social feeds has never performed as well as search advertising. General Motors pulled its Facebook ads days before the IPO. If the ad money does not eventually flow, the lavish free service we take for granted will have to change — and the version users will tolerate paying for, or being more aggressively monetized inside, may not be the version they actually want.

Third, fatigue is real. Privacy gaffes, the awkwardness of friending coworkers and exes, the realization that an offhand comment can sink a job application — these frictions accumulate. The Indian bride and the estranged American relative in the prompt are not testimonials; they are warning signs.

None of this means we go back to landlines and Christmas cards. But the specific shape of today's social web — public-by-default, identity-tethered, ad-supported, dominated by one or two giants — is more contingent than its boosters admit. Calling it a fad is too strong. Calling it a stable equilibrium is too generous.

Thomas Friedman's brick-and-mortar metaphor is worth dwelling on, because it captures the political question more honestly than the "fad versus revolution" framing.

The danger is the opposite of faddishness. It is that we mistake the ease of expression for the achievement of change. Signing an online petition feels like doing something. Retweeting an outrage feels like activism. If the lasting legacy of social networks is a generation that confuses visibility with power, that is not a fad — it is a problem.

Ask the wrong question, get the wrong answer. "Fad or telephone?" assumes the only two options are trivial novelty and permanent fixture. The more useful frame is: what stage of the technology adoption curve are we on, and what does maturity look like?

We are, I would argue, somewhere around the end of the beginning. Roughly half the connected world has a social media account. What maturity will look like is the interesting question. A few predictions.

Identity will be portable. We will look back on the era of separate accounts for every service the way we look back on dial-up.

None of these shifts make social media a fad. They make today's particular configuration transitional. The underlying behavior — networked self-presentation — is as durable as the desire to be known.

I want to defend the unfashionable position: a lot of what we currently call social media really is a fad, and we will be embarrassed about it in ten years.

Not all of it. Messaging your friends is not a fad. Sharing photos with family is not a fad. Maintaining a professional contact list is not a fad. These are durable uses, and some platform will always serve them.

But consider what else "social media" currently encompasses. Tweeting your breakfast. Checking in at a coffee shop so an app can award you a digital mayorship. Curating a public persona for an audience of acquaintances you would not invite to your home. Performing outrage for strangers. Watching teenagers stage their lives for Instagram likes. Listening to executives talk about "engagement" as if attention were a virtue rather than a finite human resource.

Much of this is the behavior of a culture intoxicated by a new toy. Every communications technology has had its giddy phase. Early telephone users made prank calls and held parties around the receiver. Early television produced quiz show scandals and the belief that the medium would solve illiteracy. CB radio in the 1970s briefly convinced America it was the future of community. The giddy phases pass; what remains is the boring useful core.

The IPO matters here because it is a marker. When a behavior gets monetized at $100 billion, every incentive in the system pushes to amplify the silly parts — the constant notifications, the gamified engagement, the manufactured FOMO — because those drive the metrics that justify the valuation. Reality eventually reasserts itself. Users get bored or burned, advertisers notice the clicks do not convert, the stock corrects, and the culture moves on to a quieter, more useful equilibrium.

That equilibrium will include social networking. It will not look much like 2012 Facebook.

Two things can be true. Social networking, as a human practice, is here to stay. Social networking, as a specific cluster of consumer products dominated by one or two American firms circa 2012, is probably not.

The case for the practice is overwhelming. Roughly 900 million people use Facebook. Twitter has changed how news breaks. LinkedIn has changed how people get jobs. Weibo has changed what is sayable in China. These are not surface phenomena; they touch courtship, employment, politics, and grief. You cannot put that back in the bottle.

The case for skepticism about the current products is also strong. The history of consumer internet is a history of incumbents who looked invincible and were not. AOL owned online socializing in 1999. MySpace owned it in 2006. The half-life of dominance in this sector is measured in years, not decades.

If they mean "will my grandchildren maintain online networks of friends," the answer is almost certainly yes. If they mean "will Facebook be the dominant such network in 2030," the honest answer is: probably not, and the people betting on it at last month's offering price may learn this the hard way.

The behavior is the telephone. The companies are the carriages.
