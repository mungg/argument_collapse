Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Thomson's central move — shifting the burden of justification onto those who would constrain a deeply valued liberty — is powerful, but it proves more than she may want. The standard she articulates ("severe constraints on liberty may not be imposed in the name of considerations that the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting") is doing enormous work, and it is worth asking whether it can bear the weight.

Consider an analogue. Many people are not unreasonable in rejecting the claim that nonhuman animals have morally significant interests, or that distant strangers have welfare claims on us comparable to our neighbors'. Does it follow that laws restricting cruelty or mandating foreign aid contributions impermissibly "exercise force" against dissenters? Thomson's principle threatens to dissolve any moral legislation about which reasonable disagreement persists.

What Thomson needs, and only gestures toward, is an asymmetry argument: that pregnancy uniquely burdens women's bodies and life prospects in ways that distinguish abortion regulation from ordinary contested moral legislation. Her closing appeal to equality hints at this, but it is precisely the argument she made famous in 1971 — bodily non-conscription — that does the real work, not the epistemic claim about reasonable disagreement.

I share her conclusion. But conceding, as she does, that fertilized eggs may have a right to life cannot then be neutralized by an epistemic principle alone. The bodily-integrity argument remains indispensable.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s essay is an important contribution to a debate in which nuance too often gives way to slogan. She rightly insists that violence against clinics is indefensible, that abortion restrictions impose real burdens on women, and that much anti‑abortion rhetoric oversimplifies the moral landscape. Yet her central argument against restrictive abortion laws—that they cannot be justified because the claim that the fetus has a right to life from conception is not one that women are unreasonable in rejecting—is less secure than she suggests. By setting the justificatory bar so high, Thomson makes it virtually impossible for any democratic polity to legislate on deeply contested moral questions, and she overlooks the more modulated reasoning that has shaped American abortion law since *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*.

Thomson’s case rests on three claims: that restrictive regulation severely constrains women’s liberty; that severe constraints on liberty may not be imposed in the name of considerations the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting; and that women who reject the doctrine of fetal personhood from conception are not unreasonable. She concludes that laws restricting abortion amount to nothing but an exercise of force, because the ground offered—the fetus’s right to life—cannot justify the constraint to those who reasonably withhold assent.

The first and third premises are largely persuasive. Few would deny that abortion restrictions limit women’s freedom, often grievously. And Thomson is on firm ground when she argues that the proposition “a fertilized human egg has a right to life from the moment of conception” is not rationally compelled. Reasonable people can and do reach divergent conclusions about the moral status of the early embryo. But the second premise—the claim that a liberty may not be constrained on grounds the constrained are reasonable to reject—is far more radical than Thomson acknowledges, and it cannot bear the weight she places upon it.

If we apply that principle consistently, much of contemporary law would become suspect. Animal‑cruelty statutes, for instance, severely constrain the liberty of those who wish to use animals in ways the law forbids. Many people who oppose such restrictions do so on the ground that nonhuman animals lack the moral standing the law ascribes to them. Are they unreasonable? A utilitarian who thinks that only sentience matters and that the aggregate benefits of certain animal uses outweigh the suffering might reasonably reject the animal‑rights view. Yet no one suggests that animal‑welfare laws are therefore an unjust imposition of force. The reason is that democratic societies regularly balance liberty against other values, including the majority’s considered judgment about the moral status of certain beings or practices, even when that judgment is not immune to rational challenge.

The same holds for abortion. Under *Casey* (1992), the Supreme Court affirmed the “essential holding” of *Roe v. Wade*—that a woman has a constitutional right to choose an abortion before viability—but it also recognized that the state has legitimate interests from the outset of pregnancy in protecting the potentiality of human life. The joint opinion in *Casey* explicitly states: “The State has legitimate interests from the outset of the pregnancy in protecting the health of the woman and the life of the fetus that may become a child.” This interest exists even if one does not accept the strong claim that the fetus is a full rights‑bearing person from conception. It is an interest grounded in what the Court called the “profound” and “moral” concern for fetal life, a concern that is not unreasonable simply because some citizens reject it.

Thomson’s argument, by contrast, seems to require that any restriction on abortion be justified solely by the full personhood of the fetus—a ground she shows is not rationally mandatory. But the actual legal framework does not demand such a stringent showing. *Casey* permits regulations that express respect for prenatal life so long as they do not impose an “undue burden” on a woman’s right to choose. A 24‑hour waiting period, for example, may be constitutionally permissible because it does not place a substantial obstacle in the path of a woman seeking an abortion, even if it is adopted in part out of a belief that the fetus has moral significance that is not yet equivalent to a right to life. Thomson dismisses such measures as “forced contemplation,” but a legislature that believes the decision to end a potential human life deserves a period of reflection is not necessarily imposing an unreasonable view; it is acting on a moral intuition that many people—including some who are pro‑choice—find coherent: that as pregnancy progresses, the moral gravity of abortion increases.

Thomson might reply that even if the state’s interest in fetal life is legitimate, the crucial question is whether the constrained are unreasonable in rejecting the particular ground on which a restriction is based. She insists that a woman who gives no weight whatever to the fetus’s claim at conception is not unreasonable. But in a pluralist polity, laws are rarely justified to every citizen’s satisfaction. What matters is whether the state can offer a public reason that is not itself unreasonable. The *Casey* Court did just that: it grounded the state’s interest not in a sectarian religious doctrine about ensoulment at conception, but in a broadly accessible concern for the developing human organism. One can disagree with that concern—many do—but it is not irrational to hold that a human embryo, though not yet a person, has a value that society may seek to protect through incremental regulation. If that were irrational, then a great deal of environmental law, which protects species or ecosystems for their own sake, would also fall.

Thomson avoids this difficulty by focusing entirely on the “right to life from conception” claim and treating any weaker form of fetal valuation as a concession that would make most abortions permissible. But that move sidesteps the more common anti‑abortion position in the political arena: namely, that the fetus’s moral status grows over time, and that the state may accordingly limit abortion in ways that reflect that growing status. Indeed, the data Thomson herself cites—that half of U.S. abortions occur within the first eight weeks—suggests that a regime permitting early abortion while restricting later ones (as *Casey* allows) might be a practical compromise. Her insistence that the real debate must be about the all‑or‑nothing claim from conception may be philosophically clarifying, but it does not engage the legislative realities that have shaped the post‑*Roe* landscape.

In the end, Thomson’s principle—that a liberty may not be constrained on grounds the constrained are reasonable to reject—would, if taken seriously, rule out much democratic lawmaking. It would require that every statute imposing a severe burden on anyone be justifiable to that person in terms she cannot rationally reject. That is an impossibly high standard, one that would paralyze government in a society marked by deep moral pluralism. It is also a standard that the Supreme Court, in *Casey*, notably did not adopt. Instead, the Court sought a workable accommodation between competing values, recognizing that the Constitution protects individual liberty while allowing the community to express its moral concern for fetal life. Thomson’s essay is a powerful reminder of what is at stake for women, but it does not offer a persuasive reason to abandon that fragile accommodation. A democracy that cannot legislate on the basis of moral convictions that are widely held yet not universally shared is a democracy that has ceded its capacity for moral judgment altogether.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Judith Jarvis Thomson makes an elegant pivot in her defense of abortion rights, shifting the burden of proof from the metaphysical status of the fetus to a principle of political justification. She argues that because the claim of fetal personhood from conception can be reasonably rejected, the state cannot use it as a justification to severely constrain women’s fundamental liberty and equality. By framing the issue this way, she attempts to bypass the intractable biological and theological stalemates surrounding conception.

However, Thomson’s standard for state coercion falters when applied to the foundational question of who belongs to the moral community. Her argument relies on a social contract framework: the state may only impose severe constraints on liberty based on reasons that the governed cannot reasonably reject. But the fetus is precisely the entity whose status as a member of the governed—a rights-holder—is the very subject of the debate. If a society genuinely disagrees over whether a certain class of beings possesses human rights, the state cannot resolve that dilemma simply by defaulting to the liberty of the undisputed rights-holders. Doing so does not remain neutral; it functionally decides the moral question against the contested class.

Furthermore, Thomson argues that it is not contrary to reason to deny rights to a fertilized egg because it lacks psychological “interests”—specifically wants, hopes, and fears. Yet this cognitive criterion for rights is itself highly contestable. If rights require conscious psychological desires, what logically protects the severely cognitively impaired or even the newborn infant? An infant in its earliest days operates largely on reflex and instinct, arguably lacking the complex psychological states Thomson identifies as the prerequisite for being wronged. If society secures rights for the newborn based on its inherent human nature or its continuous trajectory toward conscious life, it is certainly not unreasonable for opponents of abortion to trace that same biological continuity back to its genetic origin.

In the wake of the Supreme Court’s 1992 decision in *Planned Parenthood v. Casey*, our legal regime has settled into a tenuous compromise, balancing maternal liberty with the state's interest in potential life. But we should not pretend this compromise resolves the philosophical tension. When opponents of abortion assert that a fetus is a rights-bearing person, they are making a claim about basic justice. Dismissing this claim merely because it is "reasonable to reject" misunderstands the dispute; it is a profound disagreement about who gets to count in the calculus of justice in the first place.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Judith Jarvis Thomson is right to insist that the abortion debate cannot be conducted as if the interests of women were incidental. Pregnancy is not a minor inconvenience, and compulsory pregnancy is not simply a refusal to subsidize a choice. It is the use of law to require one person’s body to sustain developing life, at serious physical, emotional, social, and economic cost. Any honest moral argument about abortion must begin there. It must also begin, as Thomson does, by condemning the campaign of murder, threats, arson, and humiliation directed at clinics, physicians, staff, and patients. A movement that calls itself “pro-life” cannot treat terror as a regrettable excess at its margins while continuing to speak as though doctors are executioners and women their accomplices.

But Thomson’s argument is strongest when she is criticizing the claim that reason compels belief in fetal personhood from conception; it is weaker when she suggests that, because that claim is reasonably rejectable, it cannot justify legal restriction. Many laws rest on moral premises that reasonable citizens reject. Pacifists may reasonably reject the justice of a war and still be taxed to support it. Libertarians may reasonably reject redistributive taxation and still be compelled to pay. Religious citizens may reasonably reject divorce and still live under laws that permit it; secular citizens may reasonably reject certain obscenity laws and still be subject to them. The fact that a moral premise is disputed, even reasonably disputed, cannot by itself remove it from democratic consideration.

Still, abortion is not like every other contested question. Thomson’s emphasis on liberty has special force because the liberty at stake is bodily liberty. The state may ask much of citizens, but it ordinarily does not compel one person to place her internal organs, blood, health, and future at the disposal of another. Even when a child will die without a kidney, the law does not force a parent to donate one. We may think the refusal cruel; we do not call in the sheriff. That fact does not settle every abortion question, but it does show why the burden on those who would criminalize abortion is unusually heavy. They are not merely protecting what they regard as life; they are conscripting women’s bodies in that protection.

For that reason, the most persuasive defense of abortion rights need not deny all moral significance to fetal life. Thomson focuses on the fertilized egg because the strongest anti-abortion argument begins there. She is right that a fertilized egg cannot simply be declared a rights-bearing person by invoking its genetic code. A skin cell has human DNA; a frozen embryo has potential; neither fact alone establishes moral equality with a newborn child. Yet many people who reject personhood at conception also believe that fetal development matters morally. A pregnancy at seven weeks, at twenty weeks, and at the edge of birth are not morally identical merely because none is a born child. Consciousness, sentience, viability, bodily form, and the woman’s own deepening relation to the pregnancy may all matter, even if none supplies the simple answer that combatants desire.

That is why the framework preserved in Planned Parenthood v. Casey remains morally attractive despite its imperfections. Before viability, the woman’s liberty must be paramount; after viability, the state may give greater legal weight to potential life, while preserving exceptions for the woman’s life and health. This compromise will not satisfy those who believe abortion is always murder, nor those who believe the fetus has no independent moral standing until birth. But constitutional law often works by refusing absolutism. Casey’s “undue burden” standard is admittedly imprecise, and some states will use that imprecision to harass rather than inform. Waiting periods, biased counseling, and parental-consent rules can become instruments of obstruction, especially for poor women and rural women. But the basic structure—strong protection early, increasing state authority later—reflects the moral reality better than either total prohibition or total indifference.

Thomson is also right to challenge the rhetoric of certainty. The encyclical’s claim that abortion is murder from conception is not merely a private devotional statement; it is a public accusation. If accepted, it brands millions of women as killers and physicians as assassins. Those who make that accusation cannot be surprised when some unstable adherent concludes that lethal force is justified to stop lethal force. This does not mean that every opponent of abortion is responsible for every act of violence, and it would be unjust to say so. But public language has foreseeable effects. To call abortion murder while insisting that murderers must not be stopped by violence requires a moral discipline that the current climate plainly lacks.

At the same time, defenders of abortion rights should avoid their own evasions. It is too easy to speak as though abortion were only a medical procedure, no different from removing a cyst. That language may protect women from stigma, but it can also sound like a refusal to acknowledge loss, ambiguity, and moral seriousness. Many women experience abortion as a grave decision even when they are confident it is the right one. A humane pro-choice politics should make room for that gravity without surrendering liberty.

The better response to the present crisis is therefore not simply to assert a right, but to build the conditions in which fewer women face desperate choices: real sex education, contraception, prenatal care, child care, health insurance, income support, protection against rape and domestic violence, and respect for women’s work and autonomy. Those measures will not end the abortion conflict, because the deepest disagreement is about moral status. But they would expose a great deal of anti-abortion politics as punitive rather than protective. A society that criminalizes abortion while neglecting poor mothers, battered women, and unwanted children is not reverencing life. It is enforcing pregnancy.

The legal right to abortion should be defended, especially in early pregnancy, because the case for coercing women is not strong enough to overcome their bodily liberty and equal citizenship. But that defense should be candid: abortion is sometimes tragic, often serious, and always bound up with responsibilities that men and institutions too often escape. Thomson has done a service by showing that the charge of murder cannot simply be assumed. The next task is to defend women’s freedom in a language that recognizes both the moral weight of pregnancy and the greater moral danger of forced motherhood.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Thomson's essay offers a sophisticated defense of abortion rights, but several of its central arguments rest on questionable premises that deserve scrutiny.

Thomson argues that the asymmetry between those who want to impose severe constraints on liberty and those who want to exercise it gives the latter an advantage in moral and legal debates. This asymmetry argument is compelling in the abstract, but it depends entirely on whether the constrained party has a legitimate claim that warrants respect. If anti-abortion advocates genuinely believe that fetuses have rights, then their position is not simply an imposition of preferences but an attempt to protect what they take to be the rights of an innocent party. The asymmetry argument assumes that fetal rights claims are unreasonable, but that is precisely what is contested. Thomson acknowledges this at several points, but her argument cannot simultaneously assume that fetal personhood is an open question and that those who assert it are therefore the ones who bear the burden of justification.

Thomson is right that reason does not compel acceptance of the claim that fetuses have rights from conception, and she is right to challenge the encyclical's assertion that this doctrine is written in every human heart. But her treatment of this point is incomplete. The fact that a moral claim is not compelled by reason does not mean it is groundless or that reasonable people must reject it. Thomson herself notes that there is no conclusive reason for denying that fertilized eggs have rights, which means that reasonable people could accept the claim. If reasonable people can accept it, then the asymmetry argument becomes less powerful: those who would restrict abortion might be appealing to considerations that, while not demonstrably true, are not unreasonable. The practical upshot is that democratic deliberation might be the appropriate forum for resolving this dispute rather than judicial fiat.

This brings me to Thomson's defense of judicial review in the abortion context. She argues that vindicating fundamental liberties is exactly what courts are supposed to do, and that there can be no principled objection to the settlement of the abortion issue by the judicial branch rather than the legislative. This is too quick. The question of what counts as a fundamental liberty is itself a contested one, and in a democracy, the allocation of decision-making authority between courts and legislatures is a structural matter that cannot be resolved simply by noting that courts sometimes protect rights. Thomson acknowledges that had Roe v. Wade not been decided, access to abortion would likely be more restricted than it is, and she seems to think this justifies the Court's intervention. But this utilitarian calculation about outcomes does not address the principled objection that unelected judges should not override democratic majorities on contested moral questions. Thomson gestures toward a rights-based conception of judicial review, but she does not adequately address how we determine which rights claims are sufficiently established to warrant judicial enforcement against democratic majorities.

Thomson's critique of the claim that anti-abortion advocates really believe only that killing fetuses is intrinsically bad rather than a rights violation is strong. Her reading of the papal encyclical as genuinely asserting that fetuses have rights is persuasive, and her critique of Dworkin's interpretation exposes a real weakness in that approach. But her positive account of the metaphysics of rights is less secure. She argues that having rights might not presuppose having interests after all, and that even if it does, this does not show that fetuses lack rights. This is philosophically interesting, but it amounts to saying that the question is open. If the question is genuinely open whether fetuses have rights, then Thomson cannot use the premise that they lack rights to justify unrestricted abortion access. What she can argue is that because the question is open, those who would restrict liberty must meet a high justificatory burden—and she does make this argument effectively.

Her most compelling point is that severe constraints on liberty may not be imposed on grounds that the constrained are unreasonable in rejecting. This is a powerful constraint on legal policy in a pluralistic society. But even here, there is work to be done. Thomson assumes that women who reject the claim that fetuses have rights are not unreasonable in doing so, which she has argued, but she has not argued that women who accept the claim that fetuses have rights are unreasonable in doing so. If both positions are reasonable, then the question of how to structure legal policy in a way that respects both sets of reasonable convictions becomes more complex than her argument suggests. A regime that permits early abortion while restricting later abortion, or that permits abortion in certain circumstances but not others, might be the appropriate way to respect both fetal rights claims and maternal autonomy, rather than either unrestricted access or complete prohibition.

Thomson's essay is most persuasive when she reminds us that women who seek abortions typically do so for weighty reasons, that anti-abortion rhetoric has contributed to real violence, and that those who reject the fetus-has-rights claim cannot be dismissed as morally deficient. These observations are important for maintaining moral seriousness in the debate. But they do not settle the question of what legal policy is appropriate in a society where reasonable people disagree about the moral status of the fetus. Thomson's argument points toward a strong presumption against restricting abortion, but the strength of that presumption depends on assessments of reasonableness that her argument does not fully vindicate.

**The Asymmetry Argument Cuts Both Ways**

Professor Thomson's essay is, as always, lucid and forceful. Her central move is ingenious: rather than try to refute the claim that fertilized eggs have a right to life, she concedes she cannot refute it, and then argues that this doesn't matter. What matters, she says, is that those who deny the claim are not unreasonable in doing so, and severe constraints on cherished liberties cannot be imposed on grounds the constrained can reasonably reject. The supporter of restriction wants to "impose force"; the denier merely wants to be "free of it." The burden lies entirely on the side seeking restriction.

This is elegant. It is also, I think, too quick. The asymmetry Thomson invokes—force versus freedom—is doing enormous work, and once we examine it, we find it does not align as neatly with the abortion question as she suggests.

Consider how the argument generalizes. Whenever person A wishes to do something that person B claims harms a third party C, A can describe the situation as "I want to be free; B wants to impose force on me." But whether this description is apt depends on whether C exists as a being with moral standing—which is exactly the contested question. If C does have standing, then A is not merely seeking freedom; A is seeking license to harm C, and B's "imposition of force" is in fact protection of C. Thomson's framing simply assumes the answer to the contested question in order to allocate the burden of proof.

Take an analogous case. Suppose some citizens sincerely believe that newborns lack the cognitive sophistication required for moral standing, and that infanticide in the first month is therefore permissible. Suppose, further, that we cannot give a knockdown philosophical argument against them—and indeed, philosophers like Michael Tooley have made sophisticated cases that border on this view. Would Thomson then say: the would-be infanticidal parents seek freedom, the protectors seek to impose force; therefore the burden lies with the protectors? Surely not. The asymmetry between "force" and "freedom" only generates the conclusion she wants if we have already settled that no rights-bearing third party is at stake.

Thomson might reply that her argument requires only that the denier be "not unreasonable." But this standard is suspiciously weak. Many positions on contested moral questions are "not unreasonable" in the sense that intelligent people hold them and cannot be refuted by pure logic. If "not unreasonable" disagreement suffices to defeat any restriction on a "deeply valued liberty," then a great deal of our moral and legal life dissolves. The cruelty of certain treatments of animals, the permissibility of dueling, the obligations we owe future generations—on all these matters reasonable people disagree, yet we do not infer that no restrictions may be imposed.

There is something else worth noting. Thomson grants—remarkably—that she has no conclusive reason for denying that fertilized eggs have a right to life. She grants that the opponent's view "makes perfectly good sense" and "cannot be bypassed as nonsense." Having conceded this, she nonetheless concludes that the law must proceed as if the opposing view has no purchase at all in framing legislation. But if the question is genuinely open, oughtn't the law reflect that openness? A more honest conclusion of Thomson's premises might be: we should regulate abortion the way societies regulate other genuinely contested moral questions—through democratic compromise, with attention to the gravity of what may be at stake.

This, in fact, is roughly what Casey attempts, with its undue-burden framework and its allowance of state interest in potential life. The framework is messy, but its messiness reflects the real moral situation Thomson herself has described: a question we cannot definitively resolve, with serious interests on both sides. Thomson's "asymmetry" argument, by contrast, lets us pretend the question is closed when she has just told us it is not.

I share Thomson's conviction that the right to abortion should be protected, especially in early pregnancy. But the protection cannot rest on the rhetorical trick of treating contested moral claims as equivalent to no claim at all. It must rest on a substantive moral argument about the developing fetus, the bodily integrity of women, and the distinctive burdens of pregnancy. Thomson herself supplied such an argument, brilliantly, twenty-four years ago in "A Defense of Abortion." It is striking that in this essay she has retreated from substantive moral argument to a procedural one about the allocation of burdens. The retreat, I suspect, weakens rather than strengthens the case for abortion rights.

**What the Encyclical Actually Says**

Thomson's essay deserves a response from someone who actually accepts the teaching of *Evangelium Vitae*, since most of what will be written about her piece will come from those who share her starting assumptions. I am a Catholic, and I believe what John Paul II writes: that the deliberate killing of an innocent human being from conception onward is gravely wrong. Let me try to say why Thomson's argument, for all its sophistication, does not move me—and why I think it should not move my fellow citizens either.

First, the matter of Dworkin. Thomson is right that Dworkin badly misreads opponents of abortion when he says we don't really mean what we say. We do mean it. The encyclical means it. When the Holy Father writes that procured abortion is "the deliberate killing of an innocent human being," he is not employing pious hyperbole that secretly amounts to "intrinsically regrettable." He is making a claim about what is happening on the operating table. I am grateful to Thomson for taking this seriously, even as she denies the claim. It is a more honest engagement than we usually receive.

Second, Thomson's argument about "rights presupposing interests" turns out, as she herself concedes, to be inconclusive. She knows of "no conclusive reason for denying that fertilized eggs have a right to life." Note what this concession costs her. She has built her entire political conclusion on the supposed reasonableness of denying fetal personhood, but her own analysis cannot establish that the denial is correct, only that it is not unreasonable. Yet she then claims that her opponents' position can be set aside in framing law. This is a remarkable maneuver: a view she cannot refute philosophically becomes one that may be disregarded politically.

Third, and most importantly, I want to challenge Thomson's central political principle: that "severe constraints on liberty may not be imposed in the name of considerations that the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting." Stated this way, the principle sounds modest. But consider what it implies. Slavery was once defended on grounds that abolitionists rejected; were abolitionists therefore forbidden from "imposing" emancipation? Child labor was permitted on grounds reformers rejected; were reformers forbidden from "imposing" labor laws? The protection of the weak against the powerful has always required law to act on contested moral premises. Indeed, the entire civil rights tradition that Thomson presumably honors required the federal government to impose, on Southern whites who genuinely disagreed, a moral vision they regarded as alien. The "constrained" can almost always claim that they are not unreasonable in rejecting the premise of the constraint.

Because the deepest intuition driving abortion opponents—the intuition that a tiny, vulnerable being is being destroyed—is not going to be argued away. The principle that bodily integrity cannot be commandeered for the benefit of another is one of the deepest principles of our moral and legal tradition. Pregnancy is, among other things, the most demanding possible use of one person's body for another's sake. **Reason, Religion, and the Public Square**

Thomson disclaims any reliance on the separation of church and state. Her objection to abortion restrictions, she insists, is not that they would be religious in character but that they would constrain a deeply valued liberty on grounds the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting. I want to suggest that despite this disclaimer, her argument is in fact deeply tied to a particular vision of public reason—and that this vision, while attractive, has costs she does not fully reckon with.

The argument runs roughly as follows. Severe constraints on liberty require justifications that the constrained cannot reasonably reject. The claim that fertilized eggs have a right to life is one that opponents of abortion restrictions can reasonably reject. Therefore restrictions on abortion that rest on this claim are unjustified.

This is a recognizable variant of the Rawlsian conception of public reason that has been developed in *Political Liberalism* and subsequent work. In a pluralistic society, citizens owe one another reasons that all could in principle accept, drawn from a shared political conception of justice rather than from any particular comprehensive doctrine, religious or secular. Coercive law especially must satisfy this standard.

I find much of this attractive. But there are real difficulties when we apply it to abortion specifically.

First, the symmetry problem. Thomson tries to break the apparent stand-off by saying that one side seeks to impose force and the other seeks freedom from it. But this characterization is contestable. From the perspective of someone who believes the fetus has interests of its own, the law that permits abortion is not neutral—it is a positive license to harm one party in the interest of another. Such a person could equally well argue that the burden of justification falls on those who would permit, not those who would prohibit. The framing depends on a prior judgment about which party is the rights-holder.

Second, the reasonableness problem. Thomson's argument relies heavily on the notion that abortion opponents' core claim—that fertilized eggs have rights—can be reasonably rejected. But "reasonable rejection" is doing massive work here, and Thomson does not fully theorize it. By her own admission, she cannot refute the personhood claim; she can only say that denying it is "not contrary to reason." But on this standard, almost every contested moral claim can be "reasonably rejected"—including claims her own argument relies on. Is it "contrary to reason" to deny that severe constraints on liberty require special justification? Is it "contrary to reason" to deny that women's equality requires access to abortion? Surely not. Yet Thomson presents these as nearly self-evident premises.

Third, and most seriously, Thomson's framework risks effectively privatizing all controversial moral convictions, leaving only a thin shared morality to do the work of justifying coercive law. This is a familiar critique of Rawlsian public reason, and I think it has force. If religious citizens, or citizens with strong philosophical convictions about the moral status of nascent human life, are told that they cannot bring these convictions to bear on questions of law, they will reasonably feel that the deck is stacked. Their deepest moral commitments are ruled out of bounds at the door, while the convictions of their opponents (about autonomy, equality, the limits of state power) are admitted as the lingua franca of public discourse. This is not religious neutrality; it is a particular substantive vision masquerading as neutrality.

The Pope's encyclical, whatever else one thinks of it, makes its case in moral and philosophical terms, not by appeal to revelation. The encyclical argues, for instance, that the continuity of biological development from conception is itself a reason to treat the fertilized egg as morally significant. One can reject this argument, but one cannot fairly dismiss it as a sectarian intrusion into public reason. It is a philosophical argument, and it deserves a philosophical response.

This brings me to what I think is the genuinely valuable contribution of Thomson's essay. Her treatment of Dworkin is excellent. We owe them an engagement with the substantive moral question. Why do animals matter morally? Why do infants? Why might a fetus be relevantly similar to or different from these? What is the moral significance of the genetic, developmental, and biological continuities that anti-abortion arguments emphasize? These are hard questions, and they cannot be answered by reframing them as questions about whose ox is being gored by state coercion.

A democracy genuinely committed to liberty must also be genuinely committed to engaging the moral disputes that liberty raises. Thomson's procedural turn, however well-intentioned, threatens to substitute for that engagement rather than further it.

But courts also need constitutional warrant, not merely philosophical confidence. Roe v. Wade announced a national settlement more detailed than the Constitution’s text, history, or structure could comfortably support. Casey improved the rhetoric by speaking of liberty and equality, but it too left many citizens feeling that an immense moral question had been removed from ordinary politics by judicial decree.

Thomson replies that legislatures would likely have protected far less access. That may be true. Yet the fact that democratic politics would produce worse results does not automatically authorize courts to produce better ones. Otherwise constitutional law becomes moral emergency government by judges. Liberals should be wary of that principle, because courts will not always be liberal.

There is another cost. Judicialization encourages both sides to treat abortion as total war. If the Supreme Court is the prize, then every nomination becomes apocalyptic, every state regulation a test case, every compromise a betrayal. Pro-life citizens who might support contraception, social services, or early-pregnancy compromise are told that only reversal of Roe matters. Pro-choice citizens who might admit moral distinctions fear that any concession will be weaponized in court. The result is not deliberation but mobilization.

This does not excuse violence. The murders in Brookline and elsewhere are atrocities, and clinic access must be protected by law. Nor do I romanticize state legislatures, many of which would burden poor women first and most. But a more durable abortion liberty may require political work that Roe partially displaced: building majorities, securing statutory protections, funding services, persuading neighbors, and accepting some democratically enacted regulations that do not destroy the core right.

The constitutional right now exists, and after Casey it is unrealistic simply to wish it away. Courts should enforce it against bans and substantial obstacles. But defenders of abortion rights should not rest their case on judicial supremacy. Thomson has given us a strong public argument; it should be used in public politics, not only constitutional litigation. A right that depends entirely on courts is always one retirement, one election, or one doctrinal shift away from peril. A right defended by citizens may prove stronger.

Thomson has written a characteristically careful and rigorous essay, but I believe she has made a significant error at a crucial point in her argument. She claims that there is a "gap" in the reasoning that moves from "you cannot wrong a painting" to "paintings lack the protected moral status we call rights." She suggests we lack a compelling rationale for crossing this gap. I want to argue that this is mistaken—that the rationale is available and, indeed, compelling.

Thomson's argument against the interest-based account of rights proceeds as follows. If a creature has interests, then it can be wronged when those interests are impeded unfairly. But paintings have no interests, so they cannot be wronged. However, she objects that we still need an explanation for why the inability to be wronged entails the absence of rights. The gap she identifies is between "cannot be wronged" and "lacks rights."

But I think the gap is illusory. Let us consider what it means to have a right. Thomson helpfully characterizes rights as "trumps"—having a right means that considerations of overall utility do not automatically justify overriding your interests. But this characterization only makes sense against a background where there are interests to protect. The very concept of a trump presupposes a domain in which it operates. You cannot have a trump in a game where no one is playing; you cannot have a right protecting interests where no interests exist.

When we say that something has rights, we are claiming that there are certain protections it enjoys that cannot be overridden simply because doing so would make the world go better. But this protection must be in relation to something—specifically, in relation to that entity's welfare, its good, its interests. Without interests, there is nothing to protect, and therefore nothing that can enjoy the special status we call rights.

Thomson might respond that we could conceive of rights attached to entities that lack interests—that the protection might be for something's sake rather than for the sake of the entity itself. But this conception is deeply obscure. What could it mean to protect something "for its own sake" if it has no perspective from which things go well or poorly? We can protect great works of art, but this is always for the sake of human beings who appreciate them or who would have appreciated them. There is no independent "good of the artwork" that stands as a reason for protection.

The force of this point becomes clearest when we consider why we grant rights to human beings. It is not simply because they are members of the biological species Homo sapiens—there are members of our species, such as those in persistent vegetative states, who lack the interests that normally accompany human personhood, and whose moral status is genuinely contested. Rather, we grant rights because human beings are the kind of creatures who have a welfare—creatures for whom things can go well or badly, who can be benefited or harmed.

This suggests that the relevant question is not whether a fertilized egg is a "human being" in some biological sense, but whether it is the kind of entity that can be wronged, which requires having a welfare. The answer to this question is almost certainly no, at least for the earliest stages of embryonic development.

I want to be careful here. Thomson is right that we cannot rule out the possibility that the fetus acquires rights at some point during gestation. Viability, sentience, neural development—these may be the morally relevant markers. But this point does not rescue her argument about the gap. It simply relocates the question. If we adopt an interest-based account of rights (as I think we should, despite Thomson's misgivings), then the question becomes: at what point does the fetus develop the interests that ground rights? This is a question that requires empirical and philosophical investigation, but it is at least a tractable question. The view that rights begin at conception is not tractable—it simply asserts rights for an entity that cannot possibly be a right-holder on any defensible account.

Thomson's essay is admirable in its insistence that those who reject the claim that fetuses have rights from conception cannot be written off as irrational or morally deficient. On this point I agree with her completely. But her attempt to leave the door open for the "strong claim" about conception relies on an error about the relationship between interests and rights. That door should be closed.

Thomson's essay exhibits a familiar pattern in contemporary academic discussions of abortion: a careful philosophical apparatus deployed in service of a conclusion that was reached before the argument began. She wants to defend abortion rights, and so she sets out to show that the case against abortion rests on foundations no stronger than those supporting the position she wishes to defend. But her argument trades on equivocation, and the result is a sophisticated defense of a conclusion that the evidence does not support.

Consider her treatment of the claim that human beings have rights from conception. She correctly notes that this claim cannot be "bypassed as nonsense." But she then argues that because we cannot conclusively establish that the fetus lacks rights from conception, we cannot conclusively establish that abortion is impermissible. This is meant to clear the way for her argument that liberty should not be constrained on grounds others are "not unreasonable in rejecting."

But notice the asymmetry in her treatment of evidence. She insists that we cannot conclusively refute the claim that fetuses have rights from conception. Yet she also acknowledges that we cannot conclusively establish this claim. If we apply her own standard consistently, we should be neutral on the question of fetal personhood—neither accepting nor rejecting it decisively. Yet she uses the inability to conclusively establish fetal rights as a license for unrestricted abortion access.

This is precisely the move she warns against when made by those who believe fetuses have rights. She criticizes the Pope for claiming that the doctrine "is written in every human heart, knowable by reason itself." But she then claims that the opposing view—that fetuses lack rights from conception—is equally available to reason. If reason truly permits both views, why does the burden of proof fall entirely on those who would restrict abortion?

Thomson might respond that liberty is the default position—that we must have strong reasons to constrain it. But this assumes the question at issue. If a fetus does have rights from conception, then the "liberty" to obtain an abortion is not a liberty at all—it is a license to violate another's rights. The burden of proof question cannot be settled independently of the question of fetal personhood.

More troubling is Thomson's treatment of the violence committed against abortion providers. She attributes this violence partly to the rhetoric of those who say "abortion is murder." She criticizes what she calls exaggeration and fraud in anti-abortion materials. But she does not consider whether her own rhetoric might have consequences. If the moral status of the fetus is genuinely uncertain, then those who believe it is a person are not irrational. They are facing a tragic moral situation in which their deeply held convictions about innocent human life are in conflict with a woman's claimed liberty. Thomson's dismissal of their position as based on grounds "that neither reason nor the rest of morality requires women to accept" is itself a form of rhetorical violence.

The essay concludes by defending the Court's role in Roe v. Wade. But the proper role of courts in determining fundamental moral questions is itself deeply contested. Thomson assumes that "vindicating our fundamental liberties is, after all, exactly what courts are supposed to do." This assumes that abortion rights are fundamental liberties—which is precisely what is at stake. Courts in a democracy answer to the people. When they override democratic majorities on contested questions of value, they risk both the legitimacy of their decisions and the fabric of democratic society.

Thomson's essay deserves credit for its analytical rigor and its call for respectful dialogue. But it ultimately fails to grapple with the hardest question: whether the fetus has moral status worthy of legal protection. That question cannot be dissolved by philosophical maneuvering.

Thomson makes one point that deserves careful attention from all sides of the abortion debate: the asymmetry between those who would constrain liberty and those who would exercise it. Her argument is that if the case for restricting abortion rests on grounds that reasonable people can reject, then restricting abortion is an exercise of mere force rather than legitimate authority.

This is a powerful point, and I think it has implications that Thomson herself does not fully draw out. The asymmetry she identifies is real, but it cuts both ways.

Consider the situation of a woman who has become pregnant and who does not wish to continue the pregnancy. She faces a choice between two courses of action, one of which will result in the destruction of the fetus (if the fetus has moral status) and one of which will not. According to Thomson's own reasoning about asymmetric burdens, she cannot simply choose the option that better serves her interests if there is a reasonable possibility that doing so violates the fetus's rights. The asymmetry applies to her choice as well.

This point matters because it undermines a common assumption in abortion discourse: that women who want abortions are simply exercising their liberty, and that restricting abortion is the action that requires justification. But if the fetus might have rights, then obtaining an abortion is an action that also requires justification. The woman cannot simply proceed as if her choice were unconstrained by moral considerations.

What follows from this? Not necessarily that abortion should be illegal. Thomson is right that the question of fetal personhood is genuinely contested, and that reasonable people can reach different conclusions. But the asymmetry does not automatically resolve in favor of abortion rights. Rather, it suggests that both sides should approach the question with humility.

Those who oppose abortion should recognize that their position requires them to persuade their fellow citizens through democratic means, not through violence or judicial override. Those who support abortion rights should recognize that their position involves a risk—that if the anti-abortion view is correct, then allowing unrestricted abortion causes grave moral harm. This does not mean that unrestricted abortion is impermissible given genuine uncertainty, but it does mean that the abortion rights movement should not treat the matter as settled or its opponents as moral monsters.

Thomson herself gestures toward this humility in her discussion of Mario Cuomo, where she notes that those who disagree with Catholic teaching on abortion "act in other areas of life in a manner a Catholic respects." But she does not extend the same charity to the other direction. She treats the anti-abortion view as based on grounds that "neither reason nor the rest of morality requires women to accept."

This is too quick. The claim that human beings have rights from conception is not merely a Catholic doctrine—many non-Catholics hold it as well. And the reasoning behind it is not obviously weak. The claim that potential persons matter morally is one that people of good will can endorse. That it is rejected by many thoughtful people does not show that it is unreasonable.

What would it mean to take the asymmetry seriously? It would mean acknowledging that both positions involve genuine moral stakes, and that the resolution of the issue through political institutions is legitimate even when it goes against one's own convictions. Thomson herself notes that the empirical evidence suggests that legislative resolution would have produced less access to abortion than judicial resolution has produced. This is worth considering. But it does not settle the question of which institution should decide. Courts are not infallible, and their decisions carry costs.

The asymmetry between liberty and its constraint is real. But both liberty and its constraint can be exercised rightly or wrongly. The task is not to find a formula that tips the scales in one direction, but to reason carefully about when constraint is justified and when liberty should be exercised. Thomson's essay contributes to that reasoning, even if it does not resolve it.

The abortion debate, as she frames it, is a debate between rights-holders: the pregnant woman versus the fetus. What is missing is a sustained attention to the social, economic, and political conditions that make abortion a pressing issue for women.

She does not ask why these reasons exist in the first place, or whether they might be eliminated by changes in social policy.

Consider the woman who believes she cannot supply a decent life for another child. This is a genuine and weighty concern. But why is she in this position? These conditions are not natural facts—they are the products of social policy. A society committed to helping women avoid unwanted pregnancies would provide comprehensive sex education and readily available contraception. A society committed to supporting families would provide healthcare, childcare, and parental leave. If these social goods were widely available, the number of unwanted pregnancies—and hence the number of abortions—would presumably decrease dramatically.

She argues that women must have the right to choose abortion because their circumstances make it necessary. But this accepts those circumstances as given. A more radical feminist critique would ask whether abortion rights, however necessary in the short term, are sufficient for women's liberation. They are not.

This point has implications for how we think about the abortion debate. The framing of abortion as a question of rights— woman's right to choose versus fetus's right to life—obscures the structural conditions that make the choice so fraught. ItIndividualizes what is social. It treats a political problem as a matter of individual conscience.

There is also a point about power here. The debate about abortion is, in significant part, a debate about who will control women's bodies and women's reproductive capacity. Thomson acknowledges that restricting abortion impedes "the achievement of political, social, and economic equality for women." But she does not draw out the full implications of this point. If abortion restrictions are tools of gender oppression, then the demand for abortion rights is not merely a demand for liberty—it is a demand for power. The asymmetry she identifies between those who would constrain liberty and those who would exercise it is thus not merely a formal asymmetry of procedure. It is an asymmetry of power between those who have it and those who do not.

I do not disagree with Thomson's argument. I want to supplement it. The abortion debate will not be resolved by philosophical argument alone, because it is not fundamentally a philosophical problem. It is a political problem about power, resources, and the social status of women. Until those structural conditions change, abortion rights will remain necessary, and Thomson's defense of them will remain valuable. In the midst of her essay, Thomson offers a striking observation about the Pope's encyclical Evangelium Vitae. She writes: "The acceptance of abortion in the popular mind, in behavior and even in law itself, is a telling sign of an extremely dangerous crisis of the moral sense, which is becoming more and more incapable of distinguishing between good and evil, even when the fundamental right to life is at stake." She correctly identifies this as "a thoroughly groundless assault on [the] moral integrity" of those who disagree with Catholic teaching.

This exchange is revealing. Both sides in the abortion debate are prone to dismissing their opponents as morally deficient—not merely mistaken, but wicked. Thomson criticizes the Pope for implying that supporters of abortion rights are "incapable of distinguishing between good and evil." But her own essay contains similar implications. She writes that those who would restrict abortion want "a license to impose force," and she compares their position to that of the "French absolutist Bossuet" who declared "I have the right to persecute you because I am right and you are wrong."

This comparison is unfair. The anti-abortion position is not simply that one is right and one's opponent is wrong. It is that innocent human life is at stake, and that this stake provides a morally weighty reason for legal protection. This is a position that reasonable, good-faith people can hold. To compare it to persecution simpliciter is to refuse to engage with its strongest version.

I think Thomson recognizes this to some extent. Her essay is careful and balanced in many respects, and she explicitly disavows the view that opposition to abortion is simply religious or irrational. But the language of "force" and "persecution" creeps in despite these disavowals, and it does damage to the quality of the debate.

What would a genuinely charitable engagement with the anti-abortion position look like? It would begin by taking seriously the claim that human life begins at conception. This claim, as Thomson acknowledges, cannot be dismissed as nonsense. It is a claim about the moral status of entities that are biologically continuous with the persons they will become. Whether one finds it persuasive or not, it is not unreasonable.

From this starting point, the anti-abortion position has considerable force. If the fetus is a person with a right to life, then abortion is a serious moral wrong—indeed, a form of killing. And if it is a serious moral wrong, then it is appropriate for law to discourage and prohibit it. This is not persecution; it is the normal functioning of a legal system that takes seriously the moral status of all human beings.

The difficulty, of course, is that the premise—that the fetus is a person with a right to life—is contested. Thomson argues that we cannot conclusively establish this premise, and she is right. But neither can we conclusively refute it. This means that the abortion debate is not a debate between the reasonable and the unreasonable. It is a debate between people who have reached different conclusions on a genuinely difficult question.

How should a democratic society decide such questions? Thomson defends the judicial resolution of Roe v. Wade, arguing that vindicating fundamental liberties is what courts are supposed to do. But this assumes that abortion rights are fundamental liberties—which is precisely what is at stake. A different view is that the question of fetal personhood is the kind of question that democratic majorities should resolve, through the slow and imperfect process of political deliberation.

Both approaches have costs. Judicial resolution protects rights against majoritarian backlash, but it does so in a way that bypasses democratic deliberation and may inflame political conflict. Legislative resolution respects democratic self-governance, but it exposes fundamental rights to the vicissitudes of political fortune. Neither approach is obviously correct.

What is clear is that the quality of the debate matters. When we dismiss our opponents as morally deficient, we make it harder to find common ground. When we take their arguments seriously, we create space for genuine persuasion. Thomson's essay has much to offer in this regard, but its moments of rhetorical excess undermine its call for respectful dialogue.

The abortion controversy will not be resolved by a single essay or a single judicial decision. It will be resolved, if at all, through a long process of moral and political deliberation in which all parties treat each other with respect and take seriously the arguments on all sides. Thomson's essay is a contribution to that process, even if it does not bring it to a close.
