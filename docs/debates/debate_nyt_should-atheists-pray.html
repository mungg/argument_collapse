Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Prayer, stripped of its supernatural addressee, is still doing recognizable psychological work: focusing attention, articulating gratitude, naming fears, rehearsing intentions, and binding the speaker to a community when done aloud. An atheist who refuses to do any of these things on the grounds that religious people also do them is impoverishing herself out of mere tribal hygiene. So yes — atheists can profitably engage in something like prayer, provided they are honest about what they are doing and to whom.

The harder question is whether to call it prayer. I think candor matters here. Petitionary prayer — asking an unseen agent to intervene — is precisely what an atheist cannot coherently do, and Sunday Assembly–style services that mimic the form risk a kind of cargo-cult sentimentality. But the contemplative traditions have always contained practices that even believers describe in non-petitionary terms: lectio divina, examen, the Quaker silence, much of Buddhist meditation. These are nearer to what William James called the "religion of healthy-mindedness," and they survive translation into secular vocabulary. Call it meditation, reflection, or, following Comte-Sponville and Alain de Botton's recent arguments, "spirituality for atheists."

What is the point? For believers, prayer is at minimum a relationship; at maximum, theosis. For the rest of us, it can still be a disciplined pause against the tyranny of distraction, a way of taking stock that conversation and therapy do not quite replicate. The danger of the new atheist churches is not that they borrow too much from religion but that they borrow the externals — hymns, sermons, coffee hour — while neglecting the inward practice that gave those externals their point.

The recent spate of atheist “church” services—complete with gathering, singing, and sermons—forces a sharper question: if you can borrow the rituals, can you also borrow the central act of prayer? For atheists, traditional prayer is a category mistake. It presupposes a divine recipient, an agent who hears and might respond. To petition a nonexistent being is not just unproductive; it is intellectually incoherent. But that does not mean the impulse behind prayer is foreign to nonbelievers.

For believers, prayer serves multiple functions—adoration, confession, thanksgiving, and supplication—and its psychological yield is significant. It can calm anxiety, cultivate gratitude, and provide a sense of connection to something larger than oneself. Atheists have access to these same human needs. Secular analogues like meditation, mindful breathing, or reflective journaling can produce similar states of focused awareness and emotional regulation. The difference is that the atheist’s introspection is directed inward or toward the natural world, not outward to a supernatural listener.

The emerging atheist assemblies implicitly recognize this. They offer collective silence or guided reflection without invoking God. Whether to label such moments “prayer” is a matter of linguistic strategy. Using the word risks confusion—suggesting that atheism secretly longs for theism—but it also reclaims a rich tradition of contemplative practice. If participants understand that they are not addressing a deity but are instead deliberately focusing their intentions, expressing hopes, or acknowledging shared vulnerabilities, the activity can be fruitful.

Ultimately, prayer without God is something else: a ritual of attention, an exercise in moral imagination, or an act of communal solidarity. Atheists need not pray in any theistic sense, but they can and do practice the core elements that make prayer meaningful for believers—stillness, gratitude, and aspiration—stripped of supernatural metaphysics. That is not borrowing a falsehood; it is recognizing a universal human search for depth, and meeting it with intellectual honesty.

For religious believers, prayer is primarily a dialogue with the divine—a plea for intercession, a search for guidance, or an offering of gratitude. If we define prayer broadly as an intentional pause to express gratitude, cultivate empathy, or center one’s thoughts, it is absolutely fruitful for nonbelievers. Atheists should not pray if prayer means addressing a conscious power they believe does not exist. There is no virtue in make-believe piety, and a ritual that requires self-deception is unlikely to deepen anyone’s integrity. But that does not settle the question, because much of what people value in prayer is not reducible to supernatural petition.

For believers, prayer can be praise, lament, confession, thanksgiving, intercession and the discipline of placing one’s desires before God. Its point is not simply to get things — indeed, serious religious traditions have always warned against treating God as a vending machine — but to be changed: to become more grateful, more honest, more attentive to suffering and less enthralled by the self.

Those are not exclusively theistic goods. An atheist can pause each morning to name what she is grateful for, acknowledge whom she has harmed, rehearse the kind of person she wants to be, and hold friends or strangers in compassionate attention. She can do this silently, communally, with music, candles or repeated words. Whether we call that “prayer” is partly a semantic question and partly a question of cultural inheritance.

The danger for atheist churches is that they may import the consolations of religion while stripping away the demands. A good sermon without God can still be a moral exhortation; a bad one becomes group self-congratulation. Likewise, atheist “prayer” can become mere mood management unless it includes accountability: What will I do for the person I am thinking of? What habit must I change?

So atheists need not pray to anyone. But they may fruitfully practice prayer-like forms of attention: gratitude without illusion, confession without absolution, hope without guarantees, and solidarity without pretending that the universe is listening.

Prayer Without a Listener Is Just Talking to Yourself

The current vogue for "atheist churches" reveals something embarrassing about the New Atheist movement: having spent a decade insisting that religion is intellectually bankrupt, its adherents now find themselves lonely on Sunday mornings. The Sunday Assembly in London, and its imitators springing up in Baton Rouge and New York, are a tacit admission that Dawkins and Hitchens told only half the story. Religion isn't just a set of factual claims about the universe; it's a technology of community, ritual, and consolation. Fine. But prayer is a bridge too far.

Singing together makes obvious secular sense — humans have sung in groups since long before monotheism. Sermons are just lectures with better pacing. Potlucks are potlucks. These can be lifted out of the religious context without contradiction. Prayer cannot. Prayer is, by definition, address: it presupposes a listener. To pray to no one is not a ritual; it's a performance of belief one does not hold. It is the spiritual equivalent of writing letters to Santa as an adult — sweet, perhaps, but fundamentally confused.

Defenders will say prayer can be "reframed" as meditation, gratitude practice, or focused intention. But we already have words for those things, and they don't require the theological vocabulary. Meditation is meditation. Journaling is journaling. Calling these activities "prayer" is either rhetorical sleight of hand or nostalgia for a worldview one has supposedly outgrown. If a former Catholic finds comfort in lighting a candle and reciting words into the void, I won't begrudge them the comfort — but they should be honest that they're soothing a habit, not engaging in a coherent practice.

There's also a strategic point. The intellectual credibility of unbelief depends on its willingness to do without the consolations of belief. Atheists who borrow prayer are vulnerable to the obvious rejoinder: if the rituals are so valuable, perhaps the underlying claims deserve a second look. Better to build genuinely new secular practices — civic rituals, philosophical discussion groups, mutual-aid networks — than to wear religion's hand-me-downs and pretend they fit.

Gather, by all means. Sing. Listen to a good talk. But keep your hands unfolded and your eyes open. Honest unbelief is its own discipline, and it doesn't need to whisper to a god it has already dismissed.

What Prayer Actually Does

Most arguments about whether atheists should pray get hung up on metaphysics — is there anyone on the receiving end? — and miss what prayer empirically does for the people who practice it. As someone trained in cognitive science, I find this evasive. We should look at the function before we adjudicate the form.

Decades of research on contemplative practice, much of it summarized in journals like *Psychological Science* and in work by Richard Davidson, Sara Lazar, and others, suggests that structured, repeated, inward-directed verbal practices produce measurable changes: reduced cortisol, altered activity in the default-mode network, improved emotional regulation, and stronger pro-social dispositions. These effects don't seem to require theological assent. Buddhist monks, Catholic contemplatives, and secular MBSR practitioners all show overlapping benefits. Prayer, in other words, is a form of cognitive training, and the trainee gets the benefit whether or not anyone is listening.

Prayer also performs social work. Petitionary prayer for others has been correlated with increased empathy and reduced aggression toward the person prayed for. Gratitude practices — which are essentially prayers of thanksgiving stripped of a recipient — show robust effects on subjective well-being in studies by Emmons and others. Confessional prayer mirrors the disclosure effects Pennebaker documented in expressive writing. These are not small things. They are the everyday tools by which humans regulate themselves.

Given this, the question "should atheists pray?" almost answers itself. If a practice reliably makes you calmer, kinder, and more focused, the rational response is to adopt it, modified as needed to fit your actual beliefs. An atheist who sits quietly each morning and silently articulates what she is grateful for, whom she hopes to help, and what she resolves to do better is praying in every functional sense. She is just declining to add a metaphysical addressee she doesn't believe in.

The Sunday Assemblies and similar gatherings are experimenting in exactly this direction, and they deserve curiosity rather than scorn. The interesting question is not whether atheists should pray but which forms of prayer survive translation into a naturalistic worldview. Petition probably doesn't. Gratitude, reflection, and resolution clearly do. Atheists who refuse them on principle are letting their opposition to bad metaphysics deprive them of perfectly good psychology.

A Believer's View: Please Don't

Let me speak as a practicing Christian who finds the spectacle of "atheist churches" oddly touching and the proposal of atheist prayer oddly insulting — to both sides.

Prayer, in the tradition I inhabit, is not a self-help exercise. It is the soul's response to being addressed first. When I pray, I am not generating a mood or rehearsing my values; I am answering a Person who, I believe, has spoken. Take away that conviction and what remains is not prayer but its hollow shell — the posture without the encounter. To call that "prayer" is to misunderstand the word so thoroughly that the misunderstanding becomes its own kind of statement.

I don't say this to gatekeep. If atheists want quiet reflection, gratitude journals, group singing, ethical exhortation — wonderful. Humanity has always needed these, and the secular world's recent rediscovery that community and ritual matter is a welcome correction to a century of liberal individualism. But there are perfectly good secular names for these activities: meditation, contemplation, civic ceremony, mutual encouragement. Borrowing the word "prayer" muddies what each tradition actually claims.

It also does atheists a disservice. The intellectually serious atheist position — the one I disagree with but respect — holds that the universe is finally indifferent, that we are alone in a meaningful sense, and that meaning must be constructed by us rather than received from above. That is a hard view to live by, and its dignity lies in its refusal of consolations it considers false. When atheists fold their hands and address the ceiling, they trade away that dignity for a feeling. Pascal would have smiled; Nietzsche would have wept.

What I would say to my unbelieving neighbors is this: take ritual seriously, take community seriously, take silence seriously. These are human goods, not religious property. But if you find yourself reaching, in the silence, for Someone — pay attention to that reaching. Don't paper it over with the word "prayer" used in scare quotes. Either follow it where it leads, or be honest that you've decided it leads nowhere. The middle path — atheist prayer — strikes me as the worst of both worlds: too much religion for the skeptic, too little for the seeker.

The Wrong Question

"Should atheists pray?" is the kind of question that sounds provocative but quickly collapses under examination, because both nouns are doing too much work. There is no single thing called "prayer" and no single creature called "the atheist." Until we disaggregate, we're just arguing about words.

Consider the varieties of prayer documented by anthropologists and scholars of religion like T. M. Luhrmann, whose recent work on American evangelicals describes prayer as something closer to a learned imaginative skill than a doctrinal exercise. Within a single tradition you can find petition, intercession, thanksgiving, confession, lament, adoration, contemplation, and the rote recitation of fixed texts. Some of these presuppose a personal deity who hears and responds. Others — the Jesus Prayer, certain Buddhist chants, Quaker silence — function more as attentional discipline than as communication. Lumping all of this under one verb and asking whether atheists should do "it" is a category error.

Now consider atheists. The label covers Ayn Randian individualists who would sooner chew glass than sit in a circle, secular Jews who attend High Holy Day services for cultural reasons, ex-Catholics who miss the incense, scientific naturalists who think ritual is residual superstition, and humanists actively constructing new ceremonies for birth, marriage, and death. To ask whether "the atheist" should pray is like asking whether "the European" should eat fish on Friday.

The more useful questions are smaller. Is silent contemplation good for human flourishing? The evidence says yes. Does communal singing strengthen social bonds? Yes, demonstrably. Does petitionary prayer to a deity one does not believe in make sense? No, it is incoherent. Can ritualized gratitude improve well-being without theological commitments? The positive psychology literature suggests it can. Should new secular communities like the Sunday Assembly borrow liturgical structures from older traditions? That's a pragmatic question about what works, not a metaphysical one.

The press loves the framing "atheists are doing X that religious people do!" because it generates clicks. But the underlying reality is that humans across all worldviews are running similar experiments in how to live well together, and they borrow from each other constantly. The Sunday Assembly is one such experiment. Whether what its attendees do constitutes "prayer" matters far less than whether it helps them be better neighbors, partners, and citizens. On that, we'll have to wait and see.

Yes, and We Already Do

I am an atheist, and I pray. I don't mean this as a paradox or a provocation. I mean that there are moments — standing at the bedside of my dying father, watching my daughter sleep, hearing news of a distant atrocity — when something rises up in me that has no other name. Call it longing, call it reaching, call it the involuntary articulation of love and dread. Whatever it is, it isn't optional, and pretending otherwise would be a kind of dishonesty I'm not interested in.

The orthodox atheist position, the one I've heard at every skeptic meetup, is that this feeling is a vestige — neural plumbing left over from our ancestors, to be drained and rerouted by better cognitive hygiene. I think this is wrong, and not just sentimentally wrong. It misreads what prayer is. Prayer is not primarily a transaction with a deity; it is the human practice of speaking truthfully about what one cannot fix. The believer addresses God; the atheist addresses no one in particular. But the speaking itself — the willingness to name the grief, to thank the morning, to ask for courage out loud — is the practice. Whether anyone is on the other end is a separate question, and a less interesting one than my fellow atheists tend to think.

This is why I welcome the Sunday Assembly and its cousins. For too long, organized unbelief has been a movement of arguments rather than of lives. We have been very good at saying what we are against and very poor at building the practices by which a person actually faces down mortality, raises children, sustains a marriage, mourns the dead. The religious traditions have spent millennia working on these problems. Some of their answers are tangled up with metaphysics we can't accept. But many — the daily examen, the moment of silence before a meal, the slow communal singing — can be lifted out and used.

So yes: pray. Not because it changes the world outside you, but because it changes the speaker. Address the dawn, the dead, the unborn, the void if you like. Speak the thing that wants to be spoken. An unbelief that cannot bear to do this strikes me as a thinner thing than unbelief ought to be.

The atheist church movement, then, is not merely eccentric or ironic. It is a direct response to a diagnosed social pathology. If gathering weekly to sing, hear readings, and engage in group contemplation helps nonbelievers build durable communities, it serves a legitimate function. The fact that the content is explicitly naturalistic rather than supernatural makes it no less valid as social technology.

Whether individual participants experience anything like what believers describe as spiritual is beside the point. What matters is the outcome: are people less lonely, more connected, more likely to help neighbors and engage in civic life? If the answer is yes, then the atheist church movement deserves support, not ridicule.

What strikes me most about the atheist church phenomenon is its implicit confession of failure. Not the failure of atheism — that project has succeeded magnificently in its intellectual dimensions — but the failure of secular culture to offer anything as compelling as religious ritual. Believers have always understood that ideas alone cannot sustain a movement; what holds communities together across generations is not doctrine but practice, not belief but habit. Secular modernity, intoxicated by its own rationality, neglected the embodied, repetitive, sensory dimensions of human meaning-making.

Religious rituals work because they engage the whole person: voice, body, emotion, memory. A church service isn't just information transfer; it's a full sensory experience calibrated over centuries to produce specific psychological states. The atheist church movement is essentially attempting to reverse-engineer this effect, to extract the mechanism from its original context and repurpose it.

This might work. It might also reveal how much the supernatural elements actually contributed. Many Christian practices — confession, fasting, communal meals, meditative prayer — involve genuine psychological transformation precisely because they are embedded in a framework of belief. Take away the felt presence of God, and you may have a pleasant social gathering without the deeper transformation.

Perhaps the real question isn't whether atheists should pray, but whether secular culture is capable of producing rituals with genuine transformative power. My suspicion is that without some genuinely transcendent commitment, such rituals will remain pale imitations of the real thing.

Let me step back from the philosophical abstractions and ask a simpler question: what do people actually get out of prayer? Research in psychology and neuroscience suggests several distinct benefits, most of which require no supernatural commitment whatsoever.

First, prayer provides structured time for reflection. The busy modern mind rarely slows down enough to examine itself; a regular practice of quiet contemplation — whether called prayer, meditation, or mindfulness — produces measurable improvements in well-being and emotional regulation. Second, communal worship provides social support, which correlates strongly with physical health and longevity. Third, the ritualized use of language — speaking aloud one's gratitude, fears, or hopes — engages cognitive processes different from silent thought, often producing genuine emotional resolution.

Atheists can access all of these benefits through practices they might not call prayer but which function identically. The secular community gatherings in Louisiana and New York represent one attempt to formalize such practices within a collective setting.

The debate about whether this constitutes "real" prayer or "real" worship strikes me as terminological squabbling that obscures more than it reveals. Language is a tool; use it where it works. If "prayer" is the word that helps people engage with contemplative practice, and if that practice genuinely improves their lives, then the word has earned its keep. The gods — or lack thereof — need not be consulted.
