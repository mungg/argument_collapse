When Do Doctors Have the Right to Speak?

Two federal appellate court decisions, one allowing Florida to prevent doctors from discussing gun safety with patients, the other letting California ban “gay-conversion” therapy, raise questions about health professionals’ First Amendment rights.

Do occupational-licensing laws trump the First Amendment? What limits, if any, does the First Amendment impose on government’s ability to restrict advice?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The two rulings sit in tension, but the tension is illuminating rather than incoherent. Both cases ask the same question: when a state licenses a profession, how much of what the licensee says is "speech" protected by the First Amendment, and how much is "treatment" subject to ordinary regulation?

The cleanest principle is that the state may regulate professional conduct, including the conduct of giving treatment, even when that treatment is executed through words. California's ban on sexual-orientation change efforts for minors fits this model: the legislature found the technique ineffective and harmful, much as it could ban prescribing a dangerous drug. The Ninth Circuit was right that licensure would mean little if any therapeutic intervention could be immunized by calling it talk.

Florida's Firearm Owners' Privacy Act is harder to defend on those terms. Asking a patient whether guns are stored safely is not a treatment whose efficacy the legislature has evaluated; it is preventive counseling of the same kind doctors offer about pools, seatbelts, and household chemicals. The statute singles out one viewpoint on one political subject and tells physicians not to raise it. That looks less like occupational regulation than like content- and viewpoint-based censorship dressed in a white coat. The Eleventh Circuit's contrary conclusion strains the conduct/speech line past breaking.

The workable rule, I think, is this: states may forbid professionals from administering interventions they reasonably deem unsafe or ineffective, and may compel truthful disclosures necessary to informed consent. They may not, under cover of licensure, prescribe which honest, evidence-based topics a doctor must avoid with a patient. Wollschlaeger was wrongly decided; Pickup was not.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The tension between professional regulation and free speech is not new, but the recent appellate rulings force us to clarify a boundary that has been too foggy for too long. The question is not whether doctors have a “right to speak”—they plainly do, as citizens. It is whether the state can, through occupational-licensing laws, prohibit specific speech that occurs within the physician-patient relationship as a condition of holding a license. The answer must distinguish between speech as conduct and speech as pure expression, and in both cases the government bears a heavy burden.

Medical practice consists largely of speech: diagnosis, advice, informed consent. When a Florida doctor asks about firearms in the home, that question is not random chatter; it is part of preventive care, grounded in epidemiological evidence that unsafe storage correlates with childhood injury and suicide. To ban such questioning is not to regulate the practice of medicine—it is to distort it by carving out a politically charged topic from the exam room. The First Amendment does not permit the state to silence a profession simply because the subject makes legislators uncomfortable.

The California ban on “gay-conversion” therapy for minors is a different, though consistent, case. There, the state is not muzzling a broad health inquiry but prohibiting a specific practice that the medical consensus deems harmful and without foundation. When a form of “therapy” itself constitutes professional misconduct because it lacks scientific validity and endangers patients, restricting its delivery is a legitimate regulation of conduct, not a content-based restriction on speech. A doctor remains free to advocate conversion therapy in a op-ed; she simply cannot administer it in her clinical capacity.

The distinction, then, is not whether the topic is political but whether the restriction is truly rooted in medical science and patient safety. The Florida law fails that test by inserting a legislative veto into a standard, evidence-based screening conversation. The California law passes it by banning a discredited practice that the profession itself rejects. Courts must be vigilant lest licensing laws become a backdoor for censoring physicians who address topics that powerful constituencies would rather keep out of the examination room. The First Amendment should protect the doctor who asks about guns; it need not protect the therapist who practices fraud.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

The tension between state licensing powers and the First Amendment in the physician’s exam room is one of the most complex legal frontiers of our time. While doctors do not check their free speech rights at the clinic door, the state undeniably possesses broad authority to regulate the medical profession to protect public health. The key to resolving this tension lies in distinguishing between the regulation of medical treatment and the imposition of political censorship.

The Ninth Circuit’s decision upholding California’s ban on “gay-conversion” therapy for minors correctly recognizes this distinction. While psychological therapy relies almost entirely on speech, it is ultimately a medical treatment administered by licensed professionals. Every major medical and psychological association has concluded that conversion therapy is not only ineffective but actively harmful to youth, increasing risks of depression and suicide. By banning it, California is acting squarely within its traditional police powers to regulate medical practice and prevent professional malpractice. The First Amendment does not give a doctor the right to administer toxic medicine, nor does it protect the administration of psychologically damaging therapy simply because the mechanism of injury involves words.

Conversely, the Eleventh Circuit’s recent panel decision upholding Florida’s firearms privacy law represents a dangerous distortion of this regulatory power. Florida is not banning a harmful medical procedure; it is banning a medically recommended inquiry solely to satisfy a powerful political constituency. The American Academy of Pediatrics explicitly recommends that doctors ask parents about firearms in the home in order to discuss safe storage—just as they ask about un-fenced swimming pools, household chemicals, or seatbelts.

Florida’s law gags doctors from sharing basic, evidence-based safety advice. When the government dictates what a doctor can or cannot ask based on political ideology rather than medical efficacy, it ceases to regulate professional conduct and begins to blatantly violate the First Amendment.

The dividing line must remain anchored to established medical consensus and patient safety. Occupational licensing laws rightfully trump the First Amendment when the state is regulating the standard of care to protect citizens from quackery and harm. But the state’s power to license health professionals is not a blank check to control their speech. When a legislature uses licensing laws as a backdoor to enforce a political agenda—silencing scientifically sound, preventative health advice—the First Amendment must step in to protect both the doctor’s right to speak and the patient’s right to make informed decisions.

Licensing does not create a First Amendment-free zone. Doctors, lawyers and therapists do not surrender their constitutional rights when they receive a state license. But neither is professional advice identical to a pamphlet handed out on a street corner. The state may require competence, informed consent, recordkeeping, confidentiality and standards of care because patients rely on licensed professionals in relationships marked by expertise and vulnerability.

That distinction matters in the Florida gun case. A pediatrician who asks whether there is a loaded firearm in the home, and who advises safe storage, is doing what doctors routinely do about swimming pools, car seats, lead paint and alcohol. The state may punish harassment, irrelevant interrogation or misuse of medical records. But a law singling out gun-related counseling because legislators dislike the message looks like viewpoint discrimination, not medical regulation. If patient privacy is the concern, write a privacy rule. If professional relevance is the concern, let medical boards police abuses under ordinary standards. Government should not tell doctors that one politically favored household risk is off limits.

California’s ban on conversion therapy for minors is a harder but different case. The state is not simply barring therapists from expressing religious or moral views about sexuality, nor preventing adults from seeking counseling. It is regulating a licensed treatment offered to minors, after major professional organizations have rejected claims that homosexuality is a disorder and warned of risks from efforts to change orientation. States have long been able to prohibit medical practices judged ineffective or harmful, even when the practice consists largely of words.

The danger is that “professional conduct” can become a magic label for suppressing disfavored speech. Courts should therefore ask whether the regulation is grounded in professional evidence, tied to patient protection, and neutral as to viewpoint. Under that test, gun-safety counseling should be protected; harmful licensed therapy for minors may be restricted.

The two appellate decisions represent different attempts to balance professional regulation against constitutional speech protections, and neither can be reduced to a simple formula. Occupational-licensing laws are not simply about competence—they often embed substantive policy judgments about what advice professionals may give. When Florida prohibited gun-safety discussions, it wasn't merely regulating professional conduct; it was suppressing specific viewpoints on a matter of public health. The First Amendment's commercial-speech doctrine has always recognized that professional advice occupies a special category, but that recognition does not mean the government can forbid such speech outright.

The California ban on conversion therapy presents a starker case. The state was protecting vulnerable patients from demonstrably harmful practices while psychiatric knowledge had shifted decisively against the therapy's efficacy and safety. Here, the professional-licensing framework served legitimate health objectives rather than ideological suppression.

What limits should apply? Courts should distinguish between regulations targeting professional competence and those targeting message content. Content-based restrictions on professional speech require compelling government interests and must be narrowly tailored—Florida's approach failed because it was overbroad and ideologically motivated. Time and manner restrictions on professional conduct, by contrast, deserve more deference when they serve genuine health and safety goals.

The real tension lies in determining when regulation becomes censorship. Both cases remind us that professional status does not strip away First Amendment protections entirely, but it does alter the analytical framework. The government may set professional standards, but it may not use licensing merely as a tool to silence inconvenient truths.

**Two Bad Laws, Not One**

The Eleventh Circuit's decision upholding Florida's Firearm Owners' Privacy Act and the Ninth Circuit's decision upholding California's ban on sexual orientation change efforts for minors are being treated as parallel cases. They aren't. One is a transparent act of viewpoint discrimination dressed up as patient privacy. The other is a routine exercise of the state's authority to define the standard of care. Conflating them muddies the First Amendment doctrine that ought to govern professional speech.

Florida's law was passed because the NRA didn't like that pediatricians, following American Academy of Pediatrics guidance, were counseling parents about safe gun storage in homes with children. The legislature responded by silencing one side of a public-health conversation. That is precisely the kind of content- and viewpoint-based regulation the First Amendment exists to forbid. The state has not shown that asking about firearms harms patients; it has shown only that a politically powerful constituency finds the questions annoying. If Florida can muzzle doctors on guns, it can muzzle them on tobacco, sugary drinks, helmets, or seat belts—any topic where giving honest advice irritates a lobby.

California's law is different in kind. It does not tell therapists what to say about homosexuality in op-eds, sermons, books, or even with adult clients. It tells licensed professionals that they may not administer a particular "treatment" to minors—one that every major medical and psychological association has concluded is ineffective and harmful. States have always defined which interventions licensees may perform, and the incidental speech involved in performing them does not magically convert malpractice rules into censorship. A surgeon cannot invoke the First Amendment to perform lobotomies for depression; a psychologist cannot invoke it to practice a discredited therapy on children.

The principle that reconciles these cases is straightforward. Occupational licensing does not trump the First Amendment, but neither does the First Amendment immunize professionals from the ordinary regulation of their conduct, including the words that constitute that conduct. The relevant question is whether the state is restricting a treatment modality with reasoned medical justification, or whether it is picking sides in a policy debate by gagging professionals whose advice it dislikes. California has done the former. Florida has done the latter, and the Eleventh Circuit was wrong to let it.

**The Exam Room Is Not a Public Square**

Much of the commentary on these two cases assumes that when a doctor speaks to a patient, the First Amendment shows up in the room with the same force it has on a street corner or in a newspaper column. It doesn't, and it shouldn't.

When the state issues someone a medical license, it confers a monopoly. Only licensees may diagnose, prescribe, and treat; in exchange, they accept that the state will define what constitutes competent practice. That definition has always included speech: informed-consent requirements compel doctors to say things; malpractice law punishes them for saying the wrong things; confidentiality rules forbid them from saying still others. None of this has ever been thought to raise serious constitutional difficulty, because professional advice is not the kind of speech the First Amendment was designed to protect from democratic regulation. It is a service rendered for a fee under a license.

Once one accepts that framing, both the Florida and California decisions look defensible, even if one disagrees with the underlying policies. Florida's legislature concluded that questions about firearm ownership are not germane to most clinical encounters and can feel intrusive or coercive coming from a figure of authority whose goodwill the patient depends on. One can think that judgment wrong on the merits—I do—while still recognizing that the legislature, not the American Academy of Pediatrics, has the authority to set the scope of practice. California's legislature concluded, with overwhelming professional support, that conversion therapy on minors causes harm. Same authority, same logic.

The alternative is genuinely worse. If every regulation that incidentally touches what professionals say is subject to strict First Amendment scrutiny, then informed-consent statutes, scope-of-practice rules, prescribing limits, and most malpractice doctrines become constitutionally suspect. Chiropractors will claim a free-speech right to advise against vaccination; naturopaths will assert one to recommend laetrile for cancer. The orderly regulation of the healing professions, painstakingly built over a century, becomes a matter for federal judges applying ad hoc balancing tests.

Doctors retain their full First Amendment rights as citizens—to write, advocate, lobby, and organize. What they do not have is a constitutional right to deliver any advice they please under a state-issued license. The exam room is regulated space, and that is as it should be.

**Follow the Evidence, Not the Politics**

The most important fact about the Florida gun-counseling law and the California conversion-therapy law is not legal but empirical, and it points in opposite directions.

There is a substantial body of pediatric research—reaching back decades—showing that firearms in the home, particularly unsecured ones, dramatically increase the risk of childhood injury, suicide, and accidental death. Pediatricians counsel about pool fences, bicycle helmets, and crib safety for the same reason they counsel about guns: the data say it saves lives. The Florida legislature did not engage that evidence. It responded to political pressure. The "patient privacy" rationale offered for the law is a fig leaf; patients are always free to decline to answer any question, and doctors routinely ask about far more intimate matters than gun ownership.

Conversion therapy is the mirror image. Every major professional body—the APA, the AAP, the AMA, the American Psychiatric Association—has reviewed the evidence and concluded that efforts to change a minor's sexual orientation are both ineffective and associated with serious harms, including depression and suicidality. California did not invent this consensus; it codified it. Banning a treatment that the relevant scientific community has judged harmful is exactly what licensing boards are supposed to do.

This evidentiary asymmetry should drive the constitutional analysis. The First Amendment is not a license for legislatures to override medical science when it is politically inconvenient (Florida), but neither is it a shield for practitioners who want to administer discredited interventions to vulnerable patients (California). The courts should ask: does the speech restriction track a genuine professional consensus grounded in evidence, or does it cut against one?

By that test, the Ninth Circuit got conversion therapy right and the Eleventh Circuit got gun counseling wrong. The deeper worry is that judges, untrained in clinical research, will treat the two cases as legally indistinguishable—either deferring to legislatures across the board (which lets politicians dictate medicine) or skeptically scrutinizing both (which lets quackery hide behind free-speech claims). Neither extreme serves patients. What we need is a doctrine that takes evidence seriously, because that is what good medicine, and good law, ultimately requires.

**Whose Voice Counts in the Exam Room?**

Lost in the abstract First Amendment arguments is a simple question: who is the speech regulation actually for? When we ask whether doctors have a "right to speak," we tend to picture the doctor as the rights-holder and the state as the threat. But the third figure in the room—the patient—has interests of her own, and those interests don't always align neatly with either professional autonomy or state authority.

Patients come to doctors in a position of profound informational and emotional dependence. They are often frightened, in pain, or worried about a child. They generally cannot evaluate the quality of the advice they receive; that is precisely why they are seeking it. This asymmetry cuts in different directions in the two cases at hand.

In the Florida case, the asymmetry argues against the law. A parent who hears a pediatrician ask, "Do you keep firearms locked up?" is receiving information she may not get anywhere else, from a source she trusts. The state's intervention doesn't liberate her from professional power; it impoverishes the conversation by removing safety information her doctor wants to share. The "right to speak" here is really the patient's right to hear.

In the California case, the asymmetry cuts the other way. The "patients" are minors, often brought to therapy by parents distressed about their child's sexuality. The child has limited power to refuse, limited ability to evaluate the treatment, and substantial evidence suggests real psychological harm. Here state intervention protects a vulnerable listener from a powerful speaker peddling a service the profession has judged harmful.

What unites the two cases, then, is not a tidy doctrine about professional speech but a recognition that the First Amendment's purposes—autonomy, truth-seeking, informed self-government—are served by attending to the patient's situation, not just the doctor's claim of right. Occupational licensing does not "trump" the First Amendment; it implements one of its underlying values, which is making sure that those who depend on expert advice get advice worth depending on.

The hard work, then, is not deciding whether doctors may speak. It is deciding what patients deserve to hear, and from whom, and under what conditions. Courts that frame the issue narrowly as a contest between professionals and the state will keep getting these cases wrong—sometimes in one direction, sometimes in the other.

The tension between the First Amendment and occupational licensing is not a simple binary. To understand why states can ban certain therapies but should not ban certain questions, we must draw a clear legal line between the regulation of medical *conduct* and the censorship of medical *advice*. However, when a professional employs words to administer a psychological treatment, those words function as medical conduct. The First Amendment is not a constitutional shield for medical malpractice simply because the malpractice is executed verbally.
