Referendums occupy an uneasy place in democratic theory. They promise unmediated legitimacy — what could be more democratic than letting citizens decide? — yet they also strip complex questions of the deliberation, amendment and compromise that representative institutions are designed to provide. The Colombian and British votes illustrate the dangers. Voters were asked to render binary verdicts on documents (a 297-page peace accord, a treaty relationship four decades in the making) that few could have read, much less weighed against alternatives. Turnout in Colombia barely cleared the legal threshold; Brexit passed by under four points. In both cases, a thin and contingent majority produced an irreversible-feeling result.

That does not mean referendums should be abolished. They are appropriate where the question is genuinely constitutional — secession, sovereignty pooling, fundamental rights — and where the choice is concrete rather than aspirational. The Scottish independence referendum of 2014 worked reasonably well because the alternatives were defined and the campaign was long.

The rule of thumb should be: use referendums to ratify, not to design. Require supermajorities or turnout thresholds for consequential changes. And never use them, as David Cameron did, to manage a party-political problem.

The lurch toward government by referendum is less a triumph of direct democracy than a dangerous abdication of representative duty. The stunning rejection of the Colombian peace accord and Britain’s vote to leave the European Union share a common thread: both asked citizens to ratify enormously complex, multidimensional pacts with a blunt binary question, stripping away the nuance that legislative negotiation and public deliberation are meant to provide. When a nation’s most vital interests are at stake, plebiscites tend to magnify passion at the expense of prudence.

A referendum transforms a living, amendable set of compromises into an all-or-nothing gamble. The Colombian deal, painstakingly assembled over four years to end a half-century of bloodshed, was reduced to a single “yes” or “no” that left no room for the kind of iterative improvement that parliamentarians might have achieved. Similarly, the Brexit campaign revealed how easily a complex web of trade, migration and regulatory ties could be condensed into emotionally charged slogans about sovereignty and £350 million a week for the National Health Service — a figure later disowned by its proponents. In both cases, vital truths were buried under a flood of misinformation and tribal loyalties, and once the votes were tallied, the damage was instantaneous and likely irreversible.

Low turnout further erodes the moral authority of a referendum result. In Colombia, just 40 percent of eligible voters participated, meaning a minority of a minority imposed a decision that will shape the nation’s future for generations. That is not the voice of the people; it is the voice of the most motivated, often angriest, slice of the electorate. Worse, referendums are frequently hijacked by unrelated grievances. The Brexit ballot became a protest against austerity, immigration and a perceived London elite, while the Colombian “no” was driven partly by resentment of the government rather than a sober assessment of the accord’s terms.

The architects of modern constitutional democracy understood that cooling mechanisms — separated powers, bicameralism, judicial review — were necessary to refine and enlarge public views. Referendums bypass those filters, handing immense power to prime ministers or presidents who call them for tactical advantage. David Cameron offered a referendum not because EU membership was a burning constitutional question but to manage internal party divisions, and it backfired catastrophically. That is not responsible governance; it is gambling with a nation’s destiny.

Some will protest that the people are wise and that elites have no right to override their verdict. Yet democratic legitimacy depends not merely on counting heads but on processes that encourage informed deliberation, protect minority rights and allow course corrections. A vote cast in a fog of deceptive advertising, with no mechanism to adjust the outcome afterward, is a poor substitute for the slow, frustrating work of representative institutions. When vital issues are at stake, leaders should lead — by debating, amending and legislating — not by tossing a fuse-laden question to an unprepared public and hoping for the best.

Representative democracy was designed precisely to filter the public will through institutional deliberation, negotiation, and expertise. Referendums give voters a direct say on pivotal issues, but their legitimacy hinges on two prerequisites: an informed, engaged electorate and a clear, binary choice. When both are present, the outcome can reinforce democratic legitimacy, as happened in the 1991 Colombian constitutional referendum. In contrast, the 2016 Colombian peace accord vote and the United Kingdom’s Brexit referendum illustrate the risks of disregarding those conditions. In each case, the question was complex, the electorate was divided, and turnout was modest—about 40 % in Colombia and roughly 72 % in the UK. Low participation erodes the claim that the result reflects the will of the whole people, and complex trade‑offs are poorly captured by a simple yes or no.

Governments should therefore reserve referendums for clear, limited questions—such as a territorial status change—where the stakes are transparent, the campaign provides balanced information, and mechanisms exist to protect minority rights if the majority chooses narrowly. For intricate policy dilemmas that require balancing competing interests, representative institutions are better suited to deliberate and implement nuanced solutions. In short, the people’s voice should be trusted when it is both well‑informed and sufficiently broad; otherwise, the risk of a costly, irreversible mistake outweighs the democratic payoff.

Referendums are the wrong tool for the wrong job, and the past few months have made the case more forcefully than any political scientist could. In Colombia, voters narrowly rejected a peace accord that took four years of painstaking negotiation in Havana and ended what was the Western Hemisphere's longest-running armed conflict. In Britain, a slim majority voted to leave the European Union on the basis of campaign claims — £350 million a week for the NHS, for instance — that even the campaigners abandoned the morning after. Both outcomes share a common feature: the binary "yes/no" question concealed an immense thicket of consequences that no voter, however civic-minded, could fully appraise.

This is precisely why representative democracy exists. We elect legislators not because they are wiser than us in some aristocratic sense, but because the division of labor in a complex society demands that some people spend their days reading 300-page treaties while the rest of us go to work. A trade deal, a peace settlement, a constitutional reform — these are intricate packages whose clauses interact. Reducing them to a ballot question forces voters to express a verdict on the whole when they may object only to a part, or to vote on the basis of mood, protest, or the latest tabloid headline.

There is also the turnout problem. Colombia's referendum drew only about 40 percent of eligible voters, meaning a transformational decision about war and peace was made by roughly a fifth of the electorate. Low turnout is the norm in plebiscites that lack the personality-driven drama of a general election, and it systematically advantages whichever side is angrier or better organized — rarely the side of cautious compromise.

None of this is to say the people's voice should be ignored. It speaks loudly through general elections, where parties offer platforms, leaders are held accountable, and citizens weigh bundles of issues against each other. That process is messy, but it builds in the deliberation, expertise, and revisability that referendums strip away. When David Cameron called the Brexit vote, he treated a constitutional question as a tool to manage his party's internal politics; Colombia's President Santos staked a national settlement on a single afternoon's mood. Both gambles were reckless precisely because referendums make reckless gambling easy. Vital decisions deserve institutions that slow us down, not ones that speed us up.

The right question isn't whether referendums should decide vital national issues, but under what conditions they can do so legitimately. Treated as a magic instrument of pure democracy, they fail; treated as one carefully designed step in a broader deliberative process, they can succeed.

Three design features separate the good referendums from the bad. First, the threshold should match the stakes. Constitutional changes in many countries require supermajorities, or majorities in multiple regions, or a minimum turnout. Brexit was decided by 52 percent on a single afternoon, with no requirement that the winning side present an actual plan for implementation. Colombia's peace deal failed on a turnout of 40 percent. Decisions that bind future generations should require more than a transient plurality of the motivated.

Second, the question must be answerable. "Should the United Kingdom remain a member of the European Union or leave the European Union?" sounds clear but conceals dozens of distinct trade-offs: the single market, the customs union, freedom of movement, the Irish border, financial passporting. A well-designed referendum either ratifies a specific, drafted agreement — as the Good Friday Agreement was ratified in 1998 — or chooses among concrete alternatives. It does not delegate the hard part to politicians after the fact.

Third, the campaign environment must allow real deliberation. The Brexit campaign featured industrial-scale misinformation from both sides, and Britain's libel-friendly press has no equivalent of an FEC or a fact-checking body with teeth. Ireland's Citizens' Assembly model — convening randomly selected citizens to study an issue for months before it goes to a public vote — is a useful complement, marrying mass democracy with informed deliberation.

By these criteria, the Colombian and Brexit referendums look less like indictments of direct democracy than indictments of how leaders chose to deploy it. President Santos staked a generational settlement on a low-turnout up-or-down vote. David Cameron called a referendum to settle a quarrel inside the Conservative Party, with no required plan for the morning after. Both used the people's voice as a tactical instrument, then were surprised when it spoke back.

The lesson is not that voters cannot be trusted with vital decisions. It is that the architecture surrounding such decisions — thresholds, question design, deliberative infrastructure — matters as much as the vote itself. Build the architecture badly, and even the right answer arrives by accident. Build it well, and the people's voice becomes something more than a coin flip.

The debate over whether governments should use referendums for vital national decisions is trapped in a false dichotomy. Direct democracy is neither a perfect expression of the public will nor an inherent threat to stability. The problem highlighted by both Brexit and the Colombian peace plebiscite is not the concept of asking the people; it is the dangerously flawed architectural design of modern referendums.

If governments are going to put generation-defining questions to the public, they must implement stringent procedural safeguards, particularly regarding turnout and margins of victory.

Consider the absurdity of the Colombian referendum: a 50-year war, which cost hundreds of thousands of lives, had its resolution decided by a margin of less than half a percentage point in an election where only 37 percent of eligible voters participated. This means that a faction representing roughly 18 percent of the Colombian electorate has effectively vetoed a comprehensively negotiated peace treaty. Similarly, the United Kingdom has fundamentally altered its economic and geopolitical future based on a 52-48 vote.

In almost every democratic constitution in the world, changing the foundational rules of the state requires a supermajority—often two-thirds of the legislature—to ensure broad consensus and protect against the temporary passions of a narrow majority. Yet, we inexplicably allow decisions of equal or greater magnitude to be decided by 50 percent plus one vote in a referendum.

To make referendums a legitimate tool for vital issues, three reforms are essential. First, decisions of constitutional or irreversible magnitude must require a supermajority, such as 60 percent. Second, strict minimum turnout thresholds must be met for a referendum to be binding; a major structural change should not be enacted if half the country stays home. Finally, governments must separate the negotiation of a treaty from its ratification by utilizing neutral, citizen-led assemblies to draft the ballot language and establish objective, non-partisan educational materials.

Referendums can work, but only if we stop treating them like simple public opinion polls and start treating them like the constitutional mechanisms they actually are.
