Tide's emergence as an underground currency isn't really mysterious — it's a textbook case of what economists call the conditions for "moneyness." For something to function as money in an informal market, it has to be widely desired, easily recognizable, hard to counterfeit, durable, and reasonably divisible. Tide checks every box in a way that, say, Cheerios or Coca-Cola do not.

Start with demand. Procter & Gamble has spent decades and billions making Tide the dominant detergent in American households; surveys consistently show it commands brand loyalty rivaled by few consumer goods. People who would happily buy generic ketchup insist on Tide. That means a fence can move bottles quickly in any neighborhood bodega or flea market, often at 50–75% of retail.

Then consider the unit economics. A jug of Tide runs $10–$20, a convenient denomination — bigger than a candy bar, smaller than electronics. Unlike iPhones or cigarettes, Tide isn't serialized, age-restricted, or tax-stamped, so there's no paper trail. Unlike copper wire or jewelry, it doesn't need a specialized buyer. Unlike food, it doesn't spoil; a bottle sitting in a stash house keeps its value indefinitely.

Retail vulnerability matters too. Tide is sold in open aisles, often unsecured, in stores that prosecute shoplifting lightly. The orange jug is instantly identifiable from across a parking lot — which is a feature, not a bug, for a currency.

Put it together and Tide isn't a weird choice. It's almost the optimal choice: a Veblen-ish brand premium attached to a commodity nobody questions you for owning.

The Tide-as-currency phenomenon makes sense once you understand the economics of informal markets and the particular properties that make an item valuable in the underground economy. Tide has become a de facto street currency for several interlocking reasons.

First, laundry detergent satisfies what economists call "universal demand." Almost every household needs it, regardless of income level or geography. This means a dealer can always find someone willing to accept it in exchange for cash, drugs, or other goods. Unlike specialized items, Tide moves easily.

Second, Tide has a stable, easily verifiable value. Street participants can quickly assess quality and quantity. A bottle of Tide has a consistent retail price, making it simple to establish exchange rates. This predictability is essential for any currency, formal or informal.

Third, the product is highly portable and storable. It's compact enough to carry in a jacket pocket, doesn't spoil, and doesn't require special handling. Shoplifters recognized this and began targeting Tide specifically, flooding secondary markets with stolen detergent.

Someone can trade stolen Tide for cash or other items faster than they could liquidate most other stolen goods. Retailers noticed the spike in Tide theft precisely because organized shoplifting networks had identified this arbitrage opportunity.

Other brands like Gain or Persil lack Tide's market share and brand recognition, making them less useful in this informal ecosystem.

The mystery of Tide's emergence as informal currency isn't really a mystery at all — it's a textbook case of what economists since Carl Menger have understood about how money spontaneously emerges from barter. A good becomes money when it possesses certain qualities: it must be widely desired, durable, divisible enough, recognizable, hard to counterfeit, and liquid (easily resold). Tide checks every one of those boxes in a way that competing detergents and most other household brands do not.

Start with universal demand. Everybody, regardless of income, washes clothes. Unlike, say, premium coffee or organic food, laundry detergent is not an aspirational good that some households skip; it is a baseline necessity. That means the resale market is enormous and geographically uniform — a bottle of Tide is as fungible in the Bronx as it is in Boise.

Next, brand dominance. Procter & Gamble has spent decades and billions of advertising dollars making Tide synonymous with laundry detergent. It commands something like a third of the U.S. market and is priced at a substantial premium over store brands. Consumers genuinely believe — rightly or wrongly — that Tide cleans better, and they pay accordingly. That premium price is essential: it gives the bottle a high value-to-volume ratio, making it worth stealing in the first place. A $20 jug is more transportable wealth than a $4 jug of generic detergent.

Then consider verification. Drug-deal currencies need to be quickly authenticated in dim stairwells and parking lots. Tide's orange jug and bullseye logo are unmistakable from across the street, and the product is effectively impossible to counterfeit at a scale that would matter — nobody is going to set up a fake-Tide operation when the originals are sitting on store shelves to be grabbed.

Finally, fencing. Stolen electronics require a buyer who wants that specific item; stolen jewelry requires a fence who can move it. Tide, by contrast, has an instant secondary market: bodegas, flea markets, and individual consumers will buy it at a discount with no questions asked because they were going to buy detergent anyway. The retailer essentially launders the goods just by shelving them.

Compare this with alternatives. Razor blades are heavily locked up. Baby formula has expiration concerns and a narrower buyer base. Liquor has age-restricted resale. Cigarettes carry tax stamps. Tide sits in the sweet spot: high price, universal demand, easy to grab, impossible to trace, trivial to resell.

In short, Tide didn't become street money because of anything mystical about detergent. It became money because Procter & Gamble built the perfect commodity: expensive, ubiquitous, branded, and unregulated. Drug dealers are just the most efficient price-discoverers in the room.

We should be cautious before treating the "Tide as drug currency" story as established fact. The New York magazine piece is a fun read and the anecdotes are striking — the detective who coined "liquid gold," the cases of guys pushing shopping carts full of orange jugs — but the underlying claim that Tide functions as a meaningful medium of exchange in drug markets rests on a fairly thin evidentiary base: a handful of officers in a handful of cities, plus the inferential leap that because Tide is being stolen in bulk, it must be circulating as money.

There is a simpler explanation that doesn't require inventing a parallel monetary system: organized retail theft. Shoplifting rings have existed for decades, and they target whatever combines high retail price, high consumer demand, and low security. In the past, the favored items have been razor blades, infant formula, over-the-counter medication, and small electronics. Stores responded by locking those products behind plexiglass or moving them behind the counter. Tide, sitting openly on shelves in the back of every drugstore and supermarket, was the next logical target. The thieves resell to bodegas and flea-market vendors at roughly half retail. That's not currency — that's fencing, the same model used for every other hot product.

Where does the drug angle come from? Probably from the fact that some addicts shoplift to support their habit, and some of those addicts trade stolen Tide directly to their dealers in lieu of cash. That's real, and it's been documented for other goods too — meat, electronics, even gift cards. But the leap from "an addict sometimes pays a dealer in stolen goods" to "Tide is street currency" is journalistically irresistible and analytically sloppy. Dealers presumably accept Tide for the same reason pawnshops accept jewelry: it can be converted to cash. They aren't pricing eight-balls in fluid ounces of detergent.

The "why Tide and not Cheer?" question, then, partly answers itself and partly dissolves. To the extent Tide is being singled out by thieves, it's because it commands the highest retail premium in the category, has unmatched brand recognition, and is heavy enough to feel valuable but light enough to carry. To the extent Tide is supposedly favored by dealers specifically, the premise may simply be overstated — a vivid frame imposed on what is really a story about retail loss prevention.

This matters because policy responses follow narrative. If we think there's a "Tide economy" in the underworld, we get sensational coverage and perhaps misguided attention. If we recognize this as ordinary organized shoplifting with a colorful subplot, we focus on the boring but effective fixes: better in-store security, tighter regulation of secondary resellers, and the long-running effort to reduce the addiction that drives petty theft in the first place.

The Tide phenomenon is worth thinking about less as a quirk of criminal taste and more as a window into how Procter & Gamble's marketing apparatus works — and how informal economies parasitize formal ones.

Consider what it takes for a branded product to become "money." It needs the kind of trust ordinarily reserved for sovereign currencies: every party to a transaction must believe that the next party down the line will also accept it at a predictable value. National brands manufacture exactly this kind of trust, and they spend enormous sums doing so. Tide has been one of the most heavily advertised products in American history, dating back to its 1946 launch as the first mass-market synthetic detergent. P&G has cultivated the perception that Tide is qualitatively better than competitors and worth paying more for. That perception is the foundation of its value as an exchange medium.

Now layer on three structural features of the U.S. retail landscape. First, the price gap between Tide and store-brand detergents is wide and stable — often two-to-one or more. Second, Tide is sold in essentially every grocery store, drugstore, dollar store, and big-box retailer in the country, meaning there is no geographic discount or premium to arbitrage; a jug is a jug. Third, unlike alcohol, tobacco, pharmaceuticals, or firearms, detergent is subject to no licensing, age restriction, tax stamp, or serial number. It is one of the highest-value unregulated consumer goods in America by ounce.

Compare to plausible alternatives. Coca-Cola is cheaper per unit and bulkier. iPhones are too expensive, too traceable (serial numbers, activation locks), and too narrowly desired across socioeconomic strata. Cigarettes vary in price and legality across state lines and carry tax stamps. Gift cards have largely replaced cash in some underground transactions, but they require activation and can be frozen. Tide is, almost uniquely, a high-value, anonymous, untraceable, universally demanded, easy-to-resell good.

There's also a cultural dimension worth naming. Tide's customer base genuinely cuts across class lines in a way few premium brands do. Wealthy suburbanites and low-income renters alike buy the orange jug. That cross-class demand is what makes the secondary market liquid: a bodega in a poor neighborhood can resell stolen Tide to its regular customers without needing to find some specialized buyer. The shadow economy here is essentially piggybacking on P&G's success in being everyone's detergent.

The deeper lesson is that "money" is not a category defined by governments. It's defined by liquidity, trust, and demand. When a private company manages to produce a good that is more liquid, more trusted, and more universally demanded than many official currencies in fragile economies, it shouldn't surprise us that informal markets discover this. Tide isn't strange currency. It's just very good currency.

Journalists point to law enforcement officials citing the trend, retail security managers noting organized theft, and occasional anecdotes from arrested dealers. This is thin evidence indeed. Neither source undergoes the scrutiny that academic claims would face.

More fundamentally, the narrative conflates several distinct phenomena. Yes, shoplifting networks focus on high-value items like Tide that can be quickly fenced. Yes, drug addicts sometimes steal to support habits rather than dealing at all. Yes, barter occurs in illegal markets when participants wish to avoid cash trails. These facts do not add up to Tide functioning as a genuine currency replacing legal tender in street-level drug dealing.

The Tide narrative conflates fence-value with currency status, creating a sensation that evaporates under scrutiny.
