If Young People Could Vote, What Would Change?

There are plenty of reasons many young people don’t vote. For one thing, they consider the political system is broken, and they have good cause to be cynical. For another, they just aren’t in the habit yet. “Once you’ve made your first vote,” a developmental psychologist told The Times, “it becomes part of your routine.”

So what if the law allowed Americans to start that routine earlier? If teenagers under 18 could vote, how would the presidential candidates change their campaigns? What issues would students want them to concentrate on?

This forum is a collaboration with The Learning Network, and the responses are from students who commented in its Student Opinion series.

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

If teenagers under 18 could vote, the most immediate change would be in how candidates talk—and where they show up. We'd see fewer rallies in retirement-heavy Florida condo communities and more campaign stops at high schools, community colleges, and on platforms like YouTube and Tumblr, where younger Americans already gather. Both Obama and Romney have courted youth this cycle, but mostly through college outreach. Expanding the franchise downward would force a real conversation with people who don't yet have a vote, a paycheck, or a tax bill, but who live with the consequences of every long-term decision adults make.

The issues would shift accordingly. Student loan debt, now past $1 trillion, would move from a talking point to a central plank. The cost of college, the quality of public high schools, and the federal role in education (think No Child Left Behind and the Common Core rollout) would get sustained attention. Climate change—conspicuously absent from this year's debates until Hurricane Sandy—would be harder to ignore, since young voters consistently poll as more worried about it. Gun violence in schools, immigration policy affecting classmates under DACA, and LGBT equality would likely gain ground too.

There's a fair worry that teens would simply mirror their parents, but research on adolescent civic development suggests otherwise: voting habits formed early stick. The bigger risk isn't that young voters would distort politics—it's that candidates, faced with an electorate stretching down to 16, would finally have to plan past the next quarterly poll.

If the voting age were lowered to 16, candidates would immediately be forced to overhaul their outreach. Instead of pandering exclusively to seniors in Florida or Ohio through television ads and Medicare promises, campaigns would pivot aggressively to social media. Politicians could no longer afford to treat young Americans as a passive audience or a mere backdrop for photo opportunities; they would have to actively engage them where they actually congregate.

The policy landscape would also undergo a dramatic realignment. Today, the national debate over education often treats students as subjects rather than stakeholders. If teenagers had the ballot, candidates would have to directly answer for the soaring cost of college tuition and the crushing burden of student loan debt—issues that are deeply terrifying to high schoolers facing a sluggish, post-Recession economy. We would see concrete proposals for job creation tailored specifically to youth unemployment, rather than vague promises about general economic growth.

Climate change, which received shockingly little attention in the presidential debates until Hurricane Sandy forced the issue last week, would become a top priority for a demographic that will actually inherit the environmental consequences. Ultimately, enfranchising teenagers would force Washington to stop borrowing against the future and start investing in it.

I'm skeptical that lowering the voting age would change much of anything. The premise of the question assumes teenagers have distinctive political preferences that are currently being ignored, but the evidence suggests young people who do vote already align closely with the 18-to-24 bracket — left-leaning, but not dramatically so on most issues outside of social questions like same-sex marriage.

More importantly, turnout among newly eligible 18-year-olds is famously poor. In 2008, voters aged 18-24 turned out at roughly 49 percent, well below older cohorts, and 2012 is unlikely to break that pattern. If we extended the franchise to 16-year-olds, we should expect even lower turnout from that group. The actual marginal voter added by such a reform would be small, and probably not demographically distinctive enough to swing campaign priorities.

There's also a deeper question the prompt glosses over: why is 18 the threshold? It's tied to the draft-era logic of the 26th Amendment — if you can be sent to war, you can vote. That's a coherent principle. "If you might develop a voting habit" is not, by itself, a principled basis for franchise expansion. We don't extend other rights (contracts, marriage without consent, criminal majority) to 16-year-olds for similar reasons.

If we want young people to vote more, the answer isn't lowering the age — it's civic education, automatic registration at 18, and making Election Day a holiday. Those reforms would do more for youth participation than handing ballots to high school sophomores who, with respect, are mostly not asking for them.

Here's a contrarian thought: extending the vote to teenagers might actually make politics more conservative on some issues, not less. The standard assumption is that young voters are liberal, and on social issues — gay marriage, marijuana, immigration — they generally are. But teenagers are also more religious than their parents in some communities, more attached to their hometowns, and often more economically anxious because they're staring directly at a brutal job market.

Consider the issues that might matter most to a 16-year-old in a working-class town in Ohio or western Pennsylvania. Not abstract progressive causes, but: Will there be jobs when I graduate? Can my family afford to keep me in school? Is the local factory closing? These are the issues Romney has tried, with mixed success, to make central. A teenage electorate might not be the unbroken liberal bloc commentators assume.

There's also the question of family influence. Sixteen-year-olds still live at home and are heavily shaped by their parents' politics. Studies of political socialization consistently show that adolescents' partisan identification tracks closely with their parents'. Enfranchising them might effectively give parents an extra vote, weighted toward whichever demographics have more children — which, in current America, skews somewhat religious and somewhat conservative.

I don't say this to oppose lowering the voting age. There are good arguments for it. But the breezy assumption that teen voters would push the country leftward and force candidates to embrace a youth-progressive agenda deserves more scrutiny. The actual electoral consequences are less predictable than the prompt's framing suggests, and that uncertainty is itself an argument for taking the experiment seriously.

Lowering the voting age to 17 or 16 would add a few million potential voters, but without parallel investments in making voting accessible, we'd see the same low participation rates. Early voting locations near college campuses, online registration, same-day registration—these interventions have proven effects on youth turnout. An age change without infrastructure investment would be largely symbolic.

The focus should be on registration modernization and removing barriers, not age manipulation. We're told young people need to "make voting part of their routine," but we make registration difficult, polling places inconvenient, and election day a workday. The answer is systemic reform of how Americans vote, not a modest expansion of eligibility.

If we really want young people engaged, we should meet them where they are with systems designed for their participation. That's a more honest and effective agenda than tinkering with age limits.

From a purely strategic standpoint, extending voting rights to young teenagers would dramatically alter campaign calculus. Candidates spend enormous resources targeting demographic groups, and none is more coveted than the youth vote—except none is harder to mobilize.

Adding millions of voters from ages 16 to 17 would reshape Electoral College strategies, swing state calculations, and advertising markets. Campaigns targeting these voters would need to start earlier, use different media, and make different policy commitments. The question of what issues they'd emphasize is therefore less important than how early and how substantively engagement would begin.

We might see a new industry of youth political consulting, specialized get-out-the-vote operations for high schools and colleges, and new forms of youth-oriented political advertising. Political parties would invest heavily in registration drives at younger ages.

The unintended consequences could be significant. Campaigns might emphasize issues that resonate with younger voters but alienate older ones, deepening generational polarization. Alternatively, parties might compete more aggressively on youth-oriented policies, potentially producing better outcomes for younger Americans.

But I suspect the biggest change would be cultural. Politics would become more legitimate in young people's eyes, reducing the cynicism the question mentions. More young people would discuss elections in schools and homes, normalizing political engagement as a coming-of-age experience rather than an afterthought.

The vote itself isn't just a mechanism—it's a symbol of membership in the political community. Extending that symbol to young people matters symbolically and practically.

The debate about youth voting rights often ignores a crucial dimension: federalism. While the question addresses presidential elections, voting age rules are primarily state-level matters for most elections. Lowering the presidential voting age would require federal constitutional action, but states retain significant control over other elections.

This suggests the question's framing is somewhat artificial. If we want young people voting, we might achieve more by working state-by-state than by pursuing a constitutional amendment. Several states already allow 17-year-olds to vote in primaries if they'll be 18 by the general election—a modest but meaningful step.

More ambitious reforms might include automatic registration at 18, same-day registration, and mail-in voting systems that reduce friction for all voters, including young ones. These structural changes have bipartisan support and proven effectiveness.

The question of what "would change" if young people could vote ultimately depends on whether we think young voters would be substantively different from older ones. I suspect they would, but not in the ways typically assumed. Young people care deeply about education, opportunity, and their communities' futures. Those concerns are legitimate and underrepresented in current politics.

Rather than debating age limits endlessly, let's focus on removing barriers to participation. If young people want to vote, we should make it as easy as possible. The rest—issue priorities, campaign strategies, policy outcomes—will follow from genuine democratic engagement.
