Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Pentagon's own numbers tell the story: an estimated 26,000 service members experienced unwanted sexual contact in 2012, up from 19,000 two years earlier, while only about 3,300 incidents were even reported and a tiny fraction reached trial. When the man running the Air Force's sexual assault prevention office is himself arrested for groping a woman, and an Army sergeant at Fort Hood charged with similar duties is under investigation, it is no longer credible to argue that the current system needs only modest tinkering. The problem is structural, and the fix has to be structural too.

The core defect is the role of the commanding officer. Under the Uniform Code of Military Justice, the convening authority decides whether charges go forward, selects the jury pool, and—as Lieutenant General Craig Franklin showed when he overturned Lieutenant Colonel James Wilkerson's sexual assault conviction in February—can wipe out a jury verdict without explanation and without review. No civilian prosecutor or judge wields that combination of powers. For a victim deciding whether to report an attack by a peer or superior, the knowledge that her case will be evaluated by someone in her chain of command, who may know the accused personally and who has institutional incentives to protect unit cohesion, is a powerful deterrent to coming forward.

Senator Kirsten Gillibrand's proposal to move prosecution decisions for serious crimes out of the chain of command and into the hands of independent military prosecutors is therefore the right starting point. Allies have already done this: the United Kingdom, Australia, Canada, and Israel all stripped commanders of charging authority for serious offenses without any apparent collapse of good order and discipline. Secretary Hagel's more incremental ideas—limiting the post-trial clemency power, standardizing training, adding victim advocates—are useful but insufficient if commanders retain the final say on who gets prosecuted.

That said, "overhaul" should not mean abandoning military justice for the civilian courts. Commanders must remain responsible for climate, prevention, and lesser discipline; deployed units need a functioning legal system of their own. What needs to change is the conflict of interest at the heart of felony prosecutions. Reform the procedures, yes—but reform them deeply enough to remove commanders from decisions they have repeatedly proven unable to make impartially.

Take the Prosecutors Out of the Chain of Command

The Pentagon's own anonymous survey suggests roughly 26,000 service members experienced unwanted sexual contact last year, while only 3,374 cases were formally reported and a tiny fraction ever reached court-martial. That gap is not a paperwork problem. It is a structural one, and it cannot be closed with another round of training videos and posters.

The single most important reform is the one Senator Kirsten Gillibrand has put on the table: strip commanding officers of the authority to decide whether serious crimes go to trial, and place that decision with independent military prosecutors. Every other professional military in the English-speaking world — Britain, Canada, Australia, Israel — has already moved in this direction, and none of them has collapsed into indiscipline. The argument that "good order and discipline" requires a colonel to personally decide whether his sergeant is charged with rape is a tradition dressed up as a necessity.

The chain-of-command model creates conflicts of interest at every step. A commander is judged on the readiness and reputation of his unit. He often knows the accused better than the accuser. He may have promoted the accused. He may himself be the subject of a separate complaint. Asking that same officer to weigh charging decisions impartially is asking too much of human nature, and the data on retaliation against reporters — over 60 percent in some surveys — show that subordinates know it.

Procedural tweaks matter too. Article 60, which lets a convening authority unilaterally overturn a jury's guilty verdict, as Lt. Gen. Franklin did in the Wilkerson case in Aviano, must go. Victims need independent legal counsel, not just a victim advocate who reports up the same chain. Statutes of limitations should be aligned with civilian norms.

But none of these fixes will deliver what an independent prosecutorial corps would: the simple message to victims that the person deciding their case has no stake in the unit's image, and the message to perpetrators that a friendly C.O. cannot make the problem disappear. The military has had decades to fix this from the inside. It is time to admit that the inside is the problem.

Don't Confuse Reform with Dismantling

The wave of revulsion at recent revelations is entirely justified, but revulsion is not a policy. Before Congress hands the military justice system over to outside lawyers, it should ask whether the alternative being offered will actually reduce sexual assault — or whether it will simply move the failure to a different desk.

The Uniform Code of Military Justice is not a vestige of bygone days. It is a functioning legal system whose central feature — commander responsibility — exists precisely because the military is not a civilian workplace. A platoon leader who cannot enforce discipline within his unit cannot lead that unit into combat. Stripping commanders of authority over serious offenses sends a clear signal that they are no longer responsible for the behavior of their troops. That is not a small adjustment; it is a redefinition of command itself.

Better, then, to attack the specific failures. The Article 60 power to set aside a court-martial conviction should be sharply curtailed; there is no reason a single general should be able to nullify a panel verdict on a felony. Convening authorities should be required to follow the recommendation of their staff judge advocate when that recommendation is to prosecute, and to give written reasons in any case where they decline. The Special Victims Counsel program the Air Force has piloted should be expanded service-wide. Investigators in sexual assault cases should be specially trained and report to senior, vetted officers outside the accused's unit.

Above all, commanders who tolerate a hostile climate must themselves be relieved and, where warranted, prosecuted. The Air Force's recent embarrassment — the officer in charge of sexual assault prevention himself arrested for groping a woman — is not an argument for abolishing command authority. It is an argument for using it.

A military that cannot police itself does not deserve the public's trust. But a military whose officers are told they are no longer trusted to police their own troops will not become more disciplined. It will become less so.

The Problem Isn't the Code — It's the Culture

Everyone arguing about Article 60, convening authorities, and independent prosecutors is debating the plumbing while the house is on fire. You can reroute every pipe in the Uniform Code of Military Justice and you will not change the basic fact that hundreds of thousands of young men have been taught, implicitly, that women in uniform are interlopers whose bodies are part of the bargain of service. That is a cultural pathology, not a procedural one, and law reform alone will not touch it.

Consider what the surveys actually show. Most assaults are not stranger attacks; they involve fellow service members, often in the victim's own unit, often after alcohol, often in barracks. The reason victims do not report is not, in the main, that they think the convening authority will bungle the case. It is that they correctly anticipate ostracism, blame, retaliatory paperwork, and the destruction of their own careers. A new prosecutor in a distant office cannot fix that. Only peers and immediate superiors can.

This means the lever that matters is leadership, all the way down. Senior NCOs and junior officers are the ones who set the tone of the unit. If they treat sexual harassment as a punchline and the woman who complains as the problem, no statutory change in Washington will reach the squad bay. If they treat predators the way they treat thieves — as people unfit to wear the uniform — the problem will shrink.

That argues for reforms aimed at command climate, not just charging authority: rigorous, anonymous unit-level climate surveys whose results affect commanders' fitness reports; meaningful consequences for officers whose units show persistent problems; an end to the practice of quietly transferring offenders to other bases.

By all means tighten the legal procedures. End the post-trial clemency power, give victims their own lawyers, professionalize SVU-style investigators. But anyone selling these reforms as the answer is selling a comforting illusion. The military will stop assaulting its own when it decides, culturally, that the assailant — not the victim — is the disgrace.

The Civilian System Isn't a Model — It's a Warning

Much of the current debate assumes that if only military sexual assault cases were handled "like civilian cases," justice would follow. This is a fantasy. The civilian criminal justice system handles sexual violence badly too. Roughly half of rapes are never reported to police; of those reported, only a fraction lead to arrest; of arrests, a smaller fraction to conviction. Rape kits sit untested in warehouses from Detroit to Los Angeles. Acquaintance rape — the dominant pattern in both worlds — is notoriously difficult to prosecute anywhere.

This matters because the loudest reform proposals treat removal from the chain of command as a panacea. It is not. An independent military prosecutor will still face she-said/he-said evidence, intoxicated witnesses, delayed reports, and juries (or panels) reluctant to convict on contested facts. The conviction rate will rise modestly, perhaps; it will not approach what victims and advocates are hoping for, and the disappointment that follows could discredit the whole reform enterprise.

That does not mean nothing should change. It means we should be honest about what changes will actually accomplish. Three reforms are genuinely promising.

First, abolish the convening authority's power to overturn or reduce a jury verdict after trial. There is no civilian analogue and no good justification; it exists only to embarrass the military when an officer uses it, as one recently did.

Second, give every complainant a dedicated lawyer. The Air Force's Special Victims Counsel pilot has, by early accounts, sharply increased the willingness of victims to proceed with cases. Expand it.

Third, professionalize the investigators and prosecutors who handle these cases, with specialized training and longer tenures, rather than rotating generalists through the work.

These reforms will not produce dramatic conviction-rate headlines, because the underlying evidentiary problems are real and not unique to the military. But they will give victims more agency, reduce the most flagrant abuses of command discretion, and improve the competence of the cases that do go forward. That is more than the grand structural overhaul will deliver, and it can be done now.

Start with the Victim

Most of the current argument is structured around the accused — who decides to charge him, who can overturn his conviction, who sits on his panel. That is the wrong starting point. If you want a military justice system that actually addresses sexual assault, design it around the person who has to walk into an office and report being raped by a colleague she will see at formation tomorrow morning.

From that vantage point, several reforms are non-negotiable.

A complainant must have her own lawyer, independent of the prosecution, from the moment she reports. The Air Force's Special Victims Counsel program has demonstrated, in just a few months, that victims with counsel are dramatically more likely to stay with the process. This should be law across all services.

A complainant must be able to transfer, immediately and without career penalty, out of the unit of the accused. Right now the burden of separation falls on her — informally, through ostracism and reassignment — while the accused often remains in place. Reverse the default.

A complainant's prior sexual history, mental health records, and personal communications must be protected from fishing expeditions by defense counsel beyond what civilian rape-shield laws allow. The military's looser discovery norms are weaponized against victims.

A commander's power to grant post-trial clemency — to wave away a jury's guilty verdict, as happened in the Wilkerson case — must end. Whatever its origin, it now functions as a betrayal of every panel that does the hard work of convicting.

And retaliation against reporters must be prosecuted as a distinct offense, aggressively. Surveys show a majority of victims who report experience some form of professional retaliation. Until that changes, every other reform is theater.

The broader question of whether prosecutorial authority should be moved outside the chain of command can wait — or rather, it should be informed by whether these victim-centered reforms work. Begin where the system fails most concretely. The architecture of command can be debated; the indignity inflicted on victims cannot.

Yes, Overhaul It

The defenders of the current system keep insisting that command authority over discipline is essential to military effectiveness. Where is the evidence? Britain, Canada, Australia, and Israel have all moved prosecutorial decisions for serious crimes outside the chain of command. None of those militaries has dissolved into anarchy. The British Army still functions. The IDF still functions. The argument from "good order and discipline" is an assertion, not a finding.

Meanwhile the evidence on the American side is overwhelming and damning. An estimated 26,000 incidents last year. A reporting rate around 13 percent. Of reports, a small fraction prosecuted. Of prosecutions, convictions sometimes overturned by a single general's signature. The officer running the Air Force's sexual assault prevention office arrested for sexual battery. The Army sergeant at Fort Hood, in the same job, under investigation for pandering. This is not a system with isolated flaws. This is a system whose incentives are aligned against the victim at every stage.

An overhaul means more than tinkering with Article 60. It means a standing corps of military prosecutors, independent of operational commanders, making charging decisions on serious offenses. It means independent investigators who do not answer to the accused's command. It means trial panels selected by lot rather than hand-picked by the convening authority. It means meaningful victim representation. It means treating retaliation against reporters as a court-martial offense in itself.

Critics warn that this will undermine commanders. But commanders' authority over training, deployment, promotion, and the dozens of lesser disciplinary tools at their disposal would remain untouched. What they would lose is the power to decide whether their own subordinates face felony charges — a power that, in case after case, they have proven unable to exercise impartially.

The military has been promising for two decades that it is taking sexual assault seriously. Each scandal — Tailhook, Aberdeen, the Air Force Academy, Lackland — has produced new task forces, new training, new promises. The numbers have not budged. At some point, sustained failure is itself evidence that the structure is incapable of self-correction. We are well past that point.
