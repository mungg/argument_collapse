Should Doping Be Allowed in Sports?

This week a judo fighter became the first athlete to be expelled from the Olympics for failing an in-competition doping test, in his case, for marijuana. And on Tuesday, it was announced that the defending 50k Olympic race walk champion, Alex Schwazer, was expelled after testing positive for the blood booster erythropoietin, or EPO. Meanwhile, the “unbelievable” times of one swimmer have raised suspicions of doping.

But has doping become so common that we shouldn’t even bother with testing, or are the dangers to athletes and fair competition so great that enforcement must continue?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The recent expulsions in London illustrate exactly why we should not give up on enforcement, even as cynicism about doping grows. Yes, the cat-and-mouse game is exhausting. The BALCO scandal, Operación Puerto, Marion Jones, Floyd Landis, and the ongoing federal investigation into Lance Armstrong have all suggested that sophisticated doping is far more widespread than positive tests reveal. The temptation to throw up our hands — to say "let them all dope, at least it would be honest" — is understandable. It is also wrong.

The libertarian argument for legalization rests on two claims: that doping is ubiquitous anyway, and that adults should be free to assume risks with their own bodies. Both are weaker than they appear. If doping were legalized, the pharmacological arms race would not stop at EPO, human growth hormone, and anabolic steroids. It would push toward gene doping, untested cocktails, and ever-higher doses, because the marginal athlete must match whatever the leaders are taking. The "choice" to dope becomes coercive: every clean athlete who wants to compete at the top level must either dope or quit. We have seen this dynamic in cycling for two decades, and it is precisely what fair-play rules are designed to prevent.

The health stakes are real. The deaths of young cyclists in the late 1980s and early 1990s, attributed to EPO-thickened blood, and the long catalog of steroid-related harms — cardiovascular disease, liver damage, psychiatric effects — are not scare stories. Legalization would push these risks onto teenagers in development pipelines, who can least give informed consent.

Enforcement is imperfect, but it is improving. The biological passport, introduced in cycling and now spreading, catches abnormalities rather than specific molecules, making evasion harder. Retroactive testing of stored samples deters athletes who might otherwise gamble on today's detection limits.

We should, however, distinguish performance enhancers from recreational drugs like marijuana, which plainly do not belong in the same category. Expelling a judoka for cannabis trivializes the genuine fight against EPO and steroids. Smarter rules, not surrender, are the answer.

The recent expulsions from the London Games make plain that doping remains a stubborn reality, but the idea that we should simply surrender to it is a dangerous abdication of responsibility. The call to stop testing because cheating is widespread mistakes a difficult battle for an unwinnable one. The fundamental case against allowing doping rests not on a romantic vision of pure sport, but on the concrete harms it inflicts on athletes and the corruption of competition itself.

First, the health risks are not hypothetical. EPO thickens the blood, drastically increasing the chance of stroke or heart attack during endurance events; anabolic steroids ravage the liver and cardiovascular system. A permissive regime would pressure all athletes, from the elite to the amateur, to adopt these dangerous substances simply to stay in the game. The result would be a lethal pharmacological arms race where the winners are those best able to tolerate poison, not those who trained hardest. The judo fighter's marijuana positive and the race walker's EPO case are not equivalent moral offenses, but they share a core problem: both involve athletes seeking an edge outside the rules, undermining the premise that the contest is a test of human talent, discipline, and strategic preparation.

Allowing doping would also fundamentally alter the meaning of athletic achievement. When a swimmer shatters a world record, the public wonder is not just about speed but about what a human being can accomplish within a given set of boundaries. If those boundaries vanish, records become meaningless benchmarks of pharmaceutical access rather than markers of human potential. The “unbelievable” times mentioned in the prompt provoke suspicion precisely because we instinctively understand that sport requires a level playing field. Fairness is not a naïve luxury; it is the very product that fans pay to see and that athletes dedicate their lives to pursue. Eradicating doping entirely may be impossible, but robust testing and sanctions remain essential to protect the health of athletes and to preserve the integrity of the contest as a human endeavor, not a chemical one.

The argument usually suggests that athletes are already cheating, the testing regime is always a step behind the pharmacologists, and legalizing performance-enhancing drugs (PEDs) would finally level the playing field. High school and collegiate athletes, desperate for scholarships or professional contracts, would feel immense pressure to begin pharmacological regimens before their bodies are even fully developed. The public health damage to youth sports would be catastrophic.

Sport is fundamentally a celebration of human potential, natural physiological limits, and relentless psychological dedication. If we lift the ban on PEDs, the Olympic Games will devolve into a competition between rival pharmaceutical laboratories rather than athletes. The anti-doping system may be flawed, and the cat-and-mouse game will undoubtedly continue, but holding the line is the only way to protect both the competitors and the integrity of the game.

The system entrenches inequality while pretending to police it.

A regulated approach — perhaps permitting certain substances above a safety threshold, with full disclosure — would acknowledge what fans already half-suspect when they watch a world record fall. It would be more honest than our current ritual of moral outrage every two years, in which we feign shock that athletes pushed to superhuman performance turn out to be using superhuman means.

If Olympic champions are openly using anabolic steroids and EPO, teenage athletes chasing scholarships and contracts will follow. The doping debate is usually framed as a binary — ban everything versus allow everything — but the more honest question is: where do we draw the line, and on what principle?

Consider the substances at issue this week. Marijuana is on the prohibited list, and a judo fighter has lost his Olympic dreams over it. Yet marijuana confers no athletic advantage and may even impair performance. Its presence on the list reflects moralism, not sports science. EPO, on the other hand, dramatically enhances endurance performance and carries serious cardiovascular risks. Lumping these together under the banner of "doping" is intellectually lazy and corrodes the legitimacy of the whole enterprise.

A coherent anti-doping policy would be guided by two clear principles: athlete safety and meaningful competitive fairness. Substances that pose significant health risks at performance-enhancing doses — EPO, anabolic steroids, human growth hormone — should remain banned, and aggressively tested for. Recreational drugs that don't enhance performance should be a matter for athletes' national federations and personal lives, not Olympic expulsion. Caffeine, once banned, was sensibly removed from the list years ago; the same logic should apply to marijuana.

We should also be more thoughtful about the gray areas. Athletes routinely sleep in altitude tents to boost red blood cell production — a physiological effect identical to mild EPO use, but achieved "naturally." Cortisone injections, therapeutic use exemptions for ADHD medications, hypoxic training: these are accepted enhancements. The line between training innovation and cheating is blurrier than the rhetoric admits.

What this means in practice is that we should continue and strengthen testing for genuinely dangerous performance enhancers, while pruning the prohibited list of substances that don't belong there. We should invest in the biological passport approach, which catches doping patterns rather than relying on point-in-time tests that elite cheaters can evade. And we should be transparent about therapeutic use exemptions, which currently function as a loophole the wealthy and well-connected can exploit.

The choice isn't between prohibition and permissiveness. It's between a principled, science-based regime and the incoherent mess we have now.

The doping debate exposes a deep contradiction at the heart of modern elite sport that we prefer not to face. We have built a system that demands superhuman performances, rewards them with millions in endorsement money and national glory, and then expresses shock when athletes use the available tools to deliver what we are paying them to deliver.

They receive therapeutic use exemptions for medications that, in another context, would be prohibited. The Kenyan runner training at 8,000 feet has a different "natural" oxygen-carrying capacity than the Dutch runner training at sea level. Where, exactly, is the natural baseline against which doping is measured?

This isn't a defense of EPO injections or anabolic steroids. It's a plea for intellectual honesty. The category "doping" is a moving, culturally constructed line — caffeine used to be on it, isn't now; beta-blockers are banned in archery but not in marathon running; the contraceptive pill manipulates hormones and is unregulated.

What we are really protecting when we test for doping is a narrative — the story that athletic excellence is a pure expression of human spirit. That narrative was always partly a fiction, and it has become harder to sustain as sport has professionalized and medicalized. But narratives matter. They are why we watch.

So here's my position: continue testing, because the alternative is worse, but stop pretending the line we draw is natural or self-evident. Be willing to revise the prohibited list based on evidence, not tradition. Acknowledge that the difference between a legal performance enhancer and an illegal one is often a political and historical accident. And stop treating athletes who get caught as uniquely wicked — they are operating in a system designed to extract maximum performance from them, and they respond to the incentives we have created. If we want cleaner sport, we should look at those incentives, not just at the athletes.

Let's try a different angle on this question: what is the actual purpose of sport, and does doping serve or subvert that purpose?

If the purpose of sport is to identify who can run fastest or lift heaviest by any means, then doping should obviously be allowed. We would let athletes use whatever chemistry, prosthetics, or genetic interventions they like, and the winner would be the optimized human. This is a coherent position. It is not, however, what most people actually want from sport.

What most people want is a contest of human qualities — talent, dedication, courage, ingenuity within rules — refereed in a way that makes the outcome meaningful. Rules in sport are not like laws against theft, which exist to prevent harm. They are constitutive: they define what the game is. Offside in soccer harms no one; it just makes soccer soccer. The rule against using your hands in soccer is similar. So is the rule against EPO in distance running. Without these arbitrary-seeming constraints, the activity dissolves into something else.

Once you see the rules as constitutive, the "everyone does it, so legalize it" argument loses force. The fact that some players handle the ball illicitly doesn't mean we should abandon the handball rule. It means we should referee better. The fact that some cyclists dope doesn't mean we should allow doping; it means our enforcement needs work.

The two cases that prompted this debate illustrate the point. Schwazer using EPO is a clear violation of a constitutive rule of distance racing — he was no longer doing the same thing as his competitors. The judo fighter testing positive for marijuana is a more questionable case; marijuana doesn't really change what judo is. The first is straightforward cheating; the second is a category error in our prohibited list.

So my answer is: no, doping should not be allowed, because the rules against it help define what sport is. But the specific list of prohibited substances should be regularly examined to ensure each item actually relates to the integrity of competition rather than to historical accident or moral panic. Constitutive rules deserve respect; arbitrary ones deserve scrutiny.
