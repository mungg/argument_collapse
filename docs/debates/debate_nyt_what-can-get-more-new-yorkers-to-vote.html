Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The puzzle of New York's anemic turnout is not really a puzzle. New Yorkers stay home because, in most districts, the contest that matters has already been decided behind closed doors. When the Democratic line is tantamount to election, and when incumbents face primaries gerrymandered into safety, the rational voter concludes — correctly — that her ballot is decorative. Tinkering with civic-mindedness will not fix a structural problem.

The most promising reforms target that structure. First, open the primaries. New York's closed system locks out roughly a quarter of the city's registered voters, including the fastest-growing bloc, independents. A semi-open or top-two primary, like California is now experimenting with, would force candidates to court a broader electorate and make June elections feel consequential.

Second, ease the mechanics. New York remains one of the most restrictive states in the country: no early voting, no no-excuse absentee balloting, a registration deadline 25 days before Election Day, and a separate federal-and-state primary day that confuses even attentive citizens. Consolidating primaries, adopting same-day or automatic registration, and permitting early voting are unglamorous but proven turnout boosters, as Minnesota and Oregon have shown.

Third, change what's on the ballot. Fusion voting already gives minor parties leverage here; instant-runoff voting in primaries would let voters support insurgents without "wasting" a vote, and would discourage the plurality-winner-with-18-percent outcomes that breed cynicism. Independent redistricting — which Albany again ducked this year — would do more than any get-out-the-vote drive.

Finally, civic habit matters. Move municipal elections to even years, invest in real civics instruction, and make the Board of Elections something other than a patronage punchline. Turnout is low because the system has been designed, intentionally or not, to keep it that way.

New York’s abysmal turnout is not a mystery to be solved with gimmicks or moral pleading. It is the predictable result of an electoral system designed, whether intentionally or through neglect, to discourage participation. If we are serious about getting more New Yorkers to vote, we need to stop treating voting as a privilege that citizens must earn by navigating bureaucratic hurdles and start treating it as a routine act facilitated by a modern, accessible state. The single most powerful change would be to bring New York’s voter registration and balloting practices into the twenty-first century.

The current system requires registration a full twenty-five days before an election. In a city of transients, renters, and young people, this arbitrary deadline disenfranchises thousands who become engaged in the final weeks of a campaign—precisely when the media coverage intensifies and conversations with neighbors start to matter. Same-day registration has been adopted in states like Maine and Wisconsin without chaos, and it demonstrably boosts turnout. Allowing eligible citizens to register and cast a regular ballot on Election Day would remove the single biggest administrative barrier to participation. Coupled with portable registration that follows voters when they move within the city, it would enfranchise a significant portion of the nearly two million eligible New Yorkers who sat out last year’s mayoral election.

The second hurdle is the relic of the single Tuesday vote. Holding elections on a workday, with polls open from 6 a.m. to 9 p.m., is incompatible with the lives of working parents, shift workers, and the city’s massive service-sector labor force. Early voting is the obvious answer. A fifteen-day early voting period, including weekends, would transform the voting experience. It would shorten lines on Election Day, give flexibility to people with unpredictable schedules, and create a more reflective civic ritual rather than a rushed, end-of-day chore. Voters could fix registration problems or find a convenient site, making the act of voting feel less like a high-stakes compliance test and more like a normal civic function.

Skeptics will say that non-voters are simply apathetic, but that misreads the evidence. When brought before boards of elections for driver’s license transactions or public assistance enrollment, millions of New Yorkers do not indicate they wish to forgo registration—they are simply never asked, thanks to poor implementation of the National Voter Registration Act. A truly automated system, where interacting with virtually any city agency prompts a seamless opt-out registration update, would place the burden on the state, not the citizen. It is not the electorate that has failed democracy; it is New York’s clunky, nineteenth-century machinery that has failed the electorate. Removing purposeful friction from the process is the surest way to make the city’s dazzlingly diverse populace feel that their voice belongs inside the voting booth.

In a city overwhelmingly dominated by one party, the Democratic primary effectively serves as the general election. Holding a single, unified primary would immediately boost participation by focusing media attention and civic energy onto a single day.

We currently lack early voting, we require specific excuses to cast absentee ballots, and our registration deadlines lock out newly interested citizens weeks before an election takes place. If all candidates ran on a single primary ballot and the top two advanced regardless of party, the November elections would finally become competitive, forcing candidates to appeal to a broader coalition.

The low turnout in New York City's congressional primaries reflects a deeper problem: many residents have concluded that their votes don't matter. In a city where registered Democrats outnumber Republicans by overwhelming margins, the real contest often happens in the primary, yet the primary itself draws only a tiny fraction of eligible voters. Reversing this requires addressing both the structural barriers that suppress participation and the cultural attitudes that discourage it.

One practical step would be to move New York's primary elections to the fall. The current June primary date coincides with the end of the school year, summer vacations, and peak outdoor activity. Voters are simply less focused on politics. Equally important, the state should adopt early voting and same-day registration, measures that have demonstrably increased turnout in other jurisdictions. These reforms reduce the logistical burden on citizens who might otherwise miss Election Day due to work schedules or last-minute decisions to participate.

Beyond structural changes, the parties themselves bear responsibility. Incumbent Democratic members of Congress in New York face little real primary opposition partly because the party apparatus discourages competitive races that might expose its candidates to scrutiny. More competitive primaries—with multiple serious candidates and genuine policy debates—would generate the kind of voter interest that low-turnout elections currently lack. The current system protects incumbents while discouraging civic engagement.

The media also plays a role. Coverage of local elections, particularly primaries, is minimal. Newspapers have shrunk their political reporting, and television news devotes more attention to national than local contests. This creates a vacuum of information that leaves voters without the basic facts they need to make informed choices. Increased civic journalism, including voter guides and candidate forums focused on local races, could help fill this gap.

Finally, there is the question of whether voters have simply lost faith in the system itself. When primary results are predictable and general elections are forgone conclusions, rational citizens may conclude that participation is pointless. Rebuilding that faith requires demonstrating that individual votes produce tangible outcomes—that elected officials actually respond to constituent pressure. This is ultimately a matter of political culture, not just election mechanics. Until New Yorkers believe their participation will make a difference, no amount of reform will fully address the participation gap.

The only election that matters is the June primary, and the only primary that draws interest is one where the incumbent looks vulnerable.

When voters know their ballot can actually swing an outcome, they show up. California's experiment with a citizens' redistricting commission has produced markedly more competitive races; Iowa's nonpartisan process has done the same for decades.

Roughly a fifth of city voters are registered as independents or with minor parties, and they are locked out of the Democratic primaries that effectively decide congressional representation. A semi-open primary, allowing unaffiliated voters to participate, would instantly expand the eligible pool by hundreds of thousands.

There are smaller fixes worth pursuing. Consolidating the state's absurd four-election calendar — presidential primary in April, congressional primary in June, state primary in September, general in November — would reduce voter fatigue and save money. Same-day registration, which a handful of states use successfully, would help the many New Yorkers who only tune in to politics in the final weeks of a campaign. Early voting, which most of the country now permits, would accommodate working people who can't make it to a polling place during a 15-hour Tuesday window.

But these are tinkering measures. The fundamental issue is structural: when districts are uncompetitive and primaries are closed, low turnout is not a bug but a feature, working exactly as the incumbents who designed the system intend. Fix the maps and open the primaries, and turnout will take care of itself.

We keep asking what will get New Yorkers to vote, as if the problem were a marketing puzzle. It isn't. New Yorkers don't vote in congressional primaries because their vote, in any meaningful sense, does not count. And no amount of get-out-the-vote canvassing, celebrity PSAs, or guilt-tripping op-eds is going to change that fact.

Consider what a voter in, say, the new 13th District is being asked to do this Tuesday. Wake up early or stay late after work. Find the polling place, which may have moved. Navigate the city's notoriously confusing ballot. And for what? To ratify a choice that the district lines, the fundraising imbalance, and the party machinery have effectively already made. Rangel will almost certainly win. The general election is a formality. The voter's marginal influence is, to a first approximation, zero.

When economists model rational behavior, they sometimes joke that the puzzle is not why so few people vote, but why anyone votes at all. The standard answer is that voting is expressive — a civic ritual, an identity marker. That answer holds up reasonably well in high-salience presidential elections. It collapses in low-information primaries that the media barely covers and the candidates barely contest.

So what would actually move the needle? Honest answer: not much, short of structural overhaul. Mailing every registered voter a ballot, Oregon-style, would help — it lowers the cost of voting to near zero. Public financing of campaigns at the congressional level, modeled on New York City's successful matching-funds system, would help by surfacing credible challengers who can actually communicate with voters. And ranked-choice voting in primaries would give insurgent candidates more oxygen, since voters wouldn't have to worry about "wasting" their ballot.

What won't work is more of what we've been trying. Voter registration drives in a city where most eligible adults are already registered are largely theater. Civic-education campaigns about the importance of voting assume the problem is ignorance; it isn't. New Yorkers know that congressional primaries exist. They have decided, mostly correctly, that their participation will not alter the outcome.

The blunt truth is that low turnout is the predictable equilibrium of a system designed by and for incumbents. We can scold voters all we like, but until we change the structure — competitive districts, accessible ballots, real challengers — they will continue to make the rational calculation. Don't blame the voters. Blame the design.

The discussion about low turnout almost always focuses on procedural reforms: early voting, same-day registration, mail ballots, redistricting. These matter at the margins. But they miss something more fundamental about why so many New Yorkers — particularly young people, recent immigrants, and working-class residents in the outer boroughs — never make it to the polls.

The deeper issue is that politics in this city no longer feels connected to people's daily lives. A young woman working two jobs in Sunset Park, paying half her income in rent, watching the F train break down on her commute — what does she know about her congressional representative? Probably nothing. The representative has not knocked on her door, has not appeared at her church or her child's school, has not been mentioned in any news source she reads. Her local political party organization, if one even exists in a meaningful sense, has not contacted her in years.

This is a problem of civic infrastructure, and it is one that procedural reforms cannot fix. In the era of Tammany Hall — for all that machine's corruption — political clubs were embedded in every neighborhood. They handed out turkeys at Thanksgiving, helped new arrivals navigate City Hall, registered voters by the thousand. The machines were ugly, but they connected ordinary people to electoral politics. Today's Democratic Party in New York is largely a hollow vehicle for incumbents, with skeleton county committees and almost no street-level presence in most neighborhoods.

Rebuilding that infrastructure is harder than passing a reform bill, but it is more important. The organizations that have shown they can drive turnout — Working Families Party, some of the larger unions, immigrant-rights groups like Make the Road — do it through year-round contact, not just last-minute door-knocking. They build relationships. They connect voting to bread-and-butter issues people actually care about.

City government can help by funding civic-engagement programs, expanding language access at polling sites, and bringing voter registration into high schools, naturalization ceremonies, and CUNY campuses. The DOE could make 12th-grade civics class a serious course rather than the afterthought it has become. The Board of Elections — that perennial embarrassment — could finally enter the 21st century with better signage, online tools, and trained workers.

But none of this substitutes for organized political life in neighborhoods. Until ordinary New Yorkers are talked with, listened to, and treated as participants rather than spectators, they will continue to do what spectators do: stay home.

Let me offer an unfashionable view: low turnout in congressional primaries is not a crisis, and the standard reform menu would do less good than its advocates claim.

The premise of the question is that more voting is automatically better. But what would a higher turnout actually produce? In safe Democratic districts, expanding the primary electorate from, say, 12 percent to 25 percent would not dramatically change the kind of candidate who wins. The marginal voter pulled in by easier registration or early voting is, on average, less informed about the specific contest than the voter who already shows up. There is a reasonable argument that primary electorates — composed of more attentive partisans — produce better-vetted nominees than would a casual mass electorate dragged to the polls.

I am not opposed to making voting easier. Early voting and same-day registration are sensible. New York's election administration is genuinely shameful — the four separate election dates, the lever machines that lingered for decades, the routine purges and ballot mishaps. Fixing administrative incompetence is a worthy goal in itself.

But let's not pretend that turnout is the master variable of democratic health. Countries with mandatory voting, like Australia, do not obviously produce better government than countries without. Within the United States, turnout varies more by demographic composition and ballot competitiveness than by the procedural rules states adopt. The much-touted reforms — motor voter, vote by mail, early voting — have produced modest, sometimes barely measurable, increases in turnout.

The honest reason congressional primaries draw few voters is that the contests are low-stakes for most voters most of the time. When a primary is genuinely competitive and consequential — Espaillat versus Rangel this year, for instance, in a district reshaped by population shifts — turnout rises noticeably. When it isn't, it doesn't. That is not a failure of civic virtue; it is rational allocation of attention by busy people.

If we want to raise the stakes, we should focus on making races genuinely competitive: through independent redistricting, public financing that lets challengers compete, and perhaps open primaries. These are structural changes that affect what's on the ballot, not how easy it is to mark one.

In the meantime, I'd rather a New York that votes thoughtfully than one that votes universally. The romance of universal participation can distract from the harder question of whether the choices on offer are worth showing up for.

If we are serious about raising voter participation in New York, we should stop treating this as a question of individual motivation and start treating it as a question of design. Behavioral research over the last two decades has been remarkably clear: turnout responds powerfully to the structure of the voting environment, and the most cost-effective interventions are not exhortations but defaults.

Start with consolidation. New York holds federal primaries in June, state primaries in September, and the general in November — a schedule that confuses even attentive voters, costs taxpayers tens of millions in unnecessary administration, and guarantees that each individual election will draw a sliver of the electorate. A federal court ordered the split this year for technical compliance reasons, but the Legislature could fix it by aligning everything on one September date. One election cycle, one set of campaigns, one trip to the polls.

Next, automate registration. The single greatest barrier to voting is not motivation but registration deadlines that pass weeks before anyone is paying attention to a race. Same-day registration, used successfully in Minnesota, Wisconsin, and elsewhere, consistently boosts turnout by 3 to 7 percentage points. Better still would be automatic registration through DMV and other agency interactions, with an opt-out rather than opt-in default. Behavioral economics tells us defaults are powerful; the current default is non-participation.

Third, expand the voting window. Tuesday voting is a 19th-century artifact designed around the agricultural calendar. Roughly two-thirds of states now offer some form of early voting or no-excuse absentee balloting; New York is a conspicuous laggard. Allowing two weeks of early voting, including weekends, accommodates the realities of shift workers, parents, and the elderly.

Fourth, open the primaries — at least partially. Hundreds of thousands of New York City voters are registered as independents and locked out of the only elections that, in many districts, actually decide who governs them. A modified open primary, allowing unaffiliated voters to choose a party ballot on Election Day, would dramatically expand the meaningful electorate without requiring full fusion.

Finally, fix the Board of Elections. Its patronage structure, antiquated practices, and chronic Election Day failures actively suppress turnout. Replacing it with a professional, nonpartisan administrative body, as most large cities have done, is overdue.

None of these reforms requires inspiring voters to be better citizens. They require us to design a system that assumes the voters we have, not the ones we wish we had. That is a far more productive frame.

The diagnosis is being botched. Pundits keep blaming voter laziness, the calendar, the closed primary, the Board of Elections. All real problems. But the elephant in the room is that political journalism in New York has collapsed, and without journalism, primary elections become invisible.

Quick: who is running against your congressman? What do they stand for? What did your incumbent vote on last month? If you can't answer those questions, it is not because you are a bad citizen. It is because no one is telling you. The Times barely covers congressional primaries except in marquee races. The tabloids have largely retreated from substantive political reporting. Local TV news is a wasteland for anything below the mayoral level. Neighborhood weeklies — once the workhorses of New York political coverage — have been gutted or shuttered. The few remaining beat reporters covering Albany or the congressional delegation are stretched impossibly thin.

What's left? Press releases recycled as articles, scripted candidate forums attended by 40 people, and a thin scattering of blogs read mostly by political insiders. Into this vacuum step the campaigns themselves, but their direct mail and robocalls reach an ever-smaller share of the electorate, and voters reasonably discount what comes from a candidate's own team. The result: by Election Day, a typical voter has received virtually no independent information about who is on the ballot or why it matters.

This is why reforms aimed at the voting process itself will have limited effect. You can make voting wonderfully easy — mail-in ballots, automatic registration, two weeks of early voting — and turnout in low-information primaries will still be terrible, because the voter at the kitchen table with the ballot in hand has no idea what to do with it. Easy access to a contest you know nothing about is not real participation.

What's the fix? Some of it requires resources the city government doesn't directly control: stronger local journalism, possibly supported by foundations or a public-media expansion modeled on the BBC. Some of it could be public: a substantially expanded Campaign Finance Board voter guide, mailed earlier and in more languages, with candidate questionnaires that actually probe positions. Free airtime on city-licensed cable channels for primary candidates. Robust support for public-access debates, including in non-English media.

Until voters have actual information about actual candidates, asking them to vote in greater numbers is asking them to perform a ritual without content. Fix the information ecosystem first.

We talk about getting more New Yorkers to vote as if it’s a problem with a policy knob: turn up early voting, turn down registration barriers. But that mechanical mindset misses something deeper. Voting, at its core, isn’t a consumer experience to be streamlined; it’s a civic act that requires a sense of membership and obligation. You don’t solve a crisis of belonging by making it slightly more convenient to show up. You solve it by rebuilding the cultural infrastructure that makes voting a natural expression of community life.

For decades, we’ve systematically dismantled the institutions that taught citizenship. Civics has been squeezed out of school curricula in favor of test-prep. Fewer New Yorkers belong to unions, churches, neighborhood clubs, or even bowling leagues—the kind of mediating structures that once connected daily life to public responsibility. When you don’t know your neighbors and don’t see yourself as part of a shared project, why would you stand in line to choose a district leader? The result is a city of atomized individuals, many of whom are intelligent and informed but feel no personal stake in an anonymous government.

We should invest heavily in civic education, starting in middle school, not with dry textbook recitations but with hands-on lessons that include registering students to vote at sixteen and running mock elections that grapple with real local issues. But schools can’t do it alone. Political parties, which have become hollow fundraising operations, need to return to their old role as year-round civic hubs—hosting issue discussions, neighborhood can-vassing, and social events that make politics human. So do libraries, community boards, and even sports leagues. When voting is embedded in a web of relationships and shared norms, turnout isn’t a matter of marketing; it’s a matter of course.

The good news is that New York doesn’t lack civic energy. It’s evident in community gardens, block associations, and immigrant mutual aid societies. The challenge is channeling that energy into electoral participation. That won’t happen through a new registration app. It will happen when we stop treating voting as an individual trip to the polls and start treating it as a collective ritual that signals our membership in a city we’re actively shaping together.
