Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Yes — and the case for mandatory disclosure has only grown stronger as breaches have moved from credit-card theft to industrial espionage and journalist surveillance. The recent Mandiant report on "APT1," the revelations from The New York Times and Wall Street Journal about Chinese intrusions, and the steady drumbeat of incidents at companies like Zappos, LinkedIn, and Global Payments all point to the same lesson: silence serves the breached company at the expense of nearly everyone else.

Forty-six states already require notification when personally identifiable information is exposed, and California's pioneering 2003 law showed that disclosure regimes can work without sinking the firms they cover. But the patchwork is incoherent — definitions of "breach" vary, triggers differ, and many serious intrusions (intellectual property theft, network compromises that don't clearly touch consumer data) fall through the cracks. A uniform federal standard, of the sort proposed in successive versions of the Data Security and Breach Notification Act, would give companies clarity and customers consistent protection.

Disclosure also has positive externalities. When firms share indicators of compromise, defenders across an industry can patch faster; this is why the FBI's outreach programs and information-sharing groups like the FS-ISAC matter. Mandatory reporting — even confidentially to a body like US-CERT, with public notice when consumers are affected — would convert private pain into collective defense.

The counterarguments (tipping off attackers, harming reputations, encouraging frivolous litigation) are real but manageable through reasonable delay provisions and safe harbors for good-faith security practices. What is not defensible is the status quo, in which whether you learn your data was stolen depends on which state you live in and which company lost it.

The notion that companies should have a choice about disclosing security breaches rests on an outdated understanding of corporate responsibility. When a business loses control of customer data—credit card numbers, Social Security records, email addresses—it is not merely suffering a private misfortune. It is exposing every affected individual to identity theft, fraud, and phishing attacks that can take years to untangle. Mandatory disclosure is not an unreasonable burden; it is the bare minimum of accountability.

At present, disclosure in the United States is a patchwork of state laws, most notably California’s pioneering 2003 statute. The result is that a breach affecting residents of forty-six states may be disclosed in a letter, while residents of the remaining four are left in the dark. A federal standard would end this lottery, ensuring that a citizen’s right to know does not depend on geography. It would also force companies to internalize the true cost of lax security, which today is often passed silently to consumers.

Opponents argue that mandatory reporting harms reputations and invites lawsuits. Yet the alternative is a market in which customers cannot distinguish firms that invest in security from those that cut corners. In a world of asymmetric information, voluntary disclosure is a mirage: companies that come forward, as The New York Times did, are penalized precisely because the norm of silence makes them appear uniquely vulnerable. A universal requirement levels the playing field, so that no single firm is punished for honesty while others hide identical lapses.

Beyond consumer protection, mandatory reporting generates a public good. The technical details of an intrusion—the malware used, the server misconfiguration exploited—are intelligence that help other organizations patch their own defenses. When a breach is buried, that knowledge is lost, and an adversary can reuse the same tool dozens of times without detection. In this sense, silence is not just a betrayal of customers; it is a gift to the next attacker. For a truly resilient digital society, sunlight is not optional.

The case for mandatory breach disclosure rests on a simple principle: those who bear the risk of harm deserve to know about it. When hackers compromise customer data — whether financial information, medical records, or login credentials — the individuals affected are the ones who must change passwords, monitor accounts, or guard against identity theft. They cannot take these precautions unless they know a breach has occurred.

Critics argue that disclosure requirements harm companies economically and might even alert future attackers to vulnerabilities. These concerns have merit, but they do not outweigh the public interest in transparency. Economic harm from disclosure is itself a form of accountability — it creates market pressure for better security practices. Companies that invest adequately in protecting customer data have less to fear from disclosure than those that do not.

California's breach notification law, enacted in 2003, demonstrated that such requirements can function without crippling industry or creating chaos. Today, nearly all states have similar laws, suggesting that a workable framework exists. A federal standard would bring consistency and ensure that customers in less vigilant states receive the same protections as those in California or New York.

The objection that disclosure tips off future attackers deserves skepticism. Sophisticated hackers already share intelligence through underground networks; the security community does as well through organizations like the Internet Storm Center. The asymmetry is not between informed attackers and uninformed ones, but between informed companies and ignorant consumers.

Companies often resist disclosure not from genuine security concerns but from reputational and legal risk. That self-interest, left unchecked, has produced the culture of silence that makes mandatory disclosure necessary.

Yes, and It's Long Past Time

When the New York Times revealed last month that Chinese hackers had spent four months prowling its network, the most striking detail wasn't the breach itself—it was how exceptional the disclosure felt. Most companies hit by similar intrusions stay silent, and for an obvious reason: bad news is bad for business. That is precisely why disclosure cannot remain voluntary.

The case for mandatory reporting rests on a simple principle. When a company collects my credit card number, my medical records, or my login credentials, it is acting as a custodian of information I cannot easily protect myself. If that custodianship fails, I have a right to know promptly so I can change passwords, watch my accounts, or freeze my credit. Forty-six states already recognize this principle in their data breach notification laws, but the patchwork is uneven, riddled with loopholes for "encrypted" data and harm thresholds that let companies decide for themselves whether customers deserve a warning. A federal standard with teeth is overdue.

Critics will argue that disclosure tips off other attackers or invites lawsuits. The first concern is overstated: sophisticated attackers usually know what worked; it is defenders who are kept in the dark. The second concern is, frankly, the point. The threat of reputational and legal consequences is what forces firms to invest in security in the first place. A world where breaches are quietly absorbed as a cost of doing business is a world where security budgets stay anemic.

There is also a national-security dimension we ignore at our peril. The Mandiant report on PLA Unit 61398, published this week, makes clear that Chinese state-linked actors are systematically siphoning intellectual property from American firms. If companies are not required to report intrusions, policymakers cannot grasp the scope of what is being lost, and other firms cannot learn from a peer's compromise. Voluntary information-sharing arrangements like ISACs have value, but they are slow and incomplete.

The right framework is straightforward: rapid notification to affected individuals when personal data is exposed; confidential notification to a designated federal authority for serious intrusions regardless of data type; and meaningful penalties for concealment. We require restaurants to report food-borne illness outbreaks and hospitals to report infections. The data economy deserves no less. Sunshine is uncomfortable for the exposed, but it is the only reliable disinfectant we have.

Be Careful What You Mandate

Everyone loves transparency in the abstract. The trouble with mandatory breach disclosure is that, in practice, it can produce more theater than security.

Consider what "disclosure" actually means in most existing state laws. A company discovers an intrusion, lawyers spend weeks deciding whether the legal trigger has been met, a carefully worded letter goes out months later offering a year of free credit monitoring, and customers either ignore it or sigh and move on. The information is too generic to act on, too late to matter, and too frequent—we are now numb to breach notices. If the goal is to empower consumers, the current system mostly fails them while imposing real costs on firms.

There is a more serious worry. The Times disclosed its intrusion only after a months-long investigation, working quietly with Mandiant to understand the attackers' tools, identify backdoors, and watch how they moved. Premature public disclosure would have tipped off the intruders, who would have burned their access and re-entered through a different door. Good incident response sometimes requires silence. A rigid timetable for going public can actively harm the defender.

Mandatory disclosure also creates perverse incentives. If reporting triggers lawsuits, regulatory inquiries, and stock-price hits, firms have every reason to define "breach" narrowly, to avoid looking too hard at suspicious activity, or to push detection responsibilities onto vendors. The same dynamic explains why so many hospitals were slow to embrace infection reporting: when you punish discovery, you get less discovery.

None of this means companies should be free to hide everything. Where personal data is genuinely exposed in a way that creates risk of fraud, customers should be told, and told quickly. But that is a narrow category. For the broader universe of intrusions—espionage, intellectual-property theft, probing attacks—what we need is confidential reporting to a trusted government body that can aggregate intelligence and warn peers, not press releases that mainly serve plaintiffs' lawyers.

The right question is not "should companies tell us?" but "what kind of telling actually makes us safer?" A blanket public-disclosure mandate confuses the symbolic value of openness with the practical work of defense. We can do better than turning every hack into a press cycle.

Two Different Breaches, Two Different Rules

The debate over disclosure suffers from a category error. We keep talking about "hacks" as if they were a single phenomenon, when in fact at least two very different scenarios are bundled together, and they call for different rules.

The first scenario is the consumer data breach: a retailer, bank, health insurer, or social network loses control of names, Social Security numbers, payment information, or medical records. Here the case for prompt, mandatory disclosure is overwhelming. The harm flows directly to individuals who have no other way to learn that their information is in the wild. Affected customers need time to change credentials, monitor accounts, and place fraud alerts. State laws already address this case, imperfectly, and a uniform federal standard—with a clear, short timeline—would be a clear improvement.

The second scenario is what happened to the New York Times: a sophisticated intrusion aimed not at customer data but at the company itself—its emails, its sources, its intellectual property, its operations. The victims here are the company and, in aggregate, national security. There is little customer-protection rationale for public disclosure, and significant reasons—operational security, ongoing investigation, diplomatic sensitivity—to keep details quiet. What the public needs from these incidents is not a press release but trend data: who is being targeted, by whom, using what techniques. That argues for mandatory confidential reporting to a body like US-CERT, with aggregated public reporting on the threat landscape.

Conflating these two scenarios leads to bad policy. Apply the consumer-protection framework to espionage cases and you risk burning investigations and chilling cooperation with the FBI. Apply the espionage framework to consumer cases and you let companies sit on news that affects millions while they "investigate."

A sensible federal law would draw the line explicitly. If personal data on identifiable individuals has been exposed, notify them on a defined timeline—say, thirty days absent specific law-enforcement exception. If a serious intrusion has occurred without consumer-data exposure, report it confidentially to a designated federal entity that publishes anonymized statistics and warns the relevant sector.

We get muddled outcomes when we treat the Times intrusion and the TJX breach as instances of the same problem. They aren't. Good rules will recognize the difference.

Let the Market—and the Insurers—Handle It

Before we reach for another federal mandate, it is worth asking what problem we are actually trying to solve. The premise of the disclosure debate is that companies left to themselves will hide breaches, customers will be left vulnerable, and society as a whole will under-invest in security. Each of these claims deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets.

Companies are not, in fact, left to themselves. Forty-six states require notification when personal data is compromised. The SEC issued guidance in 2011 making clear that material cybersecurity incidents must be disclosed to investors. The FTC has brought enforcement actions against firms whose security practices fell short of what they promised. Payment-card industry rules impose detailed obligations on anyone handling credit cards. The notion that breaches happen in a regulatory vacuum is simply wrong.

Meanwhile, a robust market in cyber insurance has emerged in the past few years, and it is doing something regulators struggle to do: pricing security practices. Underwriters now demand evidence of patching discipline, employee training, encryption, and incident-response plans. Premiums reflect risk. Firms that take security seriously pay less; firms that don't pay more or cannot get coverage at all. This is exactly the kind of distributed, information-rich enforcement that command-and-control regulation handles poorly.

Add to this the brutal reputational discipline of the news cycle. The Times, Sony, Heartland, RSA, Lockheed—every major breach becomes a multi-year cautionary tale. CEOs and CIOs have watched their peers lose jobs. Boards now ask about cyber risk. The incentives have shifted dramatically without a federal disclosure statute.

A new federal mandate risks freezing this evolving ecosystem in place. Rules drafted today will be obsolete in five years. Compliance will become a tick-box exercise that crowds out genuine security investment, much as Sarbanes-Oxley arguably did for financial controls. Worse, a uniform rule may preempt more demanding state laws, leveling down rather than up.

The honest answer to "should companies be required to disclose?" is: they largely already are, in the cases that most matter to consumers. For the rest, voluntary information-sharing—through ISACs and confidential channels with law enforcement—is improving, and market mechanisms are tightening. Pile on a new federal regime and we may discover the cure is worse than the disease.

The Real Audience Isn't Customers—It's Other Defenders

Most arguments for breach disclosure focus on protecting consumers. That's understandable but incomplete. The bigger payoff from telling the truth about hacks is collective: every defender learns from every attack. Right now, that learning barely happens, and the country pays the price.

Consider what the Mandiant report on APT1, released this week, actually accomplished. By publicly naming Chinese PLA Unit 61398 and detailing its tradecraft—the malware families, the command-and-control infrastructure, the operational patterns—Mandiant gave every American security team a checklist to use tomorrow morning. Logs can be searched. Indicators can be blocked. Patterns can be recognized. That report is more useful to the average CISO than a thousand generic warnings about "advanced persistent threats."

Now imagine that intelligence multiplied across every significant intrusion in the U.S. economy. Imagine that when one defense contractor finds a novel phishing lure, every other contractor sees it within hours. Imagine that when a bank detects a new wire-fraud technique, peer banks are alerted before they are hit. This is not a fantasy; it is roughly what the Financial Services ISAC tries to do, and what other sectors do far less well. The bottleneck is not technology. The bottleneck is the legal and reputational risk attached to admitting a breach.

That is why disclosure rules matter, but they need to be designed with sharing—not shaming—in mind. A useful regime would require timely confidential reporting of serious intrusions to a federal coordinator, with mandatory de-identified sharing of technical indicators across affected sectors. Liability protections should accompany the mandate, so general counsels stop treating threat data like radioactive waste. Public disclosure to customers should remain mandatory when their data is exposed, but the broader reporting regime should be oriented toward defenders, not journalists.

The legislation now circulating in Washington, including CISPA, gets some of this right and some of it wrong. Civil-liberties concerns about handing private data to intelligence agencies are legitimate and need real guardrails. But the underlying insight—that we cannot defend a networked economy if every defender fights alone—is correct.

Disclosure, properly designed, is less about transparency for its own sake than about turning isolated victims into a learning system. That is what the country is missing.

The Question Behind the Question

"Should companies tell us when they get hacked?" sounds like a simple call for honesty, but the framing hides assumptions worth surfacing. Who is the "us"? What counts as a "hack"? And what do we expect to do with the information once we have it?

If "us" means individual customers whose personal data has been exposed, the answer is yes, and we already largely require it. State data breach laws have been on the books for nearly a decade, beginning with California's SB 1386 in 2003. They are imperfect—too many exemptions, too much variation, too much delay—but the principle is established, and a tighter federal floor would be welcome.

If "us" means the investing public, the answer is also yes, and the SEC has been moving in that direction. Cyber incidents that materially affect a company's business or risk profile are already supposed to be disclosed in securities filings. Enforcement has been spotty, but the legal architecture is in place.

If "us" means the general public reading the morning paper, the calculus is harder. There is a real public interest in knowing that Chinese state actors are targeting American journalism, or that critical infrastructure has been probed. But that interest is not best served by case-by-case corporate press releases. It is better served by aggregated, contextualized reporting from a body that can see across incidents—something closer to what the National Transportation Safety Board does for aviation accidents. The NTSB doesn't depend on airlines voluntarily issuing crash press releases; it investigates, analyzes, and publishes findings that improve the whole system.

The deeper problem with the current debate is that it treats disclosure as an end in itself, when it is only a means. The end is fewer successful attacks and less damage when attacks succeed. Disclosure helps when it warns affected individuals, informs defenders, prices risk, or pressures laggards to improve. It is unhelpful—or harmful—when it tips off attackers, drowns the public in unactionable notices, or substitutes ritual for security.

So yes, companies should be required to tell us when they get hacked. But "us" is plural, the channels should vary, and we should stop pretending that a strongly worded notification letter is a substitute for the harder work of actually defending the networks on which modern life now runs.

Law enforcement and forensic investigators need time to conduct a covert response, trace the intruders, and lock down the damage. Mandatory notification blows that cover.

The result would be a wave of prophylactic bankruptcies, not better security.

The debate over whether companies should disclose hacks is too often framed as an all-or-nothing choice: full transparency versus corporate secrecy. Reality demands something far more nuanced. We need a tiered disclosure model that distinguishes between nuisances and genuine threats, giving consumers timely, actionable information without burying them in noise or crippling security investigations.

The most important distinction is between breaches that compromise “material” personal data and those that don’t. A hacker who steals a list of email addresses for a marketing newsletter does not pose the same risk as one who walks off with Social Security numbers, financial account details, or unencrypted medical records. A rational federal law, preempting the current tangle of 46 state statutes, should set a clear threshold: disclosure is required only when the breach involves information that can be used for identity theft, fraud, or physical harm. If a company determines after a rapid investigation that no such data left its control, it should file a confidential notice with a central clearinghouse — like the Federal Trade Commission — but not broadcast a public alarm.

Timing also requires flexibility. No company can meaningfully explain a breach the moment it’s discovered. They need a reasonable window, perhaps 72 hours, to confirm the facts, contain the intrusion, and cooperate with law enforcement. But they should not be allowed to stretch that window into months of silence while consumers remain in the dark. The New York Times’s recent acknowledgment came after a forensic assessment, not the moment a firewall log blinked — and that sequence is the right one. Forced instantaneous disclosure would gut the ability of authorities to trace attackers and protect other victims silently.

We must also think about the audience. A blanket public press release is often the least helpful format. Instead, companies should be required to notify affected individuals directly, with clear, plain-language instructions on what steps to take — changing passwords, enrolling in credit monitoring, placing fraud alerts. A templated, jargon-filled statement buried on a corporate website serves no one. At the same time, a summary report stripped of identifying details should go to an industry-wide threat-sharing exchange, so that other companies can patch the same vulnerability before they become next.

The goal of disclosure isn’t to humiliate companies; it’s to empower people and protect systems. A tiered approach serves that goal by filtering out the trivial, preserving the strategic advantage against attackers, and ensuring that when we do hear about a breach, it’s information we can actually use.

Sellers of digital services know far more about the risks to our data than we do, and they have strong financial incentives to keep it that way.
