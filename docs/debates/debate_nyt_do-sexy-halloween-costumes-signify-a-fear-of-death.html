Do Sexy Halloween Costumes Signify a Fear of Death?

In her essay in The Times, the author Bess Lovejoy argues that ignoring death “allows us to imagine that our mortal trivialities and anxieties are permanent.” Some of those trivialities and anxieties can be found in our cheeky Halloween costumes, which increasingly seem to be about showing taut and toned skin, rather than it decomposing.

Are we replacing zombie looks with sexy maid and witty high-concept constructs to avoid reminders of death? Or is it time to forget the otherworldly origins of Halloween and just have fun?

The question assumes a tidier history than Halloween actually has. The "sexy nurse" did not muscle out the rotting corpse; American Halloween has been oscillating between morbidity and mischief since at least the mid-twentieth century, when it was largely a children's candy holiday with cute ghosts and friendly witches, not memento mori. If anything, the contemporary costume aisle is more death-saturated than the one I grew up with: zombies are arguably at peak cultural saturation, thanks to "The Walking Dead," and the past decade's vampire boom has been relentless. The sexy costume and the gory costume coexist — often on the same body, as the "sexy zombie cheerleader" attests.

What's really happening, I'd argue, is less denial of death than the colonization of Halloween by adults. Once grown-ups reclaimed the holiday in the 1970s and 80s as a night of costumed partying, the logic of adult parties — flirtation, display, status performance — took over. The sexy maid isn't a defense against mortality; she's a defense against being ignored at a bar.

That said, Lovejoy is onto something broader. Our culture does sanitize death — in hospice arrangements, in funeral practices, in the cosmetic refusal of aging. But Halloween is a poor exhibit for the prosecution. It remains one of the few nights when skeletons grin from suburban lawns and children are encouraged to impersonate the dead. The sexy costume is a sideshow, not a symptom.

Bess Lovejoy rightly notes our modern cultural allergy to mortality, and it is tempting to view the annual parade of sexy nurses, provocative cartoon characters, and ironic pop-culture memes as a collective flight from the grave. However, pinning the rise of the "sexy" or "witty" Halloween costume on a subconscious fear of death gives too much psychological weight to what is primarily an economic and social shift.

Over the past two decades, Halloween has fundamentally transformed from a child-centric ritual of neighborhood trick-or-treating into a multi-billion-dollar adult party holiday. For adults, the night serves an entirely different transgressive purpose. It operates as a sanctioned suspension of everyday rules. In a society that closely polices women's bodies, sexuality, and professional decorum year-round, Halloween offers a rare, culturally approved window to be deliberately provocative or absurd without lasting reputational consequence. The appeal of the sexy maid is not that it wards off the Grim Reaper, but that it defies the mundane dress codes of the office.

Furthermore, the increasing premium on cleverness and aesthetics is driven by the modern reality of social documentation. With the omnipresence of Facebook and the rapid rise of mobile photo sharing, a costume is no longer merely seen by the people at a local party; it is broadcast permanently to a sprawling digital network. A witty, high-concept pun or a flattering outfit translates much better into modern social capital than a genuinely terrifying, rotting ghoul. People want to look good or seem smart in their digital albums, which pushes costumes away from the grotesque and toward the photogenic.

We have certainly distanced ourselves from the ancient origins of the holiday, when the veil between the living and the dead was thought to be thin. But we have not replaced the macabre with the sexy out of a deep-seated dread of decomposition. Instead, we have simply repurposed the festival to serve contemporary psychological needs. Today, the phantoms we are trying to escape on October 31st are not literal spirits or reminders of our own mortality, but the dull, restrictive expectations of our everyday lives.

The argument that sexy Halloween costumes betray a cultural fear of death is the kind of claim that sounds profound at a cocktail party and dissolves under any serious scrutiny. It's a textbook example of overreading a trivial consumer phenomenon through a borrowed Continental lens — usually Ernest Becker's "Denial of Death," which has become the go-to citation for any commentator who wants to make small things sound large.

Consider what's actually happening. Halloween in its current American form is a commercial holiday, roughly the second-biggest retail event of the year. Costume manufacturers compete for adult dollars. Sex sells, as it sells everywhere else in our economy, from beer commercials to perfume ads to the covers of magazines at supermarket checkouts. The fact that "sexy nurse" outsells "rotting corpse" tells us approximately what we already knew: people, especially young people at parties where they hope to meet other young people, would rather look attractive than repulsive. This requires no Heideggerian explanation.

Furthermore, the premise that Halloween used to be a serious confrontation with mortality is largely a fiction. American Halloween has been a sanitized children's holiday for over a century, all rubber masks and Charlie Brown specials. The "otherworldly origins" invoked in these debates — Samhain, the thinning veil, the wandering dead — are real enough as folklore, but they have not been a lived part of the American observance for generations. You can't lament the loss of something most participants never had.

There's also something a little condescending in the suggestion that twenty-somethings dressing as sexy referees are unconsciously fleeing existential dread. Maybe they're just having fun. Maybe a culture that lets adults play dress-up one night a year is a culture doing something psychologically healthy, not pathological. The Puritan reflex to find moral or metaphysical failure in any expression of pleasure runs deep in American intellectual life, and dressing it up in Becker-flavored language doesn't make it less Puritan.

If we want to worry about death-denial in American culture, there are real targets: our refusal to fund hospice adequately, our medicalization of dying, our discomfort with grief. Leave the sexy witches alone.

I want to take Lovejoy's thesis seriously and push it a step further. The sexy costume inverts this entirely. It re-presents the body precisely in its most fleeting state — youthful, taut, sexually marketable — and freezes it under flattering light. Does this matter? I think it does, modestly. Cultures that cannot symbolize death tend to handle actual death badly — and ours does. We hide the dying in institutions, we sanitize funerals, we treat grief as a problem to be managed in six-week increments. A holiday that once gave us collective permission to look at the skull beneath the skin has become another occasion for looking past it.

Let Halloween be Halloween. The argument that we're failing some deeper cultural duty by dressing as sexy bumblebees rather than as moldering corpses assumes that holidays have essences they must preserve. They don't. Holidays are living practices, and living practices change with the people who keep them.

Halloween in America has been, in succession: an Irish-Scottish folk observance, a rowdy night of immigrant pranking, a community-policed children's candy ritual, a horror-movie marketing window, and now an adult costume party with strong romantic undertones. Each generation has remade it. The version that emphasized graveyards and ghosts was itself a Victorian reinvention of older Celtic practices, sentimentalized for parlor games. The trick-or-treat version was a mid-century innovation pushed partly by candy manufacturers and partly by communities trying to channel youthful mischief into something safer. Why should the current adult-party version be considered a fall from grace rather than just the next iteration?

The death-denial framing also bakes in an unexamined assumption: that confronting mortality is the higher, more authentic activity, and that flirting and dancing is the shallow, evasive one. But people facing death — actually facing it, not theorizing about it — often report that what they most want is more of the so-called shallow stuff. More flirting, more dancing, more bad costumes with friends. The medieval danse macabre wasn't a sober philosophy seminar; it was, in part, exactly the kind of release we associate with Halloween today.

There's also a small civic case for adult Halloween. It's one of the few American occasions when strangers in costume gather in public and treat each other with festive license. In a country whose communal life keeps shrinking — fewer bowling leagues, fewer church socials, fewer block parties — a holiday that gets adults out of their houses and into shared revelry is doing real social work. Even if the revelry involves a lot of fishnets.

So if you want to spend October 31 contemplating the skull beneath the skin, by all means do. Read your Donne, light a candle, visit a grave. But don't begrudge the rest of us our sexy referees. Life is short. That's the point.

There's a third possibility the framing of this debate misses: the sexy costume isn't a denial of death so much as a defiance of it. These are not the same thing, and the distinction matters.

Denial is pretending death doesn't exist. Defiance is knowing it does and dressing up anyway. The body's drive toward life — toward pleasure, toward reproduction, toward sheer ostentatious aliveness — is itself an answer to the body's mortality, not an escape from it. To dress provocatively on the one night when the dead are supposed to walk is, on this reading, very much in the spirit of the holiday.

Bess Lovejoy’s suggestion that our Halloween costume choices reveal a deep-seated fear of death is not merely clever cultural criticism — it is a thesis with robust psychological backing. For decades, terror management theory (TMT) has demonstrated that humans, uniquely aware of their own mortality, engage in a constant, largely unconscious project of death-denial. We bolster self-esteem, cling to cultural worldviews, and ritualistically affirm our vitality. What is the modern sexy Halloween costume if not a near-perfect terror-management mechanism?

Historically, Halloween was a liminal night when the boundary between the living and the dead thinned. Costumes of ghosts, skeletons, and decomposing corpses forced a direct confrontation with mortality. They were memento mori, reminders that the flesh is temporary. But in a secular, death-phobic culture, such blunt confrontation becomes unbearable. And so we have swapped the shambling zombie for the sexy nurse, the decaying ghoul for the sultry pirate. These costumes do not ignore the body’s fate; they defiantly, sexually proclaim the opposite. They are a festival of taut skin, firm muscles, and reproductive fitness — everything that decay is not.

TMT studies show that when subjects are reminded of death (induced through “mortality salience”), they subsequently rate sexual attractiveness as more important and show increased desire for physical intimacy. Sexuality serves as a symbolic bulwark against the fear of annihilation; it promises life and continuity. The Halloween party, with its binge of skimpy outfits, becomes a collective ritual of life-affirmation. By dressing as the most alive versions of ourselves, we perform a kind of secular exorcism, warding off the very specters the holiday once invited in.

Of course, the participants may not consciously think, “I am wearing this to deny my mortality.” But the cultural pattern is unmistakable. The sexy costume is the modern apotropaic charm, not against ghosts, but against the dread they once represented. We have traded the skeleton for the skin, and in doing so, we reveal precisely how frightened we are.
