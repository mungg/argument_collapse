Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Marcia Angell has spent two decades sounding this alarm, and the case she makes is largely persuasive: the financial entanglement of academic medicine with the pharmaceutical industry has corrupted research, distorted education, and warped clinical practice. Her diagnosis is essentially correct. But I want to press on three places where her analysis is either too sweeping or too narrow, and to suggest that the reforms she proposes, while necessary, will not be sufficient without also addressing the structural conditions that produced the problem in the first place.

First, on research bias. Angell is right that industry-funded trials systematically favor sponsors' products — the literature documenting this is now substantial, from Lexchin and Bero's reviews through Als-Nielsen's work on outcome reporting. But her framing risks treating "industry funding" as the primary disease when the underlying pathology is the regulatory architecture itself. The FDA permits sponsors to design their own pivotal trials, choose their own comparators (or no active comparator at all), and submit positive trials while burying negative ones. Selective publication and ghostwritten reanalyses — exposed devastatingly in the Vioxx and Paxil litigation, and in the Study 329 documents — were possible because the FDA does not require registration and reporting of all trial results. The 2007 FDAAA amendments and ClinicalTrials.gov are steps forward, but compliance is poor and the law exempts much of what matters. Angell's proposal to wall off researchers from sponsors would help, but the more decisive intervention would be to require that pivotal trials be conducted by an independent body — perhaps an NIH-administered clinical trials institute funded by a user fee on manufacturers — with sponsors paying in but not controlling design, conduct, analysis, or publication. The Institute of Medicine has floated versions of this. It is the structural fix Angell's individual-conduct reforms imply but do not name.

Second, on Bayh-Dole. Here I think Angell understates a complication. She is correct that the act has produced a patenting frenzy of dubious benefit, and that the "reasonable terms" clause has been a dead letter. But her claim that "medical research was every bit as productive before Bayh-Dole as it is now" is hard to sustain in any rigorous way. The pre-1980 baseline included a deep federal investment in basic science whose payoffs were just beginning to be harvested — recombinant DNA, monoclonal antibodies, the molecular biology of cancer. Many of the most important therapeutics of the past twenty years (imatinib, the statins, the anti-TNF biologics, HAART) emerged from a research ecology in which Bayh-Dole did play some role in moving university discoveries into development, even if the net effect of exclusive licensing is debatable. The honest position is that we do not know the counterfactual, and that the question is not whether to repeal Bayh-Dole but how to discipline it: march-in rights actually used, non-exclusive licensing as the default for federally funded inventions, and price restraints on drugs that originated in publicly funded research. The Salk anecdote is rhetorically powerful but historically misleading — the polio vaccine was not patentable under the law of its day, as Salk's lawyers told him.

Third, on continuing medical education. Here Angell is at her strongest, and I would go further. The pretense that industry-funded CME is "education" rather than marketing has been refuted so many times that its survival is itself evidence of professional capture. The ACCME's standards for commercial support are largely procedural and easily gamed; the Pri-Med model Angell describes is endemic. The solution is not merely for doctors to "pay their own way," as she suggests — physicians have shown little appetite for doing so — but for state licensing boards and specialty boards to refuse to credit any CME that accepts commercial support, full stop. A profession that cannot pay for its own continuing education is not a profession.

Finally, a word about what Angell underplays. The pharmaceutical industry's grip on academic medicine is a symptom of a larger problem: the collapse of public funding for the clinical mission of academic medical centers. As reimbursement for teaching and indigent care has shrunk, deans have been forced to find revenue wherever they can. Industry money filled the vacuum. You cannot exhort academic medicine to refuse industry largesse without replacing what would be lost. Angell's reforms — necessary as they are — require a corresponding public commitment to fund the things academic medicine is supposed to do. Otherwise we are asking the supplicant to refuse the only patron left in the room.

Marcia Angell’s indictment of the pharmaceutical industry’s reach into academic medicine is a powerful and necessary one. Her decades at the *New England Journal of Medicine* gave her a front-row seat to the distortions that financial entanglement can produce, and her central insight—that the missions of investor-owned drug companies and tax-exempt academic centers are fundamentally different, and often incompatible—deserves to be taken seriously. Too many in the medical profession have been willing to look the other way as the boundaries dissolved. Angell is right to sound the alarm. Yet her proposed cure, which amounts to a near-total severance of financial ties between industry and academia, oversimplifies a complex problem and risks discarding the genuine public benefits that well-structured collaboration can bring.

Angell’s description of the corruption of clinical research is compelling. It is now well documented that industry-sponsored trials are more likely to report favorable results, and that negative studies are sometimes suppressed. The examples she cites—from the Serzone trial author with half a million dollars in consulting fees to the Vioxx advisory panel stacked with conflicted experts—are not isolated aberrations. They reflect systemic incentives that can bias the evidence on which treatment decisions are made. I share her outrage at the spectacle of academic “thought leaders” lending their prestige to marketing campaigns while accepting lucrative consulting contracts and speakers’ fees. No reasonable person can defend a system that allows researchers to serve as both evaluators and paid promoters of the same products.

But the leap from identifying these abuses to prescribing a blanket prohibition on personal payments and an end to exclusive licensing is too sweeping. Angell herself concedes that research collaboration between academia and industry can be fruitful. Yet she insists that such collaboration should involve no payments to researchers beyond arm’s-length grant support, and that patents on publicly funded discoveries should be licensed non-exclusively or placed in the public domain. This vision of a pristine separation, while appealing in principle, would undermine the very translational research she wants to protect.

Consider the reality of drug development. The NIH funds basic science magnificently, but it does not—and cannot—shepherd discoveries through the expensive, high-risk process of clinical testing and regulatory approval. The Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 was not a giveaway to industry; it was a response to the fact that many promising discoveries funded by taxpayers languished on university shelves because no company had the incentive to invest in development without the exclusivity that a patent provides. The act recognized that transferring a molecule from a laboratory bench to a pharmacy shelf requires hundreds of millions of dollars and a decade of work, and that few firms will take that gamble if competitors can immediately copy the product. Before Bayh-Dole, the government retained ownership of patents but licensed them rarely; afterward, university technology-transfer offices, for all their flaws, helped bring hundreds of products to market. It is not a coincidence that the biotechnology sector, which has produced breakthrough therapies for cancer, HIV, and rare diseases, grew up in the wake of the law. The “lottery” mentality Angell laments is a side effect, not the main event.

Angell’s solution—non-exclusive licensing or dedication to the public domain—sounds noble, but it would largely restore the pre-1980 stalemate. A company that cannot protect its investment will simply not invest. Academic centers would still generate ideas, but patients would wait far longer, if ever, to see them become medicines. The current system does need repair: Congress should enforce the “reasonable terms” provision Angell cites, ensuring that drugs developed from federally funded research are priced fairly. But abandoning the patent incentive altogether is a recipe for stagnation, not purity.

The same concern applies to medical education. Angell is correct that much of what passes for continuing medical education is thinly veiled marketing, and that the profession’s reliance on industry money is unseemly. Doctors should pay for their own learning, as other professionals do. Yet the landscape of CME in 2010 is far more complex than her analogy of the brewery and alcoholism suggests. In many rural and underserved areas, company-sponsored programs are the only affordable educational opportunities available to physicians who cannot take time away from practice to travel to academic conferences. The problem is not that companies provide funding; it is that the accreditation system has failed to ensure independence. The solution is not an outright ban on industry support but a strict firewall: accredited providers should be prohibited from accepting direct company input on content, and speakers should be barred from receiving personal payments from firms whose products they discuss. With such rules in place—and the American Medical Association has recently moved in this direction—it is possible to preserve access without sacrificing integrity.

Angell’s most disquieting suggestion is that academic researchers should accept no payments from industry beyond grant support, with no strings attached. This idealizes a past that was never quite so pure. Even in the pre-Bayh-Dole era, researchers consulted for companies; what has changed is the scale and transparency. Prohibiting consulting fees and equity stakes would drive many of the most talented physician-scientists out of academia entirely or into the arms of industry, where their expertise would be put to purely commercial use with even less public oversight. It would also sever a feedback loop that, when properly managed, sharpens research questions and accelerates clinical application. A more realistic goal is rigorous, enforceable disclosure, independent oversight of clinical trials, and institutional policies that bar researchers from personally profiting from products they study. The medical schools Angell criticizes have been slow to adopt such measures, but that is a failure of will, not a proof that all financial relationships are inherently corrupting.

None of this is to minimize the damage that unregulated conflicts of interest have done. Angell’s essay performs a vital service by cataloguing that damage and shaming those who have acquiesced to it. But in her zeal to restore medicine’s lost innocence, she offers a cure that is more likely to harm the patient. The question is not whether to have relationships between industry and academia, but how to structure them so that the public interest is paramount. That means tough, enforced standards—not an impossible quarantine.

Marcia Angell’s diagnosis of the ethical rot at the heart of academic medicine is as precise as it is devastating. By documenting how the pharmaceutical industry has co-opted medical research, education, and clinical guidelines, she exposes a system that has allowed commercial imperatives to eclipse patient welfare. The suppression of negative trial data, the proliferation of ghostwritten literature, and the masquerade of marketing as continuing medical education (CME) are indefensible practices. However, while Angell’s diagnosis is flawless, her proposed prescription—particularly her call to effectively dismantle the Bayh-Dole Act and functionally decouple academia from industry funding—is an exercise in nostalgia that ignores the grueling economic realities of modern biomedical innovation.

Angell romanticizes the pre-1980 era of medical research, invoking Jonas Salk’s famous refusal to patent the polio vaccine as a moral baseline. Salk’s sentiment was noble, but the landscape of modern medicine is vastly more complex than the development of early viral vaccines. Today, translating a basic molecular discovery into a viable, FDA-approved therapeutic—especially in the realms of complex biologics, targeted cancer therapies, and neurological treatments—costs hundreds of millions of dollars. The National Institutes of Health (NIH) excels at funding basic, early-stage science. It does not, however, possess the budget, the infrastructure, or the mandate to conduct the massive Phase III clinical trials required to prove a new drug’s safety and efficacy.

Bridging the "valley of death" between an academic discovery and a pharmacy shelf requires staggering amounts of high-risk private capital. This is where Angell’s critique of the Bayh-Dole Act falters. Before the act was passed in 1980, the federal government held title to thousands of patents resulting from publicly funded research, but fewer than five percent of these were ever licensed for commercial development. They gathered dust because no private company is willing to invest half a billion dollars in developing and testing a drug without the promise of exclusivity.

Angell suggests that academic institutions should place discoveries in the public domain or license them non-exclusively and inexpensively. If a university discovers a promising protein target for Alzheimer’s disease and puts it in the public domain, pharmaceutical companies will simply walk away. Without intellectual property protections to ensure a return on their immense development costs, the innovation pipeline would freeze. The pharmaceutical industry’s reliance on academic start-ups for early-stage innovation is not a parasite feeding on public funds; it is an efficient division of labor. Academia excels at fundamental discovery, while industry excels at development, optimization, and large-scale clinical execution.

Where Angell is absolutely correct, however, is in how this partnership is managed at the clinical trial stage. The fact that pharmaceutical companies can sponsor trials, control the data, suppress negative results, and hire academics merely as figureheads is a profound scientific perversion. The solution is not to ban industry funding for clinical trials, but to structurally reform how that funding is administered. If a drug company wishes to test a new compound at an academic medical center, the funds should be placed in an independent, blind institutional trust. The academic investigators must retain absolute ownership of the raw data, maintain sole control over the trial design, and hold an unassailable right to publish the results regardless of the outcome. Recent initiatives, such as the mandate to register all clinical trials on ClinicalTrials.gov before they begin, are steps in the right direction, preventing companies from hiding failed trials in the dark.

Furthermore, Angell’s stance on continuing medical education and expert advisory panels is unassailable. There is no justification for doctors to accept gifts, meals, or CME funding from the very companies whose products they prescribe. If medicine considers itself a learned and independent profession, its practitioners must pay for their own continuing education, just as lawyers, engineers, and accountants do. The fact that professional societies and universities allow pharmaceutical marketing budgets to underwrite "education" is a conflict of interest that no institutional firewall can truly sanitize.

Ultimately, we must separate the necessary financial mechanisms of drug development from the ethical obligations of clinical research and practice. We cannot cure the disease of bias by killing the patient—which is what abandoning the Bayh-Dole framework and exclusively relying on public funds for drug development would do. Instead, academic medicine must reclaim its backbone. It must demand that industry money comes with no strings attached to data access or publication, and it must banish pharmaceutical influence from the classroom, the clinic, and the regulatory panel. We need the pharmaceutical industry’s capital to bring life-saving drugs to market, but we do not need to sell them our scientific integrity in the process.

Marcia Angell’s indictment is powerful because it is not anti-medicine and not even, in the usual sense, anti-industry. It is an argument about institutional character. A drug company is organized to sell drugs. A university medical center is organized—or should be organized—to discover truth, train physicians, and care for patients. When the second institution begins to borrow the habits, incentives, and language of the first, something important is lost even if no one commits fraud.

On the central point, Angell is right: disclosure is not enough. For years medicine has treated conflicts of interest as if they were a kind of footnote. The assumption has been that if readers, students, patients, or guideline users are told that a researcher consults for Pfizer, Merck, GlaxoSmithKline, or Eli Lilly, they can somehow discount the information properly. But no ordinary reader of a journal article can know how much the trial design, the choice of comparator, the dose, the endpoints, the statistical analysis, or the decision to publish were shaped before the disclosure statement ever appeared. Bias does not usually arrive wearing a black hat. It is often built into the question being asked.

The record of the last decade makes that hard to deny. Vioxx showed how a blockbuster drug could be widely prescribed before its cardiovascular risks were fully reckoned with. The controversies over antidepressant trials in children showed the damage done when negative or equivocal results remain unpublished or are presented too favorably. Litigation over Neurontin exposed the use of sponsored “education” and publication strategies to expand off-label prescribing. These are not isolated lapses by a few bad actors; they are predictable results of a system in which the evidence base for prescribing is often generated, filtered, interpreted, and promoted by companies with billions at stake.

Still, the conclusion should not be that academic medicine and industry must have no dealings with one another. The development of a useful drug requires chemistry, toxicology, manufacturing, clinical testing, regulatory work, and distribution on a scale that universities generally cannot supply. Industry has real expertise. It also takes real financial risks, including many failures for every success. A romantic picture of pure academic discovery followed by public-spirited dissemination will not produce the drugs patients need.

The question is therefore not whether collaboration should exist, but who controls the terms. Here Angell’s proposed line is the right one: industry may fund research, but it should not own the science. Academic investigators must have guaranteed access to complete data, independent statistical analysis, and the unqualified right to publish results within a defined time. Contracts that give sponsors veto power over publication should be unacceptable at any serious medical school. Institutional review boards should not approve trials unless these protections are in place. Journals, too, should refuse to publish industry-sponsored studies unless they can verify that named academic authors actually controlled and interpreted the work.

Clinical trial registration and reporting are essential parts of this architecture. The movement toward mandatory registration, strengthened by journal requirements and by the FDA Amendments Act of 2007, is one of the most important reforms in recent years. But registration alone is not enough if posted results are incomplete, delayed, or too technical to be useful. The public bargain should be simple: if human beings are asked to assume risk in a trial, the results belong in the public record. Negative findings are not commercial disappointments to be buried; they are medical knowledge.

Angell is also persuasive about medical education. There is no defensible reason for physicians to depend on drug companies for continuing medical education. The argument that doctors are too busy or too burdened to pay for their own education is embarrassing. Lawyers, accountants, pilots, and teachers manage to maintain professional competence without accepting subsidized dinners from companies whose products they may later buy or recommend. The fact that a lecture is scientifically accurate does not make the surrounding marketing harmless. Repeated exposure to branded messages, friendly representatives, free meals, and prestigious speakers changes norms. It teaches young physicians that dependence on industry is ordinary.

Some medical schools have begun to respond. Several have restricted gifts, samples, speakers bureaus, and ghostwritten articles. The Association of American Medical Colleges has urged tighter conflict-of-interest rules. The Institute of Medicine’s 2009 report called for a broad cultural change. The new federal health reform law includes “sunshine” provisions requiring public reporting of many industry payments to physicians. These are welcome steps. But transparency should be a floor, not a ceiling. If a payment would compromise trust when revealed, the problem is not secrecy alone; the problem is the payment.

Where Angell’s essay is most vulnerable is in its treatment of Bayh-Dole and commercialization of academic discoveries. She is right that universities have become too enamored of patents, start-ups, and royalty windfalls. Technology-transfer offices can create perverse incentives, especially when early scientific tools are locked up in exclusive licenses. The image of every laboratory as a potential company is corrosive. It encourages secrecy, delays publication, and tempts universities to measure success in licensing revenue rather than knowledge.

But Bayh-Dole should not be blamed for every distortion in modern biomedical research. Some discoveries do need private investment to become usable products. A promising molecule, diagnostic method, or biologic may require expensive development before it reaches patients. Without some predictable intellectual-property arrangement, many such projects would languish. The better reform is not to abolish university licensing, but to discipline it. Universities should favor nonexclusive licensing for research tools and platform technologies; reserve exclusive licenses for cases where they are truly necessary; include access provisions for poor patients and neglected diseases; and make far more serious use of the law’s requirement that publicly funded inventions be made available on reasonable terms.

The deeper issue is not patents themselves but mission drift. Academic medical centers have allowed financial scarcity to become a moral excuse. Because reimbursement is tight, because laboratories are expensive, because star faculty can leave, almost any industry arrangement is rationalized as necessary. But necessity is often exaggerated. A department that cannot function without consulting fees, ghostwriting, commercially shaped CME, or sponsor control of data is not being sustained by industry; it is being transformed by it.

Patients understand this more clearly than many physicians do. A patient entering a clinic assumes that the doctor’s recommendation is based on the best available evidence and the patient’s welfare. If that recommendation has been influenced by a paid lecture, a consulting contract, a guideline panel stacked with conflicted experts, or a published trial designed by a marketing department, the injury is not only a possibly inferior prescription. It is a breach of trust.

Medicine cannot eliminate money from medical research, nor should it pretend that commercial incentives never produce public benefits. But it can insist on boundaries. Industry can make drugs, test them under independently governed rules, and sell them honestly. Universities can study drugs, teach doctors, write guidelines, and advise regulators. What they cannot do is merge these roles without corrupting them.

Angell’s proposals may sound severe only because medicine has grown accustomed to arrangements that should always have seemed improper. The proper standard is not whether a brilliant physician can take industry money and remain personally honorable. Many can. The standard is whether the profession can design institutions that deserve public confidence even when individuals are fallible. On that test, academic medicine has been failing. It now has the chance—and the obligation—to recover its independence.

Marcia Angell's essay offers a damning portrait of the relationship between academic medicine and the pharmaceutical industry, and she marshals considerable evidence to support her claims. Yet I find myself resisting the more sweeping conclusions she draws from this evidence. While I share her concern about specific conflicts of interest and their effects on research integrity, her argument sometimes conflates several distinct problems and risks throwing out valuable collaborations along with the corrupt ones.

Angell is most persuasive when documenting specific instances of financial influence. The examples she cites are compelling: the Serzone trial with its extensive disclosures, the cholesterol guidelines panel with its industry ties, the DSM-IV contributors with financial relationships to drug companies. These are not mere coincidences but patterns that demand reform. The Vioxx panel episode is particularly troubling—if financial conflicts affected votes on a drug later shown to cause serious harm, we have clear evidence that current disclosure practices are insufficient.

However, Angell's proposed solutions raise questions she does not adequately address. Her blanket prescription for arm's-length funding ignores the real constraints facing academic medical centers in 2010. NIH budgets have not kept pace with inflation for years, and teaching hospitals face relentless pressure on their clinical margins. If researchers must choose between industry funding and no funding at all, the Arm's Length ideal becomes a luxury that serves only the most prestigious institutions.

More fundamentally, Angell tends to treat "industry" as a monolithic corrupting force, when in fact pharmaceutical companies vary considerably in their practices and their commitment to scientific integrity. The industry's recent history includes genuine therapeutic advances—HIV/AIDS treatments, targeted cancer therapies, hepatitis C drugs—that emerged from collaboration rather than despite it. A framework that assumes all industry-funded research is compromised may discard collaborations that could produce similar advances.

I also find problematic her dismissal of Bayh-Dole. The act has indeed created perverse incentives, but the pre-1980 era she invokes was not simply a golden age of pure science. Government-funded research also faced biases—toward certain disease areas, toward particular methodological approaches, toward institutional prestige. The solution is not to return to a system that arguably underperformed in translating discoveries into treatments, but to reform the current system while preserving its genuine benefits.

Angell's critique of continuing medical education is powerful, and the Pri-Med example she cites does suggest a troubling conflation of marketing and education. Yet her alternative—profession-funded CME—faces practical obstacles. Many physicians, particularly in underserved areas, rely on industry-supported programs because alternatives are scarce or expensive. Reform of CME structures is needed, but a sudden cutoff of industry support could create gaps in medical education that harm patients.

What is most valuable in Angell's essay is her insistence on clarity about missions. Academic medical centers and drug companies do have different primary purposes, and acknowledging this difference honestly would resolve many of the conflicts she describes. But this clarity need not require severing all ties. It requires, rather, transparent rules that separate legitimate collaboration from compromised relationships.

The reforms Angell proposes are directionally sound—disclosure requirements, prohibition of gifts, greater independence in research design and publication. These measures could be implemented incrementally without the wholesale restructuring her essay sometimes implies. What is needed is not a choice between complete industry dependence and complete industry exclusion, but a thoughtful calibration that preserves valuable collaboration while constraining its obvious abuses.

Angell ends with a call to resist "market ideology." Yet the status quo she critiques was not produced by market logic alone—it was shaped by specific policy choices, institutional decisions, and regulatory frameworks that can be changed through deliberate action. The answer is not to reject markets entirely but to construct better rules for how academic medicine interacts with them. That is a harder but more achievable goal than the thoroughgoing skepticism her essay sometimes seems to endorse.

Angell Is Right, and the Rot Goes Deeper Than She Says

Marcia Angell's indictment of the entanglement between academic medicine and the pharmaceutical industry is largely correct, and as someone who has spent a career in primary care, I would only add that her account, if anything, understates the problem on the ground. She writes from the vantage point of a journal editor and elite faculty member. From the vantage point of an ordinary internist seeing thirty patients a day, the corruption is even more naked, because what trickles down to us is not the data but the marketing residue: the glossy reprint, the dinner talk, the drug rep with the latest "outcomes" study tucked into a sample bag.

Consider the recent history that should haunt anyone who prescribes. Vioxx, which she mentions, was not a freak accident. Merck had internal data suggesting cardiovascular risk for years before withdrawal, and the published trials were carefully framed. The reanalysis of Study 329, which compared paroxetine to placebo in adolescents, showed that what GlaxoSmithKline marketed as a positive trial was, on the actual data, a negative one with serious safety signals. Avandia is now embroiled in a similar controversy, with Steven Nissen's meta-analysis pointing toward elevated cardiovascular risk that GlaxoSmithKline's own internal analyses had apparently flagged. The pattern is so consistent that one has to stop calling it a series of scandals and start calling it the business model.

Where I would push Angell is on her diagnosis of academic complicity. She frames it as a story of medical centers becoming "supplicants" because of shrinking reimbursements. That is too kind. The senior faculty I have watched build consulting empires were not driven into industry's arms by hunger; they were drawn by ambition and the lifestyle that a $500,000 supplemental income provides. The deans and department chairs who sit on corporate boards are not victims of austerity. They are participants in a class-formation process within medicine — the rise of a physician-entrepreneur stratum whose interests have diverged sharply from those of working clinicians and from those of patients. Conflict-of-interest rules are weak because the people who would write them are the people who would lose money under them.

Her three reforms are necessary but not sufficient. Banning personal payments to clinical trialists, banning gifts and industry-funded CME, and stripping the windfall from Bayh-Dole patents would all help. But none of these touches the central problem, which is that we ask a profit-maximizing industry to generate the evidence base for medical practice. The structural fix is to require that pivotal trials of any drug seeking FDA approval be conducted by an independent body — funded by industry fees, modeled perhaps on how the SEC funds itself, but with no contractual relationship between the sponsor and the investigator. Industry would still pay; industry simply would not choose the design, the comparator, the dose, or the publication strategy. Until that separation is made, disclosure is window dressing.

I also want to defend one of Angell's points that draws the most predictable scorn: the claim that there is "very little innovative research in the modern pharmaceutical industry." Critics will say this is unfair, pointing to imatinib, the statins, the protease inhibitors. But the question is not whether industry has ever produced an important drug; it is what fraction of current pipeline activity addresses unmet medical need versus me-too variation, lifestyle indications, and patent-extension strategies. Anyone who reads the trade press honestly knows the answer. The blockbuster model rewards drugs for chronic conditions in wealthy populations, taken daily for life. It does not reward narrow-spectrum antibiotics, vaccines for diseases of the poor, or treatments for rare conditions absent special incentives. The opportunity cost Angell mentions is not hypothetical; it is the reason we are running out of antibiotics while the eighth statin comes to market.

A Partial Defense of the Partnership

Marcia Angell writes with the moral clarity of someone who has watched abuses up close, and there is much in her essay that any honest observer of academic medicine must concede. The Vioxx panel scandal, ghostwriting, the more egregious consulting arrangements — these are real, and they need correction. Start with the historical baseline she invokes. Angell repeatedly suggests that medical research before 1980 was "every bit as productive" as it is now, and that the Bayh-Dole regime has been a windfall-seeking distortion rather than a genuine engine of translation. This claim does not survive contact with the record. Before Bayh-Dole, the federal government held title to thousands of patents derived from public funding, and licensed almost none of them — fewer than five percent by most accounts. Promising compounds sat on shelves because no firm would invest the hundreds of millions required for development without exclusivity. The biotechnology industry as we know it — Genentech, Amgen, the entire monoclonal antibody revolution — emerged in the wake of Bayh-Dole and the Diamond v. Chakrabarty decision, not by coincidence. Imatinib, trastuzumab, the HPV vaccine, modern HIV therapeutics: these came out of the post-1980 ecosystem, not the pre-1980 one. One can argue that the system has accreted abuses, but one cannot seriously argue that the prior system was equivalently productive.

Second, Angell's portrait of pharmaceutical R&D as essentially imitative — "very little innovative research" — relies on a rhetorical sleight of hand. Yes, many approved drugs are me-toos, and yes, this is a problem worth addressing. But "innovative" research is not the same as "FDA approval of a novel mechanism." A great deal of genuinely innovative work — figuring out which targets are druggable, which leads can be optimized, which formulations cross which membranes — happens inside companies and never appears in the journals Angell read at the New England Journal. The conceit that universities do the creative work and companies merely package it has always flattered academics, but it is largely false. Drug development is itself a scientific discipline, and the people who do it well are mostly not in universities.

Third, the proposal that pivotal trials be conducted at arm's length from sponsors, with no consulting relationships, sounds clean but raises real questions about who is competent to design them. A trial of a kinase inhibitor in non-small-cell lung cancer requires intimate knowledge of the molecule's pharmacokinetics, its known toxicities from earlier phases, and the specific subpopulation likely to benefit. The people who have that knowledge are, almost by definition, the people who have worked closely with the sponsor. Angell would presumably reply that they can share information without taking personal payments. Perhaps. But the line between "scientific collaboration" and "consulting" is fuzzier in practice than her clean prescriptions suggest, and an enforcement regime built on that line will produce either evasions or a chilling effect that pushes the best clinical investigators out of the field.

Industry-funded CME is indefensible in its current form, and the cozy arrangements between Madison Avenue agencies and "medical-education companies" are an embarrassment. If the medical profession truly took back education as its own responsibility, that would be wonderful. The track record on the profession actually doing this, however, is thin. Medical societies have themselves become substantially industry-funded, and there is no obvious financial substitute on the horizon.

What I want to defend is not the status quo but the basic legitimacy of cooperation between universities and industry. Translating a discovery from a research bench into a pill that a patient can swallow is enormously expensive, technically demanding, and risky. Most candidates fail. The institutions that bear that risk need a return when they succeed, or the risk-taking stops. The reasonable debate is about how exclusivity, price, and access are structured — not whether translation should occur at all. Angell sometimes writes as if the only honorable model were the Salk model, in which the discovery is given to humanity gratis. It is a beautiful story, but the polio vaccine was developed in an era of public funding levels we no longer maintain, with technical demands far lower than those of modern biologics. We cannot simply will ourselves back into that world by rhetoric.

So: reform aggressively, yes. Ban gifts, ban speakers' bureaus, ban ghostwriting, require comprehensive disclosure, and rebuild the firewall between industry and CME. Strengthen FDA conflict-of-interest rules on advisory panels. These are real reforms, mostly attainable, that would address most of the abuses Angell documents without dismantling a productive system.

What Angell Misses About Incentives

Marcia Angell's essay is a powerful piece of moral testimony, and I share her revulsion at much of what she describes. But moral testimony is not analysis, and Angell's piece suffers from a structural weakness: she repeatedly treats as scandals what are in fact predictable equilibria of the incentive system we have built. If we want different behavior, we need different incentives. Exhortation will not do it.

Consider the central pathology Angell identifies: industry-sponsored trials are far more likely to favor the sponsor's product than NIH-funded trials. She is right; this is a well-documented finding, replicated across literatures. But why? Not, mainly, because individual investigators are corrupt — though some are. It is because the sponsor controls every degree of freedom in trial design where favorable bias can be introduced: the comparator drug, the dose of the comparator, the patient population, the primary endpoint, the duration, the statistical analysis plan, the decision of whether and where to publish. Each choice, taken individually, is defensible. Taken together, they constitute what economists call a "garden of forking paths" in which a positive result is overwhelmingly likely even from a marginal drug.

Angell's response to this is to tell investigators to stop accepting payments. This addresses one channel of bias — investigator conflict — while leaving the structural channels untouched. A trial designed by a sponsor with zero payments to its academic figureheads will still produce biased results, because the design itself is biased. The right response is to take design decisions away from sponsors entirely for pivotal trials. Several mechanisms could achieve this: an FDA pre-registration process that locks in design before the sponsor sees any data; a public clinical-trials authority that contracts trial conduct on behalf of regulators; or a fee-funded independent trials office, analogous to the way the PCAOB is funded by audit fees but operates independently of auditors. None of these proposals require villainizing investigators. They require recognizing that the current system asks the regulated entity to generate the regulator's evidence base, which is a structural absurdity.

Similar logic applies to Bayh-Dole. Angell wants academic patents licensed cheaply, non-exclusively, or placed in the public domain. The intuition is appealing — public money, public benefit — but the mechanism it ignores is that downstream development requires exclusivity to be financeable. A small biotech firm cannot raise venture capital to spend $300 million on Phase III trials of a non-exclusive license. So if we forbid exclusive licensing, we will get one of two outcomes: either the public sector takes over downstream development (a defensible position, but one that requires committing the public funds Angell does not mention), or promising leads will sit on shelves. The Stanford recombinant-DNA license is not really a counterexample; it was a foundational platform technology with broad applicability, not a single therapeutic compound requiring late-stage development.

A better reform — and one I wish Angell had pushed — would be to enforce the "reasonable terms" provision of Bayh-Dole that she correctly notes has been ignored. The statute already requires that drugs licensed from federally funded discoveries be made available to the public on reasonable terms. The NIH has the authority to march in and relicense if a licensee fails to do so. It has never exercised that authority. If it did, even once, the entire pricing equilibrium for academically derived drugs would shift. This is a low-cost, legally available reform that does not require new legislation.

On CME, Angell's diagnosis is sound but her remedy is incomplete. Telling doctors to "pay their own way" sounds principled, but compare it to how other professionals handle continuing education. Lawyers' CLE is not industry-funded because there is no comparable industry; the analogy doesn't transfer. The real fix is institutional: hospitals and medical schools should fund CME out of their operating budgets as a cost of maintaining a competent professional staff, the way airlines fund recurrent pilot training. This is expensive but feasible, and unlike Angell's individualist remedy, it does not require thousands of doctors to spontaneously change their habits.

There is one place I think Angell badly underweights the difficulty: the FDA itself. She criticizes its advisory panels for conflicts, and rightly. But the FDA's user-fee structure, set up under PDUFA in 1992 and renewed several times since, has made the agency financially dependent on the industry it regulates. About half of FDA's drug-review budget now comes from industry user fees. This is the kind of structural capture that no amount of disclosure can fix. Reforming it requires Congress to appropriate more money, which Congress is unlikely to do. Until that changes, expecting the FDA to be a clean referee is naïve.

I do not want to leave the impression that I disagree with Angell's basic moral vision. The medical profession has degraded itself by accepting industry money in ways that compromise its claim to be a profession at all. The remedy, however, is not primarily to demand virtue from individuals; it is to redesign the institutional incentives so that the merely self-interested behavior of rational actors does not produce biased evidence, distorted prescribing, and corrupted guidelines. We do not solve the principal-agent problem in finance by telling traders to be more ethical. We should not try to solve it in medicine that way either.

I cannot know whether my doctor's enthusiasm for the new drug derives from a careful reading of the literature or from a series of dinners with a drug rep. The consequence is that I have lost confidence in the information environment, and I now waste enormous amounts of personal time and energy trying to construct a private epistemic system to substitute for the public one. I read the original trial papers. I read Cochrane reviews when they exist. I read consumer advocacy material from groups like Public Citizen. Even an honest doctor giving honest advice now operates under a cloud.

Angell's reforms are necessary but, I would argue, not sufficient from the patient's perspective. I would add four things.

I do not want my doctors to forfeit their privileges. I want them to be the kind of profession that deserves them. Angell is telling them how to be that profession. They should listen.

Disclosure, Not Prohibition

Marcia Angell's essay is the latest in a long tradition of reformist writing about academic medicine, and like its predecessors, it is more compelling as moral indictment than as policy prescription. The specific abuses she describes are real, and her career gives her authority to describe them. But the regime she proposes — essentially, a near-total prohibition on personal financial relationships between academic researchers and industry — would, in my view, do more harm than good. The better path is robust, mandatory, publicly accessible disclosure combined with targeted prohibitions on the most distorting practices.

The case for disclosure-plus-targeted-prohibition rests on several observations Angell underweights.

First, the population of researchers who consult for industry is not uniformly compromised. Some are bought; some take honoraria for talks that reflect their actual scientific views; some advise on trial design in ways that genuinely improve the science. A blanket prohibition collapses these distinctions, treats the honest consultant and the kept opinion-leader identically, and removes legitimate channels by which expertise flows between sectors. Industry actually does need scientific advice, and the country actually does benefit when its best researchers can provide it.

Second, prohibition regimes in practice tend to drive transactions underground or into legalistic workarounds. The history of conflict-of-interest regulation in academic medicine over the past two decades has been a sequence of prohibitions followed by adaptations — the speaker's bureau renamed as the advisory board, the honorarium routed through a foundation, the equity stake held by a spouse. A regime that depends on the integrity of categorical bans will lose its battle with creative compliance. A regime that depends on universal, granular disclosure does not require categorical bans to do its work; the daylight does the work itself.

Third, Angell underestimates how much progress has already been made on disclosure. The Cleveland Clinic published its faculty's industry relationships beginning in 2008. The Association of American Medical Colleges issued substantially strengthened conflict-of-interest guidelines in 2008. Senator Grassley's investigations have prompted multiple universities to overhaul their policies, and the proposed Physician Payments Sunshine Act would, if enacted, create a federal database of industry payments to physicians. The trajectory is in the right direction; what is needed is acceleration and enforcement, not a regulatory regime change.

Fourth, and most importantly, Angell's prescriptions confuse the source of bias. The most damaging biases in clinical research are not introduced by investigator payments. They are introduced by sponsor control of trial design, data ownership, and publication decisions. A trial in which the sponsor picks the comparator dose, owns the data, and decides whether to publish will produce a biased result regardless of whether the academic figurehead consults or not. Conversely, a trial in which the design is locked in by independent review, the data are deposited in a public repository, and publication is mandatory will produce a relatively unbiased result even if the investigator consults extensively. Banning consulting is fighting on the wrong battlefield.

What would a serious disclosure-plus regime look like? Six elements.

One: federal mandatory disclosure of every payment, in cash or in kind, from any drug or device manufacturer to any licensed physician or any employee of an academic medical center, posted to a public database with a low de minimis threshold. Failure to disclose carries professional sanctions.

Two: mandatory pre-registration of every clinical trial, with the design and analysis plan locked in before patient enrollment begins. Deviations require justified amendments, also publicly posted.

Three: mandatory publication of all trial results within twelve months of trial completion, regardless of outcome. Sponsors do not own the data; the data belong to the trial participants, who consented to their use for scientific purposes, not for marketing purposes.

Four: a categorical prohibition on three specific practices that have no defensible function: ghostwriting of journal articles, the listing of investigators as authors on papers they did not write, and the use of speakers' bureaus to deliver industry-prepared talks under academic auspices. These are the practices that come closest to outright fraud, and they should be treated as such.

Five: a categorical prohibition on serving on FDA advisory panels or guideline-writing committees while holding consulting relationships with affected manufacturers, with retroactive disclosure required for a multi-year window.

Six: industry funding of CME should be structurally separated from accreditation. The current arrangement in which the ACCME accredits industry-funded providers is, as Angell correctly notes, indefensible. CME funding should pass through a blind pool that breaks the link between sponsor and content.

This is a substantial reform agenda. It does not require us to dismantle the productive collaborations between academic medicine and industry that have, on net, served patients well over the past three decades. It does not require us to forgo the translational benefits of Bayh-Dole. It does not require us to tell the country's best researchers that their consulting income is intrinsically corrupting.

Angell, I think, has been so disillusioned by what she saw at the New England Journal of Medicine that she has come to see industry-academic relationships as inherently corrupt rather than as relationships that need to be properly bounded. I sympathize with the disillusion. I do not share the conclusion. The right metaphor is not the brewery teaching the alcoholic; it is the auditor relationship with a client. Auditors have financial relationships with the companies they audit. The system works — when it works — because of structural separations, mandatory disclosures, rotation requirements, and severe penalties for violations. We should do the same in medicine. Prohibition is the wrong tool.

This is not a system with corruption around the edges. This is a system whose central function is the extraction of rents from the sick, with academic medicine as the credentialing arm.

Tinkering with conflict-of-interest disclosures will not change this. The only adequate response is to remove drug development from the profit-maximizing private sector and place it under public control.

This sounds radical only because we have forgotten that almost every component of drug development is already publicly funded or could easily be. Basic biomedical research is publicly funded through NIH — about thirty billion dollars a year. The training of the scientists who do that research is publicly funded. The infrastructure of teaching hospitals where clinical trials are conducted is largely publicly funded, directly or through Medicare and Medicaid. The patent system that gives drug companies their exclusivity is a public creation. The FDA, which determines what can be sold, is a public agency. The only piece that is not currently public is the late-stage development, manufacturing, and marketing of drugs, and that is precisely the piece that generates the abuses Angell catalogs.

A public drug-development institute, funded at perhaps fifty billion dollars a year — less than the current marketing budgets of the major firms — could conduct pivotal trials on candidate compounds selected on the basis of medical need rather than market potential. Compounds that succeeded could be manufactured by generic manufacturers under contract, at cost plus a regulated margin. There would be no patents because the development was publicly funded; there would be no marketing because there would be nothing to market against; there would be no me-too drugs because there would be no patent races to win. Antibiotics for resistant infections would be developed because they are medically needed, regardless of return on investment. Vaccines for diseases of the global poor would be developed for the same reason.

The molecular biology underlying every important drug class of the past forty years — recombinant proteins, monoclonal antibodies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, statins, antiretrovirals — was worked out in publicly funded labs. Private firms have added development capacity, not basic creativity. Angell makes this point and it deserves to be repeated.

What the Academy Owes Itself

I want to set aside the policy debate for a moment and focus on what I take to be Marcia Angell's deepest point: that academic medicine has, over thirty years, lost a clear sense of what it is for. The conflicts of interest she describes are symptoms. The underlying disease is the erosion of an institutional self-understanding.

A university medical school is a strange institution. It teaches, it heals, it discovers, and it does so under a charter that gives it remarkable privileges: tax exemption, public funding, monopoly credentialing power, a presumption of public trust. These privileges are not granted because medical schools are particularly nice places. They are granted because the work they do — generating reliable knowledge, training competent practitioners, caring for the sick without regard to ability to pay — is understood to be of such public importance that society subsidizes it and protects it from the ordinary pressures of commerce.

The bargain implicit in this arrangement is that medical schools will conduct themselves in ways the market cannot reliably produce. They will fund research the market would not fund, ask questions a profit-seeker would not ask, teach students for the long-term benefit of the profession rather than the short-term needs of any employer, and care for patients whose care does not pay. In exchange, they receive the privileges of academic status.

What Angell documents is the unraveling of this bargain. When a department chair earns half a million dollars a year as a consultant; when a medical school sets up an office whose mission is to meet "industry's needs"; when faculty serve as listed authors on papers ghostwritten by drug companies; when the dean sits on a corporate board — these are not, primarily, problems of bias in any particular study. They are violations of the implicit contract under which the institution exists. The institution has begun to conduct itself as a business while continuing to claim the privileges of a non-business.

This is a problem of self-understanding before it is a problem of regulation. No external reform regime will work if the people inside the institution do not believe that they have a distinctive vocation. Conflict-of-interest rules can be drafted, but they will be evaded if those subject to them regard them as nuisances rather than as expressions of what their profession owes. The painful truth is that much of contemporary academic medicine no longer believes that it owes anything beyond what an honest business owes — produce good products, follow the rules, maximize your returns.

Angell knows this. Her closing line — that researchers are not entitled to whatever they want just because they are very smart — is aimed at exactly this attitude. But she frames the necessary reforms primarily in regulatory terms (rules about payments, rules about patents, rules about CME), when the deeper reform she is calling for is cultural. The question is whether the medical profession can recover the sense of itself as a profession — a body with internal standards, public obligations, and a willingness to forgo income that other comparable groups take for granted.

The historical precedents for this kind of cultural recovery are mixed. Law, accounting, finance — each profession has gone through periods of moral self-examination, and the results have been uneven. The accounting profession after Enron and Sarbanes-Oxley is arguably more rigorous than before, but no one would describe it as having recovered a pre-modern sense of vocation. Medicine's challenge is harder because the financial pressures are larger and the entanglements deeper.

What might recovery look like? I would offer three suggestions, modest in scope but, I hope, oriented toward the right target.

First, medical schools should re-articulate, in their own institutional documents, what they are for. This sounds like the kind of empty exercise that produces glossy strategic plans no one reads. But there is a real question whether faculties of medicine could currently write down, in language they would all sign, what distinguishes them from a research division of Pfizer. Forcing the question would clarify thinking.

Second, the profession should treat conflict-of-interest enforcement as a matter of professional honor rather than regulatory compliance. The relevant analogy is the bar association rather than the SEC. Egregious cases should result not just in fines or institutional embarrassment but in professional sanction — exclusion from journal editorial boards, ineligibility for academic honors, loss of standing. The profession has these tools. It does not use them.

Third, senior faculty should accept that part of their role is to model behavior they wish to see. The current generation of medical-school leaders includes many who built their careers in an era of expanding industry relationships and personally benefited from it. They cannot credibly preach to junior faculty about restraint while continuing the practices they accumulated. There is no painless way to handle this, but acknowledgment is a start.

I have spent this essay not arguing about policy because the policy arguments, important as they are, presuppose an answer to a prior question: what kind of institution does academic medicine want to be? Angell's essay is, at its best, an attempt to make her colleagues face that question. They have spent twenty years not facing it. The hour is later than they realize. The public's patience with corrupted expertise is not unlimited, and once it runs out, the privileges of academic medicine — the trust, the funding, the autonomy — will be much harder to recover than they have been to lose.
