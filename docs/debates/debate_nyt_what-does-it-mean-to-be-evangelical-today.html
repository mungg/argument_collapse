Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The current panic among evangelical elites over Donald Trump's success reveals a longstanding confusion about what the word "evangelical" actually denotes. Russell Moore's instinct to retreat to "gospel Christian" is understandable but evasive. It treats "evangelical" as a theological category that has been temporarily hijacked by political opportunists, when in truth the term has functioned as a hybrid—part doctrinal, part sociological, part political—for at least four decades.

David Bebbington's classic definition still names the doctrinal core: biblicism, crucicentrism, conversionism, and activism. By that standard, an evangelical is someone who believes the Bible is authoritative, that Christ's atoning death is central, that personal conversion is necessary, and that the gospel must be shared. By that standard, much of Trump's primary support is not particularly evangelical at all. Pew and LifeWay surveys have consistently shown that the Trump-friendly portion of the "evangelical" electorate attends church less frequently, can articulate less doctrine, and is more likely to identify culturally as "born-again" than to participate in the disciplines that the label historically implied.

This is what Moore is reacting to, and his frustration is legitimate. But the problem predates 2016. Ever since Jerry Falwell's Moral Majority fused white Protestant identity with Republican politics in 1979, pollsters, journalists, and the movement's own leaders have allowed "evangelical" to drift from a confessional descriptor to a demographic shorthand for white, churchgoing-ish, culturally conservative Americans. Once that drift was complete, it was only a matter of time before a candidate emerged who could mobilize the demographic without satisfying the confession. Trump, twice divorced, biblically illiterate, and openly contemptuous of repentance, is the predictable consequence, not the cause, of the term's hollowing out.

To be evangelical today, then, requires a choice that has been deferred too long. One option is to reclaim the doctrinal substance—to insist, as Moore is now trying to do, that the word names a way of following Jesus rather than a voting bloc. That project will require the movement to confront its entanglement with white racial identity, something nonwhite evangelicals (a growing share of the global and American church) have been asking for years. The other option is to concede the word to sociology and let "evangelical" mean what exit polls say it means: a tribe defined by grievance and cultural defensiveness.

Moore's "gospel Christian" gambit suggests that even insiders sense the first option may already be lost. But abandoning the label is not the same as solving the problem; the underlying confusion of faith with tribe will follow whatever new word is chosen. The harder work is not rebranding. It is catechesis, repentance, and a willingness to lose elections rather than lose the gospel.

The spectacle of Donald Trump, a thrice-married casino magnate who once bragged about his womanizing and has shown scant fluency with Scripture, winning a plurality of self-identified evangelical voters on Super Tuesday has laid bare a profound identity crisis. Exit polls from states across the South confirmed what early contests had signaled: for millions of Americans, the word “evangelical” no longer describes a theological commitment so much as a cultural and political tribe. To ask what it means to be evangelical today is to step into a tug-of-war between a historic religious movement and a brand that has been co-opted for partisan warfare.

Historically, evangelicalism has been defined by a constellation of beliefs: a personal conversion experience, often described as being “born again”; a high view of biblical authority; an emphasis on the atoning work of Christ on the cross; and an impulse toward activism, whether evangelistic or social. This tradition, tracing its roots to the Great Awakenings and the rise of fundamentalism, had always possessed inner tensions, but for much of the twentieth century it maintained a coherent identity centered on the gospel. Even Billy Graham, who navigated the halls of power, kept the message of redemption primary. But over the past four decades, the label has been progressively politicized. Since the formation of the Moral Majority in 1979, and especially through the culture wars of the 1990s, “evangelical” became a reliable synonym for conservative Republican. To be evangelical, in the public mind, increasingly meant less about one’s prayer life and more about opposing abortion, defending religious liberty against a secularizing state, and voting for candidates who promised to fight those battles. Church attendance and doctrinal fidelity grew less predictive of the label than partisan allegiance.

It is this hollowing out that the 2016 primaries have thrown into sharp relief. On the one hand, many evangelical leaders have spoken clearly: Russell Moore of the Southern Baptist Convention has called the evangelical designation “subverting the gospel” and prefers “gospel Christian.” Others, like theologian Wayne Grudem, have argued that though Trump is personally flawed, his platform is less hostile to religious freedom and pro-life causes than Hillary Clinton’s would be—a classic lesser-evil logic. Yet for the millions of primary voters who checked the “born-again” box while pulling the lever for Trump, the calculus seems even simpler. Polling consistently shows that white evangelicals are far more likely than the general public to fear demographic change, to see Islam as a threat, and to crave a strongman who will “make America great again.” Trump’s nativism and belligerence have spoken to a deeply felt cultural resentment that many now interpret as a spiritual battle. The “evangelical” label, for this significant segment, has become a badge of cultural conservatism and nationalistic grievance, not a statement about the state of one’s soul.

What this means is that “evangelical” today is a fractured category, and the fractures run right through the local church. There are those, like Moore, trying to reclaim the term for a gospel-centered, multiethnic, countercultural Christianity. There are political operatives who deploy the bloc for policy victories. And there is a vast, mostly white, middle-class constituency for whom being evangelical means defending a traditional way of life—family, flag, and free market—with Jesus as a supporting chaplain rather than a commanding Lord. The Trump phenomenon hasn’t created this crisis; it has simply illuminated it, forcing a long-overdue question: can a word that now denotes a voting pattern more than a creed still shepherd souls, or has the salt truly lost its savor?

To be an evangelical today is to belong to a movement whose theological meaning is clearer than its public reputation. But in American public life, “evangelical” has become a political demographic before it is heard as a religious identity. Pollsters ask whether voters are “born again or evangelical,” and the answer is often treated as a proxy for cultural conservatism. That shorthand has some basis in reality: white evangelicals have been a central part of the Republican coalition since the late 1970s, mobilized around abortion, religious liberty, school prayer, sexuality and distrust of secular elites. Yet the shorthand distorts. It flattens theology into partisanship and turns a multiracial, global Christian tradition into a voting bloc.

The Trump campaign has exposed this confusion rather than created it. His appeal to many self-identified evangelicals is not obviously rooted in evangelical doctrine. His language of strength, winning, resentment and national restoration sounds less like the Sermon on the Mount than like a politics of grievance. Many evangelicals who support him may be expressing fear about cultural loss, anger at political leaders, concern about immigration or a desire for protection in a changing country. Those are political instincts, not necessarily evangelical convictions. When church leaders recoil from the label, they are not merely being fastidious about branding. They are asking whether a word tied to “good news” can survive being identified with contempt, cruelty or celebrity.

Still, abandoning the term may concede too much. If “evangelical” is surrendered whenever it is abused, then its meaning will be left entirely to campaign strategists and exit polls. The better response is retrieval and repentance. Retrieval means insisting that evangelical faith is first about Jesus Christ, not access to power. Repentance means admitting that many evangelicals have too often confused moral witness with partisan loyalty, and have sometimes shown more alarm about losing influence than about poverty, racism, addiction, refugees or the character of their own leaders.

There is also no single evangelical future. Younger evangelicals often remain theologically orthodox while sounding less automatic in their political loyalties. Latino, Asian-American and African-American evangelicals complicate the idea that the movement belongs to white conservatives. Globally, evangelical Christianity is growing in Africa, Latin America and Asia, where American culture-war categories do not map neatly onto church life.

So to be evangelical today is to stand at a crossroads. It can mean a voting identity shaped by fear and nostalgia. Or it can mean what the word originally promised: a people formed by Scripture, conversion, the cross and mission, whose public life bears witness not only to what they oppose, but to the humility, mercy and truth they claim to preach.

Watching white evangelicals flock to Donald Trump has produced a cottage industry of hand-wringing, much of it from evangelical elites who profess themselves shocked, shocked, that the rank and file would support such a candidate. The truth is less surprising and less flattering. Consider the genealogy. The "Religious Right" did not coalesce in the 1970s around abortion, as the mythology now claims; it coalesced around defending segregated Christian academies from losing their tax exemptions. From Jerry Falwell's Moral Majority through Pat Robertson's Christian Coalition to today's Values Voter Summits, the movement has consistently traded in grievance: against feminists, against gay Americans, against secular elites, against immigrants, against Muslims. Strip away the theological garnish and what remains is a politics of cultural resentment by people who feel their country slipping away.

Trump speaks that language fluently. He cannot quote a Bible verse correctly — "Two Corinthians" — but he can promise to say "Merry Christmas" again, to keep Muslims out, to make America (which is to say, them) great again. The voters responding to him are not betraying evangelicalism; they are voting their actual priorities, which are and have long been ethnic and cultural before they are confessional.

This is why figures like Russell Moore want a new name. Moore is sincere, and his discomfort is real, but his proposal accepts a polite fiction: that "real" evangelicalism is theologically pristine and has merely been hijacked by bad actors. In fact the political and the theological have been intertwined for so long that they cannot be cleanly separated. Younger evangelicals sense this — surveys have shown them drifting toward the "nones," disaffiliating in significant numbers, citing the movement's harshness on social issues and its captivity to one political party.

If "evangelical" now means white, Republican, and culturally aggrieved, then Trump's success is not a scandal requiring explanation. It is a revelation. It is worth remembering that "evangelical" is not, and has never been, an American word. There are evangelicals in Brazil filling Pentecostal megachurches, evangelicals in Nigeria and Kenya whose Anglican bishops now lecture Canterbury, evangelicals in South Korea sending more missionaries per capita than almost any other nation. Globally, the movement is younger, browner, poorer, and far more vibrant than its American expression. To ask "what does it mean to be evangelical today" only with reference to a South Carolina primary is to make a category error.

This matters because the Trump panic obscures an extraordinary moment in Christian history. The center of gravity of world Christianity has decisively shifted to the global South. The typical evangelical today is not a Southern Baptist deacon in Tennessee but a Pentecostal woman in Lagos or São Paulo. Her concerns — economic survival, spiritual warfare, healing, the integrity of marriage in a context of male flight — are not the concerns that animate Iowa caucus-goers. Her theology may be more supernaturalist than her American cousins' and more communal than individualist. If American evangelical leaders want to recover the term, the most useful thing they could do is decenter themselves. The temptation, evident in the Russell Moore proposal, is to invent a new American label to escape American embarrassments. But the underlying problem — a movement that has confused its national-cultural identity with its faith — will not be solved by linguistic tinkering. It will be solved, if at all, by sitting at the feet of Christians elsewhere who have managed to be theologically serious without being absorbed into a single political party.

There is also something bracing about remembering that evangelicals in many parts of the world face actual costs for their faith: harassment in India, prison in Iran, violence in northern Nigeria. The disagreement between Russell Moore and the evangelicals voting for Donald Trump is not really about Trump.
