Natural Disasters or ‘Acts of God’?

A Filipino writer pointed out last week that her homeland, vulnerable to both earthquakes and typhoons, is so inured to disaster that “the phrase ‘Bahala na’ is embedded in the culture: ‘Leave it to God.’” In law in the U.S., Britain and elsewhere, events considered unforeseeable, like hurricanes and tornadoes, can even be considered “acts of God” – potentially absolving humans of liability.

Even among people who believe in God, faulting a deity for the mass destruction of natural disasters is controversial. Is it constructive to think of natural disasters as acts of God?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Calling a typhoon or earthquake an "act of God" may once have been a useful shorthand, but in 2013 it is mostly a category error — and an increasingly harmful one. The phrase conflates two very different claims: a theological one about ultimate causation, and a practical one about human responsibility. Conflating them lets us off the hook precisely when we should be most accountable.

Consider what we have learned this year alone. Typhoon Haiyan did not kill thousands of Filipinos because God willed it; it killed them because storm surges hit unreinforced coastal housing, because evacuation orders were issued for "typhoons" in a country where people had no frame of reference for a Category 5-equivalent surge, and because poverty concentrates the vulnerable in flimsy structures on exposed ground. Compare Haiyan's toll to that of comparable storms striking wealthier coastlines with better building codes and infrastructure. The hazard is natural; the disaster is largely social. The same lesson emerged from Haiti in 2010 versus Chile that same year, and from Hurricane Sandy's uneven damage across the New York metro region last year.

The legal "act of God" doctrine has its uses — no one should be sued because a once-in-a-century lightning strike toppled a tree — but courts have rightly been narrowing it. As meteorology, seismology and climate science improve, fewer events are genuinely "unforeseeable." A coastal developer in 2013 who pleads surprise that the sea rose, or a municipality that ignored its own floodplain maps, is invoking theology to dodge engineering.

There is also a theological cost. Many believers find it offensive, not comforting, to attribute mass child death to divine intent; the better strands of Jewish, Christian and Muslim thought have long resisted this move, locating God instead in the response — the rescuers, the rebuilding, the solidarity. "Bahala na" can express trust and resilience, but it can also corrode the political will to demand seawalls, codes and warning systems.

So: pray if it helps, and grieve in whatever idiom your tradition offers. But when the water recedes, the relevant agents to interrogate are human ones. That is where lives are actually saved.

The phrase “act of God” has an almost antique ring, evoking a world where plagues and storms fell inexplicably from heaven. In contracts and insurance policies, it remains a legal term of art, a way to carve out liability for events beyond human foresight. But using it to describe the mass suffering of a typhoon—like the one that just tore through the Philippines—is not merely outdated. It is actively destructive, because it obscures the human choices that turn a natural hazard into a catastrophe, and it offers a fatalistic theology that few serious believers actually embrace.

Legally, “act of God” was never meant to be a theological claim. It is a practical fiction for allocating risk when no human agent can be blamed. Yet that fiction becomes a dangerous evasion when applied to disasters that are profoundly shaped by human acts. When Hurricane Katrina drowned New Orleans, the storm was natural; the failed levees, the drained wetlands, and the decades of underinvestment in flood protection were not. The same pattern holds in earthquake zones where corruption and lax building codes fill mass graves. Typhoon Haiyan’s record winds were amplified by a warming ocean, and the coastal deforestation that might have blunted the storm surge was itself a human footprint. To call such events “acts of God” is to absolve governments, planners, and emitters of their responsibility, and to discourage the very investments in resilience that save lives.

Theologically, the phrase is just as hollow. Mainstream Christian, Jewish, and Islamic thought has long struggled with the problem of natural evil and has largely moved away from the image of a deity swatting cities with bolts of punishment. Even those who see the hand of God in the whirlwind usually interpret suffering within a larger narrative of redemption, not as a simple billboard of divine wrath. In the Philippines, “Bahala na” has indeed been a cultural reflex—but often it expresses not passive resignation so much as a steeling of resolve in the face of forces beyond one’s control. When that resolve is met with actual preparation, it can be life-giving; when it curdles into a belief that nothing can be done, it becomes lethal. A constructive faith does not wait for floods to recede; it builds arks.

What, then, is a better frame? We might take our cue from Earth scientists and emergency managers, who speak of “natural hazards”—recognizing the indifferent power of the planet’s systems while insisting that vulnerability is manmade. The earthquake is inevitable; the pancaked school is a choice. The storm comes to all the islands; who has evacuation routes, storm-resistant shelters, and mangrove buffers is a matter of policy and community effort. That reframing does not deny the awe or the grief that disasters provoke. It simply directs that energy toward the only place it can do good: into stronger buildings, early warning systems, and the hard work of reducing poverty. That is far more constructive than lifting our eyes to the sky and murmuring “God’s will.”

Calling a typhoon or earthquake an “act of God” may offer comfort to some survivors, but as public language it is mostly unhelpful. It blurs a crucial distinction: hazards may be natural, but disasters are produced by the meeting of those hazards with human vulnerability. A storm becomes a catastrophe when people live in flimsy housing on exposed coasts, when mangroves have been stripped away, when warnings do not reach the poor, when evacuation centers are inadequate, and when government capacity is overwhelmed or corrupt. None of that is divine.

This is not an argument against faith. In the Philippines, as elsewhere, religious belief often sustains people after loss and mobilizes charity, courage and solidarity. “Bahala na” can mean fatalistic resignation, but it can also mean perseverance in circumstances where individuals have little control. Outsiders should be cautious about sneering at the language people use to endure grief.

But the phrase “act of God” becomes dangerous when it shifts attention away from responsibility. Earthquakes cannot be prevented, but buildings can be made less likely to collapse. Typhoons cannot be stopped, but coastal zoning, early warning systems, storm shelters and competent evacuation plans save lives. Even poverty is not a meteorological fact. It is shaped by land policy, infrastructure, inequality and political choices. To describe mass death as simply God’s will risks excusing the people and institutions that had the power to reduce it.

The legal use of the phrase is similarly problematic. Contracts need some way to recognize extraordinary events beyond reasonable control. No port operator or farmer can guarantee against every storm. But “act of God” should not become a blanket absolution. If a company ignored known flood risks, if a developer built in a dangerous zone, or if officials failed to enforce building codes, the event was not wholly unforeseeable in the morally relevant sense. The law should ask what risks were known, what precautions were reasonable, and who had the power to act.

Climate change makes the old phrase even less adequate. Scientists in 2013 cannot honestly say that any single typhoon was “caused” by global warming. But they can say the planet is warming, seas are rising, and warmer air holds more moisture. These conditions can worsen the consequences of storms. Human beings are no longer merely spectators of weather; we are altering the background conditions in which weather occurs.

So the more constructive language is not “acts of God” but “natural hazards and human choices.” That phrase preserves humility before nature without surrendering responsibility. It leaves room for prayer, but insists on planning.

There is a strain of contemporary commentary, usually secular and usually comfortable, that wants to scrub religious language out of how ordinary people make sense of catastrophe. It is uttered by fishermen heading into uncertain seas and by students before an exam. It is not a refusal of agency; it is a way of acting in the face of conditions you cannot fully control. To mistake it for passive fatalism is to mistake faith for laziness, which is a common error among people who have never needed faith very much.

Second, the theological tradition of naming disasters as acts of God is not primarily about assigning blame. It is about acknowledging finitude. Human beings are not the masters of the planet they imagine themselves to be. Tectonic plates move; the Pacific spawns cyclones; the earth shrugs. No serious religious tradition teaches that a divine cause exempts human beings from the duty to build sea walls, evacuate the elderly, or curb carbon emissions. What religion offers in disaster's wake is something secular reasoning genuinely struggles to provide: a way to grieve, a way to forgive what cannot be undone, a way to bury the dead with dignity, and a way to believe that the next morning is worth waking up for. That is not an opiate. That is survival.

The interesting thing about the phrase "act of God" is that it appears in two very different contexts—insurance contracts and prayer meetings—and we should be careful not to confuse what it does in each.

In the law, "act of God" is a term of art. It refers to natural events of such magnitude and unpredictability that no reasonable defendant could have prevented them. It is not a metaphysical claim. A judge who rules that a flood was an act of God is not endorsing theism; she is saying that liability should not attach. As legal doctrine, it has been narrowing for over a century, precisely because science has expanded the domain of the foreseeable. Hurricanes that struck without warning in 1900 are tracked for days now. Floodplains are mapped. Seismic risk is quantified. The doctrine survives mostly for genuine outliers—a freak microburst, an unprecedented seismic event—and even then courts increasingly ask whether due diligence was performed.

In theology, "act of God" means something else entirely, and it has been controversial within religious traditions themselves for at least as long as the book of Job. Is God the author of the tsunami, or does God suffer with its victims? Is the earthquake punishment, test, accident, or mystery? Voltaire's famous response to the 1755 Lisbon earthquake—skewering the idea that "all is for the best" in a world that can swallow a city whole—remains one of the most powerful pieces of religious philosophy ever written, even though Voltaire was no orthodox believer. Rabbi Harold Kushner's "When Bad Things Happen to Good People" sold millions of copies precisely because so many believers cannot accept that God authors suffering.

So is it constructive to think of natural disasters as acts of God? It depends entirely on who is doing the thinking and to what end.

For a grieving parent in Tacloban, the language may be the only thing that holds a shattered life together, and we should not be quick to take it away. For a building inspector who waved through substandard construction, it is an alibi and should be rejected. For a legislator deciding whether to fund climate adaptation, it is a category error.

The phrase is neither inherently constructive nor destructive. Like most powerful religious language, it does whatever the speaker uses it to do. The honest question is not whether to use it, but why.

To call a disaster an act of God is to make a theological claim, and theological claims have consequences. The specific consequence I want to focus on is the one religious communities themselves rarely want to face: if God acts in the typhoon, then God acted in the deaths of the children of Tacloban.

This is the ancient problem of evil, and it has never had a satisfying answer. The standard moves are familiar. Free will: but children do not exercise free will in being crushed by debris. Greater goods: but no greater good has been articulated that could justify mass infant suffering. Mystery: but invoking mystery is conceding the argument, not winning it. Punishment: a position so morally repugnant that even Pat Robertson's coreligionists usually disown it when he voices it after each new catastrophe.

Faced with these difficulties, most thoughtful believers retreat to a softer position: God did not cause the typhoon, but God is present with the suffering, walks with the grieving, inspires the rescue workers. This is theologically attractive but it concedes the public point. If God does not cause natural disasters, then natural disasters are not acts of God in any meaningful sense. They are acts of physics, exacerbated by acts of humans. Calling them otherwise is just decorative language.

And decorative language has costs. When the Lisbon earthquake of 1755 killed tens of thousands on All Saints' Day, the Catholic establishment's instinct was to read it as divine judgment. Voltaire's outrage at this response helped birth modern Enlightenment thought. We should remember why: assigning catastrophe to divine will short-circuits the inquiry into human and physical causes that might prevent the next one. It is not an accident that the societies most prone to fatalistic religious explanations of disaster are also often those least prepared materially for them.

The Filipino "Bahala na" is more nuanced than a slogan, and I do not want to caricature it. But the cultural pattern it sits within—deep Catholic piety in a nation whose disaster preparedness budget is dwarfed by its remittance economy—should give us pause. Resignation, even beautiful resignation, is not a substitute for seawalls.

If God exists and authored Haiyan, He has questions to answer. If He didn't, we do. Either way, the phrase "act of God" obscures more than it illuminates, and we would do better without it.

We are debating the wrong question. Whether or not it is "constructive" to call disasters acts of God is a question for English departments and ethics seminars. The harder question—the one Haiyan should be forcing on us this week—is why we keep treating the entirely foreseeable as if it were unforeseeable.

Consider what we knew before Haiyan made landfall. We knew the storm was coming. We knew, days in advance, that it would be one of the most powerful tropical cyclones in recorded history. We knew Tacloban sat on a shallow bay perfectly shaped to amplify storm surge. We knew the housing stock in the affected provinces could not withstand sustained winds above a fraction of what was forecast. We knew the Philippine national disaster agency was under-resourced. We knew that climate change is loading more energy into tropical systems. None of this was hidden. None of it required revelation.

And yet, predictably, in the aftermath we are hearing the language of the unimaginable, the unprecedented, the act of God. We heard it after Katrina. We heard it after the 2004 tsunami—where, notoriously, a warning system that existed in the Pacific did not exist in the Indian Ocean, not because the science was lacking but because the political will was. We heard it after the Haiti earthquake, where the death toll reflected building codes more than seismology. The phrase "act of God" is, in practice, less a theological assertion than a political one. It is what governments and developers and insurers say when they would prefer not to discuss why their structures failed.

This is not an argument against religion. People of faith can and do hold their leaders accountable for disaster preparedness; the Catholic Bishops' Conference of the Philippines has spoken eloquently on climate change and poverty. The issue is not whether God acts in the world. The issue is whether human beings get to use God as a synonym for "not my problem."

I would propose a simple rule. Anyone who wishes to call a disaster an act of God in public discourse should first answer three questions: Was it forecast? Were the affected populations adequately warned? Were the structures built to known standards? If the answer to any of those is no, then what you have is not an act of God. It is an act of negligence wearing borrowed robes.
