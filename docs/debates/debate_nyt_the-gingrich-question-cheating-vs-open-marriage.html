Marianne Gingrich's account, if accurate, casts her former husband in a peculiar light: a man who preached family values from the Speaker's chair while asking his wife at home to tolerate his girlfriend. The hypocrisy is the headline. But the underlying question — whether explicit non-monogamy might be a saner arrangement than the cycle of vow, betrayal, discovery, divorce — deserves to be taken seriously, even if Gingrich himself is a poor advocate for it.

There is something to the proposition, in principle. Lifelong sexual exclusivity is a demanding standard, and the empirical record is unkind to it. Survey research has long suggested that something like a quarter of husbands and a smaller but rising share of wives admit to extramarital sex at some point. Divorce rates, while down from their 1980 peak, remain high, and infidelity is one of the most commonly cited proximate causes. A frank ethic of negotiated openness — the kind defended by writers like Dan Savage, and practiced quietly by some long-married couples for decades — does at least put the question on the table rather than under it. Honesty, consent, and the absence of deceit are real goods.

But it does not follow that legitimizing open marriage would strengthen marriage as an institution, and there are reasons to think it would do the opposite. First, the evidence we have on non-monogamous arrangements is thin and skewed toward unusually communicative, educated, and self-selected couples. What works for a particular subculture in Seattle is not obviously scalable to the median American marriage, which is already strained by economic insecurity, child-rearing, and asymmetric expectations between spouses. Sociologists like Andrew Cherlin have argued that American marriage suffers less from too much fidelity than from too little stability and commitment; adding another axis of negotiation is unlikely to help couples who are already struggling to maintain one.

Second, the case for marriage over cohabitation rests heavily on the distinctive promises marriage makes — exclusivity being among the most legible. Strip exclusivity out, and it is not obvious what marriage offers that a robust civil partnership or simply living together does not, apart from tax treatment and inheritance defaults. The institution's pull, especially for children's welfare, comes partly from the public, costly nature of the commitment. Open marriage may be more honest than cheating, but it is also a weaker signal, and weaker signals attract less investment from spouses, kin, and communities.

Third, the Gingrich anecdote itself illustrates the most common pattern. "Open marriage" was proposed not as a mutual project undertaken in good times but as an ultimatum from the straying party once caught. That is how the request typically arrives in real marriages, and it is closer to coercion than to liberation. Consent extracted under threat of abandonment is not the philosophical ideal the polyamory literature describes.

So: was Gingrich onto something? He was onto an old idea — one explored seriously in the 1970s and quietly since — that honesty beats deceit. That much is true. But honest non-monogamy is a niche solution, not a rescue plan for marriage, and certainly not an alibi for his own conduct.

The proposition that open marriage might rescue the institution from infidelity and divorce has a certain superficial logic: if the rule is broken, change the rule. But this logic mistakes the nature of the injury. Infidelity is not primarily about sex; it is about deception and the violation of a promise. Newt Gingrich, according to his ex-wife, did not arrive at the idea of an open marriage out of a philosophical commitment to transparency. He proposed it after he had already strayed—a retroactive renegotiation designed to dodge consequences, not a good-faith attempt at mutual honesty. That is not a model for strengthening marriage; it is a roadmap for eroding the very trust marriage exists to codify.

The evidence available by 2012 does not suggest that opening a relationship fortifies it for most people. Research consistently shows that the vast majority of Americans still expect and desire sexual exclusivity in marriage. While precise numbers on open marriages are elusive because many remain clandestine, studies in the 1970s and 1980s found that openly non-monogamous couples reported higher levels of jealousy and often struggled with inequality—one partner typically had more outside partners or more enthusiasm for the arrangement, breeding resentment. Recent longitudinal work on marital stability identifies trust and commitment to exclusivity as powerful predictors of staying together; removing that scaffolding does not automatically replace it with superior communication skills. Couples who succeed in consensual non-monogamy tend to be an exceptional minority, possessing extraordinary emotional discipline and a prior foundation of security. They are not representative of what the average married pair can sustain.

Moreover, the idea reduces cheating to a problem of forbidden fruit. But deceit can fester perfectly well in open arrangements—over unspoken rivalry, time allocation, or emotional primacy. The promise of monogamy forces a couple to confront directly the hard work of negotiating desire and discontent within the relationship, rather than outsourcing it. That confrontation, though difficult, is what deepens intimacy. Marriage as an institution grew stronger historically not by relaxing obligations but by raising the bar: the shift from arranged economic alliances to companionate love was a demand for greater mutual fulfillment, not less. To now say the remedy for cheating is to abandon the expectation of fidelity altogether would be to hollow out the promise that makes marriage distinct from cohabitation. Cohabiting couples already have the option to define boundaries flexibly; marriage’s comparative allure is precisely its public pledge of a unique, exclusive bond. Diluting that would not make marriage more attractive. It would make it redundant.

The recent allegations by Marianne Gingrich that her ex-husband, Newt Gingrich, requested an "open marriage" to accommodate his ongoing affair have thrust a fringe marital arrangement into the center of a national political debate. The question raised is a provocative one: if we were more accepting of open marriages, would the institution of marriage itself become stronger, less vulnerable to the devastating impacts of cheating, and more appealing than simple cohabitation?

To answer this, we first have to distinguish between consensual non-monogamy and what Newt Gingrich is alleged to have done. True open marriages are built on a foundation of radical honesty, explicit boundaries, and prior mutual consent. According to his ex-wife, Gingrich did not propose an open marriage as a shared lifestyle philosophy; he proposed it as a retroactive permission slip for a betrayal that had already occurred. Using the concept of an open marriage to bypass the consequences of infidelity does not strengthen a relationship; it merely weaponizes a different relationship model to avoid accountability.

However, looking past the immediate political controversy to the broader sociological question, there is a growing discourse—championed by commentators like Dan Savage, who advocates for "monogamish" arrangements—suggesting that strict, lifelong monogamy is an unnatural expectation that sets couples up for failure. From this perspective, allowing for occasional, consensual outside sexual experiences might act as a psychological pressure valve. It could theoretically save families from being torn apart by the discovery of a singular transgression, thereby lowering the divorce rate and keeping households intact.

Yet, treating open marriage as a broad societal remedy fundamentally misdiagnoses what draws the vast majority of people to the institution of marriage in the first place. For most, the primary appeal of marriage is not merely legal partnership, but the promise of total emotional and physical exclusivity—a secure attachment that guards against the profound human vulnerabilities of jealousy and abandonment. If the cultural default shifted to view openness as a standard, expected option, it would not necessarily make marriage more attractive to the general public. Instead, it would likely blur the distinct boundaries that currently separate marriage from long-term cohabitation or casual dating.

Furthermore, maintaining a successful open marriage requires an extraordinary degree of communication, emotional regulation, and time management. It is not a shortcut around the hard work of monogamy; in many ways, it requires considerably more emotional labor. Couples who struggle to communicate effectively about mundane domestic issues are highly unlikely to successfully navigate the complex emotional minefield of extramarital sexual partners without breeding resentment.

Ultimately, the institution of marriage is not weakened by the ideal of monogamy, but by the mismatch of expectations and the failure of partners to uphold the specific vows they freely chose to make. If a couple mutually agrees to an open arrangement from the outset, it can certainly work for them. But as a general prescription for society, open marriage is not a cure for infidelity. As the Gingrich saga vividly illustrates, the deepest wounds in a marriage are rarely caused by sex alone; they are caused by secrecy, deception, and the shattering of trust.

The crucial distinction is not between monogamy and nonmonogamy, but between consent and coercion. A spouse who says, after an affair has begun, “Let us call this an open marriage,” is not proposing openness so much as renegotiating under duress. That is not morally superior to cheating; it may simply be cheating with a press release.

Still, the question should not be dismissed merely because Newt Gingrich is an unappealing messenger. Some couples do consciously choose nonexclusive arrangements, and some appear to manage them with honesty, rules and care. Marriage has never been one fixed institution. It has moved from property alliance to companionate union, from rigid gender roles toward more equal partnership. If two adults freely decide that sexual exclusivity is not central to their commitment, the state has little reason to police that choice.

But it does not follow that “open marriage” would make marriage stronger as a general social institution. For most Americans, exclusivity remains part of what marriage means. It is not a decorative extra; it is tied to security, trust, parenthood, religious conviction and emotional intimacy. Jealousy is not simply a bourgeois prejudice that can be educated away. It is a powerful human emotion, and any marital model that treats it as irrational clutter is likely to produce more pain than liberation.

Nor is the main threat to marriage merely sexual temptation. Couples divorce over money, alcohol, neglect, violence, incompatible ambitions, unequal housework, child-rearing conflicts and loneliness inside the relationship. An agreement permitting outside sex would not solve those problems. In some cases, it might intensify them by giving the more powerful or less emotionally invested partner a way to preserve the benefits of marriage while outsourcing intimacy.

That is why timing and equality matter. An open marriage discussed before vows, or mutually and calmly revisited years later, is one thing. A proposal made by a famous, ambitious husband to a sick or dependent wife after he has already betrayed her is another. The vocabulary of freedom can conceal a very old-fashioned entitlement.

Marriage may become more attractive when it is less hypocritical: when couples can speak honestly about desire, boredom, temptation and changing expectations without immediate shame. But honesty is not the same as permission to do whatever one wants. If openness is to have any ethical standing, it must begin before betrayal, not after it. The institution worth defending is not monogamy at all costs; it is mutual fidelity to the promises a couple actually made.

The question of open marriage as an alternative to infidelity deserves serious consideration rather than reflexive rejection. For millennia, humans have struggled with the tension between lifetime commitment and sexual variety, and pretending this tension doesn't exist hasn't prevented cheating—it's just driven it underground. If a couple genuinely agrees to sexual openness, they've at least exchanged the deception that makes most affairs corrosive.

The open marriage suggestion, if real, wasn't a thoughtful philosophical position but a damage control Hail Mary.

Marriage's strength comes from honest expectations and mutual respect, not from finding loopholes for infidelity. Set aside Newt Gingrich for a moment — he is a poor messenger for almost any idea, and certainly for this one. The underlying question the episode raises is genuinely interesting: would marriage be a stronger, more durable institution if explicit nonmonogamy were on the menu of options couples could choose?

I think the answer is: for some couples, yes, and we should stop pretending otherwise.

Consider the math of contemporary marriage. People marry later, live longer, and expect their spouses to be best friend, co-parent, financial partner, intellectual companion, and exclusive sexual outlet for fifty or sixty years. That is a heavier load than the institution carried in 1900, when life expectancy was shorter and emotional intimacy was not the central promise of the union. Roughly half of marriages end in divorce, and surveys consistently suggest that 20 to 25 percent of married men and 10 to 15 percent of married women report having had at least one affair. The current arrangement is not exactly thriving on the strength of monogamous discipline.

What would change if openness were a culturally available option? First, some couples who currently cheat would instead negotiate. Cheating is corrosive primarily because of the deception, not the sex; honest nonmonogamy removes the lying. Second, some couples who currently divorce after an affair might survive one. The mononormative script says that infidelity ends a marriage; a more flexible script allows repair. Third — and this is the point relevant to cohabitation — marriage might look less like a cage and more like a custom-built structure, which could attract people who currently see the institution as too rigid for adult life.

None of this means open marriage is for everyone, or even for most people. The polyamory literature is candid about how much communication, jealousy management, and time it requires. It is harder than monogamy, not easier. But the existence of a harder option does not weaken the easier one; it simply gives couples a vocabulary for talking about what they actually want.

The truly weakening force in marriage is not the idea of openness. It is the gap between the vows people make and the lives they actually live, papered over with shame and lies. Gingrich, ironically, embodies that gap rather than the alternative to it. But the alternative — honest, negotiated, consensual — is worth taking seriously, even if he is not.

The trouble with the "open marriage saves marriage" thesis is that we already ran this experiment, more or less, and the results were not encouraging.

In 1972, Nena and George O'Neill published "Open Marriage," a runaway bestseller that became a touchstone of the sexual revolution. The book was less radical than its reputation — only one chapter dealt with extramarital sex — but the cultural wave it rode crested through swinging clubs, key parties, and a brief vogue for "responsible" infidelity. By the late 1970s, Nena O'Neill herself was publicly walking back the sexual implications of the book, and the divorce rate, far from stabilizing, continued its climb. The couples who experimented most enthusiastically were not, by and large, the couples who stayed together.

Why? Because the theory of open marriage runs into two stubborn features of human psychology. The first is sexual jealousy, which is not a Victorian invention but a cross-culturally documented emotion with deep evolutionary roots. You can manage it; you cannot reliably abolish it. The second is the asymmetry of desire and opportunity within most marriages. Open arrangements tend, in practice, to benefit whichever partner has more market value at a given moment, and to leave the other feeling diminished. The literature on swinging from the 1970s and on polyamory more recently both report the same pattern: small minorities make it work, larger minorities try and revert, and the dropouts often divorce.

This does not make the people who choose openness foolish. Adults can arrange their lives however they like. But the claim in the prompt is larger: that more openness would make marriage as an institution stronger and more attractive than cohabitation. There is simply no evidence for this. The countries with the most permissive sexual cultures — the Nordics, the Netherlands — have not seen marriage strengthened by liberalization; they have seen cohabitation overtake marriage as the default. Permissiveness about sex within marriage tends to come bundled with permissiveness about the necessity of marriage itself.

As for Gingrich: whatever he proposed to Marianne, he did so after the affair, not before, which makes it less a theory of marriage than an after-the-fact rationalization. The more honest version of his question — "Can I keep both?" — is one many married people have asked themselves. The historical answer, both at the individual and the institutional level, is that you usually cannot, and that pretending otherwise tends to accelerate, not arrest, the unraveling.

There are two questions tangled together here, and they deserve to be pulled apart.

The first is whether Newt Gingrich, the man, was onto something. He wasn't. Proposing open marriage to a spouse you are already cheating on is not a reform; it's a confession dressed up as a manifesto. Whatever the merits of nonmonogamy in the abstract, they are not advanced by a politician who serially traded up wives while moralizing about other people's bedrooms.

The second question is the more interesting one: would marriage as an institution be stronger if openness were a normalized option? Here I am skeptical, but for a reason different from the usual one.

Marriage's distinctive social function — the thing it does that cohabitation does not — is to make a public, costly, and difficult-to-exit commitment. That is what allows spouses to invest in each other, in children, and in joint projects with a long time horizon. The "costliness" of the commitment is not a bug; it's the entire point. It is precisely because marriage is harder to leave than a dating relationship that it can support the kind of trust and specialization it does. Anything that makes marriage softer, more flexible, more like cohabitation, weakens the very feature that distinguishes it.

Open marriage, whatever its other merits, is in this sense a softening. It says: the boundaries of this thing are negotiable. Maybe they should be. But you cannot simultaneously make the boundaries more negotiable and claim that the institution will become more attractive than its less-binding alternatives. If marriage becomes just "cohabitation with extra paperwork and a ring," cohabitation will continue to win, because it is cheaper.

The serious case for openness has to be made on different grounds: that honest nonmonogamy is better than dishonest monogamy for the particular couples who choose it, and that those couples should not be stigmatized. Fine. I agree. But that is an argument for tolerance of variety, not for restructuring the institution. Most people, I suspect, will continue to want what marriage has always promised — a partner who is, in this one important domain, theirs alone — and will continue to find that promise more meaningful, not less, in a culture where it is freely chosen rather than mandated.

So: leave the Gingriches of the world to their arrangements, and leave the rest of us to ours. The institution does not need rescuing by his example.

Marriage has always been an evolving institution, stretching to accommodate the shifting shapes of human desire. From arranged alliances to love matches, from indissoluble bonds to no-fault divorce, it adapts. The question Newt Gingrich's private life thrusts into the open is whether it might now stretch just a little further — toward honestly negotiated nonmonogamy. The answer, quietly practiced by many already, might well be yes. Far from weakening marriage, ethical openness could make it more resilient, more honest, and more attractive to people who otherwise see lifelong sexual exclusivity as a setup for failure.

The central case for open marriage rests on a simple observation: infidelity most often destroys relationships not because of the sex itself, but because of the lies. The deception is the blade, not the act. If couples can agree on boundaries — whether full transparency about outside partners, limits on emotional involvement, or time-sharing protocols — they replace catastrophic breach with manageable difference. They model a commitment that is not about erotic captivity, but about choosing to build a life together, day after day, amid honest recognition that one person cannot meet all of another’s needs.

This is not a plea for the old double standard in new language. The most robust open marriages are not the flung-open doors of a husband’s privilege, but carefully framed agreements about mutual freedom. In these arrangements, jealousy is not eliminated, but it is discussed, negotiated, and often transformed. Couples report that to know your partner has chosen you, even as they explore other connections, can deepen trust in ways that rote fidelity never touches. The presence of other intimacies forces a constant, conscious renewal of the primary bond.

Would this make marriage stronger as an institution? That depends on what we mean by strength. If strength means durability, open marriages face their own risks, but they do not bear the particular fragility of a single infidelity shattering the entire structure. For them, a more expansive, customizable contract might turn cohabitation’s uncertainty into a framework worth choosing. It would not be for everyone — nothing is — but as an option, openly considered and supported, it could strip marriage of its most suffocating expectation, leaving a core of solidarity even more essential and true.

Before we ask whether open marriage could make the institution stronger, we should ask: stronger for whom? The Gingrich story is not a neutral hypothetical dropped into a vacuum. It surfaces inside a deeply gendered history in which men’s infidelity has long been winked at while women’s has been ruinous. The ideal of negotiated openness may sound equitable in theory, but it lands in a world where men still hold disproportionate economic and social power inside marriages. In that world, “open marriage” all too easily becomes a mechanism by which a husband formalizes access to younger women while a wife manages lonely evenings and the emotional labor of pretending she consented freely.

This is not to dismiss the real minority who build ethical, mutually joyful nonmonogamous relationships. But we confuse personal exceptions with social prescription at our peril. The political question here is structural: What would happen if openness became a socially respectable, mass option? The most predictable answer is that it would widen the existing cracks in marriage, not seal them. Women, especially mothers, still perform the bulk of child-rearing and household work, which binds their sexual and social freedom far more than it binds their husbands’. When a man takes a second partner, he gains leisure and novelty; when a woman in an unequal domestic situation does the same, she often risks her economic security and community standing. The link between infidelity and divorce is, moreover, not just about broken rules. It is about shattered trust and the sense that one’s life partner was leading a secretly divided existence.
