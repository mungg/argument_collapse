Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Mie Inouye's case for social endurance lands hard because it names something most theories of solidarity skirt: the brute fact that organizing requires showing up to meetings with people who annoy you, again and again, for years. Against the moralist's hope for ethical transformation and the materialist's faith in interest convergence, she offers a virtue — the capacity to remain in the room — and a disposition — epistemic humility — as the practical conditions of any solidarity worth the name. I think she's largely right about the diagnosis. What I want to press on is whether endurance can really be cultivated as a disposition at all, or whether her own Mormon example reveals that it has to be produced by institutions thick enough to make leaving costly.

Consider what made Inouye's church attendance endure across her dislike of half the room. It wasn't an act of will. She was a child whose family's sense of belonging, social network, and cosmology depended on the building she walked into each Sunday. Exit meant losing not just a community but kin, an eschatological future, and the script by which her grandparents had reassembled a life after the camps. The same is true, in different registers, of Bob Lee's Uptown meeting, which she rightly treats as the documentary heart of her essay. The white Appalachian migrants Lee organized were not exercising endurance; they were cornered. Their housing was being condemned, their kids were being beaten by cops, and the Young Patriots had built the only structure in the neighborhood that took their grievances seriously. Lee could lay hands on a sixteen-year-old's hair because the kid had nowhere else to go that took him seriously as a political subject. Endurance, in both cases, was an output of dense institutional and material entanglement, not an input one brings to organizing as a personal virtue.

This matters because the social world Inouye actually addresses — her students, her PMC fundraiser, the post-2020 left — is one in which exit is structurally easy and almost never punished. The neoliberal subject she rightly identifies as conflict-averse is also a subject for whom the meeting is one option among many, competing with the group chat, the side project, the move to another city for a better job. Telling such a subject to cultivate endurance is a bit like telling a person with no savings to develop patience with the market. The disposition is downstream of conditions that don't obtain. This is why DuFord's distinction between constructive and destructive conflict, which Inouye sensibly worries about, may matter less than the prior question: under what circumstances does anyone bother to fight rather than ghost?

The answer the labor tradition gives — and which Inouye gestures at without quite embracing — is interdependence. People stay in the room when their material survival, or their dignity, or their kids' future, is bound up with what happens there. Unions historically generated endurance not because workers cultivated comradely virtue but because the union hall was where you got the job, the strike fund, the funeral collection, the wedding hall, the literacy class. Tenant unions today work in something like the same register: you don't leave the building meeting because you live in the building. Mutual aid networks that survive past their pandemic origins do so because they distribute something — diapers, groceries, rides — that people genuinely need. The Rainbow Coalition was, among other things, a coordinated provision of breakfast, medical clinics, and legal defense. The endurance Inouye admires was scaffolded by an infrastructure of need-meeting.

This is, I think, what Henry was getting at when he chastened her at the fundraiser. His point isn't that material interest is the most authentic motive for organizing — Inouye is right that motives are plural and opaque — but that organizations whose members are not bound by material stake tend to dissolve when conflict gets sharp, precisely because there is no cost to leaving. Her own honest answer ("I organize because I need to organize, I just need to have meetings to go to") describes a relation to organizing that the meeting cannot, by itself, sustain politically. It's a hobbyist's relation, and hobbyists drift.

None of this undercuts Inouye's deeper claim. Epistemic humility, the willingness to be transformed, the refusal of deference's evasion of conflict — these are necessary. But they are necessary conditions of solidarity, not sufficient ones, and they cannot be willed into existence by individual practitioners. They are produced by being entangled with other people in ways one cannot easily walk away from. The lesson of the Mormon Church, read this way, is not that leftists should imitate its endurance as a disposition but that they should build secular institutions thick enough to make endurance the path of least resistance. That means treating organizing less as an ethics and more as the painstaking construction of structures of interdependence — strike funds, tenant associations, party formations, schools — within which the dispositions Inouye prizes can take root and conflict can become productive rather than merely exhausting. Otherwise the meeting empties, and the most epistemically humble person in the room is the last one to leave.

Mie Inouye’s meditation on solidarity arrives at a moment when many on the left are nursing wounds from the rapid implosion of the 2020 uprisings. Her insistence that we need “social endurance”—the hard, unglamorous practice of showing up across difference, even when we dislike each other—feels like a necessary corrective to the atomized, conflict-averse habits neoliberalism fosters. The image of Bob Lee lifting a hesitant white woman to her feet and demanding she speak is a powerful rebuke to a politics that would let privilege silence itself as a form of deference. Yet for all its phenomenological richness, Inouye’s account risks substituting a psychological virtue for a political one, leaving solidarity suspended in a hall of mirrors of interpersonal transformation without a clear account of what, beyond the meeting itself, makes endurance worth the effort.

The trouble begins with Inouye’s treatment of the Young Patriots scene. She reads Lee’s organizing as a model of transformative encounter, and rightly so. But what gives that scene its urgency is not simply that Lee compels a young mother to recount her brother’s stabbing—it is that the meeting is called for a concrete purpose: to disrupt an upcoming Model Cities hearing that the Patriots believed excluded community input. Everyone in that room was gathered not just to learn from one another’s pain, but to prepare for an action that would confront power. The conflict and the listening were in service of a shared, if contested, strategic goal. Strip away that horizon and Lee’s interventions risk becoming a kind of performance of consciousness-raising, moving but ultimately untethered from the organizing that turns grievance into demand. Endurance, on its own, is a capacity; it becomes solidarity only when directed toward something beyond the room.

Inouye is aware of this, gesturing toward “ideology”—a shared vision of a just society—as a possible basis for solidarity. But ideology in her hands remains curiously unspecified, more a vague affective alignment than a set of political commitments capable of generating strategy and discipline. This is where her otherwise compelling critique of deference politics loses its sharpest edge. The problem with deference politics, she argues, is that it treats people as fully determined by social location and avoids conflict. Her proposed alternative—epistemic humility—is indeed more honest. But epistemic humility, taken alone, can slide into a kind of radical uncertainty that paralyzes collective action. If I hold my beliefs perpetually open to challenge, on what basis do I commit to a campaign? At what point does the refusal to assert a perspective become an abdication of the responsibilities of political judgment? Solidarity needs not just the humility to learn, but the conviction to act together, and that conviction must be forged around something concrete: a demand, a tactic, an analysis of who is to blame and what can be done about it.

The Mormon church, for all its limitations, illustrates this point. Inouye’s family endured its demands—the three-hour meetings, the social friction—because the church offered a thick cosmology and a dense web of material and spiritual sanctions. Leaving was costly. A democratic solidarity organization cannot and should not replicate such coercive bonds. But that means endurance in a political context must be anchored in something equally compelling: a credible promise that the collective effort can actually shift the terrain of struggle. Bob Lee’s Uptown meeting was compelling not because people liked each other, but because they believed they might win something—some say over the Model Cities funds, some measure of power against the police and the slumlords. The endurance was a means, not an end, and it was sustained by the expectation of efficacy.

Inouye’s own family story points toward this missing dimension. She wonders what might have happened if her interned ancestors had encountered a solidarity “not so closely tethered to the American Dream.” The answer, I suspect, lies less in cultivating the capacity to sit with annoyance and more in constructing organizations that could make the case that the state that interned them was the same state that underfunded their health care, broke their unions, and policed their neighborhoods—and that building power across racial lines could compel that state to behave differently. My uncle Gerald’s premature death from COVID was indeed a soft echo of George Floyd’s—a death structured by a racialized capitalism that devalues care and privatizes risk. But to see that connection is not enough; the recognition must be channeled into collective demands on the institutions that produce such precarity, from workplace safety rules to public investment in health infrastructure. Without that movement from awareness to organization, endurance can devolve into a kind of political therapy, an endless processing of trauma that leaves the structures producing it untouched.

This is not to dismiss the value of the conflicts Inouye celebrates. Democratic organizations do need internal contestation to avoid reproducing domination, and the neoliberal aversion to disagreement is a real obstacle. But as Inouye acknowledges, not all conflict is productive. The difference between a fight that transforms and one that atomizes often hinges on whether the people in the room share a commitment to winning something together. When that shared commitment is absent, the very discomfort Inouye valorizes can accelerate exit rather than build staying power. I have seen this in tenant unions: a group of neighbors who loathe one another’s personal styles will hold together for months if they believe that withholding rent can force a landlord to make repairs. Take away the specific campaign and the same group collapses into petty feuds. The endurance Inouye calls for is not a pre-political soil that can be tilled in isolation; it grows most reliably in the soil of collective struggle aimed at common objectives.

In the end, the left does need social endurance—but as a companion to, not a replacement for, strategic clarity. What makes solidarity “now” possible is not just the willingness to be annoyed and transformed by comrades we might call stupid motherfuckers, but the recognition that we are bound together by a wager: that acting together, with all the friction it entails, can wrest concessions from power and prefigure a different world. Without that wager, the room becomes a place we merely endure, not one we fight to change—and eventually, however open-minded we are, we stop showing up.

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” offers a vital, penetrating diagnosis of the fragile state of contemporary left organizing. By zeroing in on “deference politics”—the increasingly common practice wherein relatively privileged individuals reflexively defer to those perceived as more marginalized—Inouye captures a dynamic that has paralyzed coalition-building. As she rightly observes, deference is often less about genuine solidarity than it is about conflict avoidance. It is a mechanism through which the professional-managerial class (PMC) can insulate itself from accusations of privilege while simultaneously dodging the messy, uncomfortable work of true political engagement. In place of this brittle dynamic, Inouye proposes “social endurance”: the capacity to stay in the room, endure friction, and allow oneself to be transformed by the collective struggle.

Inouye’s intervention is brilliant, particularly in her willingness to rescue the concept of conflict from the dustbin of toxic behaviors. She correctly identifies that neoliberalism atomizes us not merely by making us selfish, but by encouraging us to retreat from friction. Yet, while Inouye’s prescription of social endurance and epistemic humility is exactly the right moral and psychological framework for solidarity, her essay leaves a crucial structural question unanswered. She draws her model of social endurance from her upbringing in the Mormon Church, an institution defined by its totalizing worldview and immense material resources. In doing so, she inadvertently highlights the exact missing ingredient in voluntary, democratic left organizations: a structural mechanism that makes exit costlier than staying.

To understand the limits of social endurance as a purely voluntary ethos, we must look closely at the institution that taught Inouye this virtue. The Mormon Church is highly effective at forging social endurance among disparate people, but it does not achieve this through epistemic humility or a shared commitment to democratic transformation. It achieves it through a combination of profound theological stakes—the promise of eternal family and the threat of spiritual ruin—and a massive, highly organized network of material support. For Inouye’s family in the aftermath of internment, the church provided not just a spiritual home, but a lifeline of social and professional integration that allowed them to survive the brutality of racial capitalism.

When you are bound to an institution by the very survival of your family, or by the belief in your eternal soul, you will sit through three hours of agonizingly dull or politically infuriating Sunday meetings. You will endure the presence of people you do not like, and you will learn to cooperate with them. The barriers to exiting the Mormon Church are extraordinarily high, encompassing the potential loss of one’s family, community, and safety net.

By contrast, the barriers to exiting a neighborhood abolitionist working group or a local chapter of the Democratic Socialists of America are practically nonexistent. Modern left organizing spaces are overwhelmingly voluntary associations. When the PMC activist encounters “unrealistic conflict” or simply grows tired of the endless ideological purity tests, they do not have to stay in the room. They can simply close out of the Zoom meeting, turn off their phone, and retreat to the comforts of their class position. Without a structural binding agent, social endurance remains a personal virtue—a hobby for the politically committed—rather than a reliable basis for a mass movement.

This brings us back to Henry, the working-class organizer who confronts Inouye at the beginning of her essay. Henry organizes, he explains, because of “the material changes my family needs to see to our lives in order to survive.” Inouye feels chastened by this, admitting that she organizes largely for “the meetings” and the long-tail hope of generating revolutionary possibilities. She then attempts to bridge the gap between Henry’s materialist motivations and her own ideological ones by positing that a shared vision of a just society—and the endurance to fight for it together—might be enough to bind them.

But Henry is right, and his motivation holds the key to the very endurance Inouye seeks. The reason Henry will not leave the room when the meeting gets contentious is that he cannot afford to. His family’s survival depends on winning the struggle. The PMC activist, however, can afford to leave. If solidarity is to transcend the transactional fragility of deference politics, it cannot rely merely on the PMC cultivating a tolerance for discomfort. It must involve tying the material fate of the PMC to the material fate of the working class.

Fred Moten’s quote, which Inouye highlights, points precisely in this direction: “I don’t need your help. I just need you to recognize that this shit is killing you, too, however much more softly.” The genius of the original Rainbow Coalition was not just that Bob Lee physically touched people and forced them to articulate their desires, though that organizing craft is essential. The genius was that the Black Panthers, the Young Lords, and the Young Patriots recognized a shared material enemy in the Chicago police, the slumlords, and the city’s political machine. They did not defer to one another out of a sense of liberal guilt; they fought alongside one another because their literal survival in their respective neighborhoods depended on a unified front against the state.

If we want to build the kind of social endurance Inouye experienced in the Mormon Church within a democratic, secular left, we must build institutions that provide immediate, tangible material benefits and exact real costs for abandonment. Historically, the labor union has been the primary vehicle for this kind of solidarity under capitalism. A union hall is one of the few places in American life where a progressive, college-educated barista might be forced into a room with a socially conservative, working-class shift leader. They do not have to like each other. They do not have to defer to one another’s lived experiences. But if they want a living wage and health insurance, they have to endure one another. The binding agent is the collective bargaining agreement. If one walks away, they both lose their leverage. The friction between them is unavoidable, but it is rendered productive by the shared necessity of defeating the boss.

Beyond the workplace, building institutional stickiness means creating mutual aid networks, tenant unions, and cooperative housing structures that actually sustain people’s lives. If a political organization is paying into a strike fund that keeps your electricity on, or if a tenant union is the only thing standing between your family and eviction, you will endure a great deal of interpersonal conflict to keep that organization intact. You will learn to bite your tongue when a comrade says something clumsy, and you will learn to push back constructively when a strategy is failing. Epistemic humility ceases to be an academic exercise in standpoint theory and becomes a practical necessity for keeping the heat on.

Inouye is absolutely correct that the left must abandon the paralyzing theater of deference politics. Treating individuals as perfectly transparent avatars of their demographic categories is a recipe for stagnation. Her call to embrace conflict as a site of mutual transformation is one of the most vital lessons organizers can learn today. But we cannot expect neoliberal subjects, trained their entire lives to curate their experiences and avoid discomfort, to suddenly choose social endurance purely out of ideological commitment.

To heed Inouye’s call for "Solidarity Now," we must fuse her insight about social endurance with Henry’s urgent mandate for material survival. We must build political homes that are as vital to our survival as the Mormon Church was to her family’s, but fundamentally rooted in democratic equality rather than top-down hierarchy. Only when our organizations become the literal mechanisms of our shared survival will we find the strength to stay in the room when the hour grows late and the arguments grow fierce. Only then will we look across the table at someone we profoundly disagree with, recognize that our lives depend on them, and get down to the hard work of building a new world together.

Mie Inouye’s essay makes a powerful case for one of the least glamorous virtues in politics: showing up. Against a culture that treats disagreement as contamination and exit as self-care, she reminds us that durable collective action requires the ability to remain in relationship with people who annoy, misunderstand, or even wound us. This is especially important on a left that often imagines solidarity either as perfect moral alignment or as the automatic expression of shared material interest. Neither fantasy survives much contact with actual organizing. People enter movements for mixed reasons. They misunderstand their own interests. They bring with them the very hierarchies they hope to abolish. If they cannot stay in the room long enough to be changed, no amount of correct analysis will save them.

Still, I worry that “social endurance” names a necessary condition of solidarity but not yet a sufficient one. Endurance can be radical, as Inouye argues, when it keeps us available to transformation. But endurance can also be a discipline of domination. Many people—especially women, people of color, queer and trans people, workers, immigrants, and the poor—have been trained all their lives to endure: to sit through humiliating meetings, to keep families and workplaces functioning, to absorb insult for the sake of peace, to translate themselves for the benefit of others, to return again and again to institutions that do not love them back. The political question is therefore not only how to cultivate endurance where neoliberal exit has weakened it. It is also how to distinguish emancipatory endurance from the old demand that the injured be patient.

That distinction cannot be made at the level of temperament alone. It depends on power. A white professional learning to remain in a multiracial tenants’ union after being criticized is practicing one kind of endurance. A Black tenant being asked to remain in the same union while white members repeatedly dismiss police harassment as a “secondary issue” is practicing another. Both may need to stay; both may need to be changed. But the costs are not symmetrical, and a theory of solidarity must be able to say so without collapsing into the deference politics Inouye rightly criticizes.

This is where I would put more emphasis on organization than on sociality. Meetings do not transform us simply because we attend them. Conflict does not educate simply because it occurs. The same room can produce courage, resentment, conformity, scapegoating, or burnout depending on its rules, leadership, culture, and connection to action. Endurance becomes politically generative when it is attached to practices that make conflict answerable to a common project: democratic decision-making, clear expectations, political education, leadership development, accountability procedures, and campaigns with stakes beyond the meeting itself. Without these, “staying in the room” can become a fetish of process, a way to mistake proximity for power.

Inouye’s own examples suggest as much. Bob Lee’s organizing in Chicago was not merely an exercise in presence, touch, or interpersonal confrontation. It was embedded in a disciplined political organization with an analysis, a program, and a strategy. Lee did not ask “What do you want?” as a therapeutic prompt. He asked it in the context of a campaign over housing, policing, poverty, and community control. He was eliciting needs so they could be organized into demands and action. That is why the question had force. In many contemporary spaces, by contrast, “What do you want?” floats free of any mechanism for collective power. People are invited to disclose pain, narrate identity, and perform vulnerability, but not necessarily to build the capacity to win anything. Under those conditions, endurance becomes exhausting because nothing changes except the emotional weather in the room.

The same point applies to the summer of 2020. Inouye is right that millions of people felt, however inchoately, that George Floyd’s murder was connected to unemployment, sickness, isolation, state abandonment, and the violence of everyday life under racial capitalism. She is also right that those connections were often left unorganized, displaced into allyship or deference. But the failure was not simply that people lacked the social endurance to fight through disagreement. It was also that the United States had too few mass organizations capable of receiving that insurgent energy and converting it into durable power. Protesters encountered nonprofits, ad hoc formations, social media influencers, local abolitionist groups, unions of uneven strength, electoral campaigns, and Democratic officials eager to metabolize revolt into reform. Some places produced serious organizing. Many did not. The weakness was infrastructural.

This matters because the people most in need of solidarity cannot be asked to wait for the privileged to become transformed through repeated exposure. The clock of political education runs slower than the clock of eviction, deportation, incarceration, climate disaster, medical debt, and police violence. Of course people change through struggle; indeed, they often change only through struggle. But organizations must be capable of acting before everyone has reached clarity about why they are there. A union does not wait for every member to develop a perfect account of racial capitalism before filing a grievance or striking. A tenants’ association does not postpone a rent campaign until every participant has resolved their inherited prejudices. Sometimes the discipline of a common fight precedes the moral and epistemic transformation Inouye seeks.

This is why material interest should not be demoted too quickly. Inouye is persuasive that shared interests do not appear automatically, transparently, or equally across social divisions. But material struggle remains one of the best schools for the kind of solidarity she describes. When workers strike, when tenants withhold rent, when parents fight school closures, when patients demand public health resources, people discover not only what they have in common but also what they do not. They learn who can take risks, who cannot, who is trusted, who is ignored, whose suffering has been normalized, whose anger is treated as a problem. Material campaigns force these questions because they require decisions: What are we demanding? Who is the target? What risks will we take? Who bears them? What counts as a win? In that sense, material interest is not an alternative to political education. It is one of its conditions.

Inouye’s critique of deference politics is especially welcome because deference often masquerades as humility while evading responsibility. To say “I will follow the lead of the most affected” can mean: I recognize that my perspective is partial and that others know things I do not. But it can also mean: I would rather not think, argue, or be accountable for the consequences of my choices. The first attitude is essential; the second is corrosive. Movements need people who can listen without disappearing, speak without dominating, and change their minds without outsourcing judgment. “Epistemic humility” captures part of this ethic.

Yet humility, too, has class inflections. Professional-managerial spaces often reward elaborate performances of self-interrogation while leaving underlying relations untouched. People become fluent in naming their positionality but less capable of taking direction, doing unglamorous work, accepting discipline, or losing a vote. In such settings, humility can become another form of self-display. The antidote is not less reflection, but reflection subordinated to collective work. The test of whether I have learned from others is not whether I can redescribe myself more sensitively. It is whether I act differently when the organization needs canvassers, strike support, childcare, data entry, jail support, meeting facilitation, or money.

I also want to complicate the analogy to religious endurance. Inouye is admirably clear-eyed about Mormonism’s exclusions and hierarchies, and she does not romanticize the church. But the example reveals a hard truth: institutions that reliably produce social endurance often do so by making exit costly, by sacralizing obligation, and by embedding people in thick webs of dependency and surveillance. Modern left organizations usually cannot and should not replicate those mechanisms. They cannot promise eternal family, salvation, or a total world. They cannot command the same authority over marriage, childhood, ritual, and daily life. So the challenge is sharper than “how do we get people to keep showing up?” It is: how do democratic movements create enough obligation to sustain sacrifice without reproducing coercive belonging?

Part of the answer is victory. People endure more when endurance is rewarded not only with moral meaning but with concrete evidence that collective action can alter their lives. The labor movement, at its best, has understood this. A union meeting may be boring, factional, and full of people one would not choose as friends. But if the meeting is connected to a contract campaign that raises wages, protects a coworker, or changes staffing ratios, people learn that attendance matters. Solidarity is then neither sentiment nor mere stamina. It is a practice confirmed by power.

Another part of the answer is permission to leave. This may seem contrary to Inouye’s emphasis, but democratic endurance requires boundaries. Some conflicts are transformative; others are degrading. Some organizations are imperfect vehicles for collective struggle; others are abusive, unserious, or captured by cliques. If we tell people that leaving is always neoliberal fragility, we risk trapping them in miniature versions of the institutions we oppose. The right norm is not “always stay” but “do not leave merely because you are uncomfortable, criticized, outvoted, or bored.” That is a demanding standard. It asks us to examine whether our exit protects our conscience or merely our ego. But it also preserves the possibility that departure can be an act of judgment rather than consumer preference.

The deepest insight in Inouye’s essay is that solidarity is not found; it is made. It is made among people who do not begin with identical interests, identical wounds, or identical dreams. It is made through campaigns, arguments, meals, votes, disappointments, study, repetition, and the strange intimacy of shared tasks. We should not expect unity, and we should be suspicious of organizations that demand it too quickly. But neither should we settle for togetherness as such. The point of staying in the room is to become capable of acting outside it.

So yes: solidarity now requires endurance. But endurance must be organized, bounded, and aimed at power. It must ask more of the privileged than patience and more of the oppressed than continued forbearance. It must be tied to material struggles urgent enough to clarify disagreement and institutions sturdy enough to survive it. If we can build those, then the meeting may become what Inouye hopes it can be: not a refuge from politics, not a performance of virtue, but a place where people learn how to fight for one another without pretending they are the same.

Inouye's essay does more than defend the value of showing up; it reframes what showing up actually requires. Her central claim—that social endurance, the capacity to remain present through friction and discomfort, constitutes a form of solidarity radical enough to transform participants—rests on a premise about human plasticity. We cannot know in advance how a meeting will change us, and this uncertainty is not a weakness of her argument but its foundation. I want to push this insight further by asking what institutional conditions might sustain the kind of endurance she describes, and whether her focus on individual transformation risks understating the structural obstacles that make long-term solidarity so difficult to maintain.

The strength of Inouye's analysis lies in her refusal to reduce solidarity to shared interests or moral commitments alone. She is right that interest-convergence arguments, however useful, cannot fully account for why people stay engaged when material incentives are ambiguous or absent. She is also right to be suspicious of deference politics, which treats the perspectives of privileged participants as irrelevant before the work of organizing even begins. But there is a tension in her account between the individual-level transformation she advocates and the structural conditions that enable people to remain in rooms with each other long enough to be transformed. Social endurance is not simply a disposition; it is cultivated through practices, institutions, and material arrangements that make continued participation feasible.

Consider what it actually takes to show up week after week to meetings with people whose politics you find troubling. Beyond the willingness to endure discomfort, participants need childcare, flexible work schedules, physical safety, and some baseline of trust that their contributions will be heard. These are not sentimental demands; they are structural requirements without which even the most committed organizers will eventually stop attending. Inouye's Mormon analogy is revealing precisely because the church provided material infrastructure—physical spaces, predictable rhythms, shared meals—that made endurance a collective rather than purely individual achievement. The three-hour Sunday meetings she describes were not merely exercises in tolerance; they were embedded in a social architecture designed to hold people together.

This suggests that the left's difficulty in building durable coalitions cannot be attributed solely to insufficient willingness to show up. The problem is also that contemporary movements often lack the institutional scaffolding that would allow endurance to develop. Unions once provided this scaffolding; so did churches, neighborhood associations, and mutual aid societies. Today, the institutions capable of sustaining long-term collective engagement have been hollowed out by decades of neoliberal restructuring, leaving individuals to improvise their own solidarity practices without the material supports that might make those practices sustainable.

Inouye's treatment of Táíwò's critique of deference politics is nuanced, and her appeal to epistemic humility as a practical alternative is intriguing. But epistemic humility, as she describes it, requires a capacity for self-reflection that is itself unequally distributed and unequally cultivated. People who have been subjected to constant scrutiny of their perspectives—who are constantly asked to explain their experiences, justify their views, and account for their reactions—may develop sophisticated self-understanding, but they may also be exhausted by the labor of constant self-analysis. Asking everyone to hold their beliefs open to reformulation is a demanding prescription, and it lands differently on people who already bear the weight of others' skepticism about their competence and credibility.

There is also a question about what epistemic humility is supposed to prevent. Inouye suggests that it protects against domination and exclusion within solidarity organizations, but domination and exclusion are structural phenomena, not merely attitudinal ones. An organization can be composed entirely of epistemically humble individuals and still reproduce racialized hierarchies if its decision-making processes, resource allocations, and leadership structures remain unchanged. The kind of transformation Inouye envisions—in which participants become less racist, less classist, more flexible through sustained contact—requires not only individual openness but also institutional redesign.

DuFord's argument that solidarity organizations must be premised on disunity is compatible with Inouye's emphasis on endurance, but it raises a question she does not fully address: how do we distinguish between conflicts that are generative and those that are merely exhausting? Her answer seems to be that we cannot know in advance, which is itself a kind of epistemic humility. But this answer risks becoming a counsel of despair. If every conflict is potentially transformative and potentially destructive, and if we cannot tell them apart until after the fact, then organizers have little guidance for deciding when to intervene and when to let tensions run their course.

Perhaps what is needed is not a categorical distinction between constructive and destructive conflict but a set of practices that increase the likelihood of the former. Regular facilitation, explicit norms for disagreement, rotation of leadership roles, and mechanisms for accountability can create conditions in which conflict is more likely to produce learning rather than withdrawal. These are unglamorous institutional details, and they do not guarantee that organizing will transform participants in the ways Inouye hopes. But they are the kind of infrastructure that might make social endurance more than a private virtue.

Inouye ends her essay with a question that echoes Moten: she organizes because the same structures that killed George Floyd killed her uncle, in different ways and with different degrees of agency. This framing is powerful precisely because it refuses to separate personal stakes from political analysis. But it also raises a question her essay does not fully answer: what kinds of organizations could hold together people whose material stakes are radically different, whose experiences of harm are differently distributed, and whose willingness to acknowledge shared vulnerability is variable? The answer cannot be purely structural, but it cannot be purely dispositional either. It must be both, which means that the work of building solidarity is not only the work of cultivating individual endurance but also the work of designing institutions that make endurance possible.

Endurance Is Not Enough

Mie Inouye's essay offers something rare in current debates about solidarity: a portrait of how organizing actually feels, with all its irritation, ambivalence, and surprise. The story of Henry and the name tag captures something true about the texture of political life, and the Bob Lee footage from American Revolution 2 has long deserved more attention than it gets. But for all the essay's generosity, I worry that "social endurance" — the capacity to keep showing up when you don't like the other people in the room — names a precondition of solidarity rather than its substance, and that elevating it to the central category risks turning a craft into a virtue.

Consider what made the Rainbow Coalition work. It was not endurance per se. It was a concrete organizing program oriented to material targets: the Model Cities meeting, police brutality, housing in Uptown, the Cook County Jail. Bob Lee did not walk into the Young Patriots' meeting hoping that white Appalachians would learn to tolerate his presence over time. He walked in with a theory of who held power in Chicago, what could be wrested from them, and how a multiracial bloc might do the wresting. Endurance followed from that program — people kept showing up because there were meetings worth showing up to, fights worth winning, and victories whose absence would be intolerable. Take away the program and you do not get endurance. You get attrition.

This is the deeper lesson of Frances Fox Piven and Richard Cloward, whom Inouye cites approvingly but I think too quickly. Piven and Cloward's argument is not just that protest opportunities are socially structured; it is that organizers consistently mistake the maintenance of organization for the production of political power, and that this confusion damages movements. Their critics in the organizing tradition (Jane McAlevey most prominently) have pushed back, but even McAlevey would insist that what holds people together through inevitable conflict is the credible prospect of winning something they need. "I just need meetings to go to" — Inouye's honest answer to Henry — is precisely what Piven and Cloward warned against: organization as its own end.

I do not think Inouye disagrees, exactly. She writes that "the point of organizing… is to prepare to make the most of socially structured opportunities for protest." But the essay's center of gravity drifts away from this insight. By the end, what we are left with is a disposition — epistemic humility, openness to transformation, the willingness to bump up against strangers — rather than a strategy. The Mormon Church gets credited with teaching her endurance, but Mormonism cultivates endurance precisely because it has a thick, demanding program: tithing, missionary service, temple recommends, the eternal family. Strip the program and the meetings become unbearable. This is why people leave the church, or, more often, never join.

What would it mean to take this seriously? It would mean treating "endurance" not as an attitude we can recommend to comrades but as a byproduct of organizational forms that have something at stake. The labor movement at its strongest knew this: dues, picket discipline, the strike fund, the contract. Tenant unions know it. The best community organizing traditions — IAF, PICO, the early ACORN — know it. They build endurance through escalating commitment to concrete fights, not through exhortation to remain in the room. Jodi Dean's gloss in the sidebar ("What if we emphasize not social but political endurance?") gets closer to what I think is needed: endurance with a content, organized around a program, disciplined by accountability to a base.

Here I want to push on the essay's reading of Jared Clemons. Inouye accepts Clemons's claim that the white PMC is now the most accessible base for multiracial racial-justice work, and pivots toward "working with the contradictions of the PMC." Fine — every coalition has to start with the bodies it can muster. But this move risks naturalizing a defeat. The reason multiracial working-class coalitions of the Poor People's Campaign variety are hard to assemble today is not metaphysical; it is the historical destruction of the institutions (industrial unions, Black freedom organizations rooted in workplaces and neighborhoods, ethnic mutual aid societies) that once made them possible. The question is not whether the PMC can be made into a stable solidarity partner through humility practices, but whether the painstaking, decades-long work of rebuilding mass institutions among the working class — including its enormous nonwhite majority in many regions — is being done. UNITE HERE in Las Vegas, the CTU in Chicago, the Amazon Labor Union, the resurgent UAW under Shawn Fain: these point to a different answer than Inouye's essay implies.

I also want to register a worry about the essay's treatment of deference politics. Inouye is right that deference can serve as a tool for evading conflict, and right that it produces fragile coalitions. But "deference politics," as deployed in current debates, is often a strawman or, more precisely, a real but narrow phenomenon — the rhetorical habits of a thin stratum of nonprofit and academic spaces — being asked to do explanatory work for the broader failures of post-2020 organizing. Those failures had structural causes: the end of pandemic emergency benefits, the demobilization of the streets once Biden won, the absence of institutional vehicles to channel the energy of June 2020 into durable campaigns, the relentless counter-mobilization of police unions and Republican legislatures. To name "deference" as the chief obstacle is to overstate the autonomy of discourse.

What the essay does superbly is restore the bodily, the awkward, the affective dimensions of organizing to a conversation that had drifted into abstractions. Lee's hands on Roger's hair, on the middle-aged man's shoulders, on the young mother's hands — these are not metaphors. They are what organizing is, and any theory of solidarity that cannot account for them is missing something. The story of Uncle Gerald is similarly powerful: the recognition that the same system that killed George Floyd killed him too, "however more softly and however more self-inflicted," is a genuine extension of Fred Moten's challenge.

But this recognition has to be metabolized into something. The danger in the essay's framing is that "showing up" becomes its own reward — that we cultivate endurance for a movement that never decides what it is enduring for. The Mormon Church endures because it has answers, however wrong, to ultimate questions. A democratic left has to have answers, too: about what we are building, whom we are fighting, how we win. Endurance without that content is just stoicism, and stoicism is not a politics.

So I would put the point this way. Inouye is right that solidarity requires the willingness to be transformed, right that conflict within organizations can be productive, right that epistemic humility is preferable to deference. But these are conditions, not foundations. The foundation has to be a credible theory of how a bloc of differently situated people can take and hold power against the actually existing organization of American capitalism. Without that, endurance is the patience of the powerless, and we have had enough of it.

The Thick and the Thin

There is a curious doubleness running through Mie Inouye's essay. On one side stands the Mormon ward of her childhood: three hours on Sunday, scripture study at dawn, mutual aid woven into a dense network of practices, doctrines, and obligations. On the other side stands the democratic solidarity organization she hopes we can build — open, conflictual, exit-friendly, oriented to social transformation rather than eternal family. The essay wants the first to teach the second something about endurance without inheriting its hierarchies or its exclusions. I am sympathetic to this hope, but I think it understates how much of Mormonism's endurance-producing power depends precisely on the features the essay wants to discard.

Inouye writes that "it should be easier to leave a democratic solidarity organization than it was for me to leave Mormonism." Of course it should. But the social endurance she rightly admires in her Mormon kin is not separable from the difficulty of leaving. Endurance is, in significant part, the felt cost of exit. The ward held her because exit threatened her family, her cosmology, her sense of selfhood, her standing in the only community she had. Take those costs away and the meetings become unbearable in the way that meetings of any voluntary association become unbearable: people drift, attendance dwindles, the same five people end up doing everything. This is the iron law not of oligarchy but of low-stakes association.

Robert Putnam noticed this twenty-three years ago in Bowling Alone, and his diagnosis has only grown more relevant. The "thick" institutions of mid-century American civic life — unions, churches, fraternal orders, parent-teacher associations — produced endurance because they bundled material benefits, moral identity, social belonging, and political voice into a single package. You went to the union hall because that is where the job referrals happened, where your friends were, where the picnic was held, where you knew whose son had died in the war. The thinning out of these institutions has left us with a civic landscape of low-cost, low-commitment, low-endurance associations, exactly the kind that fail to hold people through real conflict.

What Inouye calls "social endurance" is, on this reading, a property of thick institutions, and the question is whether the contemporary left can build any. The historical track record is not encouraging. The Communist Party USA in its 1930s heyday came closest, with its thick web of unions, language federations, summer camps, schools, and newspapers — and even there, endurance was sustained as much by Stalinist discipline and the felt urgency of the depression and antifascism as by anything we would now want to emulate. The civil rights movement at its peak built thick endurance in the Black church and through SNCC's freedom schools and SCLC's local affiliates. DSA, the most ambitious recent attempt at a multi-tendency socialist organization, has tens of thousands of members but, by most accounts, a thin associational life: the chapter meeting is the institution, and that's about it.

So when Inouye praises endurance as something we should "cultivate," I want to ask: cultivate how, and on what soil? The essay's answer seems to be: through norms and practices that hold people in the room. But norms and practices are downstream of institutions. They have to be embedded in something with texture — shared meals, shared work, shared crises, shared rituals — or they remain exhortations.

This is where I think the Mormon comparison is more useful than the essay quite admits. The cookie Inouye's father received at his first church social was not a metaphor. It was an actual sugar cookie, baked by an actual woman, served at an actual potluck, in an actual building owned by an actual institution that had real estate and a budget and a hierarchy of callings that obligated members to bake cookies for new families. The cookie was solidarity infrastructure. The reason it could integrate a small Japanese boy into a white Utah town in the 1950s — for better and for worse — was that it sat at the intersection of theology, practice, real property, and ritual obligation. None of those elements alone would have done the work.

What would it look like to build cookie-baking infrastructure for the left? It would look, I think, like the projects that current left organizing does best when it remembers itself: tenant unions with offices and staff and weekly clinics, labor organizations with halls and dues structures and benefits, mutual aid networks that have outlasted the pandemic by becoming durable disability-justice and food-sovereignty projects, the few remaining ethnic mutual aid societies and labor temples that have been kept alive by tireless local volunteers. It would not look like ad hoc protest formations or social media affinity groups, however essential those have been to particular moments.

I want to push, too, on Inouye's framing of conflict. Following Nathan DuFord, she rightly insists that democratic solidarity is premised on disunity and that internal conflict is a check on domination. But there is a romance of conflict in contemporary left writing that I think Inouye partly resists and partly indulges. Most political conflict is not productive. Most splits do not clarify principle; they exhaust people and end relationships. The Mormon ward she describes was not, I suspect, a hothouse of constructive disagreement — it was a place where conflict was largely suppressed in the name of unity, and where the endurance came from that suppression as much as from the productive friction of difference. When she did write her tithing op-ed, Uncle Gerald was furious and the relationship did not improve.

What thick institutions do well is contain conflict without resolving it — they let people who disagree about fundamentals stay in proximity because there is more going on between them than the disagreement. The cookie matters. The mortgage on the building matters. The funeral arrangements matter. Take away the thick texture and conflict becomes the relationship, and few relationships survive that.

So my response to Inouye's essay is to push it harder in the direction it already wants to go. Yes to endurance, yes to humility, yes to the body as the site of political education. But endurance is not a virtue we exhort each other to. It is a property of institutions that bundle obligation, identity, and material stake into something heavier than any of its parts. The left's task is not chiefly to remind ourselves to keep showing up. It is to build places worth showing up to — places with real walls, real budgets, real cookies, and real consequences for leaving. Until we have those, we will keep mistaking the absence of endurance for a moral failing rather than the predictable result of a thin associational life.

That is a more demanding project than the essay quite acknowledges, and a more hopeful one. The Mormon Church is not just a cautionary tale about antisocial solidarity. It is also evidence that thick institutions can be built in living memory, by people with very little, under conditions of acute precarity. If they could do it in a Wyoming internment camp and a Utah farm town, we can do it too. But we have to want the cookie, not just the lesson.

What the Organizers Already Know

I read Mie Inouye's essay as a professional organizer of twenty years, and I want to say first that there is a great deal in it I recognize. The scene with Bob Lee in Uptown is canonical in the trainings I have led, and the description of how Lee uses physical proximity, touch, and the demand that the young mother speak — these are skills I have tried to teach and have watched dozens of organizers learn. The honest exchange with Henry at the fundraiser is the kind of moment any of us who have worked the doors will recognize. The essay's instincts about organizing as a relational, embodied, transformative practice are right.

But I want to enter a friendly objection. Much of what Inouye presents as a theoretical intervention — "social endurance," "epistemic humility," the cultivation of the capacity to stay in the room — is already the explicit pedagogical content of most serious organizing traditions in the United States. We have known this for a long time. The reason this knowledge has not produced the multiracial working-class movements we want is not that we lack a theory. It is that the institutional vehicles for transmitting and practicing this knowledge have been hollowed out, and the political economy has shifted in ways that no amount of better disposition can overcome.

Take "epistemic humility." Every IAF training I have ever attended begins with the relational meeting: an hour-long one-on-one in which the organizer's job is to ask questions and listen, to suspend their own analysis, to surface the leader's self-interest in their own words. Marshall Ganz's public narrative methodology, taught to thousands of organizers through the New Organizing Institute and now through Leading Change Network, formalizes this same humility into the "story of self, us, and now." McAlevey's structure-based organizing, derived from her time with 1199, insists on charting workplaces, identifying organic leaders, and recognizing that the organizer is not the most important person in the room. The Midwest Academy curriculum, going back to Heather Booth in the 1970s, teaches that the campaign begins with what the constituency wants, not with what the organizer thinks they should want. The PICO/Faith in Action network has built its entire ecclesiology around the discipline of listening across difference.

I do not list these to score points. I list them because Inouye's essay reads, in places, as though the practical alternative to deference politics has not yet been invented. It has been, repeatedly, and it works. What we are missing is not the curriculum. We are missing the conditions under which the curriculum gets implemented at scale.

Consider what has changed materially. In 1970 union density was about 27 percent. Today it is around 10 percent and under 7 percent in the private sector. That collapse alone has destroyed the most important site at which working-class people of different races, religions, and politics were once forced to engage each other in sustained, conflictual cooperation — the union local. The decline of mainline religious participation, of fraternal organizations, of bowling leagues (Putnam was not wrong, just incomplete) has stripped out the secondary institutions. The professionalization of nonprofits, with their grant cycles and their executive directors and their staff-to-member ratios, has substituted advocacy for organizing in too many places. The geographic sorting of Americans by class and political tribe has thinned the natural occasions for cross-difference encounter. And the algorithmic mediation of political life through screens has created the illusion of engagement without the friction that produces actual relationships.

Against this backdrop, "social endurance" is not a virtue we lack but a capacity that no longer has anywhere to be exercised. The Mormon ward Inouye describes is one of the very few American institutions that still works the way thick institutions used to work. Of course she found in it a model. So would I. So does every honest organizer who has watched what the LDS Church, or for that matter the Catholic parish in a working-class neighborhood, or a strong local of UNITE HERE, can still do.

Here is what I would add to Inouye's argument, then, from the organizer's seat. First: stop treating discourse interventions as a substitute for institution-building. Critiques of deference politics, however accurate, will not produce the multiracial working-class movement we need. Only sustained, well-resourced, long-haul investment in dues-based membership organizations will. The labor movement is having a real moment — Starbucks, Amazon, UAW, UPS, the actors and writers — and the most important thing the left can do right now is pour bodies and resources into supporting those campaigns, not into the discourse.

Second: take seriously the asymmetry between organizing the working class and organizing the PMC. Inouye, following Clemons, considers the possibility that the PMC is "the most accessible base" right now and wonders how to work with its contradictions. With respect, this is a defeatist framing. The PMC is accessible because it shows up to protests; it is not accessible because it does the long, boring, accountable work of building base power. PMC members will donate, march, sign petitions, and post — they will rarely commit to two years of weekly meetings to win a single contract. The traditional working class is harder to reach but easier to hold once reached, because the stakes are clearer and the cultural script of mutual obligation is, in many communities, still alive. Investing in PMC mobilization at the expense of working-class organizing is a strategic error we have made for forty years.

Third: do not romanticize conflict, but do not flinch from it. The best organizers I know practice what we sometimes call "tension without rupture" — the discipline of raising hard truths inside relationships strong enough to hold them. This is not the same as conflict-avoidance, and it is not the same as conflict-seeking. It is a craft, and it is teachable, but it requires sustained relationships, which requires institutions, which brings us back to point one.

Finally: respect the political economy of organizers themselves. The reason organizing pedagogy has gotten thinner over the past decade is partly that the job has gotten worse. Organizers are underpaid, overworked, often working on yearly grant cycles that punish long-term investment, frequently caught between national strategy memos and local realities. The intellectual class writing about solidarity rarely talks to the people doing the work week in and week out, and when it does, it tends to extract anecdotes rather than transmit lessons. Inouye, to her credit, has done some of the listening. I wish more of her colleagues would.

None of this is meant as a brush-off. Inouye's essay is one of the more useful pieces of left writing I have read this year, precisely because it takes the embodied, relational, awkward character of organizing seriously. But the lesson I draw from it is different from the one she seems to draw. We do not need a new theory of solidarity. We need to put the theory we already have to work, in institutions worth being part of, on campaigns worth winning. Endurance will follow. It always has.

Some Conflicts Should Not Be Endured

Mie Inouye writes movingly about the discomfort of organizing across difference and the value of staying in rooms where one would rather not be. There is much in her essay I admire — the honesty about her own motivations, the recovery of the Rainbow Coalition footage, the willingness to think with her own family rather than against it. But I want to register a sharp disagreement with the essay's central prescription. The injunction to cultivate "social endurance," to anticipate "annoyance and heartbreak," to interpret discomfort as "a process of becoming less racist, less classist, less transphobic," sounds wise in the abstract. In practice it has too often functioned as a rationale for asking the wrong people to absorb the wrong costs.

I write as a Black queer woman who has spent fifteen years in organizations that, to varying degrees, demanded my endurance. I have endured the white liberal who needed me to walk her through her own racism for the third time this year. I have endured the male comrade whose feminism evaporated whenever a woman of color disagreed with him about strategy. I have endured the well-meaning chapter chair who could not understand why "we're all here for the same reason" did not feel inclusive to those of us for whom the reasons are not, in fact, the same. Each of these endurances was sold to me as the price of solidarity. Each was, in some measure, the redistribution of emotional labor from those who could leave to those whose attachment to the cause made leaving harder.

This is not a denunciation of Inouye's argument. She is careful, and she explicitly does not endorse abusive conditions. But the language of endurance has a long, ambiguous history in left-of-center politics, and it tends to function asymmetrically. Bayard Rustin was asked to endure being closeted by his movement for decades. Ella Baker was asked to endure being passed over by Martin Luther King's circle. The women of SNCC were asked to endure being told that "the only position for women in the movement is prone." In each case, "endurance" was the name given to a coalition's refusal to do its own internal work, and the cost was paid by those whose other options were thinnest.

The essay's framing of deference politics inherits, I think, an analytic move that deserves more scrutiny. Olúfemi Táíwò's Elite Capture, which Inouye cites approvingly, made an important argument: that "centering the most marginalized" can become a rhetorical maneuver by which elite members of marginalized groups capture institutional space in the name of those whom they do not actually represent. This is true and worth saying. But the critique of deference politics has been picked up by a much broader set of voices, many of whom are less interested in elite capture than in dismantling any practice that asks privileged participants to defer to anyone, ever. The result is that "anti-deference" has come to mean, in some quarters, the restoration of the unmarked organizer — usually but not always white, male, and credentialed — to the position of authoritative judgment, with "epistemic humility" serving as a polite cover.

I do not think Inouye intends this. Her invocation of standpoint theory is careful, and she explicitly insists that organizing must "take seriously the essential insight of standpoint theory — that social location influences what we can know about the social world." But the essay's practical conclusions — endure, stay in the room, value conflict — apply with much less symmetry than the theory requires. The white PMC member who feels chastened by a conversation about race and decides to "stay" has, in most cases, suffered nothing. The Black or brown comrade who is asked to keep teaching that white member, week after week, often suffers a great deal. Both endurances are framed as solidarity. They are not the same.

What would a less symmetric account look like? It would start by recognizing that the costs of cross-difference organizing fall unevenly, and that solidarity practices have to account for that unevenness explicitly rather than wave at it through abstractions. The young white mother in the Bob Lee footage was lifted to her feet and asked to speak — yes. But Lee did that work as a leader with structural authority and political clarity, in a meeting that the Black Panthers had a strategic interest in organizing. He did not ask Black members of the Illinois Panthers to do extra labor to make white Appalachians comfortable. The Panthers brought their analysis to Uptown; they did not abandon it. The Rainbow Coalition was an offer of partnership conditional on a shared program, not an open-ended invitation to mutual transformation.

This conditionality is what I find missing in Inouye's framework. She presents endurance as a near-universal good, modulated only by the warning that organizations need not last forever. But endurance without conditions is just absorption. The Black women who left SNCC, the queer comrades who left the early gay liberation movements when those movements failed to grapple with race, the disabled organizers who have repeatedly told us that "showing up" is not a neutral expectation — these are the people whose leaving has often been the necessary precondition for movements to change. To valorize endurance without also valorizing the strategic exit is to load the dice in favor of those whose comfort the room is built around.

I want to suggest a different image. Instead of endurance as the master virtue, think of conditional commitment with structured accountability. I will stay in this organization as long as it is willing to do its own internal work, take direction from those it claims to represent, and accept that some kinds of harm are not raw material for my political education but reasons for me to leave. Solidarity organizations should make their own accountability practices legible enough that exit becomes a meaningful threat — not because we want people to leave, but because the credible possibility of departure is what disciplines coalitions to take their internal contradictions seriously.

This is not a counsel of fragility. The organizers I most admire, including many of my Black feminist forebears, were not fragile. They worked across enormous differences and absorbed enormous costs. But they did so within frameworks of accountability that they themselves had built, and they were willing to walk when those frameworks were violated. The Combahee River Collective Statement is a document of solidarity, but it is also a document of exit — the recognition that the existing organizations had failed Black lesbian socialists, and that a new formation was required.

So my friendly amendment to Inouye's essay would be this: cultivate endurance, yes, but cultivate it together with the capacities for accountability and for principled departure. Ask not only "how do we stay?" but "on what terms?" Make those terms explicit. Build organizations that can be left without catastrophe, because the credible possibility of being left is what holds them honest. And recognize that the asymmetry of cost is the thing solidarity has to address, not the thing it asks its most burdened participants to endure quietly. Otherwise "Solidarity Now" becomes another name for "patience, please."

The Romance of the Meeting

I want to be the curmudgeon in this forum. Mie Inouye's essay is graceful and self-aware, and I have learned things from it. But I think it suffers from a peculiar disorder common to a certain kind of left writing in our moment: the romance of the meeting. The premise that what ails the left is our inability to sit through one another's company, and that the cure is a cultivated capacity to do so, mistakes a symptom for a cause.

Most political change does not happen because people sat in rooms with each other and were transformed. It happens because organized blocs, possessing material leverage, force concessions from power. The meeting is one of the places where that bloc gets coordinated. It is not the place where the bloc is constituted, and it is certainly not the place where the bloc wins. The eight-hour day was not won by Bob Lee–style relational organizing in church basements, however much such organizing contributed. It was won by strikes — illegal, often violent, sustained over years — that imposed economic costs on employers until the law changed. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was not the byproduct of interracial sensitivity workshops. It was the byproduct of a Black freedom movement that made the South ungovernable in front of television cameras until Lyndon Johnson decided he could no longer afford the spectacle.

I am not pretending that movements arise without organization, or that organizations exist without meetings. Of course they don't. But the contemporary left's obsession with the affective texture of coalitional life — who feels seen, who feels alienated, who can endure whom — has slowly displaced the question of how concentrated power gets challenged. Inouye, to her credit, gestures at this displacement. She quotes Piven and Cloward; she acknowledges that organizing exists to make use of socially structured protest opportunities. But the essay then proceeds to spend most of its argumentative energy on the interior life of the meeting, and very little on the question of what the meeting is supposed to produce.

Consider the example she returns to most often: the summer of 2020. Inouye describes her students' experiences in the streets, the felt connections among unemployment, isolation, and police violence, the missed opportunity to forge those connections into something durable. She concludes that what failed was the discursive work — that "forging those connections explicitly would have involved conflict, risk, and time," and that we defaulted to allyship and deference instead. I think this diagnosis is partial at best. What failed in 2020 was not primarily our capacity to talk to each other about our reasons for being in the streets. What failed was the absence of organizational vehicles capable of converting mass insurgency into sustained pressure on specific institutions — police budgets, prosecutor offices, prison contracts, state legislatures. The protesters who showed up in June were largely unaffiliated with mass-membership organizations because such organizations no longer exist at the scale required. The energy dissipated not because people couldn't endure each other but because there was nowhere for the energy to go.

This is a structural failure, not a discursive one. And here is my deeper worry about the genre of writing Inouye's essay represents: it tends to flatter the intellectual class by suggesting that the problems of the movement are problems that intellectuals are uniquely qualified to solve. If the bottleneck is endurance, humility, openness to transformation, then those of us who write essays and teach courses are doing the most important work — we are the ones modeling the right disposition. This is, to put it kindly, convenient. It is also wrong. The bottleneck is institutional capacity, and intellectuals can contribute to that only marginally, mostly by amplifying the work of people whose names rarely appear in essays.

Let me put it more sharply. The recent revival of labor militancy — Starbucks Workers United, the Amazon Labor Union's victory at JFK8, the UAW's "stand-up strike" beginning this very week against the Big Three automakers, the Hollywood writers' strike now in its fifth month, the threatened UPS strike that won historic gains in August — has happened largely without any of the discourse about endurance and humility that Inouye's essay treats as central. It has happened because organizers identified pressure points, built majorities, picked fights, and won them. Where there has been internal conflict over race and gender and ideology — and there has been plenty — it has been processed within campaigns that already had concrete targets and timelines. The targets and timelines made the conflict productive; the conflict did not produce the targets and timelines.

I would say something similar about the most successful electoral insurgencies of the recent left — the Working Families Party at its best, certain DSA campaigns, the Sunrise Movement's pressure on the Inflation Reduction Act. These succeeded to the extent that they identified specific demands, built coalitions around them, and applied disciplined pressure. They failed, where they failed, mostly because the pressure was insufficient, not because the coalition lacked humility.

What would I want from a theory of solidarity, then? Honestly, less theory and more case studies. I want close studies of the conditions under which interracial working-class coalitions have actually formed and held — the 1199 hospital workers' campaigns, the SEIU justice for janitors campaign in Los Angeles, the New York Taxi Workers Alliance, the Coalition of Immokalee Workers. I want close studies of the conditions under which they have fallen apart, and what could have held them. I want less Jodi Dean and Nathan DuFord and more Bill Fletcher, Jane McAlevey, Stephen Lerner, Ai-jen Poo. I want fewer essays and more campaigns.

This is not a counsel of anti-intellectualism. Theory matters. But I think the left has, for some time now, been overproducing a particular kind of theory — affective, discursive, attentive to interiority — and underproducing the kind of strategic analysis that actually helps organizers make decisions. The unspoken assumption of much current left writing is that the obstacles to solidarity are obstacles in our heads, and that if we could only think and feel and relate differently, the movements would follow. The historical evidence does not bear this out. Movements follow material conditions, organizational capacities, and strategic choices. Heads and hearts adjust to those, more often than they shape them.

So my response to Inouye is to demur, gently. The Bob Lee footage is wonderful. The Henry conversation is honest and instructive. The Uncle Gerald story is moving. But the lesson I take from these vignettes is not that we need to cultivate endurance. It is that we need to build the kinds of organizations Bob Lee built, the kind of organization that could give Henry's life and Uncle Gerald's life and the author's life a common political horizon. That building is hard, slow, mostly invisible work, and it does not lend itself easily to essays. Which may be precisely why we keep writing essays about something else.

Solidarity as Improvisation

Mie Inouye's essay does something that the genre of solidarity writing rarely does: it admits that the author does not entirely know why she organizes. This admission is the essay's most generative move, and I want to develop it in a different direction than she does. Where Inouye lands on "social endurance" as the under-theorized basis of solidarity, I want to suggest a complementary concept that I think captures something her examples actually demonstrate: improvisation.

Consider what Bob Lee is doing in the Uptown footage. He has come into a room that he does not control, full of people whose dispositions he cannot predict. He does not have a script. He has a goal — disrupting the Model Cities meeting — but the path from this room to that goal runs through micro-decisions about which body to approach, when to touch, when to question, when to insist. The hand on Roger's hair is not a planned intervention; it is a response to a particular sixteen-year-old whose bravado needed to be tempered by tenderness. The lifting of the young mother to her feet is not in a curriculum; it is a read of the room in a particular moment, by an organizer attuned to who needs to speak and what their speaking might do.

This is improvisation in the sense that jazz musicians and pickup basketball players and skilled clinicians mean it: not the absence of structure but the capacity to act effectively within structure when the structure itself does not specify what to do. Improvisation requires deep knowledge of the form, intimate familiarity with one's collaborators, and a tolerance for risk. It cannot be reduced to a method, but it can be cultivated through practice.

I want to suggest that solidarity, at the level of the meeting and the campaign, is fundamentally improvisational. The theories that try to ground solidarity in shared interest, shared morality, shared ideology, or shared identity all run aground on the same rock: the people in the room never quite share any of these things in the way the theory requires. What they share, at most, is an occasion — a particular fight, a particular building, a particular threat — and a willingness to act together within that occasion. The character of their solidarity is worked out in real time, through bodies, words, gestures, and small decisions that no advance theory could fully specify.

This reframing has consequences. First, it shifts the question from "what is the basis of solidarity?" to "what conditions allow improvisational solidarity to flourish?" These are different questions. The first invites us to look for foundations; the second invites us to look for the supportive infrastructure of practice. The improvising jazz musician relies on a deeply internalized vocabulary, on hours of practice with the same players, on a venue, an audience, an instrument, a tradition. The improvising organizer relies on the same kinds of supports: trained skills, repeated experience, a base, an opponent, a context.

Second, it makes sense of why Inouye's "endurance" matters without making endurance the master virtue. Endurance is one of the supportive conditions for improvisation, because improvisation requires sustained co-presence — you cannot read a partner's gestures if you have not spent enough time with them to know how they move. But endurance is not what makes the music. The music is made in the moment, by people who have done the work to be ready when the moment arrives.

Third, it suggests that the most important pedagogical work of left intellectuals is not to specify the basis of solidarity in advance but to study and disseminate the practices that have produced effective solidarity in particular cases. This is closer to a jazz school than a philosophy department. We learn by watching Bob Lee, listening to old organizers talk about how they handled hard meetings, reading the campaign accounts of UNITE HERE local 11 or the New York taxi drivers or Bayard Rustin's March on Washington logistics. We learn by trying things, failing, and trying again with people who can give us notes.

Inouye's essay actually models this kind of pedagogy. The Bob Lee footage works in her classroom not because it illustrates a theory but because it shows a master at work, and students can see things in it that no theory would have prepared them to see — Lee's hands, his timing, his willingness to move into discomfort. This is what improvisational learning looks like. It is also, I think, why her students struggle to imagine themselves in Lee's place. The capacity to organize like that does not come from reading essays about organizing. It comes from doing it, badly at first, with mentors who can show you what you missed.

There is a deeper philosophical point here, drawing on the pragmatist tradition. John Dewey, in The Public and Its Problems, argued that publics are not pre-given but emerge in response to particular harms — they are constituted through the recognition of shared consequences. Hannah Arendt, in The Human Condition, made a related claim about action: that it is irreducibly initiatory, that it cannot be predicted from prior conditions, and that political life consists in the bringing-into-existence of relationships and possibilities that did not exist before. Neither thinker would have had much patience for the contemporary search for foundations of solidarity. Both would have insisted that solidarity is what happens when people respond to a situation together, in ways they could not have specified in advance.

This pragmatist/Arendtian frame helps clarify what is right and what is overstated in Inouye's account. What is right is the insistence that we cannot know in advance what a meeting will do to us — that solidarity is an open-ended process, that transformation is its hallmark. What is overstated is the suggestion that endurance per se is the key virtue. Endurance matters as a condition for improvisation, but the cardinal virtue of the improviser is something else: responsiveness. The capacity to read the room, to feel where the energy is, to know when to push and when to wait, to recognize the moment when a previously silent person needs to speak.

Responsiveness is harder to teach than endurance. It is also more dangerous to romanticize, because it can shade into the cult of the charismatic organizer who reads everyone perfectly and brooks no dissent. The best improvisers are not soloists; they are deeply attentive ensemble players, and the best organizing traditions have ensemble structures — collective leadership, accountability practices, debriefs, mentoring chains — that distribute responsiveness across many people rather than concentrating it in one.

So my suggested amendment to Inouye is: keep the endurance, but add the improvisation. And take seriously what jazz teaches us about how improvisers are made. They are made through practice, through ensemble, through tradition, through proximity to masters, through the freedom to fail. They are not made through manifestos. The left's failure of solidarity in the post-2020 moment is not, in this view, a failure of theory or even of endurance. It is a failure of practice infrastructure — of the schools, the apprenticeships, the campaigns, and the elders that would have made our improvisers ready when the music started.

This is what I think Bob Lee's hands tell us. They tell us that there is a craft here, that it can be learned, and that it cannot be learned from books. The most useful thing essays like Inouye's — and like this one — can do is to point at the craft, point at the masters, and say: go find them, go practice, and come back when you have something to play.

Material Interests Still Matter

Mie Inouye's essay is generous and humane, but I want to push back on what I read as its central drift: away from material interest as the basis of solidarity and toward something thinner — ideology in her preferred formulation, social endurance as her bottom-line prescription. I worry that this drift reflects, and reinforces, a broader pattern in left intellectual life that has weakened our capacity to build the coalitions we say we want.

Inouye begins with the question Henry asks her: why do you organize? Henry's answer is straightforward: because his family needs material changes to survive. Inouye's answer is more reflexive: because she needs meetings to go to. She admits feeling chastened by the contrast, but the essay then proceeds to recuperate her own answer, treating Henry's material-needs framing as one option among several and ultimately preferring an ideology-based account in which "a shared vision of a just society that aligns with but exceeds our material interests" does the binding work.

I think Henry is right and Inouye is wrong, and I think the essay's discomfort with this conclusion is symptomatic. The most durable solidarities in modern history — the trade union movement, the Black freedom struggle in its mass phases, the welfare rights movement, the farmworkers' organizations, the recent renaissance in graduate-worker unionism — have been built on shared material interest first, with morality and ideology following. People show up, repeatedly, over years, when something concrete is at stake for them. They do not show up, repeatedly, over years, because they have been persuaded of an attractive vision in the abstract.

This is not a reductive economism. Material interest, properly understood, is not just wages. It is housing, healthcare, education, safety from state violence, the capacity to live near one's family and community, time, dignity at work. Black workers in the South had material interests in ending Jim Crow that ran far beyond pay scales. Queer workers have material interests in nondiscrimination that include the protection of their family lives. Disabled people have material interests in accessible workplaces that the able-bodied left has too often failed to register. To say that solidarity rests on material interest is not to say that it rests on the narrow self-interest of the homo economicus model. It is to say that solidarity rests on the recognition of shared stakes in concrete conditions of life.

Inouye, following Jared Clemons, acknowledges that material interest is important and even essential — but then pivots, on the grounds that under contemporary conditions, full interest convergence between the white PMC and the multiracial working class is unlikely. From this she concludes that we need a different basis for coalition, located in shared ideology or shared "visceral stakes" of some kind. I want to suggest this pivot is premature.

First, the claim that interest convergence is impossible under neoliberal conditions is overstated. The PMC's material situation has been deteriorating for years. Adjunctification has destroyed the academic labor market. Junior lawyers, doctors, and journalists face working conditions and debt loads that earlier PMC generations did not. The recent unionization waves among museum workers, journalists, grad students, and even nonprofit staff suggest that significant portions of the PMC have material interests far more aligned with the broader working class than the older sociological category implies. The Hollywood writers' strike currently in progress is a PMC labor struggle, and it has clear material objectives that converge with broader anti-monopoly and anti-AI concerns across the working class.

Second, the empirical record of "ideology-first" coalitions is mixed at best. The Popular Fronts of the 1930s held together because they were also material coalitions — unions, mutual aid societies, ethnic federations — with shared ideological commitments laid on top. When the material infrastructure withered, the ideology alone proved insufficient to hold the coalition. The same is true of the New Left at its peak. SDS could mobilize huge crowds, but it could not sustain itself once the material infrastructure of student life and draft resistance shifted.

Third, the appeal to "ideology" as the binder risks asking people to commit to abstractions before they have committed to each other through concrete struggle. This inverts the actual psychology of political development. People typically arrive at shared ideology through shared experience of fighting for material things. The Memphis sanitation workers did not strike in 1968 because they had been converted to a vision of multiracial socialist democracy. They struck because two of their co-workers had been crushed in a garbage truck and their wages were starvation wages. The ideology came later, and in many of them, it came through the fight itself.

Inouye herself, I think, half-recognizes this. The Bob Lee footage she analyzes so well is not a story of ideology converting white Appalachians to multiracial solidarity. It is a story of an organizer asking, repeatedly, "What do you want?" — that is, eliciting material concerns and then helping participants see the structural sources of those concerns and the strategic value of cross-racial alliance. Lee's work was material-interest organizing of the highest order. The Rainbow Coalition's program was a program of material demands: against police brutality, against urban renewal, for housing, for jobs.

What would it look like to take material interest more seriously than Inouye's essay does? It would mean, first, much more attention to the empirical question of how interests actually align and diverge in particular conjunctures. The PMC is not a monolith; neither is the working class. The interests of an unhoused person in a rapidly gentrifying city are not identical to the interests of a stably housed nurse, and both are different again from the interests of a tech worker whose stock options just vested. Effective solidarity work begins with these particulars, not with general claims about which classes can or cannot ally.

It would mean, second, organizing strategies that lead with concrete fights and let solidarity develop through the fight rather than asking participants to commit to solidarity in advance. The strongest multiracial coalitions in recent labor history — Smithfield, the LA hotel workers, the Chicago teachers — were built by leading with grievances that mattered to people in their daily lives and gradually expanding the political horizon as the struggle developed. None of these was a "solidarity organization" first; they were organizations fighting for specific things, which became solidarity organizations through the fighting.

It would mean, third, more skepticism about the kind of mobilization that asks privileged participants to show up out of moral conviction. Inouye is right that "allyship" turned out to be a thin foundation in 2020. But the answer is not to thicken allyship into ideology; it is to redirect privileged participants toward fights where they have actual stakes — climate, healthcare, housing, working conditions in their own industries — and to build sustained connections from those fights to the broader struggle.

Henry was right at the fundraiser. The most durable reason to organize is because something material is at stake for you and yours. Inouye's reasons — meetings, the joy of association, the generative possibilities — are real, and I don't begrudge them. But they will not, by themselves, build the kind of movement we need. The materialist intuition that Inouye half-resists is the intuition we should be reclaiming, not retreating from. Solidarity follows interest more often than interest follows solidarity, and the left's recent forgetting of this fact has cost us more than we have yet reckoned with.

The Body in the Room

I want to dwell on a part of Mie Inouye's essay that has lingered with me longest: Bob Lee's hands. The hand in Roger's hair. The hands on the shoulders of the middle-aged man with his arms crossed. The hands grasping the young mother's hands and lifting her to her feet. Inouye notices that her students cannot imagine touching strangers this way, and reads this as evidence of how thoroughly neoliberalism has stripped us of the embodied repertoire of organizing. I think she is right, and I want to develop this thread further, because I believe the body — the actually felt, breathing, touching body — is where solidarity is made and unmade, and where most contemporary theorizing about solidarity does not go.

Let me start with a confession. I have been in left organizing for two decades, and the meetings I remember are not the ones with the most sophisticated political analysis. They are the ones with the meals, the singing, the physical exhaustion of canvassing for ten hours and coming back to a house where someone had cooked. They are the moments at picket lines when a stranger handed me a coffee and our fingers touched. They are the funerals of comrades, where bodies held each other up. They are the long bus rides to protests, the shared cigarettes outside meetings, the late-night kitchen conversations after the formal session ended. These were not auxiliary to the organizing; they were the organizing. The political education happened in the body, before it happened in the head.

This is not a sentimental claim. It is a claim grounded in what we know about how human cognition and affiliation actually work. Decades of research on embodied cognition, on the social neuroscience of trust, on the role of shared physical experience in group formation, all converge on the point that human solidarity is built largely below the level of conscious deliberation, through synchronized bodies, shared rhythms, mutual gaze, touch, and the rituals of co-presence. Émile Durkheim called the resulting state "collective effervescence." Randall Collins, working in his tradition, has spent a career documenting how "interaction ritual chains" produce solidarity through embodied co-presence, shared mood, and mutual focus. The strongest organizations — religious, military, political — are those that have understood this and built ritual infrastructure to harness it.

Now consider the contemporary left's relationship to embodied co-presence. It is, to put it gently, attenuated. The pandemic dealt a brutal blow to organizational forms that depended on bodies in rooms; many groups have not recovered. The displacement of meetings to Zoom — efficient, accessible, inclusive in important ways — has come at a cost that we are only beginning to register. Touch has become politically fraught for reasons that have very good origins (the necessary critique of harassment, the consent revolution) but that have left us without a comfortable repertoire for the kind of solidarity-making touch that Bob Lee deployed so effectively. The standardization of nonprofit professional culture has imported HR norms into organizing spaces, attenuating the embodied informality that older organizing traditions took for granted.

Inouye's students cannot imagine touching a stranger because they have grown up in a culture that has, for good and ill, taught them not to. They also cannot imagine the sustained co-presence that earlier generations of organizers took for granted, because they have not lived in institutions that produced it. The Mormon ward Inouye describes is, among many other things, an embodied co-presence machine: three hours every Sunday, two hours Wednesday night, an hour every morning. The Black church traditions out of which the civil rights movement emerged were similar machines. So were the labor halls, the social clubs, the immigrant societies. These were not just spaces where political work happened; they were factories of bodily solidarity.

What does it mean to take this seriously now? First, I think it means that any theory of solidarity that does not center the body is going to miss most of what actually matters. Inouye's essay, to its credit, gestures repeatedly at the embodied: the streets in 2020, the bodies pressed against each other, Lee's hands, the awkwardness of the young white people in the room. But these gestures are descriptions, not analytic centerpieces. I would want to push the body more firmly to the center of the analysis. Solidarity is not first an idea, a moral commitment, a material interest, or even an ideology. It is first a fact of bodies in proximity, and everything else is a description of what those bodies are doing or what we hope they will do.

Second, it means that the practices of solidarity-building have to be reverse-engineered from what we know about how embodied affiliation actually forms. This is not mysterious. Eat together, frequently. Sing together. Walk together, including in the literal sense of marching but also in the everyday sense of going somewhere on foot together. Cook for each other. Show up at funerals and weddings and births. Sit in waiting rooms together. Watch each other's children. Touch each other in the small ways that are still socially licensed — handshakes, hugs, hands on shoulders, the casual contact of dense co-presence. These are not auxiliary practices that supplement the real organizing. They are the real organizing, or at least the substrate on which everything else depends.

Third, it means that we have to take seriously the ways that the built environment, the schedule, the format, and the materiality of organizing shape what becomes possible. A meeting in a fluorescent-lit room with folding chairs in rows is a different political event than a meeting around a table with food. A protest that ends in dispersal is a different event than one that ends with everyone going to a common space to debrief. A campaign that asks volunteers to phonebank from home is a different event than one that asks them to phonebank from a common office where they meet each other and the candidate. These are not aesthetic preferences. They are infrastructural decisions that determine what kinds of solidarity can form.

Fourth — and here I am offering an addition to Inouye's framework rather than a critique — the embodied frame helps us understand why "social endurance" matters in the way that it does. Endurance is not just a virtue we cultivate by gritting our teeth through hard meetings. It is the byproduct of accumulated embodied co-presence. After enough meals together, enough Sundays, enough funerals, enough picket lines, our bodies recognize each other and want to be in proximity to each other, even when our minds are in disagreement. This is why thick institutions produce endurance and thin ones do not. The body knows things the mind has forgotten.

Inouye's own family story is, in this light, an embodied story. The sugar cookie her father received at his first ward social did the political work it did because it was eaten, by a small body, in a room with other bodies who had baked it and who would soon know his name. The mutual aid network that helped her family survive postwar racism was a network of bodies showing up, repeatedly, in person, to do things together. Mormonism's power — for good and ill — is not finally doctrinal. It is bodily. The doctrine is the rationalization of practices that work on the body before they work on the mind.

If we want what the Mormon ward has, we have to build what the Mormon ward has: dense, repeated, embodied co-presence around shared practices, in physical spaces that we own or hold or steward. There is no shortcut through better ideology or thicker theory. The body in the room is the basic unit, and we have spent forty years failing to build the spaces where bodies can gather. That is the project, and Bob Lee's hands are the curriculum.

The Religious Question

I want to take seriously what almost no other respondent to Mie Inouye's essay will: the religious dimension of her argument. Inouye learned to organize in the Mormon Church. She names this explicitly, and she develops the comparison thoughtfully. But she ultimately treats Mormonism as a source of transferable techniques — "social endurance," repeated co-presence, the cultivation of cooperation across difference — that can be extracted from their theological matrix and applied to secular democratic organizing. I want to question whether this extraction works, and to ask what it would mean for the left to take religion more seriously than the secular framing of "solidarity" has typically allowed.

Let me state the worry directly. The endurance that the Mormon ward produced in Inouye and her family is inseparable from the theological commitments that gave the ward its meaning: the doctrine of the eternal family, the conviction that this life is a probationary period in a vast cosmic drama, the belief that the Saints are quite literally building Zion in preparation for Christ's return. People showed up for three hours on Sunday and an hour before school because they believed that the showing up was tied to the salvation of their souls and the preservation of their families across eternity. Strip away these beliefs and you do not get the same endurance with different content. You get a much weaker form of association that resembles the secular voluntary organizations Inouye contrasts Mormonism with.

This is not a defense of Mormon theology, much of which I do not share. It is a structural observation about how religion produces solidarity. Émile Durkheim understood this; so did William James; so did Charles Taylor more recently in A Secular Age. The thick associational life that Inouye correctly identifies as the source of her organizing formation depends, in religious contexts, on cosmological commitments that secular formations cannot replicate. The question is not whether the left can learn from religious institutions; of course it can and should. The question is whether the lessons are portable across the religious/secular divide without significant loss.

I think the honest answer is: partly, but not as much as we would like. Consider what secular formations have tried to substitute for religious cosmology over the past two centuries. Marxism, at its peak, offered a near-cosmological narrative — historical materialism as a quasi-providential account of human emancipation, the proletariat as a quasi-messianic agent, communism as a quasi-eschatological horizon. This narrative produced enormous solidarity at its strongest, including in conditions of severe repression. As the cosmological force of the Marxist narrative has faded, so has the solidarity it once anchored. This is not coincidence.

Civil rights organizing, which Inouye references, depended heavily on Black church theology — the prophetic tradition, the exodus narrative, the resurrection hope. Cornel West and others have argued that the secularization of the Black freedom struggle, however inevitable, has cost the movement some of the spiritual depth that earlier generations could draw on. Liberation theology in Latin America produced base communities and revolutionary movements precisely because it kept theology and political organizing in tight integration. Catholic Worker houses still produce remarkable endurance because they are rooted in a sacramental vision of the poor.

What does this mean for the contemporary American left, most of whose participants are secular or only loosely religious? I see three honest options.

The first is to acknowledge that secular solidarity will be, almost necessarily, thinner than religious solidarity, and to lower expectations accordingly. This is the implicit position of much liberal political theory: solidarity is a precious but limited resource, and we should design institutions that can do their work with relatively modest demands on it. The welfare state, on this view, substitutes institutional redistribution for the thick mutual aid that religious communities once provided. Whatever else one says about the postwar welfare state, it did real work, and it did so without asking citizens to share a cosmology.

The second option is to attempt to construct quasi-religious commitments within secular movements — to develop rituals, narratives, calendars, ethical practices, and forms of community that approximate what religion does without the theology. Some movements have tried this, with mixed results. The labor movement at its peak had songs, holidays, martyrs, and a near-sacramental sense of solidarity. The early Communist parties had something similar. Contemporary climate movements have tried, with their own iconographies and rituals. The results have been real but limited, and there is always the risk that quasi-religion shades into kitsch or, worse, into cultic dynamics that lack the corrective resources of mature religious traditions.

The third option, which I find most interesting, is to take religion seriously on its own terms and ask whether actually religious people, in actually religious institutions, can become significant partners in left organizing. This has historically been one of the most effective forms of multiracial working-class coalition-building in the United States, and the model still works. PICO (now Faith in Action), the IAF affiliates rooted in congregations, the network of progressive Black churches, the Catholic social teaching tradition, the prophetic Jewish institutions, the small but real progressive evangelical world, the growing Muslim civic infrastructure — these are real solidarity-producing institutions that the secular left often treats with suspicion or condescension, when it should be treating them as partners.

The Reverend William Barber's Poor People's Campaign, which Inouye references in its 1968 incarnation, has been doing significant work for the past several years precisely by reconnecting prophetic religion to economic justice across lines of race and region. The work is uneven; the institutional infrastructure is fragile; the secular media often does not know how to cover it. But it is one of the most genuine attempts at the kind of multiracial working-class coalition Inouye says we need, and it is succeeding to the extent it is because it draws on the theological resources that secular movements cannot easily replicate.

I would push Inouye to be more direct about this. Her essay treats Mormonism, in the end, as a kind of training ground from which she has graduated into a more democratic and pluralistic organizing practice. Fair enough — that is her own trajectory. But the essay's implicit framing is that the lessons of Mormonism can be carried forward into secular organizing without significant loss. I am skeptical. I think we would do better to acknowledge that religious institutions have resources for solidarity that secular ones do not, that the secular left has often been bad at partnering with religious institutions, and that any serious project of building multiracial working-class power in the United States will have to come to terms with religion — both as partner and as potential model — much more thoughtfully than it has.

This also means coming to terms with the specifically religious character of much of the conservative right's solidarity, which is one of the reasons the right has been able to mobilize working-class constituencies the left has lost. Inouye's Uncle Gerald is a real figure: a libertarian-leaning Mormon doctor who served his community fiercely, distrusted institutions, and ultimately died because the religious-political package he had inherited included epistemological commitments that proved fatal. The left's task is not to replicate the right's package, obviously. But it is also not to pretend that there is no package — that solidarity can be built on dispositions alone, without the thick metaphysical commitments that have historically anchored every solidarity worth the name.

I do not have a clean conclusion. Religion is hard. Most secular leftists are not going to become religious, and I am not arguing that they should. But the question of how to build solidarity at the scale and durability the left needs is, at bottom, a question about what holds people together across difference over time, and history is fairly clear that religion has done this better than almost any other human institution. We can refuse this fact, or we can grapple with it. Inouye's essay grapples partially. I am grateful for that, and I want to encourage her, and us, to grapple further.

A Note on Henry

I keep returning to Henry. Mie Inouye's essay frames her encounter with him as a moment of clarifying chastening — a first-generation Asian American organizer from a working-class background, asking the fourth-generation Asian American professor why she organizes, and getting an answer that strikes them both, in different ways, as insufficient. Inouye's eventual response, threaded through the essay, is roughly: Henry's material-interest framing is partial because organizing is also about meetings, ideology, and the inscrutable mixture of motives that bring real people into political life. I think this response, while honest, does Henry a kind of subtle disservice that I want to try to name.

Henry, on the essay's account, said he organizes because of "the material changes my family needs to see to our lives in order to survive." This is the answer of someone for whom organizing is not a chosen vocation but a necessity — someone whose presence in the room is conditioned by stakes that he cannot easily set aside. He is not organizing for the meetings; he is organizing because his family's survival, in some real sense, depends on the political outcomes the meetings might produce. Inouye records this exchange honestly, and acknowledges feeling chastened. But the essay then proceeds to make Henry's answer one option among several, and to develop a framework — endurance, humility, the cultivation of dispositions — that has very little to say about people in Henry's situation.

I want to ask: who is the addressee of Inouye's essay? Whom is the prescription of "social endurance" addressed to?

Read carefully, the essay's prescription is addressed primarily to people who have a choice about whether to keep showing up. The whole apparatus of cultivating endurance, of anticipating annoyance and heartbreak, of interpreting discomfort as transformation, assumes a participant whose attachment to the movement is voluntary and whose departure is a real option. Henry, on his own account, does not have this kind of optionality. His family needs the material changes. He shows up because he has to, and the relevant question for him is not whether to endure but how to win.

This is not a small matter. Most of the left writing about solidarity in our moment is, I think, addressed implicitly to people in Inouye's position — to the relatively secure, well-educated, ideologically motivated participant for whom showing up is an act of conviction rather than necessity. The advice we give this participant is sensible: be humble, stay in the room, do not retreat into deference, do not romanticize purity, allow yourself to be transformed. But this advice does not describe what Henry needs from the left, and it does not describe what the left needs from itself in order to be useful to people like Henry.

What does Henry need? At a minimum, he needs the movement to win things. Not necessarily the revolution, not necessarily even big things, but concrete material gains that demonstrate, in his life and in the lives of people like him, that the movement is capable of changing the conditions of life. A movement that cannot win small things will not win big things, and a movement that wins nothing has no claim on the loyalty of people whose participation is conditioned by material stakes.

Henry also needs the movement to be honest about the asymmetry between participants like him and participants like Inouye. The essay registers this asymmetry briefly — the contrast between Henry's reasons and Inouye's own — but does not develop it. What would it mean to develop it? It would mean acknowledging that movements that depend on the participation of people in Henry's situation owe those people something more than dispositions. They owe them strategic seriousness, accountability, and a credible theory of victory.

This is where I want to push Inouye's essay hardest. The essay's affective register — the meditative tone, the willingness to dwell in ambiguity, the move toward "social endurance" as a near-virtue — is the register of someone for whom organizing is, in the end, a chosen and meaningful form of life. This is not wrong; there is real wisdom in this register, and Inouye captures it better than most. But the register can drift, almost imperceptibly, into a kind of consolation — a way of making peace with the limits of the moment that may not serve the people whose stakes are higher than ours.

I worry about this drift in part because I have seen it in myself. I am a left academic, and the temptations of the meditative-consolatory register are well known to me. We write about solidarity in ways that make our own participation feel meaningful even when the movement is losing. We theorize the value of endurance in ways that make our own continued involvement feel virtuous even when the strategic case for our involvement is unclear. We praise epistemic humility in ways that absolve us from the harder work of forming judgments and acting on them. The genre's gravitational pull is toward the affirmation of the participant who is already in the room rather than toward the harder question of how to win things for those who cannot leave.

Henry, I suspect, does not need an essay on endurance. He needs the movement to develop a strategy capable of delivering the material changes his family needs. The essay he might write would probably look different from Inouye's — sharper, more impatient, more focused on outcomes than on dispositions. This is not because Henry is less reflective; it is because the situation he describes himself as being in does not permit the leisure of meditative ambivalence.

I do not say this to dismiss Inouye's essay. The essay is honest about its own position, which is more than most essays in this genre are. The exchange with Henry is reported faithfully, and the chastening it produced is real. What I am saying is that the essay's resolution of the chastening — its move toward endurance and humility as the central virtues — may be a resolution available to Inouye in her position that would be less available, and less useful, to Henry in his.

The deeper question Inouye's essay raises but does not fully confront is: what does the left look like when it takes its accountability to people in Henry's situation as its first commitment? I suspect it looks more strategic, more materialist, more impatient with the affective genres of meditation and consolation, more focused on the concrete tasks of building power and winning fights. It looks more like what Inouye sees in the Bob Lee footage — an organizer with a plan, working a room toward a specific objective — and less like what she gestures at in her own classroom, where the question is what it would take for her students to know why they are taking her class.

I admire Inouye for asking that question of her students. I admire her for asking it of herself in the exchange with Henry. I just wish the essay gave Henry the last word, and let his answer shape the analysis more decisively than it does. Solidarity now, in the sense that matters most, is solidarity with Henry — not as a figure to be understood and integrated into our reflections but as a participant whose stakes are higher than ours and whose needs set the terms of what we should be doing. The essay points in this direction. I hope its readers will go further.

What Comes After Endurance

I find myself, after reading Mie Inouye's essay several times, in a position of partial agreement and partial unease. The agreement is easy to state: Inouye is right that organizing is a transformative practice, right that deference politics has produced fragile coalitions, right that something like epistemic humility is preferable to either confident moralism or evasive deference, right that the Mormon ward of her childhood and the Rainbow Coalition of 1969 both have things to teach us about what it takes to hold differently situated people together over time. The unease is harder to articulate, but it has something to do with what the essay does not quite ask: what is endurance for?

Inouye's account suggests that endurance is its own reward, or close to it. Through endurance we become "less racist, less classist, less transphobic, less judgmental, more flexible, more knowledgeable, and, ultimately, more useful to the project of building the world we want to share." This list is appealing, but notice the gradient: from the personal-ethical (less racist, less judgmental) to the politically functional (more useful to the project). The essay's center of gravity is on the personal-ethical end. The political project remains underspecified, a horizon toward which the cultivated dispositions are presumably moving, but whose actual content the essay does not fill in.

I want to suggest that this asymmetry is not accidental. It reflects a particular intellectual moment in which the left's confidence about what we want has, paradoxically, declined even as our discursive sophistication about how we ought to relate to each other has grown. We have a great deal to say about the ethics of coalition, the dangers of deference, the value of conflict, the importance of staying in the room. We have less to say, with collective conviction, about what we are trying to build. The result is a body of writing — and I include Inouye's essay in this, although it is one of the more thoughtful examples — in which the dispositional questions have crowded out the substantive ones.

This is not a criticism of any individual author. It is an observation about a genre. And I think the genre's flourishing reflects, in part, a defensive crouch. After the high hopes of 2020, after the demobilization that followed, after the rise of authoritarian movements on the right that have outmaneuvered the left in arena after arena, after the disappointments of the Biden administration on the issues the left cares about most, there is something protective in turning to the interior questions of how we should relate to each other. These questions are tractable in a way the larger strategic questions are not. We can recommend humility; we cannot easily recommend a path to power.

But I think this defensive crouch is the wrong response to the situation, and I want to use my space here to suggest what a more confident posture might look like.

First, the left should be willing to specify what it wants. Not in the sense of a utopian blueprint — that has its own pathologies — but in the sense of a near-term political horizon detailed enough that people can recognize themselves in it. What is the political economy we are fighting for? A Nordic-style social democracy? A solidarity economy? A democratized version of the U.S. welfare state? Some configuration of public ownership, sectoral bargaining, robust commons? These are real choices with real implications, and the left's reluctance to choose among them has costs. Inouye's essay invokes "a shared vision of a just society" as the binder of coalition, but the essay itself does not try to specify the vision. I am not asking Inouye to do this single-handedly; I am observing that the genre of writing she is contributing to rarely tries.

Second, the left should be willing to identify its actual political opponents with the same specificity it brings to its identification of allies. Inouye's essay names "neoliberal capitalism" several times, citing Clemons and DuFord. This is fine as far as it goes, but "neoliberal capitalism" is an abstraction that does not name the actually existing institutions, individuals, and political coalitions that constitute the opposition. The fossil fuel industry, the private equity sector, the carceral apparatus, the union-busting law firms, the donor networks of the Republican Party, the financialized real estate complex, the technology monopolies — these are the actual opponents, and they need to be named, mapped, and confronted as such. A movement that only knows itself through its internal dispositions will not develop the strategic clarity to fight specific opponents on specific terrain.

Third, the left should rebuild its institutional capacity at every level — labor, electoral, community, communications, legal. This is the unglamorous work that most of the people I admire most are doing, often without much attention from the discursive left. The unionization wave currently unfolding, the strongest in fifty years, is the most important thing happening in American politics, and its outcome will shape what is politically possible for a generation. The state-level electoral work that has slowly turned places like Georgia and Arizona competitive has been done by organizers most readers of this forum have never heard of. The tenant union infrastructure being built in cities across the country, the bail funds that have professionalized into durable institutions, the climate movement's pivot from protest to policy implementation — these are the seedbeds of whatever the left will become.

Fourth, the left should make peace with its own ideological pluralism without retreating from the demand that pluralism take constructive form. Inouye and DuFord are right that solidarity organizations cannot demand unity, that conflict is constitutive, that disagreement is not a sign of failure. But this is compatible with — indeed, requires — the development of disciplined practices for processing disagreement constructively rather than letting it fester or splinter. The Chinese left's old practice of "criticism/self-criticism," for all its abuse in the Maoist era, contains an important kernel: that organizations need procedures for the surfacing and resolution of internal conflict that are neither suppressive nor explosive. The contemporary left has, by and large, not developed these procedures, and our organizations suffer for it.

Finally, the left should reclaim a confident relationship to hope. I do not mean naive optimism. I mean the kind of hope that has historically animated serious freedom movements — the conviction that the world can be different, that we can change it, that the work is worth doing even when the odds are long. The dispositional turn in left writing has tended toward a kind of meditative ambivalence — neither despair nor confidence, but a careful holding-open of possibilities. This register has its place, but it does not, by itself, mobilize. People do not endure in struggle because they have been trained in epistemic humility. They endure because they believe the struggle can be won.

This brings me back to Inouye's essay and what I admire most in it: the story of Uncle Gerald. The essay's most powerful moment is the recognition that "the same shit that killed George Floyd killed my Uncle Gerald, however more softly and however more self-inflicted, and because I, too, want my family to survive." This is not a meditation; it is a claim. It names a specific harm — racial capitalism, intersected with the pandemic — and a specific aspiration — survival, for one's own people and across difference. If the essay had built outward from this claim into a more developed political program, it would have been a different essay, and perhaps a more useful one. The materials are there.

What comes after endurance, I want to say, is not more endurance. It is the actual project that endurance has been preparing us for: building the institutions, naming the opponents, specifying the demands, contesting power, winning, and consolidating the wins. Inouye's essay is a contribution to the preparation. I hope the next one is a contribution to the project.
