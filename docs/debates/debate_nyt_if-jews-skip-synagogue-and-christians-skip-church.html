What should religious leaders take away from these numbers? How can congregations recapture these “lost sheep”?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Pew survey should not be read as a crisis of belief but as a crisis of belonging. Roughly 22 percent of American Jews now describe themselves as having no religion, yet they still claim Jewish identity through ancestry, culture, and ethics. Among Christians, weekly attendance has been sliding for decades even as self-identification holds. The pattern is consistent: Americans have not stopped finding meaning in their inherited traditions; they have stopped finding meaning in the institutions that house them. Religious leaders who treat the numbers as a marketing problem—better music, hipper sermons, livelier websites—will misdiagnose what is happening.

The deeper issue is that synagogues and churches were built for a sociological world that no longer exists. Postwar congregations functioned as ethnic anchors, civic clubs, marriage markets, and schools all at once. Suburbanization, intermarriage, dual-career households, the cost of dues and Hebrew school, and the rise of online community have stripped away most of those secondary functions. What remains—worship, study, ritual—is precisely the part that requires the most from members and offers the least obvious "return." It is no wonder that people who still treasure the holidays, the Seder, the Christmas Eve service, or the ethical tradition find weekly attendance optional.

Three responses seem more honest than the usual hand-wringing. First, leaders should stop measuring success by pews filled on Saturday or Sunday morning. By Pew's own data, Jews of no religion still light candles, fast on Yom Kippur, and care intensely about Israel and anti-Semitism. Christians who skip church still pray, read scripture, and baptize their children. A congregation that meets these people where they actually are—through life-cycle events, holiday gateways, social-justice work, adult learning, online study—will reach more souls than one that demands weekly attendance as the price of admission.

Second, clergy should take seriously that many Americans find their institutions theologically thin or politically compromised. Younger Americans in particular tell pollsters they leave because churches seem judgmental, anti-science, or partisan; Jews under 30 cite irrelevance more than disbelief. The remedy is not to soften everything into therapeutic vagueness, which only accelerates the drift, but to offer something distinctive—serious texts, demanding ethics, real community obligations—that secular life cannot replicate.

Third, congregations should lower the financial and bureaucratic walls. Mandatory dues, building campaigns, and pay-to-pray High Holiday tickets send a message that membership is a consumer transaction. Free or sliding-scale access, smaller minyanim, house churches, and partnerships with Hillel or campus ministries can rebuild the muscle of showing up.

The "lost sheep" are not lost. They are standing outside the gate wondering whether what is inside is worth the cost of entry. The answer must come from the shepherds.

The latest Pew survey on Jewish Americans confirms what many religious leaders already know in their bones: identity is not the same as practice. A robust 94 percent of Jews surveyed are proud to be Jewish, even as secularism rises and synagogue attendance falls. Similarly, the vast majority of Americans still call themselves Christian, but only a minority warm a pew on Sunday. The impulse to ask how we might recapture these “lost sheep” is understandable, but it misreads the moment. People aren’t lost; they’re quietly renegotiating what faith means to them, often outside the walls of traditional congregations.

The first takeaway is that belonging still matters deeply. The Pew data showed that Jewish identity is increasingly rooted in culture, ancestry and ethics rather than in belief in God or adherence to religious law. Many Jews who intermarry raise children with some Jewish consciousness, and most celebrate a Passover seder. Among Christians, the rapid rise of the “nones”—one in five Americans overall, and a third of adults under 30—has not erased a stubborn, diffuse sense of being Christian. This suggests that the craving for community, story and moral horizon endures, even when institutional religion feels irrelevant or alienating.

If congregations hope to reverse the drift, they should stop seeing the problem as a marketing failure or a need for more entertainment. The people who stay away often do so not out of laziness but out of a conviction that religious bodies are too dogmatic, too politicized or too inward-looking. A 2012 Pew study found that most unaffiliated Americans see religious institutions as overly concerned with money and rules rather than with justice. The path back, then, is not to lower the bar but to raise the table: to become places where honest doubt is welcomed, where interfaith families aren’t seen as a crisis but as a fact of life, and where action on poverty, immigration or the environment makes the ancient texts feel urgently moral.

The metaphor of “lost sheep” hints at a passive flock waiting to be herded home. But today’s seekers are often more like pilgrims who have left a town that no longer nourishes them. They won’t be scolded back. They can, however, be drawn toward communities that practice what they preach—that combine contemplative depth with a tangible commitment to healing the world. Jewish denominations that fully embrace patrilineal descent, and churches that halt culture wars over sexuality, remove obstacles that no longer make sense to younger generations. At the same time, vibrant, small-group initiatives—home-based Bible studies, social-justice collectives, young-adult Shabbat dinners—often outpace formal services because they foster intimacy and shared purpose.

In the end, the numbers challenge religious leaders to make a hard distinction between fortress religion and living faith. The fortress demands conformity and rewards attendance; living faith gathers around a fire that warms both insider and stranger. The challenge is not to recapture the departed but to become the kind of community they would freely choose.

The assumption that declining attendance equates to a loss of faith or identity is a fundamental misreading of the modern American spiritual landscape. The recent Pew Research Center study on Jewish Americans, alongside broader demographic data on the rise of Christian "nones," reveals a profound shift in *how* people affiliate, not necessarily *whether* they do. To label these individuals "lost sheep" is to misunderstand why they left the fold in the first place.

Increasingly, Americans are not rejecting the core narratives, cultures, or values of their respective heritages; they are rejecting rigid, top-down institutional models. For many, traditional congregations feel out of step with contemporary life. Whether it is the prohibitive cost of synagogue dues, the dogmatic political entanglements of certain Christian denominations, or simply the rote predictability of weekly services, the modern institutional model often fails to resonate. Yet, as the data shows, cultural identity and personal spirituality remain highly resilient.

If religious leaders want to bridge this gap, they must first cure themselves of the "edifice complex"—the entrenched idea that spiritual success is measured strictly by the number of bodies in the pews on a Friday night or Sunday morning. Instead of asking how to lure people back into the building, clergy and community leaders must ask how to carry the richness of their traditions out into the world.

Recapturing the disaffected requires moving away from transactional religion. Congregations should experiment with decentralized community building, meeting people where they actually live. This means prioritizing shared values like social justice, informal neighborhood gatherings, intellectually rigorous text study, and authentic interpersonal connections over formal membership metrics. When religious institutions stop demanding that modern Americans conform to a 1950s model of attendance, and instead offer their traditions as vital, adaptable resources for navigating a complex world, they will find that the sheep were never truly lost.

Many Americans still want inherited identity, moral vocabulary, ritual at life’s thresholds, community in crisis and a story larger than the self. Nominal affiliation remains high, but regular participation is much lower, and younger Americans are increasingly comfortable saying they have no religious affiliation. The Pew data on Jewish Americans and the broader patterns of Christian disengagement point to a common challenge: religious institutions have conflated institutional belonging with spiritual practice, and many people now find that equation unconvincing. The question is not merely how to fill pews again, but whether that should be the goal at all.

This suggests that for a significant portion of American Jews, religion is experienced as heritage rather than worship, and the synagogue's relevance must be measured against that reality. Rabbinic leaders who insist on equating Jewishness with synagogue attendance may be fighting a rearguard battle against a centuries-old pattern of variable observance.

Christianity presents a different but related dynamic. The "80 percent Christian" identification figure has always been inflated by cultural Christianity—people who check a box on surveys but never intended to practice their faith seriously. The more interesting data point is the decline of regular churchgoing even among those who call themselves believers. This reflects broader sociological shifts: increased geographic mobility breaking down parish ties, the privatization of spirituality, and a cultural shift toward experiential authenticity that static, predictable worship services struggle to provide.

Instead, they might ask what their communities offer that cannot be found elsewhere. Genuine fellowship across economic and generational lines, robust moral education for children, meaningful service opportunities, and spaces for honest wrestling with life's hardest questions—these remain genuinely scarce in American life. The institutions that provide them will thrive regardless of whether they resemble traditional synagogue or church models.

The "lost sheep" metaphor assumes they were ever adequately pastored. Many drifted away not from churches and synagogues, but from communities that never made them feel they belonged. Rebuilding that belonging requires humility, experimentation, and a willingness to let institutional form follow genuine mission rather than the reverse.

The Pew survey should not be read as a crisis but as an honest mirror. For decades, American religious institutions have measured their health by counting bodies in pews and dollars in plates. Those metrics are no longer adequate to the spiritual life of the country, and clinging to them will only deepen clergy despair.

What the numbers actually show is a decoupling of identity from institution. Jews still feel Jewish; Christians still consider themselves Christian. What they have stopped doing is outsourcing the practice of their faith to professionals once a week. This is not, in itself, a tragedy. It is the predictable outcome of a culture that prizes authenticity, suspects hierarchy, and has unlimited substitutes for the social functions congregations used to monopolize — community, child care, music, charity, even moral instruction.

Religious leaders have two honest options. The first is to compete on the terrain modernity has chosen: offer programming, child-friendly services, podcasts, social-justice projects, and pleasant coffee hours. Many congregations have tried this, and the better ones have stabilized their decline. But this strategy turns synagogues and churches into one more lifestyle option among many, and the people who want a lifestyle option already have yoga.

The second option is harder and more interesting: lean into what religion alone can offer — a serious encounter with mortality, transcendence, obligation, and the moral demands of a tradition older than the self. The congregations that are actually growing, from Orthodox shuls to evangelical megachurches to certain Catholic parishes, tend to ask more of their members, not less. They demand time, money, behavior, belief. Counterintuitive as it sounds, the path back to relevance may run through higher, not lower, thresholds.

The "lost sheep" framing, finally, is part of the problem. It assumes the shepherd's job is to retrieve a passive flock. But the unaffiliated are not lost; many have made considered choices, and they will not return because a rabbi sends a clever email blast. They will return, if at all, because they encounter a community whose seriousness about God, text, and neighbor makes the rest of their week look thin by comparison. That is a much taller order than a membership drive, and it is the only order worth filling.

The Pew numbers tell a story that pundits keep misreading. The headline is not that Americans are abandoning religion. It is that Americans are abandoning institutions — all of them. Union membership is down. Rotary clubs are graying. Bowling leagues, as Robert Putnam famously noticed, have collapsed. Political parties poll worse than root canals. Against this backdrop, the decline of synagogue and church attendance looks less like a spiritual emergency and more like one chapter of a much larger civic story.

This reframing matters because it changes the prescription. If the problem were specifically religious — bad theology, boring sermons, irrelevant doctrine — then theological reform might fix it. But if the problem is the broader erosion of associational life, then religious leaders need to think of themselves as civic entrepreneurs, not just spiritual teachers.

What does that mean practically? It means congregations should stop apologizing for being institutions and start defending the unique goods that institutions deliver: continuity across generations, accountability, ritual that doesn't depend on how you feel that morning, friendships with people you didn't choose. These are precisely the goods that an atomized, smartphone-mediated culture cannot provide, and they are precisely what the unaffiliated tell pollsters they miss when pressed.

It also means religious leaders should be honest about competition. Many of the functions churches and synagogues once performed have been unbundled. You can get community from CrossFit, moral seriousness from TED talks, transcendence from psychedelics or hiking, ethnic identity from food and Netflix. Each of these is a thinner substitute, but cumulatively they fill the void. Rebundling is hard work.

The Jewish case is particularly instructive. Pew found that the non-religious Jews still overwhelmingly identify with Jewish peoplehood. That's an enormous asset — most declining institutions would kill for that level of residual loyalty. The challenge is converting identity into participation, which requires giving people something to actually do that feels meaningful and not optional.

For Christians, the challenge is different. Christian identity in America has been so thoroughly mainstreamed that it costs nothing to claim, which means it asks nothing in return. Reviving church life may require recovering some of the strangeness, the demand, the countercultural edge that early Christianity had. Comfortable religion is, paradoxically, the religion people walk away from first.

Let me offer a contrarian view: the leaders should take away very little, because there isn't much they can do about it.

The decline of religious participation in advanced economies is one of the most consistent findings in the sociology of religion. It has happened in Western Europe, in Canada, in Australia, in much of Latin America, and now, on a delay, in the United States. The drivers — economic security, educational attainment, women's labor force participation, declining fertility, urbanization, pluralism — are structural. No clever programming change at the parish level reverses macro-historical currents.

This is not defeatism; it is realism. The American religious landscape has always been more dynamic than the European one, and there will continue to be growth in some places (immigrant churches, certain evangelical and Orthodox communities, Mormonism in some periods) alongside decline in others (mainline Protestantism, Conservative and Reform Judaism, white Catholicism). The aggregate trend, however, points one way, and it points there with or without better youth groups.

What religious leaders should do, then, is stop chasing the lost sheep and start tending the flock they have. A smaller, more committed congregation is not a failure; it may be the natural state of religion in a free society where belonging is a choice rather than a default. The mistake is assuming that the postwar American boom in religious participation was normal. It wasn't. The 1950s were the anomaly, propped up by Cold War civil religion, suburbanization, the baby boom, and a kind of social conformity we are not getting back.

Free of the burden of growth, congregations can focus on depth: better Torah study, more demanding catechesis, richer liturgy, sustained pastoral care, real charitable work. They can be places that form serious people rather than retention machines. If those serious people happen to attract others, wonderful. If not, the tradition is still being transmitted, which is the actual job.

The "lost sheep" metaphor in Luke is often misused. In the parable, the shepherd leaves the ninety-nine to find the one. The American religious anxiety inverts this: we have the one, and we are willing to neglect them while chasing the ninety-nine who wandered off. That's bad theology and worse strategy.

The Pew survey on Jewish Americans deserves to be read closely rather than as a generic data point about religious decline, because the Jewish case is genuinely distinctive. Judaism has always been both a religion and a people, and the American Jewish community has spent the last century negotiating, often unsuccessfully, what happens when those two strands separate.

Pew found that 22 percent of American Jews now describe themselves as having no religion, and among Jews under 30 the figure approaches a third. These "Jews of no religion" still mostly feel proud to be Jewish, but they marry non-Jews at very high rates, raise their children with weaker Jewish ties, and disaffiliate from institutions. This is the demographic time bomb the federations have been worrying about for a generation, and Pew confirms it is not going away.

The honest question for Jewish leaders is whether the non-Orthodox denominations have a sustainable model. Reform and Conservative Judaism in their twentieth-century forms were largely built around suburban synagogue membership, bar/bat mitzvah preparation, and a thin civil religion of Holocaust memory and Israel support. Each of those pillars is wobbling. Kids leave after bar mitzvah. Holocaust memory recedes with the survivors. Israel, once a source of unifying pride, is now a source of generational conflict, particularly among younger liberal Jews uncomfortable with the occupation and the rightward drift of Israeli politics.

What might work? Three things, none of them easy. First, real Jewish education — not Hebrew school once a week, but immersive programs like Jewish day schools, summer camps, and Birthright follow-ups. The data have been clear for decades that intensity of Jewish education predicts adult Jewish engagement better than almost anything else. Second, taking intermarried families seriously as Jewish families rather than as failures, which means dropping the defensive crouch and welcoming non-Jewish partners into Jewish life. Third, rebuilding the synagogue as something more than a fee-for-service provider of life-cycle events.

The Orthodox, of course, are doing fine by all these measures, and their share of the Jewish population is rising. One legitimate scenario is that American Judaism becomes more Orthodox and smaller, with a large penumbra of unaffiliated Jews who feel Jewish but don't do much about it. Whether that counts as failure or just transformation depends on whom you ask.

I want to push back on the entire framing of this question. The premise — that religious leaders should figure out how to "recapture" the lost sheep — assumes that the people leaving are confused, stray, or missing something they need. It treats disaffiliation as a problem to be solved by the institution rather than as information the institution should listen to.

Consider why people actually leave. Surveys consistently find that the unaffiliated cite reasons like: the church's stance on LGBT people, its handling of sexual abuse, its political entanglements, its hostility to science, its treatment of women, its perceived hypocrisy. These are not vague spiritual yearnings unmet by better programming. They are specific moral objections to specific institutional behavior.

If religious leaders take the survey seriously, the implication is uncomfortable: many of the people who left did so because their consciences told them to, and their consciences were often right. The Catholic abuse crisis is the clearest case — millions of Catholics did not drift away, they walked away in disgust, and they were correct to do so. The mainline Protestant decline is harder to diagnose, but the evangelical alignment with hard-right politics has plainly cost the broader Christian brand among younger Americans. American Judaism's institutional ties to a particular Israeli politics has, similarly, alienated parts of a generation.

So the first thing religious leaders should do is not strategize about retention but examine conscience. Where has the institution earned the exit? What would it look like to take responsibility, change behavior, and offer a credible account of why the next generation should trust it?

Second, religious leaders should accept that some of the lost sheep are not coming back, and shouldn't. People who left because they no longer believe the metaphysics, or because they found other communities, or because they reject the moral teachings, are exercising legitimate freedom of conscience. The religious freedom American institutions have benefited from cuts both ways.

What remains for congregations is to be honest, humble, and useful. Feed people. House the homeless. Sit with the dying. Teach the texts. Argue, even painfully, about what they mean. Stop trying to recapture; start trying to deserve. The sheep, it turns out, can read.

There is one religious group in America whose numbers are not declining, and any honest conversation about the Pew findings has to start there: the Orthodox, the evangelicals, the Mormons, the Pentecostals, and the various immigrant churches. What unites these otherwise disparate communities is not theology — they disagree on everything — but a willingness to make demands.

Demanding religions grow. This has been true since Dean Kelley wrote "Why Conservative Churches Are Growing" in 1972, and the four decades since have only confirmed his thesis. Communities that require something — distinctive dress, dietary restrictions, time commitments, missionary years, doctrinal assent, sexual ethics that differ from the mainstream — produce members who stay, marry inside, raise kids in the faith, and tithe. Communities that ask nothing in particular produce members who drift, marry out, raise kids in nothing in particular, and stop tithing.

The Pew data on Jewish Americans is a near-perfect illustration. Orthodox Jews under 30 are a larger share of young Jews than they were of older ones. Reform and Conservative shares are shrinking. The same retention rate gap appears in Christian denominations: Southern Baptists hold their kids better than Episcopalians, who hold theirs better than the United Church of Christ.

This is uncomfortable for theological liberals like me. I do not want to convert to Orthodoxy or to embrace evangelical positions on sexuality. But intellectual honesty requires admitting that the liberal religious project — accommodating modernity, embracing pluralism, softening the demands — has been a demographic catastrophe even as it has been, in many ways, a moral success.

Is there a way out? Possibly. The liberal religious traditions could try to find their own forms of demand: serious commitment to social justice work, real intellectual engagement with sacred texts, demanding spiritual practice, distinctive ethical witness. Reform Judaism has experimented with this, and so have certain mainline Protestant congregations, with modest success. The trick is generating thickness without theological authoritarianism.

What religious leaders should take from Pew is the courage to ask more. The instinct, when attendance drops, is to lower the barrier — shorter services, easier theology, fewer demands. The data say this fails. The communities that thrive raise the barrier and trust that some will rise to it.

Religion in America has always been a marketplace, and the Pew numbers describe a market correction more than a spiritual collapse. The supply of religious goods has not adapted to the demand of contemporary American lives, and the customers, as customers will, have taken their attention elsewhere.

Three observations on what religious leaders might take away.

First, the schedule problem is real. American work and family life in 2013 is not the life of 1955. Both parents work. Kids have travel soccer on Sunday morning. Extended families live in different time zones. The traditional Saturday or Sunday morning service is a piece of infrastructure built for a society that no longer exists. Smart congregations are experimenting with weeknight services, online streaming, modular participation. Many congregations are not.

Second, the content problem is real. A great deal of what happens in synagogues and churches is, frankly, not very good. Sermons are often mediocre. Music is often forgettable. Religious education for children is frequently worse than the secular education they get the rest of the week, which trains them to expect that religious institutions are unserious. Adults rarely encounter sustained intellectual engagement with their own tradition. If you compare the average synagogue Hebrew school to the average suburban elementary school, the comparison is humiliating. Reverse that, and retention will follow.

Third, the meaning problem is real. People are not less interested in meaning, mortality, gratitude, awe, or moral seriousness than they were fifty years ago. They are, if anything, more interested. Witness the explosion in yoga, meditation apps, therapy, twelve-step programs, secular Sabbath manifestos, philosophy podcasts, and so on. The market for what religion offers is robust. The market for how religion currently delivers it is weak.

Religious leaders should accept that they are competing for attention in a way their predecessors never had to, and the competition is not just other religions but the entire attention economy. Doing this well does not mean dumbing down — the evidence is that thin religion fails fastest. It means delivering the deep stuff in formats and at intensities that respect modern lives.

The lost sheep, mostly, did not lose interest in being fed. They lost interest in showing up to a particular pasture at a particular time. Move the pasture, or improve the grass, and at least some will come back.
