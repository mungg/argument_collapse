Media in the Age of Trump

Donald J. Trump canceled campaign credentials for news organizations that wrote critically of him. He taunted one reporter so badly the Secret Service offered to escort her out of his furious rally. He said he would expand libel laws. His staff has called the media the ”opposition party” and said they’d move journalists out of the White House. At his last news conference, his first in months, he refused to take questions from a reporter whose network ran an embarrassing story.

With a president so antagonistic to them, and disdainful of the truth, American journalism faces one of its greatest challenges. How should it respond?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The press is being baited, and the worst response would be to take the bait. Donald Trump's hostility toward journalists is not incidental to his political project; it is central to it. Every escorted reporter, every revoked credential, every "dishonest media" chant is content designed to delegitimize the institution that might constrain him. If journalism reacts emotionally — by becoming the opposition party Steve Bannon already accuses it of being — it will hand Trump exactly the foil he needs. The challenge is to respond to the threat without becoming a caricature of it.

That means, first, recommitting to old-fashioned reporting over performance. The cable-news habit of treating every Trump tweet as breaking news, of filling hours with pundits arguing about the president's mood, has been a gift to him for eighteen months. He understands attention economics better than the networks do. The corrective is not louder outrage but more shoe-leather: FOIA requests, document trails, sourcing inside agencies, financial records, deep reporting on the people Trump appoints and the policies they implement. The Washington Post's work on the Trump Foundation and David Fahrenthold's methodical accounting of promised charitable donations are a model. So is the long-form investigation of Trump's business entanglements that ProPublica and others have begun. Scoops, not shouting, are what an adversarial press owes the public.

Second, news organizations need to coordinate where they have historically competed. If the White House revokes one outlet's credentials, the others should refuse to attend the briefing. If a reporter is barred from a press conference, colleagues should yield their questions to her. The Obama administration's record-setting use of the Espionage Act against leakers, and its restrictions on photographers, already showed how an executive can squeeze the press; what's coming will be worse, and atomized outlets competing for access will be picked off one by one. A press pool that hangs together is harder to intimidate.

Third, journalists must be willing to use plain language about falsehood. The euphemistic vocabulary — "unsubstantiated," "controversial," "raised eyebrows" — was developed for a politics in which lying carried a cost. When a president-elect repeats a claim that millions voted illegally after it has been definitively debunked, calling it "disputed" is itself a distortion. The Wall Street Journal's editor recently said his paper would avoid the word "lie" because it implies intent; that caution is understandable but, applied reflexively, it amounts to laundering. Calling a lie a lie is not editorializing; it is accuracy.

Fourth, the press should resist the temptation to be the resistance. Reporters are not senators, and the proper check on Trump runs through Congress, the courts, and voters. Journalism's job is to give those actors the information they need. That means covering the new administration's accomplishments as fairly as its abuses, taking seriously the economic anxieties of the voters who elected him, and avoiding the smug coastal register that helped produce the credibility crisis in the first place. The post-election soul-searching about why so few national outlets saw Trump's victory coming should not be abandoned just because he is now in office.

Finally, the industry needs to think about its business model. Subscriptions to the Times have surged since November; that is encouraging, but a press dependent on partisan outrage for revenue will struggle to be trusted. Foundations, nonprofits, and local journalism deserve more investment than ever. A democracy without functioning local newsrooms is the soil in which contempt for the national press takes root.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The presidency of Donald Trump presents American journalism with a crisis unlike any in living memory. Not since the Alien and Sedition Acts has a chief executive so openly declared the press his enemy, seeking not merely to disagree but to delegitimize its very function. From blacklisting news organizations to proposing a loosening of libel laws, Trump’s assault is a deliberate strategy to dismantle independent verification of truth. The media’s response must be neither craven accommodation nor partisan warfare, but a steadfast recommitment to fearless reporting and rigorous fact-checking.

The most dangerous temptation is to normalize this behavior as mere political theater. When Trump cancels the credentials of The Washington Post and BuzzFeed, calls journalists “dishonest scum,” or refuses to take questions from CNN, these are acts of institutional sabotage. They signal that no account beyond his own is credible. Some have floated boycotts or pulling out of the briefing room, but such gestures risk fulfilling his narrative: that the media are an opposition party having a tantrum. The goal is not to become the story, but to report it.

Instead, the response must be a redoubling of the craft he seeks to undermine: patient, on-the-record, document-driven journalism. The Watergate era offers a template, not in heroic mythology, but in method: painstakingly cultivating sources, following the money, letting facts build a narrative that no bluster can refute. Today, that means more resources for investigative units, rigorous editorial oversight, and a clear distinction between reporting and opinion. The meticulous documentation of Trump’s tax avoidance and charitable giving are models—stories built on documents and named interviews, making cries of “fake news” hollow.

Crucially, this requires a new posture toward pervasive falsehoods. For decades, journalism operated under a “both sides” constraint, but that era is over. A president who claims with no evidence that millions of illegal votes cost him the popular vote, or who repeats the lie about Muslims celebrating on 9/11, is not in a good-faith debate. Objectivity does not require neutrality between truth and falsehood. The press must embrace the “truth sandwich”: state the truth, report on the false claim, then return to the truth. A headline like “Trump Repeats Baseless Voter Fraud Claim” is not editorializing; it is a factual statement in service of public understanding. This approach rejects the false equivalency that has too often muddied political coverage.

Yet, journalists must avoid becoming the emotional resistance. The role is not to vent moral outrage but to execute coolly. When the administration labels the media the “opposition party,” the correct response is not a defensive tweetstorm but continued, quiet investigation into ethics and policy. Let fury be felt in relentless pursuit of documents and precise prose. Authenticity is sensed in a reporter who refuses to be baited into a brawl, channeling rigor into the next story. The public can distinguish between a journalist and an activist, and will reward the former with credibility.

Finally, the response must be a public education campaign. This is not a time for insularity or professional vanity. The First Amendment is an abstraction unless citizens understand why a free press matters. News organizations must explain their methods with radical transparency—how stories are edited, fact-checked, sourced. They must make the case that attacking the fourth estate is an attack on constitutional architecture. This is not arrogance but a defense of democracy. History is harsh on societies that allowed their watchdogs to be muzzled. Our crisis is now, and the only answer is to do the work.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

The unprecedented hostility Donald J. Trump has directed at the press is not merely a test of journalistic skin thickness; it is a fundamental challenge to the role of the Fourth Estate in American democracy. As he prepares to take the oath of office, the incoming administration has made it clear that traditional norms of press relations will not apply. To navigate this new era, American journalism must respond not with reciprocal partisanship, but by recommitting to its foundational principles: radical transparency, professional solidarity, and a relentless focus on substance over spectacle.

First, the press must fundamentally rethink the value of access. For decades, Washington journalism has been overly reliant on the White House briefing room, anonymous senior aides, and off-the-record background dinners. The transition team’s threat to evict the press corps from the West Wing is intended as a punishment, but news organizations should treat it as an opportunity for emancipation. The most consequential reporting rarely comes from a podium. It is found in agency documents, court records, financial disclosures, and conversations with mid-level career civil servants. If the administration revokes credentials or attempts to freeze out critical outlets, reporters must double down on outside-in investigative journalism. Following the money and scrutinizing the implementation of policy requires shoe-leather reporting, not a front-row seat in the James S. Brady Press Briefing Room.

Second, news organizations must practice unprecedented professional solidarity. The president-elect’s strategy relies heavily on divide-and-conquer tactics, as seen in his recent press conference where he shouted down a CNN reporter while rewarding others. While past administrations have certainly favored sympathetic outlets, blatantly blacklisting reporters to evade accountability crosses a dangerous line. If the administration targets a specific journalist for running an unflattering story, the rest of the press corps must not meekly accept the next turn at the microphone. They must yield their time, ask the ignored colleague's question, and refuse to participate in the public humiliation of their peers. A united front makes it impossible for the administration to isolate and destroy the credibility of single outlets.

Third, journalists must learn to distinguish the signal from the noise. Trump has proven masterful at using his Twitter feed to manufacture outrage, dictate the daily news cycle, and distract from substantive issues. The press cannot afford to chase every late-night, 140-character provocation down a rabbit hole. Coverage must relentlessly prioritize actual governance. Instead of devoting endless panel discussions to dissecting a personal insult, newsrooms should allocate those resources to investigating the administration's sprawling global business conflicts, the implications of the emoluments clause, the actions of controversial cabinet appointees, and the real-world impacts of its legislative agenda on American citizens.

Finally, the media must recognize that the president-elect’s attacks are effective primarily because public trust in institutional journalism is already dangerously low. Earning that trust back is the only long-term defense against being marginalized as the "opposition party." This requires newsrooms to step outside their coastal bubbles, broaden their sourcing, and better reflect the socioeconomic and geographic diversity of the country. Journalists must demonstrate their value not by engaging in a personality feud with the commander-in-chief, but by clearly explaining how complex policies affect everyday lives.

The First Amendment was not drafted to protect reporters only when the government is cooperative. It was designed precisely for moments of institutional friction. By prioritizing unvarnished truth, standing together against intimidation, and keeping their eyes on the mechanics of power rather than the theater of politics, American journalists can meet this extraordinary challenge.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

The press should respond to Donald Trump neither with panic nor with performative defiance, but with a stricter version of its core mission: verify relentlessly, explain clearly, and refuse to be intimidated. The temptation will be to make the story “Trump versus the media.” That is a trap. The story is whether the public can know what its government is doing.

Trump’s hostility to journalists is not just a matter of tone. Revoking credentials, singling out reporters at rallies, threatening to “open up” libel laws, and refusing questions from disfavored outlets are efforts to raise the cost of scrutiny. They also exploit a real weakness: public trust in the press is low, and many Americans believe major news organizations are smug, partisan, or inattentive to their lives. Journalists cannot answer that by insisting on their own virtue. They have to earn trust story by story.

That begins with precision. Trump has shown an unusual willingness to say things that are demonstrably false: about crime, immigration, voter fraud, his business record, and even crowd sizes. News organizations should call falsehoods false when the evidence warrants it. Euphemisms like “questionable claim” or “different view of the facts” mislead readers. At the same time, accuracy requires discipline. In a climate of rumor, leaks and opposition research, being first is less important than being right. The publication of unverified allegations may satisfy curiosity, but it can also hand Trump a powerful example for his argument that the press is reckless.

The press should also abandon false balance. If one side says it is raining and the other says it is sunny, the job is not to quote both and stop; it is to look out the window. That does not mean becoming partisan. It means applying the same evidentiary standard to everyone in power, including Democrats, intelligence officials, activists, and anonymous sources. Skepticism must be universal or it will not be credible.

News organizations should stand together on access. If the administration excludes one outlet for unfavorable coverage, others should protest and, when appropriate, share pool reporting. Access is useful, but it is not the same as accountability. Some of the most important reporting on any administration comes from documents, courts, inspectors general, state officials, civil servants, lobbyists, businesses, and affected citizens. If briefings become propaganda theater, networks should not feel obliged to carry them live. Summarize what is newsworthy, fact-check what is false, and do not reward spectacle for its own sake.

Journalists also need to get out of Washington more often. Trump’s victory exposed a gap between many national newsrooms and large parts of the country. Covering his voters seriously does not mean romanticizing resentment or ignoring bigotry. It means understanding economic dislocation, cultural alienation, religious conviction, distrust of institutions, and the uneven effects of globalization. A press that treats citizens mainly as polling categories will miss important truths.

Finally, media companies must defend the legal and civic conditions that make reporting possible: strong libel protections, freedom of information laws, source confidentiality, and independence from government retaliation. The First Amendment is not a perk for reporters; it is a right held on behalf of the public.

The proper response to an antagonistic president is not to become his mirror image. It is to be calmer, tougher, more transparent, and more useful than ever.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The hostility Donald Trump displayed toward the press during his campaign and transition presents journalism with a profound test, but not an unprecedented one. American reporters have faced governmental antagonism before, from Nixon-era wiretaps to Reagan's dismissal of network anchors. What distinguishes the current moment is the scale of organized disdain, the explicit calls to restrict access, and the threat to expand legal liability for unfavorable coverage. The press must respond not with equally inflammatory rhetoric but with disciplined commitment to its foundational purpose: informing citizens so they can hold power accountable.

First, newsrooms should refuse to be baited into battles over access. The temptation to cover every slight, every Tweet, every rally insult will be strong, but it risks becoming the story rather than the substance of policy and governance. Journalism's obligation is to the public's need to know what its government is doing, not to the ego of the president. When access is threatened or revoked, outlets should document the restriction, explain its significance, and continue reporting through other channels. The investigative work of the Post and Sunlight Foundation during the transition, exposing internal conflicts and policy deliberations, demonstrates how rigorous reporting can proceed despite official hostility.

Second, news organizations must recommit to ironclad verification. When a public figure routinely offers easily disproven claims, the press's response cannot simply be "he lied." It must be more methodical: documentation, sourcing, contextualization. Readers need to understand not just that a statement is false but why it matters, what the accurate picture actually is, and what consequences flow from the misinformation. This is resource-intensive work that demands patience and precision over velocity.

Third, journalism must reject the frame that it is an "opposition party." The press is not a partisan actor; it is an institution with a constitutional function. Reporters and editors should be able to articulate this distinction clearly: they are not against Trump or for any party, but for the public's right to accurate information about those in power. This is not a defensive posture. It is a statement of institutional identity that transcends any individual presidency.

Finally, newsrooms should pursue collaboration over competition in covering this administration. When one outlet is barred from briefings, others should insist on pool coverage and immediate sharing. Shared standards for reporting on unverified claims, developed through professional organizations like the SPJ, would reduce the pressure on individual organizations and present a more unified press corps.

The stakes are real. But American journalism has met authoritarian impulses before. The response is not panic or partisanship; it is deeper investment in the craft that makes journalism irreplaceable.

**Cover Him Like a Mob Boss, Not a President**

For decades, the White House press corps has operated under a quiet bargain: in exchange for proximity and access, reporters offered a measure of decorum. They stood when the president entered, raised their hands politely, and treated his words — even when wrong — as the official position of the United States. That bargain has now been unilaterally torn up by the incoming administration, and journalists who fail to recognize this will spend four years getting played.

Donald Trump has already told us who he is. He has stripped credentials from outlets whose coverage displeased him. He has singled out individual reporters for harassment by his crowds. He has threatened to "open up" libel laws, which is an empty threat under the First Amendment but a telling indication of intent. His incoming chief strategist has called the press "the opposition party." The proper response to all of this is not to plead for a return to normalcy. It is to cover this presidency the way investigative reporters cover organized crime: assume nothing, verify everything, follow the money, cultivate sources inside the operation, and treat official statements as evidence to be tested, not facts to be transcribed.

This does not mean editorializing on the news pages. It means rigorous, document-driven reporting that takes seriously the possibility of corruption, self-dealing, and abuses of power, and that refuses to launder lies through "both sides" framing. When the president-elect says, falsely, that millions voted illegally, the headline should not be "Trump Claims." It should be "Trump Repeats False Claim." Reuters' Steve Adler recently told his staff to cover Washington the way Reuters covers authoritarian regimes abroad — by working sources, ignoring tantrums, and not getting pulled into the daily theater. That is exactly right.

Practically, this requires three shifts. First, news organizations must pool resources where they compete. If the White House revokes CNN's credentials, every other outlet should refuse to ask a question until they are restored. The press's leverage is collective; its weakness has been its willingness to be picked off one outlet at a time. Second, editors should stop chasing every tweet. Trump's social-media provocations are designed to consume the news cycle and drown substantive stories — about cabinet conflicts of interest, foreign entanglements, regulatory rollbacks — in noise. Third, reporters need to invest in the bureaucratic beats: the inspectors general, the FOIA officers, the career civil servants who will be the canaries in this particular coal mine.

Critics will say this approach abandons objectivity. It does not. Objectivity is a method, not a posture. It requires treating verifiable facts as facts and powerful people as accountable to them, regardless of party. What it does not require — what it has never required — is pretending that a politician's lies are merely a matter of opinion, or that access to a briefing room is worth more than the truth.

The press is not the opposition party. But it is, and always has been, an adversary of official power, on behalf of the public. The next four years will test whether American journalism remembers that. The institutions that do remember will earn back the trust they have squandered. The ones that flinch will deserve the irrelevance that follows.

**Before Blaming Trump, Look in the Mirror**

The temptation right now, among my colleagues in journalism, is to cast the incoming administration as an existential threat and ourselves as the brave guardians of truth standing against it. This narrative is comforting. It is also evasive. Before we can credibly respond to Donald Trump, we have to reckon with how badly we covered the election that produced him — and why so much of the country greeted his attacks on us with applause.

Consider what 2016 revealed. Newsroom after newsroom, including the most prestigious in the country, treated a Clinton victory as a near-certainty until the final hours. Forecasting models were presented to readers as serious analysis even as they assigned probabilities — 85 percent, 92 percent, 99 percent — that turned out to be worthless. Cable networks aired hours of empty Trump rally footage during the primaries because the ratings were good, then expressed surprise when he won the nomination. Reporters parachuting into Ohio and Pennsylvania diners produced a genre of coverage so formulaic it became a punchline. And the press spent considerably more energy parsing Hillary Clinton's email server than examining, say, the operations of the Trump Foundation, until very late in the game.

Trust in the news media, according to Gallup, hit an all-time low last September, with only 32 percent of Americans saying they have a great deal or fair amount of trust in mass media to report news fully, accurately, and fairly. Among Republicans, the figure was 14 percent. Trump did not cause this collapse. He exploited it. And he will continue to exploit it as long as substantial majorities of Americans believe — with some justification — that national journalists belong to a single political and cultural tribe, cluster in two coastal cities, and write largely for one another.

So what should journalism do? First, be honest about errors. Issue corrections prominently. When a story is wrong, say so without throat-clearing. When a prediction fails, examine why, publicly and at length. Second, diversify newsrooms — not only by race and gender, which matter, but by class, region, and political background. A press corps that contains almost no evangelicals, almost no military veterans, and almost no one who has worked in a factory or on a farm will keep being surprised by the country it covers. Third, separate news from opinion more clearly. Readers have a right to know which writers are paid to render verdicts and which are paid to report.

Fourth, and most important, resist the seductive pull of becoming "the resistance." If journalists style themselves as the political opposition, they vindicate every Trump supporter who already believes the press is rigged. The proper stance is not adversarial in the partisan sense; it is skeptical in the professional sense. Report what is verifiable. Distinguish carefully between what officials say and what is true. Treat readers — including readers who voted for Trump — as adults capable of weighing evidence, not as marks to be persuaded.

The next four years will be hard for journalism. But the way to meet the moment is not by donning capes. It is by relearning the unglamorous craft that long ago earned the press its constitutional protections — and by remembering that a free press is accountable not just to power, but to the public whose trust it has lost.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Solidarity, or Picked Off One by One**

The single most important fact about Donald Trump's war on the press is that it depends entirely on the press's willingness to be divided. When Trump pulls The Washington Post's credentials, the rest of the press corps shows up at his rallies. When he berates a CNN reporter, the Fox News correspondent gets the next question. When he punishes one outlet with frozen access, the others fill the vacuum. This is how a single politician — even a president — can systematically discipline an industry that is, on paper, far more powerful than he is. The remedy is obvious and old: solidarity.

Imagine a different scenario. The new press secretary holds his first briefing and refuses to call on a designated "opposition" reporter. Every other journalist in the room sets down their pen. No one asks a question until that reporter does. The briefing ends in silence. The story the next day is not whatever the administration wanted spun; it is the spectacle of an administration that could not get a single question answered because it tried to pick a target. Do this twice and the tactic dies.

This kind of coordination is not a fantasy. The White House Correspondents' Association has occasionally flexed collective muscle, and foreign press corps facing authoritarian governments have used similar tactics. What has historically prevented American newsrooms from acting in concert is competitive pride: the fear that a rival will scoop you while you stand on principle. That instinct is suicidal in the present environment. Trump's team understands exactly how to exploit it — by dangling exclusive interviews, leaks, and access to outlets that play along, and starving the rest. Unless news organizations make a binding commitment to refuse to be the beneficiaries of another outlet's punishment, they will be played against one another indefinitely.

Solidarity should extend beyond access. It should include shared legal defense funds for reporters facing libel suits, which Trump has telegraphed he intends to encourage. It should include collaborative investigations — the model of the Panama Papers, where more than 100 news organizations across 80 countries pooled documents and expertise, is the right one. The conflicts of interest emerging from the Trump Organization's global holdings will be impossible for any single newsroom to map. They are a natural target for cooperative reporting.

It should also include solidarity with non-traditional journalism: local papers being squeezed financially, online outlets without the legal resources of The Times, foreign correspondents whose visas may be used as leverage, and yes, even outlets the establishment press has long held at arm's length. The First Amendment does not distinguish between a Times columnist and a blogger, and the courts that will adjudicate the coming battles will not either.

There is a self-interested case for all of this. Subscription numbers at major papers have surged since the election; readers, it turns out, will pay for journalism that takes itself seriously. But solidarity is also a moral obligation, owed by the strong outlets to the weak. The Times and the Post will survive Trump. Smaller publications, freelancers, and individual reporters may not, unless the institutions with resources defend them.

A press that hangs together stands a fighting chance. A press that does not will hang separately. There is no third option.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

**Calm Down, Do Your Job, Stop Being the Story**

Watching journalists react to Donald Trump over the past eighteen months has been, frankly, embarrassing. The hand-wringing, the manifestos, the breathless declarations that this is journalism's "greatest challenge" — all of it serves Trump's interests more than it serves the public's. He wants the press to be hysterical, defensive, and self-absorbed, because a hysterical press is an easy press to discredit. The right response to his provocations is not louder outrage. It is professional composure.

Let us recover some perspective. American journalism has covered presidents who tapped reporters' phones (Nixon), prosecuted leakers more aggressively than any of his predecessors combined (Obama), lied the country into wars (Johnson, Bush), and presided over genuine crackdowns on press freedom. It survived all of them and produced extraordinary work — the Pentagon Papers, Watergate, Abu Ghraib, the NSA disclosures — in the process. Trump's tweets and rally taunts are unpleasant, but they are not, by historical standards, an unprecedented threat. Treating them as such inflates his power and distorts our sense of proportion.

The libel law threat is largely empty. New York Times v. Sullivan is settled constitutional doctrine, and a president cannot rewrite it by executive order or even by ordinary legislation. The "expansion" Trump promised on the campaign trail would require either a constitutional amendment or a Supreme Court willing to reverse half a century of precedent; neither is plausible in the near term. The credential revocations are bad form but legally unimportant, since no journalist needs a press pass to report on a public figure. Moving the press corps out of the West Wing would be petty and symbolic; it would not prevent a single story from being written.

What journalism should do, then, is something quite ordinary: report. Cover the policy decisions, not the tweets. Cover the cabinet appointments, the regulatory changes, the budget proposals, the bills moving through Congress, the foreign-policy realignments. Cover the consequences of these decisions in the lives of ordinary Americans — the steelworker, the immigrant, the small-business owner, the retiree. Cover what the federal bureaucracy is doing and not doing. Cover state governments, which will matter enormously over the next four years. Cover the world, which has a way of continuing to exist regardless of who occupies the Oval Office.

Avoid two specific temptations. The first is to make every story about how Trump is treating the press. Readers do not care, and shouldn't. The press is not the protagonist of this presidency; the country is. The second is to fact-check Trump into a stupor. Cataloguing every false statement is useful in moderation, but if it becomes the dominant mode of coverage it ceases to inform and starts to look like score-settling. Better to choose the consequential falsehoods, document them rigorously, and let the others go.

The press will be fine if it remembers what it is for. It exists to give citizens the information they need to govern themselves. It does not exist to vindicate its own dignity, to model resistance, or to demonstrate moral seriousness on Twitter. A reporter who does her job well — who breaks stories, gets facts right, treats sources fairly, and shows her work — is a more formidable adversary to a demagogue than a hundred op-eds about the death of truth. Less drama, more reporting. That is the assignment.

The relationship between the American press and the presidency has rarely been cozy, but Donald Trump has abandoned all pretense of mutual, if strained, respect. He blacklists critical outlets, incites crowds against individual reporters, threatens to gut libel protections, and elevates the “opposition party” smear to a governing philosophy. In the face of so belligerent and factually indifferent a chief executive, the only principled response is for journalism to treat the Trump administration as a hostile power that must be held to account with unflinching adversarial rigor.

This does not mean becoming a partisan cheering section for the Democrats. Rather, it means recognizing that the traditional framework of balanced access journalism is dangerously unfit for a leader who revels in falsehood. When a president can declare, against all evidence, that millions of illegal votes robbed him of the popular majority, polite interrogation will not suffice. Reporters must foreground the truth directly: call lies “lies,” not merely “disputed claims”; no longer platform unvetted propaganda under the guise of hearing both sides. The White House press corps should use its collective leverage to insist on regular, on-the-record briefings, and refuse to be sidelined if the administration tries to circumvent them with Twitter diatribes and handpicked friendly media.

Equally important, news organizations must invest in deep investigative reporting on the president’s business entanglements, the opaque machinery of his family’s empire, and the conflicts of interest that sit at the constitutional fault line of the emoluments clause. Trump’s aggressive posture toward journalism is not just rhetorical; it is an attempt to intimidate the press into self-censorship before the investigations even begin. Meeting that with timidity will only embolden him.

Some worry that such a confrontational approach will further erode public trust by making journalists appear as antagonists. But trust is not built by pretending that a fire-breathing executive is a normal statesman. Credibility comes from demonstrable fidelity to facts, and from showing citizens that watchdogs will not be cowed. The reporters hounded at Trump’s rallies—like the one offered Secret Service protection after a brutal verbal assault—need to see solidarity from their newsrooms, not nervous pleas for civility. This administration has declared war on the press’s role in a democracy. The proper answer is not retreat but a disciplined, evidence-based counteroffensive that refuses to normalize the abnormal and keeps the public informed about what an unfettered presidency looks like.

The temptation, after a campaign in which the media was branded the “opposition party,” credentials were revoked, and journalists menaced at rallies, is to meet fury with fury. But the surest way to fail in the age of Trump is to become exactly the opponent the new president wants. Journalism should resist the bait, double down on its core mission, and refuse to let Trump’s battle rhythm dictate the terms of its work.

To be clear, resisting the bait is not passive. It requires a muscular renewal of impartial, fact-driven reporting. The administration’s reflexive lying demands aggressive fact-checking, but the framing must arise from a dogged commitment to accuracy, not from a crusading spirit. When the president says something demonstrably false—for example, claiming his inaugural crowds were the largest ever—reporters should simply report the measurable truth and move on, without treating that lie as a sign of moral depravity to be lamented on the front page.
