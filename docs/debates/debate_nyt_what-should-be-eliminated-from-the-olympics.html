What Should Be Eliminated From the Olympics?

The Olympics provides a quadrennial opportunity to spend five minutes staring at activities you would never even have thought about otherwise. There are about 300 events. But if the Olympics were looking to pare itself down, where should it start? What sport should be eliminated? The one where women make themselves look like scary puppets?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

If the Olympics needs trimming, start with the events that exist mainly because their international federations are powerful, not because the competition is genuinely Olympian. Three categories stand out.

First, sports the world doesn't actually play. Modern pentathlon is a charming nineteenth-century fantasy about a cavalry officer escaping enemy lines—pistol, épée, swim, ride, run—but virtually no one outside the Olympic pipeline trains in it. Likewise, the equestrian events are essentially a contest between very rich people and their very expensive horses; the horse, arguably the better athlete, doesn't get a medal.

Second, sports whose Olympic version is plainly inferior to their real championships. Tennis has Wimbledon. Golf is being added in 2016, but the Masters will always matter more. Soccer caps its men's tournament at under-23s precisely so it doesn't overshadow the World Cup. NBA stars treat basketball gold as a nice summer souvenir. When the Olympic title isn't the title athletes most want, the Olympics is borrowing prestige rather than conferring it.

Third, the judged spectacles that drift toward kitsch. Rhythmic gymnastics and synchronized swimming—the "scary puppets" of the prompt—are demanding, but they're closer to dance competitions than to sport, and their scoring is notoriously opaque. If we keep them, we should at least ask why trampoline made it and squash, a genuinely global sport with millions of players, still hasn't.

A leaner Games would privilege events that are global, amateur-friendly, objectively judged, and whose gold medal is the pinnacle of the discipline. Most everything else is negotiable.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The Olympics, for all its grandeur, occasionally tests our patience with events that blur the line between athletic contest and vaudeville. The most deserving candidate for elimination is rhythmic gymnastics. The question’s wry jab about “scary puppets” cuts to the heart of the problem: the sport’s unnerving blend of exaggerated theatrical grimaces, contorted poses, and apparatus manipulation often resembles a Las Vegas lounge act more than a world‑class athletic endeavor.

This is not to deny the extraordinary flexibility and coordination required. But a defining feature of Olympic sport should be objective, transparent measurement—speed, distance, points scored. Rhythmic gymnastics, by contrast, is judged almost entirely on artistic impression. The scoring system invites the very subjectivity that has plagued figure skating, and the recent London Games have only deepened skepticism, with whispers of bloc judging and routine manipulation eroding its legitimacy. When medals hinge on whether a judge finds a ribbon twirl emotionally moving, competition becomes theater criticism.

The Olympics already celebrate the pinnacle of gymnastics in the artistic discipline, where power and precision are paramount. Eliminating its more sequined, expressionistic cousin would not diminish the Games; it would sharpen their focus on sports where the finish line, not a panel’s aesthetic sensibilities, determines the champion.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

While it is tempting to take the bait and dismiss synchronized swimming or rhythmic gymnastics for their theatricality, eliminating Olympic sports should be based on a more objective metric: whether the Olympic Games represents the absolute pinnacle of that sport. By that standard, men’s football (soccer) is the most glaring candidate for removal.

Unlike swimming, track and field, or gymnastics—where an Olympic gold medal is the highest honor an athlete can achieve—men’s Olympic soccer is a compromised, second-tier tournament. Because FIFA fiercely protects the prestige of its World Cup, the Olympic men's tournament is restricted to under-23 teams, with only three overage players allowed per roster. The result is a watered-down competition that inherently lacks the world's best talent. We do not get to see the likes of Cristiano Ronaldo or Lionel Messi competing at the peak of their powers here in London.

Furthermore, soccer demands a massive logistical footprint. It requires multiple stadiums across the host country and adds hundreds of athletes to the Olympic Village, straining resources for an event that football fans barely prioritize. If the committee genuinely wants to pare down the Games, it should cut sports that treat the Olympics as an afterthought.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

If the Olympics must shrink, it should not begin with the sports that look odd to people who watch them once every four years. That is a poor test. Synchronized swimming and rhythmic gymnastics may invite easy jokes about makeup and fixed smiles, but they demand strength, breath control, flexibility, timing and years of specialized training. Disliking the costume is not the same as finding a sport unworthy.

The better test is whether the Olympics are the sport’s summit. By that standard, men’s soccer is the obvious candidate for removal. The World Cup is the championship every player and country wants most. The Olympic tournament is deliberately compromised: men’s teams are largely restricted to under-23 players, with only three older exceptions, because FIFA and clubs have no interest in letting the Games rival their main property. That age limit is an admission that the Olympic event is not the best against the best.

It also consumes enormous space. A soccer tournament requires many teams, many matches and multiple stadiums, often beginning before the opening ceremony and spreading beyond the host city. For a Games already struggling with cost, sprawl and television bloat, that is a lot to devote to a secondary championship.

Women’s soccer is a harder case, because the Olympics remain one of its premier stages, and the women should not be punished for the men’s game’s excess of bigger prizes. But if the question is where to pare first, the principle should be clear: remove events that do not need the Olympics, not events that merely look strange to casual viewers. The Games should showcase excellence, not duplicate richer spectacles.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The Olympics presents a paradox: more events do not necessarily create more meaning. With roughly 300 competitions across summer and winter programs, audience attention fragments and sustained engagement becomes difficult to maintain.

If cuts become necessary, synchronized swimming warrants serious consideration. While technically demanding, the sport exemplifies two persistent Olympic problems. First, its judging system relies heavily on subjective artistic impression scores, creating vulnerabilities to controversy—the scoring scandals in 2004 and 2008 demonstrated this shortcoming. Second, competitive participation remains concentrated among a handful of wealthy nations rather than reflecting the global reach the Games should champion.

Removing synchronized swimming would streamline an increasingly bloated program while preserving sports that better demonstrate both broad international competition and objective standards of athletic achievement. The decision should rest on participation reach and judging integrity, not subjective judgments about visual appeal.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Cut the judged sports. All of them.

The Olympics should be a contest of measurable human achievement: who ran fastest, jumped highest, lifted most, scored more goals, crossed the line first. The moment a panel of judges hovers over a scorecard deciding whether one gymnast's toe-point was prettier than another's, we have left sport and entered the realm of dance recital. That includes rhythmic gymnastics (yes, the scary-puppet ribbon-twirlers), synchronized swimming, synchronized diving, dressage, and the artistic components of figure skating in the Winter Games.

I am not saying these aren't difficult. Rhythmic gymnasts are extraordinary athletes. Synchronized swimmers can hold their breath longer than most of us can hold a thought. But difficulty is not the criterion. The criterion is objectivity. Every judged sport at every Olympics produces a fresh scandal — the 2002 Salt Lake City pairs skating fiasco, persistent rumors of bloc voting in gymnastics, boxing decisions in London this week that have left commentators sputtering. (Boxing, by the way, belongs on this list too: it's nominally objective, but the scoring system is so opaque it might as well be interpretive dance with gloves.)

Compare that to the 100-meter final. Usain Bolt crosses the line; the clock stops; nobody argues. That is what the Olympics should be.

The counterargument is that the Games would lose color and television appeal without the sequins and swan dives. Maybe. But the Olympics already has plenty of color: the opening ceremonies, the human-interest packages, the flags. The competition itself should be a pure meritocracy of stopwatch and tape measure. If we keep adding subjective sports — and the IOC keeps trying, with breakdancing apparently on deck — the Games will eventually feel less like a sporting championship and more like "America's Got Talent" with national anthems.

The instinct to mock rhythmic gymnastics is lazy, and I want to push back on it before suggesting what really should go.

Rhythmic gymnastics, synchronized swimming, and the rest of the so-called "scary puppet" sports get singled out partly because they look feminine and weird to viewers raised on stick-and-ball culture. But these athletes train at the same brutal intensity as marathoners, and the events are no less arbitrary than, say, the pommel horse or the triple jump. Every Olympic sport looks ridiculous to someone who didn't grow up with it. Curling looks ridiculous. Race walking looks ridiculous. Equestrian dressage — in which rich people in top hats make horses prance — looks ridiculous. Mockery is not an argument.

What should actually be eliminated are the sports where the Olympics is plainly not the pinnacle of the discipline. Men's soccer is the obvious one: it's an under-23 tournament with a couple of overage players, and no one in the football world treats it as remotely comparable to the World Cup. Tennis has Wimbledon, the US Open, and three other majors that every player would rather win. Golf, set to return in 2016, will have the same problem. NBA stars treat Olympic basketball as a vacation with patriotic branding. When the gold medal is not the trophy an athlete most wants, the event has no business being in the Games.

The Olympics should be reserved for sports where Olympic gold is the summit — track, swimming, gymnastics, rowing, weightlifting, wrestling, the entire backbone of the ancient and modern Games. Strip out the professionals who are slumming, and you'd free up dozens of medal slots and hundreds of athlete spots for sports that genuinely need the Olympic stage to exist.

That, not synchronized swimming, is the real bloat.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

If we are going to eliminate something from the Olympics, let's start with the sports that depend on expensive equipment, private facilities, or geographic accident — the ones that are essentially competitions among rich people from rich countries.

Look at the medal table for equestrian events: Germany, Britain, the Netherlands, Sweden, the United States. Sailing: the same plus Australia and New Zealand. Modern pentathlon, with its bizarre Cold War origins and its requirement that athletes ride, fence, shoot, swim, and run, is a country club triathlon that almost no nation on earth bothers to develop. These sports persist on the Olympic program largely because European aristocrats and their institutional descendants have always sat on the IOC.

Meanwhile, sports that billions of people actually play — cricket, squash, rugby (returning in sevens form, finally), karate — struggle for inclusion. Wrestling, one of the original Olympic events accessible to anyone with a mat and an opponent, gets fewer weight classes every cycle. Baseball and softball were cut in 2005 in part because the IOC felt the major leagues didn't take it seriously enough, while dressage sails on undisturbed.

The Olympics is supposed to be a global festival. It should privilege sports that are globally played, cheap to enter, and meritocratic in the sense that talent from a Kenyan village or a Brazilian favela has some hope of reaching the podium. A horse capable of Olympic dressage costs more than most people in the world earn in a lifetime. That alone should disqualify the sport from a competition that markets itself as a celebration of universal human striving.

Cut equestrian. Cut modern pentathlon. Cut sailing down to one or two classes. Use the spots for sports the world actually plays. The Games would be leaner, fairer, and a lot more honest about what they claim to be.

The premise of the question — that the Olympics has too many events and needs paring — deserves more scrutiny than it usually gets. Why, exactly, should anything be eliminated?

The most common answer is television: there's only so much primetime, and viewers supposedly tune out during handball. But this confuses the American broadcast experience with the Olympics themselves. In Europe, handball is huge. In South Korea, archery is appointment viewing. In India, field hockey carries enormous historical weight. The Olympics is a global event, and the diversity of its program is one of its genuine virtues. Those five-minute encounters with a sport you've never seen — that the question itself describes — are arguably the best thing the Games offer. Where else would an American casually watch team handball, or a Brazilian discover the biathlon?

The second argument is cost. Host cities are buckling under the expense, and every new sport means another venue. This is real, but the solution isn't to cut sports; it's to stop demanding that every host build everything from scratch. Use existing facilities. Spread events across multiple cities or even countries. The bloat that's killing the Olympics is the bloat of construction contracts and security theater, not the bloat of having 28 sports instead of 25.

The third argument is aesthetic — that some sports are silly or ugly or unworthy. This is just taste dressed up as principle. The synchronized swimmers, the rhythmic gymnasts, the race walkers, the trampolinists: each of them represents a community of athletes who have organized themselves, developed international competition, and earned a place. Who are we, channel-surfing from our couches, to vote them off the island because they look funny?

Eliminate nothing. Let the Games be sprawling, weird, and gloriously plural. That's the point.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Eliminate boxing. It's past time.

Boxing has been part of the modern Olympics almost from the beginning, and there's a romance to it — the working-class kid with nothing but his fists, the Cassius Clay-to-Muhammad Ali origin story, the Cuban dynasty, Teófilo Stevenson refusing millions to stay an amateur. I understand the romance. I still think the sport has to go.

First, the medical case. We have known for decades that repeated blows to the head cause cumulative brain damage. The science on chronic traumatic encephalopathy in boxers — dementia pugilistica, as it was called for most of the twentieth century — is unambiguous. The Olympic version uses headgear and shorter bouts, which reduces but does not eliminate the harm, and the IOC has been moving toward removing headgear for men. An institution that piously talks about athlete welfare cannot keep sponsoring a competition whose explicit object is to concuss the opponent.

Second, the judging. Olympic boxing has been a scoring scandal for as long as anyone can remember. Roy Jones Jr. in Seoul in 1988 is the textbook case, but every Games produces more. The current London tournament has already generated disputed decisions that the AIBA has had to overturn or defend. A combat sport whose outcomes are routinely decided by visibly compromised officials offers neither the catharsis of a knockout nor the legitimacy of clean scoring.

Third, the alternatives. Wrestling, judo, taekwondo, and fencing all preserve the combat tradition of the ancient Games without the specific pathology of repeated head trauma. The Olympic movement does not need boxing to honor martial competition.

Keep the rhythmic gymnasts with their ribbons. Keep the dressage horses, even. But a sport that systematically damages its participants' brains has no place at a festival of human flourishing.

The IOC could preserve the athleticism by folding synchronized elements into a streamlined, purely technical artistic swimming discipline, but as it stands, the event is an ornate anachronism. If we want to eliminate something, let’s start by draining the makeup bowl and sending the puppet show home.

Let’s be blunt: if the Olympics is a celebration of human athletic achievement, the sport that must go is one where the primary athlete isn’t human. Equestrian events—dressage, jumping, eventing—are a fascinating collaboration between horse and rider, but they are fundamentally equine competitions. The horse provides the strength, speed, and the spectacular leap; the rider supplies guidance. No rider has ever competed on foot and won. When a medal is draped around a human neck, we all know who did the real work. This isn’t a cruel joke about equine consciousness; it’s a matter of the Olympic Charter, which envisions a festival of human sport.

Beyond the philosophical muddle, equestrian sport is the Games’ most starkly elitist holdover. The cost of purchasing, training, and transporting a world-class horse runs into the millions, effectively barring athletes from any nation without a wealthy equestrian tradition. In London, the field of dressage riders reads like a guest list for a European country estate. The sport is wildly inaccessible, requiring not just a talented body but a massive fortune. Contrast that with the democratic simplicity of running or swimming. Finally, the 2012 Games arrived amid growing unease about the training methods in dressage, where hyperflexion (“rollkur”) raises genuine ethical questions. The Olympics shouldn’t be in the business of adjudicating animal welfare controversies. For the sake of purity and equity, let the horses have their own competition, and give the Olympic slots back to athletes who show up alone.

It is time to walk—not race walk—race walking right out of the Olympics. This is the track event where athletes contort themselves into a pained, hip-swiveling shuffle while enforcing a rule that no human can obey at top speed: one foot must be on the ground at all times. The result is a farce in slow motion, a competition less about who can move fastest and more about who can best fool the judges. Every race becomes a study in concealment, as competitors push the airborne boundary until they’re two-stepping on the edge of a jog, hoping the unaided human eye misses the telltale flicker of both heels leaving the track.

The judgment of “lifting” is capricious at best. Officials with no technological assistance scan a bobbing pack and try to spot infractions that often happen in a fraction of a second. Disqualifications in the final meters crush careers on the basis of an interpreted gait, not a clear line or a clock. This turns a sport into a legal proceeding. Meanwhile, the event itself is a relic of 19th-century pedestrianism, a fad for long-distance heel-and-toe races that the modern Olympics inherited but never truly modernized. Track already has the 20 kilometer run, the marathon, the 50 kilometer—events where you can just run, as your body intends. There’s no need for a separate event gated by a stylized walk that nobody outside the track uses for anything. Eliminate race walking, and give the distance slots to an event where the fastest athlete actually crosses the line first, not the fastest actor who kept up the most convincing mince.

The call to pare down the Olympic program is understandable, but it’s wrong. The Games’ clutter of 300 events, including the “scary puppets” of synchronized swimming, is not a bug—it’s the point. The Olympics is the one moment every four years when the world’s most niche, oddball, and historically rich sports get a global stage. Shave away the events that don’t fit a modern broadcast sensibility, and you lose the very texture that makes the Games a unique cultural ritual rather than just another all-running, all-swimming championship.

Yes, race walking looks strange. Yes, rhythmic gymnastics can seem trivial. That encounter is worth more than a tidy, ratings-optimized schedule. The IOC’s bloat problem isn’t that there are too many sports; it’s that the scheduling treats every final as a prime-time obligation. Put the more esoteric events in the morning, give them a dedicated streaming channel, and let curiosity do its work. A leaner Olympics would be a poorer Olympics. The “scary puppets” deserve their five minutes of your baffled stare; it’s a tradition as venerable as the marathon.

If any sport cheapens the Olympic ideal, it’s tennis. Reintroduced as a full medal sport in 1988 after a 64-year absence, tennis has become the most flagrant example of a professional tour stop masquerading as an amateur pinnacle. For the world’s top players, an Olympic gold is a line item on a resume already bursting with Grand Slam titles and millions in prize money. In sports like track and field, swimming, and gymnastics, an Olympic gold remains the absolute zenith of achievement—the one medal that defines a career. In tennis, a Wimbledon trophy eclipses whatever happens at the All England Club’s Olympic cousin. The Olympics should showcase sports where its gold is the gold. Tennis already has its four cathedrals. It doesn’t need a fifth. Eliminate it, and let the professionals play their own circuit.

If the International Olympic Committee is truly looking to trim the fat from the Summer Games, the guiding principle should be simple: eliminate events where the outcome is decided by a panel of subjective judges rather than a clock, a measuring tape, or a scoreboard.

Boxing and traditional gymnastics at least have heavily structured point systems based on landed punches or specific physical maneuvers. But synchronized swimming and rhythmic gymnastics lean entirely on subjective interpretation of artistry. Let’s return the Olympics to measurable athletic feats and leave the choreographed pageantry to the theater.

As we learn more about the devastating, long-term effects of traumatic brain injury, the International Olympic Committee must take a hard look at its inclusion of Boxing. The Olympic Games are supposed to represent the pinnacle of human health, vitality, and physical excellence. If the Olympics must shed a sport, it should begin with modern pentathlon. Not because its athletes lack courage or skill; quite the opposite. Fencing, swimming, riding, running and shooting are each demanding. The problem is that their combination is not a sport so much as a historical joke that stopped being funny.

Modern pentathlon was designed to approximate the skills of a 19th-century cavalry officer: ride an unfamiliar horse, fight with a sword, shoot, swim and run. That origin gives it charm, but charm is not enough to justify Olympic permanence. The Games should not be a museum of aristocratic military fantasies. They should showcase sports with broad participation, intelligible stakes and a living connection to how people actually compete around the world.

Pentathlon also suffers from incoherence. In triathlon, the disciplines are at least part of a continuous endurance challenge. In decathlon, the events are variations on track-and-field athleticism. Modern pentathlon is a grab bag.
