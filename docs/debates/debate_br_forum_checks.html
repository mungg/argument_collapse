Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Lisa Miller's essay performs a useful service by puncturing the reflexive piety with which Democratic politicians have invoked "checks and balances" since January. She is right that the phrase often functions as incantation rather than analysis, and right that America's profusion of veto points has long protected entrenched wealth against popular majorities. The story she tells about health care—a century of broadly supported reform strangled in committee, killed by filibuster, hollowed out by federalism—is largely correct, and it should embarrass anyone who treats our constitutional architecture as self-evidently virtuous.

But her essay collapses two very different things under one heading, and in doing so reaches conclusions that, in the spring of 2025, are dangerous.

The two things are these. First, there is the veto-point-heavy structure of American lawmaking: bicameralism, the filibuster, committee chairs, federalism, judicial review of statutes. This is the system Miller diagnoses, and her critique is on solid ground. Comparative political science—Lijphart, Tsebelis, Hacker and Pierson—has documented for decades that the United States is an outlier in how many points of obstruction it offers organized minorities, and that this generally favors capital over labor and incumbents over reformers.

Second, there is the system of restraints on executive power: an independent judiciary, an independent civil service, congressional oversight, inspectors general, state attorneys general, a free press, the constitutional separation between the president and the agencies that prosecute crimes. These are also commonly called "checks and balances," but they do something quite different. They do not block popular legislation. They constrain the unilateral exercise of arbitrary power by a single official.

Miller's essay slides between these two senses as if they were the same problem. They are not. The Trump administration is not primarily a story about veto points frustrating a popular agenda; it is a story about executive aggrandizement against duly enacted statutes and constitutional limits. When the administration fires inspectors general en masse, impounds appropriated funds, deports people in defiance of court orders, and lets a private billionaire roam through Treasury payment systems, the "checks" that matter are not committee chairs sitting on bills. They are judges, state AGs, career lawyers willing to resign, and the residual capacity of federalism to keep blue-state institutions out of federal capture. To dismiss all of this as "doubling down on dysfunction" is to give up the firewall while the building burns.

The comparative record should give Miller more pause than it seems to. The democracies that have suffered the sharpest authoritarian erosions in the past fifteen years—Hungary, Turkey, India, arguably Israel—are mostly more majoritarian than the United States, with fewer veto points and weaker federalism. Orbán did not need to defeat a Senate filibuster to capture the Hungarian judiciary; the absence of one made his task easier. The United Kingdom, the paradigm Westminster system Miller's framework would seem to prefer, executed Brexit on a thin parliamentary majority over deep public ambivalence, with consequences few defend. The lesson is not that veto points are good. It is that the relationship between institutional thickness and democratic resilience is contingent and direction-specific: veto points that block redistribution can be the same veto points that block autocratic consolidation.

This matters for Miller's reform agenda, much of which I support. Abolishing the filibuster, ending the Electoral College, expanding the House, curbing gerrymandering, regulating money in elections, allowing term limits on judges—these are good ideas, and they target the obstructive function of our system rather than the restraining function. But notice that her own list quietly concedes the distinction: she wants to "clarify the scope of executive power," which is to say, strengthen a check. A genuinely majoritarian system in which the president, having won 49.8 percent of the vote, faced no judicial review and no federalist friction would not be a more democratic system. It would be the system Viktor Orbán built.

Miller's deepest move is to identify mass mobilization itself as a check on power, and here she is genuinely illuminating. The New Deal, civil rights, the labor movement—these did more to discipline American elites than any constitutional provision. A democratic politics that mobilizes broad majorities around tangible material interests is, she rightly insists, what "good, broad-based politics" looks like, and it is the only thing that has ever durably overcome elite obstruction.

But mass movements take years to build, and they require the institutional space in which to operate: courts that will enjoin retaliatory prosecutions, states that will protect organizers, universities and unions and newspapers that are not yet captured. The civil rights movement she invokes did not flourish in spite of federalism; it exploited federalism, finding refuge in northern states and federal courts when local majorities were hostile. Mass politics and institutional checks are complements, not substitutes.

The honest synthesis is this: reform the veto points that strangle legislation, defend the restraints that constrain executives, and build the movements that can do both. Miller has written half of that essay brilliantly. The other half—why, in May 2025, the courts and the states and the inspectors general still matter—needs writing too.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Lisa L. Miller’s bracing indictment of American checks and balances arrives with the force of a gale-force wind. She is right to expose how the system’s multiple veto points have routinely empowered the few to block the many, turning the Framers’ vision of restrained government into a machine for minority rule. Her historical narrative—from the AMA’s sabotage of health care reform to the filibuster’s role in preserving racial hierarchy—is a necessary corrective to the sanitized civics textbook story. And her call for a revitalized mass politics that forces institutional reform is urgent and welcome. Yet, at this perilous moment in May 2025, the case for bypassing checks and balances risks discarding the very tools that, however imperfect, are currently holding back an administration bent on dismantling democratic norms.

The flaw is not in Miller’s diagnosis of elite capture, but in a prescription that too hastily assigns responsibility to the architecture of government itself, as if the structure were inherently oligarchic rather than one that can be made more or less democratic depending on who wields it. The fact is, checks and balances are not a fixed monolith. They are a set of levers that, depending on the balance of organized power, can either entrench the few or protect the many. During the current administration, those levers are being pulled by state attorneys general, federal judges, and civil society organizations to block the dismantling of public services, the gutting of regulatory protections, and the lawless actions of DOGE. When a district judge halts an executive order that would strip health coverage from millions, or when California and other states refuse to enforce unconstitutional immigration directives, those are not nostalgic appeals to a golden age—they are contemporary acts of democratic self-defense. Calling them part of a “dead end” narrative risks demobilizing the very coalitions that need to hold the line right now.

Miller’s own reform agenda implicitly acknowledges that the solution lies not in abolishing checks and balances but in recalibrating them. Ending the filibuster, expanding the House, eliminating the Electoral College, and amending judicial tenure all aim to make the system more responsive to majorities without removing the institutional friction that can sometimes guard against sudden authoritarian capture. Those reforms would still leave the United States with an unusually fragmented government by comparative standards, but they would tilt the playing field away from narrow interests. In other words, the problem isn’t that checks exist; it’s that they’ve been rigged to favor those who can afford the most influence. The answer is to democratize those checkpoints, not to treat them as permanently anti-democratic.

The historical evidence that Miller marshals is powerful, but it cuts both ways. The same Jim Crow filibusters that she cites were eventually defeated by the Civil Rights Act of 1964—a legislative achievement that required overcoming multiple veto points, yes, but that also relied on the Supreme Court’s check on state-sponsored segregation in *Brown v. Board of Education* and on the executive branch’s enforcement powers. These were not victories against the system; they were victories *through* a system that, under sustained pressure from mass movements, bent toward justice. Similarly, the New Deal and Great Society programs she rightly celebrates were enacted during periods of unified government, but they also survived constitutional challenges precisely because the Supreme Court eventually validated the national government’s authority to address economic crises. That Court was itself a check that had to be won over, not abolished.

The danger of framing checks and balances primarily as a narrative trap is that it underestimates how much the current authoritarian threat depends on discrediting any institution that can say “no” to the executive. Trump’s open flouting of court orders, his denigration of Congress as a rubber stamp, and his allies’ attacks on “deep state” bureaucrats all aim to remove friction from the system. In this context, defending the principle that power must be accountable to law—even when the law is imperfect and the courts are inconsistent—is not a sentimental attachment to a failed order. It is a direct challenge to the logic of executive supremacy. Majorities will only be able to reclaim democratic control if they first preserve the institutional infrastructure that makes counter-mobilization possible: an independent judiciary, subnational governments that retain some autonomy, and a legislature that, however malapportioned, still has the constitutional tools to investigate and push back.

Miller’s vision of a mass democratic politics is inspiring and necessary. But that politics cannot be conjured from thin air; it needs breathing room. The urgent task is to use the checks we have—the courts, federalism, the First Amendment protections that allow protest and press to function—to slow the assault on the rule of law long enough for deeper organizing to take root. That means, yes, making the case for structural reform while simultaneously blocking the worst excesses of the current administration through the very veto points that have often served elite ends. The story of American democracy is not a straight line from oligarchy to popular rule; it is a zigzag in which the same institutional features that once protected planters or industrialists were later turned, by movements and strategic litigation and electoral victories, into shields for workers, minorities, and dissidents. The system’s engineering is not destiny; its operation depends on the political forces animating it.

In 2025, it would be a tragic mistake to abandon the defensive power of institutional checks just when they are most needed. The better path is to democratize them relentlessly while recognizing that, for all their flaws, they remain the bridges that can carry us from a moment of grave peril to the more open, responsive politics Miller rightly envisions. The goal isn’t to resurrect a mythical constitutional order but to keep the rudder in democratic hands long enough to steer toward a new one.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Lisa L. Miller’s diagnosis of the American constitutional system is as compelling as it is sobering. By stripping away the romanticized veneer of our system of checks and balances, she correctly identifies a fundamental feature of our government: an abundance of veto points that disproportionately empowers entrenched, wealthy elites to block popular reforms. She is entirely right that the graveyard of American social policy—most notably the failure to secure universal healthcare—has been dug by special interests exploiting the Senate, the filibuster, and an increasingly reactionary Supreme Court. However, while Miller’s historical diagnosis is acute, her prescription—to effectively abandon our reliance on institutional checks in favor of unfiltered majoritarianism—misjudges the profound and immediate peril of the political reality we occupy in May 2025.

Miller asks us to stop looking to the courts and progressive federalism to stall the abuses of Donald Trump’s second administration, arguing that doubling down on these "veto points" only entrenches a broken system. Instead, she advocates for mass mobilization and sweeping structural reforms, such as eliminating the Electoral College and stripping power from the Senate. These are worthy long-term aspirations. But in the present moment, asking Americans to relinquish their remaining institutional shields while an authoritarian-leaning executive occupies the Oval Office is akin to demanding a city dismantle its fire department because the hoses are outdated, right as a massive inferno sweeps through the streets.

The central flaw in Miller’s argument is the unexamined assumption that a streamlined, majoritarian political system will naturally default to progressive, public-minded outcomes. She writes that "mass publics already support economic and social policy reforms that would benefit the vast majority of Americans," and suggests that cross-racial, progressive majorities are merely waiting to be unleashed from the chains of institutional restraint. This is a comforting thought, but it glosses over the deep, often reactionary populist currents that animate nearly half the American electorate.

If the guardrails of federalism, judicial review, and bicameralism were suddenly lowered today, there is no guarantee that the result would be a new New Deal. A system stripped of its veto points is highly efficient, but efficiency is morally neutral. In the hands of a right-wing populist coalition, an unimpeded federal government could rapidly enact nationwide abortion bans, dismantle federal civil rights enforcement, or execute draconian mass deportation programs without the friction of the courts or the Senate to slow them down. We do not have to look far to see the dangers of unchecked majoritarianism; we need only look at how effectively single-party control in states like Florida and Texas has been used to roll back minority rights, academic freedom, and bodily autonomy.

Furthermore, Miller’s dismissal of federalism overlooks its critical defensive function in our current crisis. She rightly points out that "states’ rights" has a dark, brutal history, having been weaponized by Southern elites to defend slavery and Jim Crow. But institutions are not frozen in their historical origins; their valence changes depending on who holds power. In 2025, federalism is the primary mechanism preventing the Trump administration from fully nationalizing its agenda. When California Governor Gavin Newsom and other blue-state leaders invoke federalism, they are not protecting oligarchy; they are sheltering immigrants, defending environmental regulations, and preserving reproductive healthcare access for millions of Americans. To abandon the legal and political leverage of federalism now, merely because it was used for evil a century ago, is to disarm ourselves in the face of an administration that has made clear its desire to bypass Congress and govern by executive fiat.

Miller correctly observes that Trump is relying heavily on executive orders to push his unpopular agenda, particularly through Elon Musk’s extra-governmental Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE). She uses this to argue that traditional checks and balances are failing. But the appropriate response to aggressive executive overreach is not to abandon the concept of checks and balances; it is to use the remaining checks—namely, the courts and state-level resistance—to challenge those executive actions. Yes, the current Supreme Court has a strong conservative tilt, but federal courts remain one of the few venues where executive overreach can be paused or struck down on procedural grounds. Even an imperfect, conservative-dominated judiciary can serve as a speed bump against outright kleptocracy.

There is also a profound practical paradox in Miller’s roadmap. She argues that the multitude of veto points makes it impossible to pass even basic legislation like minimum wage increases. Yet, her proposed solutions—constitutional amendments to eliminate the Electoral College, democratize the Senate, or institute citizens’ assemblies—require overcoming the highest, most formidable veto points in the entire American constitutional architecture. If we cannot pass a public health insurance option through a simple legislative majority, how can we possibly expect to achieve the two-thirds congressional supermajorities and three-fourths state ratification required to amend the Constitution?

By setting her sights on total constitutional overhaul, Miller risks promoting a fatalism about the tools we currently possess. Mass mobilization and institutional maneuvering are not mutually exclusive; in fact, they must be inextricably linked. The Civil Rights movement, which Miller cites, did not succeed merely by mobilizing mass majorities to change the Constitution. It succeeded through a masterful, pragmatic combination of street-level mobilization, legislative pressure, and highly strategic litigation that utilized the very federal courts Miller now views with skepticism.

None of this is to say that we should romanticize the status quo. The American system is deeply flawed, biased toward inaction, and structurally tilted to favor the wealthy and geographically dispersed rural populations over diverse, urban majorities. The anger that voters feel toward a government that cannot deliver on basic promises is justified, and Miller is right that Democrats must offer a bold, proactive vision for how government can work for ordinary people, rather than simply defending the procedural norms of the past.

But as we navigate the treacherous waters of 2025, we must be clear-eyed about the weapons available to us. Checks and balances were designed by the Framers with a pessimistic view of human nature, built on the assumption that leaders would inevitably seek to aggrandize power and that majorities could sometimes become mobs. That pessimism feels uniquely appropriate today. The friction in our system is frustrating when we seek rapid progress, but it is essential when we are trying to prevent rapid democratic backsliding. In the long run, we must certainly build a mass movement to reform our calcified institutions. But in the short term, those imperfect, frustrating institutions are the only bulwarks standing between the republic and the authoritarianism at our doorstep.

Vanilla GPT-5.5

Lisa Miller is right that reverence for “checks and balances” often functions as a substitute for thought. In American political rhetoric, the phrase has become a civic lullaby: if institutions are divided, liberty is safe; if courts exist, rights are protected; if states retain authority, tyranny is restrained. That story is plainly inadequate to the present crisis. A constitutional system that makes it easy to block popular legislation and hard to hold anyone accountable is not an immune system. It is often an incubator for cynicism, oligarchy, and executive adventurism.

But the conclusion should not be that checks and balances are a dead end. The problem is not checking power as such. The problem is that the United States has built too many of the wrong checks, lodged them in the wrong places, and deprived ordinary people of the organized power needed to make those checks democratic. A better response to Trumpism requires both halves of the argument: more majoritarian capacity for government to act, and stronger democratic constraints on officials, parties, judges, police, billionaires, and presidents.

Miller’s strongest point is that inaction is not neutral. When Congress fails to raise the minimum wage, guarantee paid leave, protect voting rights, or create universal health care, power does not disappear. Employers, insurers, landlords, creditors, fossil fuel companies, and state officials continue to govern people’s lives. The American habit of treating public authority as uniquely dangerous and private authority as merely “freedom” has always tilted the constitutional imagination toward those who already possess wealth and leverage. In that sense, veto points are not just procedural inconveniences; they are distributive institutions. They decide who can stop whom.

This is especially clear in health care. The United States did not fail to achieve universal coverage because Americans uniquely love medical bankruptcy or because doctors, insurers, and hospitals are powerful only here. It failed because organized opponents could repeatedly exploit committees, party divisions, federalism, courts, and the Senate’s supermajoritarian norms. Even the Affordable Care Act, a major achievement, was shaped by the need to survive this gauntlet: private insurance was preserved, the public option abandoned, Medicaid expansion routed through the states, and then the Supreme Court made that expansion optional. A system with so many vetoes does not reliably produce moderation. It often produces incoherence.

Nor is there any honest way to tell the history of federalism without acknowledging its role in protecting racial hierarchy. “States’ rights” has sometimes meant local experimentation, but it has also meant the local denial of citizenship. From slavery to Jim Crow to resistance against voting rights enforcement, state authority has repeatedly allowed dominant local elites to deprive minorities of national guarantees. The same remains true in less dramatic but still consequential ways when state lines determine access to Medicaid, abortion, labor rights, public education, or clean water.

So Miller is right to reject nostalgia. The pre-Trump constitutional order was not a golden age of well-functioning republican restraint. It was a system in which many people experienced government as distant, captured, and incapable. Trump’s promise to smash institutions gains force because so many institutions have failed to deliver.

Yet it matters greatly how they are smashed. Trump’s attack on courts, civil servants, prosecutors, inspectors general, universities, immigrants, journalists, and political opponents is not a democratic revolt against elite vetoes. It is an effort to replace pluralistic government with personal rule, surrounded by loyalists and allied with private wealth. If that project succeeds, ordinary people will not gain power over oligarchs. They will lose the remaining tools by which oligarchs and rulers can be investigated, sued, exposed, regulated, and defeated.

That is why the language of checks and balances, though insufficient, cannot simply be discarded in the current moment. When an administration defies court orders, purges independent officials, threatens opponents, or uses the machinery of government for retribution, the immediate question is not whether the Senate is malapportioned or whether judicial review has often served elites. Those questions remain urgent. But the immediate democratic need is also to preserve lawful constraint on executive power. A court order protecting due process is not the same kind of “veto point” as a filibuster blocking a voting rights bill. A civil service rule preventing partisan purges is not the same as a committee chair burying health legislation. An inspector general investigating corruption is not equivalent to the Electoral College overriding the popular vote.

The task, then, is to distinguish democratic checks from anti-democratic vetoes. The United States does not suffer from too much accountability. It suffers from too little accountability combined with too much obstruction. The Senate can block legislation while representing a minority of the population. The Supreme Court can invalidate acts of Congress while its members serve for life and are selected through a politicized, countermajoritarian process. The Electoral College can elevate the loser of the national popular vote. Gerrymanders can insulate legislators from voters. The filibuster can turn minority rule into routine governance. These are not checks that empower the people. They are mechanisms that blur responsibility and protect entrenched power.

By contrast, democracies need institutions that check domination: independent election administration, enforceable voting rights, transparent campaign finance, anti-corruption rules, legislative oversight, professional public administration, labor rights, free media, and courts capable of protecting basic liberties. Other democracies with more parliamentary or majoritarian systems do not lack checks. They often have stronger parties, clearer accountability, proportional representation, more robust social rights, independent bureaucracies, and easier paths for legislative majorities to enact programs. Their governments can act, and voters can punish them for what they do. That combination is healthier than the American pattern: government fails, no one is clearly responsible, and demagogues blame shadowy enemies.

Miller also rightly emphasizes mass politics. The great democratizing changes in American history did not come from institutional benevolence. Abolitionists, labor organizers, suffragists, civil rights activists, disability rights advocates, feminists, immigrant organizers, and LGBTQ movements forced institutions to move. But mass politics itself needs institutional channels. Movements can open possibilities; parties and legislatures turn them into durable law. If the channels are too clogged, movements burn out or turn anti-system. If the channels are too unconstrained, electoral victories can become plebiscitary mandates for repression. Democracy requires mobilization and mediation.

This is where the defense of vulnerable minorities needs more care than Miller sometimes gives it. She is right that “minority rights” rhetoric has often protected the wealthy few. But some minorities really are vulnerable, and majoritarian politics does not automatically protect them. Public opinion has moved dramatically toward equality in many areas, but it remains uneven, especially when fear is mobilized against immigrants, Muslims, trans people, prisoners, protesters, or the very poor. The answer is not to empower judges as philosopher-kings or to let elite institutions define rights without democratic input. But neither is it enough to trust broad majorities. A democratic constitution should make it easier for majorities to govern on social and economic policy while making it harder for any majority—or president—to strip persons of equal citizenship, bodily liberty, speech, counsel, or the vote.

That suggests a reform agenda close to Miller’s, but framed less as abandoning checks and balances than as democratizing them. Abolish or radically limit the filibuster. Protect voting rights nationally. End partisan gerrymandering. Expand the House. Replace the Electoral College with a national popular vote. Give Congress authority to regulate money in politics. Impose term limits and ethics rules on Supreme Court justices. Make it easier for Congress to override certain judicial invalidations by supermajority or subsequent election, while preserving strong protections for core political rights. Strengthen inspectors general, whistleblower protections, and congressional oversight. Rebuild unions and civic organizations so ordinary people are not represented only as isolated voters in a money-drenched media environment.

The near-term and long-term strategies must reinforce each other. In 2025, it is necessary to use courts, states, Congress, civil society, universities, unions, journalists, and local officials to resist authoritarian action. That does not mean sanctifying every feature of American constitutionalism. It means using the tools available to prevent personal rule while building the power to replace dysfunctional institutions with better ones.

The old civics-book story is false: separated powers have not reliably saved American democracy. But a democracy without checks is not democracy either. The goal should be a constitutional order in which government can actually deliver what majorities vote for, rights do not depend on geography or presidential grace, and concentrated wealth cannot hide behind procedural fragmentation. We do not need fewer restraints on power in general. We need restraints that bind the powerful, not the public.

The lead essay offers a compelling diagnosis of how America's constitutional architecture systematically frustrates democratic accountability. Miller correctly identifies that veto points in our system empower economic elites to block popular reforms, and she rightly connects this to the rise of authoritarian populism. Yet the essay's proposed remedy — mass mobilization for structural reform — underestimates how thoroughly the very mechanisms she critiques have already reshaped political consciousness in ways that complicate the path forward.

Miller's historical examples are instructive. The failure of health care reform, the obstruction of civil rights legislation, and thefilibuster's perpetual thwarting of majority will all demonstrate how concentrated minorities exploit institutional friction. The ACA's tortured journey through veto points, emerging as a compromised patchwork rather than comprehensive reform, exemplifies the dynamic precisely. These are not incidental glitches but structural features working as designed.

But here the argument becomes more complicated. Miller concludes that "democracy erodes from the top," emphasizing elite failure over mass majoritarianism. This is partly right. Yet the Trump phenomenon illustrates something more nuanced: it is precisely the perception that formal democracy has failed ordinary people that fuels support for anti-democratic movements. When rural and working-class Americans experience a political system that consistently delivers outcomes contrary to their interests, they become susceptible to leaders who promise to "drain the swamp" while actually serving plutocratic ends. The grievance is legitimate even when its political expression is dangerous.

Miller correctly warns against championing federalism as a bulwark against authoritarianism, given its history as a shield for segregation and reaction. The brutal calculus of Southern resistance to civil rights enforcement, blocking anti-lynching legislation and voting rights protections through Senate obstruction, demonstrates how decentralization can protect oppression rather than liberty. This historical point deserves emphasis in an era when some advocate state-level resistance to federal overreach.

However, the essay's implicit optimism about mass mobilization's transformative potential warrants scrutiny. The same structural obstacles Miller documents — voter suppression, gerrymandering, differential turnout capacity, corporate dominance of political finance — systematically disadvantage the very constituencies most likely to support democratic reform. The filibuster, Senate malapportionment, and lifetime judicial appointments persist precisely because the institutional pathways for their elimination require navigating the same veto points they would reform. Structural reform from within a structure designed to prevent structural reform poses genuine chickens-and-eggs problems.

Moreover, Miller's suggestion that we might amend our way toward a more democratic system understates current barriers to constitutional change. The amendment process requires supermajorities that the essay's analysis suggests are precisely what our system prevents. Proposals like eliminating the Electoral College or reducing Senate power face the same obstacle as any reform: those empowered by current arrangements control the tools of preservation.

This suggests that mobilization alone, while necessary, may be insufficient without first addressing how the existing distribution of political resources shapes whose voices are heard in democratic discourse. The intuition is plausible. But Canada, which Miller implicitly contrasts with the U.S., has a Westminster system layered onto a federal structure with strong provincial autonomy over health policy. Germany, often celebrated for Bismarckian universal coverage, has a federal structure, a powerful Bundesrat that operates as a second chamber with real veto authority, and a Constitutional Court whose activism makes John Roberts look restrained. Switzerland has more veto points than the United States by almost any measure—a collegial executive, mandatory referenda, deep cantonal federalism—and yet provides universal coverage. The proliferation of veto points is not destiny.

Eliminate the filibuster and they will gerrymander harder. Expand the House and they will weaponize state legislatures. The fundamental problem is asymmetric polarization combined with a media ecosystem that rewards intransigence.

The Seventeenth Amendment is perhaps the only clear example, and its long-run effects on Senate accountability are contested. The structural reforms Miller wants—eliminating the Electoral College, neutering the Senate, ending judicial life tenure—are precisely the sort of changes that the existing structure makes impossible. So her program is not a program; it is a wish.

This is the recurring fantasy of majoritarian reformers: that beneath the institutional brambles lies a coherent popular will waiting to be liberated. But polling consistently shows that Americans hold contradictory preferences. They want lower taxes and more services. Veto points—at their best—force that work to happen.

The PATRIOT Act passed quickly through a panicked Congress. The Iraq War authorization sailed through with bipartisan supermajorities. Veto points slow down both good change and bad change. The relevant question is whether, on average, the changes that get blocked are worse than the changes that get through. That is a deeply uncertain empirical question, and Miller's confidence that more action would mean better action is unwarranted.

This is not a contingent feature of American government; it follows logically from the unequal distribution of political resources. Expand the House and the same gerrymandering technologies will be deployed in new districts. It means the kind of structural reform that the New Deal coalition fought for and that has been steadily unwound since the 1970s.

Miller's frame of "checks and balances" versus "mass democracy" obscures this. The genuine alternative to elite checks on government is not just popular mobilization; it is popular institutions powerful enough to check elites in their actual habitat, which is the workplace, the housing market, and the boardroom, not just the Capitol. The American left's century-long fascination with constitutional politics—first with using courts to advance civil rights, now with reforming the Constitution to make space for redistribution—has consistently underestimated how much political democracy depends on prior economic democratization.

This is too quick, both as history and as institutional analysis. Federalism is a structure, not a politics. It can be used by reactionaries to entrench local hierarchies, and it has been—Miller's examples are not wrong, just selectively chosen. But it can also be used by progressives to build the political experiments and material bases of power that later become national policy. The history of American social democracy, such as it is, runs through Wisconsin's progressive era reforms, New York's Triangle Shirtwaist aftermath, California's environmental regulations, Massachusetts's health insurance experiment that became the ACA template, and dozens of state-level innovations on labor standards, minimum wages, family leave, voting rights, and climate policy. Strip out subnational policy experimentation and the American welfare state would be drastically smaller than it is, not larger.

This is not a quirky historical accident. It reflects a basic fact about democratic politics: in a country as large, diverse, and economically heterogeneous as the United States, no single national majority for any specific policy reform exists in the abstract. Such majorities have to be built, and building them generally requires demonstration projects. Workers' compensation existed in dozens of states before it existed federally. Women voted in Wyoming before they voted nationally. Same-sex marriage was legal in Massachusetts a decade before Obergefell. The federalism that enables Mississippi to be Mississippi also enables California to be California, and the model that California provides is part of how national politics shifts.

Miller's response is that this argument "obscures the many economic experiences and preferences that Americans have in common." But the formulation begs the question. If those common preferences existed in politically actionable form, the federal government would already be enacting them, regardless of veto points. The reality is that policy preferences are heavily mediated by local political culture, partisan identification, regional economy, and trust in institutions. National policy on contentious questions almost always tracks behind subnational policy precisely because the national level requires aggregating across these differences. Eliminating that aggregation requirement does not produce a coherent national majority for progressive reform; it produces a coherent national majority for whichever coalition happens to control the federal government at a given moment. In 2025, that coalition is the one led by Donald Trump.

This brings me to the present crisis. Miller writes the federalism section as if the choice were between embracing state authority as a constitutional principle or rejecting it. But this is not how federalism is functioning right now. Right now, state attorneys general in California, New York, Massachusetts, Illinois, and a dozen other states are litigating to block illegal federal actions on immigration, environmental regulation, education funding, and civil rights enforcement. State election officials, sheriffs, and local prosecutors are refusing to cooperate with federal directives they regard as unlawful. Sanctuary jurisdictions are protecting immigrant residents from deportation operations. State legislatures in blue states are passing legislation to protect reproductive rights, gender-affirming care, voting access, and labor standards in defiance of federal hostility.

This is not the abstract veto of the national public interest. It is the concrete check on a specific federal administration that is testing the limits of authoritarian governance. To reject federalism in principle while accepting these specific defenses is incoherent. The same constitutional structures that allowed Mississippi to nullify civil rights enforcement in 1960 allow California to nullify mass deportation in 2025. You cannot have one without the other, and a reformer who proposes to eliminate state authority over federal cooperation should be honest about what she is also eliminating.

Miller's analogy to the antebellum slaveholders' constitutional arguments is striking but misleading. The slaveholders did not just want state authority; they wanted the federal government to actively enforce their interests against free states, as the Fugitive Slave Act demonstrates. The Confederacy was not a federalist project; it was a project of national supremacy in defense of slavery that turned to secession only when national supremacy could no longer be guaranteed. Reading the history of American federalism as primarily a story of reactionary state-level resistance to progressive national policy gets the directionality wrong as often as it gets it right. The federal government has been the source of progressive policy at some moments and the source of reactionary policy at others, and the same is true of states. A constitutional reformer who wants to centralize authority is making a bet about which level of government will be more progressive in the long run, and there is no good empirical basis for that bet.

This connects to a broader analytical point. Miller treats "ordinary Americans" as a relatively coherent political category whose preferences are systematically blocked by elite-controlled veto points. But "ordinary Americans" is not a unitary actor. Working-class voters in West Virginia have different policy preferences than working-class voters in Vermont, and these differences are not just artifacts of elite manipulation. They reflect genuine differences in local economies, religious traditions, family structures, and lived experience. The institutional question of federalism is, in part, a question about how to accommodate genuine political pluralism within a single polity. Centralizing authority does not eliminate pluralism; it imposes one set of preferences on dissenting communities, with the political costs that follow.

None of this is to defend the status quo against reform. The Senate is genuinely malapportioned in ways that are democratically indefensible. The Electoral College is an embarrassment. Lifetime tenure for federal judges is poor institutional design. These reforms are worth fighting for. But "federalism" is not on the same list. Federalism is not a discrete institution to be reformed; it is a structural feature of a continental democracy with profound regional heterogeneity. Eliminating it is neither possible nor desirable. Improving it—through better protection of voting rights, fairer redistribution of fiscal resources across states, stronger national floors on civil and economic rights—is the actual progressive agenda.

Miller's essay would be stronger if it focused on those reforms rather than dismissing federalism as such. The current moment requires defending the institutional structures that are actually slowing authoritarian consolidation, even while critiquing the structures that enable elite obstruction of progressive policy. These are not the same structures, and conflating them is bad analysis and worse strategy.

Historically, she writes, "federal courts have often served elite interests," and in recent decades they have "weakened congressional legislation" across the policy spectrum. The historical claim is partially correct but considerably more complicated than Miller suggests, and the strategic conclusion does not follow from the history. Let me make the case for a more nuanced view of judicial review—not as a savior, but as one institutional tool among many that progressives abandon at their peril, especially right now.

Miller is right that the Lochner-era Court was a brutal obstacle to labor regulation and the early welfare state, and that the Roberts Court has weakened voting rights, campaign finance regulation, environmental law, and reproductive rights. These are facts. But the framing of "courts versus democracy" obscures what courts actually do in a system like ours. The Supreme Court does not simply block democratic majorities; it adjudicates between conflicting democratic claims, between federal and state authority, between current majorities and constitutional commitments made by past majorities, between individual rights and collective interests. Some of these adjudications are deeply objectionable. Others are essential.

Consider the period Miller dismisses as anomalous. From roughly 1937 to 1971, the federal judiciary was a critical instrument of progressive change. It dismantled Jim Crow when Congress could not (Brown), expanded criminal defendants' rights against state abuse (Gideon, Miranda), protected free speech against McCarthyite repression (Brandenburg), guaranteed reproductive autonomy (Griswold, Roe), enforced voting rights (Reynolds, the early Voting Rights Act cases), and protected the press against government suppression (Sullivan, Pentagon Papers). Was this just elite policymaking? Some of it, perhaps. But the alternative—leaving these questions to state legislatures dominated by white supremacists or to a Congress structurally biased toward rural and Southern interests—would have produced vastly worse outcomes for the marginalized minorities Miller claims to want to protect.

Miller's framework collapses the distinction between two different things federal courts can do: enforce constitutional rights against state and local majorities that violate them, and override federal legislation enacted by Congress. The first is what we usually mean by "rights protection" and is largely uncontroversial as a principle of constitutional democracy, even if controversial in specific applications. The second is what we usually mean by "judicial activism" and is genuinely contestable. The Warren Court was mostly doing the first. The Roberts Court is mostly doing the second. These are different activities with different normative valences, and treating them as a single phenomenon called "judicial review" produces analytical mush.

This matters strategically because in May 2025, federal courts are the only institution that has meaningfully constrained the Trump administration's most flagrant abuses. Judges across the ideological spectrum have blocked unconstitutional executive orders on birthright citizenship, paused illegal deportations under wartime statutes, ordered the reinstatement of unlawfully fired federal employees, and protected federal funding from impoundment. Some of these decisions have been ignored by the administration—Miller is right about that—but most have not, and even the ones that have been ignored impose political and legal costs that constrain future actions.

If Miller's prescription is to discard the courts as a meaningful site of struggle and place all our hopes in mass mobilization, she should explain what we should be doing right now, while mass mobilization is being built, to constrain an authoritarian president. The answer cannot be "wait for the social movement." Authoritarian consolidation works on a faster timescale than that. Litigation, court orders, and the legitimacy of judicial review are part of the toolkit that allows other forms of resistance—including mass mobilization—to operate at all. Take away the courts and dissident speakers face longer prison sentences, protest organizers face more aggressive prosecution, and the political space for mass politics narrows.

There is a deeper issue here about the relationship between law and politics. Miller writes as if law is essentially epiphenomenal—a reflection of the underlying balance of political forces, mediating elite interests through the language of rights. This view has a long pedigree on the left, from Marxist legal theory through critical legal studies. It contains real insight. But it underestimates the relative autonomy of legal institutions and the way legal norms, once internalized by enough actors, can constrain even the powerful. The reason Trump has not simply ordered the arrest of his political opponents is not because he respects them; it is because too many actors in the apparatus of government still believe they are bound by legal norms. Those norms are sustained, in part, by the institution of judicial review. Erode the institution and you erode the norms.

This is not an argument for complacency about the federal judiciary. The Roberts Court is a problem. The Trump appointments to the lower federal courts have shifted the judiciary rightward in ways that will take a generation to undo. Court reform—jurisdictional reforms, term limits for justices, expansion of lower court capacity, ethics enforcement—is urgent and overdue. Some forms of court expansion may be necessary, though the politics is treacherous. But "court reform" is different from "court abandonment," and Miller's essay slides too easily between them.

The deeper lesson of American political history is not that mass mobilization makes courts irrelevant, but that mass mobilization and constitutional litigation work together. The civil rights movement of the 1950s and 1960s was both a mass movement and a sophisticated litigation campaign coordinated by the NAACP Legal Defense Fund. The marriage equality movement combined state-level political organizing with carefully sequenced federal litigation. The labor movement at its peak combined strikes and organizing with strategic use of the National Labor Relations Board and federal courts. Treating mass politics and law as alternatives, rather than as complementary tools, misreads the actual history.

Miller's essay is at its strongest when it argues that progressives have over-invested in courts at the expense of building popular political power. That criticism is fair. But the corrective is balance, not abandonment. We need a mass democratic politics capable of winning elections, governing competently when in power, and building durable popular constituencies for progressive policy. We also need a legal infrastructure capable of defending civil liberties against authoritarian government, enforcing constitutional rights against majoritarian abuse, and constraining the executive when it exceeds its powers. These are not in tension. They are both necessary, and right now, in the spring of 2025, the second is what is keeping the first possible.

The Madisonian System Is Not the Author's Real Enemy

Lisa Miller has written a smart and pugnacious essay, and I find myself agreeing with much of its diagnosis even as I think its account of American constitutionalism is historically tendentious in ways that matter. The essay treats the Madisonian system—separation of powers, federalism, judicial review, bicameralism—as an originalist project of elite restraint that has functioned, by design, to obstruct popular government. This is too simple. The actual American constitutional order has always been a contested terrain on which different visions of democracy have struggled, and the version of constitutionalism Miller decries is itself a relatively recent and specifically conservative construction.

Consider the institutions Miller treats as the core of the problem. The filibuster, which she rightly identifies as a major obstruction to popular legislation, is not in the Constitution. It is a Senate rule, codified in its modern form in the twentieth century, that could be eliminated tomorrow by majority vote. Lifetime judicial tenure is constitutional, but the size of the Supreme Court is statutory and has been changed multiple times. Judicial review itself is not in the constitutional text—it is a doctrine the Court asserted in Marbury v. Madison and that has fluctuated in scope ever since. The Electoral College is constitutional but its functioning is shaped by state-level winner-take-all rules that are themselves not required by the Constitution. The "checks and balances" Miller decries are partly Madisonian inheritance, partly nineteenth-century innovation, and partly twentieth-century accretion.

This matters because Miller's polemic conflates the genuinely problematic features of contemporary American government with the constitutional structure itself, and in doing so makes reform seem more daunting than it actually is. We do not need a constitutional amendment to abolish the filibuster. We do not need a constitutional amendment to expand the Supreme Court, regulate its jurisdiction, or impose term limits through statute on the lower federal bench. We do not need a constitutional amendment to expand the House of Representatives, which is held at 435 only by an act of Congress passed in 1929. We do not need a constitutional amendment to enact the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact, which would effectively neutralize the Electoral College once enough states sign on. The constitutional structure is more reformable than Miller's framing suggests, but the reform agenda is largely statutory and political, not constitutional.

This connects to a deeper historical point. Miller treats the Framers as architects of elite restraint, citing the familiar critique that the Constitution was designed by propertied men to protect their property against democratic majorities. This is the Charles Beard reading of American constitutionalism, and it has a long tradition. But it is not the whole story. The Federalist papers themselves contain extended arguments for energetic government—Hamilton's vision in particular was of a strong national state capable of governing a commercial republic. The Anti-Federalists, whose worries Miller's essay sometimes echoes, were not advocates for centralized democratic majoritarianism; they were advocates for localism and small-republic government of exactly the kind Miller criticizes when she talks about federalism's tendency to entrench local hierarchies.

The actual constitutional tradition is more contested than either the "elite restraint" reading or the "democratic majoritarianism" reading allows. Madison himself, by the 1790s, was already arguing for stronger popular control of government and a more vigorous Republican party as a check on Hamiltonian elitism. The Reconstruction Amendments rewrote the Constitution in fundamentally majoritarian and nationalist directions, creating federal authority over civil rights and citizenship that the original Constitution lacked. The progressive amendments of the early twentieth century—direct election of senators, income tax, women's suffrage—pushed the structure further toward popular government. The constitutional order Miller decries is the result of a long political struggle in which democratic forces have repeatedly won and lost ground.

This suggests a different framing of the present crisis than Miller offers. The problem is not that the Constitution as such is anti-democratic, nor that "checks and balances" as such are anti-democratic. The problem is that the specific configuration of American institutions in the early twenty-first century has been captured by a coalition that uses constitutional and quasi-constitutional mechanisms to entrench minority rule. Senate malapportionment was always a feature, but it has become more politically consequential as the geographic distribution of partisan support has shifted. Gerrymandering has always existed, but new technologies have made it vastly more effective. Campaign finance was always influenced by wealth, but Citizens United and the rise of dark money have changed the scale. The filibuster was always available, but the norm of using it sparingly has been abandoned.

In other words, the present sclerosis is not a destiny inherited from 1787; it is a contingent political achievement of the contemporary American right, built on top of constitutional structures that are more flexible than they appear. The corollary is that reversing this achievement is not necessarily a multi-decade constitutional project. Some of it could be done quickly with a determined congressional majority. Filibuster reform plus statehood for D.C. and Puerto Rico plus statutory protection of voting rights plus an expanded Supreme Court plus a return to historical norms of executive restraint would dramatically change the operation of American government within a single congressional session.

The obstacle to this program is not the Constitution; it is the absence of a stable congressional majority willing to enact it. Building such a majority requires the kind of mass politics Miller calls for, and on that she is exactly right. But the framing of "the system itself is the problem" risks pointing political energy at the wrong target. The system has been worse in the past and has been reformed before, and within the existing constitutional framework. What we need is not to overthrow Madisonianism but to recapture it for democratic purposes.

A final note. Miller writes that "nearly all of the substantive amendments to the Constitution since the Eleventh in 1795 have made it more inclusive, more democratic, or sought to strengthen the national government and make it more effective." This is true, and it is a more hopeful observation than the rest of her essay suggests. The constitutional tradition is not just a story of elite restraint frustrating popular majorities. It is also a story of those majorities periodically breaking through and reshaping the structure. The Reconstruction Amendments. The progressive amendments. The expansion of civil rights enforcement under the Fourteenth Amendment. The constitutional tradition Miller decries also contains the resources for its own reform.

The challenge is not to escape Madison. It is to remember that Madison's own tradition is more capacious than the version his contemporary defenders want to enforce.

First, the parties are unusually ideologically sorted. Through most of American history, both major parties were heterogeneous coalitions containing significant ideological diversity. Northern liberal Republicans and conservative Southern Democrats were normal categories. The most conservative Democrat is more liberal than the most liberal Republican, with virtually no exceptions. These changes are harder to articulate as a reform program because they involve cultural and organizational evolution as much as formal rule changes. Emergency powers, war powers, appropriations, confirmations, oversight, and agency delegations all need renewal. Members of Congress should be forced to vote on major questions rather than hide behind procedural fog. If Republicans support Trump’s actions, they should own them publicly. If Democrats oppose them, they should offer legislation that clarifies the stakes. Accountability begins with recorded responsibility.

Third, prioritize democracy reforms that can be explained in one sentence. “Every citizen should be able to vote easily and have that vote count equally.” “Politicians should not choose their voters.” “The candidate with the most votes should win.” “No president should be above the law.” “Billionaires should not be able to buy elections.” “Judges should not hold power for life without enforceable ethics.” Reform movements fail when they sound like seminars. They succeed when institutional design is tied to felt unfairness.

Fourth, pair process reforms with material commitments. Miller is exactly right that democracy cannot be defended as procedure alone. A voting rights bill attached rhetorically to nothing but institutional repair will not mobilize enough people. It must be connected to wages, health care, housing, schools, child care, debt, safety, and freedom from corruption. People should understand that the same veto points that block voting rights also block lower drug prices, labor rights, disaster response, and clean water. Institutional reform must be made concrete: the rules are why you cannot get what you voted for.

Fifth, build state and local capacity without romanticizing it. In the short term, states and cities are indispensable. They can protect election administration, sue the federal government, maintain benefits, defend immigrants, preserve reproductive access, enact labor standards, and demonstrate governing competence. But localism should not become an excuse for national abdication. The message should be: we will protect people wherever we have power, and we will fight for national floors so protection does not depend on geography.

Sixth, reduce dependence on courts even while using them. Liberal politics became too court-centered in the late twentieth century, and Miller is right that this weakened mass politics. Litigation is necessary in 2025, but it cannot be the strategy. Court victories can be narrow, temporary, and demobilizing if citizens assume lawyers will save them. Every major lawsuit against authoritarian overreach should be accompanied by organizing, public education, congressional pressure, state action, and electoral work. Legal defense must become political offense.

Seventh, prepare for reform windows. Constitutional and institutional change often seems impossible until suddenly it is not. Crises open windows; organized movements decide what passes through them. Reformers should have ready proposals: filibuster abolition, House expansion, national voting standards, independent redistricting, public financing, Supreme Court term limits, enforceable ethics, emergency-power reform, anti-corruption statutes, and protections for civil service independence. Even if these cannot pass today, they can structure political demands and reveal who benefits from the status quo.

The most difficult challenge is coalition. Miller sees the Democratic Party as the most plausible vehicle for mass democratic politics, and she is probably right. But the party is an uneasy alliance of professionals, union households, racial minorities, young progressives, suburban moderates, nonprofit networks, and disaffected anti-Trump conservatives. It cannot become a majoritarian reform party merely by adopting better white papers. It needs a language that joins anti-authoritarianism to anti-oligarchy, patriotism to pluralism, and competence to solidarity.

That language should avoid two traps. The first is institutional nostalgia: “Save our democracy” cannot mean “restore the old system that many people experienced as unresponsive.” The second is anti-institutional romanticism: “The people versus the system” cannot mean giving presidents, parties, or movements a blank check. The right message is that Americans deserve a government they can actually hold accountable: strong enough to solve problems, constrained enough to prevent abuse, and fair enough that every vote and every person counts.

Miller’s essay is valuable because it forces readers to confront a truth many elites prefer to avoid: American democracy did not become vulnerable only because Trump arrived. It became vulnerable because too many people concluded that constitutional democracy was a machine for disappointment. But if that is true, the response must be staged. In the short run, defend every lawful barrier against authoritarian consolidation. In the medium run, win elections with a program that materially improves lives. In the long run, change the constitutional rules that make minority rule and elite veto so durable.

The dead end is not the idea of checks and balances. The dead end is believing that inherited checks will automatically produce democratic balance. They will not. Balance must be built through power, organization, reform, and delivery. The task now is to survive the immediate threat without losing sight of the deeper reconstruction that survival is meant to make possible.

Miller's essay raises a fundamental question about the relationship between democracy and constitutionalism that deserves more careful treatment than it receives. Populist majorities have supported policies that impoverished farmers, excluded immigrants, restricted speech, and violated individual rights. The Trump movement is itself a mass phenomenon, not an elite conspiracy; its leaders came to power through democratic elections and maintain support through genuine popular appeal. If we accept Miller's premise that mass majorities reliably support policies benefiting ordinary people, we must also accept that those same majorities can be mobilized in deeply troubling directions. The checks and balances narrative she critiques has merit precisely because it protects vulnerable minorities—including racial and ethnic minorities, immigrants, religious minorities, and political dissidents—from majoritarian tyranny. The challenge is not to eliminate these protections but to ensure they operate symmetrically: constraining elite power as effectively as they constrain majority power. This requires both structural reform and cultural renewal, but it does not require abandoning the constitutional framework that has made American democracy possible for more than two centuries.
