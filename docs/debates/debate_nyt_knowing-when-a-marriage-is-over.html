When is it time to give up on a marriage or a relationship? What should be a definitive breaking point?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

There is no universal breaking point, but there are some clarifying questions that can cut through the fog of a struggling marriage. The first is whether the relationship is unsafe. Physical violence, sustained emotional abuse, or coercive control are not problems to be worked through with better communication; they are reasons to leave, and the cultural reluctance to say so plainly has kept too many people in danger for too long.

Short of that, the harder cases involve what John Gottman has called the "Four Horsemen"—contempt, criticism, defensiveness, and stonewalling. Contempt in particular is the strongest predictor of divorce in his research, and a marriage in which one or both partners no longer respects the other is in graver condition than one marked merely by conflict. Frequent fighting is survivable; settled disdain usually isn't.

A useful test is whether both partners are still willing to do the work—therapy, honest conversation, behavioral change. If only one person is trying, the marriage is effectively already over; it just hasn't been named yet. Repeated betrayal without remorse, or addiction that the addicted partner refuses to treat, falls into the same category.

The op-ed that prompted this debate is right that we marry imperfect strangers and that romantic disappointment is not, by itself, grounds for divorce. But its argument can curdle into a counsel of endurance for its own sake. The point of staying is not to prove resilience; it is to build something good. When the relationship is actively diminishing both people, and when good-faith efforts to change that have failed, staying is not a virtue.

The op-ed’s insistence that we must abandon romantic perfectionism is a necessary corrective, but it risks obscuring a harder truth: some marriages are not simply imperfect; they are destructive. The line between a challenging union and one that should end is not found in fleeting unhappiness or routine disagreements. It is found in the entrenched, contemptuous patterns that corrode the very selfhood of the partners.

Decades of research by psychologist John Gottman offer a powerful framework. His work identifies four communication styles that signal a marriage’s collapse—criticism, defensiveness, stonewalling, and, most lethally, contempt. Contempt, expressed through mockery, sneering, or hostile name-calling, is not merely a sign of frustration. It is a statement that one partner regards the other as beneath respect. Once that sentiment becomes routine, the relationship ceases to be a source of support and mutates into an engine of emotional degradation. This is not a rough patch to be weathered with mature acceptance; it is a foundational rupture.

A definitive breaking point, then, is not a single dramatic betrayal. It emerges when attempts at repair—therapy, honest conversation, genuine effort—repeatedly fail to shift a dynamic of disrespect or abuse. Physical violence, of course, is an immediate and non-negotiable end. But sustained emotional cruelty and the withering of any shared vision for the future are equally final. When the union persistently diminishes your capacity for joy, erodes your self-worth, or harms your children by modeling toxic conflict, the moral obligation shifts from preserving the marriage to protecting the human beings within it. The wisdom of accepting a “good enough” partner must not be confused with the folly of accepting a soul-destroying one.

By dismantling this myth, he offers a pragmatic, even comforting, defense of enduring the inevitable friction of sharing a life with another deeply flawed human being. Staying for the sake of vows—or, as is often argued, for the children—frequently perpetuates a cycle of trauma rather than demonstrating resilience.

Giving up on a marriage should rarely be an impulsive response to a dormant phase or a heated argument. Yet, we must carefully distinguish between the ordinary sorrow of unmet romantic ideals and the acute damage of a destructive dynamic. When a relationship persistently demands the sacrifice of your fundamental well-being, walking away is not a failure of commitment; it is an act of necessary self-preservation.

A marriage is not over because it is disappointing. Every marriage is, at times. The fantasy that the right partner will spare us loneliness, boredom, sexual frustration or the need to apologize has done real damage. But the opposite fantasy — that endurance is always noble — is just as dangerous.

A definitive breaking point is not ordinary unhappiness; it is the collapse of safety, good faith or mutual responsibility. Physical violence, credible threats, coercive control, sexual violation or intimidation should end the presumption that the marriage deserves preservation. In those cases, the urgent question is not whether the couple has “tried hard enough,” but how the endangered partner can leave safely.

There are other breaking points that may be less visible but still decisive: an untreated addiction that repeatedly endangers the family; chronic infidelity accompanied by deception rather than repair; financial recklessness hidden from the other spouse; or a persistent refusal to engage in counseling, accountability or change. A marriage can survive a betrayal. It cannot survive indefinitely when only one person is doing the work of keeping it alive.

The crucial distinction is between pain that is part of growth and pain that is evidence of destruction. Couples often pass through grim seasons — after childbirth, illness, unemployment, grief, or years of accumulated resentment. In such cases, patience, therapy and renewed habits of kindness may be warranted. But contempt, humiliation and fear are not rough patches. Research on marital stability has long suggested that contempt is especially corrosive; common sense says the same. A home organized around dread is not a sanctuary.

Children are sometimes invoked as a reason to stay no matter what. They are better served by witnessing respect, repair and emotional steadiness than by being raised inside a war zone.

The question is not whether a marriage is imperfect. All are. The question is whether both people remain willing and able to protect each other’s dignity. When that answer is no, and remains no, leaving is not failure. It is recognition.

The romantic ideal of marriage as an endless project of mutual reinvention is seductive but dangerous. It can justify both heroic perseverance and quiet resignation, which is precisely why the recent debate has produced such heat and so little light. There is no universal breaking point in a marriage, and anyone who claims otherwise is selling a fantasy of moral clarity that real relationships rarely provide. But there are warning signs serious enough that I would call them non-negotiable: physical violence, sexual coercion, sustained emotional abuse, and the kind of compulsive deception (around money, infidelity, addiction) that makes partnership structurally impossible. In these cases, the question isn't whether to leave but how to leave safely.

Beyond those bright lines, the harder cases require something the "stay or go" framing tends to obscure: an honest accounting of whether both people are still trying. John Gottman's research on what he calls the Four Horsemen — criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling — is useful here precisely because it doesn't measure conflict (all marriages have conflict) but measures the quality of repair. A marriage in which both partners still attempt to repair after rupture is salvageable, even when it feels unbearable. A marriage in which contempt has become the default register is, in most cases, already over; the legal dissolution is just paperwork catching up.

The Alain de Botton essay that prompted this debate is right that we all marry the wrong person, in the sense that no spouse can fulfill the fantasy we project onto them. But that insight cuts two ways. Yes, it should make us patient with ordinary disappointment. It should also make us suspicious of the opposite move — using "everyone is flawed" as a reason to tolerate genuine cruelty or chronic neglect.

The honest answer to "when is it time?" is: when staying requires you to become a smaller, more frightened, or more dishonest version of yourself, and when your partner is unwilling to engage with that fact. That isn't a single moment. It's usually a long, sad recognition that arrives in pieces.

We have become culturally addicted to the idea that there should be a "definitive breaking point" in marriage — some clean threshold that, once crossed, authorizes departure without guilt. This is a category error. Marriage is not a contract with clauses; it is a practice, like medicine or teaching, and you can no more identify the exact moment it has failed than you can identify the exact moment a garden has been neglected past saving.

The demand for a bright line is, I suspect, a symptom of the same consumerist romanticism that the de Botton essay was trying to puncture. We want marriage to be a product with a warranty. If the product malfunctions in specified ways, we are entitled to a refund. But the people I know who have left long marriages do not describe a single galvanizing moment. They describe years of slow erosion, followed by a quiet recognition. And the people who have stayed and rebuilt do not describe a moment of vindication either. They describe the same slow work, redirected.

This is not an argument for staying in destructive marriages. Abuse, addiction left untreated, repeated betrayal — these are reasons to leave, and our culture has rightly moved past the era when women were told to endure them. But outside of those cases, I think the question "when should I leave?" is often the wrong question. The better question is: "what am I unwilling to do to repair this, and why?" Sometimes the honest answer reveals that one partner has already left emotionally and is looking for permission. Sometimes it reveals that the marriage is recoverable but neither partner wants to do the unglamorous work.

A breaking point is a story we tell afterward to make sense of a decision that was really made in a thousand smaller moments.

I want to offer a simpler answer than the therapeutic literature tends to provide: a marriage is over when one person has decided it is over. That sounds glib, but it's actually the most respectful framing available, because it takes seriously the autonomy of the person who wants to leave.

We spend enormous cultural energy litigating whether someone's reasons for leaving a marriage are "good enough" — whether the affair was bad enough, whether the neglect was sustained enough, whether they tried therapy long enough. This litigation falls disproportionately on women, who are still expected to justify exits that men are permitted to take without explanation. It also falls on people in marriages that look fine from the outside: no violence, no addiction, no infidelity, just a slow suffocation that the spouse leaving cannot adequately translate into terms a judgmental in-law will accept.

You do not owe anyone a defensible reason to end a marriage. "I am no longer willing" is a complete sentence. The de Botton essay, for all its charm, participates in a long tradition of telling people — historically, women — that their dissatisfaction is a failure of philosophical maturity rather than useful information about their own life.

Of course there are obligations: to children, to honesty during the process, to financial fairness, to not blowing up a partner's life through cruelty or deceit. These obligations are real and they shape how one leaves. But they do not determine whether one is permitted to leave.

The question "when is it time?" presumes there is an external arbiter who will tell you. There isn't. There is only you, paying honest attention to your own life, and deciding whether you can keep living it. That is a heavier responsibility than waiting for a definitive breaking point, but it is also more truthful.

Speaking as someone who has been married for twenty-three years, I want to defend the much-maligned position that you should stay longer than you think you should — not forever, not at any cost, but longer.

Here is what I have noticed in my own marriage and in those of my friends: the worst years are not necessarily a sign that the marriage is failing. They are sometimes a sign that one or both partners is going through something — a parent's death, a career collapse, the depression that ambushes people in their forties, the strange grief of children growing up and leaving. During those years a spouse can seem like a stranger, or worse, like the problem itself. Couples who leave during these years often discover, too late, that they were leaving a phase rather than a marriage.

This is not an argument for endurance as a virtue in itself. The exceptions are real and important: violence, addiction unaddressed, chronic contempt, betrayals that the betraying partner refuses to repair. In those cases, leaving sooner is almost always better than leaving later, and our grandparents' generation paid a terrible price for not understanding this.

But for the great middle ground of unhappy marriages — the ones where two basically decent people have become strangers to each other — I think the honest counsel is patience. Not passive patience, but the active kind: therapy, honesty about what each person has stopped giving, a willingness to be embarrassed by one's own contribution to the distance. Marriages that survive this work are often described by the people inside them as deeper than they were before.

The breaking point is real, but it is rarer than our divorce rate suggests. A lot of marriages end not because they were broken but because the work of repair felt harder than the work of starting over. It usually isn't.

The framing of this debate — "when is it time to give up?" — quietly assumes that giving up is the alternative to a stable continuation. But for many marriages, particularly those with children, the real alternatives are: a difficult, mutual transformation; a continued slow decay; or a divorce that is itself a transformation, not a termination. None of these is "giving up."

I think the more useful question is: what does this marriage owe to the people inside it, and what do they owe back? A marriage exists to enlarge the people in it — to make them more themselves, more capable of love, more able to face the world. When it stops doing that for both partners, and when sustained, honest effort fails to restart it, then continuing the marriage becomes a kind of fraud against everyone involved, including the children we often imagine we are protecting by staying.

Children, in fact, are usually worse off in a household defined by contempt or coldness than in two functional separate households. The folk wisdom that one should "stay for the kids" deserves more skepticism than it usually receives. Kids absorb the emotional weather of the home; they do not need their parents to be married, they need their parents to be reasonably whole.

So my answer to "what should be the definitive breaking point?" is that the breaking point arrives when continuing the marriage requires sustained dishonesty — when staying together means pretending, in front of each other and the children, that something is alive which is not.

That standard is harder than "abuse" or "infidelity" because it asks something of the people inside the marriage that those external markers do not: it asks them to be honest about what they actually feel and what they are actually doing. Most marriages that end probably should end. Many that continue probably shouldn't.

I read Alain de Botton's essay as neither a tribute to marriage nor a rationalization for staying, but as a useful description of expectations. Once you accept that your spouse cannot complete you, the question of when to leave gets clearer, not murkier — because it strips away the most common bad reason for leaving, which is that this particular person has failed to be everyone you needed them to be.

So when should you actually leave? I would propose three criteria, all of which need to be present.

First, the harm has to be real and ongoing, not theoretical or episodic. Every marriage has bad months. The question is whether the harm — whether through cruelty, neglect, betrayal, or simple incompatibility making both people miserable — is the steady state rather than a phase.

Second, you have tried, genuinely tried, to change it. This means therapy, individual and couples; it means saying the hard things out loud; it means being willing to discover that you are part of the problem. People who skip this step often repeat the same dynamic in the next relationship and are surprised.

Third, your partner is either unwilling to engage or incapable of it. This is the criterion that separates a hard marriage from a doomed one. Two people who are both still trying, even badly, have a marriage. One person trying while the other has checked out does not.

When all three are present, leaving is not failure — it is honesty. When any one is missing, leaving is probably premature, and the dissatisfaction will follow you into the next chapter of your life.

This is unsatisfying as a "definitive breaking point" because it isn't definitive. But marriages are not the kinds of things that have definitive breaking points. They have honest reckonings, and the reckonings sometimes conclude in divorce and sometimes in something better.
