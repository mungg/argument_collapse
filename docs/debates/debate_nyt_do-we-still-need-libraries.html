The New York Times reports that, as big stores like Borders disappear, “many public libraries are seeing an opportunity to fill the void created by the loss of traditional bookstores.”

Is that the right direction for libraries to take? What are libraries for, and how should they evolve?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Libraries have never been just warehouses for books, and the temptation to recast them as bookstore replacements misreads both their history and their present. Andrew Carnegie's libraries were civic infrastructure: places where immigrants learned English, workers self-educated, and citizens encountered ideas they could not afford to buy. That mission is, if anything, more urgent now than in 1900.

Consider what libraries already do that Borders never did. They provide free internet access — a lifeline for the roughly one in four Americans without broadband at home, and increasingly a prerequisite for job applications, unemployment claims, and school assignments. They host story hours, ESL classes, tax-help volunteers, and after-school programs. The Pew Internet study released earlier this year found that the majority of library users value these services as highly as the books themselves.

So filling the "void" left by Borders is fine as a marketing line, but it sells libraries short. A bookstore's job is to move inventory; a library's job is to guarantee access — to information, to literacy, to the digital tools required for modern citizenship, and to a noncommercial public space that is becoming rarer in American life.

The right evolution, then, is not toward retail ambience but toward deeper civic function: better e-book lending (the current publisher standoffs are an embarrassment), maker spaces, digital literacy training, and partnerships with schools. Libraries should not imitate the stores that died. They should outlast them by being something stores never were.

Libraries Should Not Try to Be Bookstores

The temptation for libraries to fill the Borders-shaped hole in American downtowns is understandable, but it would be a strategic mistake. Bookstores failed, in part, because the economics of selling physical books to browsers cannot compete with Amazon's warehouses and the Kindle. Libraries that lean harder into "browsing the new releases like at Barnes & Noble" are chasing a business model that the market has already rejected.

What libraries offer that bookstores never did is something more fundamental: a non-commercial public space. You do not have to buy anything to sit there. You do not have to be a customer. In an economy where nearly every square foot of indoor space is rented to people with money to spend, that civic function is rare and precious. Teenagers without a car, job-seekers without broadband, immigrants studying for citizenship tests, retirees on fixed incomes — these are not Borders' lost customers. They are the library's actual constituents.

The right evolution is to deepen that civic identity rather than mimic retail. That means more reference librarians, not fewer; more programming around literacy, citizenship, and job training; more meeting rooms; better public computers and Wi-Fi; and serious investment in digital lending infrastructure so that patrons without e-reader budgets are not locked out of contemporary reading. The American Library Association's recent fights with publishers over e-book lending terms are precisely the right battles — they are about preserving public access in a digital age, not about staging author signings.

If libraries try to be cozy bookstores, they will be judged by bookstore metrics — foot traffic, bestseller turnover, café receipts — and they will lose. If they double down on being the last truly public indoor space in most American towns, they have a future that no Kindle can disrupt. The Borders comparison flatters libraries in the short term and misreads their purpose in the long term.

Yes, Fill the Void — Communities Need Browsing Space

There is a strain of library purism that recoils at the idea of libraries acting "like bookstores." I think that purism is misplaced. The bookstore, at its best, was not just a retail operation; it was a place where people discovered books they didn't know they wanted, sipped coffee, attended a reading, ran into a neighbor. When Borders closed in town after town, what disappeared wasn't just commerce — it was a particular kind of literary commons. Libraries are exactly the right institution to inherit that role.

Consider what bookstores did well that traditional libraries often did not: comfortable seating, late hours, face-out displays, staff recommendations that felt personal rather than bureaucratic, cafés that made lingering acceptable, and event calendars that turned a building into a hub. None of those things compromise the mission of free public access to information. They enhance it. A library that hosts a packed author talk on a Thursday night is doing more for literacy than one that closes at 5 p.m. with its new arrivals shelved spine-out.

Some libraries already get this. The new central libraries in places like Seattle and Salt Lake City treat reading as a social activity, not a monastic one. Smaller branches are partnering with local coffee vendors, opening makerspaces, and curating displays the way independent booksellers do. Critics worry this dilutes the "real" library, but the real library has always evolved — from subscription societies to Carnegie reading rooms to lending institutions with DVDs and databases.

The risk isn't that libraries become too much like bookstores. The risk is that, by clinging to a 1965 model of hushed stacks and overdue fines, they make themselves irrelevant to the very communities whose taxes sustain them. Filling the Borders void is not a betrayal of mission. It is, in many towns, the most direct way left to keep reading a public, shared experience.

The Question Is Wrong

"Do we still need libraries?" is the kind of question that gets asked about institutions only after they have already proved indispensable. We do not ask whether we still need fire departments because most houses do not burn down. The Pew Internet survey released earlier this year found that 91 percent of Americans over 16 consider public libraries important to their communities, and use of library computers and Wi-Fi has surged precisely as Borders has collapsed. The premise of the debate is, frankly, a thin one drawn from the assumption that books equal libraries.

They never did. Andrew Carnegie did not fund 1,600 buildings so people could borrow novels; he funded them because he believed access to information was a precondition of democratic citizenship. That mission is more urgent now, not less. The digital divide is real: roughly a third of American households still lack reliable home broadband, and that share is much higher among the poor, the elderly, and rural residents. The library is the last reliable on-ramp. During the 2008–2010 recession, librarians effectively became frontline unemployment counselors, walking people through online job applications that no longer accepted paper.

So whether libraries should "fill the void" left by Borders strikes me as the least interesting framing available. Borders sold a commodity. Libraries provide infrastructure. The right questions are about funding: why are municipalities cutting library budgets at the exact moment demand is rising? Why are publishers like Macmillan and Simon & Schuster refusing to license e-books to libraries on any reasonable terms? Why is library science as a profession being treated as optional when information literacy has never mattered more?

Yes, libraries should evolve — toward broader digital lending, deeper community programming, and more aggressive advocacy for public access in a privatizing information economy. But evolving is not the same as auditioning to replace the mall bookstore. Libraries outlasted Borders. They will outlast the question.

A Skeptic's View: Be Honest About Trade-offs

I love libraries, but I am wary of the everything-bagel approach now being marketed as their future. In the span of a single op-ed cycle, libraries are supposed to replace bookstores, close the digital divide, host makerspaces, run job programs, serve as homeless day shelters, lend power tools, and somehow still maintain serious collections. This is not a strategy. It is a wish list, and it is being asked of an institution whose budgets are flat or falling in most of the country.

The Borders comparison illustrates the problem. A bookstore had clear metrics — sales per square foot — and still couldn't survive. When libraries take on bookstore-like functions (cafés, lounges, bestseller-driven displays), they incur real costs: staff time, space reallocation, capital investment, and a subtle drift in identity that affects how trustees and voters perceive the institution. Every square foot devoted to a café is one not devoted to reference services or quiet study, which happen to be the things patrons consistently rank highest in user surveys.

I would push libraries to do less, better. Pick the two or three things your community actually cannot get elsewhere and excel at them. In an affluent suburb with three independent bookstores, that may mean deep research collections and children's programming. In a rural county with no broadband provider, it may mean computer access and digital literacy classes — full stop. In an inner-city branch, it may mean a safe afternoon space for kids and serious adult-education partnerships. Few libraries need to be all of these things at once.

The danger in framing libraries as Borders' successor is not that the comparison is undignified. It is that it encourages mission creep at precisely the moment libraries need clarity. Evolve, yes. But evolve toward a defensible purpose, not toward whatever vacancy the retail sector happens to leave behind this year.
