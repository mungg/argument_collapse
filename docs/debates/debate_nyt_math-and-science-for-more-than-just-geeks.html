Math and Science for More Than Just Geeks

The first in a series of editorials on reforming math and science education noted that the number of students pursuing engineering or computer science jobs is plummeting as the need for those workers is soaring. Nearly 90 percent of high school graduates say they’re not interested in STEM — science, technology, engineering and math – largely because classes in those fields are dull, curriculums are outdated and teachers are ill-prepared. The number of women and minorities pursuing these fields is particularly bleak.

Greater resources would help. But how else can STEM be made more interesting and attractive to young people?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The framing of the problem—that STEM is "dull"—misdiagnoses what's actually happening in most classrooms. Math and science aren't inherently boring; they become boring when taught as procedural drills divorced from the questions that motivated them in the first place. A student who memorizes the quadratic formula without ever wondering why anyone cared to solve quadratics is being trained, not educated.

The most promising reforms I've seen point in three directions. First, project-based learning that lets students actually build, measure, code, and break things. Robotics clubs, FIRST competitions, and Maker spaces consistently pull in kids—including girls and minorities—who had written off STEM as not for them. Second, computer science deserves a place in the core curriculum, not as an elective afterthought. Code.org's Hour of Code campaign this week shows the appetite is there; what's missing is institutional commitment. Third, we need to fix the teacher pipeline. A physics teacher who never majored in physics cannot convey what's exciting about it. Programs like UTeach and loan-forgiveness for STEM-credentialed teachers address this directly.

Underrepresentation deserves particular attention. The research on stereotype threat and on the "leaky pipeline" for women in computing—where female CS enrollment has actually fallen since the 1980s—suggests that role models, mentoring, and classroom climate matter as much as content. Making STEM interesting is partly about making students feel they belong in it.

The crisis in STEM education isn’t a shortage of intelligence—it’s a failure of imagination. For too many students, math and science remain a joyless grind of rote formulas and disconnected facts, taught by teachers who often lack deep content knowledge and receive little support to inspire. If we want to fill the pipeline with a diverse generation of innovators, we must stop treating these subjects as filter courses designed to weed out all but the “geeks” and start showing every student how science and math are tools to solve problems they actually care about.

The single most powerful lever is relevance. Young people are passionate about climate change, clean water, video games, medical breakthroughs, and building things—yet they rarely see the math and physics behind these endeavors. Project-based learning that ties directly to real-world challenges makes the abstract concrete. When a student writes code to model an epidemic or designs a wind turbine blade in geometry class, the equations stop being a punishment and become a power. Schools need the flexibility and resources to adopt this approach, moving beyond textbooks that still pretend the internet doesn’t exist.

Equally critical is smashing the stale image of the solitary lab-coated genius. Role models matter enormously, especially for women and minorities who remain dramatically underrepresented. Programs that bring engineers, game designers, environmental scientists, and medical professionals into classrooms—particularly those who reflect the students’ own backgrounds—can shatter stereotypes. Mentorship and after-school clubs like robotics, coding, or science Olympiads offer additional entry points where students discover that technical skill is collaborative and creative, not just an endurance test.

Teacher preparation must be overhauled. A shocking number of middle and high school math and science instructors never majored in their subject. Deep content knowledge paired with immersive training in inquiry-based pedagogy is essential. We should reward expert teachers with better pay and time to collaborate, not leave them isolated with outdated materials.

Finally, we must normalize struggle. Too many students, especially high achievers, are taught that if math isn’t effortless, they lack “talent.” A culture that celebrates persistence, welcomes mistakes as part of learning, and praises effort over raw speed can keep students engaged long after the first stumbling block. The goal isn’t to force everyone into a lab coat, but to give every young person the confidence and curiosity to see that science and math are not a secret language for a chosen few—they are the common language of our future.

The framing of this debate is wrong from the start. We keep hearing that 90 percent of high school graduates aren't interested in STEM, as though this is a crisis comparable to a polio outbreak. But why should we expect any other number? The fraction of jobs that require deep, daily engagement with calculus or molecular biology is small. Most people are going to be nurses, teachers, accountants, salespeople, mechanics, electricians, and managers. Some of those jobs are technical, but they don't require the kind of STEM identity the panic-mongers want to manufacture.

The real problem is narrower and more honest: we have a shortage of engineers and computer scientists, and we are doing a particularly bad job recruiting women and minorities into those fields. Those are tractable problems. They do not require remaking the entire K-12 curriculum around the priorities of Silicon Valley CEOs who would like a larger labor pool.

What would actually help? First, computer science should be a normal high school subject, not a niche elective taught by whoever happens to be free fifth period. Code.org's recent push around the "Hour of Code" this week is a nice gesture, but a real curriculum requires trained teachers, and trained teachers require pay scales that can compete with industry — which they can't, almost anywhere.

Second, the gender and race gap is partly cultural and starts early. Studies of stereotype threat by Claude Steele and others show that performance is sensitive to social cues that begin in elementary school. The fix is not pep rallies for girls in lab coats; it is consistent, sustained mentoring and intervention through the leaky pipeline, especially at the transition to college.

Third, we should stop pretending "interest" is the bottleneck. Many students who would thrive as engineers get filtered out by a poorly taught Algebra II class or a weed-out freshman calculus course. The biggest gains will come from teaching the existing curriculum better, not from gamifying it with robots and 3D printers.

The country does not need 90 percent of its graduates fired up about STEM. It needs the 20 or 30 percent who could plausibly do this work to be taught well, encouraged consistently, and not driven away by bad pedagogy. Focusing on that smaller, real problem would be more useful than another round of national hand-wringing.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Anyone who has sat through a typical American high school chemistry class knows exactly why 90 percent of students walk away uninterested. The class is a forced march through a textbook written by committee, taught by someone who may or may not have a chemistry degree, assessed by multiple-choice tests aligned to a state standard, and divorced from any question a teenager would actually ask. The wonder is not that nine out of ten students are bored. The wonder is that one in ten survives.

If we want more young people drawn to STEM, the single most important change is to let them do science rather than memorize about it. Real scientists ask questions, design experiments, fail, argue, and revise. Real engineers build things that don't work and then figure out why. Almost none of this happens in a typical classroom, where "lab" means following a recipe to confirm a result the teacher already knows.

Project-based learning is not a new idea — John Dewey was making this argument a century ago — but the evidence for it has grown stronger. Schools like High Tech High in San Diego have shown that you can teach rigorous content through extended, open-ended projects and get students who actually care about what they're doing. The recent Next Generation Science Standards are a step in the right direction, emphasizing scientific practices over content recall, but they will be hollow if classrooms don't change.

Two other things matter. One is the teacher. We will not solve this problem until we can recruit and retain people who actually love math and science, which means paying them, respecting them, and giving them time to plan. Finland and Singapore figured this out; we have not.

The other is the early grades. By the time a kid hits high school, attitudes toward math are largely set. Elementary teachers, most of whom were not math majors and many of whom are themselves math-anxious, transmit that anxiety to their students with disturbing reliability. Investing in math content training for K-5 teachers might do more than any high school reform.

The technology will come and go. Khan Academy and MOOCs are interesting, but they are not the answer. The answer is older and harder: better teachers doing better teaching, with the freedom to let kids actually think.

Let's name the elephant in the room: a lot of the STEM-shortage talk is industry lobbying dressed up as education reform. The claim that we have a desperate shortage of scientists and engineers has been challenged repeatedly by labor economists. Hal Salzman at Rutgers, Michael Teitelbaum at Sloan, and the Economic Policy Institute have all published analyses suggesting that the United States produces more STEM graduates than the market absorbs, that wages in many STEM fields have been flat or declining, and that the loudest voices warning of shortages are companies that benefit from a larger, cheaper labor pool, particularly through expanded H-1B visas.

This matters for the debate question because if you misdiagnose the problem, you'll prescribe the wrong cure. If the real issue were a shortage, salaries would be rising sharply, which would do more to attract young people than any curriculum reform. They aren't, in most subfields. Petroleum engineers do well. Many biology PhDs spend years in postdoc purgatory. Computer programmers do fine but face age discrimination and offshoring pressure. The picture is not the simple one painted by the editorial.

That said, there are real reasons to want better math and science education that have nothing to do with the labor market. A democratic citizenry needs scientific literacy to make sensible decisions about climate, vaccination, energy, and technology policy. Quantitative reasoning is essential to evaluating claims in journalism, advertising, and political rhetoric. These are arguments for STEM education as liberal education, not as vocational pipeline.

If we reframe the question that way, the path forward looks different. We should worry less about funneling more kids into engineering majors and more about ensuring that every graduate, regardless of major, can read a graph, evaluate a study, write a simple program, and understand basic probability. That is a genuinely universal goal, not a sectoral one.

The diversity question is separate and important. Underrepresentation of women and minorities in STEM fields is a real problem of equity, not of national competitiveness, and it deserves its own response. But conflating it with workforce panic only confuses the issue.

In short: be skeptical of the premise. The dull curriculum is real. The teacher shortage is real. The shortage of engineers, less so.

I teach high school math in a public school in a working-class neighborhood, and I want to push back on the abstractions that dominate this debate. When people say "make STEM more interesting," they usually picture robotics clubs, maker spaces, and field trips to tech companies. Those things are nice. They are also expensive and mostly available to suburban kids whose parents drive them to enrichment activities.

What my students need is more basic. They need textbooks that aren't a decade out of date. They need a working calculator for every kid in Algebra II. They need a science lab where the gas valves haven't been disconnected for safety reasons since 2007. They need a second math teacher so I'm not the only one in the building, and they need that teacher not to be a long-term sub on an emergency credential.

The conversation about engagement is downstream of resources. Of course classes are boring when the teacher has 38 students, three preps, and no planning time. Of course the curriculum is outdated when there's no money to replace materials. Of course teachers are ill-prepared when districts can't compete with private-sector salaries for anyone with a quantitative degree.

The editorial concedes that "greater resources would help" and then quickly moves on to ask what else can be done, as if resources were a small piece of the puzzle. They are most of the puzzle, especially for the students whose absence from STEM fields the country claims to lament. Women and minorities are concentrated in under-resourced schools. If you want to know why they aren't pursuing engineering, walk into one of those schools and look around.

So my answer to "how else can STEM be made more interesting" is: don't change the subject. Pay teachers more. Fund schools equitably. Reduce class sizes. Provide functional equipment. These are unglamorous proposals, and they don't generate think-pieces or TED talks, but they would do more than any curriculum redesign.

After we've done the boring, expensive work of building schools that function, then we can talk about pedagogy and projects and partnerships with industry. Trying to skip past resources to the fun stuff is a particularly American form of magical thinking.

There is a piece of this puzzle the editorial doesn't mention: culture. American culture has, for a long time, been ambivalent at best about intellectual achievement, and openly hostile to it in the domains of math and science. Being bad at math is socially acceptable, even charming. "I'm just not a math person" is a sentence you can say at a dinner party without embarrassment. Try saying "I'm just not a reading person" and watch the reaction.

This is not true in every country, and the difference shows up in achievement data. The PISA results released just last week show American 15-year-olds clustered around the OECD average in reading, below average in math, with no real movement in the past decade. East Asian students dominate, and while there's plenty to criticize about cram schools and test pressure, part of what those societies have is a baseline cultural assumption that math is something everyone does and everyone can get better at with effort.

Carol Dweck's work on growth mindset and Jo Boaler's research on math identity both point in the same direction: students perform according to what they and the adults around them believe is possible. American kids absorb, by middle school, the idea that math ability is innate and largely fixed. The kids who decide they're "not math people" are then making a rational decision to stop investing in something they've been told they can't change.

Changing this is harder than rewriting a curriculum, but it's not impossible. It starts with how we talk to small children about ability. It continues with how teachers respond to wrong answers — as data to learn from, not evidence of inability. It runs through popular culture, which still loves to portray scientists and mathematicians as socially stunted oddballs. The word "geek" in the title of this debate is itself part of the problem. Why should math and science be the province of geeks? In other countries, they're just school subjects.

Resources matter. Curriculum matters. But the deepest barrier is a story Americans tell themselves about who is and isn't a "math person." Until we change that story, the other reforms will run into a headwind.

The cleanest leverage point in STEM education is also the least sexy: elementary school teachers and their relationship with mathematics.

Consider the pipeline. Most American elementary teachers are themselves products of a math-anxious culture; surveys consistently find that a majority chose elementary education in part because it didn't require advanced math. They then teach kindergarten through fifth grade math to every child in the country during the years when basic numerical fluency and attitudes toward the subject are being formed. The result is predictable: math-anxious teachers produce math-anxious students, especially math-anxious girls, who pick up on cues from adult women in their lives.

Research by Sian Beilock and others has documented this transmission directly, showing that female elementary teachers' math anxiety predicts their female students' (but not male students') math achievement and beliefs about gender and math by the end of the school year. This is a remarkable finding, and it points to a clear intervention.

If we want more young people, and especially more girls, drawn to math and science, we should invest heavily in the math content knowledge of elementary teachers. Liping Ma's classic comparison of American and Chinese elementary teachers showed that the Chinese teachers, with less formal education overall, had deeper conceptual understanding of the elementary topics they taught. American teachers often know procedures without understanding why they work, which means they can't help students who get stuck and can't recognize creative reasoning when they see it.

This is fixable. Math specialist programs, where one teacher with strong content training handles math instruction for multiple classrooms, have shown promise in places like Virginia. Sustained professional development that focuses on the math itself, not on pedagogy in the abstract, has better evidence behind it than most fads. Better preparation in ed schools — meaning real math courses, not "math methods" — should be a licensure requirement.

This is unglamorous policy. There are no robots involved. No coding camps, no flashy apps, no celebrity endorsers. But the entire STEM pipeline runs through these classrooms, and the leak is largest there. Fixing the high school curriculum while ignoring the K-5 foundation is like remodeling the kitchen of a house with a cracked foundation. We should start at the bottom and work up.

The question asks how to make STEM more "interesting and attractive," but I want to flip the framing. The goal of math and science education isn't to be attractive. It's to develop the habits of mind that allow people to think clearly about the natural world and quantitative problems. Sometimes those habits are exhilarating to develop. Often they're hard work. Pretending otherwise — promising students that learning calculus will be like playing Angry Birds — sets up disappointments and trivializes the subject.

That doesn't mean classes need to be boring. There's a difference between rigorous and tedious, and most American math and science classes are tedious without being particularly rigorous. The tedium comes from drill divorced from context; the lack of rigor comes from coverage-driven curricula that move too fast to allow real understanding.

Here's a concrete proposal: teach less, deeper. The "mile wide, inch deep" criticism of American math curricula has been around since the TIMSS results of the 1990s. The Common Core standards, whatever their flaws, do try to address this by emphasizing focus and depth in the early grades. Whether implementation lives up to the design is an open question, and the political backlash is intensifying, but the underlying instinct is correct.

A second proposal: take problem-solving seriously as the central activity. The Putnam-style or Olympiad-style approach, where students wrestle with a small number of hard problems over an extended period, is closer to what mathematicians actually do than the worksheet model. Singapore's textbooks, much imitated and rarely understood, do something like this. The point is not to train future olympiad winners; it is that struggling productively with a real problem is the experience that hooks people.

Third: separate the assessment from the learning. American math classes are constantly assessing — quiz Friday, test next week, midterm, final, standardized exam in the spring. Students learn to optimize for short-term recall and forget afterward. If we want them to develop deep interest, we have to give them time to be confused, to play, to try and fail without it counting.

Make math and science demanding, intellectually honest, and given enough time, and a much larger fraction of students will find them attractive. Soften them into edutainment and we lose both the rigor and, eventually, the interest.

The most striking feature of the STEM debate, to me, is how persistently it ignores the role of computing. Computer science is the field driving most of the actual labor demand the editorial mentions, and it is almost entirely absent from the American high school curriculum. Fewer than 10 percent of high schools offer any computer science course. The AP Computer Science exam is taken by a tiny fraction of students compared to AP Calculus or AP Biology, and the demographics are even more skewed by gender and race.

This is bizarre. Computing is the most economically consequential technical skill of our era and arguably one of the most intellectually generative ways to learn mathematics. Writing a program forces you to think precisely, decompose problems, and confront your own confusions in a way that solving a textbook problem does not. Seymour Papert was making this argument with LOGO in the 1980s, and the basic insight has only become more relevant.

The current Hour of Code campaign, which started this week as part of Computer Science Education Week, is a small but useful intervention. The bigger goal should be making computer science a routine high school subject available everywhere, counted for graduation credit, taught by teachers with real training. Some states are moving in this direction; most aren't.

A few concrete moves would help. First, count computer science as a math or science credit, which only a minority of states do. Second, build a pipeline of CS teachers, probably by retraining existing math teachers and offering loan forgiveness for new entrants.
