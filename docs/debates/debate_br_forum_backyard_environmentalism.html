Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have identified something real. The regulatory experiments they describe—TRI, TURA, the Chesapeake Bay Program, the southern California HCPs—do exhibit family resemblances that conventional categories of "command-and-control" versus "market mechanism" fail to capture. The authors deserve credit for naming a pattern that has been emerging piecemeal and for taking seriously the democratic possibilities latent in it. But the essay oversells its cases, underestimates the persistent role of coercion and adversarial politics in making any of these regimes work, and skates past distributive questions that should make us cautious about declaring a new democratic dawn.

Consider how the essay reads its own evidence. TRI is treated as a triumph of "benchmarking" and "alarming information." Yet TRI works precisely because it is a mandatory federal disclosure statute, backed by citizen-suit provisions, layered on top of the Clean Air and Water Acts and the Superfund liability regime. Take away those background commands and the "right-to-know" loses its bite: firms reduce reported emissions because lawsuits, share-price reactions, and the threat of additional command regulation impose real costs. The deliberative architecture is parasitic on the hierarchical one. The authors hint at this when they concede that Responsible Care has stalled in the absence of public authority, and that INPO became effective only when the NRC adopted its standards. The honest reading of these paired cases is not that "rolling-rule" regimes substitute for command, but that they require it as scaffolding.

The Chesapeake Bay case raises similar doubts. The 1987 Agreement committed the signatories to a 40-percent reduction in nutrient loadings by 2000. As of 1999, with the deadline a few months away, nitrogen reductions remain well short of that figure, and the program has quietly re-described its targets in tributary-specific terms—an institutional move the authors celebrate as adaptive learning, but which an unsympathetic reader could call goal-shifting in the face of failure. "Adaptive management" is a real virtue when it tracks new ecological knowledge; it is a vice when it absorbs political failures into ever-revised baselines. The essay never specifies how, in a rolling-rule regime, one distinguishes the two.

The HCP case is where the argument is most vulnerable, despite—or because of—the authors' enthusiasm. The 1994 "No Surprises" policy, codified in regulation earlier this year, guarantees permittees that once an HCP is approved, the federal government will not require additional land, water, or financial commitments if circumstances change, even if a covered species declines further. Permits run for periods of up to a century. This is the opposite of rolling-rule logic: it locks today's scientific understanding into a long-term contract precisely so that landowners will come to the table. The authors acknowledge that the southern California plans depend on "adaptive management" provisions, but they do not grapple with how those provisions interact with No Surprises, nor with the criticisms—voiced by Kareiva and colleagues in the very report they cite—that biological monitoring in most HCPs is too weak to detect failure in time to act. The Miller Bill might fix some of this, but it has not passed and shows no signs of imminent passage; building an architectural theory on prospective legislation is a thin foundation.

A second blind spot is distributive. The "deep user" sounds appealing in the abstract, but participation in tributary teams, planner certifications, and habitat-plan negotiations is enormously time-intensive. It systematically favors organized landowners, agricultural interests, and professionalized environmental groups over poor and minority communities whose backyards bear disproportionate environmental burdens. The environmental-justice scholarship of the past decade—Bullard's work on toxic siting, the United Church of Christ studies, the executive order on environmental justice—has documented that local deliberative bodies can entrench rather than correct these inequities. The essay's section on Jacksonian and Progressive antecedents is elegant, but it omits the populist tradition's darker side: that local control was historically a mechanism for racial exclusion as often as for democratic empowerment. A theory of deliberative environmental democracy that does not address who actually shows up at the table, and who pays the costs of showing up, is not yet a theory of democracy.

Third, the essay's pluralist counterfactual is a straw man. The authors mock the Interior Department's faith that national groups will police local under-enforcement and property owners will police over-enforcement. But this is what is actually happening: it is the National Wildlife Federation, the Center for Biological Diversity petitions, and litigation under the ESA that have forced FWS to draft the guidance the authors approve of. The pluralist machinery has not been displaced; it has produced the very correctives the essay celebrates.

None of this defeats the core insight. Performance-based regulation that pools information, requires self-monitoring, and revises standards iteratively is a genuine advance over both rigid command and naive market simulation. But its democratic credentials depend on hard law, robust monitoring, real sanctions for non-performance, and explicit attention to who participates. Without those, "rolling-rule" risks becoming what its critics already suspect: a sophisticated name for negotiated under-enforcement.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The vision of a “rolling-rule regime” outlined by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen is an important and in many ways attractive departure from the stale debate between rigid command-and-control regulation and the false promise of market-based panaceas. They are right that the old models have failed to cope with complexity, discouraged local innovation, and, in the case of tradable permits, demanded levels of information that regulators cannot muster. Their case studies—from the Toxics Release Inventory to the Chesapeake Bay Program—genuinely show that pragmatic, information-driven collaboration can produce environmental improvements that top-down rules alone could not. Yet as we consider embracing this new architecture for environmental governance, we must tread carefully. The essay’s optimism about the transformative power of deliberation and local autonomy glosses over deep problems of power, equity, and democratic accountability that, left unaddressed, could make the “rolling-rule regime” a vehicle for well-intentioned failure or, worse, a legitimating cover for inaction.

The first difficulty is that the essay treats “backyard environmentalism” as if it were a single, homogeneous tradition. In fact, the community-based struggles of Woburn and Love Canal were profoundly shaped by inequalities of race and class. The emerging environmental justice movement has made clear that low-income communities and communities of color disproportionately bear the burdens of toxic exposure, and they are often the very communities least able to mount the sophisticated, resource-intensive forms of participation the new regime demands. The “tributary teams” of the Chesapeake and the multi-stakeholder negotiations of the Southern California HCPs sound admirably inclusive, but they tend to draw on people with the time, expertise, and political connections to sit at the table. A rolling-rule regime that devolves authority to local units without strong, enforceable federal safeguards risks replicating—or even deepening—these disparities. It is no accident that the most celebrated successes, such as the Massachusetts TURA, occurred in a wealthy, well-organized state with a strong public-interest infrastructure. Without a deliberate effort to build capacity in overburdened communities and to ensure that their voices are not merely present but genuinely influential, performance-based regulation may simply shift power from distant bureaucracies to local elites.

A related concern is enforceability. The essay rightly insists that the new framework is not voluntarism, and that central authorities must hold local units accountable. But it is remarkably vague about the mechanisms. When a firm or a tributary team fails to meet its own performance targets, what happens? The authors point to peer pressure, reputational penalties, and the publication of comparative data as the primary discipline. TRI’s effect on stock prices is offered as proof that information alone can drive improvement. But stock markets react to many factors; the initial TRI releases led to headline-grabbing drops, but as investors grew accustomed to the data, the shock value likely diminished. More fundamentally, many of the actors in environmental governance are not publicly traded companies: they are private landowners, small farms, or municipalities. For them, public comparison may sting but not compel if the cost of compliance is high. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission retained formal authority to impose sanctions on power plants that failed INPO standards. By contrast, the Chesapeake Executive Council’s directives are of “dubious legal pedigree,” and TURA penalizes only willful violations of reporting duties, not failure to meet reduction targets. If the rolling-rule regime is to avoid the fate of Responsible Care’s vacillation—where a trade association couldn’t bring itself to discipline its own members—it must be backed by real regulatory teeth. The essay envisions Congress eventually conferring legitimacy on the new architecture, but until such a legislative framework is in place, the line between accountability and voluntarism remains dangerously thin.

The essay also makes a grand democratic claim: that the new regulatory form points toward a “directly deliberative participatory democracy” that resolves the century-old tension between efficiency and self-rule. The ambition is bracing, but the evidence falls short. In many of the examples, the circle of genuine decision-makers is narrow. HCPs, as the authors concede, often amount to a deal between a permit applicant and a field agent, with little meaningful public oversight. Even where participation is broader, it frequently mimics expert knowledge: citizens are trained to monitor water quality according to scientific protocols, effectively becoming “quasi-experts.” That is a valuable civic contribution, but it is not the same as deliberating about values, priorities, or the distribution of environmental harms and benefits. The risk is that this style of participation privileges technical learning over the messy, adversarial politics through which marginalized groups can challenge entrenched interests. The essay dismisses pluralist interest-group bargaining as prone to wild swings between over- and under-enforcement, but pluralism at least provides multiple access points for organized dissent. A system that funnels conflict into collaborative problem-solving teams may, in practice, suppress exactly the sort of fundamental criticism that the early backyard environmentalists wielded so effectively against corporate and governmental power.

Perhaps the most telling blind spot concerns the role of science itself. The essay repeatedly emphasizes that we can never have enough information and that surprise is inevitable. That is a wise starting point. But it then places enormous faith in benchmarking, monitoring, and adaptive management to generate continuous improvement. Yet the history of environmental policy is littered with monitoring programs that produced torrents of data but only modest action. The Chesapeake Bay Program is again instructive: despite two decades of agreements, ambitious targets, and an army of citizen volunteers, by 1999 nutrient loadings have not fallen nearly enough, and the 40 percent reduction goal for 2000 will almost certainly be missed. The problem is not that the collaborative process lacks intelligence, but that the economic and political incentives running against tough action remain formidable. A rolling-rule regime can refine standards over time, but it cannot by itself create the political will to impose costs on powerful agricultural or development interests.

None of this is to reject the pragmatist impulse that infuses the essay. The current regulatory system is indeed overloaded and often counterproductive, and the experiments with information pooling, local experimentation, and collaborative planning deserve to be expanded and refined. But they should be seen as supplements to, not replacements for, a strong federal regulatory floor. The most successful innovations—like TRI—worked because they were built on statutory disclosure requirements with clear penalties for noncompliance, and because they armed citizens with information they could use in traditional legal and political arenas. A genuine democratic renewal in environmental policy will require not just better collaboration between citizens and experts, but also robust legal rights, genuine enforcement capacity, and an unwavering commitment to ensuring that the poorest and most vulnerable backyards are not sacrificed to a consensus they had little part in shaping. Anything less risks turning a promising reform into a technocratic mirage.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Archon Fung, Charles Sabel, and Bradley Karkkainen offer a deeply appealing vision of environmental governance for the twenty-first century. Their "rolling-rule regime" suggests that we can move beyond the adversarial gridlock of 1970s-era command-and-control regulation and the theoretical artificiality of market-based mechanisms. By embracing decentralized experimentation, centralized information pooling, and continuous benchmarking, the authors argue we can achieve both better ecological outcomes and a more vibrant, directly deliberative democracy. However, while they accurately identify a set of genuinely innovative administrative tools that have emerged in the 1990s, their optimism glosses over the structural power imbalances that define environmental conflicts. In their eagerness to transcend traditional regulation, the authors fail to recognize that the collaborative successes they celebrate exist entirely within the shadow of the very command-and-control mandates they wish to leave behind.

The authors’ reliance on Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs) as the crown jewel of their new architecture is the clearest example of this blind spot. It is true that in places like San Diego and Orange Counties, developers, local officials, and conservationists have come together to negotiate complex, multi-species landscape plans. But we must ask why these historically antagonistic parties are sitting at the same table. The answer is not a spontaneous realization that directly deliberative problem-solving is superior; the answer is Section 9 of the Endangered Species Act. The sweeping, inflexible prohibition against the "taking" of listed species acts as an absolute hammer. It is precisely because the traditional regulatory baseline is so rigid and punitive that landowners are willing to endure the costly, time-consuming HCP process. If we move beyond command-and-control and view flexibility as the primary goal, we risk removing the baseline threat that compels industry to negotiate in good faith. The authors treat the ESA’s inflexibility as a flaw to be corrected, when in reality, it is the vital prerequisite for the collaboration they praise.

We see a similar dynamic in the authors’ treatment of information-based regulation, such as the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) and the Massachusetts Toxics Use Reduction Act (TURA). There is no denying that TRI has been a powerful tool, substituting the heavy hand of direct emission limits with the "sunlight" of mandatory disclosure. But information pooling does not automatically yield improved performance unless there is a mechanism to translate data into leverage. TRI works largely because poor environmental performance can depress stock prices and invite massive public relations crises. This relies on a specific set of market conditions—usually involving consumer-facing corporations vulnerable to public shaming. When the polluter is a local monopoly, a municipality, or a diffuse network of agricultural operations, information alone does not alter behavior. Even the Institute of Nuclear Power Operations (INPO), which the authors cite as a model of private standard-setting, only achieved its safety improvements because the Nuclear Regulatory Commission stood behind it, holding the ultimate authority to shut down non-compliant reactors.

Furthermore, the concept of the "deep user"—the citizen who moves beyond voting or protesting to actively formulate and implement environmental solutions—is theoretically elegant but practically fraught. In a performance-based regime, local units are tasked with setting targets, devising metrics, and monitoring outcomes. Participating effectively in a Habitat Conservation Plan negotiation or a Chesapeake Bay tributary team requires immense resources: attorneys, hydrologists, biological modelers, and, most importantly, time. Corporate stakeholders and large developers possess these resources and treat them as standard business expenses. For local citizens and volunteer environmentalists, this level of engagement represents a staggering, often unsustainable burden.

By demanding "deep use," the rolling-rule regime effectively privatizes the costs of regulatory design and enforcement, shifting them onto civil society. Over time, this dynamic is highly susceptible to local regulatory capture. As citizen volunteers inevitably exhaust their limited resources and burn out, well-funded corporate interests are left to dominate the supposedly collaborative tributary teams and working groups. When the center devolves authority to the periphery without heavily subsidizing the capacity of ordinary citizens to participate, "direct deliberation" simply becomes a venue where the most well-resourced voices dictate the terms of their own regulation.

The authors also dismiss pluralist interest-group politics as anachronistic, suggesting that disciplined deliberation can lead protagonists to "discover unanticipated solutions provisionally acceptable to all." This fundamentally misreads the nature of many environmental disputes, which are often rooted in zero-sum conflicts over values and material interests. A real estate developer’s financial imperative to pave a coastal sage scrub habitat and a conservationist’s moral imperative to preserve it for the California gnatcatcher cannot always be harmonized through "continuous benchmarking" or "adaptive management." Pluralist politics, with all its messy, centralized legislative and judicial battles, exists precisely to arbitrate between competing values that cannot be seamlessly integrated at the local level.

Ultimately, the innovations the authors describe—flexible compliance, information disclosure, and local adaptation—are highly valuable administrative tools that are already improving how the EPA and the Department of the Interior do their jobs. But they do not constitute a new architecture of democracy that can stand independent of centralized command. They are, instead, specialized grafts on the sturdy, if inflexible, trunk of the bedrock environmental statutes of the 1970s. To ensure that "backyard environmentalism" serves the public interest rather than the interests of the regulated, we must maintain the unyielding federal standards that force private actors to the table in the first place.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are right to notice something important: American environmental regulation is no longer adequately described by the old contest between centralized command and deregulation. Watersheds, habitat plans, toxics-use reduction, community right-to-know laws, and firm-level pollution prevention all show that information, participation, and iterative learning can do work that neither fixed technology standards nor simple markets can do. Their essay is especially persuasive in arguing that citizens are not merely consumers of expert judgment. In many environmental settings, citizens possess indispensable knowledge about land use, workplace routines, neighborhood illness patterns, local streams, seasonal changes, and institutional evasions. A regulatory system that ignores that knowledge will often be blind.

But the authors’ enthusiasm for “rolling-rule” regulation risks underestimating the virtues of the very adversarial environmentalism they hope to move beyond. Backyard environmentalism did not arise because citizens failed to appreciate deliberation. It arose because ordinary people had repeatedly been lied to, ignored, or patronized by firms and public agencies. Love Canal, Woburn, and Bhopal generated a politics of mistrust for good reason. The question, then, is not whether collaborative, performance-based regulation can supplement command-and-control law. It plainly can. The harder question is whether it can do so without dissolving the coercive public commitments that made collaboration worth pursuing in the first place.

Consider the examples in the essay. TRI works not because it convenes everyone in a trusting conversation, but because it compels disclosure. Firms do not volunteer toxic-release information out of civic generosity; they report because law requires them to report, and because communities, investors, journalists, workers, and regulators may use the information against them. TURA likewise works because Massachusetts put a legal structure around planning, certification, technical assistance, and public evaluation. The Chesapeake Bay Program has been successful partly because the Bay has unusually high public salience, a long-standing advocacy community, and an institutional structure capable of keeping governors, EPA, scientists, farmers, and citizen groups at the table. Habitat Conservation Plans, by contrast, show how quickly “local flexibility” can become private bargaining between a permit applicant and an under-resourced field office.

This suggests a less romantic lesson than the authors draw. Experimental regulation works best in the shadow of enforceable law. Without a credible threat of prohibition, penalty, or permit denial, deliberation easily becomes delay. Without baseline rights to information and participation, collaboration privileges those who already have lawyers, consultants, and access. Without national floors, local experimentation can produce local sacrifice zones. The rolling rule must roll forward, not sideways or backward.

The authors acknowledge this danger, especially in their discussion of Responsible Care and weak HCP oversight. But they do not give enough weight to inequality among participants. “Deep use” sounds empowering, but deep participation is expensive. Developers attend meetings with engineers, attorneys, biologists, and financial models. Chemical firms employ compliance staff. Farmers may participate only after a day’s work, and low-income residents may lack transportation, child care, technical training, or confidence that their testimony will matter. Communities already overburdened by pollution often have the least capacity to become the “deep users” on whom the new regime depends.

This is not a theoretical worry. Environmental justice advocates have spent the last decade showing that poor communities and communities of color often bear disproportionate exposure to toxic facilities, waste sites, and degraded urban environments. A performance-based system that relies on local bargaining may reproduce those disparities unless it contains affirmative supports: funding for independent technical assistance, translation, citizen monitoring, intervenor participation, and legal representation. If regulatory innovation simply tells disadvantaged communities to deliberate with better-organized adversaries, it may convert exclusion into a more polite procedure.

There is also a problem of measurement. Performance-based regulation depends on reliable indicators, but environmental harms are often cumulative, delayed, synergistic, and uncertain. Toxic releases can be estimated inaccurately. Biological monitoring may detect decline only after damage is difficult to reverse. Species recovery cannot always be reduced to near-term measurable goals. Non-point pollution can be notoriously difficult to attribute. The authors are correct that traditional regulation often pretended to know too much. Yet experimental regulation can make the opposite mistake: mistaking what can be conveniently measured for what matters most.

Adaptive management is attractive because it promises correction in light of experience. But adaptation after irreversible loss is not enough. If an HCP permits destruction of habitat on the assurance that monitoring will reveal whether mitigation works, what happens if the mitigation fails? If a watershed program allows continued nutrient loading while models are refined, what happens to fisheries in the meantime? Precaution is not the enemy of learning. In many ecological settings, it is the condition for learning responsibly.

That is why the authors’ call for congressional validation is important, but it should be more demanding. Congress should not merely bless experimental local problem-solving. It should define minimum substantive obligations: no avoidable jeopardy to listed species, no undisclosed toxic use, no weakening of existing health-based standards, no exemption from citizen suits, no secrecy in negotiated compliance arrangements. It should also require independent auditing of data, public access to plans and results, and periodic review by agencies with enough money and staff to do the job. A national information infrastructure is not a decorative supplement to rolling-rule regulation; it is the mechanism that prevents decentralization from becoming fragmentation.

The authors are also too quick to dismiss market mechanisms as information-hungry and inadequate. The sulfur dioxide trading program created under the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments has shown, at least so far, that cap-and-trade can achieve substantial reductions at lower-than-expected cost when the pollutant is measurable, sources are identifiable, and the cap is firm. Markets are no panacea, and they are poorly suited to many toxics, habitat, and justice concerns. But the right lesson is not that markets fail where deliberation succeeds. It is that different environmental problems require different institutional mixtures: fixed bans for some hazards, technology standards for others, tradable permits where emissions are fungible and monitored, disclosure where reputational pressure matters, and collaborative planning where ecological complexity demands local knowledge.

The most promising part of the essay is its insistence that democracy can be practical rather than merely expressive. Citizens should not be confined to voting every few years or speaking for three minutes at a public hearing after decisions have effectively been made. They can monitor streams, challenge emissions inventories, help design habitat corridors, and bring local knowledge into regulatory judgment. That is a democratic advance.

But democracy also requires conflict, rights, and enforceable public authority. The citizen who says “not in my backyard” may be narrow, but she may also be the first person telling the truth. The task is not to move beyond backyard environmentalism so much as to preserve its moral energy while building institutions capable of asking the next question: if not this, then what; if not here, then where; if development proceeds, under what binding safeguards; and who will know whether promises are kept?

Performance-based environmentalism deserves a future. It will earn one only if collaboration remains accountable to law, science remains open to challenge, participation is materially supported, and national standards prevent local experiments from becoming local defeats. The backyard still needs defending, even when its defenders are invited inside the meeting room.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

The lead essay presents an elegant vision of environmental governance that promises to reconcile democratic participation with regulatory effectiveness. Yet this promise warrants closer scrutiny before we embrace what may prove to be an incomplete answer to enduring challenges of environmental protection.

The authors rightly identify the limitations of both command regulation and market-based approaches. Centralized rules cannot anticipate every contingency, while tradable permits demand information requirements that regulators cannot satisfy. But the alternative proposed—a rolling-rule regime that combines local experimentation with centralized pooling of experience—depends on conditions that may not reliably obtain. In particular, the success of this framework hinges on two assumptions that deserve examination: that meaningful participation by ordinary citizens is achievable, and that information pooling will effectively discipline local actors.

Consider first the democratic theory embedded in the performance-based framework. The authors argue that the new regime requires "deeper" participation than traditional forms—not merely expressing opinions or demanding solutions, but helping formulate and implement them. This vision is appealing, but it presupposes civic capacities that remain unevenly distributed. The tributary teams described in the Chesapeake Bay Program may work well when committed volunteers and well-resourced participants engage in sustained deliberation. Yet the communities most burdened by environmental hazards often lack the time, technical resources, and institutional support to participate in such demanding processes. The essay acknowledges this tension only obliquely, noting that organizing participation places "additional demands" on citizens. This understated observation may conceal a fundamental difficulty: deep participation may remain the province of the engaged and the educated, while those most affected by pollution remain on the margins.

The authors' treatment of the Chesapeake Bay Program illustrates both the promise and the limits of adaptive management. The program has undoubtedly achieved significant innovations in multi-jurisdictional coordination and public engagement. Yet after nearly two decades of effort, the Bay's impairment persists. The authors acknowledge that "the more we learn about the ecology of the Bay, the more surprising new findings will be." But surprises, however intellectually stimulating, do not automatically translate into improved outcomes. The essay would benefit from grappling more directly with the question of why adaptive management, despite its theoretical elegance, has proven insufficient to restore the Bay.

The discussion of Responsible Care offers a useful counterpoint that the authors use to illustrate the necessity of public accountability. Their analysis is sound: industry self-regulation tends toward capture when private trade associations must simultaneously discipline members and maintain consensus. Yet this case also reveals a broader pattern that the rolling-rule framework may not adequately address. The authors argue that transparency and public accountability can resolve the stalemate that afflicts purely private regimes. Yet they provide limited guidance on how such public accountability can be instituted and maintained against industry resistance. The INPO example demonstrates that public oversight can enhance private efforts, but nuclear power generation is a concentrated industry with a small number of major players. Diffuse pollution sources—farms, households, small manufacturers—present different challenges that may not be amenable to the same institutional solutions.

The Miller Bill referenced in the essay represents a potentially promising legislative step, but its fate remains uncertain as of this writing. The authors recognize that administrative reform unbacked by law may prove vulnerable when political conditions shift. This concern is well-founded. The HCP experience reveals how even nominally promising innovations can degenerate when national coordination is weak. The authors note that the Fish and Wildlife Service "has so far proved far better at dispersing authority to local decision makers than at reviewing the ensuing decisions." This structural weakness undermines the rolling-rule framework's core mechanism: local experimentation disciplined by centralized pooling. Without effective pooling, decentralization becomes merely devolution without accountability.

The essay concludes by suggesting that the confluence of expertise and local participation holds "the promise of transcending their separate limitations." This aspiration is genuine, but its realization depends on institutional conditions that remain incompletely developed. The authors are right that command regulation alone cannot address modern environmental challenges. They are also right that markets cannot substitute for deliberative processes that connect scientific knowledge to local circumstances. But between these acknowledgments and the confident assertion that the new framework will prove "more effective than current arrangements, and more democratic," lies a substantial gap that requires not merely architectural imagination but political will and institutional capacity that cannot be assumed.

The environmental challenges that prompted the first generation of backyard environmentalism persist. The regulatory innovations described in this essay offer genuinely useful tools for addressing those challenges. But we should approach them with clear-eyed recognition of their limitations—and with continued insistence on the robust public accountability that even the most sophisticated rolling rules cannot substitute for.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have written a captivating piece, and that is exactly what worries me. Let me concede what is worth conceding. The Toxics Release Inventory has indeed been one of the most successful environmental statutes of the last quarter century, precisely because it harnessed reputational pressure rather than relying solely on administrative penalty. Aggregate toxic releases as reported under TRI have fallen substantially since 1988, and we have a generation of environmental journalists and community organizers who would not exist without that data. The Massachusetts TURA program builds on that base intelligently. The 1987 Chesapeake Bay Agreement marked a real institutional achievement. None of this is in dispute.

What is in dispute is the inference the authors draw from these cases—that we are witnessing the birth of a new regulatory paradigm that supersedes both command-and-control and market-based approaches, and that we should accelerate the transition. I think this inference rests on three confusions.

The first is a confusion about what made these programs work. TRI works because behind it sits the residual threat of substantive command-and-control statutes—RCRA, CERCLA, the Clean Air Act—that give the disclosed information bite. A firm that releases enormous quantities of a TRI chemical is not merely embarrassed; it is also exposed to citizen suits, to potential listing decisions, to scrutiny under permit programs. Strip away that surrounding architecture and TRI becomes a press-release exercise. Similarly, the Chesapeake Bay Program operates against the backdrop of point-source permitting under the CWA. The "directives" that the authors describe as having "dubious legal pedigree" are persuasive precisely because the agencies signing them have real legal authority elsewhere. The rolling-rule regime is, in this sense, parasitic on the very command-and-control system the authors are eager to declare exhausted.

The second confusion is about Habitat Conservation Plans, which the authors hold up as their most ambitious example. The reality of HCPs as practiced in the 1990s is considerably uglier than this essay lets on. The Service has issued plans with "No Surprises" assurances locking in landowner obligations for fifty or even one hundred years—periods over which we cannot remotely predict the trajectory of climate, hydrology, or species behavior. Take Babbitt's flagship Southern California plans: the gnatcatcher reserves were drawn on maps largely by developers, with biologists brought in to bless the result. The authors themselves admit, in a footnote that deserves more attention than they give it, that as of July 1998 no one in Washington had even collected the HCPs that had been negotiated, much less read them. This is not "deep use." It is decentralization without the conditions that would make decentralization accountable. To call this proto-democratic is to mistake an enforcement vacuum for a participatory triumph.

The third confusion is about deliberation itself. The authors imagine a process in which "protagonists discover unanticipated solutions provisionally acceptable to all" through "disciplined consideration of alternative policies." This is a beautiful picture. It is also one that ignores everything we know about the bargaining power differentials that structure environmental conflicts. The Pacific Lumber HCP, the Plum Creek HCP, the Murray Pacific HCP—these were not Habermasian conversations. They were negotiations between a highly capitalized resource extraction firm with experienced counsel and a federal agency under enormous political pressure to "demonstrate the workability" of flexibility, in the authors' own approving phrase. The local conservation groups that the authors imagine as equal participants either lacked the technical capacity to engage meaningfully, or were deliberately excluded, or—most often—participated in good faith and were rolled. John Kostyack's work for the National Wildlife Federation, which the authors cite, makes this point with considerably more bite than the authors acknowledge.

There is a deeper issue here about the politics of regulatory reform. The case for command-and-control was never that bureaucrats in Washington possessed "modest omniscience." It was that diffuse, vulnerable interests—future generations, voiceless species, downstream communities, poor neighborhoods—are systematically underrepresented in any bargaining-based process, and that the function of national rules is to set non-negotiable floors that protect those interests against the immediate pressures of local politics. The genius of the Clean Air Act was not that EPA knew best; it was that EPA could insist on lead-free gasoline, on the phase-out of CFCs, on acid rain controls, over the heads of industries and state governments that would have bargained those gains away locality by locality. Lead in children's blood fell by roughly three-quarters between 1976 and the early 1990s because of a command, not a conversation.

I am not against learning. I am not against monitoring, benchmarking, or local experimentation. I am against the rhetorical move that uses the genuine successes of information-disclosure programs to argue for the dismantling of mandatory standards. And I notice that this move is being made not only by Sabel and his co-authors but by precisely those regulated industries that have the most to gain from rolling rules that, in practice, roll only in one direction.

The authors anticipate this objection and respond that pluralist politics has not in fact produced "exquisite balance" but rather "a welter of clashing rules" and "self-canceling swings of policy." Fair enough. But the answer to dysfunction in pluralist democracy is not to replace it with an unaccountable network of stakeholder negotiations whose outputs no elected body has ratified. The authors notice this problem, gesture toward the Miller Bill, and hope Congress will somehow bless the new architecture. Yet they do not engage seriously with the possibility that Congress, looking at the actual record of HCPs, might decide to constrain rather than authorize them—and that this would not be a failure of political imagination but a reasonable response to the evidence.

What I would propose instead is something more modest than the authors' transformative vision. First, retain mandatory national floors—including substantive standards for endangered species, ambient air and water quality, toxic releases—as the non-negotiable baseline. Second, embrace information-disclosure regimes like TRI as supplements that intensify pressure for performance above the floor, not as replacements for the floor itself. Third, demand from any locally-negotiated arrangement (HCP, watershed plan, etc.) the procedural safeguards the Miller Bill outlines—objective biological goals, monitoring, public participation, periodic review—and the legal authority to revoke approvals when those safeguards fail. Fourth, fund the national environmental groups and academic centers that can serve as independent monitors, because without them the "pooling of experience" the authors celebrate will be done by the regulated industries themselves.

This is not a sexy program. It does not promise a "novel form of democracy." It does promise that when a developer in San Diego County wants to take the last reasonable habitat for a listed species, we will say no, and mean it.

The authors close with an inspiring quotation from John Dewey, the spirit of which I share. Dewey believed that democracy required ongoing inquiry and experimentation. He also believed, less often quoted, that publics form around problems they cannot solve through immediate face-to-face deliberation, and that the function of the state was to constitute those publics. The administrative state we have, for all its flaws, is the institutional sediment of that insight. Before we celebrate its supersession, we should ask whether what is replacing it really has more democratic credentials, or merely a more flattering vocabulary.

I have personally watched my firm spend tens of millions of dollars retrofitting equipment to meet a technology-based standard that, by the time we installed it, had already been superseded by cheaper and cleaner alternatives we were not allowed to use. The "Best Available Control Technology" framework is the regulatory equivalent of buying a 1992 computer in 1999 because that's what the catalog says.

The authors are also right that the Toxics Release Inventory has been transformative. I was in the room when our senior leadership saw the first published TRI rankings in 1989. I have rarely seen anything concentrate executive attention so quickly. Within eighteen months we had a corporate-wide reduction program that produced cuts no rule-based system would have extracted from us, because we found efficiencies our engineers had never been incentivized to find. The Massachusetts TURA experience the authors describe is consistent with what we saw: once you require a plan, the planning itself reveals waste, and the waste, once revealed, gets reduced because reducing it is profitable.

The reason TRI worked is not that it activated deep deliberative citizenship; it is that it created a competitive comparison among firms that imposed reputational costs on laggards. The work was done not by community deliberation but by Wall Street analysts and trade press journalists. The authors note in passing that TRI disclosures move share prices, and then proceed to talk as if the engine of the reform were town-hall democracy. The actual engine is more prosaic: in a world where firms compete for capital, customers, and skilled workers, making environmental performance visible makes it expensive to be dirty. This is closer to a Hayekian story about information than a Deweyan story about deliberation.

Responsible Care, the chemical industry's program, gets a glancing dismissal in the essay—"so far inconclusive," "risks degenerating into a public-relations maneuver." I think this is unfair. Responsible Care has produced real improvements in process safety across CMA member firms, and it has done so without the prodding of an external monitor. It struggles where the authors say it struggles—with members who refuse to discipline themselves—but the answer to that problem is not necessarily the importation of "public institutions and authority" along the lines of INPO. The INPO model worked in nuclear power because there are about a hundred commercial reactors in the United States, all owned by utilities with deep regulatory entanglements, and because a single Three Mile Island can destroy the industry. The chemical sector is more diffuse, more competitive, and less existentially exposed. Different sectors will need different governance mixes.

The ISO 14001 standard, which has spread rapidly through global manufacturing since its publication in 1996, provides a generic template for this. Firms that have implemented 14001 seriously can engage productively with the kind of performance-based regulation the authors describe; firms that have not, cannot. The authors are correct that formal cost-benefit analysis as practiced by OIRA does not deliver the orderly decentralization its promoters promised. The discipline of asking "what does this rule actually cost, and what does it actually deliver" is not the enemy of the rolling-rule regime; it is one of its essential intellectual tools. Without something like cost-benefit reasoning, the "minimal performance standards" and "plausible targets for improvement" the authors talk about cannot be calibrated. The choice is not between cost-benefit analysis and deliberation; it is between cost-benefit analysis done well and cost-benefit analysis done badly.

They have, in my view, slightly mistaken its character by giving it a participatory-democratic gloss that the underlying mechanisms do not quite support. The new architecture works because it harnesses competitive pressure, measurement, and continuous improvement—tools that come as readily from operations research and quality management as from democratic theory. Those of us who have been working on environmental performance from inside firms welcome the shift. A Deweyan Cheer, and Some Hard Questions About Scale

This essay does something rare and valuable. It identifies a genuinely novel institutional pattern emerging across diverse policy settings and offers a philosophical framework—broadly pragmatist, recognizably Deweyan—for understanding what binds those settings together. Whatever one ultimately concludes about its prescriptions, Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have given the rest of us a vocabulary and a set of cases to argue about. That itself is a contribution.

I want to make the case that they are onto something deep, while pressing them on three questions they do not adequately answer: the question of scale, the question of inequality, and the question of which problems this architecture can and cannot address.

First, the deep insight. The twentieth century has been dominated, in democratic theory, by a fundamental tension between two models of the polity. On one side stands the Schumpeterian conception of democracy as electoral competition among elites, with citizens reduced to periodic ratifiers of leadership choices. On the other stands the participatory ideal, of which Dewey's "The Public and Its Problems" is the canonical American expression, holding that democracy is meaningless unless ordinary citizens engage substantively in the inquiry by which collective problems are framed and addressed. Most defenders of participation have struggled to specify institutional forms that could realize the ideal at scales above the New England town meeting. The familiar candidates—worker cooperatives, neighborhood councils, "strong democracy" of the kind Benjamin Barber sketched in the 1980s—either remain marginal or operate at scales too small to engage with serious policy problems.

What the authors are arguing, in effect, is that environmental regulation has stumbled onto an institutional form that resolves this scale problem. The local "deep user" units are small enough to permit genuine face-to-face deliberation and the integration of local knowledge. The pooling infrastructure—TRI databases, INPO benchmarking, Chesapeake monitoring—operates at the larger scale where comparison and discipline become possible. The center authorizes and enforces a framework rather than dictating outcomes. If this works, it is a real institutional discovery, comparable in conceptual significance to the joint-stock corporation or the regulatory agency itself.

The Chesapeake Bay case is, on this reading, the most important case the authors discuss, precisely because it shows the iterative redefinition of citizen-expert relations over thirty years. The initial conception of "participation" as public education gives way to participation as voluntary monitoring under expert protocols, which gives way in turn to the tributary teams as composite expert-citizen entities producing locally adapted knowledge. This is exactly the kind of institutional learning Dewey described as the lifeblood of democratic inquiry.

Now for the hard questions.

The first is the question of scale upward. The authors are persuasive about local-to-regional aggregation, but they are largely silent about the genuinely global environmental problems that have come to dominate the policy agenda: climate change, ozone depletion, biodiversity loss as a global phenomenon, persistent organic pollutants. These problems do not decompose neatly into the local units the rolling-rule regime requires. Carbon dioxide emitted in Pittsburgh affects the climate of Bangladesh. Who is the "deep user" community for global climate regulation? The 1997 Kyoto Protocol negotiations made clear that solutions at this scale will require some combination of treaty-based commitments, international market mechanisms (the Protocol's flexibility provisions), and national implementation policies. The authors' architecture has little to say about any of this. One might respond that climate is sui generis, but I think the truth is that the rolling-rule regime is suited to a particular class of problems—those involving spatially distributed sources whose effects are felt in identifiable localities, where local knowledge has real value—and is less well suited to others.

The second hard question is about inequality. The authors imagine local units composed of citizens with the time, education, and resources to participate as quasi-experts in technically demanding deliberations. The Chesapeake tributary teams, the Southern California HCP negotiations, the workplace toxics reduction planning committees—all of these are populated, in practice, by people of a particular class. They are not representative of the surrounding population. The waters and lands of poor and minority communities in this country have historically been treated as sacrifice zones, and there is little in the authors' architecture that would automatically correct this. The environmental justice movement, drawing on the work of Robert Bullard and others, has documented systematic disparities in the location of hazardous facilities and the enforcement of environmental laws. The 1994 environmental justice executive order acknowledged the problem at the federal level but has changed little on the ground. A regulatory regime that hands decision authority to local deliberative bodies, without first addressing whose voices count in those bodies, risks consolidating rather than correcting these disparities. The authors should engage this critique directly.

The third hard question concerns the relationship between deliberation and substantive values. The rolling-rule regime, as the authors describe it, is essentially proceduralist. It specifies how decisions should be made—through pooled information, local experimentation, iterative revision—but is largely agnostic about what values should govern outcomes. The endangered species case reveals the limits of this agnosticism. The Endangered Species Act expresses a particular substantive commitment: that the avoidance of extinction is a non-negotiable obligation, not subject to local cost-benefit weighing. The HCP process, as it has actually developed, has tended to soften this commitment, treating species protection as one consideration to be traded off against development imperatives. The authors present this as institutional learning. It can equally be read as a process by which a strong substantive norm has been quietly weakened by reframing it as procedural. Whether that is good or bad depends on whether you accept the underlying ethical claim of the ESA—a claim about which the authors' framework has nothing to say.

These three questions—scale, inequality, substance—do not refute the authors' argument. They locate it. The rolling-rule regime is, I think, a genuine institutional innovation suited to a particular range of problems, but it is not the architecture of all future democracy, and it cannot do the work that constitutional commitments and electoral accountability must continue to do.

I want to close with a thought about the political conditions for the architecture's survival. The authors note, rightly, that consolidation of the new regime will require open validation through Congress. But Congress has been moving in directions that make such validation unlikely. The 104th Congress's assault on EPA and the Endangered Species Act, the regulatory takings movement, the persistent attempts to roll back the CWA—these reflect a political coalition that wants devolution as a means of weakening environmental protection, not as a means of deepening democratic engagement with it. The authors' rolling-rule regime risks becoming, in this political context, the friendly face of a much less friendly project. They need to think harder about how to defend their architecture against capture by interests that share none of their democratic commitments.

That said, this essay has done what important essays do: it has named a phenomenon that needed naming and given us terms in which to argue. I would rather argue these questions in the authors' framework than in any of the alternatives currently on offer.

Don't Bury Command-and-Control Yet

There is a fashion in regulatory scholarship for declaring traditional environmental regulation obsolete. The Fung-Sabel-Karkkainen essay is the latest and most sophisticated entry in this genre, but the genre itself rests on a misreading of the historical record. Before we move beyond command-and-control, we ought to be honest about what command-and-control has actually accomplished.

Between 1970 and the late 1990s, the United States cut emissions of the six criteria pollutants under the Clean Air Act by about a third even as the economy grew by more than a hundred percent and vehicle-miles-traveled more than doubled. Lead in ambient air fell by about ninety-eight percent. Sulfur dioxide emissions fell by roughly forty percent. Stratospheric ozone-depleting substances were phased out under the Montreal Protocol and the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments, in what is arguably the most successful global environmental treaty in history. Major rivers that were biologically dead in 1970—the Cuyahoga, the lower Hudson, the Connecticut—now support fisheries and recreation. The bald eagle has been delisted in much of its range, the peregrine falcon delisted entirely, and the alligator long since recovered. None of this happened by accident, and none of it happened because EPA was running deliberative workshops with regulated entities.

It happened because Congress, in a series of landmark statutes from 1970 to 1990, set substantive standards backed by enforcement, technology-forcing requirements, deadlines, and citizen-suit provisions. The agency promulgated rules. Industries complied, often grumbling. When they did not comply, they were sued, by the agency or by citizens, and they paid. This is the unglamorous architecture that produced the environmental gains we have. To read Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen, you would think this whole apparatus has been failing for decades and that we are now lurching toward a more enlightened model. The data say otherwise.

Let me address the authors' specific complaints. They argue that command-and-control "attempts too little and too much." Too little, because it draws sharp lines around what is regulated. Too much, because it locks in particular technologies that may become outdated. Both criticisms contain a kernel of truth, but neither is decisive.

On the "too little" point: yes, the Clean Water Act has done more for point sources than for non-point sources. But this is partly because point sources were the easier problem and were addressed first, and partly because non-point source regulation runs into property-rights and federalism objections that have nothing to do with the limitations of command-and-control as such. The 1987 amendments to the CWA created the Section 319 nonpoint source program, and the Total Maximum Daily Load program is now beginning to bite on impaired waterways. The architecture is extending itself.

On the "too much" point: technology-based standards have indeed sometimes locked in suboptimal approaches. But the New Source Performance Standards under the CAA are revisable, and have been revised, on the basis of demonstrated improvements. The "Best Available Control Technology" determinations are made case-by-case in PSD permitting and explicitly take into account technological evolution. The system is not as rigid as the authors imply. And where it has been rigid, the rigidity has often been a feature, not a bug: it has compelled industry to find solutions it claimed were impossible. The phase-out of leaded gasoline, the catalytic converter, the reduction of acid rain precursors—these all involved compulsion, not deliberation.

I take the authors' point that programs like TRI have been valuable. But TRI is not an alternative to command-and-control; it is a complement to it, riding on top of the substantive obligations of RCRA, CERCLA, and the CAA. Strip away the underlying enforcement architecture and TRI becomes voluntary corporate sustainability reporting—useful, but no substitute for binding rules.

The Habitat Conservation Plans the authors celebrate are, in my view, the weakest part of their case. The HCP program is a workaround for the Endangered Species Act's strict take prohibition. It exists because landowners and developers complained about ESA enforcement, and because the Clinton administration wanted to defuse the political backlash that the spotted owl litigation had provoked. Whether HCPs produce better conservation outcomes than strict Section 9 enforcement is genuinely contested among ecologists, and the authors' own footnotes acknowledge serious monitoring and accountability problems. To hold up this fraught and recent experiment as the leading edge of a new regulatory paradigm is premature.

The authors also misunderstand, I think, the politics of the moment. They write as if environmentalism is now a settled fact and the only question is how to refine it. But the 1990s have seen sustained, well-funded attacks on the entire structure of federal environmental regulation: the Contract with America's regulatory reform agenda, the Lucas decision on regulatory takings, the unfunded mandates legislation, the persistent attempts to weaken the ESA. The rolling-rule rhetoric of decentralization, flexibility, and stakeholder negotiation is being deployed not only by sincere reformers but by interests that want to gut environmental protection while appearing reasonable. Anyone who proposes to dismantle existing enforcement architecture without securing alternative protections is, intentionally or not, providing cover for that project.

What I would urge instead is a more modest narrative. Command-and-control has accomplished an enormous amount and remains the indispensable core of American environmental regulation. Information-disclosure programs like TRI are valuable supplements that intensify pressure for performance. Market-based instruments, where well-designed (the acid rain SO2 trading program is the obvious success story), can deliver compliance more cheaply than uniform technology standards. Collaborative regional processes like the Chesapeake Bay Program have a role in addressing problems that don't fit neatly into existing statutory categories. All of these are tools in a toolkit. None of them, individually or collectively, replaces the substantive regulatory commitments that have produced the gains of the last thirty years.

What worries me about essays like the one under discussion is not the empirical observations, most of which are accurate. It is the framing: the suggestion that we are leaving an era of command-and-control behind. We are not, and we should not. The eagles, the rivers, and our own lungs are evidence enough.

Where Are the Markets in This Picture?

The Fung-Sabel-Karkkainen essay performs an interesting maneuver. It positions the "rolling-rule regime" as an alternative to both command-and-control regulation and market-based instruments. It then proceeds to discuss command-and-control regulation in some detail while dismissing market-based instruments in roughly two paragraphs. This rhetorical asymmetry matters, because the most important environmental policy innovation of the last decade in the United States has been a market mechanism—the acid rain SO2 trading program under Title IV of the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments—and the authors' framework cannot account for it.

Let me start with the empirical record. The Title IV program created a cap on sulfur dioxide emissions from electric utilities and allocated tradable allowances to existing emitters. Phase I, which began in 1995, covered the dirtiest 110 plants and required substantial reductions. Phase II, beginning in 2000, will extend the program to essentially all utility units above 25 MW with a substantially tighter cap. By any reasonable measure, the program has been a striking success. Compliance has been near-universal. The achieved emissions reductions have exceeded the cap, with substantial early banking of allowances. The cost of compliance has been dramatically lower than pre-program estimates by EPA, industry, and academic analysts—by some estimates an order of magnitude lower. Allowance prices have been roughly half what was projected. The program achieved environmental targets at a fraction of what command-and-control would have cost. It has worked.

The authors' two-paragraph dismissal of tradable permits asserts that such programs require "extraordinary quantities and varieties of information" from a central regulator and that markets "depend on secure ownership rights" which limit post-hoc corrections. Both assertions are misleading in light of the Title IV experience. The information required to run the SO2 program was minimal: existing emissions inventories (which were already required for SIP purposes), an aggregate cap, and continuous emissions monitoring at covered units. The regulator did not need to know abatement costs at each source; the whole point of trading is that the market discovers those costs. Nor have property-rights concerns prevented program adjustment: the cap has built-in tightening from Phase I to Phase II, and Congress retains authority to alter the program prospectively.

What the authors miss, I think, is that well-designed market mechanisms are themselves a form of decentralized learning. The allowance market aggregates information about abatement costs from thousands of separately-managed sources, and produces a price signal that guides further investment. This is exactly the kind of decentralized discovery process the authors say they value—they just don't recognize it when it operates through prices rather than through deliberation.

I want to suggest a different way of organizing the regulatory landscape. The choice in environmental policy is not between command-and-control, markets, and deliberation, but between regulatory instruments well-matched to the problems they address and instruments poorly matched. Different environmental problems have different characteristics, and different instruments are appropriate to different characteristics.

Where a pollutant is well-mixed across a broad airshed, where sources are relatively homogeneous and easily monitored, and where the science of the dose-response relationship is reasonably well established, tradable allowances are the right tool. SO2 is the canonical case. NOx trading programs in the Northeast are working on similar logic. CO2, if and when we get serious about climate, will eventually demand something similar.

Where a pollutant is localized in its effects, where exposure varies sharply by site and demographic group, where toxicity is high at low doses, and where source-specific information is critical—the rolling-rule regime the authors describe may indeed be the right tool. Toxics fit this profile. Habitat conservation, with its irreducibly site-specific character, fits this profile.

Where the science is contested, where the threat is catastrophic and irreversible, and where standard-setting is itself a profound political choice—command-and-control with citizen-suit enforcement remains the right tool. Endangered species protection at the listing-and-take stage fits this profile. So does the protection of ambient air and water quality at health-based standards.

Notice that this taxonomy does not endorse one architecture as the regulatory framework of the future. It treats environmental regulation as an applied problem requiring instrument choice on a case-by-case basis. This is, I would argue, the appropriately pragmatic stance, and it is closer to what the best environmental economists have been arguing for years—people like Robert Stavins, Richard Schmalensee, Paul Portney, the Resources for the Future group.

The authors do gesture at instrument choice in passing, but they cannot bring themselves to take market mechanisms seriously because their underlying intellectual project is to vindicate "directly deliberative" democracy. They want to find an architecture that makes participation efficient, so that democratic engagement is no longer in tension with effective regulation. This is a noble project. But it leads them to misdescribe the policy landscape and to undervalue tools that work for reasons that have nothing to do with deliberation.

Let me also press the authors on the question of cost. Nowhere in the essay is there a serious engagement with the costs of the rolling-rule regime. The "deep participation" they advocate is not free. Citizens who participate seriously in tributary teams or HCP planning give up substantial time, often without compensation. It would not mean privileging one architectural form across all problem types. The Title IV SO2 program is a pragmatist success. So, in their own ways, are TRI and the Chesapeake Bay Program. Whose Backyard? Environmental Justice and the Limits of Deliberation

Reading "Beyond Backyard Environmentalism" from the perspective of the environmental justice movement is a frustrating experience. The essay is sophisticated, theoretically ambitious, and largely indifferent to questions of race, class, and power that have come to define environmental politics outside its academic frame. I want to use this response to make those questions visible, because if they remain invisible the rolling-rule regime the authors champion will likely deepen rather than alleviate the injustices it ignores.

Let us begin with the framing. The authors open with a celebration of Woburn, Love Canal, and "countless other communities" that have organized against pollution in their neighborhoods. They acknowledge the NIMBY tradition and propose to move beyond it. What goes unmentioned is that the locations of the country's most contaminated sites are not random. Robert Bullard's "Dumping in Dixie" (1990) and his subsequent work, the United Church of Christ's 1987 report on toxic waste and race, the Warren County PCB protests of 1982, and the establishment of EPA's Office of Environmental Equity in 1992—a whole body of scholarship and advocacy has documented that hazardous facilities, contaminated water, polluted air, and incomplete enforcement disproportionately affect communities of color and low-income communities. The 1994 environmental justice executive order acknowledged this at the federal level. None of it appears in the authors' essay.

This omission is not incidental. The "deep user" model the authors propose imagines local communities as effective participants in regulatory deliberation. In practice, the capacity to participate effectively is unequally distributed. Communities with educated, well-resourced, organized residents—the kind of communities that produced the Chesapeake Bay Foundation in 1966 or that staffed the early environmental movement—can play the role the authors describe. Communities that lack these resources cannot. The result of devolving regulatory authority to "local units" will, predictably, be that some local units exercise it powerfully and others do not.

Consider the Southern California HCPs the authors celebrate. The negotiations that produced these plans involved developers, public officials, conservation biologists, and representatives of the major national environmental organizations. Notable for their absence were the predominantly Latino and immigrant communities of inland Southern California, whose neighborhoods bore the brunt of the development the HCPs accommodated, and whose access to the remaining open space the agreements limited. The "exemplary" Southern California HCPs are exemplary in part because they served the interests of relatively privileged constituencies on both sides of the deal. They are not models for communities with different demographic profiles and different bargaining capacities.

The TRI experience presents a more complicated picture. On the positive side, TRI data has empowered some environmental justice organizing. Communities that suspected they were being poisoned could finally document the suspicion. Groups like West Harlem Environmental Action, Concerned Citizens of South Central Los Angeles, the Southwest Network for Environmental and Economic Justice, and many others have used TRI data effectively. On the negative side, the technical complexity of TRI data favors organizations with research capacity, and the reductions TRI has produced have not been evenly distributed. Some studies suggest that releases have declined more in white and affluent areas than in communities of color, though this finding is contested. At a minimum, TRI does not automatically correct disparities; it requires concerted advocacy to do so.

The Massachusetts TURA experience, similarly, has produced substantial reductions in toxics use, but the regulated firms have considerable discretion in which toxics to reduce and where. There is no requirement that reductions be concentrated in the communities most exposed. A facility in Lowell can meet its TURA obligations through reductions that primarily benefit workers and surrounding residents who are already comparatively protected, while leaving more exposed sites elsewhere unchanged.

These observations should not be read as a wholesale rejection of the architecture the authors propose. The capacity-building, planning, and disclosure elements of the rolling-rule regime are, in principle, available to environmental justice communities as much as to anyone. The question is whether they will be available in practice, and what conditions would have to obtain for them to be.

I would suggest four such conditions, none of which the authors adequately address.

First, the rolling-rule regime needs robust national substantive floors that protect vulnerable communities against the local bargains they may lack power to influence. The Endangered Species Act's strict take prohibition is a substantive floor of this kind. The Clean Air Act's National Ambient Air Quality Standards are another. The authors' enthusiasm for negotiated flexibility tends to push against such floors. They should be defending floors more vigorously, on environmental justice grounds.

Second, the regime needs serious resource transfers to underrepresented communities. The TURI in Massachusetts is a model of public capacity-building, but it serves regulated firms, not affected communities. We need a counterpart institution—well-funded, accessible, and organizationally independent of regulated industry—that builds the technical capacity of environmental justice communities to engage with rolling-rule processes. Without such an institution, the "deep user" framework will reproduce existing inequalities in participatory capacity.

Third, the regime needs procedural requirements specifically designed to incorporate marginalized voices. "Public participation" as currently practiced—public meetings, written comments, notice-and-comment rulemaking—is a procedural fiction in most communities. Real participation requires outreach, translation, child care, schedule accommodation, compensation for citizen time. These are mundane requirements, but in their absence "deliberation" is a euphemism for elite negotiation.

Fourth, the regime needs disaggregated outcome monitoring. If the rolling-rule regime is to produce environmentally just outcomes, we need to measure outcomes by community demographics, not just in aggregate. The Chesapeake Bay Program tracks nutrient loads and oyster populations; it does not track exposure disparities among Bay-region residents. The TRI tracks releases; it does not adequately track exposure. Until we monitor for justice, we will not produce it.

I want to be clear: I am not arguing that the rolling-rule regime is intrinsically hostile to environmental justice. I am arguing that it is not intrinsically friendly to it either. Like most institutional forms, it produces outcomes that depend on the surrounding distribution of power and resources. The authors' essay is largely silent about this distribution, which is itself a political choice. To speak of "deep users" and "directly deliberative democracy" without specifying whose participation is deep and whose democracy is direct is to risk romanticizing a process that, in actual practice, can be deeply exclusionary.

There is a deeper problem here about democratic theory. The authors invoke John Dewey and the pragmatist tradition, and they are right that this tradition has powerful resources for thinking about participatory democracy. But Dewey was writing in a society he himself recognized as deeply structured by inequalities of wealth, race, and gender. He did not assume that institutional design alone could overcome those inequalities. The participatory ideal, for Dewey, required a much broader transformation in education, economic security, and the structures of public life. To extract the participatory architecture from this broader project and propose it as a self-sufficient regulatory reform is to risk a thin Deweyanism that delivers process without the substantive equality it presupposes.

The "backyard environmentalism" that produced Love Canal-style activism was, in its original form, a movement of relatively privileged communities defending themselves against contamination. The environmental justice movement has insisted, with some success, on broadening that frame to include the communities historically excluded from it. Any regulatory architecture that aspires to move "beyond" backyard environmentalism must reckon with this history. The essay before us does not.

A Conservation Biologist's Doubts About Adaptive Management as Magic

I read this essay as someone who has spent twenty years doing conservation biology in working landscapes, much of it on issues directly relevant to Habitat Conservation Plans and watershed-scale planning. I am sympathetic to many of the authors' instincts, but I want to register serious doubts about how the science is actually working out in the regulatory experiments they celebrate. The phrase "adaptive management" appears in this essay with the magical valence it has acquired in policy discourse over the past decade, and I think the time has come to take some of the shine off it.

Let me begin with the California HCPs, which the authors hold up as exemplary. The 1996 Natural Communities Conservation Plan for coastal sage scrub in San Diego County is, I agree, an ambitious and in some ways admirable experiment. It identified large reserve areas, set aside substantial acreage, and committed signatories to a multi-decade implementation framework. But the biological foundations of the plan were and remain shakier than the policy enthusiasm would suggest.

The California gnatcatcher, the listed species at the center of the plan, has a poorly understood demography. The reserve design relied on habitat models calibrated against a few years of survey data, in a system that experiences high inter-annual variability driven by rainfall and fire. Coastal sage scrub is a fire-dependent ecosystem; the plan does not adequately address fire regime management, partly because fire is politically toxic in suburban-adjacent landscapes. The reserves are fragmented, edge effects are substantial, and connectivity is constrained by the very development the plan accommodates. The "No Surprises" assurances locked in landowner obligations on the assumption that the reserve configuration would suffice for fifty to seventy-five years, a forecast no responsible ecologist would endorse given what we know about regional climate variability, much less plausible climate change scenarios.

The authors note in passing that HCPs include "adaptive management" provisions allowing adjustments to be made in light of monitoring. The reality of these provisions is considerably less robust than the term suggests. Most HCPs have weak or undefined adaptive management commitments. Monitoring budgets are typically inadequate. There is no enforceable mechanism by which monitoring data trigger management changes. The Kareiva et al. report from the National Center for Ecological Analysis and Synthesis that the authors cite—"Using Science in Habitat Conservation Plans"—was scathing on these points, finding that most HCPs failed to articulate testable biological goals, failed to specify monitoring protocols capable of detecting change, and failed to define the management responses that monitoring would prompt. The authors mention this report in a footnote but do not reckon with its implications.

This matters because the entire rolling-rule architecture, in the ecosystem-management domain, depends on the assumption that monitoring will reveal problems in time for management adjustments to correct them. If monitoring is weak, slow, or unenforceable, the adaptive feedback loop is broken, and what remains is unilateral concession to development with a fig leaf of scientific language.

I want to make a related point about the timescales involved. Ecosystem responses to management interventions often play out over decades to centuries. The recovery of a depleted fish population, the re-establishment of a forest canopy, the response of riparian systems to changed hydrology—these are slow processes. The rolling-rule regime envisions iterative learning cycles in which monitoring informs revision. But if the relevant biological signals emerge over fifty-year timescales, the regime cannot in fact learn from them within human policy time. We are committing to management trajectories whose consequences we will not detect until they are largely irreversible.

This is not an argument against adaptive management as a discipline. Carl Walters and others developed adaptive management precisely as a way to embrace uncertainty in resource management, and the framework has produced real insights in fisheries, forestry, and waterfowl management. But "adaptive management" as practiced in fisheries (Walters' Pacific salmon work, for example) is much more rigorous than "adaptive management" as it appears in most HCPs. It requires explicit hypothesis-testing, controlled experimentation, and willingness to act on findings even when they are politically inconvenient. Almost none of these conditions obtain in HCP implementation.

The Chesapeake Bay Program, which the authors also celebrate, has a better record but its limitations are illuminating. The 1987 commitment to reduce nutrient loadings by forty percent by 2000 has not been met. The program's monitoring has indeed produced an impressive understanding of the Bay's nutrient dynamics, and the tributary strategies represent real institutional creativity. But the underlying problem—non-point source pollution from agriculture, especially poultry operations on the Delmarva peninsula—has resisted the program's deliberative approach. Voluntary best management practices have been adopted unevenly. Regulatory authority over agricultural runoff remains weak under federal and most state law. The Bay continues to experience large summer dead zones. The tributary teams are an interesting institutional experiment, but they have not solved the underlying political-economy problem of who bears the costs of nutrient reduction.

What worries me about the essay's framing is that it treats institutional innovation as if it could substitute for hard substantive choices. The Chesapeake's nutrient problem is not, fundamentally, an information problem or a deliberative problem. It is a problem of allocating economic costs across an industry (agriculture) whose political organization protects it from those costs. No amount of pooled monitoring will solve this. Solving it would require either explicit subsidies or explicit regulation, both of which run into political constraints the rolling-rule regime does not relax.

Similarly with endangered species: the binding constraint on species recovery is usually not insufficient deliberation but insufficient habitat. The HCP framework, however cleverly designed, cannot create habitat that does not exist. It can only allocate take among competing claims on shrinking habitat. The most rigorous ecological assessments of HCPs—the Kareiva report, John Kostyack's National Wildlife Federation analyses, the work of the Society for Conservation Biology working group—suggest that many HCPs are accommodating more take than the listed species can sustain. We will only know which were wrong in retrospect, when the species cross thresholds from which they cannot recover.

I want to make one constructive suggestion. The rolling-rule architecture would be much improved by what fisheries managers call "precautionary defaults": substantive presumptions, embedded in law, that hold absent affirmative scientific demonstration to the contrary. The default for an HCP affecting a listed species, for instance, might be no take above a specified threshold, with the burden on the proponent to demonstrate that higher take is sustainable. The default for a watershed plan might be no degradation of existing ambient quality, with the burden on the plan to demonstrate that proposed activities will not degrade. Precautionary defaults preserve the deliberative architecture the authors value while protecting against the slow-feedback problems that adaptive management cannot solve.

The essay before us is intellectually exciting, but it leans too heavily on a confidence in monitoring and adjustment that the actual scientific track record does not yet warrant. We are making large, often irreversible commitments under the banner of "adaptive management," and we will be living with the consequences long after the academic literature has moved on to its next paradigm.

Going Further: The Rolling-Rule Regime as a Way Station

Most of what will be written in response to Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen will, I expect, argue that they have gone too far—that they have romanticized deliberation, undervalued command-and-control, overlooked inequalities, and so on. I want to argue the opposite. The authors have not gone far enough. The architecture they describe is a genuine institutional advance, but they treat it as if it were the destination when it is more accurately understood as a way station toward a deeper reorganization of environmental governance—and, eventually, of governance more generally.

What the authors have correctly identified is that environmental problems do not respect the jurisdictional categories of the modern administrative state. A watershed is not a state. A species range is not a county. A toxic plume is not a zip code. The mismatch between the scale of environmental problems and the scale of the institutions we have inherited has produced decades of suboptimal regulation, and the rolling-rule regime is an early attempt to construct institutions that match the actual scale of the problems.

But the authors stop short of the more radical implication of their own analysis, which is that this scale-matching project ultimately demands the construction of new political units that do not correspond to existing federal, state, or local jurisdictions. The bioregional movement has been making this argument since the 1970s, drawing on figures like Peter Berg, Kirkpatrick Sale, and Gary Snyder. The idea is straightforward: political authority over watersheds, airsheds, and ecosystems should be vested in institutions whose boundaries correspond to those biophysical units, rather than in institutions whose boundaries correspond to the historical accidents of colonial settlement.

The Chesapeake Bay Program, which the authors celebrate, is implicitly bioregional. It coordinates across state lines because the Bay is one ecosystem regardless of state lines. Tributary teams are even more explicitly so. What the program lacks, and what the authors do not press for, is genuine political authority commensurate with its ecological scope. The Executive Council issues "directives" of "dubious legal pedigree," in the authors' candid phrase, because the political system has not granted it real authority. If the rolling-rule regime were to mature, it would mature by acquiring such authority—by becoming, in effect, a fourth tier of government nested between federal, state, and local levels, with jurisdiction defined biophysically rather than politically.

This sounds utopian until you remember that the United States has created intermediate jurisdictions before. The Tennessee Valley Authority is the most famous example, but there are many lesser-known ones: special districts for water, mosquito control, ports, transit, school administration. Some of these are quite powerful. The model of a Chesapeake Bay Authority with regulatory teeth, bonding capacity, and democratic legitimacy is not so far-fetched. The authors should be pressing for it.

A second area where the authors stop short concerns the workplace. They mention in passing that TURA planning is often conducted by "problem solving teams that include production workers as well." This is the most important sentence in the essay, and they walk past it. Worker participation in environmental decision-making links the environmental movement to the labor movement in ways that have enormous strategic potential, and that the authors do not develop.

Workers know the production process better than managers or regulators. They are the first to detect leaks, the first to be exposed, and the most invested in long-term improvements. The right-to-know laws of the 1970s and 1980s, which the authors mention, were originally workplace right-to-know laws. The Bhopal disaster, which catalyzed TRI, was as much a workplace safety failure as a community exposure event. The connection between occupational safety and community environmental protection runs deep, and rolling-rule architectures that take it seriously would build worker participation rights into their design—not as a hortatory afterthought but as a structural element. Imagine TRI but with worker representatives on plant-level toxics planning committees with real authority. Imagine HCPs but with farmworker organizations at the table when agricultural lands are subject to species-protection requirements.

The authors gesture at this kind of broadened participation but do not articulate the institutional changes that would make it real. To take worker participation seriously would require revising labor law, expanding OSHA's role, building union capacity in the environmental domain. The authors' framework points toward these reforms but does not name them.

A third area concerns the relationship between environmental governance and economic democracy more generally. The authors note that participatory architectures challenge the conventional distinction between public and private spheres. They are right. But this challenge has implications beyond environmental regulation. If we can imagine workers, neighbors, and downstream users participating in the management of a firm's environmental footprint, why can we not imagine them participating in other aspects of the firm's operations? The rolling-rule regime in environmental policy is, philosophically, of a piece with proposals for codetermination, community benefit agreements, public-utility-style regulation of essential services, and other forms of economic democracy. The authors cite Dewey, but Dewey's political vision was much more capacious than environmental regulation. He saw participatory inquiry as the basis for reconstructing economic life as well as political life.

I am not arguing that the authors should have written a different essay. I am arguing that the essay they wrote opens onto a much larger reform agenda than they themselves acknowledge. They treat the rolling-rule regime as a destination. I treat it as evidence that something larger is possible.

Let me close with a methodological point. The authors do their best work when they describe actual institutional experiments in detail: TRI, TURA, Chesapeake, HCPs, INPO. They are weakest when they generalize the philosophy that supposedly underlies these experiments. The reason, I suspect, is that the institutional discoveries are running ahead of the theoretical vocabulary available to describe them. We do not yet have an adequate political theory of the rolling-rule regime. The authors gesture at pragmatism and deliberative democracy, but neither tradition was developed with this architecture in mind. We need new theory, and new theory requires that we let the experiments stretch our imaginations rather than constraining them within familiar frames.

The next generation of work on these questions ought to ask: What institutional units can match environmental scales? What participation rights should attach to workers, neighbors, and other affected parties? What property-rights revisions are required to make collaborative ecosystem management durable? What revenue structures can fund the participatory infrastructure the regime requires? How should the new architectures interact with existing electoral institutions? These questions are not answered by the essay before us. But the essay clears the ground on which they can be asked, and that is no small contribution.

Love Canal, Times Beach, and the “Cancer Alley” corridor in Louisiana were not accidents of geography; they reflected a pattern of siting hazardous facilities where residents had the least political power. The first wave of citizen activism was intertwined with the emerging environmental justice movement, which documented that race and class were the most reliable predictors of proximity to environmental harm. The new model, for all its talk of local autonomy and contextual intelligence, is disturbingly silent on how it will prevent a similarly skewed distribution of power within the very participatory processes it champions.

Consider the local units at the heart of the rolling-rule regime: tributary teams, HCP stakeholder groups, toxics-use planning teams. Who will participate? The affluent, well-educated residents of a Chesapeake Bay waterfront community will have the time, resources, and social capital to organize, attend meetings, and challenge the technical reports of developers or farmers. A low-income, rural community in the same watershed, or a largely Spanish-speaking neighborhood adjacent to a refinery in Texas, will have far less capacity to engage in the kind of sustained, technically sophisticated deliberation the model demands. The result will be what we might call “participatory redlining”: the communities with the most clout will craft plans that protect their own air and water, while the more marginalized communities remain stuck with the polluting facilities and degraded habitats. The rolling-rule regime decentralizes decision-making, but it does not decentralize power, wealth, or education. It simply translates existing inequalities into environmental outcomes under the legitimizing veneer of collaboration.

The Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) is often hailed as a democratizing tool, because it makes information about emissions publicly available. But information alone does not produce justice. The TRI data show that while overall releases have declined, the reductions have not been evenly distributed. Facilities in wealthy, predominantly white zip codes have often reduced emissions faster than those in poorer, minority areas. Why? Because firms respond to pressure, and those communities have more of it. Local citizens groups can use TRI data to sue, but they need lawyers and funding. The same dynamic will play out in a system where local “governance” replaces uniform national rules. A developer seeking an HCP permit in a rich county will face an army of well-funded environmental lawyers and scientists demanding stringent conservation measures. In a poor, rural county desperate for jobs, the same developer will face a much more compliant set of “stakeholders” who can be persuaded that a modest conservation set-aside is a fair price for economic development.

The essay’s treatment of the Chesapeake Bay Program is instructive. The tributary teams are praised for devising stream-specific goals that are “more effective and equitable” than uniform statewide measures. But are they equitable? A tributary team may indeed tailor measures to local circumstances, but that tailoring will reflect the local balance of interests. A watershed dominated by large agribusiness operations may produce a plan that relies heavily on voluntary “best management practices” with little real enforcement, effectively subsidizing agricultural pollution at the expense of downstream water users and the Bay’s ecology. Meanwhile, a suburban watershed might impose stringent stormwater requirements that raise the cost of housing, further excluding lower-income families. The disaggregation of regulation into a thousand local negotiations destroys the principle of environmental justice: that all citizens are entitled to the same minimum level of protection, regardless of where they live.

The progressive tradition in American environmental law has long recognized that national standards are essential to counteract local races to the bottom and to protect politically weak groups. The Clean Air Act and Clean Water Act imposed uniform, technology-based standards in part because it was known that states and localities could not be trusted to protect their own citizens from powerful industries. The rolling-rule regime, by devolving standard-setting to the local level and replacing uniform mandates with procedural benchmarking, risks undoing that safeguard. The center, in the authors’ vision, is supposed to coordinate, pool information, and set “minimally acceptable levels of performance.” But how will those minimums be determined? If they are set too high, they stifle local “experimentation” and revert to command-and-control. If they are set too low—as political pressure from industry and labor will constantly push them to be—they become a meaningless floor that legitimizes a patchwork of environmental quality discrimination.

The authors might respond that the new model inherently corrects for this because it demands transparency and provides channels for national environmental groups to intervene. But that is a thin reed. National groups like the National Wildlife Federation, as the essay notes, are beginning to position themselves as monitors and poolers of best practices. That is a worthy adaptation, but it is no substitute for a federal government that is legally obligated to enforce a uniform standard. Nonprofit organizations have limited resources, are themselves often dominated by white, upper-middle-class professionals, and are not democratically accountable to the communities they purport to serve. The new model’s reliance on such intermediaries to ensure equity is troubling.

The rolling-rule regime holds genuine promise for making environmental regulation smarter and more adaptive. But it cannot succeed as a democratic project unless it explicitly builds in mechanisms to counteract inequality. That would require, at minimum, dedicated public funding for technical assistance and legal representation for low-income and minority communities; strong, non-waivable federal minimum performance standards that guarantee a baseline of protection; and a rigorous, centrally enforced requirement that participatory processes represent the full diversity of the affected population, with veto rights for marginalized groups when equity is threatened. Without these safeguards, “deep use” will simply mean deeper entrenchment of environmental privilege for the few, and deeper exposure for the many.

The authors’ critique of tradable permits is instructive but ultimately superficial. They note that markets in pollution permits require regulators to define the commodity, allocate initial rights, and set trading rules—a daunting informational task. That is true, but it is equally true of any regulatory system. The rolling-rule regime also requires a center that sets minimum performance standards, defines acceptable metrics, and polices local reporting. The informational burden is simply shifted from the front end (defining the tradable commodity) to an ongoing process of synthesizing local experimentation. There is no escape from the need for central judgment. The difference is that a well-designed market harnesses the price mechanism to do the heavy lifting of coordination once the initial rules are set, while the rolling-rule model relies on continuous bureaucratic oversight and committee deliberation. That is precisely why the SO2 trading program under the 1990 Clean Air Act amendments has been so dramatically successful, achieving sharp reductions at a fraction of the predicted cost. The result is an inefficient allocation of abatement effort. A market sidesteps this by letting the price emerge from decentralized trades, revealing the marginal cost of abatement in real time without any central authority ever needing to know it. The regulator need only adjust the overall cap over time—a task that requires scientific knowledge of environmental harm, not detailed knowledge of production technologies—and the market reallocates effort automatically. The environmental policy innovations surveyed by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are undeniably intriguing. They suggest that government can be more adaptive, more collaborative, and more effective. But in their haste to praise the pragmatist philosophy of experimentalist governance, the authors sidestep a more fundamental tension: the “rolling-rule regime” is on a collision course with the basic structures of American administrative law. The new architecture, if allowed to expand without a corresponding legal framework, will undermine the rule of law, erode democratic accountability, and ultimately delegitimize the very environmental gains it seeks to achieve.

The traditional environmental regulatory state is built on a foundation of legislative delegation and procedural regularity. Congress passes a statute—the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Endangered Species Act—that sets forth broad goals and authorizes an agency to issue rules. Those rules are developed through notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), subject to judicial review to ensure they are not arbitrary and capricious and stay within the bounds of the statute. This system, for all its sluggishness, provides a clear chain of legal authority and public accountability. When the EPA sets a national ambient air quality standard, it must justify that decision based on a public rulemaking record, and anyone aggrieved can challenge it in court. The result is a body of law that is stable enough for regulated entities to rely on, yet open to revision through prescribed, transparent processes.

The rolling-rule regime, as described, unravels this web. In the Chesapeake Bay Program, the Executive Council issues “directives” that the authors frankly acknowledge are of “dubious legal pedigree and status.” They are treated as “morally binding commitments” by the governors and the EPA administrator. But a moral commitment is not a law. If a governor decides that the tributary team’s plan is too onerous for his state’s farmers and withdraws his support, under what legal authority can he be forced to comply? The Clean Water Act gives the EPA authority to regulate point sources, but not to mandate specific non-point source pollution reductions by states, which was precisely the legislative compromise that created the Bay’s problems in the first place. The program’s elaborate governance structure is essentially an elaborate, extra-statutory framework, operating in the shadow of the law but without the law’s binding force. It works because of the goodwill of the current participants. A change in administration in Richmond or Washington, or a lawsuit by a determined developer, could expose its legal frailty overnight.

The Habitat Conservation Plan process under the Endangered Species Act is similarly adrift. The FWS issues “incidental take” permits based on negotiated HCPs that incorporate all manner of requirements—monitoring regimes, adaptive management triggers, habitat set-asides—that go far beyond anything explicitly authorized by Section 10 of the ESA. The Act says the Secretary can permit a taking if it will not “appreciably reduce the likelihood of the survival and recovery of the species.” It does not say the Secretary can convert the permitting process into a de facto regional land-use planning authority, bundling multiple species, unlisted species, and vast acreages into a comprehensive deal. The Babbitt administration’s aggressive use of HCPs is, in many ways, a heroic effort to make a broken statute work. But it is an effort that operates in a legal gray zone. When a conservation group sues, claiming an HCP does not adequately ensure recovery—as has happened repeatedly in California—the courts are left to navigate a situation where there is almost no hard law to apply, only a mass of agency guidance, informal promises, and complicated local documents. The very flexibility and context-sensitivity that the authors praise is a source of legal arbitrariness. A developer denied a generous permit in one county may point to a more lenient deal struck in another and claim unequal treatment under the law.

The Toxics Release Inventory and TURA raise a different set of legal concerns. TRI requires disclosure, not pollution reduction. But as the authors note, the data is then used “as a lever by which to apply public pressure for improvements,” and often to support citizen suits under other statutes. The line between a neutral information-gathering statute and an informal regulatory scheme that coerces firms through reputational sanctions without due process is dangerously thin. When the EPA issues press releases highlighting the “Dirty Dozen” worst polluters based on TRI data, it is effectively imposing a penalty—stock price depression, consumer boycotts—without any adjudicatory proceeding. The agency disclaims any responsibility for the accuracy of the data, and does not verify it. Yet the practical effect is a powerful regulatory instrument that circumvents the procedural safeguards built into enforcement actions. If a rolling-rule regime extends this logic, establishing “minimum performance standards” based on data pooled from local participants, what recourse will a firm have if it believes the pooled data is flawed, or the benchmark is set unfairly? The APA’s protections against arbitrary government action are largely sidelined in a system where standards “emerge from practice” rather than being promulgated as rules.

The authors’ preferred solution is for Congress to pass something like the Miller Bill, giving explicit statutory blessing to the new architecture. They imagine Congress shifting its role from “setting some relatively circumscribed public goal” to “authorizing and conferring pluralist political legitimacy on the constitutive framework” of experimentalist governance. This is a profound redefinition of the legislative function. It would mean that Congress no longer decides what level of protection is required—that the air be safe for asthmatics, that rivers be fishable and swimmable, that species be pulled back from the brink—but instead merely sets the rules of a game in which those substantive questions are perpetually up for local renegotiation. Such a framework law would raise serious constitutional questions under the nondelegation doctrine, which holds that Congress cannot delegate its legislative power without providing an “intelligible principle” to guide its exercise. “Thou shalt run a collaborative process with good monitoring and public participation” may not be a sufficiently intelligible principle to determine whether a species goes extinct or a community’s water is poisoned.

Moreover, a framework that relies on local negotiation and benchmarking radically reduces the transparency and accountability that the APA was designed to ensure. The public can read a rule in the Federal Register and know what the law requires. How does an ordinary citizen know what standards apply under a rolling-rule system? She would have to study the local HCP, the tributary plan, the pooled benchmarks, and the guidance documents. This information is scattered, often highly technical, and not easily accessible even on the Internet. The promise of “deep use” is that citizens will become co-authors of regulation. The reality is that the power to author will accrue to those with the resources to master an extraordinarily complex, non-public system of governance, while the rule of law, which protects the rest of us by insisting that the rules be publicly known, stable, and equally applied, will be fatally weakened. We should not dismantle the hard-won procedural safeguards of the administrative state in pursuit of a theoretical ideal of deliberation.

State environmental agencies are even more strapped. The polluting factory, the sprawling subdivision, the toxic chemical—these are no longer adversaries to be banished, but partners to be brought into a deliberative process. The goal shifts from elimination to “acceptably sustainable” management. The danger is that the new rolling-rule institutions, precisely because they substitute negotiation for clear rules, will reduce the effective transparency of the system. Each failure becomes evidence that more learning is needed before tough decisions can be made, while the species at risk continue their slide toward extinction.

The Chesapeake Bay Program presents a similarly troubling picture. While the authors celebrate the program's evolution toward participatory tributary teams, they acknowledge that "the very fluidity of the internal governance of the program becomes a liability, as external interlocutors seek, in vain, to determine the authoritative voice of an institutional ensemble that adjusts precisely by not having one." This institutional opacity makes accountability extremely difficult. When nitrogen loading targets are not met, when fish populations continue to decline, when agricultural operations evade their obligations—where is the mechanism for corrective action? The program's governance structure seems designed to diffuse responsibility so thoroughly that no actor can be held accountable for failure.

The broader philosophical commitments underlying the rolling-rule regime also warrant scrutiny. The authors embrace what they call a "pragmatist" approach that "rejects immutable principles." But environmental law embodies fundamental commitments—to preventing extinction, to protecting public health, to ensuring that polluters bear the costs of their contamination. These are not bureaucratic rigidities to be transcended through clever institutional design; they are moral commitments that constrain the range of acceptable outcomes. A regime that treats these commitments as merely provisional, subject to revision through deliberative processes among affected stakeholders, may prove unable to resist the relentless pressure that economic interests bring to environmental debates.

The authors note that some environmentalists prefer "strict application of Section 9" and dismiss this position as insufficiently sophisticated. But there is wisdom in this preference. When statutory protections can be traded away through negotiated agreements, when minimum standards are understood as merely temporary benchmarks subject to continuous revision, when accountability is diffused across multiple levels of governance—environmental protection becomes whatever powerful interests will allow. The rolling-rule regime may be innovative, but innovation in service of accommodating development is not necessarily progress.

The Information Paradox in Performance-Based Regulation

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen make information availability the centerpiece of their new regulatory architecture. The Toxics Release Inventory demonstrates how mandatory disclosure can discipline polluters through reputational mechanisms. TURA extends this logic by requiring firms to develop reduction plans based on their own monitoring data. The authors argue that information pooling across localities allows regulators to establish progressively more demanding standards.

Yet this emphasis on information reveals a fundamental paradox. The authors criticize market-based approaches partly on grounds that "artificial markets, created from the center, the information must first be accumulated (or specified) by the regulator." But the rolling-rule regime faces essentially similar challenges. How do local actors determine what data to collect? Who establishes the metrics by which performance will be assessed? How are baseline conditions determined against which improvement is measured?

Consider the Chesapeake Bay Program's evolution through successive phases of monitoring and target-setting. The 1987 Agreement established a 40 percent nutrient reduction goal by 2000. When further studies revealed that loadings in various tributaries had "differential impacts," parties revised their system-wide goals and developed tributary-specific targets. This adaptive revision is presented as a virtue—evidence that the regime responds to new scientific understanding. But consider what it implies. The original 40 percent target was established with apparently insufficient information about tributary-specific dynamics. Why should we have greater confidence in the revised tributary strategies? Each adjustment may itself rest on incomplete understanding that subsequent learning will reveal as inadequate.

The authors acknowledge that "the more we learn about the ecology of the Bay, the more surprising new findings will be." If this is true, then the entire project of establishing performance targets and monitoring progress becomes uncertain. If knowledge is fundamentally incomplete and subject to continuous revision, on what basis are current benchmarks established? The pragmatic philosophy the authors embrace offers no guidance beyond "institutionalize better ways of learning from the inevitable surprises that experience offers us." But this is circular—learning is defined as discovering what we need to learn to improve learning, and improvement is defined relative to learning. The framework provides no external criteria for evaluating whether particular learning processes are adequate.

Furthermore, the quality of information generated by local actors depends on their capacity and incentives. TURA works, in part, because Massachusetts established a Toxics Use Reduction Institute to develop planning curricula and provide technical assistance. Small firms in other states may lack comparable resources. The authors note that HCPs vary enormously in their monitoring quality—"local circumstance, seldom corrected by national discipline, determines whether an HCP monitors its progress well or poorly." If monitoring quality varies so dramatically, how can information pooling produce reliable standards? Poor local data will generate unreliable conclusions when aggregated and compared.

The INPO example the authors invoke is instructive. They argue that INPO succeeded only when it placed self-monitoring "under the aegis of public institutions and authority." But INPO operated in a domain—nuclear power generation—where firms had strong incentives to avoid catastrophic accidents that would destroy their investments and threaten their survival. Environmental compliance lacks comparable self-disciplining incentives. Firms may report favorable data to avoid regulatory scrutiny, to satisfy community expectations, or to maintain competitive positions. The authors acknowledge that TRI's reputational penalties give managers "strong incentives to either reduce their toxics emissions or shade their reporting estimates to appear cleaner than they are." If reporting can be "shaded" in this fashion, what confidence can we place in the information infrastructure on which the entire rolling-rule regime depends?

Devolution, Accountability, and the Federalism Problem

The lead essay's most provocative claim concerns the relationship between the rolling-rule regime and democratic accountability. The authors argue that this framework creates "a collaborative and mutual accountability of center to parts, parts to center, parts to other parts, and all to the whole enterprise—and to the public generally." But what exactly does this mean in our constitutional system? And how does it function in practice?

Federal environmental law typically operates through a framework of national standards that states implement and enforce. The Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, and the Endangered Species Act establish minimum requirements that states may exceed but not undercut. This structure reflects constitutional allocations of authority and political judgments about the appropriate distribution of regulatory responsibility. The rolling-rule regime proposes something different: broad local autonomy within general guidelines, with accountability achieved through information pooling and performance comparison rather than hierarchical command.

The Chesapeake Bay Program illustrates the institutional complexity this entails. The program's signatories include EPA, the governors of Maryland, Virginia, and Pennsylvania, and the mayor of the District of Columbia. The Executive Council and implementation committee operate through consensus, with directives that possess "dubious legal pedigree and status" yet are considered "morally binding." When loadings exceed targets, when tributaries fail to achieve water quality standards, when agricultural operations evade their obligations—which legal authority can compel corrective action? The program's flexibility enables adaptation to local conditions, but it also enables evasion of accountability.

The authors acknowledge that HCPs vary enormously in their quality and rigor, with "nearly unsupervised local autonomy" determining whether plans monitor effectively or provide genuine public access to decision-making. They propose the Miller Bill as a remedy—requiring the Secretary of the Interior to review HCPs triennially and publish annual reports on the status of all plans. But this proposal faces practical obstacles. The authors note that "no one in Washington had even collected the HCPs that had already been negotiated up until that point—much less read them, or attempted to absorb any generally-applicable lessons." If administrative capacity for oversight is so limited, how will triennial reviews function?

The deeper issue concerns democratic legitimacy. When EPA establishes air quality standards under the Clean Air Act, it does so through notice-and-comment rulemaking subject to judicial review. Affected parties may challenge standards as insufficiently protective or excessively burdensome. This process is cumbersome and often produces rigid rules that fail to account for local conditions. But it provides a clear framework for accountability: national standards, publicly developed, with identifiable responsible officials.

The rolling-rule regime operates through less visible processes. Local deliberation among affected stakeholders determines what counts as acceptable performance. National authorities aggregate information and adjust benchmarks. But who is accountable when local agreements prove inadequate? When tributary strategies fail to achieve water quality goals? When species protected by HCPs continue to decline? The mutual accountability the authors describe may function well when all parties share a commitment to environmental improvement. It may prove far less effective when powerful economic interests dominate local negotiations.

Beyond the Backyard: Scale, Resources, and Real Participation

The lead essay celebrates the evolution from first-generation "backyard environmentalism"—where communities organized to resist harmful activity in their neighborhoods—toward second-generation forms of participation that involve citizens as joint architects of environmental solutions. This trajectory is compelling in theory. But practice reveals severe constraints on the "deep participation" the authors endorse.

The Chesapeake Bay Program's evolution through successive phases of citizen involvement illuminates these constraints. In the 1960s and early 1970s, participation meant "conventional public education through publications, public meetings, hearings, and mass media." When monitoring requirements exceeded governmental capacity, emphasis shifted to teaching volunteers "to mimic the monitoring and reporting protocols developed by scientific experts." Citizens became "quasi-experts by imitation." This approach—replicating expert protocols—remains limited because it does not enable genuine deliberation about what should be monitored and how data should be interpreted.

The tributary strategy that emerged later attempts something more ambitious: devolving responsibility to semi-autonomous teams comprising government officials, scientific experts, agricultural and industry representatives, and citizen volunteers. The authors describe how these teams "draw on a unique mix of local knowledge, expert science (adapted to local needs), and basin-wide experience." But what enables ordinary citizens to participate effectively in such teams? The authors offer no systematic account of the resources, training, and time that meaningful participation requires.

Consider the Habitat Conservation Plans in Southern California. These agreements involve developers, public officials, conservationists, and scientists negotiating complex conservation strategies for millions of acres. The participants bring vastly different resources to these negotiations. Developers and industry representatives typically employ professional lobbyists, attorneys, and environmental consultants. Conservation organizations may have staff and technical expertise, though often at lower levels than their adversaries. Local community members—those who will experience the environmental consequences of decisions—frequently lack the time, expertise, and financial resources to participate effectively.

The authors acknowledge that "the quality of participation in local deliberation is everywhere uneven." They propose that national environmental organizations "reinvent themselves as independent monitors of local performance and poolers of best practices." But this vision reconstitutes rather than democratizes expertise. Instead of federal regulators making decisions, we have national environmental organizations reviewing local agreements. The citizen's role may remain as spectator rather than participant.

Genuine deep participation requires capacities that the rolling-rule regime does not systematically create. Training in ecological science, legal expertise, negotiation skills, and access to technical information—these resources are not equally distributed. A framework that assumes participatory equality while ignoring the structural inequalities that pervade environmental negotiations risks mystifying power relations. The language of deliberation and mutual accountability may mask the reality that some voices carry authority while others remain marginal.

Markets and Mandates: Why Not Both?

The lead essay's critique of both command regulation and market-based approaches provides a useful framework for understanding the limitations of each. But the authors' proposed alternative—rolling-rule regulation emphasizing local experimentation and centralized information pooling—may not represent the only viable path forward. A more practical approach might combine elements of all three paradigms in ways tailored to specific environmental challenges.

Consider the Toxics Release Inventory. The authors correctly identify TRI as a disclosure mechanism rather than a substantive regulation. Facilities report their toxic releases, but TRI imposes no binding emission limits. The discipline that TRI provides comes through reputational mechanisms—journalists and activists compare polluters, stock prices respond to apparent poor performance, community activists pressure companies to improve. These mechanisms work reasonably well for large publicly-traded corporations in visible industries. They may function poorly for small facilities, private companies, and industries where community monitoring is minimal.

For such cases, traditional command regulation retains value. Technology-based standards that require specific pollution control equipment may be less elegant than performance-based approaches, but they provide certainty that local experimentation cannot always achieve. When communities lack the expertise, resources, or political power to enforce performance expectations against resistant polluters, national standards ensure minimum protection.

Market-based mechanisms also retain advantages in specific contexts. The sulfur dioxide trading program under the Clean Air Act has achieved dramatic reductions at lower cost than traditional regulation. The key insight of cap-and-trade systems—that achieving environmental goals at minimum cost requires flexible response by regulated entities—remains valid. The authors' critique of market approaches focuses on information requirements and property rights establishment. But these challenges vary across contexts. For widely-dispersed pollutants with well-understood environmental impacts, trading programs may function effectively.

The rolling-rule regime the authors describe may be most appropriate for environmental challenges that are genuinely novel and complex—emerging contaminants, ecosystem management, diffuse pollution sources. Here, the local experimentation and continuous learning that the framework emphasizes may outperform fixed rules. But even in these domains, mandatory standards may remain necessary for worst-case actors who will not participate constructively in deliberative processes.

What environmental policy requires is not a single paradigm but a sophisticated mix of approaches calibrated to specific problems. The authors' typology—command regulation, market simulation, rolling-rule regimes—provides useful analytical categories. But the conclusion that rolling-rule regulation represents the emerging standard may overstate the case. Existing programs combine elements of all three approaches, and this hybrid character may represent the practical reality rather than any clean paradigm.

John Dewey Would Not Recognize This Deliberation

The authors explicitly invoke John Dewey as the visionary whose "confidence...could himself scarcely imagine" the participatory possibilities they describe. This invocation is revealing—and disturbing. Dewey's philosophy of democracy rested on a conception of public life radically different from the stakeholder negotiation that the rolling-rule regime endorses.

For Dewey, democracy was not a mechanism for aggregating pre-existing preferences or for facilitating negotiation among affected parties. It was a form of life—a mode of associated inquiry through which communities developed shared understandings and fashioned common purposes. Dewey worried profoundly about the conditions that sustained or undermined democratic community. He emphasized the importance of shared public spaces, common experiences, and ongoing conversation about collective concerns. The "public" he envisioned was not a collection of interest groups but a community in the fullest sense.

The deliberative processes that the rolling-rule regime would create bear little resemblance to Dewey's vision. Local actors—neighbors on a tributary, workers and managers in a plant, landowners and conservationists negotiating an HCP—deliberate about environmental performance targets and implementation measures. They report data to centralized authorities who aggregate information and adjust benchmarks. The "public" that emerges from this process is not a community but a system of information flows and performance comparisons.

The authors argue that "expertise without local participation remains ignorant of crucial detail, while localism unprovoked by expertise remains haplessly parochial." This formulation reduces democratic participation to a source of local information. Citizens contribute their "contextual intelligence" to expert deliberations; they do not constitute a public that defines its own purposes. The transformation the authors anticipate involves citizens becoming "quasi-experts by imitation" or participants in "semi-autonomous" teams. It does not involve the development of democratic community as Dewey understood it.

Dewey believed that genuine democracy required active engagement with the full range of collective concerns—not merely environmental management but education, economic organization, artistic expression, and community life. The rolling-rule regime operates within a technocratic frame that assumes environmental protection is the goal and that deliberation serves to achieve it more efficiently. This framing forecloses the deeper democratic question: What should we seek, and how should we organize our common life to pursue it?

The authors celebrate the "directly deliberative form of participatory democracy" that environmental regulation might exemplify. But this celebration conflates two very different things: deliberation among stakeholders about implementation measures, and democratic self-governance through which communities define their purposes. The first may be valuable; it is not the second. Dewey's vision demanded nothing less than the transformation of all social institutions according to democratic principles. The rolling-rule regime offers a technocratic refinement of environmental management—useful perhaps, but not the democratic renewal the authors claim.

The Miller Bill and the Future of Experimentalist Regulation

The lead essay concludes with an extended discussion of the Miller Bill (HR 960), which would establish minimum requirements for Habitat Conservation Plans and create a framework for monitoring their implementation. The authors see this legislation as potentially transformative—a way to "crystallize this democratic regulatory reorientation in political discussion" by inserting it explicitly into the reform agenda. I want to examine whether this optimistic assessment is justified.

The Miller Bill's core provisions require HCPs to incorporate "objective, measurable biological goals aimed at species recovery," monitoring regimes, regularized reporting, and "appropriate adaptive management measures." Large-scale HCPs would require "substantial public participation," and the Secretary of the Interior would review each plan triennially, publishing annual reports on the status of all HCPs.

These requirements respond directly to the deficiencies the authors identify: inadequate monitoring, insufficient public access, and the inability of the current system to learn from local innovations. If enacted, the Miller Bill would create the informational infrastructure that the rolling-rule regime requires. Triennial reviews would provide the higher-order mechanism that the authors argue earlier programs lacked—the capacity to "adjust the frame of intervention itself according to the findings of initial investigations."

Yet the Bill's prospects are uncertain. Environmental organizations remain divided about HCPs, with some preferring strict Section 9 enforcement to negotiated development permits. Property rights advocates view HCPs as government coercion disguised as collaboration. The Bill attempts to thread between these positions by establishing minimum standards while preserving local flexibility. This balancing act may satisfy neither side.

More fundamentally, the Bill addresses a single program—HCPs—rather than the broader regulatory transformation the authors envision. TRI, TURA, and the Chesapeake Bay Program operate under different statutory authorities with different institutional structures. Creating an effective framework for one program does not guarantee that the rolling-rule regime will diffuse to others. The authors acknowledge that "Congress is noticing the anomalies of the new regimes as viewed in the light of the legislation from which their authorization is derived." Reforming each statute to accommodate experimentalist governance would require sustained political attention that may not materialize.

The authors suggest that passage of the Miller Bill would "subtly modify both its own legislative role and that of the administrative agency." Congress would shift from setting circumscribed goals and delegating implementation to authorizing the framework within which citizens determine how to pursue broad purposes. The Department of the Interior would police the framework rather than crafting agreements and determining acceptability.

This vision of institutional transformation is compelling. But it requires not merely legislative action but a change in how environmental policy is understood. The current frame—national standards implemented by federal and state agencies, enforced through administrative and judicial mechanisms—has deep roots in Progressive Era reforms. Transforming this frame to accommodate experimentalist governance would require not only new statutes but new patterns of agency behavior, new forms of public participation, and new expectations about what environmental regulation can achieve.

The Miller Bill offers an opportunity. Whether it becomes a stepping stone to broader transformation or merely a technical fix for HCP implementation remains to be seen. The authors have provided a compelling intellectual framework for understanding what is at stake. The political work of translating this framework into institutional reality lies ahead.
