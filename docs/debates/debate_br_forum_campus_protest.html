Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Robin Kelley has written the most clarifying piece on the current campus uprisings I have read, and its clarifying power derives precisely from his refusal to flatten the movement into a single posture. The distinction he draws between students who want the university to honor its self-image and those who treat the university as an institution to be subverted from within is one that participants themselves often blur. Naming the tension is itself a political act.

Still, I want to press on two places where the argument feels too tidy.

First, the critique of trauma discourse. Kelley is right that "triggers" and "micro-aggressions" can substitute psychological vocabulary for political analysis, and that university administrators happily prefer the former because it lets them rename a building rather than rewrite an admissions formula. But the binary between trauma and agency may be a false one. The students at Mizzou did not stop at counseling demands; they ousted a system president. The Yale students surrounding Nicholas Christakis were not asking for therapy; they were asking, however inarticulately, why the institution that recruited them refused to defend them. The language of trauma is sometimes the only registered grievance an administrator will hear, which is less an ideological capture than a tactical concession to a managerial culture students did not invent. Kelley knows this. But the essay risks underestimating how much "I am hurt" functions, in this context, as a translation of "this institution is unjust" into a dialect HR will entertain.

Second, the celebration of fugitive study via Harney and Moten deserves a sharper question. The undercommons is materially parasitic on the very institution it sneaks through; adjuncts and graduate students can "steal what one can" only so long as someone is paying tuition and someone else is signing keys out. This is not a defect of the model — Kelley would say it is the model — but it does mean that "in but not of" cannot be the whole strategy when the football team's leverage at Mizzou came precisely from being constitutive of the university's revenue, not fugitive from it. The Black Liberation Collective demand for free tuition for Black and Indigenous students is not a request for inclusion; it is a claim on the institution's wealth. Refuge and confrontation are not alternatives but moments in the same struggle.

What Kelley gets exactly right, and what the present moment most needs, is his insistence that political education cannot be outsourced to a curriculum committee. The Mississippi Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon women remind us that study has always been something Black people did for themselves, with or without the university's permission.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay is a powerful and necessary provocation, a reminder that the university is not a neutral site and that the transformative visions so many of us share will never be realized by scraping and bowing before administrators. His call for love, study, and struggle outside the formal mechanisms of the academy is compelling. Yet, as someone who has spent the past months watching students on my own campus block intersections and occupy buildings, I am left uneasy by the ease with which he dismisses the demands those same students are making on the university itself. Kelley correctly identifies the contradiction between wanting the university to be a home and rejecting it as a cog in the neoliberal order, but he leaps from that contradiction to a false choice: either you seek reform and surrender to the institution’s logic, or you retreat into the undercommons. This misses the strategic texture of what is unfolding right now, in March 2016.

Most students are under no illusion that the university is a place of love; they know it has profited from misery. They feel the weight of debt, the casual racism, the threat of campus police. The call for safe spaces, cultural competency, and diversity—however much it risks being co-opted—is not an end in itself but a survival strategy for students who feel they are drowning. When a black first-year student at a predominantly white institution demands a counselor who shares her cultural context, she is not buying into neoliberal logic; she is trying to stay alive long enough to find the study group, the radical reading circle, the off-campus organizing that Kelley and I both admire. The freedom to enact the kind of fugitive study he champions often depends on a baseline of security the university currently denies. To reduce those demands to a desire for belonging is to ignore their function as a pressure tactic: they are forcing the institution to expend resources—its only currency—on the very bodies it has historically excluded. That is not victimhood speaking; it is the patient work of building power in hostile terrain.

Kelley’s deep suspicion of liberal multiculturalism is well taken, but he conflates the content of a demand with its effect. A demand for more faculty of color can be a reformist trap; it can also be the condition under which radical black studies can even be whispered inside the institution, providing cover and a foothold for the guerrilla intellectual work he prizes. The students at UCLA whom Kelley celebrates are able to host their undercommons in part because they themselves exist in a university that has admitted them, however grudgingly, and provides some minimal support for their intellectual community. That access was won by earlier waves of protest. The present movement, which has generated a national reckoning with campus racism, is doing the same work for future radicals. Winning a police-free campus or a living wage for campus workers is not an abdication of revolutionary politics; it demystifies the university’s exploitative practices precisely by rearranging its power relations in the present.

There is also a conceptual danger in holding up the undercommons as the primary site of political work. When Kelley writes that universities will never be engines of social transformation, he is of course right. But they are fronts in a broader struggle. The campus is a logistical hub where a thousand workers—dining staff, cleaners, adjuncts—already engage in labor struggles, where student debt connects directly to the financialized economy, and where the police who brutalize Ferguson also patrol the quad. Withdrawal from that site in the name of refusal risks ceding the terrain to the very forces he abhors. The 2015-2016 wave of protests succeeded not because students quietly stole away into the undercommons; it succeeded because they disrupted business-as-usual, embarrassed the powerful, and revealed the university’s “family” for the corporate enterprise it is. That disruption forced a conversation, however superficial, that can be leveraged by the radicals who remain engaged.

The language of trauma and interiority does not need to be abandoned; it needs to be connected to a structural analysis, as Kelley partly concedes but then too quickly dismisses. A student who experiences campus racism as trauma is not wrong to ask for mental health support; the radical act is to demand that service and then to ask: why is the institution that produces this trauma also the one that provides the therapist? That question opens the door to the kind of structural critique Kelley wants. Student activists I spoke with after the Mizzou hunger strike understood this. They were not fooled by the new administrators sent in as damage control. They were demystified not by a reading group, but by watching a president fall and then seeing the remaining power unscathed. That experience is a form of study.

What Kelley offers as history—the freedom schools, the study groups—is a vital inheritance. But those projects did not exist entirely outside the university; they were structurally parasitic on institutions, public libraries, churches, courtrooms, and yes, university buildings denied to black people. The lesson of 2015-2016 is that the campus, for all its violence, is still a site where a relatively transient population can inflict immediate political damage and, in the process, educate one another. It would be a tragedy if the most radical students abandoned that terrain entirely for the fugitive refuge, leaving the work of managing the institution to the diversity trainers and HR consultants Kelley rightly despises. Love and study thrive not in pure outside spaces but in the messy, compromised, and often brutal encounter with power. That is the struggle Kelley invokes. We do his essay an injustice if we hear it as a final word rather than as part of the conversation he calls for. The task is to hold on to his ruthless criticism while refusing his division between reform and revolution. Both are undone by the same world. The students know this. They are building, in real time, a more porous boundary between the campus and the street. That is the undercommons in action, not in retreat.

Robin D. G. Kelley’s diagnosis of the ideological fissures in recent campus protests cuts right to the heart of the modern university’s contradictions. As he observes, the uprisings that swept through campuses like Mizzou and Yale in the fall of 2015 reveal a profound tension between reformist desires for inclusion and radical demands for liberation. Students are indeed asking an institution structurally designed for professionalization, wealth hoarding, and neoliberal efficiency to suddenly act as a loving, nurturing family. Kelley correctly warns that this is a fundamentally impossible request.

However, while Kelley’s critique of the movement’s reliance on the language of trauma is theoretically rigorous, it risks underestimating the utility of that language as an entry point for a generation stripped of a radical political vocabulary. Kelley worries that reading the black experience primarily through trauma, microaggressions, and triggers reduces students to victims rather than agents. He notes that such framing allows the university to transform demands for economic and social justice into psychological and human resources issues—resolved by cultural-competency consultants rather than structural upheaval.

This danger of depoliticization is real. Yet, for young activists in 2016, articulating personal and collective trauma is often the necessary first step toward political consciousness. We live in an era where public education has largely sanitized civil rights history into a triumphant narrative of post-racial progress, while smartphones concurrently broadcast the endless, looping executions of black citizens. When students arrive on campus, the vocabulary of psychology and self-care is often the only cultural script they possess to describe the deep, structural alienation they feel. Trauma is not the destination, but it is the bridge. The challenge is not to dismiss this affective language, but to channel it into the very "Love, Study, Struggle" that Kelley advocates, connecting personal pain to historical and material realities.

Furthermore, the students’ demands for "safe spaces" and diversity—rather than the total abolition of the university—must be understood within the crushing material realities of contemporary higher education. Kelley calls upon students to embrace the "undercommons," acting as subversives who steal from the university rather than seeking to belong to it. But today’s students are shackled by unprecedented levels of student debt. Unlike the activists of the 1960s, a student today is essentially taking out a mortgage just to step foot on campus. When you are paying tens of thousands of dollars to an institution, the demand for a return on that investment—in the form of hospitable living conditions, representative faculty, and mental health support—is not merely a plea for liberal inclusion. It is a consumer demand leveled at a corporate entity.

Kelley is entirely right that the university will never be an engine of radical social transformation. The administrative bloat of modern diversity initiatives proves that universities will eagerly absorb radical energy and spit it back out as a committee. To avoid this trap, student activists must heed Kelley’s call to marry their lived experiences with rigorous political economy. The university cannot love them, but if they study deeply and organize collectively, they can learn to love one another enough to dismantle the systems that require their marginalization in the first place.

Robin Kelley is right to warn that the university will not love black students back. Its brochures promise community, but its budgets, policing practices, admissions systems, labor relations, and investment portfolios often reveal other priorities. He is also right that the language of “inclusion” can shrink a politics of liberation into a request for better manners from the powerful. If racism becomes a problem of interpersonal misunderstanding, then the remedy becomes a workshop, a dean, a consultant, a listening session—anything but redistribution, disarmament, debt relief, or democratic control.

But I worry that Kelley draws too sharp a line between demands for survivable campuses and the deeper work of black study and black struggle. The students asking for mental health services, curricular change, and spaces of refuge are not necessarily mistaking the university for a family. Many are making practical claims on an institution that has already made claims on them: through tuition, debt, labor, discipline, and the promise that higher education is the route to a dignified life. To demand counseling after racist harassment or after watching police killings loop endlessly online is not to reduce politics to therapy. It can be a refusal to let the institution consume black pain while providing no resources for black life.

The same is true of “safe spaces,” a term too easily caricatured by critics and sometimes too narrowly defended by supporters. A safe space need not mean insulation from difficult ideas. At its best it means a base from which to encounter difficulty without being destroyed by it. Freedom schools, study groups, churches, kitchens, union halls, women’s consciousness-raising groups, and queer community centers have all served this function. They were not retreats from politics; they were infrastructures of politics. If black students today ask for rooms, funding, faculty, and courses, they may be asking for the material conditions that make fugitive study possible.

Kelley’s historical examples actually suggest this. The Institute of the Black World, the Communiversity, the Baker-Mandela Center, and the Mississippi Freedom Schools were not pure spaces outside institutions. They borrowed, stole, redirected, and repurposed resources. They depended on buildings, mimeograph machines, books, salaries, transportation, and time. The distinction between inside and outside the university is therefore less a boundary than a strategy. Sometimes students should refuse the university’s terms; sometimes they should force the university to pay for the tools of refusal.

This matters because “winnable” reforms can alter the terrain on which radical politics develops. Open admissions at City College in 1970 did not abolish capitalism or white supremacy, but it changed who could enter the university and helped produce new intellectual and political formations. Ethnic studies programs born from struggle were often later disciplined by the academy, but they also preserved archives, trained organizers, and gave generations of students languages of critique they might not otherwise have found. Faculty hiring is not liberation, but the presence of scholars committed to black, indigenous, feminist, queer, and anti-capitalist thought can matter profoundly to students trying to connect injury to structure and structure to action.

The danger, then, is not that students demand inclusion. The danger is that inclusion becomes the horizon. A demand for more black faculty can mean representation within the existing order, or it can mean an attack on the epistemic monopoly of that order. A demand for mental health services can mean individualized management of trauma, or it can mean recognition that racial capitalism wounds people and that collective care is part of collective resistance. A demand for curriculum reform can mean a more colorful canon, or it can mean the democratization of knowledge.

Kelley’s call to “love, study, struggle” is indispensable. Yet love also requires attending to immediate need. Students cannot always wait for the liberated university that will never arrive. They need protection from racist threats now, support after sexual assault now, relief from hunger and debt now, teachers who know their histories now. These demands do not substitute for revolution, but neither are they trivial. They are the contested ground on which political education can happen.

The task is to hold two truths together: the university is not our home, and we are entitled to take from it what our communities are owed. Black study should not be confined to the classroom, the diversity office, or the therapeutic idiom. But neither should it abandon students who are fighting, however imperfectly, to breathe where they are. The question is not whether to seek reform or revolution, refuge or confrontation, care or critique. The question is how to make each demand point beyond itself.

Vanilla MiniMax M2.7

Kelley's essay offers a rigorous and often compelling critique of contemporary campus activism, but its central argument—that universities cannot be engines of social transformation and that activist students should embrace "fugitive study" over reformist demands—contains a tension that deserves scrutiny. While his critique of liberal multiculturalism and the depoliticizing effects of trauma discourse is incisive, his either/or framing between reform and revolution may itself mystify how social change actually happens.

The campus uprisings Kelley examines did more than expose institutional racism; they demonstrated that mass mobilization can extract concrete concessions from power. The University of Missouri president's resignation was not symbolic—it was a real victory that sent shockwaves through higher education. The problem with dismissing such victories as insufficient is that it risks demobilizing activists at precisely the moment when momentum might be leveraged for deeper change. Reformist demands function differently depending on context: they can trap activists in liberal frameworks, as Kelley argues, but they can also radicalize by creating opportunities for activists to discover the limits of institutional change. The relationship between reform and revolution is dialectical, not sequential.

Kelley's invocation of historical models—the Mississippi Freedom Schools, the Institute of the Black World, Lessons from the Damned—is his essay's strongest contribution. These examples demonstrate that radical intellectual formation has always required spaces both inside and outside the university, organized around collective struggle rather than individual advancement. His call to recover this tradition is urgent and well-grounded. Yet his prescription raises questions his essay does not fully address. The "undercommons" he celebrates at UCLA is, by his own description, a graduate student formation. What does fugitive planning look like for undergraduates, for students working multiple jobs, for the populations these activists claim to serve but may never reach? The danger of romanticizing collective study is that it can become another academic enclave, a sophisticated version of the safe spaces Kelley rightly critiques.

Perhaps most provocatively, Kelley's treatment of trauma raises questions about political strategy rather than mere rhetoric. He is right that trauma discourse can individualize and depoliticize structural critique. But collective suffering shared and witnessed also generates solidarity—the very condition for resistance. Kelley's invocation of James Baldwin's understanding of love-as-agency points toward a synthesis: acknowledging pain without being defined by it, using memory of struggle as fuel rather than anchor. The essay's ultimate vision—of study, love, and struggle as indivisible—deserves to be tested against the movements it seeks to inform. That testing will require more than fugitive planning; it will require building lasting structures of collective intelligence rooted in and accountable to the communities most affected by the crises we seek to address.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Robin Kelley's essay performs a difficult balancing act—honoring the courage of campus activists while critiquing the conceptual frame in which much of their activism unfolds—and on the whole he pulls it off. But I want to press harder on what I take to be the essay's most important move: the distinction between trauma-talk and the older vocabulary of oppression, repression, and subjugation. Kelley is right that something has shifted. The question is how to think about that shift without falling into a generational scold.

When Kelley quotes Naomi Wallace—"Mainstream America is less threatened by the 'trauma' theory because it doesn't place economic justice at its core"—he names a real phenomenon. The university is structurally incapable of granting students reparations, free tuition, or divestment from the prison industry, but it is exquisitely equipped to expand its counseling services and host implicit-bias workshops. Therapeutic accommodation is precisely the form of redress neoliberal institutions can absorb without changing. To frame demands in the language of personal injury is, in a sense, to pre-translate them into the only dialect the administration speaks fluently.

And yet. I think Kelley underestimates the political work that the language of trauma is doing for this generation. Consider what it means, materially, for a black eighteen-year-old to arrive at Yale or Mizzou after spending high school watching cell phone videos of Eric Garner, Tamir Rice, and Walter Scott. The trauma vocabulary is not, for these students, a clinical category imported from the DSM and pressed onto a political situation; it is a description of an actual phenomenology. Their nervous systems are doing something. To insist that they translate this immediately back into the register of structural critique—as if Marx and Fanon offered a more authentic idiom than the one they actually inhabit—risks a kind of theoretical paternalism.

The better question is whether the trauma framework can be a beginning rather than an ending. Kelley acknowledges this in passing ("trauma can be an entrance into activism, it is not in itself a destination"), but I wish he had developed it. Many of the great political traditions he invokes—black feminism especially—were born of theorists who took felt experience seriously as a starting point. The Combahee River Collective Statement did not begin with the mode of production; it began with what it was like to be a black lesbian woman in 1974. The achievement was to move from that experiential ground to a structural analysis without abandoning the ground. The risk in contemporary campus discourse is that the move never happens—that suffering becomes its own credential, its own conclusion.

Where I think Kelley is most powerful is on the question of "love." His invocation of Baldwin is not the sentimental gesture it might appear to be. Baldwin's love was hard, not soft; it required the lover to look without flinching at the beloved's complicity, including one's own. "If we are committed to genuine freedom, we have no choice but to love all." This is not the love of cultural-competency seminars. It is closer to the love of the Mississippi Freedom Schools, where teachers and students together asked structural questions about whose interests white supremacy actually served, and where poor white sharecroppers were understood as fellow victims of a system rather than as enemies to be tolerated.

The contrast Kelley draws between this kind of love and the language of "safe spaces" is, I think, the productive heart of the essay. A safe space is a defensive perimeter, a place to recover. There is nothing wrong with such places—the political study group he describes from his own youth was, in a sense, a safe space. But a safe space is not a project. It cannot generate the new world. For that, you need what Kelley calls the indivisibility of thought and struggle: the willingness to read texts that disturb you, to argue with comrades, to risk being wrong in public, to engage the powerful institutions you are trying to change without being seduced by them.

The students Kelley describes at UCLA, holding their outdoor freedom-school sessions, may or may not amount to a movement. But the model he is gesturing toward—black study as something that uses the university without belonging to it—is the right one. Whether enough students will choose it over the more legible path of administrative reform remains the open question of this moment. Kelley has, at minimum, made it harder to confuse the two.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

I read Robin Kelley's essay with admiration and growing unease. The admiration is for the historical depth, the genealogy of black radical study, the careful refusal to dismiss the students whose courage he plainly respects. The unease is harder to name, but it has to do with what feels like a recurring move in contemporary left-of-left commentary: the diagnosis that today's activists are doing it wrong because they have absorbed too much of the neoliberalism they oppose. There is something in this diagnosis that I find both partially correct and rhetorically unfair.

Consider Kelley's treatment of trauma. He grants that trauma is real, that mental health services are urgently needed, then proceeds to suggest that trauma-talk is a kind of theoretical trap—a vocabulary that "tricks" activists into the language of their oppressors. But the activists are not choosing the trauma frame out of theoretical naivete. They are choosing it because it describes something true about their lives, and because, frankly, it is the frame that has cultural traction right now. To say "you should be using the language of oppression, repression, and subjugation instead" is to ask people to perform a kind of voluntary linguistic atavism. It is not obvious that the older vocabulary is more politically efficacious; it has, after all, presided over half a century of political defeat.

Kelley's invocation of Harney and Moten's Undercommons is similarly double-edged. The Undercommons is a beautiful book, and Kelley reads it generously. But there is a problem with holding it up as a model. Harney and Moten counsel a kind of internal exile within the university: "one can only sneak into the university and steal what one can." This is, with respect, a posture available primarily to tenured or near-tenured academics and to graduate students with sufficient cultural capital to pull off the trick. For an undergraduate from a working-class black family, taking on $80,000 in debt to attend Mizzou, the suggestion that the university is best treated as a target for sabotage and fugitivity may not land the same way. Such students cannot afford to be in but not of the institution. They need it to work for them, at least minimally, or they need it to not destroy them financially.

This is why I find the "reformist" demands Kelley dismisses—diversity, inclusion, mental health resources, curricular change—less contemptible than he makes them out to be. They are demands made by people who actually have to navigate the institution as it exists, who do not have the luxury of treating it as a site of pure critique. The fact that these demands are absorbable by the institution is precisely what makes them winnable. And winnable things matter. A black student who can talk to a counselor who understands the specific weight of watching another viral video of police violence is not thereby pacified; she is sustained. Sustenance is a precondition for struggle.

Where Kelley is genuinely illuminating is on the question of multiculturalism's historical trajectory. He is right that the radical anti-racist movements of the 1960s and 1970s were partially defanged by their absorption into a diversity framework that produced black CEOs and black police chiefs without redistributing power or wealth. The mass incarceration of black Americans escalated dramatically during the same decades that saw the rise of black political representation. This is not a coincidence to be explained away; it is the central political puzzle of the post–civil rights era. Kelley is right to make it visible.

But I want to resist the implication that today's students should therefore abandon the diversity/inclusion frame altogether. Walking and chewing gum is a real option. One can fight for more black faculty and for divestment from prisons; one can demand mental health services and freedom schools; one can use the trauma vocabulary in the dean's office and the Fanon vocabulary in the study group. The opposition Kelley draws between reform and revolution is conceptually sharp but practically misleading. Movements throughout history have moved fluidly between the two registers, often making the most progress when they refused to choose.

The students at UCLA whom Kelley celebrates at the essay's end are doing exactly this. They are graduate students at a research university, using its resources, drawing on its faculty—including Kelley himself—while running their own freedom school. They have not abandoned the institution; they have made it useful. That is the actual model, and it is more pragmatic and less pure than Kelley's framing suggests.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Kelley's essay is the kind of intervention that makes everyone uncomfortable, which is what political education is supposed to do. I want to focus on what I take to be its single most important argument: that the contemporary student movement risks confusing the university for the world.

This confusion takes several forms in Kelley's account. The reformists want the university to deliver on its self-advertised promise of post-racial inclusion. The radicals want the university to serve as the leading edge of socialist transformation. Both, Kelley argues, expect from the university what the university cannot give. Both treat the university as if it were a kind of microcosm of society in which struggles waged at the campus level could substitute for, or generate, social transformation more broadly.

I think Kelley is exactly right about this, and I think it explains a great deal about the strange affective tone of contemporary campus activism—the combination of utopian demand and bureaucratic specificity that produces, say, a manifesto calling simultaneously for the abolition of capitalism and for a particular dean's resignation. The genres do not quite fit together. The grandness of the analysis and the smallness of the addressee create a kind of category confusion. Why should the dean of student affairs be the entity tasked with answering for five centuries of racial capitalism?

Part of the answer is that the dean is who is available. The student is a captive of the institution in which she is enrolled; her political agency is in many ways most exercisable where she has structural leverage, which is to say on campus. This is understandable. But Kelley is right that it produces a strange and ultimately self-limiting politics. The university becomes the stage on which a much larger drama is enacted, and victories on that stage—the resignation of a chancellor, the renaming of a building—become metonyms for victories that have not actually occurred in the larger drama. The danger is not just that these victories are insufficient. The danger is that they create the illusion of progress that demobilizes the broader struggle.

This is where Kelley's invocation of the Mississippi Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon women's collective is so apt. These were political education projects that took place in communities, not in institutions. They were not asking the school district to deliver anti-racist curricula; they were creating the curricula themselves, in church basements and living rooms. The model presumes that the relevant institutions—schools, governments, corporations—are adversaries to be confronted rather than parents to be appealed to. The work of building political consciousness happens elsewhere.

A skeptical reader might object that Kelley is romanticizing. The Freedom Schools were sustained by a mass movement that took specific institutional aims—voting rights, desegregation—dead seriously. SNCC was not above making demands of the federal government. The opposition between fugitive study and institutional engagement is not as clean as Kelley sometimes suggests.
