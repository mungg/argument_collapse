The very phrase "guilty pleasure" is one we should examine before we use it. Why should any reading be guilty? The notion presumes a hierarchy in which Proust is virtue and a Jackie Collins paperback is vice, and that the reader's soul must be accounted for at the end of August. I reject this framework entirely.

Summer reading is not a confession booth. It is one of the few stretches in the calendar when adults give themselves permission to read the way children read: greedily, indiscriminately, for the sheer narrative rush. A nine-year-old does not feel guilty for tearing through a stack of paperbacks at the public library's summer reading program. Why should her parents?

The novelists asked to confess their secret pleasures inevitably name things that are not, in fact, embarrassing — the ancient Greeks, mid-century memoirs, genre classics. This is itself revealing. Even when invited to slum it, the literary class performs taste. The real guilty pleasure — the airport thriller bought in a hurry, the celebrity tell-all read in a single sweaty afternoon — rarely makes the list, because admitting to it costs cultural capital.

I would rather we drop the pretense. Read what holds you. If that is Aeschylus on the porch, wonderful. If it is a Danielle Steel novel inherited from your aunt's beach house, equally wonderful. The page does not know who is looking at it, and neither, in July, should you.

Let me make a modest case for the beach book as a literary form in its own right. We talk about it as if it were a category of shame — something less than literature — but the requirements of a good beach read are exacting. The prose must be transparent enough to survive distraction. The plot must reassert itself after a swim. The characters must be vivid enough to retain their outlines through suntan oil and a glass of rosé. Pick up a poorly constructed thriller and you'll see how often these conditions are not met.

Elmore Leonard, who is having a quiet renaissance, is a master of this form. So was Patricia Highsmith. So, in his way, is Carl Hiaasen, whose Florida novels are practically engineered for the latitude in which they tend to be read. These are not lesser writers slumming for a paycheck; they are craftsmen working within demanding constraints.

The "guilty pleasure" framing, then, does these writers a disservice. It implies that what makes their books readable in July is some lapse of seriousness, when in fact it is the result of considerable skill. A bad literary novel is a chore on the beach; a good genre novel is a gift.

I'd propose retiring the apologetic tone altogether. The summer reading question is not "what trash will you indulge in" but "what books reward this particular kind of attention" — diffuse, interrupted, sun-dazed. The answer turns out to involve a great deal of very good writing.

I'm skeptical of the whole genre of the summer-reading roundup, and the Times' version is no exception. These features are essentially marketing dressed up as conversation: novelists with books to promote and publishers with backlists to move are invited to chat amiably about what they're reading, and we are invited to feel that we've eavesdropped on something intimate. I'd rather see honest accounts of how writers actually read — distractedly, professionally, often for work, sometimes resentfully. Or, better, honest accounts of how ordinary people read in summer, which would include falling asleep on the porch with the same page open for an hour, abandoning books a third of the way through, and re-reading the same comfort novel for the fifth August in a row.

There is a real subject in summer reading. The roundup format mostly buries it under charm.

I want to push back, gently, on the implicit premise that summer reading is about pleasure and pleasure is about ease. The shift toward digital reading complicates the summer-book ritual in ways worth noting. The Kindle and the iPad now sit on the beach blanket alongside, or instead of, the paperback. Pew's most recent reading surveys show e-book reading rising steadily, especially among adults under fifty. The implications for summer reading are not trivial.

For one thing, the public performance of taste disappears. The whole point of carrying around a fat copy of "Anna Karenina" in July is that other people on the train can see it. The Kindle obliterates this signal. Everyone is reading the same gray rectangle. The guilty pleasure becomes truly private — no one will know if you are reading the new Donna Tartt or the latest in a series about telepathic werewolf detectives.

For another, impulse acquisition has eaten the carefully curated summer pile. Why pack a stack when the whole bookstore is in your bag? Reading becomes more responsive to mood, less to advance planning. This is, I think, mostly good for readers and mostly bad for the cultural rituals — like the newspaper summer-reading list — that grew up around the older model.

I don't mean to lament this. Reading is reading, and a book read on a screen is still a book. But the "guilty pleasure" feature, with its implication that we will be observed making our choices, belongs increasingly to a vanishing world. Soon nobody will know what we read on vacation. We will have to confess it ourselves, or not.

It is time to permanently retire the phrase "guilty pleasure" when it comes to reading. The implication that we should feel a sense of shame for enjoying a romance novel, a fast-paced thriller, or a celebrity memoir is rooted in a lingering, elitist view of what constitutes "real" literature.

The primary purpose of fiction, after all, has always been to transport the reader. If a mass-market paperback by Stephen King or Nora Roberts succeeds in keeping you glued to the page while the ice melts in your lemonade, it has achieved precisely what literature was invented to do.

The literary establishment often creates an artificial hierarchy where dense, emotionally taxing novels are placed at the top, while anything featuring a gripping plot or a happy ending is relegated to the bottom. But reading should not be a chore, and it certainly shouldn't be an exercise in public posturing. If you want to spend your July afternoons tearing through *Gone Girl* or the latest James Patterson novel, you should do so with zero guilt. Reading is one of the few entirely solitary, selfish acts we have left in our hyper-connected world. It belongs to you, not to the critics. Let’s reclaim the joy of reading purely for the sake of enjoyment.

For some, summer creates anxiety. There's pressure to use vacation for self-improvement, to emerge from August having read everything on the "serious" list. The relaxation becomes another form of productivity, and the guilt around pleasure reading intensifies rather than diminishes.

I know readers who feel genuine shame about their summer choices. They read genre fiction secretly, apologizing for their preferences even as they consume books hungrily. This is absurd. Pleasure requires no justification.

Meanwhile, some readers use summer precisely to tackle ambitious projects: reading through a particular author's complete works, finally studying that philosophy they'd avoided, working through dense historical research. For them, the slower pace enables concentration rather than escape.

The diversity of reading experiences is the point. Summer offers time, and readers will fill that time with whatever satisfies their needs. Some need rest; others need challenge. Neither is wrong.

What matters is that people read at all, in whatever form brings them back to the page.

The mention of novelists sharing their guilty pleasures reveals an interesting dynamic: when authors discuss their reading habits, we listen differently than when critics or academics do. There's an implicit permission granted by the creator.

Perhaps this is because novelists are assumed to understand something about the craft of narrative that readers crave. Their recommendations carry authenticity. A novelist confessing they love detective novels legitimizes those books for their audience.

I find myself curious about the answers the willing novelists gave, the specific books they confessed to reading. This curiosity reflects our broader culture's appetite for insider information, for seeing behind the curtain of artistic production. We want to know what fuels the creators we admire.

Summer reading, in this framework, becomes an act of connection. When a writer I respect mentions a guilty pleasure, I feel a kind of solidarity—here is someone else who abandons pretension in favor of pure engagement.

Maybe that's the real value in discussing summer reading: it reminds us that readers share more than they differ, that the love of story transcends literary hierarchies. Whether we're reading ancient Greeks or contemporary memoirs, we're after the same thing: transport, understanding, the pleasure of spending time in imagined worlds.

That community should be celebrated, not categorized.
