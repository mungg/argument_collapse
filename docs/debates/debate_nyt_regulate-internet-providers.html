More Internet Regulation

The chairman of the Federal Communications Commission today proposed that the Internet be regulated as a public utility, to ensure net neutrality, prohibiting higher-priced fast lanes that would give some content providers an advantage.

It would also set up a regulatory framework to enforce certain consumer protections, like privacy. But will regulation create or stifle innovation?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The framing of "regulation versus innovation" obscures what is actually at stake in Chairman Wheeler's proposal. Title II reclassification is not a sweeping new regulatory regime imposed on the web; it is a legal vehicle to preserve the network's existing character. The internet that produced Google, Netflix, Etsy, and countless garage startups grew up under a de facto common-carrier expectation: that the company carrying your bits would not inspect, prioritize, or extort the applications running over its pipes. The question is whether we keep that arrangement or let it erode.

Critics warn that Title II will deter broadband investment. But this prediction sits uneasily with the evidence. Wireless voice has been regulated under Title II for years without collapsing carrier capex. Cable operators continued to build during the pre-2002 period when DSL was a common-carrier service. Meanwhile, the FCC has signaled it will forbear from rate regulation, tariffing, and unbundling — the parts of Title II that genuinely chill investment. What remains is essentially a no-blocking, no-throttling, no-paid-prioritization rule.

The real innovation question runs the other way. If ISPs can charge content companies for prioritized delivery, the next Netflix needs not only a better product but also a check large enough to match Comcast's incumbents. That is a barrier the founders of YouTube did not face.

Regulation can certainly stifle, and the FCC's rulemaking deserves close scrutiny — particularly on privacy, where the agency has limited expertise. But preserving a level playing field for edge providers is not the enemy of innovation. It is the precondition for it.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The question of whether regulating internet service providers as common carriers will create or stifle innovation is not merely theoretical—it is already being answered by the very architecture of the internet that gave rise to the modern digital economy. Chairman Wheeler’s proposal would restore the legal foundation that allowed the internet to become the greatest engine of innovation in a generation, precisely because it prevents the gatekeepers from picking winners and losers.

The internet’s most transformative companies—from Google and Amazon to Netflix and countless startups—were born in an environment where anyone with a good idea could reach users on equal terms. That environment was not an accident. It was the product of decades in which the underlying network functioned as a neutral conveyance, first under the Computer Inquiry rules and later under FCC principles that, until court challenges gutted them, prevented blocking and discrimination. Reclassifying broadband under Title II, with forbearance from rate regulation and other legacy telephone-era rules, restores that baseline. Innovation flourishes when the next garage entrepreneur knows her streaming service, telemedicine app, or educational platform won’t be throttled into a slow lane because she cannot pay the tolls that entrenched incumbents can easily afford.

Critics warn that utility-style regulation will depress investment in network infrastructure. The evidence, however, points the other way. The most investment-intensive period of broadband deployment occurred when DSL providers were subject to common-carriage obligations, and wireless networks have thrived under a similar light-touch regulatory framework for decades. The real risk to innovation is not that ISPs will invest less—it is that they will redirect their capital away from improving the network for everyone and toward creating artificial scarcity, toll booths, and preferential fast lanes that monetize their control over access. When a broadband provider can extract rent from content companies, it has every incentive to let the “public” internet languish while steering customers toward its own affiliated services or those willing to pay for priority.

Regulation here does not mean heavy-handed micromanagement. The FCC’s plan wisely proposes to forbear from hundreds of Title II provisions, focusing only on the core rules against blocking, throttling, and paid prioritization, along with basic consumer privacy protections. This targeted approach preserves flexibility while ensuring the network remains a dumb pipe that enables intelligence at the edges, where true innovation lives. In that sense, the proposed regulation is not a new intervention but a restoration of the conditions that allowed the internet to out-innovate every closed network that came before it.

Regulation can stifle innovation when it freezes business models or invites micromanagement. But the F.C.C.’s net neutrality proposal, if confined to nondiscrimination and basic consumer protections, is more likely to protect innovation than suppress it.

The Internet’s greatest economic achievement has been its openness at the edges. A student, a small start-up or a nonprofit can reach users without first negotiating with Comcast, Verizon or AT&T. That design helped produce Google, Facebook, Skype, Netflix and thousands of less famous services. If broadband providers may sell priority lanes or disadvantage services that compete with their own video or voice offerings, innovation shifts from “build something users want” to “strike a deal with the gatekeeper.” That is not a free market; it is a tollbooth on a market with few alternative roads.

The argument against reclassification under Title II is not frivolous. Broadband networks require large capital investment, and investors dislike regulatory uncertainty. The F.C.C. should therefore forbear from rate regulation, tariffs and other old telephone rules that do not fit the Internet. It should make clear that it is not setting prices or approving products. A light-touch common-carrier framework can prohibit blocking, throttling and paid prioritization while leaving network engineering and pricing flexibility intact.

Nor is the status quo regulation-free. When a household has only one or two realistic high-speed broadband choices, the provider already exercises private regulation over access to speech, commerce and culture. Public rules can be less arbitrary than private leverage.

The right comparison is not regulation versus innovation. It is rules that preserve an open platform versus deals that let incumbents pick winners. Done narrowly, net neutrality is not a burden on the Internet’s future; it is insurance for it.

Chairman Wheeler's proposal to reclassify broadband under Title II is overdue, and the alarmism about it killing innovation deserves real scrutiny. The most consequential innovations of the past two decades — search, streaming video, social networks, cloud computing, voice over IP — happened on top of a network that was, for most of its formative years, treated as a common carrier under the old dial-up regime. The "light-touch" hands-off framework of the past decade coincided not with an explosion of ISP innovation but with consolidation, rising prices, and a handful of regional monopolies. The innovation we care about happens at the edges, and edge innovators need a neutral pipe.

The fast-lane question makes this clearest. If Comcast or Verizon can charge Netflix, YouTube, and Hulu for priority delivery, the next streaming startup faces a tax that incumbents can absorb and it cannot. We have already seen previews of this: the Netflix-Comcast interconnection dispute last year, where streaming quality mysteriously degraded until Netflix paid up, is exactly the dynamic neutrality rules are meant to prevent. A garage startup cannot negotiate paid peering with every major ISP.

Critics warn that Title II will discourage broadband investment. But Title II has been applied with significant forbearance to wireless voice for years, and the wireless industry has hardly suffered for capital. Wheeler has explicitly said the FCC will forbear from rate regulation, tariffing, and unbundling. What remains is the legal authority to enforce no-blocking, no-throttling, and no-paid-prioritization rules — the minimum needed after the D.C. Circuit struck down the 2010 Open Internet Order last year in Verizon v. FCC precisely because the Commission had not classified ISPs as common carriers.

There is a legitimate worry about regulatory capture and creeping scope. The FCC should publish bright-line rules and resist the temptation to micromanage network engineering. But the alternative — letting vertically integrated ISPs decide which applications thrive — is worse. Innovation policy is about who gets to permission whom. Title II keeps permission with users and developers rather than with the cable company. That is the pro-innovation choice.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The romance of "net neutrality" obscures a simple fact: applying Title II of the 1934 Communications Act — written for Ma Bell's copper monopoly — to the dynamic, packet-switched internet is a category error. Chairman Wheeler is reaching for the heaviest hammer in the FCC's toolbox to solve problems that are either hypothetical or better handled by antitrust and consumer-protection law.

Start with the supposed harm. Despite years of warnings, there is no evidence of systematic blocking or throttling of lawful content by major U.S. ISPs. The Comcast-BitTorrent incident from 2007 was resolved without Title II. The Netflix peering disputes were commercial negotiations over interconnection costs — the kind of thing that happens constantly in the backbone market — not consumer-facing throttling. Regulating to prevent a parade of horribles that has not materialized is a recipe for unintended consequences.

Those consequences are predictable. Title II brings with it decades of accumulated rules, tariff requirements, and litigation. Wheeler promises "forbearance," but forbearance is revocable, contestable in court, and subject to the political composition of future Commissions. ISPs facing this uncertainty rationally pull back on the $70-plus billion a year they currently invest in network upgrades. Verizon has already signaled it would revisit FiOS expansion under Title II. Smaller wireless ISPs and rural cooperatives, which lack armies of regulatory lawyers, will be hit hardest.

There is also a real innovation cost on the network side. Differentiated services — low-latency lanes for telemedicine, telepresence surgery, autonomous vehicles, real-time gaming — may genuinely require prioritization. A flat ban on paid prioritization forecloses business models we have not yet imagined. The internet's genius has been permissionless experimentation at every layer, including the transport layer.

If Congress believes the FCC needs new authority to police genuine abuses, it should legislate narrowly: bright-line prohibitions on blocking and anticompetitive discrimination, with FTC-style enforcement. What we should not do is shoehorn the 21st-century internet into a Depression-era utility statute and then trust regulators to "forbear" from the parts that would obviously do damage. That is regulation by promise, and promises change.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The net neutrality debate has been framed as innovation-versus-regulation, but that is the wrong axis. The real question is: whose innovation? Title II reclassification is fundamentally a distributional choice between two classes of innovators — the ISPs that own the pipes and the application developers that ride on them — and it is worth being honest about that.

Under a paid-prioritization regime, ISPs gain a new revenue stream and can innovate in service differentiation: managed video, enterprise-grade low-latency offerings, tiered consumer plans. Under strict neutrality, application-layer companies — the next Twitch, the next Snapchat, the next telemedicine startup — get the certainty that their packets will be treated like everyone else's. Both are forms of innovation. Policy has to pick.

The case for picking the edge is empirical. The American broadband market is not competitive in the textbook sense. Most households have one or two realistic choices for high-speed wired internet. In a competitive market, neutrality might emerge naturally — discriminate and customers leave. In a duopoly market, ISPs can extract rents from edge providers without consequence, and consumers have nowhere to go. Common-carrier rules are a classic remedy for exactly this market structure; that is why they were applied to railroads, telegraphs, and telephone networks in the first place.

The wrinkle is that Title II is overbroad. It carries baggage — tariffing, accounting rules, retail price regulation — that nobody actually wants applied to broadband. The Commission's plan to forbear from these provisions is legally plausible but will be litigated for years. A cleaner solution would be Section 706 authority combined with narrow bright-line rules, but the D.C. Circuit's Verizon decision last year made clear that path is constrained without common-carrier classification.

So Wheeler is doing the second-best thing because Congress will not do the first-best thing, which is to pass a modern statute giving the FCC tailored authority. In the meantime, Title II with forbearance is a defensible bridge. It protects the conditions under which permissionless innovation has flourished, while leaving room for the Commission to retreat from overreach. Imperfect, but on balance, correct.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Lost in the shouting about Title II is the part of Chairman Wheeler's proposal that may matter most in the long run: extending consumer protections, including privacy, to broadband service. Net neutrality gets the headlines, but the privacy implications of ISP regulation deserve more attention than they are getting.

Your ISP sees everything. Even with the steady migration toward HTTPS, your broadband provider knows which sites you visit, when, for how long, from which devices, and increasingly — through DNS queries and traffic analysis — what you are doing there. Unlike a website you choose to visit, your ISP is largely unavoidable; in most American markets you have one or two options. Yet today, ISPs operate under a patchwork of FTC oversight and self-regulation, while telephone carriers' handling of customer information is governed by detailed Section 222 rules under Title II.

Reclassification would bring broadband under that same Customer Proprietary Network Information framework. That is a significant upgrade in user protection, and it is one of the strongest under-discussed reasons to support Wheeler's approach. As ISPs increasingly look to monetize subscriber data — Verizon's "supercookie" controversy a few months ago is a warning shot — the absence of clear federal rules is becoming untenable.

Will this stifle innovation? Some, yes — specifically, innovation in surveillance-based advertising by ISPs. That is innovation we can do without, or at least innovation that should require meaningful user consent. The broader concern that privacy rules deter network investment does not hold up; European carriers operate under far stricter privacy regimes and continue to build out fiber and LTE.

There are legitimate worries about regulatory overlap with the FTC and about the FCC's relative inexperience with privacy enforcement. The Commission will need to build that expertise, coordinate jurisdiction, and resist mission creep. But the alternative — leaving the most intimate window into Americans' digital lives governed by ISPs' own terms of service — is not a defensible status quo. Whatever one thinks about fast lanes, the privacy piece of this proposal is overdue, and the innovation-stifling rhetoric should not be allowed to bury it.

The explosion of internet applications, from streaming video to cloud computing, occurred precisely because broadband was lightly regulated. By outlawing even beneficial prioritization, the FCC closes the door on business models that could fund the next wave of infrastructure upgrades. The question of whether the FCC’s sweeping Title II proposal will create or stifle innovation is a false choice. It will likely do both, and the real debate is whether the plan’s cure is more dangerous than the disease. The intention — preserving an open internet where startups can compete without permission from broadband gatekeepers — is noble and deserves a surgical tool. What Chairman Wheeler has laid on the table is a sledgehammer that could crack the foundation of infrastructure investment even as it protects the attic full of apps.

On one hand, explicit rules against paid prioritization, blocking, and throttling would remove a genuine threat to application-layer innovation. Venture capitalists have already signaled that they would be reluctant to fund a bandwidth-intensive start-up that might have to pay tolls to reach customers. A clear, enforceable ban creates certainty that the next Netflix or Skype can bloom without an ISP’s blessing. Additionally, baseline privacy protections would encourage the adoption of data-driven services, as consumers grow wary of unchecked surveillance by internet providers.

Yet the means matter enormously. Reclassifying broadband under Title II opens the door to a host of regulatory powers originally designed for the Bell System monopoly. Even with the FCC’s promise of forbearance, legal ambiguity will spawn years of litigation and chilled investment. Small and rural ISPs, already operating on thin margins, have warned that the compliance burden alone will force them to halt expansion plans. The risk is not just a hypothetical future of rate regulation; it is the immediate slowdown of fiber buildouts and wireless upgrades that disproportionately affect underserved communities, where connectivity itself is the first stage of innovation.

A smarter path exists. Congress, or the FCC using a different legal authority, could enact bright-line net neutrality rules while explicitly forswearing the most intrusive Title II provisions, locking those forbearances into statute. That approach would protect the open internet without treating broadband like a stagnant water utility. Innovation demands both a free-flowing network at the application layer and a rapidly improving network at the physical layer. The current proposal overcorrects, safeguarding the first at the likely expense of the second.
