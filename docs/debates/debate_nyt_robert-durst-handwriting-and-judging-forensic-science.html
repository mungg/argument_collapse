Robert Durst, Handwriting and Judging Forensic Science

The murder case against Robert Durst is the latest controversy involving forensic science — can analysis of handwriting on two envelopes demonstrate guilt, or will contradictory analysis and doubts about the technique lead to acquittal? That discipline is one of several in which reliability has been called into question over the past few years. A National Commission on Forensic Science is considering changes in procedures and techniques. How can forensic science be made more dependable and professional?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Durst case spotlights a problem the 2009 National Academy of Sciences report laid out plainly: outside of nuclear DNA analysis, most forensic disciplines have never been rigorously validated. Handwriting comparison, bite-mark analysis, hair microscopy, tool-mark and even some fingerprint comparisons rest largely on examiner judgment, trained by apprenticeship and defended by tradition rather than measured by known error rates. The FBI's recent admission that its hair-analysis testimony was overstated in case after case—some of them capital—should disabuse anyone of the idea that confident courtroom assertions equal scientific certainty.

Three reforms would do the most good. First, every pattern-matching discipline needs published, peer-reviewed validation studies establishing how often examiners are right, wrong, and inconclusive on realistic samples. Until handwriting examiners can tell a jury "studies show practitioners err X percent of the time under these conditions," their conclusions are opinions dressed as science.

Second, examiners should work blind. They should not know what investigators believe, what the suspect has confessed, or which sample is the "questioned" one when alternatives can be presented. Cognitive-bias research, including Itiel Dror's experiments showing fingerprint examiners reversing themselves with context, makes this indispensable.

Third, crime laboratories should be structurally independent of police and prosecutors, mandatorily accredited, and subject to routine proficiency testing whose results are disclosed in discovery. The National Commission on Forensic Science, paired with NIST's scientific role, can drive uniform standards—but only if courts stop reflexively admitting techniques under Frye-era precedents and start applying Daubert seriously.

Whether Durst is convicted or acquitted matters less than whether the case finally pushes judges to demand evidence that forensic evidence works.

The Robert Durst case, with its dramatic duel of handwriting experts, is not an anomaly—it is a symptom of a forensic science system in dire need of reform. When two seasoned document examiners can look at the same envelope scribble and reach diametrically opposed conclusions about authorship, the discipline itself is on trial, not just the millionaire accused of murder. This case lays bare the central weakness of the “pattern matching” fields, from bite marks to hair microscopy: they evolved as police crafts, grounded in apprenticeships and anecdotal experience rather than in rigorous, peer-reviewed science.

The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report delivered a devastating verdict on these disciplines, finding that with the exception of DNA, no forensic method has been proven to consistently and reliably link evidence to a specific individual. It highlighted the lack of standardized protocols, the absence of meaningful error rates, and the powerful, often unchecked, influence of cognitive bias—where an examiner who knows the suspect is a prime target might see a match where none exists. The newly formed National Commission on Forensic Science is the direct result of that wake-up call, and it has a historic opportunity to codify what true scientific practice requires.

To become truly dependable, forensic science must first be founded on empirical research that establishes the validity and limits of each technique. For handwriting analysis, this means large-scale studies that measure how often examiners correctly identify the writer, how often they disagree, and under what conditions errors spike. Second, all labs and practitioners must be subject to mandatory, blind proficiency testing and accreditation, severing the institutional tie to law enforcement that breeds confirmation bias. Finally, judges must act as rigorous gatekeepers, refusing to admit testimony that overstates certainty or implies a level of accuracy without quantitative backing. The Durst trial will likely turn on a jury’s gut reaction to expert theater. Our justice system should demand more: a forensic science that earns its credibility rather than borrows it from a detective’s case file.

The Durst case illustrates both the promise and the danger of forensic evidence. A similarity between the handwriting on two envelopes may be relevant, but it should not be treated as a scientific trump card. Handwriting comparison depends on judgments about slant, spacing, letter formation and habits of abbreviation. Those judgments can be useful, especially when combined with other evidence, but the field has not always established clear error rates, uniform standards or the limits of what an examiner can responsibly say.

The larger problem is that American forensic science grew up mostly inside law enforcement, not inside the scientific research culture. Many techniques became courtroom staples before they were subjected to rigorous validation. The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report made this point forcefully: with the exception of nuclear DNA analysis, many forensic disciplines lacked strong empirical foundations. Hair microscopy, bite-mark comparison, tool marks, fingerprints and handwriting have too often been presented in language far more certain than the underlying studies justify.

Making forensic science dependable requires changing both institutions and courtroom habits. Crime laboratories should be independent from police and prosecutors, or at least insulated from investigative pressure. Analysts should receive only the information needed to perform the test, not a narrative about the suspect’s supposed guilt. Blind testing and regular proficiency exams should be routine, and their results should be public enough to build confidence. Accreditation should be mandatory, not aspirational.

Equally important, forensic testimony must become more modest and more quantitative. Examiners should not say that a mark, print or handwriting sample “matches” to the exclusion of all others unless the science can support that claim. They should explain the basis for their conclusion, the quality of the samples, known error rates where available, and the possibility of disagreement among competent analysts. Judges, applying Daubert or similar standards, should demand validation studies rather than deferring to tradition.

Defense lawyers also need fair access to evidence, experts and laboratory notes. A forensic system is not reliable if only one side can afford to test its claims. Finally, scientific research should be funded outside the pressures of individual prosecutions, so that methods can be tested before liberty depends on them.

Forensic science can help courts find the truth. But it becomes dangerous when subjective expertise is dressed up as certainty. The goal should not be to exclude forensic evidence categorically, but to make its strengths, weaknesses and limits visible to juries.

The Durst case exposes a truth that defense attorneys have been shouting for decades: much of what we call "forensic science" is not science at all. It is a craft, often a guess, dressed up in the white coat and authoritative cadence of a laboratory expert. Handwriting comparison, bite-mark analysis, hair microscopy, even certain kinds of fingerprint matching — these were developed by police, for police, and validated mostly by tradition and courtroom acceptance rather than by the rigorous error-rate studies that distinguish actual science from educated intuition.

The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report, "Strengthening Forensic Science in the United States," should have been a wake-up call. It found that, with the partial exception of nuclear DNA analysis, no forensic method had been "rigorously shown to have the capacity to consistently, and with a high degree of certainty, demonstrate a connection between evidence and a specific individual or source." Six years later, we are still in roughly the same place, watching prosecutors offer handwriting matches to juries as though the comparison rested on physics rather than on the unverified judgment of an analyst.

The fix is not mysterious. It requires three things. First, every forensic discipline must produce published, peer-reviewed error rates derived from blind proficiency testing. If a handwriting examiner cannot tell us how often examiners misattribute disguised writing, the examiner should not be permitted to tell a jury anything at all. Second, crime labs must be physically and administratively independent of police and prosecutors. The cognitive bias literature is overwhelming: analysts who know what the investigator expects are more likely to find it. Sequential unmasking and case-blind review should be standard. Third, courts must finally take Daubert seriously. Judges have functioned as gatekeepers in name only, admitting techniques that would not survive a single round of peer review in a real scientific journal.

Whether Durst is guilty or innocent of killing Susan Berman is, in some sense, beside the larger point. He may well be convicted, or acquitted, on the basis of handwriting testimony that no statistician would endorse. That is the scandal. A criminal-justice system that locks people up on the strength of disciplines it has never bothered to validate is not administering justice; it is administering theater. The National Commission on Forensic Science has a chance to change that — but only if it is willing to tell judges, prosecutors, and the public that some long-accepted techniques simply do not meet the standard of evidence a free society should require.

There is a temptation, watching the Durst case unfold alongside a documentary's dramatic flourishes, to treat handwriting analysis as junk and move on. That would be a mistake. The right question is not whether forensic disciplines are perfect, but whether they are useful, and what institutional arrangements make them more or less reliable.

Handwriting comparison is a good test case. Unlike bite-mark analysis, which compares an impression on flesh that distorts and heals, handwriting comparison operates on stable physical artifacts. Studies by Moshe Kam and others in the 1990s found that trained document examiners outperformed laypeople in distinguishing genuine from simulated writing, sometimes substantially. That is not nothing. It does not mean any individual examiner's opinion in any individual case should be treated as gospel — but it does mean the discipline has empirical support that, say, hair-fiber identification largely lacks.

So how do we improve forensic science without throwing out the parts that work? Four practical reforms suggest themselves.

One: mandatory blind proficiency testing for every practicing examiner, with results disclosed to defense counsel. If a handwriting expert has missed three out of ten disguised samples in recent testing, the jury deserves to know.

Two: standardized terminology. Right now, examiners testify to "matches," "identifications," "consistent with" findings, with no uniform meaning. The recently proposed move away from categorical conclusions toward probabilistic statements is the right direction, even if it makes for less satisfying courtroom drama.

Three: contextual bias controls. Analysts should receive only the evidence necessary to perform their comparison, not the narrative the detective has constructed around it. This is cheap, easy, and resisted mostly out of inertia.

Four: a credentialing regime with teeth. Forensic disciplines should look more like medicine and less like a guild. Board certification, continuing education, and the ability to revoke credentials for misconduct would professionalize fields that currently rely on apprenticeship and reputation.

The National Commission on Forensic Science, established last year, can push these changes. Whether it will depends largely on whether the FBI and state crime labs treat it as a partner or as a threat. The Bureau's painful admission, just months ago, that examiners overstated microscopic hair analysis evidence in hundreds of cases should be a chastening precedent. Reform from within is possible; resistance is no longer credible.

Durst's jury will do what juries do, weighing handwriting alongside everything else. Our job is to make sure the next jury gets better evidence than this one.

We are having the wrong conversation. The problem with forensic science is not primarily one of methodology — it is one of incentives. Crime labs in America are overwhelmingly housed within police departments or prosecutors' offices, funded by them, supervised by them, and evaluated by them. Asking these institutions to deliver neutral scientific judgment is like asking the home team's coach to umpire the World Series.

Consider the structural pressures. A handwriting examiner employed by a district attorney sees the same investigators every week. She knows which case is high-profile. She is told the suspect's name, sometimes shown the suspect's confession, often briefed on the theory of the crime before she ever looks at a questioned document. Then she is asked, in effect, to render an opinion that could embarrass her colleagues if it goes the wrong way. Even an examiner of perfect integrity is subject to the unconscious cognitive biases the psychological literature has documented exhaustively. Itiel Dror's work on fingerprint examiners showed that contextual information can flip experts' conclusions on the same prints. There is no reason to think document examiners are immune.

The fix begins with independence. Forensic laboratories should be removed from law enforcement and reconstituted as neutral scientific institutions, accountable to the courts or to independent science agencies rather than to prosecutors. The medical examiner reforms in some jurisdictions provide a model: coroners answerable to politicians produced bad death investigations, and replacing them with independent physician-led offices improved outcomes considerably.

Independence alone is not enough. Funding parity matters too. Defense access to forensic experts is laughably underfunded in most jurisdictions. A defendant facing handwriting testimony from a state-employed examiner with thirty years' experience often cannot find — much less pay for — a comparably qualified expert to challenge the conclusion. The adversarial system depends on rough equality of arms, and forensic evidence has become one of its most lopsided battlefields.

Finally, transparency. Bench notes, error rates, and case files should be available to defense counsel as a matter of course, not as the result of contentious discovery fights. Black-box conclusions delivered by a lab the defense cannot scrutinize are not evidence — they are pronouncements.

The Durst case will turn on what twelve jurors make of dueling handwriting opinions. But the deeper question is whether we have built institutions capable of producing reliable forensic conclusions in the first place. Right now, the answer is no, and tinkering with techniques will not change that until we change who controls the labs.

The push to remake forensic science is overdue, but reformers should beware of overcorrection. There is a real risk that, in our enthusiasm to apply pristine laboratory standards to messy investigative work, we end up excluding genuinely probative evidence and tilting the system toward acquittal of the obviously guilty.

Consider what handwriting analysis actually accomplishes. In the Durst matter, the question is whether the same hand wrote the "Beverley" note alerting police to Susan Berman's body and an admitted exemplar from Durst himself, including the same distinctive misspelling. This is not phrenology. It is not a divining rod. It is a trained comparison of physical features that, when distinctive enough, carries real evidentiary weight. To pretend otherwise — to say that because some handwriting examiners have been wrong, no handwriting testimony should ever be heard — would deprive juries of useful information.

The 2009 NAS report, valuable as it was, is sometimes invoked as if it concluded that all non-DNA forensic evidence is worthless. It did not. It said that disciplines vary widely in their empirical foundation, that error rates need study, and that practices need standardization. Those are reformist recommendations, not abolitionist ones. The proper response is to do the studies, set the standards, train the examiners — not to retreat to a regime where only DNA counts.

It is also worth remembering that exclusion of forensic evidence is not cost-free. Crimes go unsolved. Victims go without justice. Communities lose confidence in the courts. The defense bar, understandably, focuses on wrongful convictions, and these are real and serious. But there are also wrongful acquittals, and a system that throws out every forensic discipline short of mathematical proof will produce many of them.

What I would urge instead is calibration. Probabilistic testimony rather than categorical claims. Clear disclosure of error rates and examiner training. Cross-examination, not exclusion, as the primary check. Juries are capable of weighing competing experts; the Durst case will feature exactly that, with each side calling document examiners and the jury sorting it out. That is the adversarial system functioning as designed.

The National Commission on Forensic Science should pursue improvement, not purism. Better protocols, accreditation, independence from prosecutorial pressure — yes to all. But let us not pretend that a handwriting examiner with decades of training has nothing to say about whether two distinctive samples were written by the same person. Sometimes the obvious answer is the right one, and forensic science exists in part to confirm it.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

The Robert Durst case is being treated as a referendum on handwriting analysis, but it should really prompt a broader question: why do American courts admit expert testimony that would never survive scrutiny in any other empirical field?

In medicine, a new diagnostic test is required to demonstrate sensitivity, specificity, and reproducibility before clinicians may rely on it. In engineering, a structural calculation must be backed by published methods and validated assumptions. In epidemiology, a causal claim must clear journal review and replication. In forensic science, by contrast, an examiner may walk into court and declare that two signatures were written by the same person, basing the conclusion on training, experience, and a methodology that has often never been subjected to controlled testing at all.

The mechanism that allows this is Federal Rule of Evidence 702 and the Daubert framework, which empower judges to act as gatekeepers screening out unreliable expert testimony. In theory, Daubert demands consideration of testability, error rates, peer review, and general acceptance. In practice, judges are not scientists, are reluctant to disturb long-standing forensic disciplines, and frequently default to admitting evidence on the basis of "general acceptance" within a small community of practitioners who all trained one another. The result is a closed loop: the discipline is reliable because its practitioners say it is, and the practitioners say it is because the discipline has long been admitted in court.

Breaking this loop requires changes in three places. First, judicial education. Judges should receive serious training in evaluating scientific claims, perhaps through a dedicated science advisory office attached to the federal courts. Second, an external validation requirement: forensic disciplines seeking admissibility should have to point to independent, published studies of their accuracy, not just to internal proficiency tests run by friendly labs. Third, a willingness by appellate courts to actually reverse trial judges who admit shaky evidence. Right now, abuse-of-discretion review essentially insulates admission decisions from meaningful scrutiny.

Durst may be convicted or acquitted. The handwriting on those envelopes may indeed be his — common sense suggests as much, given the misspelling — but conviction should rest on evidence whose reliability has been demonstrated, not asserted. Forensic science will become more dependable when courts stop accepting "trust the examiner" as a substitute for "show your work." Until then, every conviction built on weakly validated techniques is a conviction the legal system may someday have to revisit, as the FBI is now doing with hundreds of hair-comparison cases. That is not a sustainable model of justice.

Most discussions of forensic reform focus, understandably, on the science: error rates, peer review, validation studies. These are essential. But there is a parallel problem we discuss less often, which is the performance of forensic expertise in the courtroom. Even good science can become bad evidence when filtered through the theatrics of a trial.

Consider what jurors actually experience. An examiner takes the stand in a crisp suit, recites credentials, displays magnified images on a screen, and offers a conclusion in confident language: "in my professional opinion, to a reasonable degree of scientific certainty, the same person wrote both documents." That phrase — "reasonable degree of scientific certainty" — has no defined meaning. It is a rhetorical construct that sounds rigorous but conveys nothing about probability or error. Jurors hear authority; they do not hear uncertainty.

This is a problem of communication as much as of method. Even where the underlying analysis is sound, the way it is presented systematically misleads. The CSI effect cuts both ways: jurors expect dramatic certainty, and experts deliver it, because hedged answers feel weak under cross-examination. The result is testimony that overstates what the science can support, not necessarily because anyone is lying, but because the courtroom format rewards confidence and punishes nuance.

Several practical reforms could help. First, ban the phrase "reasonable degree of scientific certainty" outright, as some reformers have urged. Replace it with explicit statements of likelihood: how often examiners agree, how often they err, what the comparison can and cannot establish. Second, require written reports that conform to a standardized template, with sections devoted to limitations and known sources of error. The report, not the live testimony, would set the bounds of what the examiner could claim. Third, give juries clear instructions about how to weigh forensic testimony, including reminders that experience is not the same as accuracy and that experts can be wrong in good faith.

The Durst case is unusual because the public has been treated to a sustained look at forensic uncertainty, with competing examiners reaching different conclusions on the same envelopes. That is healthy. It shows ordinary citizens what specialists have long known — that handwriting comparison is judgment, not measurement. If the National Commission on Forensic Science wants to make a real contribution, it might start by demanding honest courtroom language. Better protocols matter, but so does telling jurors the truth about what the experts in front of them actually know, and how surely they know it.

Handwriting analysis has been practiced for over a century and can provide valuable insights when performed by qualified experts. The Robert Durst case, like many high-profile trials, revolves around dueling experts who reach opposite conclusions, but this adversarial back-and-forth obscures the real progress being made. The issue is not whether forensic document examination should exist, but how it can be made rigorous enough to merit a courtroom’s trust.

The field suffers from uneven standards. Some practitioners work with extensive documentation and controlled comparisons, while others render sweeping conclusions from scant materials. The key is to distinguish the two. Accredited laboratories that require examiners to complete standardized training, to use widely accepted protocols like the ASTM guidelines, and to undergo annual proficiency testing can produce analyses that are far more reliable than the ad-hoc methods of a hired gun. Similarly, requiring examiners to detail exactly which similarities and differences they observed, and to present the written work for peer review before trial, would reduce the kind of bare assertion that juries find persuasive but scientists find hollow.

Blind testing is a necessary next step. When examiners know which side retained them, confirmation bias creeps in. Just as police departments now use double-blind lineups, forensic labs should submit occasional blind cases—disguised as routine work—to measure an examiner’s accuracy against ground truth. This would generate error rates that allow courts to evaluate the weight of the evidence, rather than treating it as infallible.

Handwriting comparison will never be DNA typing. It rests on human judgment about patterns that an expert has learned to recognize. But methods can be standardized, examiners can be tested, and testimony can be restrained. When those conditions are met, jurors should be allowed to hear the opinion and combine it with the rest of the evidence. The Durst trial may turn on fonts and loops, but the deeper lesson is that no forensic discipline reforms itself; it takes sustained pressure from judges, scientific bodies, and the public to make it truly dependable.

If a technique cannot demonstrate a known error rate under controlled conditions, it has no place in a criminal courtroom. Handwriting identification, the very “science” at the heart of the Robert Durst case, fails that test by a mile. The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report minced no words: the scientific basis for handwriting comparison is “limited,” and there is a “dearth of peer-reviewed, published studies” on its accuracy. In the years since, little has changed.

Proponents of forensic document examination will point to a few small studies where experts performed well. But these studies rarely reflect the messy reality of a criminal case, where the comparison is often based on a tiny sample of writing produced under stress, on different paper, with different pens. Worse, many laboratories still refuse to subject examiners to blind proficiency tests that mirror casework conditions. When real-world tests are conducted—like the famous proficiency exam given by the American Board of Forensic Document Examiners where a third of participants made incorrect identifications—the error rates are alarming. And those tests involved high-quality, non-misleading handwriting; genuine questioned documents often present deliberately disguised or distorted writing, which amplifies error.

The courtroom response is typically a two-part dodge. First, the expert says the technique is “generally accepted” in the field, satisfying the outdated Frye standard. Second, the expert asserts that his or her conclusion is “to a reasonable degree of scientific certainty,” a phrase that has no agreed scientific meaning and is purely a rhetorical prop. The jury is left with the impression of a settled science, when in reality the whole exercise is a subjective opinion dressed in technical jargon.

The Durst case will be decided by which expert the jury finds more credible, not by any reliable method. That is precisely the roll of the dice that modern evidence law claims to prevent. Handwriting analysis should be excluded from criminal trials—or at most, admitted only with a strict caveat that it is an untested art, not a science. Anything less invites wrongful convictions built on nothing more than persuasive script.

The weakness in forensic evidence is not always the science itself, but the gatekeepers who let it in. For more than two decades, the Supreme Court’s Daubert ruling has instructed federal judges to serve as active screeners, evaluating the methods behind expert testimony. Yet judges repeatedly admit techniques like handwriting analysis—the very evidence front and center in the Durst trial—without demanding the rigorous validation the law theoretically requires. The solution is not to ban entire fields, but to force judges to do their job better.

Handwriting comparison often comes into court as a “specialized knowledge” rather than scientific knowledge, a loophole that softens the judicial scrutiny. But whether you call it craft or science, the judge must still ask: what is the known error rate? What standards control the technique? Has it been tested? Many document examiners cannot answer these questions with specifics drawn from methodologically sound research. Yet judges accept generalized statements about training and experience and allow the testimony.

The fix is twofold. First, judicial education must improve. The National Judicial College and the Federal Judicial Center offer some programs on forensic evidence, but attendance is voluntary and sporadic. Every judge who presides over criminal cases should receive mandatory, regularly updated training on the scientific method and the specific limitations of each forensic discipline. Second, trial courts should be required to appoint a neutral expert in forensic science when a party challenges the admissibility of a technique like handwriting analysis. A court-appointed expert can walk the judge through the empirical literature without the filter of advocacy, making gatekeeping less of a rubber stamp.

Once admitted, testimony must be bounded. Even if an examiner may testify to similarities, the witness should be barred from using absolute language like “match” or “identification.” Instead, judges should insist on plain-English descriptions of the findings' limits and require the jury to be instructed that handwriting analysis is not infallible. That modest procedural reform would not impair the prosecution’s ability to present relevant evidence, but it would blunt the misleading aura of certainty that so often overwhelms the truth-seeking process.

Forensic science will only become dependable when it learns to speak the language of probability rather than certainty. The handwriting analysis central to the Robert Durst case is a perfect example. When two experts look at the same envelope and one declares the writing a match for Durst while the other calls it a forgery, the public sees a contradiction; a statistician sees a system that desperately needs error rates and likelihood ratios.

In every other domain of science-based risk assessment—epidemiology, aeronautical engineering, even weather forecasting—conclusions are expressed with quantified uncertainty. Why should handwriting be an exception? A competent examiner can, at best, note that the questioned writing shares particular features with the known sample, and that such features appear in, say, one in 500 samples from the general population. But that statement requires a foundation: large validation studies that measure how often examiners are correct, and how often they wrongly call a disguised writing a match or a genuine one a forgery. Those studies barely exist.

Proficiency testing is the cheapest and most direct tool we have right now. In an ideal system, every document examiner would be tested several times a year using blind samples: realistic cases prepared by a third party, unknown to the examiner, with a known correct answer. The examiner’s conclusions—and their confidence levels—would be recorded. Over time, these tests would generate individual and lab-wide error rates that could be shared with judges and juries. An examiner who knows her personal false-match rate is 3% could testify accordingly, and a jury could weigh that against other evidence.

The National Commission on Forensic Science should make probabilistic reporting mandatory for all pattern-matching disciplines, from hair to handwriting. And Congress should condition federal forensic funding on the implementation of blind proficiency testing programs. Without these steps, juries in trials like Durst’s are left to guess which guru is right, a process that has nothing to do with science and everything to do with gamblers’ luck.

The most stubborn barrier to reliable forensic science is not a lack of technique, but a lack of independence. As long as the analysts who compare handwriting, fingerprints, or ballistics are housed in police departments or answer to prosecutors’ offices, their conclusions will be subtly—or not so subtly—skewed toward conviction. The Robert Durst saga, with its parade of paid experts, only underscores the institutional rot. Real reform means removing forensic laboratories from law enforcement entirely and placing them under independent, scientifically governed agencies.

The evidence for contextual bias is overwhelming. In study after study, fingerprint examiners given the same prints reach different conclusions depending on whether they are told the suspect confessed. Document examiners reviewing a handwriting sample will be more likely to see a match if they know it comes from a suspect who has already been implicated by other evidence. That is not misconduct; it is normal human cognition. The only way to neutralize it is to ensure that the analyst is blind to irrelevant details about the case and works for an institution whose culture and career incentives do not depend on helping prosecutors win.

A model exists in the form of medical examiners’ offices, which often operate with some structural separation from the police. Forensic science writ large needs the same firewall. Each state should create a Forensic Science Commission that oversees all crime laboratories, hires and evaluates analysts, and mandates blind procedures.
