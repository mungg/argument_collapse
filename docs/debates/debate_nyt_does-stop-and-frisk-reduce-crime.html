Does ‘Stop and Frisk’ Reduce Crime?

The New York City Police Department’s policy of stopping and frisking thousands of people, mainly young black and Hispanic men, has come under increased attack. A federal judge in Manhattan granted class action status to a lawsuit over the practice and said she had troubling questions about its constitutionality, especially because in many cases there is no evidence of crime or cause for suspicion. Other judges have raised questions of its fairness. And Philadelphia has reduced its use of the tactic under a consent decree.

The tactic is definitely controversial. But does it work? Do stop-and-frisk policies lower crime, or are they counterproductive?

The honest answer is that we don't know whether stop-and-frisk, as practiced by the NYPD, reduces crime — and the evidence we do have suggests its marginal contribution is small and probably outweighed by its costs.

Defenders point to New York's remarkable two-decade crime decline as prima facie proof that aggressive proactive policing works. But the same decline has occurred in cities that never adopted anything like NYPD's stop-and-frisk regime — San Diego, Los Angeles, even Dallas — and crime in New York fell fastest in the 1990s, before the explosion of recorded stops from roughly 100,000 a year in 2002 to nearly 700,000 in 2011. If stops drove the decline, we should see crime falling fastest when stops rose fastest. We don't. Franklin Zimring's careful work attributes much of New York's success to more cops, smarter deployment, and CompStat — not to mass frisking.

The strongest direct evidence comes from Jeffrey Fagan's analyses for the Floyd litigation and Andrew Gelman's earlier work with Fagan and Kiss. They find that stops are heavily concentrated in minority neighborhoods even after controlling for precinct-level crime rates, that roughly 88 percent yield no arrest or summons, and that the weapons-recovery rate is well under 2 percent — lower than random sampling might predict. A program nominally justified by getting guns off the street is recovering remarkably few guns per stop.

Meanwhile the costs are real. Tom Tyler's research on procedural justice indicates that when young men experience policing as arbitrary and humiliating, they become less willing to cooperate with police, report crimes, or serve as witnesses — which over time can erode the very legitimacy that effective policing depends on. The class-action certification in Floyd reflects an accumulating record of stops made without the reasonable suspicion Terry requires.

A defensible policy might use Terry stops surgically, in hot spots, against specific suspect descriptions. What New York is doing instead — stopping hundreds of thousands of mostly innocent young men of color annually — looks less like crime control than like a constitutional liability searching for a justification.

The assertion that the NYPD’s stop-and-frisk program is responsible for New York’s historic crime drop collapses under scrutiny. While it is tempting to credit aggressive policing, the data available in mid-2012 tells a more complicated story in which the tactic’s contribution to public safety is marginal at best, and its corrosive effect on communities likely undermines the very trust that effective policing requires.

Proponents often point to the raw numbers: as stops skyrocketed from 97,000 in 2002 to over 685,000 in 2011, major felony crime fell by more than 30 percent. But this correlation masks a crucial timeline problem. New York City’s crime decline began in the early 1990s, well before the NYPD embraced wide-scale stop-and-frisk. Homicides peaked at 2,245 in 1990 and had already plunged to under 600 by 2002, before the exponential rise in stops. The sharpest crime drops occurred under the Dinkins and early Giuliani administrations, not during the period of mass stops. If anything, the rate of decline slowed during the peak stop years, suggesting that the additional stops added little extra downward pressure.

Even within the Bloomberg era, the neighborhoods with the highest stop rates did not experience proportionally larger reductions in crime compared to similar areas with fewer stops. And when Philadelphia scaled back its use of pedestrian stops under a consent decree, violent crime continued to fall there too, further weakening the causal claim.

The effectiveness argument also hinges on the idea that stops disarm dangerous people. Yet the NYPD’s own reports reveal an extremely low “hit rate.” In 2011, only about 1 percent of stops resulted in the seizure of a weapon; guns were recovered in just 0.15 percent of stops. The vast majority of people stopped—nearly 90 percent—were innocent of any wrongdoing, receiving neither a summons nor an arrest. It is hard to argue that such an inefficient dragnet is a precision crime-fighting tool. The resources devoted to conducting and processing nearly 700,000 yearly stops could instead be redirected toward targeted investigations of the small population driving violence.

Perhaps most damaging is the tactic’s counterproductive effect on community cooperation. A growing body of research by 2012, including work from the Vera Institute and the Center for Constitutional Rights, shows that heavy-handed street stops breed cynicism and fear, particularly among young black and Latino men. When law-abiding citizens view the police as an occupying force, they are less likely to report crimes, serve as witnesses, or share tips. This erosion of “procedural justice” can make neighborhoods less safe over the long run, as police lose the eyes and ears of the community.

New York’s crime drop is a complex phenomenon driven by factors like the end of the crack epidemic, demographic shifts, improved economic conditions, and smarter management tools such as CompStat. Stop-and-frisk, with its staggering racial disparities and negligible yield, is more of a drag on police legitimacy than an engine of safety. The question should not be whether the tactic reduces crime on the margins, but whether the profound constitutional and social costs are worth a strategy that, at best, cannot prove it works.

Defenders of New York City’s stop-and-frisk program routinely point to the city’s historically low crime rates as proof that the tactic works. But confusing correlation with causation is a dangerous basis for public policy. When we look closely at the data, the argument that stopping hundreds of thousands of citizens is the engine of our public safety rapidly falls apart.

Casting an incredibly wide net to catch a handful of weapons is not precision policing; it is a dragnet.

Furthermore, the timeline undermines the official narrative. New York City’s remarkable crime drop began in the early 1990s and had largely stabilized by the time the Bloomberg administration radically expanded stop-and-frisk. In 2003, there were just over 160,000 stops. By 2011, that number had more than quadrupled, yet crime rates did not drop by a commensurate magnitude. Other major American cities have experienced dramatic, long-term declines in violent crime without subjecting their minority populations to systemic street interrogations.

The most significant threat of the current stop-and-frisk policy, however, is that it is fundamentally counterproductive to actual crime-solving. Effective policing relies heavily on community cooperation. Detectives need witnesses to come forward, victims to report crimes, and neighborhoods to trust that officers are there to protect them. By disproportionately targeting young black and Hispanic men—many of whom report being stopped, questioned, and physically searched multiple times a year for simply walking to school or standing outside their own homes—the N.Y.P.D. is eroding that essential trust. When an entire generation of young men grows up viewing the police as a harassing force rather than a protective one, the flow of vital information stops.

We do not have to choose between constitutional rights and safe streets. Targeted policing, rooted in genuine reasonable suspicion and focused on actual high-risk individuals, is a proven law enforcement tool. But the mass, indiscriminate use of stop-and-frisk is an illusion of effective policing. It sacrifices fundamental civil liberties and community trust for a statistical drop in the bucket, jeopardizing the very public safety it claims to protect.

Stop-and-frisk can reduce crime in the limited sense that any focused police intervention can reduce crime when it is tied to particular places, times and people for articulable reasons. A patrol officer who sees a bulge, hears a credible description, or observes conduct suggesting an imminent robbery should stop the person. The Supreme Court’s Terry doctrine permits that, and cities would be foolish to abandon it.

But that is not the same as saying New York’s mass stop-and-frisk program is responsible for the city’s historic crime decline, or that its current scale is justified. The evidence for that broader claim is weak. New York’s crime drop began in the early 1990s and was already dramatic before stops reached today’s levels. Murders fell from more than 2,000 a year to far lower levels before the city was recording anything like 600,000 stops annually. In 2011, the police reported roughly 685,000 stops. Nearly nine in ten ended without an arrest or summons, and the number of guns recovered was tiny compared with the number of people stopped. If the principal goal is getting guns off the street, hundreds of stops for each gun is not a strong showing.

Supporters often answer that the true benefit is deterrence: young men do not carry guns because they fear being stopped. That is plausible in theory, but difficult to prove. Deterrence also depends on legitimacy. If large numbers of innocent people, overwhelmingly black and Hispanic, experience the police not as protection but as arbitrary humiliation, the policy may make investigations harder, reduce cooperation with witnesses, and deepen alienation in neighborhoods that most need effective policing. A tactic that produces a few weapons but teaches thousands of law-abiding teenagers that the police see them as suspects can impose hidden crime-control costs.

The better evidence from policing research points toward precision, not volume. Hot-spots policing, careful use of crime data, focused deterrence against repeat violent offenders, and problem-solving strategies have shown promise because they concentrate attention where violence is actually occurring and on those most likely to commit it. They do not require treating whole categories of young men as presumptively suspicious. Indeed, the more stops are driven by quotas, vague “furtive movements,” or presence in a high-crime area, the less they resemble policing based on reasonable suspicion and the more they resemble dragnet surveillance.

So the fair answer is: stop-and-frisk sometimes works when it is truly investigative, narrowly targeted and constitutionally grounded. But as a mass policy measured by sheer numbers of stops, it is neither proven nor wise. New York should not abolish reasonable street stops; it should abandon indiscriminate ones. Crime reduction and civil rights are not opposing goals. Policing that communities view as legitimate is more likely to prevent violence than policing that makes innocence feel irrelevant.

The evidence on stop-and-frisk's crime-reduction effectiveness is genuinely contested, and the honest answer is that we lack the precision to isolate its specific contribution. What we do know is troubling enough to demand serious scrutiny.

The NYPD's own data presents the strongest case against the practice's efficiency. Officers stopped over 600,000 people in 2011, yet weapons were recovered in barely 1.2 percent of those stops. This figure has remained consistently low even as the number of stops has grown dramatically. If stop-and-frisk were a targeted, intelligence-driven tool, we would expect far higher yields. The low hit rate suggests the tactic is generating massive numbers of unproductive contacts rather than focused interventions.

Proponents will rightly point out that New York City's dramatic crime decline coincided with the policy's expansion, and that the city's experience has been held up as a national model. But this correlation alone doesn't establish causation. Crime fell across the country during this period, driven by factors including demographic shifts, the removal of lead from gasoline, and economic changes. Moreover, stop-and-frisk was part of a broader constellation of policing strategies—broken windows enforcement, CompStat accountability, and increased officer presence—that were implemented simultaneously.

The constitutional concerns raised by Judge Shira Scheindlin deserve particular attention because they speak to unintended consequences. When law enforcement routinely contacts young men of color in their own neighborhoods, it generates resentment that can undermine the cooperative relationships police need to solve crimes and gather intelligence. Community policing principles suggest that legitimacy matters—that people who believe they're treated fairly are more likely to cooperate with investigations.

Philadelphia's experience under its consent decree is instructive: the city reduced stops substantially without seeing crime rise. This suggests alternatives exist.

I'm not prepared to conclude that stop-and-frisk has zero crime-fighting value. But given its disparate impact, its low hit rate, and the plausible mechanisms by which it might be counterproductive in certain contexts, the burden of proof lies with defenders to demonstrate that the specific tactic—not merely increased police presence—causes crime reductions that wouldn't occur through less invasive alternatives. That burden hasn't been met.

Stop and frisk, as practiced by the NYPD, is sold as the secret ingredient behind New York's astonishing crime decline. But the evidence for that claim is far weaker than its champions admit. Yes, New York's violent crime has fallen dramatically since the early 1990s. So has crime in San Diego, Los Angeles, Dallas, Boston, and most other large American cities — many of which never adopted anything resembling the NYPD's aggressive stop regime. If stop and frisk were the engine, we would expect New York's decline to dramatically outpace cities that didn't use it. It hasn't, at least not in any way researchers have been able to isolate.

What we do know is that the number of stops has exploded — from roughly 100,000 in 2002 to nearly 700,000 in 2011 — while crime has continued a downward trend that began before the explosion. The marginal stop is producing extraordinarily little: by the NYPD's own data, roughly 12 percent of stops result in an arrest or summons, and guns are recovered in less than 0.2 percent of stops. If the program were a fishing expedition for weapons, it would be one of the least efficient policing tools ever devised.

Meanwhile the costs fall almost entirely on young black and Latino men, who account for an overwhelming share of stops despite being a minority of the city's population. Jeffrey Fagan's analysis for the Center for Constitutional Rights found that a substantial portion of stops lacked the constitutionally required reasonable suspicion. That is not a small footnote; it is the legal basis for the practice. When Judge Shira Scheindlin grants class certification and flags constitutional concerns, she is responding to a documented pattern, not a hunch.

Defenders will argue deterrence: even unproductive stops, they say, raise the perceived cost of carrying a gun. Maybe. But deterrence cuts both ways. Communities that experience policing as harassment lose trust in officers, are less willing to cooperate with investigations, and less likely to report crimes. Tom Tyler's work on procedural justice has shown for years that perceived legitimacy is itself a powerful crime-control tool. Burning legitimacy to chase a marginal deterrent effect is a bad trade.

The honest answer to the debate's question is: we don't have strong evidence that stop and frisk, at the scale New York practices it, reduces crime beyond what better-targeted policing would achieve. We do have strong evidence that it imposes serious costs — constitutional, civic, and psychological — on hundreds of thousands of innocent people each year. A policy that hassles seven young men of color for every weapon it doesn't find should have to clear a high evidentiary bar. Stop and frisk does not clear it.

The debate over stop and frisk usually proceeds as if there were two crisp options: keep doing what New York is doing, or stop doing it. Both framings miss the more interesting question, which is about dosage and targeting. There is reasonably good evidence that proactive policing in micro-locations with high crime concentration — what criminologists call "hot spots" policing — reduces crime. There is essentially no evidence that scaling that approach up to 685,000 stops a year, the vast majority of innocent people, adds further crime-control value. The research literature, including the Braga meta-analyses and the Lawrence Sherman work going back to the 1995 Kansas City Gun Experiment, supports concentrated, intelligence-driven stops. It does not support dragnet stops.

Look at what NYPD data actually show. Stops have grown roughly sevenfold since 2002. Crime over that period has continued to decline at roughly the pace it had been declining beforehand. If the marginal stops added since 2002 were producing significant crime reductions, we'd expect to see an acceleration. We don't. We see diminishing returns at best — and on the cost side, escalating community resentment, hundreds of thousands of innocent young men subjected to public searches, and the constitutional problems Judge Scheindlin has flagged.

This is what a well-designed program looks like in theory: officers, equipped with current intelligence about a specific block, a specific crew, or a specific pattern, make stops grounded in articulable suspicion. They document them carefully. Supervisors review the documentation. Stops that don't pan out get the supervisor's scrutiny, not just the productive ones. Officers receive regular feedback on hit rates and on community complaints.

What New York actually appears to be doing is closer to a quota-driven model in which precinct commanders are pressured to generate stop numbers, and officers down the chain are pressured to produce them. That is how you end up with the documented pattern of stops based on "furtive movements" — a category so vague it functions as a placeholder for the officer's gut. It's also how you end up with the racial disparities that have made the program politically untenable.

So: does stop and frisk reduce crime? A focused, evidence-based version of it almost certainly does, at the margins, on top of other strategies. The expansive version New York currently practices probably produces little additional crime reduction beyond a well-targeted core, while imposing very large social and constitutional costs. The smart response to the lawsuit isn't to defend every stop or to renounce the tool entirely. It's to cut the volume dramatically, invest in supervision, and demand that every stop be justified the way the Constitution and the research literature both require.

Let's set aside, for a moment, the question of whether stop and frisk reduces crime in some measurable, aggregate sense. The deeper problem is that we are asking the wrong question. We don't usually evaluate policing tactics purely by their crime-control output. We also ask whether they comport with the Fourth Amendment, with equal protection, with the basic civic compact between police and the policed. A policy can "work" in narrow utilitarian terms and still be intolerable.

Stop and frisk, as actually administered in New York, is constitutionally suspect on its face. Terry v. Ohio authorized brief investigatory stops based on reasonable, articulable suspicion of criminal activity. It did not authorize the suspicionless stopping of hundreds of thousands of people because they happened to be young, male, black or brown, and outside in the wrong neighborhood. NYPD's own paperwork — the UF-250 stop forms — frequently cites "furtive movements" or "high-crime area" as the basis for a stop. Those are not reasonable suspicion. They are post-hoc rationalizations.

Imagine the practice applied at equivalent intensity in a wealthy, predominantly white neighborhood. Imagine 500,000 young men on the Upper East Side patted down on the sidewalk over a decade, the overwhelming majority innocent. The political reaction would be ferocious and immediate; the courts would have shut it down years ago. The practice persists because its victims are politically marginal and because the rest of the city tolerates costs it does not bear.

This matters even for the consequentialist. Studies of procedural justice, particularly Tom Tyler's, show that perceived legitimacy of police shapes whether citizens cooperate with investigations, serve as witnesses, and obey the law in the future. A whole generation of young men in Brownsville, Bed-Stuy, and East New York are being taught, by personal experience, that the police regard them as suspects by default. That lesson will be paid back in noncooperation for decades — in unsolved homicides, in witnesses who won't testify, in young people who learn to view the badge as the enemy.

Judge Scheindlin's class certification matters less as a legal milestone than as a recognition that something systemic, not episodic, is going on. The plaintiffs aren't claiming bad luck with a few rogue officers. They're claiming that the department has built a machine that produces unconstitutional stops in volume, and they have the paperwork to prove it.

Even if some version of focused stops genuinely reduces crime — and I think it probably does — the current regime sails well past what the Constitution permits and what civic legitimacy can sustain. The question isn't whether to keep stop and frisk. It's whether we are still serious about the Fourth Amendment when its protections are inconvenient to crime statistics.

I want to push back on a premise that runs through most of this debate: the idea that we can answer "does stop and frisk reduce crime?" with the data we currently have. We can't. And the confidence on both sides should be much lower than it is.

Consider the empirical landscape. New York's crime decline began around 1991, well before the explosion of stops in the 2000s. The decline tracks broader national trends that economists and criminologists have attributed variously to demographic shifts, the waning of the crack epidemic, lead abatement, mass incarceration, improved economic conditions, the legalization of abortion (controversially), and the diffusion of new policing technologies. The largest single-city decline happened in New York, but the second-largest happened in Los Angeles, which had nothing like NYPD's stop volume. Disentangling stop and frisk from the dozen other things going on at the same time is genuinely hard, and most published analyses do it badly.

Defenders point to Franklin Zimring's work showing that NYC's decline was deeper and longer than the national average, and attribute the difference to policing. Critics point to the timing — stops surged after most of the decline had already happened — and conclude that stops weren't the cause. Both are partly right. There's probably some crime-reduction signal in there, and it's probably much smaller than the NYPD's loudest advocates claim.

What about the natural experiments? The 2002–2011 increase in stops should give us something. But several analyses have noted that the rate of decline in crime didn't change much when stops surged. That's not zero evidence, but it's not nothing, either: if you sevenfold a treatment and don't see a corresponding response, the treatment effect at the margin is probably modest.

Then there's Philadelphia, which is now reducing stops under a consent decree. In a few years we'll have a useful comparison. We don't have it yet.

What we should do in the meantime is acknowledge uncertainty and let other considerations carry more of the weight. We know with high confidence that the stops impose real costs on innocent people. We know with high confidence that the racial disparities are large. We know with high confidence that the constitutional basis for many stops is dubious. We do not know with high confidence that the program produces large crime reductions at its current scale.

Under uncertainty about benefits and certainty about costs, the right move is to scale back, gather better data through pilot programs and randomized variation across precincts, and require the department to demonstrate effectiveness rather than asserting it. The burden of proof should sit with the policy, not against it.

The debate about stop and frisk has been framed almost entirely from the perspective of outside observers asking whether the numbers add up. I want to suggest that the more important perspective is that of the residents of the neighborhoods where the stops actually happen — and that their view is more complicated than either side acknowledges.

Polling and ethnographic work in places like Brownsville and the South Bronx consistently reveal two things at once. First, residents desperately want safer streets. They are not anti-police. Many have lost children or neighbors to gun violence; they want shooters off the corner; they call 911 and expect a response. Second, those same residents experience the current stop regime as humiliating, frightening, and often arbitrary. A young man can be stopped two or three times walking home from school. A father can be patted down in front of his children. The encounter is rarely explained. Apologies for being wrong are rarely offered. The same officer who is supposed to be a protector becomes, in the texture of daily life, an antagonist.

This duality is the key to the policy question. The communities most affected don't want less policing; they want different policing. They want officers who know their names, who recognize their kids, who can tell the difference between a corner crew and a teenager waiting for the bus. They want investigation when their family member is shot, and they want it to feel personal, not bureaucratic. What they get instead is a dragnet that scoops up the innocent because the innocent are easier to find than the guilty.

The NYPD's defenders argue that volume is the point — that mass stops deter weapon-carrying. Perhaps. But there is another model, sometimes called focused deterrence or "pulling levers," pioneered in Boston's Operation Ceasefire and refined by David Kennedy and others. It identifies the small number of people actually driving violence, communicates directly with them, offers services to those who want out, and promises swift, certain enforcement against those who keep shooting. It treats the community as a partner, not a suspect pool. Cities that have implemented it carefully — Boston, High Point, Cincinnati — have seen real reductions in gun violence without saturation stops.

So the answer to the debate question isn't simply yes or no. Aggressive stop and frisk likely reduces some crime, but at a price the affected communities increasingly refuse to pay, and probably less efficiently than focused alternatives. A serious policymaker would listen harder to the people inside the policy rather than the people defending or denouncing it from outside. They are telling us, if we'll hear it, that there is a better way to keep them safe.
