In response to what is being called a heroin epidemic, Massachusetts Gov. Charlie Baker has proposed legislation that would give hospitals the power to force treatment on drug addicts. Is this an effective approach to the problem?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Governor Baker's proposal—allowing hospitals to hold addicts for up to 72 hours against their will—is an understandable response to a genuine crisis. With heroin and prescription opioid overdose deaths climbing sharply in Massachusetts and across New England, doing nothing feels indefensible. But coerced treatment, especially the brief inpatient variety Baker envisions, is unlikely to deliver what its proponents hope.

The evidence on involuntary commitment for addiction is thin and mixed. Massachusetts already has Section 35, which permits family members or police to petition a court to commit someone for up to 90 days. A 2014 study by researchers at the Massachusetts Department of Public Health found that people committed under Section 35 had higher subsequent overdose mortality than those who entered treatment voluntarily—possibly because forced detoxification lowers tolerance without instilling the motivation or pharmacological support needed to stay off opioids. When a patient leaves a 72-hour hold and uses again at their old dose, the result can be fatal.

There is also a clinical reality that political rhetoric tends to obscure: the most effective treatments for opioid use disorder are medications—methadone and buprenorphine—taken over months or years. A short hospital hold that detoxes someone and discharges them without maintenance medication, recovery housing, and outpatient support is not really "treatment"; it is a pause. Compounding this, Massachusetts, like most states, has nowhere near enough licensed treatment slots, particularly for medication-assisted treatment, to absorb even the patients who currently want help.

That said, a narrowly drawn power to hold someone in immediate medical danger—say, a patient revived by naloxone in an emergency room who is about to walk back out—has some justification, much like psychiatric holds for imminent suicide risk. The key is what happens during and after those 72 hours. If the hold is used to initiate buprenorphine, arrange a warm handoff to an outpatient clinic, and engage family, it could save lives. If it is simply a detox-and-discharge, it risks making things worse.

Before expanding coercion, Massachusetts should expand capacity. Otherwise we are forcing people into a system that isn't there.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

The case for forcing drug addicts into treatment rests on a compassionate impulse—save them from overdose, restore them to health—but it collides with a grim reality: coercion is a poor therapeutic foundation, and it often does more harm than good. Addiction is a chronic, relapsing brain disease, not a moral failing that can be corrected through confinement. Research consistently shows that treatment is most effective when it is voluntary and patient-centered. The National Institute on Drug Abuse notes that outcomes are strongly linked to a person’s readiness and motivation to enter treatment; external pressure alone rarely sustains long-term recovery.

Massachusetts’ existing Section 35, which allows judges to commit people involuntarily for substance use treatment, illustrates the pitfalls. A state-funded study found that while some individuals did access care they would have otherwise refused, many were poorly matched to programs and discharged with no aftercare plan, leading to rapid relapse. Worse, forced detoxification can lower tolerance without delivering the skills and support needed for sobriety, leaving individuals at dramatically higher risk of fatal overdose immediately upon release. There is also the deep ethical concern of stripping a competent adult of bodily autonomy, particularly when the intervention is not a proven cure but a temporary reprieve.

Evidence from other nations points toward a more effective paradigm. Portugal’s 2001 decriminalization of drug possession, paired with a massive expansion of voluntary treatment, harm reduction, and social reintegration, halved problematic drug use and dramatically cut overdose deaths without resorting to forced treatment. Closer to home, medication-assisted therapies like buprenorphine and methadone, when readily accessible on demand, have a substantial evidence base for suppressing cravings and stabilizing lives. Rather than empowering hospitals to detain the addicted, Massachusetts should invest in a robust, accessible continuum of voluntary care: immediate walk-in access to medications, housing, mental health services, and peer recovery support. A public health crisis demands public health solutions, not a revival of institutionalized paternalism that too often backfires.

The devastating rise in opioid addiction and subsequent overdose deaths demands urgent action, but giving hospitals the power to force individuals into involuntary treatment is the wrong prescription. While Massachusetts Governor Charlie Baker’s proposal is born of a genuine desire to save lives, relying on coercion to treat a complex medical condition is both counterproductive and dangerous.

First, the threat of involuntary commitment could have a deadly chilling effect on emergency medical interventions. When someone overdoses, the immediate priority is survival, which often requires a bystander to call 911. If drug users and their peers know that seeking medical help will result in a mandatory 72-hour psychiatric or medical hold, they will be far less likely to call for an ambulance or visit an emergency room. In a crisis where seconds matter, fear of being locked up will drive drug use further underground, leading to more unattended overdoses and fatalities.

Second, there is little clinical evidence to suggest that forced treatment yields long-term success. Addiction is a chronic, relapsing brain disease. Recovery requires sustained, personal commitment, behavioral therapy, and often medication-assisted treatment like methadone or buprenorphine. Detoxifying an individual against their will and releasing them days later without their buy-in severely lowers their tolerance to opioids. If they relapse upon release—which is highly likely without voluntary engagement—their risk of a fatal overdose is actually heightened.

Furthermore, mandating treatment ignores the glaring reality of our current healthcare infrastructure: we already lack the capacity to treat those who are actively begging for help. Across the country, voluntary patients wait weeks or even months for a bed in a residential facility. Flooding a severely overburdened system with involuntary patients will only displace those who are ready and willing to enter recovery.

Instead of stripping individuals of their civil liberties and risking a public health backfire, our resources must be directed toward proven, evidence-based solutions. We should vastly expand funding for voluntary treatment facilities, increase the availability of medication-assisted therapies, and broaden access to the overdose-reversing drug naloxone. The opioid epidemic is a profound tragedy, but we cannot arrest or coerce our way out of a public health crisis. We must invite people into recovery, not force them.

Forcing people into treatment may sound like a compassionate answer to an emergency: parents are watching children overdose, emergency rooms are seeing the same patients again and again, and heroin deaths are rising faster than ordinary public health systems can respond. But compulsory treatment is a blunt instrument for a chronic, relapsing illness, and it is unlikely to be effective unless used very narrowly and paired with real medical care.

The central problem is that “treatment” often means little more than short-term confinement or detoxification. For opioid addiction, detox alone is not evidence-based treatment; it lowers tolerance and can leave people at greater risk of fatal overdose when they return to use. The best-supported approaches include medication-assisted treatment — methadone, buprenorphine and, for some patients, extended-release naltrexone — combined with counseling, recovery supports and help with housing, work and mental health. If hospitals are empowered to hold people but the system cannot rapidly provide these services, coercion becomes warehousing.

There are cases where temporary involuntary intervention may be justified: someone who is acutely intoxicated, suicidal, psychotic or medically unstable may lack the capacity to refuse care. Families also need a way to act when a loved one is in imminent danger. But that power should be surrounded by due process, prompt judicial review, medical standards and a clear path into continuing treatment. Addiction by itself should not erase civil liberties.

Massachusetts and other states would do better to expand treatment on demand, increase buprenorphine access, support methadone programs, distribute naloxone widely, train doctors, improve prescription monitoring and reduce the stigma that keeps people from seeking help early. Coercion may occasionally interrupt a crisis. It cannot substitute for a treatment system people can enter voluntarily, immediately and without shame.

Governor Baker's proposal deserves cautious support, but only as one tool among many. The reality on the ground in Massachusetts is grim: emergency rooms are seeing record numbers of overdose patients, many of whom are revived with naloxone, briefly stabilized, and then walked back out the door to use again within hours. Under current law, a hospital can hold someone in acute crisis for only 72 hours, and addicts in withdrawal frequently sign themselves out the moment they feel well enough to seek their next dose. Allowing hospitals to extend that hold — Baker has suggested 72 hours of involuntary commitment without a court order — recognizes a clinical truth: the window between overdose and the next use is short, and the addict's own judgment in that window is compromised by neurochemistry, not just willpower.

Massachusetts already has Section 35, which permits family members and police to petition a court to commit someone for up to 90 days. The proposal would essentially extend a similar logic to medical providers, who are arguably better positioned than judges to assess clinical need. Critics will argue, correctly, that coercion violates autonomy. But we already accept involuntary holds for people who are suicidal or psychotic; the question is whether opiate dependence, in its acute phase, produces a comparable inability to act in one's own interest. Increasingly, the medical consensus says yes.

That said, forced treatment is meaningless without somewhere to send people. Massachusetts has chronic shortages of inpatient detox beds and longer-term residential treatment. Women committed under Section 35 have been sent to MCI-Framingham, a prison, simply because no clinical bed was available — a scandal the legislature has only partially addressed. If Baker's law results in more people being held without an increase in capacity, it will collapse under its own weight or, worse, route addicts into correctional facilities under medical pretext.

The other essential ingredient is medication-assisted treatment. Forced abstinence-only programs have poor outcomes and high post-discharge overdose rates because tolerance drops during the hold. Any expanded commitment authority must come with mandated access to buprenorphine or methadone, not just a locked room and a counselor.

So: yes to the proposal, conditional on bed capacity, MAT access, and clear judicial review. Treating addiction as a medical emergency rather than a moral failing is overdue. But coercion without infrastructure is just a more expensive form of the revolving door we already have.

The instinct to force addicts into treatment is understandable, especially for families who have watched loved ones cycle through overdoses. But as policy, it is likely to fail, and it sets a dangerous precedent.

Start with the evidence. The research on involuntary or coerced treatment is, at best, mixed. A 2016 systematic review in the International Journal of Drug Policy — and earlier work from researchers like Wild and Klag — has consistently found that compulsory treatment produces outcomes no better, and sometimes worse, than voluntary treatment. People who are held against their will tend to leave as soon as legally possible, are less engaged in therapy, and in the case of opiate dependence specifically, face elevated overdose risk upon release because their tolerance has dropped. A locked door does not produce recovery; it produces a delay.

Second, consider the civil liberties question. Involuntary commitment for mental illness in the United States generally requires a finding of imminent danger to self or others, adjudicated with at least some procedural protections. Baker's proposal would lower that bar substantially, giving hospital staff broad discretion to detain a class of patients defined by their disease. This is the same population that, under a slightly different statutory regime, gets routed into the criminal justice system. The history of how we've treated addicts — disproportionately poor, disproportionately nonwhite when we're talking about heroin in cities, though the current epidemic has a different demographic face — does not inspire confidence that expanded coercive power will be used judiciously.

Third, the proposal misdiagnoses the bottleneck. The problem in Massachusetts is not that addicts refuse treatment; it is that treatment is unavailable. Wait lists for detox stretch for days. Methadone clinics are oversubscribed. Buprenorphine prescribers are capped by federal law at 100 patients each. People who want help cannot get it. Spending political capital on the power to compel treatment, rather than on building the treatment system itself, is backwards.

There are better levers. Expand naloxone distribution. Lift the buprenorphine cap. Fund warm hand-offs from emergency rooms to same-day MAT induction — a model that has shown real promise in Rhode Island and elsewhere. Decriminalize possession and redirect savings to treatment, as Portugal did fifteen years ago with striking results.

Force is the lazy answer to a hard problem. The harder answer — building a real treatment system — is also the one likely to work.

Of those three, the hospital seems to me the least bad.

Governor Baker's proposal would let doctors hold a patient for 72 hours after an overdose. Seventy-two hours. That is the time it takes to get through the worst of acute withdrawal, to begin a conversation, to arrange a warm hand-off to longer-term care. It is not internment. The framing of this debate — should we force addicts into treatment? — already concedes too much. It assumes that the central problem is the addict's refusal, and that the central solution is some form of compulsion. Both assumptions are wrong, and the Baker proposal, however well-intentioned, will deepen rather than resolve the heroin crisis.

Consider what the data actually show about this epidemic. The current wave of opiate addiction did not begin with people refusing help. It began with pharmaceutical companies aggressively marketing OxyContin and similar drugs in the late 1990s, with doctors writing prescriptions at unprecedented volumes, and with federal regulators looking the other way. When the prescription supply tightened around 2010, users transitioned to heroin, which was cheaper and more available. Anne Case and Angus Deaton's recent work documenting rising mortality among middle-aged white Americans points to a constellation of causes — economic dislocation, stagnant wages, declining communities — that addiction sits within, not above. This is not a crisis of individual willpower. It is a crisis with structural origins, and structural origins demand structural responses.

Forced treatment, by contrast, individualizes the problem. It locates the failure in the addict and the solution in the addict's compelled rehabilitation. This is satisfying politically — it lets governors look decisive — but it sidesteps every causal factor that actually matters. It will not reduce the supply of cheap heroin. It will not address the despair that drives demand. It will not increase the number of buprenorphine prescribers or methadone slots. It will not undo the marketing of opiates as low-risk analgesics.

What it will do is grow the apparatus of coercion. Once hospitals have the power to detain, the next question is how that power is exercised, who is detained more often, and what happens to those detained. The history of coercive responses to drug use in this country — from the Anslinger era through the crack panic — is not encouraging. Coercion expands; it rarely contracts.

If we want to reduce overdose deaths, the playbook is well-established and does not require new powers of detention: harm reduction, syringe exchange, widespread naloxone, low-barrier MAT, decriminalization of possession, and serious investment in mental health and economic opportunity in the hollowed-out communities where this epidemic is concentrated. Massachusetts could lead on any of these. Instead, it is debating handcuffs in hospital rooms. That is a failure of imagination.
