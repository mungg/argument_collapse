Is Yemen America’s Fight?

The United States, Saudis and Yemeni leaders have coordinated airstrikes on Al Qaeda targets in Yemen for years. But when another foe of Al Qaeda, Houthi militias, allied with Iran, swept the Yemeni government out of power, the Saudis organized a military operation against them with fellow Sunni Arab nations. The United States has backed its Saudi allies with logistical and intelligence support. But could that only make the region more unstable and cost more civilian lives?

Should the United States avoid getting involved — even indirectly — in regional military efforts to fight the Houthis in Yemen?

Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Yemen crisis exposes the incoherence at the heart of America's posture in the region, and that is precisely why Washington should keep its indirect support to the Saudi-led campaign minimal and conditional, rather than embrace it as our fight.

Consider the contradiction. In Iraq, U.S. air power is effectively flying cover for Iranian-backed Shia militias battling the Islamic State. In Yemen, we are providing targeting intelligence and logistics to a Sunni coalition bombing Iranian-backed Shia militias. Whatever else this is, it is not a strategy — it is a series of reflexes shaped by which capital is asking. The Houthis are not Hezbollah; their grievances are largely domestic, their relationship with Tehran is more opportunistic than command-and-control, and they have spent years fighting AQAP, the Al Qaeda franchise that genuinely does threaten Americans. Helping degrade the Houthis militarily risks handing AQAP — and possibly the nascent Islamic State affiliate — exactly the ungoverned space they need. The collapse of our counterterrorism partnership in Sana'a and the evacuation of U.S. personnel from al-Anad should already be sobering lessons.

The humanitarian dimension compounds the strategic one. Yemen imports roughly 90 percent of its food. A sustained air campaign and naval blockade against the poorest country in the Arab world will produce a famine the Saudis cannot fix and Americans will be blamed for enabling. The early reports of strikes on refugee camps and residential neighborhoods in Sana'a are a preview.

None of this means abandoning Riyadh. The Saudis have legitimate anxieties about a hostile militia on their southern border firing across it, and the alliance matters — especially as nuclear talks with Iran enter their decisive phase. But alliance management is not the same as endorsement. Washington should press hard for a negotiated settlement that restores a power-sharing government, condition intelligence-sharing on compliance with the laws of war, and keep its own counterterrorism operations against AQAP narrowly focused.

Yemen is a Saudi war of choice fought in an Arabian civil war we do not understand well. We should help our ally avoid losing it badly — not help them wage it.

Saudi Arabia and its coalition partners have both the capacity and the strategic interest to address the Houthi challenge. U.S. support should be conditional and limited—targeted assistance only where it demonstrably reduces civilian harm and advances verifiable security goals, not blank checks to allies whose conduct we cannot control.

Most critically, there is no clear endgame. Airpower cannot restore a legitimate government or reconstruct a shattered state. The United States should engage where its direct interests are at stake—counterterrorism operations against AQAP leadership, for instance—but should avoid becoming a party to an intra-Yemeni conflict whose outcome remains uncertain and whose costs could extend far beyond what any administration has acknowledged.

Yemen's tragedy demands caution, not commitment.

Walk Away From This One

The United States has no compelling national interest in helping Saudi Arabia bomb Yemen, and every reason to expect that involvement — even "indirect" involvement of the kind currently underway — will end badly. Washington should pull back its logistical and intelligence support and tell Riyadh that this is its war to fight or, better, to end.

Start with the basic question of who the Houthis are. They are a Zaydi Shia movement from northern Yemen with a long list of local grievances against the Sanaa government. They are not an Iranian proxy in any meaningful sense, however convenient that label is for the Saudis. Tehran provides some money and rhetoric; it does not direct Houthi operations, and the Houthis advanced into Sanaa last September on the back of an alliance with forces loyal to former president Ali Abdullah Saleh — the same Saleh the United States once worked with against Al Qaeda. The notion that we are blunting an Iranian march to the Red Sea is a Saudi talking point, not a fact.

Meanwhile, the Houthis are at war with Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, the single most dangerous Al Qaeda franchise to the American homeland. AQAP has tried repeatedly to blow up U.S.-bound airliners. By degrading the Houthis, the Saudi coalition is directly relieving pressure on the group we have been hunting from drones for a decade. That is not an alliance of convenience; it is a strategic contradiction.

Add the humanitarian dimension. Yemen is the poorest country in the Arab world, dependent on imports for roughly 90 percent of its food. A Saudi air and naval campaign that closes ports and destroys infrastructure will produce famine, displacement and exactly the kind of state collapse in which jihadist groups thrive. The civilian toll will be attributed, fairly, to American bombs and American targeting cells.

Finally, consider the precedent. We are being drawn in not because anyone has made a case to Congress or the public, but because the Saudis asked and the White House did not want to say no while nuclear talks with Iran are at a delicate stage. That is a terrible reason to enter another Middle Eastern war. If the lesson of Iraq, Libya and Syria is anything, it is that American firepower in Arab civil conflicts tends to deepen the chaos it was meant to resolve.

The Houthis are not our enemy. AQAP is. Helping the Saudis fight the wrong war, in the wrong country, against the wrong adversary is a mistake we should refuse to make.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Stand With the Saudis — Carefully, but Firmly

It has become fashionable in some quarters to argue that Yemen is "not our fight." That view misreads both the stakes and the alliance structure on which American power in the Middle East has rested for forty years. The United States is right to back the Saudi-led coalition against the Houthis, and should do so with more, not less, conviction.

The first reality is Iran. Tehran is currently the most aggressive revisionist power in the region, with proxies, militias or allied governments in Lebanon, Syria, Iraq and now Yemen. Whatever one thinks of the nuclear negotiations, allowing Iran to consolidate a fourth Arab capital — and to acquire influence over the Bab el-Mandeb, through which roughly four million barrels of oil transit daily — would be a significant strategic setback. Denying Iran that prize is a legitimate American interest, and the Saudis, Emiratis and Egyptians are doing the work for us.

Second, alliances matter. Saudi Arabia has watched the United States negotiate with its principal adversary, fail to enforce its red line in Syria, and walk away from Hosni Mubarak. The Gulf states are openly questioning whether Washington can still be relied upon. If we refuse them logistical and intelligence support in a campaign they regard as existential, we should expect them to hedge — by buying weapons elsewhere, by pursuing their own nuclear options, and by freelancing in ways far less coordinated than what is happening now. American involvement is, paradoxically, a restraint on Saudi behavior.

Third, the counterterrorism objection cuts the other way. Yes, AQAP and the Houthis are enemies. But AQAP has thrived precisely in the chaos created by Houthi expansion and the collapse of the Hadi government. A stable Yemen with a legitimate Sunni-led government in Sanaa, supported by its neighbors, is a far more durable answer to AQAP than periodic drone strikes launched from a country in civil war.

None of this means writing a blank check. The Saudi air force has limited experience with precision targeting, and the humanitarian risks are real. The United States should condition its support on credible measures to protect civilians, keep humanitarian corridors open, and pursue a political settlement that returns the Houthis to the negotiating table rather than seeking their annihilation. We should also push hard for an eventual unity government that includes Zaydi representation.

But the choice is not between involvement and clean hands. It is between shaping a campaign our allies will wage anyway and watching from the sidelines as it goes wrong. The former is the responsible course.

The Wrong Question

"Is Yemen America's fight?" is the wrong frame. Yemen is already several fights, and the United States is already involved in some of them. The honest question is which of those fights we should be in, and on what terms.

We have been at war with Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula in Yemen since at least 2009. That campaign — drones, special operations, intelligence sharing with whoever holds Sanaa — has been bipartisan, low-profile, and broadly defensible on self-defense grounds. AQAP has tried to bring down American airliners. There is a direct line from Yemen's ungoverned spaces to attempted mass-casualty attacks in the United States. Nobody serious is proposing that we abandon that fight, and we should not.

The new question is whether to enlist in a second, very different war: a Sunni Arab coalition campaign against a Zaydi Shia insurgency that happens to be Iran-friendly. Here the answer should be much more cautious. The Houthis did not attack the United States. They are, in fact, fighting AQAP on the ground. Their rise was enabled less by Iranian machinations than by the breathtaking incompetence and corruption of the Hadi government and the cynicism of Ali Abdullah Saleh. Treating them as an Iranian fifth column conflates a local power struggle with a regional cold war, and risks turning a manageable Yemeni civil conflict into a proxy bloodbath.

The Obama administration's current posture — providing logistics and intelligence to the Saudis while not flying strike missions ourselves — tries to split the difference. It buys goodwill with Riyadh at a moment when the Gulf is rattled by the Iran talks, without committing American forces to a campaign whose objectives are unclear. As tactical diplomacy, that is understandable. As strategy, it is incoherent. We are simultaneously helping degrade the most effective ground force fighting AQAP and signing on to a Saudi war plan that has no obvious endgame.

What should we do? Three things. First, make our support conditional and finite: explicit limits on duration, on targets, and on the humanitarian impact, with real consequences if those limits are violated. Second, push relentlessly for a negotiated outcome that includes the Houthis rather than seeking to expel them from Sanaa by force, which is almost certainly impossible. Third, keep our own counterterrorism mission against AQAP separable from the Saudi campaign, even if that means awkward operational compartmentalization.

Yemen is partly America's fight. Pretending it is wholly ours, or wholly not, are both forms of evasion.

A War Sold on a Slogan

Before American officials sign off on another Middle Eastern campaign, they might consider how thin the public case for this one actually is. We are told that Iran is taking over Yemen through the Houthis, that Saudi Arabia is defending the regional order, and that backing Riyadh is therefore a natural extension of longstanding U.S. policy. Almost every part of that story is exaggerated or false, and the consequences of acting as if it were true will fall, as usual, on Yemeni civilians.

The Houthis are not Hezbollah. They are a homegrown movement rooted in the Zaydi communities of Saada, with deep grievances against decades of marginalization. Iran's support has grown, but U.S. intelligence officials themselves have repeatedly said that Tehran did not order the Houthi advance on Sanaa and does not control their decision-making. To recast this as Iranian aggression is to grant the Saudis a sectarian framing that suits their domestic politics far more than it describes reality.

Saudi Arabia, meanwhile, is not a status-quo power being forced into reluctant action. It is an absolute monarchy that has spent decades exporting an intolerant strand of Sunni Islam, that crushed a peaceful uprising in Bahrain in 2011, that is currently flogging a blogger for the crime of free expression, and whose air force has, in its first week of operations in Yemen, already hit a refugee camp. Pretending that American "intelligence and logistical support" comes without moral responsibility for what those bombs do is precisely the kind of laundering that got us into trouble in Central America in the 1980s.

And then there is Yemen itself: 25 million people, the poorest country in the region, dependent on imported food and fuel, with cholera and malnutrition already endemic. A sustained air campaign and naval blockade will not produce a clean Saudi victory. It will produce hunger, displacement, and a generation of Yemenis who associate their suffering with American hardware. That is, incidentally, a recruiting bonanza for the very AQAP we claim to be fighting.

The honest answer to the question posed is yes: the United States should avoid involvement, including indirect involvement. That does not mean abandoning counterterrorism operations against AQAP, which rest on a different legal and strategic footing. It means refusing to be the quartermaster of a Saudi war whose aims are unclear, whose conduct is already troubling, and whose human costs are being borne by people who have done nothing to us. "Standing by our allies" is not a strategy. It is a slogan, and a dangerous one.

The question is not whether Yemen is “America’s fight” in the sense of a vital, direct threat. It isn’t. The United States must support the Saudi-led military coalition in Yemen, not because the Houthi rebellion directly threatens the American homeland, but because abandoning our closest Arab allies at this critical juncture would have disastrous strategic consequences for the broader Middle East.

We are currently in the final, delicate stages of negotiating a nuclear framework with Iran. These P5+1 talks have deeply unsettled our traditional Sunni partners in the Gulf, primarily Saudi Arabia, who view an unshackled, nuclear-threshold Iran as an existential threat. In recent years, Tehran has expanded its influence through proxies in Lebanon, Syria, and Iraq. Now, with the Houthis violently ousting the internationally recognized government of Abdu Rabbu Mansour Hadi in Sana, the Saudis see the Iranian noose tightening around their southern border.

If the United States were to refuse logistical and intelligence support to Riyadh’s Operation Decisive Storm, we would send a devastating signal: that Washington is willing to abandon its traditional security guarantees in a bid to appease Tehran. Such a move would likely prompt Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states to act even more aggressively and unilaterally, untethered from American restraint, or worse, to pursue their own nuclear deterrents.

Providing targeting intelligence, aerial refueling, and logistical backing is a low-risk, high-reward strategy. It requires no American boots on the ground, yet it secures vital Saudi cooperation on other pressing American priorities, including the ongoing air campaign against the Islamic State in Syria and Iraq. Furthermore, the Bab el-Mandeb strait, which borders Yemen, is a critical chokepoint for global energy markets.
