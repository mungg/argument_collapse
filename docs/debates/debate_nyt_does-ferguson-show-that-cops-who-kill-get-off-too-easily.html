Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

The Ferguson grand jury's refusal to indict Darren Wilson does point to a real structural problem, but the problem is less that officers are "let off easily" and more that the system used to evaluate police shootings is designed to defer to officers at almost every stage. That deference, taken cumulatively, produces outcomes that look like impunity even when individual decisions are defensible.

Start with the legal standard. Under Tennessee v. Garner and Graham v. Connor, an officer's use of deadly force is judged by what a reasonable officer would have perceived in the moment, not by what we know after the fact. That standard is appropriate in principle — split-second decisions shouldn't be second-guessed with 20/20 hindsight — but in practice it gives officers a doctrinal cushion no civilian defendant enjoys.

Layer onto that the institutional incumbencies. Local prosecutors depend on police cooperation for every other case they try, and they know it. When prosecutor Bob McCulloch presented essentially all the evidence to the grand jury and let Wilson testify uncrossed for four hours, he was doing something he would never do for an ordinary suspect. Grand juries indict, the saying goes, ham sandwiches; they apparently do not indict officers. The asymmetry is the story.

Then add the data gap. The FBI's 461 "justifiable homicides" is a voluntary count from a fraction of departments; the real number is unknown, as is the racial breakdown. ProPublica's 21-to-1 disparity is a striking estimate precisely because the government itself doesn't track these killings reliably. A society serious about either exonerating or charging officers would at minimum require uniform reporting.

So the answer isn't that cops face too little criticism or too much. It is that we have built a system in which the same office that works with police decides whether to prosecute them, under a legal standard tilted toward their account, with data too thin to evaluate the pattern. Ferguson didn't create that system; it exposed it.

The Ferguson grand jury decision reveals something more fundamental than whether Darren Wilson personally deserved indictment: it exposes a structural problem in how American prosecutors handle police shootings. Grand juries indict in well over 99 percent of federal cases brought before them. The old adage that a prosecutor can "indict a ham sandwich" exists for a reason. This isn't accidental. Local prosecutors depend on local police every single day to build cases, testify at trials, and provide investigative manpower. The fix is not to lower the legal standard for charging officers, which would create its own injustices. The fix is structural: independent special prosecutors, drawn from outside the jurisdiction, should handle every case involving a police killing. Several states have considered such reforms, and Wisconsin recently passed a law requiring outside investigation of officer-involved deaths. Some jurisdictions have proposed standing civilian review boards with prosecutorial authority.

We should also be honest that the substantive law gives officers tremendous latitude. Under Tennessee v. Garner and Graham v. Connor, the question isn't whether the officer was right but whether his perception of threat was "objectively reasonable" in the moment. That's a forgiving standard, and probably should be somewhat forgiving—we ask officers to make split-second decisions in dangerous situations. The question framing this debate misses the point. Ask not whether officers "get off too easily," but whether anyone is genuinely trying to hold them accountable in the first place. Indictment and conviction are downstream of investigation, and the investigation of police shootings in this country is broken at the source.

When a civilian shoots someone, detectives arrive, the shooter is detained, statements are taken under pressure, witnesses are canvassed quickly while memories are fresh, and physical evidence is collected by people who view the shooter as a suspect. When an officer shoots someone, the officer is often allowed to go home, consult with a union representative and an attorney, and give a statement days later after reviewing reports. The investigators are frequently colleagues. In many departments, officers under investigation are granted procedural rights—a "law enforcement bill of rights"—that no civilian suspect enjoys.

By the time such a case reaches a prosecutor, the investigative deck has been thoroughly stacked. The prosecutor, in turn, has his own conflicts: he works with these officers and depends on their cooperation. By the time the case reaches a grand jury, if it gets there at all, the prosecutor controls what evidence is presented and how. The grand jury system is designed to produce whatever outcome the prosecutor wants—which is why, in Ferguson, it produced exactly the outcome McCulloch seemed to want from the beginning.

The infrequency of charges, then, doesn't tell us officers are mostly innocent. It tells us we have built an architecture in which charges are nearly impossible to bring. That architecture rests on several pillars: the legal doctrine of qualified immunity, the Supreme Court's deferential "objective reasonableness" test, prosecutorial dependence on police, the secrecy of grand juries, union contracts that shield officers from immediate accountability, and the cultural deference of judges and juries to law enforcement.

Fixing this requires more than one reform. It requires special prosecutors for police shootings, mandatory body cameras, transparent investigations, revisiting qualified immunity, and renegotiating union protections that obstruct discipline. It also requires the federal government to collect real data—the FBI's 461 figure is voluntary and almost certainly an undercount, as journalists have documented. We can't fix what we refuse to measure.

So yes, cops who kill get off too easily—but the "getting off" happens long before any courtroom. It happens in the investigation, in the prosecutor's office, in the law itself. Ferguson didn't create the problem; it merely made it visible.

I support independent investigators for in-custody deaths, because even the appearance of conflict damages public trust. There are two distinct questions tangled together in this debate, and conflating them produces confusion. The first is whether the legal standards governing police use of force are appropriate. The second is whether those standards are being applied fairly. Reasonable people can answer yes to one and no to the other.

On the legal standard, I think the current rule is roughly right. Under Graham v. Connor, an officer's use of force is judged by "objective reasonableness" from the perspective of a reasonable officer at the scene. This rule recognizes that policing involves rapid decisions in dangerous environments. We don't want a standard so strict that officers hesitate to defend themselves; that costs officer lives. We don't want a standard so loose that officers can shoot anyone they claim to fear; that costs civilian lives. "Objective reasonableness" attempts to balance these, and I think it does so adequately.

On application, however, I have serious concerns. The same standard, applied to the same facts, often produces different outcomes depending on who the officer is, who the victim is, who the prosecutor is, and what jury or grand jury sits in judgment. This is not a defect of the standard; it's a defect of implementation. And the disparate outcomes in cases involving black victims suggest that implementation is systematically biased.

The remedy, then, is not to change the substantive law of self-defense but to change the procedures by which it is applied. Three reforms strike me as essential. First, independent investigation and prosecution of police shootings by entities not embedded in the same jurisdiction as the officer. Second, mandatory data collection. The fact that we don't actually know how many people police kill each year—the FBI's voluntary figures are widely acknowledged to be incomplete—is itself scandalous. Third, body cameras, which constrain both bad-faith officers and bad-faith claimants.

These are modest, technocratic reforms. They will not satisfy those who believe the entire system is rotten, nor those who believe nothing needs to change. But they would substantially improve a process that currently strains public confidence to the breaking point.

Ferguson did not show that the law is wrong. It showed that the public has lost faith in how the law is administered. That loss of faith is corrosive—to policing, to communities, to the rule of law itself. Restoring it requires acknowledging that something is broken, identifying what specifically is broken, and fixing those specific things, without burning down the whole edifice.

It's worth stepping back from the legal weeds to ask a sociological question: why are American police killing people at all, in numbers that vastly exceed those of comparable democracies?

British police, who patrol a country of 65 million, killed zero people in 2013. German police killed eight. Japanese police effectively never use lethal force. American police, in a country of 320 million, killed 461 people by the FBI's conservative voluntary count—probably closer to 1,000 by independent estimates. Even adjusting for population, American police kill at rates an order of magnitude higher than their peers in the developed world.

This isn't because American cops are uniquely vicious. It's because they operate in a uniquely armed society, are uniquely trained to expect armed resistance, and are uniquely deployed as the front line of social problems that other countries handle through mental health systems, social services, and gun control. An American officer pulling over a driver has to assume a gun could be present, because often it is. An American officer responding to a man behaving erratically on a street corner is responding to a mental health crisis that, in Sweden, would never have involved the police at all.

So when we ask whether cops who kill get off too easily, we're asking the wrong question. The right question is: why are American cops in so many lethal situations to begin with? Reduce that number, and the question of post-hoc accountability becomes much smaller.

This reframing suggests reforms that go beyond prosecutors and grand juries. Substantial gun control would reduce the threat environment officers face. Decriminalizing low-level offenses would reduce the number of police-civilian encounters that escalate. Diverting mental health and addiction calls away from armed responders would prevent confrontations that needn't be lethal. Investing in community-based public safety would reduce the demand for police in the first place.

None of this excuses individual unjustified shootings, and yes, the legal accountability structures need reform too.
