Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Gourevitch is right that the recent campus crackdowns are unprecedented in their severity and that the "right to feel safe" framing has metastasized into something genuinely dangerous to political life. But his essay underplays a tension at the heart of his own argument: the right to protest he wants to defend is not really, or not only, a right against administrators. It is a right that depends on the kind of robust civil society—labor solidarity, organized publics, mass disobedience—that a half-century of neoliberal restructuring has gutted. Defending the right by appealing to administrators to behave better, or by exhorting students to tolerate more, won't reconstitute the conditions that made tolerant campuses possible in the first place.

Consider the 1969 comparison he draws. Four thousand arrests out of millions of protesters wasn't just a kinder administrative culture; it reflected the simple fact that universities then could not credibly threaten to ruin a student's life with expulsion and visa revocation, because students had outside options, because legal exposure was lower, and because faculties had real governance power. Today's punishment regime is so disproportionate partly because it can be. The credentialing chokehold on professional life, the precarity of international students under an authoritarian executive, and the collapse of tenured faculty governance all mean that even modest sanctions function as catastrophic deterrents. Any honest defense of campus protest has to grapple with this asymmetry, not just with the doctrinal slippage from "safe" to "feeling safe."

Gourevitch's critique of the subjective-harm standard is largely correct, and his observation that the same logic gets selectively applied—Muslim and Arab students' fears ignored, elite Jewish institutional voices privileged—is devastating. But I'd push him further: the problem isn't only that "hostile environment" doctrine has been stretched. It's that the entire infrastructure of Title VI enforcement was built to address structural exclusion of historically subordinated minorities, and is now being weaponized by the executive branch to discipline anti-war speech. The lesson isn't merely to insist on objective standards; it's to recognize that civil rights law, once routed through a politicized federal apparatus and donor-captured boards, can be turned against the very constituencies it was meant to protect.

Finally, Gourevitch's call for a "culture of toleration" is necessary but insufficient. Toleration is a disposition; it doesn't organize anyone. What protected protest in earlier eras wasn't tolerance but countervailing power—unionized faculty, organized students, a press willing to dissent. Rebuilding that, not just clarifying principles, is the harder task ahead.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Alex Gourevitch’s stirring defense of an uncompromising right to protest rightly identifies the authoritarian drift of recent campus crackdowns and the poverty of rationales based on mere feelings of unease. But in his zeal to shield dissent, he overcorrects, brushing past the genuine difficulty of distinguishing harassment from protected expression—a distinction we cannot afford to abandon.

His core complaint is that university administrators treated subjective reports of feeling unsafe as conclusive evidence of a hostile environment under Title VI, collapsing the line between hurt and harm. No one disputes that mere discomfort cannot justify suppressing speech. Yet the Gaza protests were not a monolith. Alongside the vast majority of peaceful demonstrators, a visible, vocal fringe chanted slogans lionizing the October 7 massacres or calling for intifada—in contexts where those terms are understood by many Jewish and Israeli students as endorsements of violence against them. When such chants are repeated daily at a central encampment that occupies a campus’s main thoroughfare, they do more than cause hurt feelings. They can plausibly constitute severe or pervasive conduct that effectively denies equal access to a public space—the very standard Gourevitch himself says would justify intervention. The essay dismisses this by asserting that none of what he heard amounted to “imminent and likely harm directed at specific individuals,” but the legal framework for hostile environment does not demand individualized threats; systemic exclusion from a shared commons can suffice. The question is not whether speakers *intend* violence but whether the cumulative effect of their words and symbols, in their full context, communicates that certain students are not welcome.

Gourevitch’s own illustrations undercut his case. He points to the Genocide Awareness Project’s campus displays—graphic images of aborted fetuses alongside Nazi flags—as a protest long tolerated. But if a university permitted that, then surely it has created a standard: some deeply offensive speech, even when it borrows imagery of historic atrocities, is understood as political advocacy, not harassment. Yet Jewish students confronted with signs equating Zionism with Nazism or with explicit celebrations of the murder of Israelis might reasonably see a distinction: those displays were not embedded in a sustained, multi-week occupation that physically reconfigured the commons and was explicitly directed against a campus’s Jewish community. The context matters, and Gourevitch’s insistence that protesters only rarely “blocked” access elides the cumulative message that the quad—and by extension the university—was a zone where full-throated loathing of a protected group was the daily norm, with official blessing withheld.

No one who values democratic life wants to return to a regime where administrators, cringing before donors, can silence dissent at will. But a right to protest that includes the license to engage in sustained, targeted vilification of a minority group in shared spaces is not a right to protest but a right to dominate. The more defensible principle is not that protest must never be restricted, but that restrictions must be viewpoint-neutral, narrowly tailored, and enforced only when conduct genuinely crosses into harassment—not when it merely offends. The crackdowns of the past year were indeed disproportionate, punitive, and often driven by bad faith. Yet the cure is not a blanket permission for any speech short of a direct threat. It is a rebuilding of trust in procedures that can fairly adjudicate those boundaries, without assuming that every cry of pain is a demand for censorship—or that none is.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

In his provocative and impassioned essay, Alex Gourevitch articulates a defense of campus protest that is both historically resonant and deeply timely. Writing against the backdrop of the Trump administration’s aggressive, heavy-handed financial threats against universities like Columbia, Gourevitch correctly identifies a crisis in American higher education. He warns that a confluence of donor pressure, administrative cowardice, and the weaponization of civil rights law is actively dismantling the right to free expression on campus. On this foundational premise—that the university must remain a place where intensely unpopular and even offensive ideas are freely expressed—Gourevitch and I are in complete agreement.

However, in his zeal to defend the pro-Palestinian encampments and protests that have roiled campuses over the last two years, Gourevitch makes a series of categorical errors about the nature of the university, the physical reality of the encampments, and the legal and moral difference between expressing a hostile *idea* and engaging in hostile *conduct*. By romanticizing disruption and minimizing the profound difference between a public square and an academic institution, Gourevitch proposes a framework that would not save the university, but fundamentally dissolve its primary educational mission.

Gourevitch’s argument rests on a crucial redefinition of campus space. He acknowledges that campus commons are legally the private property of the university, but insists that they must be treated as "public fora" where community members can congregate to protest, even if that entails the extended occupation of space. "A commons is not ungoverned," he writes, "but it is less governed—precisely so that members of the community may give voluntary expression to their relationship to the community."

This is a fundamental misunderstanding of what a university is. A university is not a municipality. It is not a city park, a public street, or a purely civic square. A university is a purpose-built, telos-driven institution whose primary mission is the pursuit of truth through research, teaching, and structured pedagogical discourse. The physical spaces of a campus—including its quads, plazas, and lawns—are entirely subordinate to this academic mission. They exist to facilitate the life of the mind, providing spaces for students to study, converse, and transit between classes and libraries.

When Gourevitch argues for an "absolutist defense of the right to protest," including "public, disruptive acts," he collapses the distinction between the civic sphere and the academic sphere. The First Amendment appropriately protects highly disruptive protest in a public city square because the public square belongs to no single enterprise. But a campus is a curated environment. To suggest that students have an inherent right to occupy these spaces indefinitely is to argue that the political desires of a mobilized faction should supersede the foundational purpose of the institution itself.

This brings us to Gourevitch’s treatment of the encampments themselves. He writes that the protests "sometimes interfered with ordinary foot traffic," characterizing the encampments as "genuinely inconvenient, but only that." He asserts that students could still access their dorms, faculty could still go to their classrooms, and that obstacles were erected primarily "to defend and protect themselves."

This is a staggering sanitization of the spatial and physical reality of the 2024 and 2025 encampments. The encampments were not merely noisy picket lines or temporary rallies; they were physical occupations that effectively privatized shared university space. By erecting barricades, pitching tents, and in many documented cases, establishing physical checkpoints where protestors demanded to see the IDs or ascertain the ideological commitments of those trying to pass, protestors did not just use the commons—they monopolized it.

When a group of students physically cordons off a central quadrangle, declaring it a "liberated zone," they are not engaging in speech; they are asserting physical dominion over shared resources. Gourevitch defends this by pointing out that universities have historically tolerated encampments for causes ranging from the Occupy movement to anti-apartheid protests. That universities have shown leniency in the past does not establish an unfettered right to occupy space in perpetuity. The fact that an administration tolerates a rule violation out of prudence or sympathy does not mean the rule itself is illegitimate.

Furthermore, Gourevitch’s claim that disruption must be tolerated if we are to have any right to protest at all creates a false binary. He insists that if noise and disruption are punishable, "there is effectively no right to protest." But academic freedom and free speech have never precluded content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions. A university can absolutely guarantee the right of students to hold a loud, hostile, and deeply offensive rally on the quad at noon, while simultaneously maintaining a strict prohibition on pitching tents overnight, blocking walkways, or using amplified sound outside of a library during finals week. Enforcing these basic rules of shared physical space does not persecute individuals "for protesting the actions of Israel," as Gourevitch alleges; it simply enforces the baseline conditions required for a community of thousands of diverse individuals to live and work together.

Where Gourevitch's essay is most compelling, and most tragic, is in his dissection of the "hostile environment" justification. He rightly observes that university administrators, pro-Israel advocacy groups, and now the Trump administration have weaponized Title VI of the Civil Rights Act by equating subjective feelings of unsafety with an objectively hostile environment. He notes with alarm that under current pressures—including the suspension of $400 million to Columbia—administrators are treating the mere claim that a student feels harmed as incontrovertible evidence of actionable harassment.

Gourevitch is entirely correct that democracy and intellectual life demand that we distinguish between subjective discomfort and objective harassment. The testimonies he quotes from the Harvard Jewish Alumni Alliance—students feeling a "fight or flight reaction" from chanting, or being unable to focus in a library because protesters in keffiyehs are nearby—are indeed descriptions of deep psychological discomfort. But Gourevitch is right that in a free society, hearing political chants that distress you, or seeing symbols of political movements you find abhorrent, does not constitute a denial of your educational access. The right to be secure in a university does not include the right to be insulated from viewpoints or political expressions that cause you emotional pain.

If Gourevitch had stopped here, his essay would be a waterproof defense of free speech. But he sidesteps the instances where the protests moved far beyond offensive chanting and into the realm of targeted, objective harassment. While many Jewish students merely felt uncomfortable by the presence of keffiyehs or the chanting of "From the river to the sea," there were numerous documented instances across the country where Jewish students were physically blocked from pathways, spat upon, or subjected to targeted vitriol based on their presumed national origin or religion.

Title VI requires universities to prevent discrimination that is "severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive" such that it denies a student equal access to education. Gourevitch assumes that administrators used subjective feelings as the *only* metric for their crackdowns. In reality, the crackdowns—however clumsy and heavy-handed—often occurred because universities realized they had lost physical control of their campuses, creating environments where objectively discriminatory conduct (like physical blockades or the vandalism of Jewish centers) was becoming indistinguishable from the surrounding political expression. By failing to rigorously police the boundary between speech (which must be protected) and conduct (which must be regulated), the protesters provided the exact ammunition the Trump administration needed to launch its draconian intervention.

Which brings us to the profound irony of the current moment, an irony Gourevitch touches upon but does not fully metabolize. He admits that the current crackdowns are "the coercive endpoint of a way of thinking about harm and harassment that has been developing on and off campus for a long time, coming from the left as well as the right." He mentions the progressive history of demanding speech codes, trigger warnings, and the deplatforming of speakers who made marginalized students feel "unsafe."

This is the crux of the institutional failure. For the better part of a decade, elite universities actively cultivated and institutionalized the very "safetyist" rationale that Christopher Rufo and the Trump Department of Education are now ruthlessly deploying against them. Administrators expanded massive Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion bureaucracies predicated on the idea that speech is indistinguishable from violence, that impact matters more than intent, and that marginalized groups have an absolute right to dictate what constitutes harm. Universities routinely launched lengthy investigations into professors for microaggressions or controversial pedagogical choices because a student reported feeling "traumatized."

You cannot spend ten years teaching an entire generation of students and administrators that subjective emotional injury requires a coercive institutional response, only to suddenly pivot to First Amendment absolutism the moment the speech in question is directed against Israel. The reason universities capitulated so quickly to the "anti-antisemitism" advocates is because they had completely surrendered the philosophical vocabulary needed to defend free expression. When pro-Israel advocates adopted the precise language of progressive intersectionality—claiming trauma, demanding safe spaces, and pointing to the disparate impact of campus discourse on their mental health—administrators were paralyzed. They were hoisted by their own ideological petard.

Gourevitch recognizes this hypocrisy, quoting Henry Louis Gates Jr. to warn that limiting speech always backfires. Yet, his solution is to demand a "more specific and absolutist defense of the right to protest." But you cannot demand an absolutist defense of protest while ignoring the institutional architecture that makes such a defense impossible. Universities cannot successfully resist the Trump administration’s cynical weaponization of Title VI unless they are willing to dismantle the entire internal apparatus of safetyism they have built. They must return to strict institutional neutrality, as outlined in the Kalven Report, refusing to take official stances on geopolitical conflicts, and they must uniformly dismantle speech codes for *all* groups, not just when it protects pro-Palestinian activists.

Gourevitch correctly identifies the terrifying overreach of the state in the summer of 2025. The Trump administration’s decision to withhold hundreds of millions of dollars in federal research funding from Columbia and to threaten sixty other universities is an unprecedented escalation in the culture war. It is a blatant attempt to use the power of the federal purse to force private and public institutions to adopt the administration's preferred geopolitical orthodoxies. The disappearance of Mahmoud Khalil and the imposition of extreme disciplinary procedures are chilling markers of democratic backsliding.

Faced with this "hostile government takeover," Gourevitch argues that the way to fight back is to legitimize and elevate the very disruptive tactics that provoked the crisis. He writes, "rescuing the rights now being rolled back will require a range of tactics, including vigorous protest itself."

This is a disastrous miscalculation of both strategy and principle. The state’s assault on the university is predicated on the narrative that universities are chaotic, ungoverned spaces captured by radical ideologies that are incapable of protecting their own students or fulfilling their educational mandates. By insisting that universities must tolerate the indefinite occupation of their quads and the disruption of their libraries, Gourevitch is demanding that universities validate the exact caricature the right wing is using to destroy them.

To save the university from political purges and authoritarian defunding, the university must prove that it is actually a university. It must demonstrate that it is capable of self-governance. It must prove that it can foster the most vigorous, hostile, and uncomfortable debates imaginable, while simultaneously maintaining the physical and procedural order necessary for learning to take place.

If we adopt Gourevitch's standard—where the right to protest "must accommodate fervent dissent" to the point where noise makes it "harder to study and teach," and where occupying administration buildings warrants "only modest punishment"—we surrender the university's distinct character. A student studying for a medical exam, or a first-generation student trying to attend a seminar they have gone into debt to afford, has a fundamental right to access the educational resources of the institution without navigating physical blockades or enduring the deafening sound of bullhorns outside their classroom window. To dismiss these disruptions as "genuinely inconvenient, but only that" is to treat the core function of the university with contempt.

Gourevitch asks us to tolerate the intolerable. He writes, "The less we can tolerate each other’s freedom, the more we become subject to the authorities to whom we end up appealing for protection." He is right that a culture of fragility invites authoritarianism. We must demand that students tolerate hearing ideas they find abhorrent. We must demand that Jewish students tolerate the sight of Palestinian flags and the sound of anti-Zionist rhetoric, just as we must demand that Palestinian students tolerate the sight of Israeli flags and the arguments of staunch Zionists. We must definitively sever the link between "feeling unsafe" because of a political argument and "being unsafe" due to targeted harassment.

But tolerating another person's freedom of speech does not require tolerating another person's physical dominion over shared space. We can, and must, draw a bright, unyielding line between expression and occupation.

If a student group wants to stand on the library steps at noon and shout that the university is complicit in genocide, the university must protect their right to do so with every resource at its disposal, pushing back aggressively against any donor or politician who demands their expulsion. The university must tell the uncomfortable students that part of their education is learning to exist in a world of fierce disagreement.

But if those same students attempt to pitch tents on the quad, barricade the doors to the administration building, or physically harass individuals walking to class, the university must intervene immediately, decisively, and neutrally. Punishment for trespassing, vandalism, or the unauthorized occupation of space is not a violation of free speech; it is the enforcement of the spatial contract that makes free speech possible in a shared community.

Gourevitch views the administrative enforcement of time, place, and manner restrictions as "persecuting individuals for protesting." But neutral enforcement is the only shield the university has. The moment a university allows an encampment to stand because it implicitly sympathizes with the cause, or because it fears the PR backlash of clearing it, it loses all moral and legal standing to enforce its rules against anyone else. And as we have seen in 2024 and 2025, when universities selectively abandon their own rules, they open the door for bad-faith actors in the federal government to step in and enforce their own draconian agendas.

The crisis we face in July 2025 is dire. The right to dissent is under organized, well-funded assault by a political movement that has explicitly outlined a "counterrevolution blueprint" to purge higher education. Gourevitch is to be commended for sounding the alarm with such clarity and moral force. But his remedy—a romantic embrace of disruption and a dismissal of the necessary boundaries of academic space—will only accelerate the university's demise.

To defend the right to be hostile, we do not need to turn the campus into a battlefield. We need to restore the university as a sanctuary for the mind, a place where ideas can clash with absolute ferocity precisely because the physical safety and academic access of every student is unconditionally secured. We must reject the safetyism that views words as violence, but we must equally reject the radicalism that views physical disruption as an untouchable right. Only by reclaiming its unique identity as a governed space for rigorous, unobstructed inquiry can the American university survive the storm that is currently battering its gates.

Alex Gourevitch is right about the central danger: universities have treated protest against the war in Gaza less as a normal, if unruly, feature of campus life than as a security emergency. They have summoned police too quickly, punished too harshly, and allowed donors, politicians, and litigation fears to set the terms of academic government. The resulting regime is not one in which Jewish students are safer, Palestinian and Muslim students are protected, or universities are more faithful to their educational mission. It is one in which everyone learns that rights exist only until a sufficiently powerful constituency finds them intolerable.

But the case for a “right to be hostile” needs a more careful account of institutional obligation than Gourevitch gives it. The hard question is not whether students may express fierce, offensive, even morally repugnant political judgments. They must be able to. Nor is it whether subjective discomfort should be enough to shut down protest. It should not. The hard question is how a university can protect robust protest while also preserving the equal membership of students who live, study, teach, and work in the same spaces. A campus is a commons, but it is also a workplace, a residence, a classroom, and, for many students, the center of daily life. That complexity does not justify repression. It does require rules.

Gourevitch’s strongest point is his critique of the slide from safety to feeling safe. If the claim “I feel unsafe” automatically triggers discipline, then the most censorious or politically connected group wins. This is especially dangerous because all serious political protest is meant to unsettle. Antiwar protest tells supporters of war that they are complicit in killing. Labor protest tells administrators and trustees that they are exploiting workers. Anti-abortion protest tells women seeking abortions and doctors providing them that they are participating in murder. Anti-racist protest tells institutions that their ordinary procedures reproduce domination. None of these claims can be made forcefully without causing anger, shame, fear, or alienation.

It is therefore essential to distinguish offense from harassment, and fear from threat. A keffiyeh is not a threat. A chant denouncing Zionism, Israel, Hamas, the United States, or a university’s investments is not, without more, harassment. A poster that uses the word “genocide,” however bitterly contested, cannot be treated as an act of violence. If universities collapse these distinctions, they will not produce inclusion; they will produce a politics of complaint routed through administrators and lawyers.

Still, the distinction between subjective fear and objective exclusion cannot do all the work. A hostile environment is not created only by explicit individualized threats. It can also be created by repeated conduct that predictably denies people access to education on equal terms. The legal standard is demanding, as it should be: harassment must be severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. But that standard recognizes something real. A student need not wait for a punch, a death threat, or a blocked doorway before a university has any responsibility to act.

This matters because some campus conduct during the last year and a half has not fit neatly into Gourevitch’s description of noisy but essentially nonobstructive protest. Some protesters did block entrances, surround students, interrupt classes, occupy buildings, vandalize property, or refuse identification when enforcing self-created access rules. Some counterprotesters, most notoriously at UCLA, attacked encampments while authorities failed to intervene. Some slogans and signs, depending on context, moved from denunciation of a state or ideology into celebration of slaughter or intimidation of a people. These incidents did not justify mass arrests, blanket suspensions, or the political weaponization of Title VI. But they did require universities to do what many conspicuously failed to do: investigate facts, identify individuals, and apply narrow, viewpoint-neutral sanctions.

The danger in Gourevitch’s formulation is that “protest” can become too capacious a category. Protesters often break rules for conscientious reasons, and institutions should treat such rule-breaking differently from ordinary misconduct. But civil disobedience has never meant immunity from proportionate consequences. The moral seriousness of a protest may mitigate punishment; it does not erase the interests of others in accessing libraries, classrooms, dormitories, hospitals, laboratories, or graduation ceremonies. A university that cannot maintain those functions has not protected a commons. It has ceded it.

That does not mean encampments are inherently illegitimate. They have a long and honorable history, and the mere fact that they are continuous, visible, and inconvenient is not a reason to destroy them. Universities should be far more tolerant of sustained protest than they have been. But duration and control matter. A daytime rally on a quad, a weeklong vigil, or even a tent encampment that leaves pathways open and does not monopolize essential space is one thing. An indefinite occupation that claims authority to decide who may enter, what symbols may be displayed, or which university activities may continue is another. The first tests whether public space exists. The second may begin to replace a public space with factional control.

Nor should we ignore the special responsibilities universities have toward students who are subject to discrimination. Gourevitch is right that “listen to the oppressed” cannot mean “delegate coercive authority to whoever claims injury.” Groups are internally diverse; elites often speak in the name of vulnerable people; and concepts such as antisemitism, racism, Islamophobia, and anti-Palestinian racism are themselves contested. Yet universities should listen seriously to students who report that they are being targeted because they are Jewish, Israeli, Palestinian, Muslim, Arab, Black, Asian, queer, or anything else. The answer to weaponized vulnerability is not indifference to vulnerability. It is fair process, public standards, and equal concern.

Such standards are not mysterious. Universities should presumptively protect political expression, including expression that is hostile, uncivil, accusatory, and offensive. They should regulate time, place, and manner only when rules are clear, content-neutral, and necessary to preserve access to core functions. They should punish violence, true threats, vandalism, targeted harassment, and material obstruction, but only after individualized findings. They should not outsource discipline to police except in genuine emergencies. They should not treat masks, tents, chants, or controversial slogans as presumptive evidence of danger. And they should disclose enough about their reasoning that students and faculty can tell whether rules are being applied evenly.

The deepest issue is governance. Universities are asking students to trust administrators who have given them little reason to do so. A president who calls police on peaceful protesters, rewrites rules under congressional pressure, or disciplines students to appease donors cannot credibly claim to be neutrally balancing rights. If campuses are to sustain both protest and equal membership, faculty, students, and staff need a real role in making and reviewing protest rules. Otherwise “safety” will remain a language through which trustees, donors, politicians, and risk managers govern from above.

So yes: universities need a stronger right to protest, including a right to be harsh, accusatory, and disruptive. But they also need a clearer account of the commons as shared rather than captured space. The alternative to repression is not administrative paralysis. It is disciplined toleration: broad protection for expression, narrow punishment for misconduct, and a refusal to confuse either discomfort with danger or protest with unlimited entitlement. That is the only settlement likely to protect Jews, Palestinians, Muslims, Israelis, dissidents, conservatives, radicals, and everyone else when the authorities are not on their side.

**The Property Question Is the Whole Question**

Of the three "bad justifications" for the campus crackdowns that Gourevitch identifies—disruption, trespass, and hostile environment—he gives the second the least attention. This is a mistake, because the trespass argument is the one with the most legal teeth, the deepest historical roots, and the most consequential implications for how we should think about universities going forward.

Gourevitch acknowledges that universities formally own their campuses and that this ownership underwrites academic freedom (universities must be able to decide what happens on their grounds, free of state interference). But he then argues that university commons function as quasi-public fora and that administrators "deeply betray" the university's nonprofit mission when they treat such spaces as ordinary private property. This is an attractive argument, but it papers over a tension that the rest of his essay never resolves.

If university commons are public fora, then the First Amendment applies and the state, not the university, sets the rules. The university cannot ban a speaker because it disagrees with the speaker; cannot impose viewpoint-discriminatory time-place-manner restrictions; cannot expel protesters for content. This is roughly the regime governing public universities, and it has worked reasonably well, although not without controversy.

But if university commons are public fora, the university also forfeits much of its claim to institutional autonomy in governing campus life. It cannot, for instance, ban a Klan rally or a Hamas-celebration teach-in or a Christian nationalist street preacher from setting up shop on the quad. The same doctrine that protects the Gaza encampments also protects the white supremacists. Gourevitch seems to accept this implication, citing Henry Louis Gates on the perils of turning bigots into First Amendment martyrs, and noting that "whatever right protesters claim for themselves is a right they are also claiming for their enemies." Good. Then he should also accept that universities, in this model, are essentially small cities—open infrastructure rather than mission-driven institutions.

This is, I think, where the deepest disagreement lies. The traditional case for university autonomy is that universities are mission-specific institutions devoted to research and teaching, and that the conditions of serious inquiry sometimes require restrictions that would be intolerable in a town square. A library is not a public forum. A seminar room is not a public forum. A classroom is not a public forum. Universities have always reserved the right to say: you are not welcome here unless you are doing the thing we exist to do. That is what makes them universities rather than convention centers.

The encampments tested this principle at its weakest point, the quad—a space that is neither classroom nor town square but ambiguous between them. Gourevitch's solution is to treat it as a town square, governed by something like First Amendment principles. The administrators' (panicked, often hypocritical) response was to treat it as private property to be managed at administrative discretion. There is a third position, which Gourevitch never quite articulates: that the quad is part of the educational mission, and that protest on the quad is legitimate insofar as it contributes to that mission—to the collective work of inquiry, debate, and learning. This is a higher standard than mere viewpoint neutrality, and it might rule out some protests (those that primarily aim to intimidate, or that hijack the space for non-educational purposes) while protecting others.

The mission-based view has costs, of course. It requires someone to judge what counts as educational, and that someone will inevitably be biased. But the alternative Gourevitch proposes—a "less governed" commons where almost anything goes provided it does not pose imminent physical harm—has costs too. It transforms universities into shouting matches and concedes the field of inquiry to whoever can shout loudest. The libraries and laboratories will continue to function, but the cultural center of campus life will be the quad-as-rally, which is no one's idea of a good education.

What makes the trespass question central, then, is that it forces us to ask what universities are for. Gourevitch's answer—communities where people "work, play, study, sleep, argue, fall in love, pray, compete, credentialize, and develop themselves"—is sociologically accurate but normatively thin. It describes a residential college as a kind of small town with classrooms attached. If that is what universities are, then yes, they should be governed like small towns, with broad speech rights and modest restrictions. But if universities are something more specific than that—institutions dedicated to a particular kind of intellectual work—then their property rules should reflect that specificity, and protest will sometimes be in tension with mission in ways that property rights help adjudicate.

I lean toward the second view, which is why I find Gourevitch's argument incomplete. The right to protest is real, but it is not the only thing universities owe their members or themselves.

**Against the Right-to-Protest Frame**

I want to make an argument that, I suspect, will be unpopular with most readers of Gourevitch's essay and with Gourevitch himself: the entire "right to protest" frame, as deployed in this debate, obscures more than it reveals, and the campus crisis it is meant to address will not be resolved at the level of rights at all.

The Trump administration's deployment of antidiscrimination law as a weapon against universities is a transparent abuse, and the universities that have folded under pressure deserve every bit of contempt they receive. The Khalil case is a constitutional disgrace. The disparity between the punishment of anti-war protesters and the impunity of their attackers (most spectacularly at UCLA) demonstrates that we are not living under viewpoint-neutral rules. None of this is in dispute, at least not from me.

What I dispute is the frame in which Gourevitch's essay places these facts. The frame is: there is a right to protest, this right has been violated, the violation must be reversed. The frame's grammar is liberal-individualist; its hero is the individual protester confronting an overbearing institution; its remedy is procedural (better rules, more proportionate discipline, clearer standards). And the frame's promise is that, if we can just get the rules right, the underlying conflict will become manageable.

I do not believe this. The conflict on campus is not, fundamentally, about whether protesters have rights. It is about what universities are for and to whom they belong. The Gaza protesters were not just expressing views; they were trying to force their institutions to stop being complicit in mass killing. The donors, trustees, and federal officials cracking down on them are not just enforcing rules; they are reasserting that universities exist to serve their preferences, not the preferences of students and faculty. This is a political conflict over institutional control, dressed up as a legal debate about speech and discipline.

When you frame a political conflict as a rights conflict, several things happen, none of them good for the weaker party.

First, you concede the terrain of legitimacy to the institution. The very act of demanding that the institution recognize your right is the act of accepting its authority to grant or deny rights. The South African divestment movement of the 1980s did not primarily win through arguments about the right to protest. It won through years of disruptive, sometimes unlawful collective action that eventually made the cost of refusing divestment higher than the cost of conceding it. The Black liberation movement did not win the Civil Rights Act by establishing its right to peaceful assembly; it won by making segregation ungovernable.

Second, you displace organizational power-building with juridical argument. Once the question becomes "what are our rights and how should they be adjudicated?" the actors are no longer collectives but lawyers, plaintiffs, and amici. The relevant venues become courtrooms and editorial boards. The relevant skills become litigation and op-ed writing. The protest movement that built the encampments was, at its best, a genuine collective political formation; the post-encampment phase, dominated by disciplinary hearings and federal investigations, has dispersed that formation into atomized individuals defending themselves.

Third, you create a discursive trap where every defense of protest must be coupled with a denunciation of "bad" protest. Gourevitch's essay performs this ritual carefully: yes, some statements were genuinely antisemitic, yes, some celebrations of violence were repugnant, yes, the universities had a legitimate interest in preserving order. These concessions are not strategically wrong. But they reflect a defensive posture that the original protesters did not occupy. The protesters were not asking permission. They were trying to make a divestment movement that universities could not ignore.

Fourth, and most importantly, the rights frame mistakes the audience. Gourevitch's essay is addressed, ultimately, to administrators, faculty colleagues, and liberal opinion. It is an argument for restraint, proportionality, and principle.
