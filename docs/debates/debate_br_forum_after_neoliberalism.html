Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written something both useful and revealing. Useful, because their Economics for Inclusive Prosperity briefs concretize an emerging center-left policy program at a moment when the U.S. Democratic Party is being pulled, productively, leftward — Elizabeth Warren's wealth tax proposal (built on Zucman's own work with Saez), the Sanders Medicare-for-All bill, Ocasio-Cortez's just-introduced Green New Deal resolution. Revealing, because the essay is in part an act of professional self-defense, and the shape of that defense tells us something about where mainstream economics still resists reckoning with its complicity in the order it now wants to revise.

The defense runs roughly like this: neoliberalism is not economics but its "primitive, simplistic perversion"; the discipline today is empirical, pluralistic, attentive to market failures, and equipped with tools for designing better institutions. The textbook caricature — competitive equilibrium, "markets always work" — is the property of Econ 101 and conservative think tanks, not of the working researcher. Therefore, do not throw the baby out with the bathwater: economists have ideas, and you need them.

Much of this is right. Anyone who has watched the credibility revolution in applied microeconomics, the post-2008 rethinking of macroprudential regulation, the rise of behavioral and historical work, the granular Chetty-style mobility research, or the Card-Krueger-to-Dube reassessment of minimum wages, knows that the discipline has moved. The Diaz-Alejandro line the authors quote is more true than ever: economic theory is now a near-infinite menu, and the empirical literature can sustain almost any ideologically inflected reading. That is precisely why "what does economics say?" is increasingly a question about which economists you ask, and about which findings get picked up by which institutions — that is, a question about politics and sociology, not theory.

And here is where the lead essay is too easy on itself. The claim that neoliberalism is a "perversion" of economics rather than its mainstream expression treats the relationship between disciplinary knowledge and political ideology as one of misunderstanding. But the historical record — readable in Burgin, Mirowski, Slobodian, and Stedman Jones — is that the Mont Pelerin project knew exactly what it was doing, and that its consolidation in U.S. policy circles from the 1970s onward was not a misreading of "true" economics so much as a successful colonization of the discipline's prestige. When the IMF imposed structural adjustment, when Larry Summers signed off on derivatives deregulation, when graduate trade economists asserted that NAFTA's losers could in principle be compensated and then shrugged when they weren't — these were not low-status cranks or Heritage Foundation hacks. They were card-carrying members of the profession in good standing, deploying its tools and authority. The discipline's mainstream cannot simultaneously claim credit for the empirical sophistication of 2019 and disown the policy authority it exercised in 1995.

This is not a gotcha. It is a question about the present. If neoliberalism's grip on policy was made possible in part by features of how economics is done — methodological individualism, a strong prior toward Pareto-style analysis, the habit of holding politics fixed while optimizing within it, the reflex to "take the side of markets because no one else will" — then the corrective is not just better proposals. It is institutional and pedagogical change inside the profession. The authors acknowledge "bad habits" but treat them as foibles around the edges of a fundamentally healthy science. I am less sure. The CORE curriculum project, heterodox programs, and feminist and stratification economists have for years been making more thoroughgoing critiques of how the discipline trains its young, what it counts as rigorous, and which questions get top-five publications. Their absence from this lineup is conspicuous — all the authors of the briefs are, by their own account, "tenured academic economists, working in broadly mainstream subfields." That is a fine bench, but it is not the whole problem.

A related worry concerns the rhetorical center of gravity in the proposals: the "free lunch." The authors emphasize that we are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, so we can have more fairness without sacrificing growth — perhaps even gain on both. This is empirically defensible in many of the cases they cite (monopsony in labor markets, financial-stability gains from higher capital requirements, the deadweight loss of tax competition). And it is rhetorically attractive precisely because it sidesteps the harder distributional fight. But the technocratic move of dissolving political conflict into Pareto improvements is itself a neoliberal habit. Some of what an inclusive economy requires — redistributing wealth, breaking up incumbent firms, reallocating property rights — will not be a free lunch for the losers, who are often powerful and well-organized. The political-economy challenge is then not "how do we identify the free lunches?" but "how do we win the fights that aren't free lunches?" Naidu's union piece and Kaplan's political-economy proposal point in this direction; the framing of the introduction sometimes pulls the other way.

Two more lacunae are worth naming. First, on macroeconomics: the briefs treat capital markets and inequality and financial regulation, which is welcome, but the deeper post-2008 macro debate — about secular stagnation, the appropriate size and composition of public balance sheets, the relationship between fiscal policy and full employment, the conditions under which deficits matter — is barely visible. With unemployment under 4 percent and inflation still subdued, with MMT-flavored arguments forcing their way into mainstream commentary via Stephanie Kelton and the Green New Deal, and with Olivier Blanchard's recent AEA address pushing back on debt panic, the EfIP project's relative silence on aggregate demand and the public sector's macro role is a missed opportunity. "Inclusive prosperity" is much easier to deliver in a hot labor market.

Second, on climate and race. The authors gesture at climate (carbon pricing) and mention persistent racial disparities in the opening, but neither becomes structural to the program. Carbon pricing, as economists have long recommended, is necessary and politically inert; what is now needed is industrial policy, public investment, and distributional design — the substance of the Green New Deal conversation. And on race, the U.S. wealth gap, mass incarceration, and labor market discrimination are not addressable by minimum-wage policy and wage boards alone. Darrick Hamilton, William Darity, and colleagues have been making the case for baby bonds and reparative policy; that intellectual tradition belongs in any serious economics of inclusive prosperity.

None of this is to dismiss the EfIP project. Better to have a group of mainstream economists making this case than not, and several of the proposals — Zucman's formulary apportionment, Admati on bank capital, Dube on wage boards, Rodrik on social safeguards in trade — are concrete, implementable, and overdue. The authors are also right that the discipline contains the resources to do much more than apologize for incumbents. But the deeper task they invite — building a post-neoliberal economic policy paradigm — will require both more humility about how the profession got us here and more political imagination about how to get us out. Tools are not a program. Whose tools, in whose hands, on whose behalf — those are the questions that come next.

The authors of “Economics After Neoliberalism” offer a compelling vision of a discipline that has moved well beyond market fundamentalism and stands ready to supply the policy tools for a more inclusive prosperity. Their diagnosis is welcome, and the sheer range of ideas assembled under the EfIP banner—from wage boards to social safeguards in trade—demonstrates that economists are indeed generating proposals that take inequality and power seriously. Yet the essay invites a deeper question that its authors, for all their candor about the profession’s “bad habits,” never quite settle: if contemporary economics is so rich in resources for combating inequality, why has the neoliberal policy paradigm proved so durable, and why should we now trust economists to lead the way out of it?

The authors insist that neoliberalism is not the consistent application of modern economics but its “primitive, simplistic perversion.” This is a familiar move. It draws a sharp line between the profession’s sophisticated internal debates and the slogans deployed in policy circles. But it underestimates how much the structure of the discipline itself—not just its public-facing caricatures—has contributed to the intellectual climate that sustained deregulation, financialization, and hyper-globalization. The essay notes that the competitive equilibrium model is dominant only in introductory courses, that thoughtful economists quickly move beyond it. But those introductory courses shape the worldview of countless future policymakers, lawyers, and business leaders. If the first and often last impression students get of economics is that markets are efficient, and if the discipline’s cultural prestige is built on the very mathematical formalism that gives “markets work” arguments their apparent rigor, then the damage is not merely a matter of poor public relations. The authors themselves acknowledge that many economists share a bias toward market-based solutions and that first-best reasoning has justified policy excesses. That bias is not a superficial add-on; it is cultivated by a curriculum and incentive system that rewards internal technical elegance over institutional and political context.

Moreover, the empirical revolution that the authors celebrate—the shift toward applied work and credible causal identification—is a double-edged sword. Yes, it has yielded findings that complicate market idolatry: minimum wages do not kill jobs, trade shocks devastate communities, financial liberalization breeds crises. But empirical economics is rarely a neutral arbiter. For every study showing minimal employment effects of a higher minimum wage, there has been intense methodological debate. The authors cite work by Arindrajit Dube on wage boards, yet the broader minimum-wage literature in 2019 remains contested terrain, with reasonable economists reaching different conclusions. The very model of the policy “free lunch”—the idea that we can push toward justice without sacrificing efficiency—depends on a specific reading of often fragile empirical results. If the evidence were as unambiguously progressive as the essay implies, one wonders why the policy world has not already been reshaped by it. The answer, of course, is that technical debates are easily weaponized, and the authority of “sound economics” is claimed by all sides. When the authors assert that neoliberal policies are “supported neither by sound economics nor by good evidence,” they risk simply defining soundness in terms of their own preferred conclusions, a move that can further erode public trust rather than rebuild it.

This points to a deeper tension. The EfIP project is presented as a set of policy ideas to be plugged into the existing machinery of democratic deliberation. But the essay’s own political economy analysis, drawing on Ethan Kaplan’s work, shows that the machinery is broken: money distorts representation, turnout is stratified by class, and the policy process is captured by special interests. If the deck is so stacked, then the road from a clever, evidence-based proposal to enacted legislation is all but impassable without a prior shift in political power. The authors propose to counter asymmetries of power with smarter policies—unions and wage boards to rein in monopsony, restrictions on campaign contributions to increase accountability. But these very policies must first be adopted by the same political system that the power asymmetries have compromised. The circularity is glaring. The essay implicitly assumes that good ideas, once demonstrated by economists, will muster the political coalition needed to implement them. Yet that assumption has repeatedly failed under neoliberalism, not for lack of ingenious policy blueprints but because the balance of organized interests made such blueprints politically nonviable. A genuine break with market fundamentalism might require a more confrontational politics than the essay’s technocratic framing allows—a politics that challenges the legitimacy of concentrated wealth and corporate power, not merely one that designs better tax formulas or wage-setting procedures.

The authors are right that economists have an indispensable role in laying out trade-offs and highlighting institutional alternatives. But their essay often slides into a more expansive claim: that economics, properly applied, provides the intellectual architecture for inclusive prosperity. This too easily equips the discipline with a primacy it has not earned. The design of institutions is irreducibly normative, involving choices about distributional weights and democratic procedures that economics does not supply. In a second-best world “rife with market imperfections,” where power matters enormously, as the authors concede, the most urgent task is not to devise the optimal carbon price or contingent credit contract but to restructure the political economy that decides which expertise counts and whose preferences are heard. That task lies squarely in the domain of democratic mobilization and institutional reform, and economists who wish to contribute must be far more open than the essay suggests about the limits of their own analytical tool kit. They must join a broader conversation in which economic expertise is one voice among many, not the arbiter of what counts as sound policy. Until then, the suspicion that economics is just another name for neoliberalism will persist, no matter how many creative policy briefs the profession produces.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

The launch of the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network, as outlined by Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman, represents a vital and overdue intervention in our public discourse. The authors accurately diagnose the cascading crises of our time: historic levels of inequality, wage stagnation, declining life expectancy, and a nativist political backlash fueled by deep economic insecurity. More importantly, they offer a compelling vision for how the discipline of economics can be leveraged to solve these crises rather than exacerbate them. By arguing that contemporary economics is rife with the tools necessary to dismantle the inequities of the current era, they provide a much-needed bridge between academic rigor and progressive policy.

However, in their eagerness to rehabilitate the economics profession and marshal its tools for a post-neoliberal era, the authors let the discipline off the hook a bit too easily. To construct a genuinely inclusive prosperity, we must first be honest about how we arrived at our current gilded age.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman argue that neoliberalism—with its attendant deregulation, financialization, and hyper-globalization—is not the logical conclusion of modern economics, but rather its “primitive, simplistic perversion.” They lay the blame for this perversion primarily at the feet of conservative foundations, think tanks, and rent-seeking special interests who weaponized a cartoonish version of economics to justify their preferred policies.

This is a comforting narrative for economists, but it is historically incomplete. The architects of the neoliberal order were not merely conservative ideologues operating on the fringes of the discipline; they were often the most celebrated and highly credentialed members of the economics profession itself. From the rational expectations revolution that justified a retreat from macroeconomic management, to the law and economics movement that systematically dismantled antitrust enforcement, to the consensus models of international trade that dismissed the localized devastation of offshoring, mainstream economics actively built the intellectual scaffolding of the last forty years. Policy failures like the repeal of Glass-Steagall, the aggressive push for capital account liberalization in the developing world, and the decades-long insistence that labor unions were mere monopolistic distortions were not forced upon economists by politicians. They were championed by the commanding heights of the academy.

By framing neoliberalism as a mere misunderstanding or hijacking of economics, the authors obscure the ways in which the discipline’s core methodologies inherently bias its practitioners toward certain worldviews. The authors correctly point out that modern economics is highly empirical, and that any bright graduate student can tweak assumptions to yield a variety of policy recommendations. But this defense ignores the immense normative power of the baseline.

In economics, the baseline remains the perfectly competitive market equilibrium. As the authors admit, this is the dominant framework in introductory courses. Even when economists advance to more sophisticated models that account for "frictions," they are still beginning from a paradigm where the unfettered market is the natural, "first-best" state of the world, and government intervention must bear the burden of proof to justify correcting a specific "market failure." When society is viewed through a lens where markets are natural and interventions are artificial, it is unsurprising that generations of students and policymakers have absorbed a default hostility toward the regulatory state.

This is why, despite the brilliance of the EfIP policy briefs, the continued reliance on the language of "market failures" to explain our current dysfunction feels insufficient. The crises we face today are not merely the result of asymmetric information, externalities, or imperfect competition. They are the result of deliberate distributions of power and wealth.

To their immense credit, the authors recognize this. They state explicitly that "power matters enormously in shaping market outcomes," and they highlight power asymmetries between labor and capital, between multinational corporations and sovereign states, and between the wealthy and the disenfranchised. The proposals they cite reflect a genuine desire to rebalance these scales. Arindrajit Dube’s proposal for sectoral wage boards directly tackles the systemic monopsony power of employers. Gabriel Zucman’s ingenious plan for apportioning the global profits of multinationals based on sales offers a tangible mechanism to break the blackmail of international tax competition. Dani Rodrik’s call for "social safeguards" in trade agreements elegantly subordinates textbook efficiency to the preservation of the domestic social contract.

Yet, a tension remains between the profound political shifts these policies require and the technocratic language often used to describe them. Consider the essay’s discussion of organized labor. The authors suggest that mechanism design, behavioral economics, and experiments can be mobilized to "ease the pervasive collective action problem facing unions." While this is undoubtedly true in a technical sense, the decline of the U.S. labor movement was not primarily a failure of mechanism design. It was the result of a coordinated, decades-long political and legal assault by corporate interests. Reviving labor power will require fierce political struggle, mass organizing, and legislative warfare. Economics can offer blueprints for what an optimal union structure might look like, but it struggles to conceptualize the raw, unquantifiable social friction required to build it.

This highlights the ultimate limitation of relying on economics to save us from neoliberalism: economics is fundamentally a science of optimization, but politics is a clash of values and power. The EfIP network’s proposals demonstrate that economics can design fairer, more inclusive institutions. But as the authors note, policies often "break on the rock of government capture by special interests." Ethan Kaplan’s work on democratic political economy is an excellent inclusion to address this, but it underscores a daunting reality. If our political system is already captured by the winners of the neoliberal era, then the most beautifully designed, empirically sound policy proposals will remain locked in academic journals.

Writing in 2019, as we stare down the barrel of an escalating climate crisis and a deeply fractured democracy, the necessity of a new economic paradigm cannot be overstated. The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity project proves that the discipline contains the analytical firepower to help chart a new course. The empirical turn in economics has successfully dismantled the theoretical idols of market fundamentalism, proving that minimum wages do not inherently destroy jobs and that financial liberalization does not automatically generate growth.

However, moving beyond neoliberalism will require the profession to do more than produce better empirical studies and more creative models of market failure. It will require economists to step outside the safety of methodological individualism and engage deeply with history, sociology, and political science—not just as sources of variable data, but as equal partners in understanding human society. It will require abandoning the pretense of the detached technocrat and acknowledging that economic policy is always, inevitably, an exercise in moral philosophy and political power. The authors of this lead essay have bravely taken the first step. The challenge now is for the rest of the discipline to follow them out of the seminar room and into the messy, contested reality of democratic life.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right about two crucial things. First, neoliberalism should not be confused with economics as such. The discipline has always contained more than one politics, and its best recent work has complicated, not vindicated, market fundamentalism. Second, the country’s most urgent problems—stagnant wages, racial wealth gaps, insecure work, climate change, financial fragility, declining life expectancy—cannot be solved without careful attention to incentives, institutions, empirical evidence, and tradeoffs. A politics of inclusive prosperity that refuses economics altogether will be weaker for it.

But the authors’ defense of economics is also a little too easy. It is not enough to say that neoliberalism was a “primitive, simplistic perversion” of modern economics. Perversions do not become governing common sense by accident. For decades, highly credentialed economists helped provide neoliberalism with its authority, vocabulary, and prestige. They did not merely fail to correct politicians who misused their ideas. They often supplied the models, metaphors, and policy rankings that made deregulation, tax cutting, privatization, trade liberalization, and austerity appear not just contestable, but scientific.

This matters because the public distrust of economics is not based only on misunderstanding. It is based on experience. Many people were told that trade agreements would raise overall welfare and that the losers could be compensated later. The first part was emphasized; the second was deferred or ignored. Workers were told that unions were labor-market distortions, that minimum wages priced the least skilled out of jobs, and that financial innovation allocated risk efficiently. Then came deindustrialization in many regions, the weakening of organized labor, a financial crisis caused in part by opaque and overleveraged institutions, and a recovery in which many gains accrued to asset holders. It is understandable that citizens suspect the discipline’s caveats arrive after the damage is done.

The lead essay recognizes some of this, especially the tendency of economists to reason in partial-equilibrium terms and then leave institutional repair to someone else. But that habit is not a minor professional tic. It is one of the main ways economics becomes political while pretending not to be. To say “free trade increases aggregate income” while treating compensation as a separate administrative detail is already to make a political judgment about whose losses count, when they count, and how credible future redistribution is likely to be. To say “carbon pricing is the efficient climate policy” while paying insufficient attention to regional dependence on fossil-fuel employment, distrust of government, and the political power of carbon-intensive industries is to risk designing a beautiful policy with no constituency.

The authors’ emphasis on second-best reasoning is therefore welcome. Yet the second-best world is not only a world of market failures. It is a world of state weakness, racial hierarchy, organized business power, constitutional veto points, and deep social distrust. Economists have become much better at estimating causal effects under specific conditions. They are less consistently good at explaining whether a policy can survive the institutions that must enact it. Evidence that a wage board could reduce inequality is important. So is the question of how wage boards would be staffed, how they would avoid capture, how they would interact with immigration status and fissured workplaces, and whether courts would tolerate them. These are not objections to the policy. They are part of the policy.

For that reason, “economics after neoliberalism” must be more than better economics. It must be more democratic economics. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman rightly stress that economics can clarify tradeoffs but cannot provide distributional weights. That concession should be pushed further. If the weights come from democratic politics, then economists should be careful not to smuggle their own weights back in under the language of efficiency. Cost-benefit analysis, for example, can illuminate choices, but it can also obscure moral judgments by translating everything into willingness to pay, a measure already shaped by unequal income. A poor person’s need and a rich person’s preference are not morally equivalent simply because the latter commands more dollars.

The same point applies to “inclusive prosperity.” The phrase is attractive, but it risks treating inclusion as a distributive add-on to an otherwise familiar growth project. Many citizens want higher wages and better public services, but they also want dignity, voice, stability, and control over the conditions of their lives. A laid-off worker does not experience job loss only as a decline in lifetime consumption. A community that loses an industry loses institutions, identity, tax capacity, and intergenerational expectation. These losses are hard to capture in the standard language of welfare, but they are politically explosive. If economics is to serve democracy, it must learn to hear such claims before translating them into its own categories.

This is especially important for race. The lead essay mentions racial disparities at the outset, but the policy architecture it summarizes does not place racial domination at the center. In the United States, inequality has never been merely a matter of impersonal market outcomes. Slavery, land theft, Jim Crow, exclusion from New Deal protections, discriminatory housing finance, unequal schools, mass incarceration, and segmented labor markets have shaped wealth and opportunity. Economists have produced valuable work on discrimination, neighborhood effects, criminal justice, and intergenerational mobility. But an inclusive prosperity agenda that does not explicitly confront racialized state action and racialized markets will reproduce the very abstraction that makes many people skeptical of the discipline.

The same is true of gender and care. A great deal of socially necessary labor is unpaid, underpaid, or treated as peripheral because it does not fit comfortably within market production. Child care, elder care, domestic labor, and emotional labor sustain the economy, but they are often invisible in policy debates organized around productivity and wages. A post-neoliberal economics should put care infrastructure near the center: paid leave, child allowances or credits, universal pre-K, professionalized care work, and schedules compatible with family life. These are not merely “social” policies. They shape labor supply, human development, bargaining power, and equality.

The authors are strongest when they insist that institutions are not given by nature. Property rights, corporate governance, patent rules, bankruptcy procedures, labor law, and trade agreements are all designed. Once that is acknowledged, the old opposition between “state” and “market” collapses. The question is not whether government intervenes, but on whose behalf, through which rules, and with what accountability. A corporation is not a natural market actor; it is a legal construction with privileges granted by the state. Intellectual property is not simply property; it is a policy choice about how to reward innovation and allocate access. Labor markets are not spot markets for widgets; they are structured by dependence, authority, law, and social norms.

This institutional perspective opens the door to bolder reforms than economists have often endorsed: sectoral bargaining, codetermination, public options, postal banking, stronger automatic stabilizers, public investment in green infrastructure, antitrust revival, and taxation of wealth and inheritances. Some of these ideas can be justified in efficiency terms; others rest on democratic and egalitarian commitments. That is fine. The effort to redescribe every egalitarian reform as a free lunch is tempting but limiting. There are free lunches in an economy full of monopoly, monopsony, externalities, and informational failures. But sometimes justice will cost something, at least for some people, and a mature politics should be able to say so.

Climate change makes this unavoidable. Carbon pricing is a powerful tool, but climate policy cannot be reduced to finding the right price. The transformation required is infrastructural, technological, regional, and political. It involves public investment, regulation, planning, industrial policy, and compensation for workers and communities tied to fossil fuels. Economists can help design these policies, but they must abandon the reflex that treats direct public coordination as suspect and price mechanisms as presumptively elegant. The atmosphere does not care whether emissions reductions are achieved through the most textbook-efficient instrument. It cares whether they happen quickly enough and durably enough.

So yes: economics can serve inclusive prosperity. But it will do so only if economists change not just their policy conclusions, but their public posture. They should speak with more humility about uncertainty, more candor about values, and more respect for other forms of knowledge. They should treat history, sociology, political science, law, and moral philosophy not as decorative supplements but as necessary partners. They should teach introductory students that markets are instituted, power is real, and efficiency is not a synonym for justice. And they should be judged not only by the sophistication of their models, but by whether their advice helps build durable democratic capacity.

The lead essay is therefore an important invitation. It shows that mainstream tools can support egalitarian, institution-building reforms. But the deeper task is to make economics answerable to the society it seeks to improve. After neoliberalism, economics should not aspire to be the queen of the social sciences, dispensing rigor to the less formal disciplines. The Discipline Is the Problem

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written a generous and well-intentioned defense of their profession. They argue that neoliberalism is not the consistent application of modern economics but its "primitive, simplistic perversion," and that the actual frontier of the discipline is full of tools and findings that point toward a more egalitarian politics. This is true. It is also, I want to argue, beside the point.

The question is not whether contemporary economics, properly understood, supports inclusive prosperity. The question is what economics, as an institution operating in the world, has produced over the last forty years. By that measure—the measure that matters for politics—the discipline has been an instrument of upward redistribution. Pretending otherwise requires ignoring a great deal of what economists themselves did.

When Larry Summers and Robert Rubin steamrolled the Brooksley Born proposal to regulate derivatives in 1998, they were not engaged in some primitive perversion of economic thought. They were trained at the same departments, published in the same journals, and held the same intellectual commitments as the authors of this essay. When the IMF imposed structural adjustment on country after country in the 1980s and 1990s, the economists writing those programs were drawing on the best of their training. When trade economists assured policymakers for two decades that the effects of trade with China would wash out in a few years of reallocation, they were not perverting the discipline; they were practicing it. The "China shock" papers by Autor, Dorn, and Hanson that overturned this consensus appeared in 2013, after the damage was done. The empirical turn the authors celebrate has overwhelmingly been a turn that corrects the discipline's earlier errors—errors that had enormous human costs.

This pattern matters because the authors' framing implies that bad policy came from bad economics that has now been superseded by good economics. But the bad economics was the mainstream economics of its day, taught from the same Mankiw and Krugman-Obstfeld textbooks, published in the AER and QJE, endorsed by Nobel laureates. If the discipline produced confident bad advice for forty years and is now producing better advice, the natural question is: what is the half-life of the current consensus? Why should we believe the new findings about minimum wages or trade or financial liberalization are more durable than the old ones? Humility on this point would be welcome, and is absent.

There is also a sociological problem the essay touches but does not really grapple with. Economists do not float free of the political economy they study. Departments are funded by donors. Think tanks bridge academia and policy. Consulting work, expert witness fees, and revolving-door positions create powerful incentives. The Mont Pelerin Society did not become hegemonic because Hayek's ideas were unusually persuasive on their merits; it became hegemonic because a network of foundations and businesses chose to fund it. The "creative ferment" the authors describe is not similarly resourced. EfIP is a website. The Kochs are a movement. Saying the technical content of the discipline supports a wide range of policy conclusions is true and irrelevant if the institutional architecture of policy advice is structured to select for one kind of conclusion.

Finally, I want to push on the celebrated "it depends" character of economic reasoning. The authors quote Diaz-Alejandro: a bright graduate student can produce a model yielding any conclusion he favored at the start. They mean this as a charming illustration of the discipline's flexibility. I read it as devastating. If the formal apparatus does not constrain conclusions, then conclusions are being driven by something else—prior commitments, professional incentives, what is fundable, what is publishable, what makes one sound serious to the editorial board of the Financial Times. The mathematics is camouflage. The actual selection mechanism is sociological, and it has not changed.

None of this is an argument for abolishing economics or replacing it with something else. The empirical findings the authors cite are real gains in knowledge, and the policy proposals at the end of the essay are mostly excellent. Arindrajit Dube on wage boards, Zucman on formulary apportionment, Admati on bank capital—these are the kinds of ideas that ought to be at the center of policy debate. I would vote for every one of them.

But I would vote for them not because economics has, after long deliberation, blessed them, but because the political coalition that wants a fairer economy now has the technical capacity to translate its commitments into workable proposals. The economists are useful. They are not authoritative. And the profession's pretension to authority—its insistence that it speaks for what science permits—is precisely what allowed neoliberalism to clothe a political project in the borrowed dignity of expertise. If the authors want a different politics, they should worry less about defending economics and more about dismantling the asymmetry that lets economics overrule democracy in the first place.

The tax has not happened, and would not be remotely sufficient if it did. Meanwhile actual decarbonization will require building enormous amounts of physical infrastructure, retiring trillions of dollars of stranded assets, and confronting incumbents who will fight to the death. The framework of "inclusive prosperity" with its careful attention to market failures and free lunches is not adequate to a problem that demands wartime mobilization.

Or consider the treatment of corporate power. Zucman's proposal on apportionment is excellent and I support it. But the deeper question—why corporations have become the dominant institutions of contemporary life, why their internal governance is autocratic, why workers have essentially no voice in the production decisions that shape their lives—is not asked. The authors gesture at Polanyi but draw none of his conclusions. Polanyi's point was that the labor market is fictitious, that treating human beings as commodities produces social catastrophe, and that the response is not to fine-tune the commodity market but to decommodify. None of the EfIP proposals decommodify anything. They re-regulate, redistribute, and reform. They do not transform.

Or consider the treatment of finance. Admati and Mian are right that the banking system is fragile and dangerous. Higher capital requirements would help. So would breaking up the largest banks. But the deeper question is why an enormous share of national income flows through a sector that does very little productive work, why the allocation of credit is delegated to private profit-maximizers, and whether a public banking option, postal banking, or even nationalization of the payments system might better serve the public. These options are not on the EfIP menu. The menu stays within the bounds of what is sayable in a faculty seminar at Harvard.

The constraint here is not, I think, intellectual cowardice. It is the form of argument. Economists are trained to identify discrete market failures and propose corrective interventions. The framework presupposes the legitimacy of the underlying market and treats deviation from competitive equilibrium as a tractable engineering problem. This is fine if you think most existing markets are basically legitimate and competently designed. It is inadequate if you think the basic shape of contemporary capitalism is the problem—if you think, for example, that the conversion of housing from shelter into a financial asset is a categorical mistake, or that the patent system is a giant rent extraction machine that we would never adopt if we were designing the institutions of innovation from scratch.

The authors actually have one excellent example of the more ambitious mode: Korinek's observation that the direction of technological change is itself a policy choice, and that we routinely intervene to shape it. This kind of thinking points beyond market-failure economics toward what we might call constitutive policy—policies that do not just correct outcomes within given institutions but design the institutions themselves. The same logic could be applied much more broadly. Who owns data? Who owns biotech patents developed with public money? Who owns the upstream platforms that mediate most of contemporary commerce? These are not market failure questions. They are ownership questions, and the existing distribution of ownership is the result of political choices that can be remade.

I do not want to be ungenerous. EfIP is a step forward from the Washington Consensus that preceded it. The proposals are better than what we have. If they were adopted tomorrow the United States would be a meaningfully better country. I want to be clear that I support this work.

But I also want to register that the political moment calls for more. The rise of right-wing authoritarianism in the United States and across Europe is not a problem that can be solved by better tax design and better bank regulation. It is the product of a generation in which a substantial portion of the population came to believe, correctly, that the institutions of liberal democracy were not delivering material improvement in their lives and were not responsive to their preferences. Recovering legitimacy will require a politics that visibly transforms the relations of economic power, not one that tweaks them. EfIP supplies the tweaks. Someone else will need to supply the vision.

I think each of these claims is wrong, or at least much more contestable than the authors allow.

Start with the diagnosis. The authors lead with U.S. inequality, wage stagnation, declining life expectancy, and so on. These are real concerns. But the framing of these as the fruits of neoliberalism is a parochial American story dressed up as a global verdict. The period the authors lament has also been the period in which global poverty fell more rapidly than at any time in human history. Roughly a billion people have escaped extreme poverty since 1990. Child mortality has halved. Literacy has soared. Life expectancy worldwide has risen by nearly a decade. If "neoliberalism" means trade liberalization, integration of poor countries into global capital and goods markets, and the spread of property rights and the rule of law, then neoliberalism is the most successful poverty-reduction program in history. To indict it for the relatively modest distributional troubles of the rich world is to weigh a stubbed toe against a cured cancer.

Within the United States, the picture is also more complex than the essay admits. Median household income, properly measured to include in-kind transfers and adjusted for household size, has risen substantially since the 1970s. Consumption inequality has grown much less than income inequality. The share of Americans in absolute poverty as conventionally measured has fallen. Life expectancy declines in the last few years are concerning, but they are driven heavily by opioids and suicide—not phenomena well explained by "market fundamentalism," and not phenomena obviously remediable by better minimum wage policy or financial regulation. The story that everything has gotten worse for ordinary Americans for half a century is rhetorically powerful and substantively misleading.

Now the policy prescriptions. The proposals collected under the EfIP banner are a grab bag of varying quality. Some are sensible (Zucman on multinational taxation, Admati on bank capital). Some are dubious (the suggestion that minimum wage hikes do not reduce employment is true for modest increases at low wage levels and false at higher levels, as the authors surely know but do not say). Some are dangerous (Rodrik on protectionism justified by "social safeguards" is essentially a license for any rich country to block imports whenever a domestic constituency objects, which is to say, always; the casualties of that regime would be poor workers in poor countries, not the constituencies Rodrik wishes to protect).

The deeper problem is the authors' theory of how policy goes wrong. They suggest that neoliberalism succeeded because of intellectual capture: economists became cheerleaders for markets and policymakers listened. This is not a serious history. Neoliberal reforms in the 1980s and 1990s happened because the alternatives had visibly failed. The British state nationalized substantial portions of its economy in the postwar period and the result was relative decline so severe that the United Kingdom went to the IMF in 1976. The Soviet economic model collapsed because it could not produce goods anyone wanted. Latin American import-substitution industrialization produced hyperinflation and debt crises. The "neoliberal" reforms that followed were not imposed by Hayekian ideologues; they were attempts, often grudging, to fix systems that had broken down on their own terms.

The success of the reforms is also clearer than the essay suggests. India's liberalization in 1991 lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty. China's market reforms—which the authors curiously cite as evidence for institutional pluralism—are textbook examples of marketization, including the introduction of private property, profit incentives, foreign investment, and integration into global trade. Eastern European countries that liberalized rapidly outperformed those that did so slowly. The Nordics, often invoked as alternatives to neoliberalism, have actually liberalized substantially since the 1980s while maintaining strong welfare states; they are not models of resistance to markets but of competent markets paired with redistribution.

I am also skeptical of the authors' confidence that they have identified "free lunches." The history of economic policy is largely a history of free lunches that turned out to have prices. Rent control was a free lunch until cities found themselves with chronic housing shortages. Bank regulation was a free lunch until depositors were trapped in failing institutions. The Community Reinvestment Act was a free lunch until subprime lending unwound. Even "no-brainer" interventions usually have second-order effects that economists, including these economists, will discover after the fact and write papers about. Humility, which the authors recommend at one point in the essay, is in short supply when they describe their own proposals.

There is one point at which I want to agree wholeheartedly with the authors. They are right that economics, properly practiced, is not a set of fixed policy conclusions but a framework for analyzing trade-offs. Where I part company is in their suggestion that the framework, when properly applied, more or less supports their politics. It does not. It supports their politics in some cases, opposes it in others, and is genuinely indeterminate in many. The honest version of "Economics After Neoliberalism" would be a much more divided essay, with full-throated defenses of trade liberalization sitting next to wage board proposals, and with serious attention to the costs as well as the benefits of every intervention. What the authors have produced is closer to a manifesto: a presentation of one political vision as the natural conclusion of contemporary economic science. We have seen this move before, from the other side. It was not honest then and it is not honest now.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman's essay is the latest installment. It is more thoughtful and more honest than most. It is also a reminder of how far the imperial discipline still has to travel.

Economics, the authors concede, has problems: it is insular, formalist, methodologically individualist, and prone to first-best reasoning that ignores institutional and political context. Fair. But these problems, the authors continue, are being addressed. Economists are doing economic history. They are doing behavioral economics. They are studying culture. The implicit message is reassuring: the discipline is fine, it is reaching out, it is learning.

What the message obscures is the structure of the reaching out. When economists do economic history, they do not generally cite the historians who have been doing economic history for a century. They reinvent the wheel using their own methods and publish in their own journals. When economists do "culture," they do not engage with the immense anthropological and sociological literatures on culture; they construct minimalist proxies, often a single Hofstede dimension or a survey question, and run regressions. When economists do political economy, they treat the field as if it began with Acemoglu and Robinson, ignoring decades of work in comparative politics. The reaching out is real, but it goes in one direction: economists absorb topics, not interlocutors.

This matters because the substantive claims of the EfIP project depend on understandings of politics, power, and institutions that economics has been notably bad at handling. Take the central concept of the essay: power. The authors note, in a striking concession, that asymmetries of power "abound" and that policies must counter them. They list bargaining power in labor markets, market power, mobility power, tax evasion, agenda-setting in trade negotiations, voting rights. Good. But how does economics actually theorize these phenomena? Mostly by introducing search frictions, monopsony parameters, or principal-agent setups into otherwise standard models. The deep traditions of social theory that have grappled with power—Weber, Marx, Foucault, Bourdieu, the relational sociologists—do not appear. Power becomes a modifier inside an otherwise market-shaped frame, rather than a constitutive feature of how markets themselves come into being.

The same is true of the discussion of institutions. The authors note that markets are "embedded" in institutions, citing Polanyi. But Polanyi's argument was not that markets need institutional supports; that is a banal point any institutional economist would grant. His argument was that the attempt to construct a self-regulating market society is a utopian project that produces its own catastrophic backlash—the rise of fascism, in his case. The argument is sociological and historical. It cannot be operationalized into a list of market failures. Citing Polanyi as a friendly authority while continuing to work within the market-failure framework is a kind of category mistake.

Or consider the discussion of "democratic political economy." The authors note that policies break on the rock of government capture and media distortion, and they endorse Ethan Kaplan's empirical political economy proposals. These are useful: campaign finance limits, easier voting, and so on. But the conception of democracy here is procedural in a thin way. The richer traditions in political theory and democratic sociology that ask what kinds of social structures enable substantive democratic participation, what role workplaces play in shaping democratic capacities, how informal racial hierarchies shape political voice, what associational life does for civic culture—none of this appears. Democracy gets flattened into a turnout problem.

I do not mean to be harsh. The authors are trying. They are aware of the limitations of their discipline and they want to correct them. The proposals collected under the EfIP banner are mostly good and would help if adopted. But the essay reveals, against its own intent, how much remains to be done. The authors describe their project as developing "an overall vision for economic policy." A vision, as the term suggests, requires more than a set of policies. It requires an account of how society fits together, what counts as a good life, what the relevant agents and relations are, how change happens. Economics on its own cannot supply this. What economics can supply is a set of analytic tools that, in the hands of people informed by adjacent disciplines and broader social experience, can help implement a vision developed elsewhere.

The honest version of "Economics After Neoliberalism" would not be a defense of the discipline as the source of a new framework. It would be a humbler statement: that economics is one input among many into the politics of inclusive prosperity, that the framework will have to come from elsewhere—from political philosophy, from social movements, from comparative-historical sociology, from the lived experience of the affected—and that the role of economists in this project is to serve, not to lead. I suspect the authors would resist this formulation. I think it is closer to the truth.

What "Neoliberalism" Means, and Why It Matters

The authors use the term "neoliberalism" with a kind of weary frequency, occasionally accompanied by parenthetical synonyms—market fundamentalism, market fetishism, market worship. The term does considerable work in their essay: it names the enemy, identifies the past forty years as a coherent period, and locates the source of contemporary discontent. But the term is doing rather more work than its history can bear, and the slippage matters for the argument.

A brief reminder of the history. "Neoliberalism" was coined in the 1930s by a group of liberals who met at the Colloque Walter Lippmann in Paris in 1938, including Hayek, Mises, Lippmann himself, and others. Their concern was that classical liberalism had failed to defend itself against socialism and fascism, and that a renewed liberalism—a "neo" liberalism—needed to embrace an active state that would construct and maintain the legal and institutional conditions of competitive markets. The neoliberals were not advocates of laissez-faire. They were advocates of a particular kind of state, one strong enough to defend the price system from democratic interference.

This intellectual lineage matters because the actual neoliberal tradition, as documented by historians like Quinn Slobodian and Philip Mirowski, was not principally about deregulation or shrinking the state. It was about insulating market mechanisms from democratic politics by embedding them in transnational legal structures—the EU's internal market rules, the WTO's trade disciplines, central bank independence, investor-state dispute settlement, constitutional debt brakes. The neoliberal project was constitutional, not just policy-level. Its great victory was not in any particular tax cut but in constructing an architecture in which whole domains of economic policy were placed beyond the reach of ordinary politics.

Why does this matter for Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman? Because their critique of "neoliberalism" is in part a critique of laissez-faire economics, and laissez-faire was not what neoliberalism principally delivered. The state did not shrink. Public spending as a share of GDP in OECD countries has been remarkably stable or rising. What changed was the character of state action: it became more friendly to capital, more disciplinary toward labor, more committed to the protection of property and contract, more willing to use coercion in some domains (criminal justice, border control) and more reluctant in others (industrial policy, labor regulation). The neoliberal state is a strong state, just one organized around different priorities.

This means that "more state" is not, by itself, an alternative to neoliberalism. The contemporary right understands this perfectly well: Viktor Orban and Donald Trump are perfectly happy to use state power, including against markets, in pursuit of ethnonationalist projects. The state has been wielded against migrants, against political opponents, against the press. None of this contradicts neoliberalism in the historically specific sense. It accompanies it.

What would actually contradict neoliberalism would be the re-democratization of economic decision-making: the return of disputes about wages, prices, investment, and credit to ordinary political contestation rather than to courts, central banks, and international institutions. This is a much more demanding project than the EfIP proposals suggest. It would require not just better technical policy but the dismantling of significant portions of the constitutional architecture that the neoliberal project built. It would require, for example, rethinking central bank independence, rethinking trade dispute settlement, rethinking the protection of capital mobility, rethinking the legal status of corporate personhood and free speech.

The EfIP project, to its credit, gestures at some of this. Rodrik's social safeguards in trade are a step toward giving democratic publics a claim against transnational disciplines. Zucman's formulary apportionment is a step toward asserting public taxing authority against transnational capital mobility. These are real interventions. But the framing of the essay—as a contest between "neoliberal" policies and better economic ideas—obscures the deeper struggle, which is about who decides, not just what is decided.

I want to add one more historical observation. The authors note that conservatives have monopolized the banner of economics in policy circles. This is true but incomplete. The actual story of the last forty years is that center-left parties—the Democrats, New Labour, the SPD—largely accepted the neoliberal consensus on economic management while differentiating themselves on social issues. Clinton, Blair, Schröder were enthusiastic privatizers and globalizers. The economists who advised them—Summers, Rubin, Mandelson—were not conservative. They were mainstream. They were drawing on the same discipline the authors of this essay come from. The neoliberal era was a bipartisan achievement, and the bipartisanship was held together by a set of beliefs about what economic science permitted.

The authors are right that the science permits more than this. But the asymmetry between what the science permits and what the policymakers believed was sociologically constructed and politically enforced. Changing it will require not just better ideas but a different relationship between economic expertise and democratic publics—one in which economists are visibly accountable to constituencies other than their professional peers and elite policymakers.

I am hopeful about the EfIP project as a contribution to that change. But I want to flag the risk that calling for "economics after neoliberalism" will produce a slightly more progressive technocracy with the same democratic deficits as the one we have now. The point is not to replace bad expert rule with good expert rule. The point is to subordinate expertise to politics, while keeping it competent. That is harder than the essay suggests.

The EfIP manifesto leans heavily on a specific story about contemporary economics: that the discipline has become much more empirical since the 1990s, that systematic evidence has displaced ideological priors, and that the new evidence supports a substantially more interventionist policy stance than the old conventional wisdom. I want to push on each of these claims as someone who works inside this empirical tradition.

The empirical turn is real. The share of empirical papers in top journals has risen substantially, and the methodological standards for credible causal identification have tightened. Where once a regression with a few control variables passed as analysis, the modern paper requires natural experiments, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, or randomized controlled trials. This is progress. Many findings that survived for decades in the textbooks—negative employment effects of moderate minimum wages, large growth effects of trade liberalization, neutral distributional effects of capital account opening—have not held up under more rigorous scrutiny. The authors are right to celebrate this.

But the celebration should be more guarded than the essay allows. The credibility revolution has its own pathologies, and they matter for the political project the authors describe.

First, the credibility revolution biases the discipline toward questions that admit of clean identification, often at the expense of larger and more important questions. We have a great deal of careful evidence about the effects of small changes in policy parameters at the margin of existing institutional arrangements. We have much less evidence about the effects of large institutional changes, because such changes do not produce the kind of variation that credibility-focused designs require. The minimum wage literature is illustrative: we know a great deal about the local effects of $1-$2 minimum wage hikes from neighboring-county designs. We know much less about what a $15 federal minimum wage would do, because such a change is outside the support of the available data. The EfIP project includes proposals (wage boards, contingent debt contracts) that are similarly outside the support of clean U.S. evidence. We can extrapolate, we can use Australian or German comparisons, but the confidence the empirical literature affords for marginal interventions does not carry over to structural ones.

Second, the credibility revolution has a partial-equilibrium bias. The cleanest identification strategies isolate local effects, often by exploiting the fact that one place got a policy and a similar place didn't. But many of the most important effects of policy are general-equilibrium effects that wash out in local comparisons. A minimum wage that raises wages in restaurants might lower wages in agriculture as displaced workers flow elsewhere; a local design won't catch this. A trade liberalization that decimates manufacturing communities might still raise national income on net through gains in other sectors; a place-based design will document the losses but not the offsetting gains. The empirical literature is good at the parts of policy questions that admit of local variation and worse at the parts that don't. The authors lean on the empirical literature without acknowledging that the parts of their proposals that are well-supported empirically are often not the parts that matter most.

Third, the credibility revolution has done less than it claims to discipline ideological priors. The same methods can be applied with different framings, different samples, and different control variables. Studies of the same question often reach divergent conclusions. The minimum wage literature is again illustrative: there is now a large body of credible studies, and they continue to disagree. The Card-Krueger tradition finds small or zero employment effects. The Neumark-Wascher tradition finds meaningful negative effects. Both use modern methods. The disagreement is not resolved by better identification; it is reproduced inside better identification. The authors' confidence that the empirical evidence has moved the field is, in part, a selection effect: they cite the findings consistent with their politics and elide the rest.

Fourth, the credibility revolution has been less successful at the macroeconomic questions that matter most for the EfIP project than at microeconomic questions. We do not have good causal estimates of the effects of capital account liberalization, or of corporate tax cuts, or of public investment, because the relevant variation is at the country-year level and there are not many countries. The authors' confidence that "financial liberalization produces crises rather than faster economic growth" rests on findings that, while suggestive, do not have the identification credentials of the minimum wage or trade literatures. This is not a fatal problem—we do the best we can with the data we have—but it is a reason for more caution than the essay exhibits.

I want to be clear: I support the EfIP project. I think the proposals are mostly good. I think the empirical literature, properly handled, leans in the direction of more interventionist policy than the conventional wisdom of the 1990s. But the rhetorical move the authors make—from "economics has become more empirical" to "economics now supports our policy program"—is faster than the evidence warrants. The empirical literature is a tool, not a verdict. It supports some of the EfIP proposals more than others, and it leaves many of the most important questions open.

The honest version of the empirical case for inclusive prosperity would acknowledge the gaps. It would distinguish between proposals that are well-supported by clean evidence (Dube on local minimum wage variation, perhaps), proposals that are supported by reasonable extrapolation (wage boards informed by Australian evidence), and proposals that rest mostly on theory and analogy (Korinek on directing technological change). All of these can be defended, but the defenses look different. Treating the whole portfolio as if it is uniformly underwritten by the credibility revolution does the project a disservice and gives critics an easy target.

If economics is to play the role the authors hope for it, it needs to be honest about what it knows and doesn't. The credibility revolution is a powerful tool but it does not magically deliver progressive policy conclusions. It delivers, mostly, more cautious and more local conclusions than the old confident generalities. Sometimes those are progressive, sometimes they are not, and sometimes they leave the big questions unresolved. EfIP should embrace this uncertainty rather than paper over it.

Where Are the Women?

Reading "Economics After Neoliberalism," I made a list of the substantive topics addressed: labor markets, trade, finance, banks, monetary policy, technology, taxation, electoral institutions. I then made a list of the topics not addressed: care work, the family, gender, reproduction, the household as an economic unit, sexual and racial divisions of labor. The second list is shorter, but the silences are louder.

This is not a complaint about authorial omission so much as about disciplinary blind spots that the EfIP project reproduces. Mainstream economics still struggles to take seriously the enormous productive activity that occurs outside paid employment, principally in households and disproportionately performed by women. The U.S. economy depends on the unpaid labor of childcare, eldercare, domestic maintenance, and emotional labor. By plausible estimates, this work is worth trillions of dollars annually—larger than entire major sectors of the formal economy. It is the substrate on which paid employment runs. And it is almost entirely missing from the EfIP framework.

This matters substantively. Many of the policy proposals on offer presume a worker who is free to engage with the labor market as a relatively unencumbered individual. Wage boards, union rebuilding, monopsony correction—all of these address the conditions of paid work. They do little for the worker whose labor market participation is constrained by care responsibilities, whose ability to take a higher-paying job in another city is limited by family ties, whose wages must be discounted by the cost of childcare. These constraints are not minor. They are a principal mechanism through which gender inequality is produced and reproduced. They also bear heavily on racial inequality, since care responsibilities and care access are distributed very unequally across racial lines.

A serious framework for inclusive prosperity would put care at the center. It would include universal childcare, paid family leave, eldercare support, and recognition of unpaid care work in social insurance entitlements. It would consider how the structure of paid work—hours, scheduling, location—accommodates or excludes those with care responsibilities. It would treat the household, not the individual, as a basic unit of analysis. It would notice that the great middle-class welfare state of the postwar period was sustained by a gendered division of labor that is no longer available, and that any new welfare settlement will have to construct its own infrastructure for what households used to do for free.

There are economists doing this work. Nancy Folbre's career is a sustained engagement with the economics of care. Heather Boushey has written extensively on the economics of family policy. Susan Himmelweit and other feminist economists have developed sophisticated frameworks for analyzing the care economy. The EfIP project could have invited these voices. It did not.

The same is true for race. The authors note in passing that "racial disparities in wealth and well-being remain stubbornly persistent." This is the only mention of race in the essay. There is no engagement with the substantial literature on the racialized character of American labor markets, housing markets, credit markets, criminal justice, or public goods provision. There is no engagement with proposals for racial repair, from baby bonds to reparations. The authors' framework treats inequality as a one-dimensional phenomenon to be addressed by policies that lift all boats; the boats are assumed to be similar. They are not.

I want to anticipate a response. The authors might say that EfIP is a beginning, that the topics addressed are not the only topics that matter, that future iterations will broaden the agenda. Fair enough. But the choice of starting topics is itself a statement of priorities. When the discipline begins its self-reform with labor markets, trade, and finance—and not with care, reproduction, race, and gender—it reproduces, even in its progressive moments, the gendered and racialized hierarchies of its own intellectual history. The "core" of economics is what gets addressed first; everything else is a "specialty" or an "application" to be added later. This is what feminist economists have been pointing out for decades and what the EfIP framing, despite its good intentions, has not absorbed.

There is also a deeper conceptual issue. The competitive market model, which the authors note still serves as a kind of background benchmark even when they criticize it, treats the household as a unit that consumes and supplies labor without examining what happens inside it. The "household" is a black box with a single utility function. This is not just an empirical inadequacy; it is a theoretical commitment that obscures the production, distribution, and conflict that occur in households. Feminist economists from Marilyn Waring onward have argued that this black-boxing is not a methodological convenience but a substantive bias that makes large portions of economic life invisible. Until the discipline opens the black box, its claims to be a science of "the economy" will remain partial.

The authors are right that economics has reached out to other disciplines—behavioral economics from psychology, culture from anthropology, history. The most striking absence in this list is feminism. Mainstream economics has not seriously engaged with feminist theory, despite four decades of feminist economic scholarship. The reasons are partly intellectual (feminist analysis resists individualist methodology, emphasizes social construction, attends to power in ways economists find uncomfortable) and partly sociological (feminist economists have been marginalized in hiring, funding, and citation networks). The result is that the discipline's progressive turn, as represented by EfIP, looks remarkably like its conservative past in terms of who counts as a serious interlocutor.

If economics is to serve inclusive prosperity, it will need to be inclusive at the level of its own intellectual community, not just at the level of its policy recommendations. The authors' list of contributors is all credentialed in mainstream subfields. The next iteration should reach beyond. Care economics, feminist economics, stratification economics, ecological economics—these are not exotic specialties to be brought in after the main agenda is set. They should help set the agenda.

Inclusive Prosperity on a Burning Planet

The framing of "inclusive prosperity" is humane and worth defending. It also is, I think, inadequate to the situation we are actually in.

The authors mention climate change three times. Once in the opening paragraph as one of several crises shaping the contemporary moment. Once in passing as a topic on which economists have done research. Once in a sentence noting that economists are likely to support a carbon price. That is the full substantive treatment in an essay of more than four thousand words about the future of economic policy.

We are facing a civilizational emergency. The IPCC's October 2018 special report on 1.5 degrees concluded that limiting warming to 1.5 degrees requires global CO2 emissions to fall by about 45 percent from 2010 levels by 2030 and to reach net zero by around 2050. We are nowhere close to this trajectory. Global emissions are still rising. The carbon budget is being depleted at roughly 40 gigatonnes per year. Even the much weaker 2 degree target requires transformations of energy, transport, industry, agriculture, and land use that the current policy regime is not delivering and shows no sign of delivering.

In this context, "inclusive prosperity"—a framework focused on making the existing economy more equitable—is too thin. The existing economy is the problem. Its scale, its composition, its energy basis, its material throughput are incompatible with ecological stability. Making it more equitable without changing its physical character would distribute disaster more fairly, which is something, but it would not avert the disaster.

The framework that climate demands is closer to what people are now calling a Green New Deal: a coordinated public mobilization to rebuild the physical economy on a low-carbon basis within a decade or two. This is not principally a market-failure correction project, though carbon pricing has a role. It is an investment project on the scale of the Second World War economic mobilization, requiring direct public investment in renewable energy, public transit, building retrofits, electrified industry, and land restoration. It requires phasing out fossil fuels on a regulated timeline rather than waiting for prices to do the work. It requires reckoning with the trillions of dollars of stranded fossil fuel assets and the political power they represent. It requires international coordination on a scale not seen since Bretton Woods.

The EfIP project is mostly silent on this. The authors describe themselves as developing a framework for economic policy that responds to contemporary challenges, but they do not address the challenge that is most urgent and most likely to define the politics of the next two decades. The closest they come is Korinek's proposal on directing technological change, which is in the right spirit but is framed around distribution rather than decarbonization.

This is not just an omission of subject matter. It is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the kind of economic thinking the project claims to represent. A Green New Deal raises every kind of question the authors say economics is good at: How do we coordinate large-scale investment? How do we manage the distributional consequences of phasing out carbon-intensive industries? How do we tax windfall gains from decarbonization? How do we structure public ownership of new infrastructure? How do we design international agreements that allow rapid decarbonization without triggering carbon leakage? How do we manage the macroeconomic implications of a major investment surge financed by public borrowing? These are first-rate economic questions. They are also urgent. The EfIP project is well-positioned to address them and has not.

There is, I suspect, an intellectual reason for the silence. Mainstream environmental economics has been dominated by the Nordhaus tradition: model climate change as an externality, calculate the social cost of carbon, recommend a Pigouvian tax. The recommendation has been intellectually stable for decades and politically dead for almost as long. Economists, having made their recommendation, have largely moved on, with the result that the climate emergency has not produced a corresponding emergency in economic thinking. The discipline's instinct is to assume that if its preferred instrument is not adopted, the problem is political and not its problem.

This is precisely the kind of "first-best reasoning" the authors elsewhere criticize. In a world where carbon pricing at the levels economists recommend is not politically achievable—because of distributional concerns, because of concentrated fossil fuel power, because of the unwillingness of voters to accept large increases in energy prices—the second-best question is what other instruments can achieve substantial decarbonization. Sectoral standards, public investment, public ownership, industrial policy, just transition programs for affected communities: these are the tools of a politically feasible climate policy, and they require exactly the kind of comparative institutional analysis the authors champion. But the analysis has not been done at the scale required, and the EfIP project shows no signs of doing it.

I want to be constructive. Let me suggest what an EfIP project that took climate seriously would look like. It would have at least one major component focused on the macroeconomics of decarbonization: how to finance investment at the scale required, how to manage the inflation risks, how to handle the implications for monetary policy and fiscal rules. It would have a component on industrial policy for clean technology, drawing on the recent revival of industrial policy scholarship. It would have a component on the distributional politics of climate transition, including just transition programs for fossil fuel workers and communities. It would have a component on international coordination, including carbon border adjustments and climate clubs. It would include voices from ecological economics—Herman Daly, Tim Jackson, Kate Raworth—who have been thinking about prosperity beyond growth for decades.

The framing of "inclusive prosperity" would also need to be revisited. Prosperity, even inclusive prosperity, suggests continued material expansion. The ecological question is whether expansion in the conventional sense is compatible with planetary stability. The answer for rich countries is probably no. We need a framework in which improvement in human well-being is decoupled from increases in material throughput, which means rethinking what counts as economic success. This is hard but unavoidable.

I do not want to overstate the difference between my position and the authors'. They are clearly aware of the climate problem; they would clearly support a serious climate policy; they would clearly endorse much of what I have written above. The point is that the EfIP project, as launched, does not put climate at the center, and the climate problem is too large to be addressed from the periphery. If economics is to serve inclusive prosperity in the twenty-first century, it will need to be inclusive across generations as well as within them, and that means subordinating much of the existing agenda to the ecological emergency. The authors' framework can accommodate this, but it has not yet.

Free Lunches and the Question of Values

I want to focus on one revealing phrase in the EfIP manifesto: "free lunch." The authors write that the proposals reflect "the sense that economies are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, and that there are numerous policy 'free lunches' that could push us towards an economy that is morally better without sacrificing (and, indeed, possibly enhancing) prosperity."

This phrasing is meant to disarm critics. The standard objection to egalitarian policy has been that it imposes efficiency costs, and the authors want to push back: no, equality and efficiency can be improved together. The argument is empirically often correct. The U.S. economy is full of inefficiencies that fall disproportionately on the poor and that could be corrected to everyone's benefit. Monopsony in labor markets, predatory lending, the structure of healthcare, the geography of public goods—all of these are sites where redistributive interventions would likely raise aggregate welfare. The authors are right about this.

But the framing has a cost. "Free lunch" frames the project of progressive policy as one of seeking out interventions that are Pareto improvements or close to it. The standard for action is whether we can improve outcomes without anyone losing. This is a stringent standard. It privileges interventions that face minimal opposition and rules out those that require redistribution from concentrated winners to dispersed losers. It implicitly accepts existing distributions of property and power as the baseline against which interventions are to be judged.

The deeper progressive case for economic transformation has never rested on free lunches alone. It has rested on a substantive view of what we owe each other, of what a decent society looks like, of what is unacceptable about the current distribution regardless of whether changing it imposes efficiency costs. The civil rights movement did not justify desegregation primarily on efficiency grounds. The labor movement did not justify the eight-hour day primarily on efficiency grounds. Universal suffrage was not enacted because it was efficient. The case in each instance was a moral case about what people are owed.

When economists frame progressive policy as the discovery of free lunches, they implicitly accept that the burden of proof is on those who would change the status quo to show that the change is costless. This burden is unfair and self-defeating. It is unfair because the status quo is itself the result of countless interventions—property law, contract law, corporate law, intellectual property regimes, financial regulation—whose distributional effects are not subject to the same scrutiny. It is self-defeating because many of the most important progressive interventions will not pass the free-lunch test; they will require somebody to lose, and the case for the policy will rest on the moral importance of what is gained.

Take the case of a wealth tax of the kind proposed by some of the authors' colleagues. The case for such a tax is principally that extreme concentrations of wealth are corrosive of democracy, of equal opportunity, and of social solidarity, and that taxing them is therefore desirable even if it has some efficiency cost. The "efficiency cost" framing—does the tax raise revenue net of behavioral responses, does it distort savings, does it slow growth—is part of the analysis but it is not the heart of the matter. The heart of the matter is the moral and political claim that the current distribution of wealth is incompatible with the kind of society we want to live in.

The EfIP framing makes this kind of argument harder to articulate. By presenting progressive policy as a series of technical corrections to market failures, it strips out the moral content that gives the project its energy. It also limits the range of available policy. Many of the proposals in the project are good; they would not be sufficient even if all adopted, and the next steps will require arguments the free-lunch frame cannot support.

I want to note one place where the authors do break from the free-lunch frame: Rodrik's proposal on trade restrictions justified by "social safeguards." Rodrik explicitly refuses to clothe protectionism in efficiency language. He argues that trade restrictions can be justified by the requirements of the domestic social contract, full stop. This is the right kind of argument. It treats values—the value of social solidarity, of democratic self-government, of egalitarian relationships among citizens—as ends to which efficiency must be subordinated when necessary. The authors note this with approval and connect it to Polanyi.

I would like to see this mode of argument extended through the rest of the project. The case for unions is not just that they correct monopsony; it is that workplaces are sites of human dignity and that workers deserve voice in their conditions. The case for public goods is not just that markets undersupply them; it is that some goods—education, health, security—should be available to all as a matter of citizenship rather than purchasing power. The case for progressive taxation is not just that the marginal utility of money diminishes; it is that great concentrations of wealth distort the political community we share.

Economics does not have to answer these value questions. But it should leave room for them rather than absorbing them into the technical apparatus of welfare maximization. The free-lunch frame, well-meant as it is, narrows the moral imagination of the project. The authors are right that there are many free lunches to be had. They should also remember that the lunches that matter most are sometimes not free, and that the case for them is something the discipline of economics is not equipped to make on its own.

The View from the South

The EfIP manifesto is a document of and for the rich world, and especially the United States. Its diagnosis is American (rising U.S. inequality, U.S. life expectancy, U.S. opioid deaths), its proposals are mostly designed for U.S. or rich-world implementation, and its political horizon is the recovery of a humane social settlement in the wealthy democracies. This is fine as far as it goes. I want to register, from a developing country perspective, what is missing.

The first observation is that "neoliberalism" looks different from where I sit. The authors describe it as a domestic policy regime—deregulation, financialization, welfare cuts, anti-labor reforms—that produced inequality and discontent. From the developing world the more salient face of neoliberalism is structural adjustment: the package of policies imposed on indebted countries by the IMF and World Bank from the early 1980s through the 1990s, which included rapid trade liberalization, capital account opening, fiscal austerity, privatization of state enterprises, and the dismantling of import-substitution industrialization. These policies were not, on balance, successful. Countries that implemented them most thoroughly often grew most slowly. Countries that resisted them—China, India, much of East Asia—grew faster by following heterodox strategies. The lesson here is straightforward: the neoliberal model failed development as a project of structural transformation, and the cost was borne by poor people in poor countries.

The EfIP project does not address this history. The international economic order it implicitly accepts—global trade rules, capital mobility, the international monetary architecture—is the same one that produced structural adjustment. Reforms are proposed at the margins (Zucman on corporate taxation, Rodrik on social safeguards) but the basic shape is not questioned. The poor world is asked to take on faith that a more equitable rich world will translate into a more equitable global order. The historical record gives little support to this faith.

The second observation is that the politics of inclusive prosperity look different when "inclusion" is understood globally. The authors are concerned, properly, about the working class in the United States. But the global working class—billions of people in low and middle income countries—has been the principal beneficiary of the trade integration the authors are now equivocal about. Rodrik's social safeguards, well-intentioned, threaten this. If rich-country publics can block imports whenever they feel their social contract is threatened, the result is likely to be a regression to a more closed global economy in which the poor of poor countries pay for the social peace of the rich. There is a genuine ethical trade-off here that the authors gesture at but do not really grapple with.

The third observation is about development as a topic. Development economics has been one of the most empirically active subfields, with the RCT revolution and the work of Banerjee, Duflo, and others getting Nobel-level recognition. But the EfIP project does not treat development as central. The proposals address labor markets, taxation, finance, and so on in the rich world. The corresponding questions for the developing world—how to industrialize in the twenty-first century, how to escape the middle-income trap, how to build state capacity, how to manage commodity dependence, how to navigate the relationship with China—are not on the agenda. This is a significant omission given that most of humanity lives in developing countries and that the global political economy is being reshaped by their trajectories.

The fourth observation is about climate, which I want to register briefly because others have addressed it more fully. Climate justice has a strong distributional dimension: the rich world has emitted most of the historical carbon and the poor world will bear most of the damage. Any serious framework for inclusive prosperity must include large transfers from rich to poor countries to finance adaptation and clean development. The EfIP project does not mention this.

I want to be fair. The authors are American economists writing for a primarily American audience about American problems. They are not obligated to solve every problem in the world in one essay. The work they are doing is valuable and the proposals they put forward would, if implemented, make the United States a more humane country. I support this work.

But I want to register that "Economics After Neoliberalism" as currently framed remains a recognizably Northern project, organized around Northern concerns, and inattentive to the ways in which Northern policy reverberates through the rest of the world. The intellectual tradition the authors come from—mainstream economics, with its universal claims and its mathematical apparatus—has a poor record of taking seriously the experience and analysis of economists from the global South. The structuralists, the dependency theorists, the heterodox development economists who worked on these questions in the postwar period have been largely written out of the discipline's memory. The EfIP project, in reaching back for an alternative to neoliberalism, could have drawn on this tradition. It does not.

There are signs of change. Justin Lin's new structural economics, Ha-Joon Chang's institutional development economics, the work of Mariana Mazzucato on the entrepreneurial state—all of these point toward a more pluralist economics that takes development seriously. The EfIP project could and should engage with these voices in future iterations. The poor world has been a laboratory for economic ideas for a century, often with great human costs. It has also produced thinkers who have much to teach economists in the rich world about the limits of the universal market model. A truly post-neoliberal economics will require their participation, not as junior partners but as colleagues whose intellectual traditions are taken seriously on their own terms. We are still some distance from this. The EfIP project, for all its merits, is one step on a longer road.
