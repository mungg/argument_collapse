Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Daron Acemoglu has written a bracing and largely persuasive essay, and the argument's structure — that the trajectory of AI is a political choice, not a technological destiny — is the right place to start any serious public discussion of this technology. But I want to push on three points where I think the analysis, while correct in its broad strokes, papers over difficulties that will determine whether his program is workable.

The first concerns the labor-share measurement framework Acemoglu proposes as the policy lever for distinguishing "good" AI from "bad" AI. He suggests that since automation tends to reduce labor's share of value added while new-task technologies tend to raise it, governments can use this distinction to tilt incentives toward the latter. This is elegant in theory, but as a basis for actual policy it strikes me as fragile. Labor share is determined by an enormous number of forces — bargaining power, market concentration, trade, the price of capital goods, the composition of output across sectors — and disentangling the contribution of a specific class of AI applications would be extraordinarily difficult, especially in real time, before tax credits or subsidies are issued. Worse, the metric is gameable. A firm that fires call-center workers and rehires them at lower wages as "AI-assisted customer experience associates" could plausibly raise its measured labor share while doing exactly the kind of low-road automation Acemoglu worries about. We saw analogous problems with R&D tax credits in the 1980s, where firms reclassified ordinary engineering as research. Without sharper categories — perhaps tied to occupational task content, not aggregate labor share — the policy lever will bend.

Second, I think Acemoglu underplays the degree to which the postwar "shared prosperity plus democracy" compact was historically peculiar rather than a template easily reconstructed. He acknowledges that the Trente Glorieuses depended on a particular configuration of antitrust, union strength, federal R&D funding, and competitive product markets — but he tends to treat that configuration as a choice we could simply make again. The international comparison he draws to clean energy is instructive but also somewhat misleading. The carbon-abatement story worked in part because emissions are physically measurable, because the EU and a handful of large states could create a price signal that propagated globally through trade, and because the relevant innovations (solar panels, wind turbines, batteries) were embodied in discrete capital goods that could be subsidized at the point of deployment. AI is software. It diffuses through APIs and open-source repositories at near-zero marginal cost, the relevant talent is globally mobile, and there is no physical analogue to a tonne of CO2 against which to denominate "harm." This makes the coordination problem he flags at the end — what if China keeps pushing surveillance and automation? — not a peripheral worry but the central one. A national policy that taxes automation in the U.S. while Chinese firms continue down the current path could simply shift rents abroad without changing the underlying trajectory.

Third, and most importantly, I think the essay underestimates how much of the "automate everything" bias of current AI is downstream not just of who funds research but of the underlying technical paradigm. Acemoglu rightly notes that the modern AI revival came from reframing services as prediction problems amenable to statistical learning over big datasets. But this framing has a built-in directional bias: the cheapest and most legible applications of supervised learning are precisely those in which a human is already performing a repetitive labeled task whose outputs can be used as training data. In other words, the technology learns to do what humans already do, because that's what the training signal contains. Building AI that creates *new* tasks — that augments a teacher's adaptive instruction, or extends a nurse's diagnostic range — requires a different research program: one focused on interfaces, on tacit knowledge elicitation, on human-AI collaboration, and on domains where labeled data does not yet exist. This is not just a matter of redirecting funding from automation projects to augmentation projects; it requires investing in fundamentally less mature subfields (HCI, cognitive science, mixed-initiative systems) that have languished while deep learning has absorbed nearly all the oxygen. Acemoglu mentions these areas but doesn't quite grapple with the fact that they are scientifically harder, not just politically neglected.

Where I think Acemoglu is unambiguously right, and where his essay should be required reading, is on the inadequacy of the "let the market choose, then redistribute" position. The argument that good jobs are not merely instruments for income but constitutive of democratic citizenship is the strongest move in the essay, and it cuts cleanly through the universal-basic-income discourse that has filled this space for the past few years. A polity in which most people receive transfers from a small productive elite is not one in which democratic accountability survives long; the political economy of redistribution requires a citizenry that is also a producing citizenry.

So I read Acemoglu's essay less as a finished blueprint than as a successful reframing: AI policy is industrial policy and democratic policy, not consumer-protection policy. The measurement problems, the international coordination problems, and the technical-paradigm problems are real and may be binding. But they are problems to be worked on, not reasons to accept the current path as fate.

Daron Acemoglu’s diagnosis of AI’s present trajectory is both trenchant and urgent, and his refusal to accept the inevitability of job-destroying automation and surveillance is a vital corrective to a discourse that too often oscillates between techno-utopianism and fatalism. Yet for all its moral clarity, his proposed redirection of AI relies on an overly sanguine reading of postwar history and underestimates the structural obstacles that make a repeat of that era’s human-centered innovation far more difficult today. The vision of a three-pronged approach—government leadership, shifting norms, and democratic oversight—is admirable, but it elides the degree to which AI itself is already transforming the very conditions for democratic agency, and it places too much faith in the state as a neutral steward of technological direction.

Acemoglu’s master analogy is the postwar compact, where technological change was channeled into productivity-enhancing tasks rather than pure automation, aided by vigorous government funding, strong labor institutions, and competitive markets. The picture is compelling, but it obscures crucial differences. Postwar America invested heavily in science and technology at a moment of extraordinary geopolitical dominance and domestic political consensus. The Cold War created a bipartisan appetite for research, and the state’s role as a lead purchaser—particularly through the Department of Defense—was not simply a benign coordinator but an actor with its own security imperatives that often drove innovation toward surveillance and control from the very start. The same military-industrial complex that underwrote the internet and semiconductors also pioneered early AI, including the very pattern-recognition techniques that now power facial recognition. It is not an accident that today’s surveillance giants draw on the same government funding pipelines that Acemoglu wants to redirect; the state has always been deeply ambivalent, not a pure force for the democratic good.

The challenge is deeper still when we examine the market-reshaping tools he proposes. Acemoglu suggests a measurement framework based on the labor share of value added to distinguish “good” AI that creates new tasks from “bad” AI that automates them away. The intuition is elegant, but it assumes a stable, measurable link between a given AI application and aggregate labor share. In reality, most technologies do both: a medical diagnostic AI might displace some tasks for radiologists while simultaneously creating new roles for technicians who interpret and communicate results, and the net effect on labor share depends on bargaining power, organizational choices, and regulatory context—all of which are already skewed by the very political dynamics Acemoglu laments. A tax code that penalizes labor and subsidizes capital is one such distortion; but fixing that tax asymmetry, while sensible, does not guarantee that the new equilibrium will produce good jobs. In an economy where firms face little competitive pressure and workers have limited bargaining power, productivity gains from AI can simply be captured as rents by a handful of dominant companies, even if the AI itself is task-creating. The lesson from the postwar period is not merely that technology was human-friendly, but that a dense web of institutions—strong unions, sectoral bargaining, progressive taxation, vigorous antitrust—forced firms to share the gains. That institutional ecosystem has been dismantled over decades, and AI itself now accelerates its erosion by enabling platform monopolies, gig-work arrangements, and algorithmic management that further undermine worker power. Redirecting research funding without simultaneously rebuilding those countervailing institutions risks producing a kinder, gentler automation that still leaves labor disempowered.

Then there is the democratic oversight prong. Acemoglu acknowledges that democracy is weakening, but he treats this as a reversible trend—something that can be revitalized by the very process of redirecting AI. This risks circularity. Social media platforms powered by AI have already proven adept at inflaming polarization, spreading disinformation, and enabling micro-targeted manipulation. The 2020 U.S. election and the January 6 Capitol riot made plain that a significant portion of the electorate now inhabits an epistemological bubble actively maintained by recommender systems. In such an environment, the kind of broad public consensus on what AI applications are socially harmful is extraordinarily hard to achieve. The same technologies that need to be governed are corroding the shared factual basis required for democratic governance. Moreover, even if a democratic awakening miraculously occurred in the United States, Acemoglu’s international dimension becomes an almost insurmountable hurdle. China’s AI trajectory, with its deep integration of surveillance and social credit systems, is not merely a competing paradigm—it is an alternative political-economic model that exerts competitive pressure on Western democracies. If U.S. firms voluntarily forswear lucrative facial recognition contracts, Chinese firms will eagerly fill the gap, and authoritarian regimes around the world will have a ready supplier. International coordination on AI is not just difficult; it is structurally opposed by the very geopolitical rivalries that Acemoglu invokes as a spur to innovation. One cannot wish this away with a call for “a modicum of international coordination.”

Finally, the norms-shifting component places a heavy burden on individual researchers and public pressure. The examples Acemoglu cites—NSO Group’s reputational crisis, IBM and Microsoft temporarily halting facial recognition sales—are notable but hardly transformative. They occurred in a context of heightened racial justice protests and exceptional media scrutiny, not sustained structural change. Tech companies have already resumed lobbying against facial recognition bans and quietly continue developing the underlying capabilities. Academic researchers remain dependent on corporate funding and data access; the revolving door spins ever faster. Changing norms is possible, but without altering the material incentives that channel the brightest minds toward automation and surveillance, moral exhortation will remain a niche pursuit.

None of this means Acemoglu’s project is hopeless. His insistence that AI’s direction is a choice, not a destiny, is a necessary first step. But realizing that choice requires confronting the deep entanglement of AI with the very structures—political, economic, geopolitical—that need to be reformed. The postwar analogy offers inspiration, but the conditions that made Les Trente Glorieuses possible were historically exceptional, and the digital era has produced a form of capitalism far more resistant to the shared-prosperity compact. If we are to redirect AI, we must not only measure the labor share but also rebuild the institutional muscle that once forced technology to serve broad-based prosperity. That is a taller order, and one that AI itself makes harder with each passing year.

Vanilla gemini-3.1-pro-preview

Daron Acemoglu accurately captures the defining anxiety of our current technological era: artificial intelligence, left to its current trajectory, threatens to fracture the foundations of shared prosperity and democratic governance. His diagnosis of the post-1980s shift—where technology ceased to reliably create new, wage-boosting tasks for human workers and instead became an engine for pure automation and capital concentration—is both historically grounded and deeply unsettling. We are indeed drifting toward a political economy dominated by algorithmic surveillance and routine job displacement. However, while Acemoglu’s call to deliberately redirect AI research toward "human-complementary" applications is morally compelling, the practical mechanisms he proposes to achieve this face insurmountable economic, epistemological, and geopolitical hurdles.

The core of Acemoglu’s solution relies on government policy, shifting norms, and democratic oversight to actively steer AI research away from automation and toward augmentation. To do this, he suggests developing a measurement framework based on how technologies affect the labor share of value added, allowing policymakers to subsidize the good AI and tax the bad. The fatal flaw in this approach is the assumption that we can accurately categorize the economic impact of general-purpose technologies before they are fully developed or deployed.

Unlike the transition to green energy, which Acemoglu uses as a primary comparison, the "pro-labor" AI transition lacks an objective, easily measurable metric like carbon emissions. A ton of carbon avoided is a universal good. A new machine learning architecture, however, is fundamentally neutral and highly malleable. Consider the recent breakthroughs in large language models, such as OpenAI’s GPT-3. Is natural language processing an "automating" technology or an "augmenting" one? The answer depends entirely on the business model of the firm deploying it. It could be used to entirely replace frontline customer service representatives, driving down the labor share. Conversely, it could be used as an adaptive tutoring tool—exactly the kind of application Acemoglu praises—empowering educators and increasing their productivity. Because the same algorithmic breakthrough enables both the dystopian and the utopian application, attempting to steer the basic research via government grants or tax incentives based on unpredictable downstream labor impacts is an exercise in futility.

Furthermore, relying on shifting cultural norms among researchers and tech companies to prioritize human-centric AI underestimates the sheer force of market incentives. Acemoglu is absolutely correct that our current tax code artificially subsidizes capital investments at the expense of human labor, and correcting this imbalance should be an immediate legislative priority. But even with a neutralized tax code, the fundamental drive of the modern firm is to reduce variable costs and achieve scale. Human labor is expensive, complex, and unscalable in the way software is. As long as algorithms can perform cognitive tasks at a fraction of the cost of human wages, the gravitational pull of automation will persist. We cannot simply shame corporations into abandoning massive cost-saving efficiencies, nor can we expect researchers to artificially kneecap the capabilities of the systems they are building just to keep humans in the loop. The "automation" vs "augmentation" binary is often a false one; true augmentation makes humans so productive that fewer of them are needed to achieve the same output, which inevitably looks like automation to the workers who are rendered redundant.

Most critically, Acemoglu introduces the international dimension as an afterthought, treating it as a complicating factor rather than the central, defining constraint on domestic AI policy. In 2021, we are in the midst of a fierce geopolitical competition over artificial intelligence, primarily with China. Just months ago, the National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence explicitly warned that the United States must aggressively accelerate AI development to avoid losing its technological edge. The authoritarian model of AI—characterized by pervasive surveillance and state-directed automation—is advancing rapidly.

If the United States and its democratic allies impose heavy bureaucratic friction on AI development in the name of preserving the labor share, we risk ceding global technological leadership. An approach that deliberately slows down automation or restricts commercial surveillance technologies without a binding international framework—which, as Acemoglu admits, is highly unlikely given the current geopolitical climate—could hollow out the very tech industries that underpin Western geopolitical strength. We cannot afford to unilaterally disarm in the AI arms race, even if the domestic consequences of rapid automation are painful. Authoritarian regimes will not hesitate to maximize the efficiencies of AI, and if they dominate the global market, they will export their technological paradigms, surveillance and all.

If we cannot reliably predict which research will help or hurt workers, and if geopolitical realities prevent us from deliberately stifling automation, how do we avoid the dystopia Acemoglu fears? The answer is that we must lean far more heavily on the redistributive and institutional mechanisms that he dismisses as insufficient. Acemoglu argues that shared prosperity based predominantly on redistribution is a "fantasy," insisting that "good jobs" must remain the engine of society. But if AI truly lives up to its promise as a transformative general-purpose technology, the link between human labor and economic output may be permanently weakened.

Instead of trying to micromanage the direction of AI research to preserve the 20th-century model of labor, we must build 21st-century institutions capable of managing a highly automated economy. This means aggressively exploring universal basic income, expanding the social safety net, decoupling healthcare from employment, and fostering new models of collective bargaining that allow citizens to share in the wealth generated by digital capital. The post-war era of shared prosperity that Acemoglu highlights was not just a product of lucky, pro-labor technological developments; it was forced into existence by muscular labor unions, high marginal tax rates, and robust public investments.

We cannot force AI to be inherently pro-labor, but we can build an economy where the automation of human labor is a collective triumph rather than a personal tragedy. Our democratic oversight should not be focused on regulating the algorithms in the lab, but on fiercely redistributing the unprecedented wealth those algorithms will generate in the market.

Daron Acemoglu is right to insist that AI’s future is a political choice rather than a technological fate. The most important contribution of his essay is to move the debate away from two sterile fantasies: that artificial intelligence will either magically deliver abundance if markets are left alone, or inevitably usher in mass unemployment and authoritarian control. The historical record does not support either view. Technologies arrive embedded in institutions, incentives, and social conflicts. They can complement workers or replace them; they can broaden access to knowledge or concentrate power; they can help citizens govern themselves or help states and corporations govern citizens more efficiently.

But if we accept that the direction of AI can be changed, we should be even more concrete about what kind of political economy could change it. Acemoglu emphasizes government funding, professional norms, and democratic oversight. These are indispensable. Yet on their own they may not be enough, because the problem is not only that AI research is pointed in the wrong direction. It is also that the organizations deploying AI often have too little accountability to the people most affected by it. The crucial missing actor in much of today’s AI governance is not merely the state or the enlightened researcher, but the worker, the patient, the student, the tenant, the defendant, and the citizen whose life is reorganized by algorithmic systems.

Consider the workplace. Acemoglu’s distinction between automation and technologies that create new tasks is powerful, but in practice the boundary between the two is often determined after invention, at the point of deployment. A scheduling algorithm in a hospital can be used to deskill nurses, intensify shifts, and monitor “productivity” minute by minute. The same broad family of tools could also reduce administrative burdens, flag dangerous understaffing, and give nurses more time with patients. A warehouse robot can be introduced as a partner that reduces injury and expands the range of work humans can safely perform, or as part of a surveillance regime that pushes workers to meet machine-set rates until their bodies give out. The technology matters, but so do bargaining power, job design, and the right to contest managerial decisions.

This is why labor institutions should be treated not merely as compensation for technological disruption, but as part of innovation policy itself. Strong unions, works councils, sectoral bargaining, and worker representation on corporate boards can affect which technologies firms demand and how they are implemented. Germany’s system of codetermination is not perfect, but it illustrates a broader point: when workers have a formal voice, firms are more likely to pursue productivity-enhancing adjustment rather than simple labor shedding. In the United States, where private-sector union density has collapsed, employers face fewer internal constraints when they adopt software that fragments jobs, classifies workers as contractors, or uses algorithmic management to shift risk downward. Redirecting AI therefore requires rebuilding institutions of worker voice, not only changing tax incentives for capital.

The same applies outside the workplace. In education, Acemoglu imagines AI that helps teachers adapt instruction to students. That is a worthy goal. But many educational technologies already promise personalization while producing data extraction, standardized nudging, and new forms of vendor dependence. A school district under budget pressure may adopt AI not to empower teachers but to reduce staff, outsource curriculum, or monitor students in ways that disproportionately affect poor children and children of color. The question is not simply whether “AI in education” complements teachers in the abstract. It is who procures it, who evaluates it, who owns the data, whether teachers can refuse harmful tools, and whether parents and students have meaningful rights.

Health care presents a similar dilemma. AI systems may improve diagnosis, triage, and care coordination. They may also encode existing inequalities if trained on biased data, deny care through opaque risk scores, or become another layer of administrative control imposed by insurers and hospital systems. The history of American health care should make us cautious about assuming that productivity gains will automatically serve patients or clinicians. Without institutional safeguards, AI may optimize billing, throughput, and liability avoidance rather than healing.

For this reason, democratic oversight must be more than periodic public outrage after scandals. It must be built into procurement, auditing, liability, and rights. Governments should require algorithmic impact assessments before public agencies deploy high-stakes AI systems in policing, welfare, immigration, education, and health. Affected communities should be able to examine, challenge, and halt systems that fail basic standards of fairness, accuracy, and necessity. There should be clear lines of responsibility: agencies and firms should not be allowed to hide behind trade secrecy when an algorithm denies benefits, misidentifies a suspect, or determines workplace discipline. Transparency alone is insufficient if no one has the power to act on what transparency reveals.

Acemoglu is also right that surveillance is not an accidental side effect of AI but one of its most profitable and politically attractive uses. Facial recognition, predictive policing, targeted advertising, and behavioral analytics all grow out of a business model and a governing model that treat human activity as raw material. The response cannot be limited to better technical privacy tools, valuable though differential privacy and cryptography may be. Some uses should simply be prohibited. Democratic societies should be willing to ban real-time facial recognition in public spaces, restrict biometric databases, prevent employers from continuous worker surveillance, and sharply limit behavioral microtargeting in politics. If certain technologies are structurally hostile to freedom of association, protest, and equality before the law, then “responsible use” may be the wrong frame.

At the same time, I would qualify Acemoglu’s skepticism toward redistribution. He is correct that redistribution cannot substitute for meaningful work, social status, and political voice. A society of billionaire-owned robots plus universal basic income would not be a healthy democracy if most people had no role in production or collective decision-making. But redistribution and predistribution are not rivals. Public health insurance, child allowances, wage subsidies, paid leave, and stronger unemployment insurance can increase workers’ bargaining power and reduce desperation in the face of technological change. A person who can leave a bad job is better positioned to resist degrading technology. A community with fiscal capacity is better able to invest in complementary tools. The safety net is not only a mop for market outcomes; it is part of the structure that shapes them.

We should also avoid nostalgia for the postwar compact. Its achievements were real: rising wages, strong productivity growth, and a broad sense of economic inclusion for many workers. But that compact excluded many people, especially Black Americans, women, migrant workers, domestic workers, and agricultural laborers. Some of the “good jobs” of the era rested on racial hierarchy, gendered unpaid labor, and environmental destruction. The goal should not be to restore the mid-twentieth century, but to build a more inclusive version of shared prosperity suited to a service-heavy, care-intensive, decarbonizing economy. AI could help with that project, but only if care work, teaching, maintenance, and public administration are treated as sites of innovation rather than as cost centers to be squeezed.

The international challenge is real, but it should not become an excuse for paralysis. The United States and Europe have market power, research institutions, standards-setting capacity, and public procurement budgets. They can condition government contracts on labor-complementing design, privacy protection, and auditability. They can restrict exports of surveillance tools to authoritarian governments. They can fund open, public-interest AI infrastructure so that universities, nonprofits, small firms, and public agencies are not wholly dependent on the priorities of a few dominant platforms. They can use antitrust law to reduce the concentration of data, talent, and cloud infrastructure that gives today’s technology giants such influence over the research agenda.

Ultimately, the question is not whether AI will be “good” or “bad.” It is whether democratic societies can still make collective choices about powerful technologies before those technologies narrow the space for choice. Acemoglu’s essay rightly calls for a redirection of AI. That redirection should be understood as a broader democratization of technological development: public funding with public obligations, worker voice in deployment, enforceable rights for affected people, limits on surveillance, and institutions that reward complementing human capabilities rather than bypassing them. Acemoglu's Diagnosis Is Right; His Remedy Is Too Modest

Daron Acemoglu has written a clarifying essay, and its central insight deserves to be repeated until it lodges in policy debates: the direction of technological change is a choice, not a fate. For decades, economists treated technology as a kind of weather system, an exogenous force whose distributive consequences we could only try to soften through redistribution after the fact. Acemoglu's career-long argument that "technology is endogenous"—shaped by tax incentives, research subsidies, market structure, and prevailing norms—is one of the most important corrections to that complacency. His diagnosis of where AI is currently headed is also largely correct: too much algorithmic automation of routine tasks, too little investment in technologies that genuinely augment human capabilities, and a frightening expansion of surveillance infrastructure.

Where I part company with him is on the remedy. His three-pronged program—government policy, shifting norms, and democratic oversight—is correct as far as it goes, but it treats the problem as primarily one of technological direction, when the underlying problem is the distribution of power. You cannot redirect AI without first redirecting who has standing to make decisions about it, and you cannot expect a research community largely captured by seven or eight companies to suddenly develop a conscience because of "norm change." Norms change when power changes.

Consider Acemoglu's own historical account. The postwar compact he describes did not arise because mid-century engineers were more enlightened than today's machine-learning researchers. It arose because a militant labor movement, a New Deal regulatory state, wartime planning institutions, and a credible socialist alternative abroad forced capital into a corner. Henry Ford did not pay five dollars a day out of solidarity. The "good jobs" of Les Trente Glorieuses existed because workers could shut down production, because antitrust was wielded aggressively, and because capital feared something worse if it did not concede. Productivity-enhancing technology followed from this balance of power; it did not create it.

If that reading is right, then Acemoglu's proposed reforms understate what is required. Removing the tax code's bias toward equipment over labor would be useful but marginal. A "measurement framework" for the labor share effect of AI applications is intellectually elegant but politically toothless: any such framework will be gamed by the firms it is meant to discipline, and enforcement will be hostage to the same revolving door he correctly identifies between Big Tech and the universities. Even the climate analogy he reaches for is double-edged. Three decades of carbon measurement frameworks, voluntary norms, and disclosure regimes have produced exactly the kind of incremental progress that is too slow to matter. Real decarbonization, where it has occurred, has come from blunt instruments: feed-in tariffs, outright bans, public investment at scale, and the political defeat of incumbent fossil capital in specific jurisdictions.

What would the AI analogue look like? It would begin with sectoral bargaining. If we want workplace AI deployments to augment rather than displace, the most reliable way to ensure that outcome is to give the workers affected a legally enforceable seat at the table where deployment decisions are made. German codetermination is imperfect, but it has demonstrably slowed the most predatory forms of workplace automation and forced firms to negotiate productivity-sharing arrangements when new technologies are introduced. The U.S. has nothing comparable. A genuine pro-worker AI agenda would build it.

It would also include aggressive structural remedies against the dominant AI firms. Acemoglu notes, almost in passing, that "a handful of tech giants… account for the majority of money spent on AI research." This is not a footnote; it is the heart of the problem. When the same firms set the research agenda, employ the leading academics, supply the compute infrastructure, and capture the rents from deployment, the idea that "norm change" within the research community will redirect them is naïve. Concentrated industries change behavior when they are broken up, regulated as utilities, or subjected to public-option competition. We are long past the point where AT&T-style consent decrees should be on the table for the platform companies.

A serious agenda would treat data as a collectively held resource. Much of what makes modern AI valuable is training data extracted from billions of users who received nothing for it. Treating that data as a commons—governed through data trusts, with enforceable consent and revenue-sharing—would shift the economics of AI development away from surveillance-heavy business models in ways no carbon-style measurement framework can.

And it would invest publicly, at a scale Acemoglu hints at but does not specify. The federal government once funded the development of the internet, GPS, and the transistor. A public AI research agenda comparable in ambition to the postwar NSF and DARPA—oriented explicitly toward augmentation in education, healthcare, and skilled trades—is not utopian. It is what we used to do. The CHIPS-style industrial policy currently being debated for semiconductors should have an AI analogue focused on socially valuable applications.

I anticipate Acemoglu would agree with much of this, and indeed his footnotes gesture in these directions. But the essay's rhetorical center of gravity is on persuading researchers, policymakers, and citizens to see AI differently. That is necessary but insufficient. The deeper lesson of his own historical analysis is that the postwar compact was built on institutions that constrained capital—and those institutions were won through struggle, not consensus. The civil rights movement did not "shift norms" and then secure voting rights; it organized, marched, faced fire hoses, and forced the issue.

The same will be true of AI. We will not get a humane technological trajectory because Stanford computer science departments adopt better ethics curricula, or because IBM declines to sell facial recognition to police departments for a few news cycles. We will get it because gig workers unionize, because copyright holders sue, because regulators in Brussels keep tightening the screws, because antitrust enforcers in Washington remember how to use the tools they have, and because the workers whose jobs are being algorithmically reshaped insist on a voice.

He suggests that even a U.S. democratic awakening might fail because China and other countries will keep developing surveillance and automation AI. But regulatory leadership cascades. The EU's GDPR has reshaped global data practices not because Europe could force compliance everywhere, but because firms operating at scale find it cheaper to adopt the strictest applicable standard. A serious U.S. or EU regime governing facial recognition, workplace surveillance, or algorithmic management would discipline global firms in similar ways. China's domestic surveillance practices are genuinely alarming, but they are not a reason to lower our own standards; they are a reason to raise them, and to make export controls and procurement rules contingent on them.

The essay's final line—"the direction of future AI and the future health of our economy and democracy are in our hands"—is exactly right. But "our hands" must mean something more concrete than the diffuse "we" of policy commentary. It must mean labor unions with bargaining rights over technological change, antitrust agencies with the will to break up platforms, public investment institutions with the budgets to seed alternatives, and democratic publics with enough security and standing to demand all of this. The technology will follow.

The Case Against Redirecting AI

Daron Acemoglu's essay is the latest in a now-familiar genre: the eminent economist who, having identified a genuine social problem, concludes that what is needed is a wiser hand steering the engine of innovation. The argument is presented with admirable clarity and the usual scholarly caveats. It is also, I think, mostly wrong—both in its diagnosis and in its prescription. The market is not doing as badly as Acemoglu suggests, the postwar past is not as glorious as he remembers it, and the cure he proposes is likelier than the disease to leave us worse off.

Start with the diagnosis. Acemoglu rests much of his case on the contrast between rapid postwar productivity growth and the slower growth of recent decades. The implication is that something has gone wrong with the direction of technological change. But the productivity slowdown is one of the most disputed puzzles in economics, and the leading explanations have little to do with AI being "misdirected." They include the natural exhaustion of one-time gains from electrification and the internal combustion engine, mismeasurement of digital services (which are increasingly free at the point of use), regulatory accretion in healthcare and construction, declining business dynamism caused largely by occupational licensing and zoning, and the simple fact that ideas are getting harder to find. None of these point to "too much automation."

Indeed, if anything, we have too little automation, not too much. Healthcare costs are crushing American families precisely because the sector has resisted productivity-enhancing technology for fifty years. Construction productivity is flat or declining. Higher education is more expensive in real terms than it was a generation ago, with little evidence that the product has improved. These are sectors where algorithmic systems—Acemoglu's villain—have made almost no inroads. The problem is the opposite of what he describes.

His historical narrative is also selective. The postwar boom was not produced primarily by a benign technological trajectory. It was produced by the fact that the United States emerged from World War II as the only major industrial economy with its productive capacity intact, while Europe and Japan rebuilt for thirty years under American technological tutelage. The "shared prosperity" of that era was real, but it was also accompanied by structural racial exclusion, the legal subordination of women in the labor market, and the systematic suppression of competition through cartel-like arrangements that we would today consider scandalous. The decline of unions that Acemoglu laments was not simply a political choice; it was substantially driven by the fact that the heavily unionized industries—steel, autos, textiles—lost ground to genuine global competition that benefited consumers worldwide.

More importantly, the postwar compact contained the seeds of its own dissolution. The good-jobs equilibrium Acemoglu admires worked partly because workers had no alternatives and women had few options outside the home. A more open, more competitive, more meritocratic economy looks worse on some distributional measures but is in many ways more just. Acemoglu writes as though we lost something pure; in fact, we traded one set of trade-offs for another, and the trade is not obviously bad.

Now the prescription. Acemoglu wants government policy, funding, and leadership to redirect AI toward "socially beneficial" applications, distinguished by metrics like the labor share of value added. This sounds technocratic and benign, but consider what it actually involves. A government agency, presumably staffed by political appointees, would decide which AI applications are "labor-augmenting" enough to deserve subsidy or favorable tax treatment, and which are not. Researchers and firms would orient their work toward whatever the official criteria reward. The criteria themselves would be set through lobbying processes that, as Acemoglu himself notes elsewhere in the essay, are dominated by the very tech giants whose behavior he wants to change.

This is the basic difficulty of industrial policy that critics from Hayek onward have identified. The state lacks the local knowledge to pick winners ex ante, and the political processes through which it tries to do so are systematically captured by incumbents who know how to write rules in their own favor. The postwar successes Acemoglu invokes—the internet, biotech—are real but selectively recalled. For every ARPANET there was a Synthetic Fuels Corporation; for every NIH there is a list of cleantech investments that wasted billions. We are very bad at this, and the assumption that we will suddenly be good at it when the topic is AI seems unfounded.

The climate analogy he leans on is particularly weak. Climate policy has a relatively clean physical metric—tons of CO2—and decades of scientific consensus around that metric. "Labor-augmenting AI" has neither. The labor share is endogenous to wages, hours, and capital prices in ways that any first-year graduate student could exploit to make almost any technology look good or bad. Firms would optimize for the metric, not the underlying goal, and we would end up subsidizing technologies that game the measure while doing nothing useful.

There is also a deeper objection. Acemoglu treats automation as if displacement were a uniquely modern wound. But the entire history of economic progress is the history of automation. Agricultural productivity gains displaced 95 percent of the workforce that used to farm; this is the foundation of modern prosperity, not a tragedy to be undone. The reason that displacement was bearable was not that policymakers cleverly engineered "new tasks" but that markets, over time, reorganized to put displaced labor to use—often in ways no central planner could have imagined. The same will happen with AI, provided we allow it to.

What Acemoglu calls the "market illusion" is, on closer inspection, the recognition that no one is smart enough to plan a technological trajectory. The right policy response to AI is not to redirect research toward state-approved goals but to address the actual problems caused by transitions: better unemployment insurance, portable benefits, wage insurance for displaced workers, and serious reform of land use and licensing rules that prevent labor from moving to where the new jobs are. Where AI is genuinely abused—facial recognition for state repression, deepfakes used for fraud—we should regulate the abuse, narrowly. We should not turn the National Science Foundation into an industrial planning ministry on the strength of a labor-share statistic.

I share Acemoglu's concern about surveillance, though I would note that the most aggressive surveillance is conducted by governments, including democratic ones, and that the proposed cure—giving governments more power over the AI research agenda—does not obviously alleviate that problem. Concentration in the tech sector is a real issue, but the remedy is antitrust enforcement against specific anti-competitive conduct, not central direction of research priorities.

Acemoglu's essay ends by saying the future is in our hands. So it is. But "our hands" is not the same as the hands of a federal funding agency armed with a labor-share metric. It is the hands of millions of workers, entrepreneurs, and consumers making decentralized choices in a system that, for all its flaws, has produced more prosperity in the past two centuries than the previous two thousand. Let us not, in our anxiety about AI, throw that system overboard for an industrial policy whose record is not encouraging.

What Acemoglu Misses About AI Research Itself

Daron Acemoglu's essay is a useful contribution to a public conversation that desperately needs economists' input. As someone who has spent the last decade working in machine learning, I want to register both my broad agreement with his political-economic framing and some sharper disagreements about how AI research actually works. The disagreements matter because the prescriptive heart of his essay depends on a model of technological choice that I do not recognize from the inside.

First, the agreements. Acemoglu is right that the industry is dominated by a handful of firms with shockingly aligned business interests. He is right that academic AI has been hollowed out by the migration of senior researchers to those firms. He is right that the field has not, on the whole, taken its social responsibilities seriously, and that the current direction of research is heavily biased toward applications that displace rather than augment. And he is right that this is a policy choice rather than a technological inevitability.

Now the disagreements. The central one concerns the binary at the heart of the essay: automation bad, augmentation good. This distinction is conceptually crisp but technically incoherent. Almost every interesting AI system is both, depending on whose job you look at and on what time horizon. The classic example is radiology. A deep learning system that reads mammograms automates a task that a radiologist used to perform. But it also augments the radiologist by handling routine cases so that human attention can be focused on hard ones. Whether it ends up "destroying" radiology jobs or "enhancing" them depends not on the technology but on staffing decisions made by hospital administrators, on reimbursement rules set by Medicare, on the medical malpractice regime, and on the training pipeline for new radiologists. The same model deployed under different institutional arrangements will produce different labor-market outcomes.

This matters for Acemoglu's policy prescription. He proposes that we use the labor share of value added as a metric for distinguishing good AI from bad. But the labor share effect of any given system is determined downstream of the technology itself, by how it is deployed. A system that could be augmenting can be made to be displacing by management decisions; a system that displaces in one firm augments in another. Any policy that tries to channel research funding based on this metric will end up adjudicating not technology but business models. That may be the right thing to do, but it should be called what it is. The lever is labor law and corporate governance, not the research agenda.

Second, Acemoglu underestimates how much the current direction of AI research is dictated by infrastructure rather than ideology. The reason that contemporary AI is so heavily focused on large-scale pattern recognition over text and images is not that researchers decided automation was more interesting than augmentation. It is that we discovered, in the early 2010s, that very large neural networks trained on very large datasets with very large compute budgets produce surprisingly good results on a wide range of tasks. The economic activities most amenable to this paradigm are those for which large labeled or labelable datasets already exist: clicks, queries, transactions, images on the web. The activities least amenable are those for which no such corpus exists—which includes almost all of the augmentation use cases Acemoglu wants more of. Personalized education, real-time clinical decision support, augmented reality for skilled trades: these require data that does not exist at scale and that is expensive to generate. The asymmetry of data availability shapes the field far more than tax policy does.

This implies a different policy lever than Acemoglu's. If we want more augmenting AI, we need public investment in the foundational datasets and benchmarks that augmenting applications require. This is not glamorous work. It involves things like building open clinical-outcomes registries, instrumenting classrooms in privacy-preserving ways, and creating shared evaluation suites for educational AI. The U.S. government did exactly this kind of foundational data work for image recognition (the ImageNet ecosystem was substantially supported by NSF) and for genomics (the Human Genome Project, dbSNP, and so on). The fields that received this investment flourished. The fields that did not, languished. If we are serious about redirecting AI, the answer is to build the corpora and benchmarks that augmenting applications need to exist.

Third, Acemoglu's account of norm change inside the AI research community is overly optimistic in one direction and overly pessimistic in another. The good news is that the field has shifted dramatically in the past five years. Concerns about fairness, accountability, transparency, and safety have moved from the margins to the mainstream. Major conferences now require ethics statements; thousands of researchers signed open letters opposing lethal autonomous weapons; Google researchers publicly opposed Project Maven and prevailed. The "norm change" Acemoglu calls for has already begun, and faster than he seems to recognize.

The bad news is that this norm change has not translated into changed practice at firms, because individual researchers have very little leverage. The asymmetry between what researchers want and what their employers do has produced a generation of demoralized people writing earnest position papers while their work is deployed in ways they did not sanction. The recent firing of Timnit Gebru from Google is the canonical example. Norms inside the research community are not enough when the institutional architecture concentrates decision-making power in a few executive suites. This is an argument for institutional reform, not just normative reform.

Fourth, Acemoglu treats the China question as a coda when it should be central. The technical reality is that the leading Chinese AI labs are highly capable and well-resourced, and their research is substantially open. A U.S. policy that redirects American AI research toward augmentation will not slow the development of surveillance AI globally; it will simply mean that surveillance AI is developed in Shenzhen instead of Mountain View. This is not an argument against American redirection, but it does mean that the surveillance side of Acemoglu's concern requires international governance instruments—export controls, treaties, cross-border standards—that he barely mentions.

Finally, a methodological note. Acemoglu argues persuasively for democratic oversight of AI but does not engage with the technical question of what such oversight could plausibly look like. Most of what AI labs do is invisible to outsiders, even other technical experts. Deciding what counts as "labor-augmenting" or "surveillance" requires either deep expertise or proxies that are easily gamed. The most promising governance models I have seen—algorithmic impact assessments, ex-ante red teaming, mandatory model documentation—exist mostly in academic papers, not in practice. Building the technical capacity for democratic oversight is itself a research agenda, and one that needs the same kind of public investment that Acemoglu wants directed toward augmenting applications.

Acemoglu is right that the future of AI is a choice. But the choice is being made in a hundred small technical and institutional decisions that economists and policymakers rarely see. Closing the gap between the public conversation and the research reality is the precondition for everything else.

The Geopolitical Realities Acemoglu Underweights

Daron Acemoglu's essay devotes one paragraph at the end to "the international dimension." This is a serious analytical error. The international dimension is not a footnote to AI governance; it is the dominant constraint on every policy he proposes. Any account of AI's future that does not begin with great-power competition will produce recommendations that cannot survive contact with reality.

Consider the basic strategic situation as it stands in 2021. The United States and China are the only two countries with the combination of compute infrastructure, data, talent pools, and capital markets necessary to lead in frontier AI. Both governments have explicitly identified AI as a domain of national-security competition. China's "New Generation AI Development Plan," released in 2017, set the goal of global leadership in AI by 2030 and has been backed by hundreds of billions of yuan in state, provincial, and municipal investment. The U.S. response, slower and more fragmented, has nonetheless coalesced around the Defense Department's Joint AI Center, the work of the National Security Commission on AI, and a growing list of export controls on advanced semiconductors and equipment. The third pole, the European Union, has neither the firms nor the compute to lead technically, but is staking a claim to regulatory primacy through the proposed AI Act.

In this environment, Acemoglu's proposal that the United States redirect its AI research toward labor-augmenting applications faces an immediate problem: half the political coalition that would have to support it views AI primarily through the lens of competition with China, and from that perspective, the priority is not labor share but capability. The Pentagon's AI investments are not aimed at making American workers more productive; they are aimed at not losing the next war. The intelligence community's investments are not aimed at protecting civil liberties; they are aimed at signals intelligence superiority over peer adversaries.

You can argue that this securitization of AI is itself the problem—that we should be coordinating with Beijing rather than racing. I have some sympathy for that view, but it is not where the political center of gravity is and not where it is going. The Trump administration's tariffs, the Biden administration's continuation of most of them, the bipartisan support for the Endless Frontier Act and CHIPS funding, and the genuinely worsening conditions inside China (Xinjiang, Hong Kong, escalating threats against Taiwan) have produced a foreign-policy consensus that Acemoglu cannot wish away.

This has several implications his essay does not engage. First, much of the "automation bias" he laments is overdetermined by national-security imperatives. Autonomous systems—for logistics, for surveillance, for warfighting—are precisely what the Pentagon wants. The U.S. government is one of the largest customers of automation-heavy AI in the world. Telling the National Science Foundation to redirect its $700 million AI portfolio toward labor-augmenting applications would barely move the needle against a Defense Department investing many multiples of that in the opposite direction. Any serious redirection has to address the DoD, and any address to the DoD has to grapple with the actual threat environment, not pretend it does not exist.

Second, the surveillance technologies Acemoglu rightly worries about are being developed at scale in China regardless of what the U.S. does. The Chinese surveillance state in Xinjiang is built on a technical stack—facial recognition, gait recognition, voice ID, predictive policing—that exists whether or not American researchers participate. A U.S. moratorium on certain technologies will not slow their development; it will simply concede the technical frontier to Chinese firms whose products are then exported, often through Belt and Road infrastructure deals, to authoritarian governments across Africa, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Latin America. This is happening now. Hikvision and Dahua surveillance cameras are deployed in roughly 180 countries. The right American response is not unilateral restraint; it is a combination of export controls on the relevant inputs (advanced semiconductors, lithography equipment), sanctions on firms tied to human rights abuses (the Entity List is being used for exactly this purpose), and the development of competing technical standards through international bodies.

Third, the comparison to climate policy that Acemoglu reaches for actually cuts against him on the international question. Climate is a coordination problem with a common physical metric and decades of multilateral institutional development. Even there, progress has been agonizingly slow precisely because of the collective-action problem: countries fear being undercut by free riders. AI competition is worse on every dimension. There is no equivalent of CO2 ppm; there is no IPCC; there is no Paris Agreement; the leading competitor is a one-party state with no interest in transparent compliance; and the technology in question confers immediate military and economic advantage to defectors. If we cannot coordinate on carbon, we will not coordinate on AI.

This does not mean nothing can be done. It means the achievable agenda is narrower and harder than Acemoglu suggests. Realistic priorities include: (1) hardening democratic institutions against AI-enabled foreign interference, which requires platform regulation and election security investments at scale; (2) building coalitions of like-minded democracies—the G7, the OECD, perhaps a "Tech 10"—that can set technical standards on facial recognition, biometric data, and algorithmic accountability that have enough collective market power to shape global firm behavior even without Chinese participation; (3) maintaining the U.S. technological edge in critical inputs through CHIPS-style industrial policy and immigration reform that retains foreign technical talent; and (4) pursuing the narrowest possible bilateral arms-control conversations with Beijing on the highest-stakes military applications, such as nuclear-weapons command-and-control.

Acemoglu's domestic agenda—labor-augmenting research priorities, antitrust against the platforms, democratic oversight—can fit inside this larger geopolitical frame, but only if it is presented as part of the case for democratic resilience rather than against the security state. American workers will not benefit from a Chinese-led global AI order. American democracy will not benefit from Beijing setting the international rules for facial recognition. The labor-augmenting AI Acemoglu wants is a complement to, not a substitute for, the AI capabilities the U.S. needs to maintain a favorable strategic balance.

A final point. Acemoglu writes as though democracy and AI development are in tension, with the latter threatening the former. But the historical pattern is more complicated. Democracies have been the source of almost every important AI innovation. Open scientific publication, contested funding processes, immigrant talent, and academic freedom have proven enormously productive. The Chinese system is catching up partly by free-riding on this openness. If democracies tighten too much, we may slow ourselves more than we slow our competitors. The challenge is to preserve openness for civilian research while restricting it for the narrow set of capabilities with direct military or repressive applications. That is a hard line to draw, but it is the actual policy problem—not the universal redirection Acemoglu proposes.

The future of AI is in our hands, as he says. But "our" hands include the hands of a strategic competitor that does not share our values, and any policy framework that pretends otherwise will fail.

We Need More Redistribution, Not Less

Daron Acemoglu's essay contains a passage that surprised me and, I suspect, will surprise many readers sympathetic to his broader argument. After making a powerful case for redirecting AI toward labor-augmenting applications, he turns to redistribution and dismisses it: "building shared prosperity based predominantly on redistribution is a fantasy," he writes. Tax-and-transfer schemes are "supporting pillars," not "the main engine." Jobs, especially good jobs, are what matters.

I want to push back. The dismissal of redistribution rests on assumptions about work, dignity, and political economy that deserve more scrutiny than they receive in the essay. And the conclusion—that we should focus our policy energy on technology direction rather than transfers—gets the priorities backwards. The most reliable path to a humane AI transition runs through dramatically more redistribution, not less.

Begin with the empirical claim. Acemoglu suggests that postwar shared prosperity was built on jobs rather than transfers, citing the Nordic countries as exemplars. But this misreads Nordic political economy. The Nordics are extraordinary redistributors. Denmark's tax-to-GDP ratio is 46 percent. Swedish social spending is 26 percent of GDP. The Finnish state delivers free university education, generous parental leave, universal childcare, and tuition-free higher education to all citizens. The famous Danish "flexicurity" model couples weak employment protections with extraordinarily generous unemployment insurance (up to 90 percent of previous wages) and active labor market policies. The reason workers in these countries do not fear displacement is not that they have unusually "good jobs"—though many do—but that the state catches them when they fall and retrains them when they need to move. Take away the redistribution, and the Nordic model collapses.

The same is true of the American postwar period Acemoglu admires. The middle-class prosperity of the 1950s and 1960s was built on the GI Bill, federally subsidized mortgages, public universities funded by states with progressive tax codes, Social Security, Medicare (from 1965), and a top marginal income tax rate that exceeded 70 percent for most of the period. To say that the "main engine" was jobs and that redistribution was a "supporting pillar" obscures the fact that millions of those good jobs existed precisely because the state was directly or indirectly underwriting them—through defense contracting, through highway construction, through veterans' benefits that financed home purchases and educational credentials, through farm subsidies that propped up rural employment, through public-sector hiring at every level of government. The line between "good jobs" and "redistribution" is much blurrier than Acemoglu's framing implies.

Now to the normative claim. Why does Acemoglu privilege jobs over transfers? His argument has two parts. First, work provides "social purpose" that mere income cannot replace. Second, without meaningful employment for most people, democracy will not work and transfers will not survive politically.

Both claims deserve interrogation. The first leans on a particular sociology of work that may have been broadly true in the postwar economy but is increasingly questionable today. Surveys consistently find that majorities of workers in advanced economies describe their jobs as not particularly meaningful and would happily work fewer hours given the chance. Bullshit jobs, in David Graeber's phrase, account for a substantial share of contemporary employment. The implicit theory that humans need waged labor for dignity is empirically thin, and it is worth noting that no actually-existing society has tested the alternative at scale. The closest experiments—from the negative income tax trials of the 1970s to the recent Finnish basic income pilot—find that recipients do not become listless layabouts. They start small businesses, care for relatives, return to school, and report higher well-being.

The second claim, about political sustainability, is more interesting but cuts in unexpected directions. Acemoglu worries that without good jobs, democracy will not produce or sustain redistribution. But this gets the causality backwards in important ways. The most generous welfare states in the world were built not by people with good jobs voting for transfers, but by labor and social movements that demanded universal provision as a right of citizenship. The Beveridge Report, the Marshall Plan, the postwar settlement in continental Europe—these were elite responses to existential political pressure from the working class, and they took the form of universal entitlements precisely because targeted, work-conditional programs are politically fragile. Programs for the poor become poor programs, as the saying goes. The most durable redistributive institutions are universal: Social Security, the NHS, Canadian single-payer healthcare, Nordic family policy.

This suggests that the political-economy argument for jobs over transfers has it backwards. If AI displaces large numbers of workers, the political response should be to expand and universalize the welfare state, not to chase a receding horizon of "good jobs" through technology subsidies. A robust basic income, universal healthcare not tied to employment, expanded Social Security, and aggressive public investment in care work (paid at decent wages) would provide a floor under disruption that does not depend on the technical contingencies of whether AI happens to be labor-augmenting or labor-displacing in any given sector.

This matters because Acemoglu's proposed framework—measuring AI applications by their labor-share effect and subsidizing the ones that score well—is going to be slow, contestable, and easily gamed. Even if implemented perfectly, it would take a decade to show effects, during which displaced workers would still need to live. A high-quality, universal safety net is the only humane response to that transition. It is also the precondition for the bolder labor-market reforms (sectoral bargaining, codetermination) that I think Acemoglu would support: workers organize more effectively when they are not desperate.

There is also a deeper issue. Acemoglu's framework implicitly assumes that the labor share is the right normative target. But why? In an economy where most production becomes genuinely automated, why should we want the labor share to remain high? The deeper goal is presumably that broad swaths of the population have access to the income, security, and political voice they need to flourish. If automation increases overall productivity enormously, the natural response is to socialize the gains—through public ownership stakes, sovereign wealth funds, data dividends, and progressive taxation—rather than to deliberately slow the productivity gains to preserve labor's share.

The Alaska Permanent Fund is a small but instructive example: oil rents are partly captured by the state and distributed as an annual dividend to every resident. Norway's sovereign wealth fund is a larger one. If we think AI will generate huge productivity gains concentrated in a few firms and their shareholders, the response should include mechanisms for ensuring the broader public has a claim on those gains. A data dividend, a public-stake provision in publicly funded AI research, or a steep tax on automation profits earmarked for a citizens' fund would all do more for shared prosperity than tinkering with the labor share would.

Acemoglu and I want similar things: an AI transition that does not leave huge populations stranded, a democracy resilient against new forms of capture, and a political economy in which the gains from technological progress are broadly shared. We disagree about the means. He bets on jobs; I bet on universal entitlements and socialized productivity gains. His bet, I fear, defers the security people need now in pursuit of a labor market that may never return.

The Democratic Crisis Is the Crisis

Daron Acemoglu's essay treats the threats to democracy and the threats to labor as parallel problems, two arms of the same AI octopus. I want to suggest a sharper claim: the democratic crisis is the master problem, and everything else—including the labor-market disruption he describes—follows from it. Get democracy right and the rest is tractable. Get democracy wrong and no amount of cleverness about labor-augmenting AI will save us.

Consider the asymmetry between the two challenges. A misdirected AI labor market produces lower wages, more inequality, and political resentment—all serious harms. But these harms are reversible. The 19th-century industrial economy went through similar disruptions and was eventually disciplined by union organizing, progressive taxation, and the regulatory state. The harms were real, but the political system that produced the remedies remained functional. People could vote, organize, publish, and protest. The feedback loop between popular grievance and political response, however imperfect, worked.

A democratic crisis is different. If the institutions through which we make collective decisions are themselves degraded—captured by oligarchs, polluted by disinformation, surveilled into self-censorship, or simply broken by polarization—then we lose the ability to respond to anything, including the labor-market problems Acemoglu describes. Democracy is not just one valuable thing among many; it is the meta-institution through which we adjudicate everything else. Damaging it makes all our other problems insoluble.

The evidence that AI is doing exactly this damage is, I am afraid, mounting fast. Acemoglu mentions the Cambridge Analytica scandal and Facebook's algorithmic amplification of polarizing content. These are not isolated incidents; they are features of an attention economy that has emerged in the last fifteen years. Recommendation algorithms optimize for engagement, which empirically correlates with outrage, fear, and tribal identity. The result is information environments in which calm, fact-based deliberation cannot compete with rage and conspiracy. The January 6 attack on the U.S. Capitol was not produced by AI alone, but it would have been unimaginable without the recommendation systems that channeled tens of millions of Americans into self-reinforcing alternative-reality bubbles over the preceding five years.

Surveillance is the other half of this story, and the more alarming half. We are now living through the construction, in plain sight, of an infrastructure for monitoring every aspect of human activity. The components are familiar: facial recognition in public spaces, license-plate readers, smart-home devices, cellphone location tracking, social-media monitoring, biometric employee surveillance, and—coming fast—aggregation services that fuse these data streams into comprehensive profiles. China's social-credit system is the most visible example, but the United States is building something not entirely unlike it through the private sector, with very little democratic discussion. The capabilities now exist, in commercial form, to track individuals' political opinions, religious practices, sexual behavior, medical conditions, and personal relationships at granular scale.

Why does this matter for democracy? Because the democratic citizen presupposed by liberal political theory is one who can think, speak, and act without being monitored by powerful actors who may retaliate. Once that condition fails, the social fabric on which democracy depends—the spaces of free conversation, dissent, association, and political experimentation—begins to wither. People do not need to be jailed for their views; they simply need to know that their views are being recorded and could be used against them. This produces a chilling effect that operates through self-censorship rather than overt repression. We can already see this in surveys of journalists, activists, and ordinary citizens who report changing their behavior in response to perceived surveillance.

Acemoglu sees this, but I want to push the implications further than he does. The first implication is that the labor-market agenda has to be subordinated, where conflicts arise, to the democratic agenda. If we have to choose between an AI policy that produces marginally more good jobs and one that produces a marginally healthier information ecosystem, we should choose the latter. The labor market can be fixed through ordinary politics; a degraded politics cannot be fixed at all.

Second, the platform problem is the locus of the democratic crisis, and it requires interventions far more aggressive than anything Acemoglu proposes. The current platform regime—a small number of advertising-funded firms operating recommendation algorithms whose details are trade secrets, shielded by Section 230, and accountable to no one—is structurally incompatible with a functioning democracy. Reforms that have been seriously discussed and should be on the table include: (1) significant amendment of Section 230 to make platforms liable for the specific design choices that amplify particular content; (2) interoperability requirements that allow users to take their social graphs to alternative providers, breaking the network effects that protect incumbents; (3) bans on micro-targeted political advertising; (4) data-portability and data-minimization mandates akin to GDPR but stronger; (5) algorithmic transparency requirements, including the right of independent researchers to audit recommendation systems; (6) outright bans on certain manipulative design patterns, like infinite scroll and engagement-maximizing notifications, in contexts where minors are involved; (7) a public-option social network funded through fees on commercial platforms.

These reforms will be opposed ferociously by the platform companies, which is the third implication: the political economy of reform requires breaking the power of those companies. Acemoglu notes that a handful of firms dominate AI research and the public sphere. He treats this as a problem to be worked around through norm change and government redirection. I think it has to be confronted directly through antitrust. The pending FTC case against Facebook, the Google antitrust suit, the various state actions—these are not distractions from the AI debate; they are at its heart. A platform sector with five or six dominant firms cannot be effectively regulated; their resources for lobbying, litigation, and regulatory capture are too great. Breaking them up is a precondition for the rest of the agenda.

Fourth, surveillance specifically requires legal regimes that do not yet exist. The Fourth Amendment was written for an era of physical searches and has been ill-adapted to an information economy in which the most sensitive personal data is held by third parties and accessed by the state through subpoena rather than warrant. We need a comprehensive privacy statute on the European model, but stronger: hard limits on the collection and retention of biometric data, bans on facial recognition in public spaces (with narrow, court-supervised exceptions), strict purpose-limitation requirements for any commercial data use, and a private right of action for violations.

Fifth, electoral integrity requires updated infrastructure: paper ballots, risk-limiting audits, public funding of campaigns, restrictions on coordinated inauthentic behavior on platforms, and serious cybersecurity investments in state and local election systems. Acemoglu rightly notes that AI threatens democracy in ways we are only beginning to understand. The defensive infrastructure has to be built before the next wave of attacks.

Acemoglu's essay is appropriately worried about democracy but treats the democratic agenda as parallel to the economic one. I think it has to be primary. The good news is that the agenda is fairly concrete; the bad news is that it requires confronting powerful incumbents who have so far succeeded in blocking even modest reforms. Whether democracy survives the AI transition depends less on what we do about jobs than on whether we can find the political will to discipline the platforms and reclaim the public information environment they have privatized.

The Nostalgia Problem

Daron Acemoglu's essay is animated, throughout, by a particular memory: the postwar decades of broadly shared prosperity, secure employment, robust unions, and functioning bipartisan democracy. This is the implicit normative benchmark against which contemporary AI development is judged and found wanting. The argument is that we have lost something and that, through wiser policy, we might recover it.

I want to question this framing. Not because the postwar years were not in many ways admirable—they were—but because nostalgia is a poor guide to policy, and the past Acemoglu invokes is selectively remembered, partially mythologized, and impossible to restore. A future AI policy worth pursuing has to start from where we are and what is now possible, not from a postwar tableau that, even at the time, was less golden than it now appears.

Begin with the demographic and social facts. Postwar prosperity coincided with the legal exclusion of women from large parts of the labor market, the systematic denial of skilled employment to Black Americans, the persistence of formal segregation across much of the country, and the closed-door immigration policy of the National Origins Act, which was not substantially liberalized until 1965. The "shared prosperity" was shared among a narrower demographic group than the present-day workforce. When women, racial minorities, and immigrants are included on equal terms—as they now largely are in formal policy if not always in practice—the same level of compression in the income distribution is harder to achieve, because the population of competitors for jobs is much larger and more heterogeneous. This is not a counsel of despair; it is a reminder that the postwar baseline was not just an institutional achievement but also a demographic accident that we should not, and could not, replicate.

Begin too with the geopolitical facts. American manufacturing dominance in 1955 reflected the destruction of every other industrial economy in the war. Detroit's autoworkers earned middle-class wages partly because they had no global competition. As Europe and Japan rebuilt, and as new manufacturing centers emerged in Asia, the rents that had supported postwar industrial labor began to dissipate. This was not, fundamentally, a failure of American policy; it was the inevitable result of the rest of the world catching up. Any contemporary policy framework that treats the relative wages of the 1950s as a feasible target is implicitly assuming an end to globalization—which neither voters nor the broader economy actually want.

Begin too with the productivity facts. Acemoglu rightly notes the postwar growth in TFP and the slowdown since. But this comparison flatters the past. The postwar years drew on a backlog of pre-war innovations—electrification, the internal combustion engine, the chemical industry, mass production—that were diffusing through the economy. We were exploiting an existing technological frontier, not pushing it forward. The decades since have produced genuinely new general-purpose technologies, but their diffusion takes time, and the measurement of their value is genuinely hard. Robert Gordon's pessimism notwithstanding, it is plausible that we are currently in the middle period of a productivity wave whose impact will look very different in retrospect.

What does all this imply? Several things.

First, the goal of restoring "good jobs" of the postwar variety—stable, full-time, single-employer, lifetime-tenured—is probably unachievable and may not even be desirable. The workforce that wants and needs such jobs is a much smaller fraction of the population than it once was. Many workers prefer flexible, portable arrangements; many would happily work fewer hours; many cycle through multiple careers in a lifetime. Policy should make these arrangements work for the people in them—through portable benefits, sectoral bargaining that crosses employer lines, and unemployment systems adapted to non-linear careers—rather than chasing a model of work that fewer and fewer people want.

Second, the political coalitions that produced the postwar settlement cannot be reassembled. The industrial unions are gone, and they are not coming back; the service-sector workforce is too dispersed, too transient, and too demographically diverse to organize in the old way. New forms of worker representation—platform-cooperative models, sectoral bargaining, workers' councils, fiduciary requirements for pension funds—will have to be invented, not restored. Acemoglu hints at this but his rhetoric keeps gesturing back to a Wagner Act paradigm that is increasingly anachronistic.

Third, the productivity-and-augmentation agenda risks becoming a kind of technological cargo cult: if we can just get the research direction right, the prosperity will follow. But postwar prosperity was not produced by augmenting technology alone; it was produced by augmenting technology in the context of strong unions, accommodative monetary policy, capital controls that prevented offshoring, an internal migration of Black workers from the rural South to industrial cities (which created exploitable labor surplus), and a tax-and-transfer system far more progressive than today's. Take away those features and the same technology would have produced very different distributional outcomes. The "redirect AI" agenda, by itself, will not move the macroeconomic needle.

Fourth—and this is the deeper point—the postwar period is not the only template for a humane technology transition. Other models are available. The Nordic flexicurity model decouples worker security from job security in ways that the Detroit model never did, and it appears to produce better outcomes for both workers and firms. The Singapore SkillsFuture model invests heavily in continuous skill development without trying to preserve specific industries. The German Mittelstand combines high productivity, codetermination, and strong technical training, but in a very different structure from postwar America. The Quebec child-care system shows what a serious public investment in care work can do for women's labor force participation. None of these are reproductions of the 1950s, and all are arguably better suited to the present moment.

Fifth, AI itself offers possibilities that the postwar economy did not. Personalized education could finally break the link between zip code and educational opportunity. Translation technologies could dramatically reduce the friction of immigration. Diagnostic AI could extend health-care access to underserved communities. Augmented-reality training tools could make skilled trades accessible to people without years of apprenticeship. These are not pipe dreams; they are imminent possibilities. A future-oriented agenda would aggressively pursue them rather than pining for a manufacturing employment level that 1965 will never see again.

Sixth, the political pessimism in Acemoglu's essay—the worry that democracy is too weakened to act—itself owes something to the nostalgic frame. By comparing today's polarized politics to the bipartisan postwar consensus, the recent past looks uniquely degraded. But the postwar consensus was itself an unusual moment, produced by the existential pressures of war and Cold War, and underwritten by the racial and gender exclusions noted above. Compared to other periods of American history—the Gilded Age, the partisan press wars of the 19th century, the political violence of the 1960s and 70s—contemporary polarization is unpleasant but historically unexceptional. The democratic agenda is hard but not as hopeless as the comparison to 1955 suggests.

I share Acemoglu's commitment to a humane AI transition. I just think we get there by looking forward, not back. The postwar era was an unrepeatable moment, and the policy frameworks suited to it will not fit the very different society we now are. A future-facing agenda would invest in care work, in continuous education, in portable benefits, in new forms of collective bargaining, in public-option services, and in the deployment of AI for the underserved. That is a less nostalgic vision than Acemoglu's, but I think it is a more useful one.

What about the Care Economy?

Daron Acemoglu's essay is a contribution I welcome, but reading it I was struck by a curious absence. For 6,500 words about the future of work and shared prosperity, there is almost nothing about the largest, fastest-growing, and most labor-intensive sector of the modern economy: care work. Education gets a paragraph. Healthcare gets a paragraph. Childcare, eldercare, social work, home-health aides, and the entire infrastructure of social reproduction get almost nothing. This is a serious gap, because it is precisely in care work that the choices Acemoglu wants us to make about AI will be most consequential—and where the binary of "automation" and "augmentation" is most likely to mislead us.

Consider the demographics. The U.S. workforce is aging. By 2030, one in five Americans will be over 65. Demand for home-health aides is projected to grow by more than a third over the next decade, making it the largest source of net job growth in the entire economy. Childcare and pre-K education are similarly expanding, both because labor-force participation by mothers continues to rise and because we are belatedly recognizing the developmental importance of the first five years. The jobs that AI will or will not "augment" over the next twenty years are disproportionately care jobs. Whatever we decide—or fail to decide—about AI in these sectors will shape the working conditions and wages of tens of millions of workers, most of them women, and many of them women of color.

Acemoglu's framework, oriented as it is toward "productivity" and the "labor share of value added," struggles to handle care work for a fundamental reason: care work is not productivity-improvable in the same way that manufacturing is. The "output" of a home-health aide is a frail elderly person who feels cared for, eats regular meals, takes their medications, and avoids hospitalization. The "output" of a kindergarten teacher is a class of five-year-olds who learn to share, to attend, to read. These outputs are not amenable to the kind of scaling that defines productivity growth in goods production. As William Baumol pointed out half a century ago, sectors like these face a "cost disease": their relative cost rises over time because their productivity cannot keep pace with the rest of the economy, while their wages must rise enough to retain workers who could go elsewhere.

This has two implications that Acemoglu's essay does not address.

First, the labor share framework is the wrong lens for care work. In a sector where productivity gains are intrinsically limited, the labor share will always be high, but this tells us nothing about whether the work is being done well or whether workers are being paid fairly. Currently the labor share in U.S. home-health care is very high, but home-health aides earn a median wage of less than $13 per hour, frequently with no benefits and no scheduled hours. The labor share is misleading because the value the workers produce is captured by households (in the form of below-market-rate services) and by Medicare and Medicaid (in the form of suppressed reimbursement rates). What matters in care is not the labor share but the wage level and the quality of working conditions, and these are set by labor law, immigration policy, public funding, and union density, not by the labor-displacing or labor-augmenting character of technology.

Second, AI in care work raises questions that the automation-versus-augmentation binary cannot resolve. Consider a "smart" home-monitoring system that tracks an elderly person's movements, medication compliance, and vital signs. Is this automation (replacing the human aide who would otherwise check in) or augmentation (giving the aide better information about her client)? The answer depends entirely on how it is deployed. If the system replaces visits, it is automation, and the elderly person loses the human contact that is, for many, the most valuable part of the aide's visit. If it supplements visits, it is augmentation, and the aide can focus her time on what matters most. The same technology, two opposite outcomes—determined by reimbursement rules, staffing decisions, and labor power, not by the technology itself.

A serious AI agenda for care work would start with a different question than Acemoglu's: not "how do we redirect research toward labor-augmenting applications?" but "how do we make care work into work that is dignified, decently paid, and meaningfully supported by technology?" The answer would involve several pieces that the essay does not address.

It would require public investment in care infrastructure. The American Jobs Plan and the American Families Plan currently under discussion include hundreds of billions of dollars for home- and community-based services, universal pre-K, and childcare subsidies. These are exactly the kinds of investments that, combined with sectoral wage standards, would transform care work from precarious low-wage employment into something resembling the "good jobs" Acemoglu admires. Without this public funding, no AI redirection will matter, because the demand for care will continue to be met by underpaid, often immigrant, often informal labor.

It would require sectoral bargaining for care workers. Domestic Workers United and the National Domestic Workers Alliance have spent two decades trying to bring home-care workers under standard labor protections, often against the resistance of employer interests and against the legal exclusion of domestic workers from the National Labor Relations Act. A revitalized labor framework for the care economy would establish industry-wide wage floors, training standards, and collective bargaining rights. This is the closest analog to the postwar industrial unions that Acemoglu invokes, and it is the appropriate model for a 21st-century care workforce.

It would require regulation of care-tech specifically. As AI penetrates eldercare and childcare, we will face questions about whether algorithmic monitoring substitutes for human attention, whether facial-recognition and emotion-detection systems in classrooms are appropriate, and how to balance the privacy of care recipients against the efficiencies of monitoring. The current regulatory framework barely exists. Acemoglu's general worry about surveillance applies with particular force in care settings, where the people being monitored often cannot consent meaningfully (because they are children, or have dementia, or are economically dependent on the household where the system is installed).

It would require a feminist reframing of the productivity debate. The reason care work has been underpaid and undervalued for so long is not a market failure or a technological accident; it is that care has historically been done by women, often within the household, as unpaid labor. When it has been brought into the formal economy, it has been brought in at correspondingly low wages. The story Acemoglu tells about postwar prosperity tends to elide this: the male breadwinner with the good union job was supported by an unpaid female workforce doing care, household management, and emotional labor. A 21st-century version of shared prosperity has to internalize that workload as paid work, with social respect and decent compensation. This is a project not just of redirecting AI but of revaluing labor that has long been invisible.

I do not mean to suggest that Acemoglu would disagree with any of this; my guess is that he would endorse most of it. My point is that the essay's framing—productivity, labor share, automation versus augmentation—is poorly suited to the sector where most of the action will actually be. We need a vocabulary for thinking about care work and technology that does not treat it as a residual category. Without that, "redirecting AI" will mean redirecting toward the sectors where Acemoglu's framework applies most easily—manufacturing, logistics, white-collar professional services—while the largest and most consequential transformation of work happens in care, in the dark.

The future of work is the future of care work. Any AI agenda that does not put care at the center is missing the main event.

The Quiet Case for Optimism

After reading Daron Acemoglu's essay, one could be forgiven for thinking that the only honest response to contemporary AI is alarm. Inequality, surveillance, democratic decay, joblessness, monopoly, capture, militarization—the catalog of harms is dense, and the prescription is a politically difficult program of redirection that Acemoglu himself concedes faces "formidable challenges." I want to suggest, against this prevailing mood, that the situation is less dire than the essay implies, and that the trajectory of AI's development contains reasons for guarded optimism that the essay underweights.

This is not a Panglossian argument that everything is fine. The concerns about facial recognition and algorithmic amplification are real, and the platform economy has produced harms that demand redress. But Acemoglu's essay assembles the dark side of the ledger without sufficient attention to the bright side, and the resulting picture is more lopsided than the evidence warrants.

Consider, first, the trajectory of AI's actual benefits to ordinary people. Over the past five years, we have seen machine-translation systems become genuinely useful, allowing a Spanish-speaking grandmother to communicate with her English-speaking grandchildren, an Iraqi refugee to navigate German bureaucracy, a Chinese tourist to read a Parisian menu. This is a massive humane gain, distributed broadly, with negligible negative externalities. We have seen voice interfaces become accessible to people with mobility impairments and the visually impaired. We have seen image recognition deployed in radiology to detect cancers earlier and in agriculture to reduce pesticide use. We have seen AI tutoring systems—Acemoglu's own example—deployed at increasing scale, with at least suggestive evidence of learning gains. We have seen large language models become useful research assistants, code generators, and writing aids for millions of professionals. None of these uses fits neatly into the surveillance/displacement narrative.

Consider, second, the macroeconomic data. Yes, productivity growth has been slower than in the postwar period, and yes, the labor share has fallen. But the U.S. unemployment rate before the pandemic was at a fifty-year low. Real wages at the bottom of the distribution had begun to rise meaningfully in the late 2010s. African-American unemployment hit historic lows. Labor-force participation among prime-age workers was recovering. This is not the picture of an economy being hollowed out by automation; it is the picture of a labor market reasonably tight, with the technology-driven productivity gains evidently passing through, at least partially, to ordinary workers. The pandemic disrupted this trajectory, but the underlying signals before March 2020 were broadly encouraging, not catastrophic.

Consider, third, the institutional response. Acemoglu writes as if AI's social challenges have produced little serious institutional pushback. But in fact the past five years have seen a wave of regulatory and civil-society activity: the EU's GDPR, the proposed AI Act, California's CCPA and CPRA, the FTC's antitrust cases against Facebook and Google, the Justice Department's case against Google search, the New York City algorithmic-hiring law, multiple state and local bans on government use of facial recognition, the algorithmic-accountability proposals before Congress, the Federal Trade Commission's new enforcement directives on algorithmic harm, and dozens of similar initiatives globally. The institutional immune system is responding. It is responding imperfectly and slowly, but it is responding, and the trajectory is one of increasing regulatory engagement rather than decreasing.

Consider, fourth, the field of AI itself. Five years ago, AI ethics was a fringe specialty within computer science; today it is a major subfield, with dedicated conferences, journals, departments, and hiring lines. Major companies have AI ethics boards (even when these boards function imperfectly, the fact that they exist is significant). Open letters opposing autonomous weapons have been signed by thousands of researchers. Internal worker activism has changed company behavior: Google withdrew from Project Maven, Microsoft and Amazon paused facial-recognition sales to police, IBM exited the business altogether. The "norm change" Acemoglu calls for is happening, in real time, and faster than the political process he hopes will support it.

Consider, fifth, the diversity of AI itself. Acemoglu's essay sometimes treats "AI" as a monolith dominated by surveillance and automation. But the field is actually enormously varied. Climate modeling, drug discovery, protein folding (the DeepMind AlphaFold result is a genuinely transformative scientific advance), materials science, fusion plasma control, accessibility tools, language preservation for endangered tongues, archeological text reconstruction, sign-language interpretation—these are all serious AI research programs whose outputs are broadly beneficial. The picture of an AI field uniformly captured by automation and surveillance is a caricature of the actual research landscape.

Consider, sixth, the historical record. Every general-purpose technology in history has produced anxiety and disruption, and every one has eventually been integrated into productive use with broadly shared gains, though usually with painful transitions. The Luddites had legitimate grievances about the textile machinery of their era, but the long-run consequence of mechanized textiles was a cheaper, more abundant clothing supply that benefited everyone. The early decades of electrification, the automobile, the telephone, and the personal computer all produced predictions of mass unemployment and social decay that did not come to pass. This is not an argument that AI cannot, in principle, be different. But the prior should be heavily weighted toward eventual adaptation rather than permanent disruption, and the burden of proof is on those who claim that this time is different.

What does this more optimistic reading imply for policy? Several things.

First, we should pursue Acemoglu's general agenda—better antitrust, smarter research funding, stronger privacy protections, sectoral bargaining—but with calibrated rather than emergency urgency. The framing of AI as an existential threat to labor and democracy invites overreach, and overreach often produces worse outcomes than the original problem. The EU's GDPR, for all its strengths, has had the unintended effect of entrenching incumbents who could afford the compliance costs. Heavy-handed AI regulation could similarly cement the dominance of the very companies it intends to discipline. Better to regulate specific harms—facial recognition in public spaces, algorithmic discrimination in housing and credit, deceptive deepfakes in elections—than to attempt a sweeping redirection of the field.

Second, we should invest heavily in the institutional capacity to monitor and respond. The FTC, the SEC, OSHA, the Department of Education, and other agencies need substantial technical expertise to enforce existing laws in algorithmic contexts. This is unglamorous capacity-building, but it matters more than grand legislative gestures.

Third, we should bet on the upside. The augmentation use cases Acemoglu wants more of—in education, healthcare, accessibility—are also enormous commercial opportunities. Modest public investment in foundational research and data infrastructure, combined with smart procurement (Medicare reimbursing AI-supported care, the Department of Education funding adaptive-learning pilots, the VA deploying veteran-support tools) would do more to shift the AI frontier toward augmentation than any direct redirection of research priorities.

Fourth, we should resist the temptation to despair about democracy. Democratic institutions have weathered worse than the current moment. The U.S. survived the Civil War, the Gilded Age, the Great Depression, the McCarthy era, and the political violence of the 1960s. The challenges posed by AI are real but tractable, and the public has shown surprising readiness to demand and accept regulation when the harms become tangible.

Acemoglu ends his essay by saying that the future of AI is in our hands. He is right. But the hands holding that future are not just those of policymakers and researchers; they are also those of users, voters, workers, journalists, regulators, judges, and citizens, all of whom are responding, in their own ways, to the challenges he describes. The story of AI is not preordained, and it is not, on the evidence I see, headed in the dystopian direction. Vigilance is warranted; despair is not.

The Climate Analogy Is Worse Than Acemoglu Thinks

Daron Acemoglu's essay reaches, near its end, for a comparison: just as we have begun to redirect technological progress toward low-carbon energy through a combination of policy, norms, and democratic oversight, so can we redirect AI toward labor-augmenting and rights-respecting applications. The analogy is meant to provide reassurance—we have done this before, we can do it again—and a template for action.

I want to challenge this comparison, not to abandon his project but to clarify what it actually requires. The climate analogy is, in important ways, misleading. AI governance differs from climate governance in features that make Acemoglu's prescription harder to implement than he acknowledges. Understanding these differences should sharpen the agenda rather than soften it.

First, climate has a measurement victory that AI lacks. The success of climate policy, such as it is, rests on a clear, physically grounded metric: tons of CO2-equivalent emitted into the atmosphere. This metric is not always easy to measure at the source, but it is conceptually clean, scientifically validated, and aggregable across jurisdictions and sectors. Disagreements exist about how to count, but the underlying quantity is real. Decades of work by climate scientists produced this measurement infrastructure before the political battles began, and that infrastructure is what allows carbon taxes, cap-and-trade systems, and emissions disclosures to function at all.

AI has nothing remotely comparable. Acemoglu proposes the labor share of value added as a candidate metric, but the labor share is endogenous to dozens of variables—wages, hours, capital stocks, organizational form, accounting practices, sectoral composition—that have nothing to do with the technology itself. Two firms deploying identical AI systems can show wildly different labor-share effects depending on their existing workforce composition, their reimbursement rates, their tax planning, and their accounting choices. Any policy that subsidizes "labor-augmenting" AI based on this metric will be a subsidy for accounting cleverness, not for technology direction.

What would be required to give AI policy a measurement foundation comparable to climate? Something like a basic-research agenda within the social sciences, on the order of decades, dedicated to developing valid measurements of AI's labor-market and political effects. This is not a luxury; it is a precondition for the kind of redirection Acemoglu wants. We are nowhere near it.

Second, climate has a stationary target; AI does not. The carbon problem has been understood, in its essentials, for at least forty years. The basic science of greenhouse forcing has not changed materially since the 1980s. This stability has allowed institutions to develop, scenarios to be modeled, and policies to be iterated against a fixed target.

AI is a moving target. The state of the art changes every six months. The applications that exist today were unimaginable five years ago, and those that will exist in 2026 are unimaginable now. Building a regulatory framework against this kind of moving target is extraordinarily hard, because any specific rule risks being either over-broad (capturing technologies that should be encouraged) or instantly obsolete (failing to capture the actual harm). This is a structural problem that no amount of climate-style framework-building can solve. AI governance has to be principle-based and outcome-focused, not rule-based and technology-specific, and this changes what "policy" can look like.

Third, climate has a global cooperation problem that is genuine but bounded; AI's is unbounded. The climate problem is shared—every country emits, every country suffers, and no country can solve it alone. This creates real coordination challenges (free riding, monitoring, enforcement), but it also creates a shared interest that can, with effort, be operationalized in treaties.

AI is not like this. AI is dual-use, with massive military and economic implications that make it the focus of strategic competition rather than cooperation. The U.S. and China are not collaborating on AI governance; they are racing. The leading developers within each country are competing for talent and markets, not coordinating on standards. Climate, even with all its difficulties, has a Paris Agreement and an IPCC. AI has neither and is unlikely to get either in the next decade. The international coordination Acemoglu calls for is not just hard; it is structurally less likely than the climate analogy suggests.

Fourth, the political economy of redirection differs in critical ways. Climate redirection involved displacing incumbents (oil and gas) in favor of new entrants (renewables, EV makers). The incumbents had political power, but they also had geographically concentrated political weakness: their plants and jobs were in specific places, while the costs of their pollution were diffuse. This made it possible, over time, to build coalitions for restraint, especially as renewables became cheaper and produced their own constituencies.

The political economy of AI is different. The incumbents (Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft) are not geographically concentrated and do not have visible local liabilities. Their products are loved by consumers; their political contributions are diffuse and bipartisan; their workforce is mobile and well-paid. The constituency for restraint is harder to build because the harms are diffuse and probabilistic, while the benefits are immediate and tangible. This is the inverse of the climate situation, where harms are increasingly visible (wildfires, hurricanes) and benefits of the status quo are taken for granted.

Fifth, climate has had decades of slow institutional accretion that AI has not. The EPA was founded in 1970; the IPCC in 1988; the UNFCCC in 1992; the Paris Agreement in 2015. The institutional infrastructure for climate policy is now massive and is the product of half a century of effort. AI policy, by comparison, is starting from near zero. The EU AI Act is in draft; the U.S. has no comprehensive AI legislation; the major international bodies (OECD AI Principles, GPAI) are voluntary and toothless. Building the institutional infrastructure for AI governance is itself a project of decades, and we do not have decades.

What follows from all this? Three things.

First, Acemoglu's optimism about redirection is too optimistic about its feasibility. The tools that worked for climate—measurement, treaties, market mechanisms—will work less well for AI, for the structural reasons above. This does not mean we should give up; it means we should pursue different tools.

Second, the practical agenda has to be more localized and more functional than the climate analogy suggests. Rather than trying to redirect "AI" as a category, we should regulate specific high-stakes applications in specific sectors: hiring algorithms in employment, credit-scoring in lending, recommendation systems on social media, facial recognition in policing, autonomous systems in safety-critical settings. Each of these has tractable governance questions that can be addressed with existing legal frameworks (anti-discrimination law, consumer protection, securities regulation, election law). The accumulation of these sector-specific interventions will, over time, shape the broader trajectory of the field.

Third, the most realistic levers are competition policy and procurement. The U.S. government is a massive purchaser of AI through the DoD, the intelligence community, Medicare, and federal contractors. If federal procurement required vendors to meet certain standards—on transparency, on bias auditing, on labor practices—the market for AI would shift quickly. Antitrust enforcement against the dominant platforms would create space for alternative business models that do not depend on surveillance and behavioral manipulation. These are blunt tools, but they have the advantage of being usable within existing legal frameworks and political coalitions.

Acemoglu's diagnosis is right; his analogy is misleading. The path he wants requires recognizing how unlike climate AI really is, and adapting accordingly. Otherwise we will spend years building measurement frameworks and international coordination mechanisms that will not arrive in time to matter.
