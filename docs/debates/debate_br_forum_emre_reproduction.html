Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Merve Emre's essay makes a claim that is both modest and audacious: that all reproduction is assisted, and that the appearance of unassistance is itself a kind of privilege. The modest version is undeniable. Prenatal vitamins, ultrasounds, hospital deliveries, epidurals, formula, pediatricians, parental leave, kin networks — none of these is "natural" in any rigorous sense, and the women who do not need to think about them as technologies enjoy a luxury invisible to themselves. The audacious version goes further: that the line between unassisted and assisted reproduction is not a line at all, only a gradient organized by what a culture has agreed to render unmarked. On this point Emre is largely persuasive, and her case is strengthened by the fact that her four narrators — S, B, K and N, micha cárdenas — keep stumbling into versions of "the natural" even as they undergo procedures that should have inoculated them against it. The discourse is sticky precisely because it is not a lifestyle choice; it is, as Emre says, a political problem.

Still, I want to press on a few places where the slogan does too much work. To say that all reproduction is assisted is true at the level of description but risks flattening distinctions that matter for the politics Emre wants to build. There is a difference between assistance that one can refuse and assistance one cannot conceive without, between assistance an insurer will cover and assistance one finances by borrowing from friends, between the assistance offered to a married straight couple at a teaching hospital and the assistance withheld from a fat lesbian whose obstetrician thinks she should not reproduce. Emre obviously knows this — her essay is structured around such gradations. But the rhetorical move from "all reproduction is assisted" to a politics in which "assisted reproduction" becomes "the preferred strategy by which we fight" elides the question of who pays for the assistance and on what terms. The default of the natural is exclusionary, yes; but the default of the technological is, at present, a market.

This matters because the essay's most vivid set piece — S at the unnamed Silicon Valley biotech, whose $20,000 lifetime cap for elective oocyte cryopreservation was won partly through advocacy by an LGBT employee association — is described as a "happy product of Silicon Valley's marriage of capitalist competition and social justice." Emre largely defends S against Suzanne McGee's Guardian piece, and rightly: McGee's contempt landed on the women rather than the firms. But the defense leaves the structural picture uncontested. Egg freezing as a tech perk is not just covered infertility care extended to gay and trans employees; it is also a benefit whose logic dovetails with employer interests in delaying the childbearing of high-performing women. The two things can be true at once. To name only the emancipatory side of the bargain is to reproduce, in miniature, the universalizing move Emre criticizes in feminist manifestos: a technology calibrated to one set of bodies and budgets is offered as a model for reproductive freedom in general.

I think this is why Dorothy Roberts hovers a little uneasily at the end of the essay. Roberts's reproductive justice framework, developed in *Killing the Black Body* and elaborated by SisterSong and others, is not primarily about access to ART. It is about the long American history of forced sterilization, the criminalization of pregnancy among Black and poor women, the prosecution of drug-using mothers, the family-policing functions of the child welfare system, and the disproportionate maternal mortality of Black women — a figure that, by 2018, is roughly three to four times that of white women in the United States. The positive right to have children and raise them in safety is, for the women Roberts writes about, mostly threatened not by the discourse of the natural but by the state and the carceral apparatus that adjoins it. Emre cites Roberts in her closing pages, but the four narratives that organize the essay sit at some distance from Roberts's archive. S, B, K, N, and cárdenas are not interchangeable — cárdenas in particular speaks from inside the violence Roberts describes — but the gravitational center of the essay is the professional woman in a coastal city navigating an under-insured but still navigable system. A politics that wants the technological to catch up to the political will need to be honest about whose technological catching-up is at stake.

The other place I want to push is on the persistence of the biological child as object of desire in nearly every story Emre tells. S has fifteen frozen eggs and a clear path to adoption or donor embryos and yet wonders why she can't "just have the baby." B considers adoption, attends a ten-week foster training, and decides against it because she does not want to rehearse loss. N and K, lectured against biological reproduction, double down on it. cárdenas, the most theoretically radical of the four, banks her sperm and writes movingly about making babies with queer trans bodies. Emre presents this as evidence that bodies are unruly sites for politics — that belief and behavior do not always align — and that this unruliness is itself a kind of anti-naturalist truth. I find this generous and largely right. But it raises a question the essay does not quite answer: if the discourse of the natural is as ideological as Emre argues, why is the wish for genetic continuity, for the child who is "a mixture of us two in human form," so resilient even in those most equipped to see through it? One answer, congenial to Emre, is that ideology runs deep. Another, less congenial, is that the desire for biological kinship is not reducible to ideology, and that an emancipatory politics of reproduction will have to make space for it rather than treat it as residue to be cleared away.

None of this is to argue against the essay's central move. The redescription of "natural" reproduction as merely a form of assistance whose costs are socially absorbed is exactly the kind of conceptual work feminism needs. It is to argue that the slogan, to do the political work Emre wants it to do, has to carry within it the question of who is assisted, by whom, on what terms, and at what cost. Otherwise "all reproduction is assisted" becomes a description of the present rather than a demand on the future.

The lead essay’s insistence that all reproduction is assisted is a bracing intervention. It exposes the ideological work performed by the term “natural,” revealing how it erases the infrastructures—medical, legal, economic, technological—that make parenthood possible for some while rendering others’ choices suspect. By centering narratives of egg freezing, IVF, and queer family-making, the author demonstrates that the binary of natural versus artificial is not merely a philosophical error but a mechanism of exclusion. Yet as compelling as this reframing is, it risks conflating two distinct feminist claims: the denaturalization of reproductive labor, and the embrace of technological solutions as inherently liberatory. In the process, the essay sometimes risks dismissing the political valences of embodied experience and underestimating the way capitalist logics can co-opt even the most emancipatory technologies.

The essay’s critique of “natural” childbirth discourse is well aimed when it targets the consumerist, often white, appropriation that transforms birth into a lifestyle performance. But it is worth recalling that appeals to the “natural” have not always been reactionary. The 1970s women’s health movement, exemplified by *Our Bodies, Ourselves*, reclaimed birth as a physiological process precisely to wrest control from a medical establishment that pathologized female bodies and treated them with casual violence. The insistence on natural childbirth was, for many, a fight for bodily autonomy and informed consent—not a retreat into Rousseauian fantasy. The problem with contemporary natural-parenting culture is not its insistence on embodiment but its transformation of that insistence into a consumer choice available only to the privileged. The essay’s blanket dismissal of the “Great Experience” rhetoric thus threatens to silence women who find genuine agency in resisting medicalized birth, while failing to distinguish between structural critique and individual empowerment.

More troubling is the underlying optimism about reproductive technologies as routes to justice. The stories of S, B, and N and K are harrowing and revealing, but they also illustrate the paradox of such “assistance”: it often intensifies bodily discipline and market dependence. S’s egg-freezing perk, born of a push for LGBT equality, ended up extracting from her a punishing regimen of daily injections, constant monitoring, and an emotional accounting that the essay describes as “shackled to the specter of the natural.” The fact that her employer offered the benefit to compete with Google and LinkedIn—not out of any structural commitment to reproductive justice—should give us pause. By 2018, we have seen how corporations use “fertility benefits” to extend their hold on workers’ lives, encouraging delayed childbearing not as a genuine choice but as a way to maximize productive years. When the response to structural inequality is more technology rather than shorter work weeks, universal healthcare, or paid parental leave, we risk legitimizing the very conditions that make child-rearing so difficult in the first place.

The essay acknowledges these structural constraints but ultimately frames the answer as broadening access to assisted reproduction rather than questioning the model of reproduction as a private, technologically mediated endeavor. Yet the desire for a biological child, as the author notes, is not rational; it is shaped by norms that can be as coercive as the natural/artificial binary itself. The fear that a child is “not yours” without a genetic link—a fear that haunts B and drives K and N to undergo repeated transfers—is itself a residue of a culture that equates kinship with biogenetic substance. Expanding IVF coverage without challenging that equation does not dissolve the hierarchy; it merely invites more women to climb its rungs. Reproductive justice, as Dorothy Roberts and others have insisted, demands more than the right to choose among existing technologies; it requires transforming the social conditions that make some children desirable and others disposable, some families “natural” and others suspect.

This is not to denigrate the lives and hopes documented in the essay. The women’s voices are a necessary corrective to abstract manifestos. But their stories also reveal that the line between liberation and discipline is thin. When K feels her body become an alien territory of hormones, when B calculates paid leave to the day, the assistance they receive is inseparable from the very structures that constrain them. If all reproduction is assisted, then the political task is to distinguish between forms of assistance that enlarge collective care and those that simply reinsert women into hierarchies of market and medicine. The call for universal access to reproductive technologies must be paired with a far more robust fight for universal childcare, anti-discrimination protections, and a living wage—things that would make the experience of reproduction less “haunted” regardless of whether it occurs in a womb or a lab.

The essay’s greatest gift is its insistence that no reproduction is innocent of technology or politics. But we should not let that insight lead us to valorize the technological as inherently feminist. A truly anti-naturalist politics would not simply dismantle the myth of unassisted reproduction; it would build a world in which the assistance we offer one another—whether through midwife’s hands or petri dish—is guided by solidarity, not profit, and by an expansive vision of kinship that owes nothing to the “natural” but everything to justice.

The lead essay brilliantly dismantles the oppressive ideology of the “natural” in our cultural discourse surrounding reproduction. By tracing the genealogy of the artificial womb from early science fiction to the techno-utopian visions of Shulamith Firestone, and by grounding these theories in the lived realities of individuals navigating fertility clinics today, the essay exposes how the rhetoric of “natural” childbirth and conception serves as a cudgel. It operates as an exclusionary, consumerist logic that obscures the profound physical, emotional, and financial burdens of creating a family. The conclusion drawn—that all reproduction is, in fact, assisted, and that recognizing this could foster solidarity across lines of race, class, sexuality, and gender identity—is a vital paradigm shift.

However, while the essay eloquently maps the structural inequalities that shape access to assisted reproductive technology (ART), it places too much faith in the emancipatory potential of the technologies themselves. The author suggests that acknowledging the positive right to reproduce could make assisted reproduction “the preferred strategy by which we fight” for reproductive justice. But as long as these technologies remain deeply embedded in a neoliberal, profit-driven healthcare system, relying on them as a primary engine for political liberation risks generating new hierarchies rather than dissolving old ones. We must fiercely interrogate not only the biological limits of reproduction, but the economic architecture of the industry that purports to assist it.

This tension is most glaring in the essay’s treatment of S, the biotech employee whose egg freezing is subsidized by her Silicon Valley employer. S views her frozen eggs as a “happy product of Silicon Valley’s marriage of capitalist competition and social justice,” a triumph pushed forward by her company’s LGBT association. While we must celebrate the material benefit this provides to queer employees who have historically been denied care, we cannot ignore the broader, more insidious motives of corporate-sponsored fertility preservation. In 2018, the corporate drive to fund egg freezing is less an act of feminist liberation and more a strategy for extracting maximum labor during a worker’s peak productive years.

By offering elective oocyte cryopreservation, tech giants are not dismantling the structural antagonism between work and family; they are merely privatizing its solution. They shift the burden of reconciling career advancement and motherhood onto the individual’s biological timeline, medicalizing what is fundamentally a social failure. Instead of instituting robust paid family leave, shortening the workweek, or providing on-site childcare—policies that demand structural accommodation for parents—corporations offer a technological workaround that keeps employees tethered to their desks. If we embrace this as a victory, we risk conflating corporate optimization with reproductive freedom.

Furthermore, expanding access to ART without fundamentally altering the market logic of the American healthcare system ignores how deeply commodified human reproduction has become. The fertility industry is currently a multi-billion-dollar enterprise characterized by aggressive marketing and stark stratification. If, as the essay suggests, we are to view all reproduction as assisted, we must ask who profits from this assistance and whose bodies bear its hidden costs. The push for technological intervention often relies on a shadowy gig economy of reproduction. Egg donors are recruited on college campuses and paid premiums for specific racial, physical, or educational traits, turning genetic material into a luxury commodity. Surrogates, both domestic and international, contract their bodily labor to gestate children for those who can afford their fees. Simply universalizing access to the clinic without dismantling the capitalist logic that underpins it risks treating human reproduction as just another site of resource extraction, where the bodies of lower-income women are leveraged to assist the family-building of the wealthy.

The essay also glosses over the ways in which the patriarchal dynamics of the “natural” birthing suite have simply been transferred to the fertility clinic. Firestone’s dream of the artificial womb sought to free women from the physical burdens of gestation. But as the narratives of B, N, and K reveal, engaging with ART is an intensely physical, often agonizing experience. From exhaustive regimens of hormone injections to the terrifying risks of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome, the burden on the female body is not erased by technology; it is merely heavily medicalized.

The medical establishment, as these stories show, is still rife with the same dismissiveness and normative assumptions that plague traditional reproductive care. The male doctors who ignored B’s retroverted uterus, or who persistently assumed N and K were just friends rather than partners, demonstrate that clinical spaces are deeply saturated with heteronormative and patriarchal biases. Substituting the tyranny of “nature” with the tyranny of a profit-driven medical establishment is not true liberation. It merely changes the architects of women’s alienation from their own bodies.

Dorothy Roberts’s framework of reproductive justice, cited at the end of the lead essay, offers the correct path forward, but we must remember that Roberts’s vision extends far beyond the clinic. Reproductive justice is not inherently techno-utopian; it is fundamentally about the social conditions of parenting. Safe housing, clean water, freedom from state violence, and a non-discriminatory healthcare system are its bedrock. If we elevate assisted reproduction to our “preferred strategy,” we risk focusing on the high-tech creation of children while neglecting the low-tech, everyday structural supports required to keep them alive and thriving. micha cárdenas’s fear of dying before she can raise a child is a profound reminder of this reality. The technology to create a child matters little if the state refuses to protect the parent’s right to survive.

To acknowledge that all reproduction is socially and medically assisted is an essential step in demystifying the biological family. It validates the experiences of queer, trans, and infertile people, and opens the door to expansive, inclusive kinship structures. But we must be careful not to conflate technological intervention with political emancipation. We are writing in a moment where venture capital is flooding the fertility market and digital apps monetize our menstrual data. Our demand must not merely be for equal access to the fertility clinic. True solidarity requires demanding the complete overhaul of the socio-economic system that makes building and sustaining a family a luxury in the first place.

Merve Emre is right that “natural” reproduction is neither natural nor autonomous. It is scaffolded by pregnancy tests, sonograms, epidurals, antibiotics, neonatal intensive care units, insurance codes, grandparents, paid and unpaid leave, immigration status, marriage law, and the willingness of other people to recognize one’s family as a family. The fiction that some people simply reproduce while others require “assistance” is a political fiction, and it does real harm. It ranks families by presumed authenticity; it makes queer parents, single parents, disabled parents, trans parents, infertile parents, and adoptive parents explain themselves; it turns the desire for a child into evidence of either selfishness or pathology when that desire does not travel through heterosexual intercourse.

But I hesitate at the suggestion that assisted reproduction should become “the preferred strategy” of a feminist reproductive politics. If all reproduction is assisted, then the central feminist question is not whether we should embrace assistance. We already do. The question is: which forms of assistance, controlled by whom, paid for by whom, and at whose risk?

That question matters because “assistance” is not innocent. It can mean a friend driving you home from an egg retrieval. It can mean a state Medicaid program paying for prenatal care. It can mean a paid doula, a lactation consultant, a union contract, a grandmother’s unpaid labor, or a safe apartment with clean water. It can also mean a clinic selling hope at prices most people cannot afford, a resident learning on a patient’s body without adequate consent, an employer offering egg freezing while leaving workplace timelines intact, or a surrogacy arrangement that transfers bodily risk down class and national hierarchies. To say that all reproduction is assisted should widen our field of vision beyond the clinic, not make the clinic the privileged site of feminist imagination.

The stories Emre tells make this plain. S’s egg freezing is not simply a triumph of technology over biology. It is also a story about a workplace that can absorb the cost of preserving reproductive possibility better than it can imagine reorganizing labor so that workers may have children without penalty. B’s IVF is not simply a story about individual choice. It is a story about debt, contingent academic employment, the absence of universal paid leave, and the peculiar cruelty of a medical system in which one can produce embryos before one can secure the conditions to raise a child. N and K’s experience shows that access to reproductive medicine does not by itself undo heterosexism; the clinic can reproduce the very norms it appears to bypass. The doctor who asks for a “sample” from an imaginary husband is not an incidental comic detail. He is part of the technology.

This is why the opposition between “nature” and “technology” can mislead even when we reject the natural. Reproductive technologies are not merely tools that liberate us from biological destiny. They are institutions, markets, protocols, habits of speech, training regimes, liability calculations, and cultural fantasies. They arrive already shaped by race, class, disability, gender, and nation. A feminist politics cannot be simply pro-technology, any more than it can be pro-nature. It must be pro-accountability.

Consider IVF. It has enabled many people to have children who otherwise could not, and any feminist account that treats this desire with contempt is morally unserious. Yet IVF also dramatizes inequality. In the United States, access depends heavily on geography, insurance, employer, marital status, diagnosis, and wealth. The procedures are physically demanding, emotionally consuming, and uncertain. Success rates vary sharply by age and medical history. Patients often pay not only for retrievals and transfers, but also for medications, genetic testing, storage, donor gametes, legal agreements, and time away from work. Meanwhile, the labor of egg donors and gestational surrogates is often discussed in the language of generosity while being organized through markets that depend on unequal bargaining power. Feminists should defend the legitimacy of assisted reproduction without romanticizing the industries that provide it.

The same is true of artificial wombs, whose history Emre deftly excavates. Ectogenesis has long carried both emancipatory and coercive possibilities. It might reduce the dangers and burdens of pregnancy; it might help extremely premature infants survive; it might expand the ways people form families. But in a country where abortion rights are already fragile, the fantasy of fetal transfer could also become a weapon. If gestation can be imagined as separable from the pregnant person, anti-abortion arguments may shift from “you must remain pregnant” to “you must submit to extraction.” The burden would not disappear; it would change form. A technology that appears to free women from pregnancy could, under conditions of reproductive unfreedom, strengthen the state’s claim over pregnant bodies.

This is not an argument against such technologies. It is an argument against assuming that technological capacity generates political freedom. The history of reproduction in the United States gives us little reason for that confidence. Women of color, poor women, disabled women, incarcerated women, and Indigenous women have been subjected to sterilization abuse, coerced contraception, child removal, punitive welfare policy, and criminal prosecution during pregnancy. At the same time, many have been denied infertility care, prenatal care, safe housing, and the resources necessary to raise children. Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework is indispensable precisely because it refuses to isolate the right not to have a child from the right to have one and to parent in safety. That framework should also keep us from treating access to IVF or egg freezing as the horizon of reproductive equality.

The “natural” has indeed done ideological damage. It has made pain virtuous, mothers self-sacrificing, breastfeeding compulsory, vaginal birth morally superior, genetic connection mystical, and heterosexual reproduction ordinary in ways that render everyone else derivative. Yet there are reasons some people reach for the language of nature that are not merely reactionary. For some women, especially those who have been overmedicalized, experimented on, dismissed, or harmed by doctors, “natural” can name a demand for bodily autonomy against institutional arrogance. For some Black women facing stark maternal health disparities, the desire for midwives, doulas, and community birth workers may reflect not consumerist Edenism but a rational distrust of hospitals that do not listen to them. For some disabled people, resistance to genetic screening may arise from the knowledge that medicine often confuses disability with a life not worth living.

The task, then, is not to replace reverence for nature with reverence for technology. It is to make reproduction less governed by reverence altogether. People should not have to prove that their reproductive choices are natural, radical, efficient, selfless, or politically exemplary. They should not have to turn their embryos, pregnancies, abortions, miscarriages, adoptions, transitions, or childlessness into public pedagogy. A lesbian couple should not have to reproduce queerly enough. A trans woman should not have to renounce genetic parenthood to validate her womanhood. A single woman should not have to present IVF as either empowerment or tragedy. A woman who wants an epidural, a scheduled cesarean, donor sperm, no children, six children, or a child genetically related to her should not be conscripted into someone else’s symbolism.

What would better assistance look like? Universal health care that includes contraception, abortion, prenatal care, childbirth, infertility treatment, gender-affirming care, and mental health care. Paid parental leave not tied to a particular employer’s generosity. Childcare treated as infrastructure. Legal recognition of diverse families without forcing parents through expensive, humiliating procedures. Strong protections against pregnancy discrimination and anti-LGBT discrimination. Ethical oversight of fertility clinics, egg donation, embryo storage, and surrogacy. Serious attention to maternal mortality, especially the disproportionate deaths of Black women. Support for disabled parents. A foster and adoption system that does not treat poverty as neglect or biology as destiny. These are not glamorous technologies, but they are reproductive technologies in the broadest sense: social arrangements that make life possible.

Emre’s most powerful claim is that reproduction exposes the insufficiency of choice as a political ideal. The people in her essay make choices constantly, but rarely under conditions they would have chosen. They choose between debt and delay, between bodily risk and lost time, between legal vulnerability and medical alienation, between disclosure and loneliness. Feminism should defend their choices, but it should not stop there. It should change the conditions under which those choices become necessary.

All reproduction is assisted. The next step is to insist that assistance be collective, equitable, and noncoercive. Not because technology will save us from nature, and not because nature will save us from technology, but because no one creates or sustains life alone.

Emre's essay makes a compelling case for recognizing the inherent technicity of all reproduction, but I want to push back on what seems to be its underlying optimism: that once we acknowledge "all reproduction is assisted," we can begin to close the gap between women of different classes, races, and sexes. The problem is that universalizing assistance doesn't dissolve structural inequality—it may simply make it more legible without changing the fundamental distribution of power.

Consider how Emre's own case studies expose this tension. S, the tech worker with fully covered egg freezing, and B, the lecturer financing IVF through small loans from friends, both undergo "assisted reproduction," yet their experiences are shaped by radically different material conditions. The technology may be the same, but the politics couldn't be more different. Emre gestures toward this problem when she notes that B's brother and sister-in-law were "fully covered by their insurance" while B had to borrow money, but she doesn't fully reckon with what this asymmetry means for her argument. If "assistance" is distributed along class lines, then universalizing the concept doesn't liberate anyone—it just reveals the contours of their captivity more clearly.

This is where I think Emre's analysis needs to go further. She quotes Dorothy Roberts on reproductive justice—a framework that encompasses not just the right not to have children but the right to have them in safe and supportive environments—and suggests that acknowledging the positive right to reproduce "could begin to close the gap" between women. But closing that gap requires more than changing how we talk about reproduction; it requires dismantling the economic and legal structures that make some reproductive choices possible and others merely aspirational. Universal healthcare, paid parental leave, robust protections for LGBTQ+ families—these aren't natural extensions of recognizing that all reproduction is assisted. They're political demands that require organizing against interests that benefit from keeping reproduction unequal.

Emre's narrative approach is effective at conveying the experiential texture of assisted reproduction, but it also carries risks. By presenting multiple individual stories, the essay can inadvertently suggest that the problem is one of visibility or attitude rather than structure. The solution might seem to be more stories, more recognition, more acknowledgment of the "unruliness" of bodies. But the women in Emre's essay aren't struggling because their experiences are invisible; they're struggling because they lack resources, legal protections, and time. S can freeze her eggs, but she still has to choose between career advancement and parenthood on someone else's timeline. B can technically afford IVF, but only by going into debt and navigating a medical system that treats her as an unusual case. N and K can pursue IVF, but only because N's family has money to lend.

There's also something to be questioned in Emre's relationship to Firestone's techno-utopianism. She distances herself from Firestone's enthusiasm while endorsing the general project of expanding reproductive technologies as a path to liberation. But Firestone's warning about technology being justified as "lifesaving" rather than "laborsaving" remains pertinent. The history of reproductive technology is not a history of women gaining control; it's a history of medical institutions gaining control over reproduction. Egg freezing is marketed as a "perk" that benefits workers, but it also allows employers to defer questions about parental leave and workplace flexibility. IVF was developed to help "infertile couples," but it has also generated a reproductive industrial complex that profits from desperation. Surrogacy promises to liberate women from gestation, but it also creates new forms of exploitation in which poor women's bodies gestate children for wealthy intended parents.

Emre gestures toward this problem when she notes that "as was the case with many debates over reproduction, what started out as a critique of capitalism quickly became a critique of women's choices." But she doesn't adequately explain why this keeps happening or how to prevent it. The answer, I suspect, lies not in narrative but in political organization. The egg freezing benefit that S helped design wasn't won because tech workers recognized that all reproduction is assisted; it was won because an LGBT association pushed management to address discrimination that kept gay and trans employees from accessing infertility coverage. The lesson isn't that consciousness precedes politics, but that politics requires collective action on material demands.

My concern is that Emre's focus on language and narrative—on how we talk about reproduction—can inadvertently depoliticize the very structural issues she identifies. Calling conception "assisted" rather than "artificial" or "natural" is a small victory. It might change how some women feel about their choices, and that's not nothing. But it won't change the fact that B has to delay implantation to qualify for paid maternity leave, or that K was told she was "too overweight" and too mentally ill to reproduce, or that trans women of color face violence that renders their reproductive timelines contingent on survival rather than choice.

What I want from a feminist reproductive politics is more than recognition of the assisted nature of all reproduction. I want a politics that takes seriously the relationship between reproductive technologies and the structures that distribute them unequally—capitalism, racism, ableism, transphobia. Emre's essay is valuable for insisting that we think about reproduction more capaciously, but thinking isn't enough. We need a politics that can match the complexity of the situations she describes, one that doesn't mistake the acknowledgment of difference for the achievement of solidarity.

In Defense of the Natural (Sort Of)

Merve Emre's essay is a beautifully constructed argument, and it earns its central provocation: yes, all reproduction is assisted, and the unmarked category of "natural" childbirth lets a great deal of ideology smuggle itself in under the cover of biology. I agree with much of her diagnosis. I want to push back, though, on the rhetorical move that runs through the piece — the suggestion that "the natural" is essentially a reactionary fiction, useful mostly as a stalking horse for sexism, racism, and heteronormativity. That move is too clean. It throws away something worth keeping.

Consider what people often mean when they reach for the word "natural" in this context. Sometimes they do mean the moralizing, lifestyle-brand version Emre rightly mocks: the $500 hypnobirthing tutorial, the Edenic prenatal supplement, the Instagram doula in a clean white kitchen. But often they mean something more modest and defensible: a preference for interventions that have been used long enough to be well understood, a wariness of unnecessary medicalization, a desire for continuity with one's body, one's animal life, one's mammalian inheritance. These preferences are not irrational and they are not, intrinsically, anti-feminist. The women who fought to bring midwives back into American hospitals in the 1970s, who exposed the routine over-prescription of episiotomies, who pushed back against unnecessary C-sections — they were not Rousseauean fantasists. They were doing concrete, hard-won feminist work against a medical establishment that had treated childbirth as something to be sedated through.

Emre treats Firestone's "shitting a pumpkin" passage as a kind of foundational truth-telling. It is funny and bracing. But it is also, as a description of childbirth, partial and tendentious. Plenty of women experience labor as painful, boring, traumatic, alienating; plenty of others experience it as something else — intense, transformative, even, yes, meaningful — and these are not all dupes of the School of Great Experience. To grant that some women have been ideologically coerced into glorifying their suffering is not to grant that any positive valuation of embodied gestation is false consciousness. Firestone's contempt for the body has its own ideological history, and it is not obviously more emancipatory than the natural-childbirth pieties she ridicules.

There is also a quieter empirical problem. Emre's frame is "the natural is invisible privilege." But the technologies she profiles are not free of their own asymmetric distributions and harms. Egg freezing is a procedure with modest live-birth yields per thawed egg, performed on healthy women who undergo hormonal hyperstimulation and surgical retrieval. IVF treatments increase the rate of multiple pregnancies, low birthweight, and OHSS — which Emre's interviewee B nearly suffered. Preimplantation genetic screening, which Emre describes with notable tenderness ("I have two girls and a boy"), is an instrument that decides which embryos live, on what genetic criteria, with what consequences for our collective tolerance of disability. To treat these procedures simply as liberatory technologies awaiting wider distribution is to repeat Firestone's optimism rather than to interrogate it.

I want to suggest a friendly amendment. The opposition is not natural vs. assisted; Emre is right that this is a false dichotomy. But the opposition that actually does feminist work is something more like: which assistances do we want, on what terms, for whom, accountable to what? A midwife is an assistance. So is a doula, a birthing pool, a doctor, an epidural, an IUI catheter, a frozen embryo, a paid maternity leave, a co-parent, a state. Some of these assistances are continuous with longstanding human practice; some are very new. Some are administered by communities and kin; some by corporations and clinics. To say "all reproduction is assisted" is true but flattening. The political question is what kinds of assistance we are building, and Emre's strongest examples — N and K, B, micha cárdenas — are not really arguments for more technology so much as arguments for more solidarity, more capacious kin networks, less hostile clinics, more honest doctors. The technology is incidental; the relationships are not.

This matters because the "natural" critique, pushed too hard, has a way of arriving at conclusions that are not actually feminist gains. If the body is just one more substrate to be hacked, why not corporate egg freezing as a perk? Why not gestational surrogacy at scale in countries where women are cheap? Why not commercial gamete markets that disproportionately recruit poor women as donors and wealthy women as buyers? The xenofeminist answer is: universalize access, distribute the technologies more equitably. But the technologies as currently configured carry an economic and ideological freight that more equitable distribution doesn't simply dissolve. They were developed inside an infertility industry that treats women's bodies as raw material, and they reinforce a particular form of life — the late-twentieth-century professional career structured around postponed reproduction — that itself deserves criticism.

This is why I think the most important sentence in Emre's essay is not the title but the line near the end, quoting Dorothy Roberts: reproductive justice is not just the right not to have a child but the right to have children and raise them "with dignity in safe, healthy, and supportive environments." Roberts's framework explicitly refuses the choice between celebrating technology and celebrating nature. It says: the question is whether the material conditions of reproduction — housing, healthcare, paid leave, freedom from violence, freedom from coercive sterilization, freedom from family separation — make raising children possible. From that vantage, the corporate egg freezing perk and the natural-birth Instagram empire are mirror-image symptoms of the same underlying failure. Both are private workarounds for the absence of a society that supports parents.

I do not want to defend the "School of Great Experience." Firestone's mockery still bites, and it should. But I do want to defend a more modest claim: that human beings are mammals, that gestation and birth and lactation are mammalian capacities, that wanting to know and inhabit those capacities — when they are available to you, when you want them — is not, in itself, ideological capture. The natural is a contested word with a long history of misuse, but the body is real, and a feminism that cedes the body to its enemies, or that treats the body as merely a problem to be solved by better engineering, is a feminism with a thin grip on most women's lives.

The right horizon, then, is neither Firestone's ectogenesis nor the lifestyle-natural's aestheticized return to Eden. It is the unglamorous infrastructure of mutual aid Emre's own narratives keep gesturing toward: the friends who drove B home from her retrieval, the queer TTC Facebook group, the supervising ob-gyn who held B's hand, the trans women who taught each other to bank sperm. That is the assistance worth scaling.

Egg Freezing Is Not a Perk

I want to focus on the part of Merve Emre's essay that has gotten the least attention in feminist debate and that, to my mind, deserves the most: the moment when S explains, with no apparent irony, that her frozen eggs are a "happy product of Silicon Valley's marriage of capitalist competition and social justice." That sentence should stop us cold. It is a near-perfect example of how the language of inclusion has been absorbed by, and made indispensable to, the very forms of corporate power feminism once defined itself against.

Let's recover the basic facts about the egg-freezing benefit. When Facebook, Apple, and the rest announced these policies in 2014–2015, they were lavishly praised in the business press as forward-looking pro-woman gestures. But the demographic context was unmissable. Tech firms were under intense public pressure over their dismal gender ratios, their pipeline rhetoric, and their well-documented hostility to women in their thirties — the age at which engineers begin having children and at which female engineers, in particular, leave the industry in droves. The egg-freezing benefit did not address that hostility. It restructured it. It told women: the problem with your career is your fertility, and we will pay to delay your fertility, so you can keep working.

Compare what these companies could have offered instead. They could have offered, as Suzanne McGee pointed out in the Guardian piece Emre dismisses, on-site childcare, generous parental leave, lactation rooms, schedule flexibility, the ability to take a year off without career suicide. These benefits would have made it possible for women to have children when they actually wanted to and remain in the workforce. They would also have cost more, structurally, because they would have required the company to accommodate parents as parents, not as freelancers with extracted ovaries. Egg freezing, by contrast, is a one-time medical reimbursement. It privatizes the cost of having children later and externalizes the risk — physical, emotional, financial — onto the worker herself.

Emre is too quick, I think, to wave away McGee's critique as misdirected at women's choices rather than at corporate policy. McGee is plainly criticizing the policy. And what is being criticized is not whether women should make use of available technology, but whether the technology — at this price, in this institutional configuration — actually represents the choice it is sold as. S is sympathetic and articulate and her sense of agency is real. But her account is precisely the account a successful corporate program of this kind would produce. She does not regret the choice. She also tells us, in detail, about the bruises, the weight gain, the mood swings, the OHSS risk, the anxiety of the daily call, the prohibition on exercise and sex, the not-thinking-about whether the procedure will fail. This is a description of labor — bodily, gendered, unpaid labor — undertaken on behalf of an employer who has bought a future option on her reproductive timeline. It is exactly the kind of labor Firestone wanted to abolish.

The LGBT-inclusion framing of the benefit is, I think, the most ideologically clever part of the package, and Emre does the reader a disservice by accepting it on its own terms. Yes, lesbian and gay employees were locked out of insurance-mandated infertility coverage because they could not produce a diagnosis. Yes, the company's LGBT association pushed for inclusion, and yes, that pressure helped extend the benefit. But the benefit that resulted is not, in the main, a benefit for queer family formation; it is an egg-freezing benefit that disproportionately serves straight women postponing childbearing. The queer-justice framing is, in effect, the rhetorical engine that made an expensive, image-burnishing perk politically saleable. This is the standard logic of contemporary "diversity" politics within capital: take a real, legitimate grievance from a marginalized constituency, redesign the response so that its primary beneficiaries are not that constituency, and then describe the result as social justice. Google and LinkedIn did not start covering elective egg freezing because lesbians needed it. They started covering it because they were competing for talent in a market that demanded such signals.

There is a longer history here, and feminists of color have been telling it for decades. Dorothy Roberts, whom Emre invokes at the end, has documented how the reproductive choices made available to differently situated women have always reflected the labor and population-management needs of capital and the state. Wealthy white women got the pill and abortion rights, often before poor women and women of color did; poor women and women of color got coerced sterilization, Norplant mandates, and family-cap welfare rules. Egg freezing fits comfortably in that genealogy. It is a technology that helps a thin layer of high-skill professional women time their childbearing to the rhythms of their employers' demand cycles. It is not a technology that has, so far, done much for the women who clean those employers' offices or care for those employers' children.

I am not arguing against egg freezing as such. Like Emre, I think the technology has real uses, and I am sympathetic to S and to anyone who avails herself of what is available. I am arguing against the seductive framing in which the corporate version of the procedure is celebrated as feminist progress. To accept that framing is to accept that the appropriate scale of feminist victory is the employer-sponsored benefit package — that the relevant unit of analysis is the individual woman optimizing her reproductive options within an institutional structure she does not control and is not invited to change.

What Emre's essay accomplishes, in spite of itself, is a quiet exposure of the costs of that bargain. The most haunting line in the S section is not S's, but Emre's own observation: "She has greater control over her reproductive future than ever before, yet she seems even more shackled to the specter of the natural now that the choice is hers to make." I would put it differently. S is not haunted by the natural. She is haunted by the fact that an enormous technological apparatus has been mobilized to defer a decision that, in a society organized differently, could simply have been made. The unmade child whose timing she is now responsible for is also the future child whose support — housing, schooling, healthcare, time with parents — is not, and will not be, socially guaranteed.

Firestone wanted technology to free women from gestation. What Silicon Valley has given them is technology to time their gestation around quarterly earnings. These are not the same thing. Feminism needs the discernment to tell them apart.

Whose Reproductive Justice?

Merve Emre's essay is rich, careful, and humane. It treats its subjects with attention. It also, in ways that I do not think the author would deny if pressed, takes for its primary frame the reproductive worlds of relatively well-resourced women in the contemporary United States: a Bay Area biotech employee, a university lecturer with a book contract, a queer couple with insurance and family loans, an artist with the cultural and intellectual capital to make bioart out of her own gametes. These are not trivial perspectives. They are, however, a narrow slice of the actual landscape of reproductive politics, and they shape the argument in ways worth naming.

The reproductive justice framework, articulated by Black women activists in the mid-1990s — SisterSong, Loretta Ross, Dorothy Roberts, and others — was built precisely against the tendency Emre's essay tilts toward: the assumption that the central feminist battle around reproduction is the battle to expand individual choice through technology and law. RJ insisted on three linked rights: the right not to have a child, the right to have a child, and the right to parent children in safe and sustainable environments. The third right is the one most easily forgotten in mainstream feminist discourse, and it is the one whose absence most distorts the picture Emre offers.

Consider what the essay does not contain. It does not contain a Black woman whose teenage son was killed by police, raising the question of what it means to bring a Black child into a world structured by anti-Black violence. It does not contain an undocumented woman whose pregnancy was supervised by ICE, or whose child was separated from her at the border in the policy now operative as Emre writes. It does not contain an incarcerated woman shackled during labor, a practice that remains legal in most U.S. states. It does not contain a Native woman whose grandmother was sterilized without consent in an IHS hospital in the 1970s, or whose own children were removed by a state agency under the discredited but lingering logic of Indian boarding schools. It does not contain a woman whose Medicaid does not cover the medications she needs to manage a high-risk pregnancy, or who lives in a county with no obstetric care. It does not contain a woman who works two jobs, neither offering maternity leave at any wage, who is contemplating a third abortion not because she does not want children but because she cannot afford one.

The relevance of these absences is not that Emre should have written a different essay. It is that the rhetorical conclusion she draws — "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight, until the natural no longer looms so large" — does not, in fact, follow from the lives of most women trying to reproduce in this country. For most women, the "natural" is not the central obstacle to reproductive flourishing. The central obstacles are poverty, racism, violence, lack of healthcare, immigration enforcement, mass incarceration, and a welfare state designed to surveil and punish poor mothers, especially poor Black mothers, rather than to support them. Replacing the natural with the assisted does not address these obstacles. It is, at best, orthogonal to them.

I want to be careful here. Emre quotes Roberts approvingly at the end of her essay and signals that she understands the limits of choice-based reproductive politics. The closing paragraphs do good work. But the essay's center of gravity is elsewhere — in the close observation of cryopreservation protocols, IVF retrieval techniques, hormonal dosages, the moral phenomenology of choosing among embryos. These are intimate, important details. They are also, structurally, details of a reproductive landscape that exists only for women who have access to clinics, doctors, insurance, savings, and the cultural authority to demand that their reproductive desires be taken seriously. The injunction to make assisted reproduction the "preferred strategy" speaks within that landscape. From outside it, the injunction can sound peculiar. Preferred over what? When you cannot get a standard well-woman exam, the question of whether to undergo preimplantation genetic screening is not a live one.

There is a deeper issue, too. The reproductive-justice tradition has been suspicious not just of natalist ideology but also of the unmarked progressivism of reproductive technology. The history of contraception, sterilization, and assisted reproduction in the United States is a history of differential application: technologies that liberate some women while disciplining or harming others. The same medical infrastructure that offers IVF to professional women in San Francisco also performs disproportionate hysterectomies on poor Black women, also sterilized 1,400 women in California prisons between 1997 and 2010, also coerced consent from Mexican-American women at LA County–USC Medical Center in the 1970s. To celebrate the assisted, in this context, requires a careful accounting of who is being assisted, by whom, and what is being done in the same hospitals to women whose names rarely appear in the manifestos.

This is why I think the essay's category of "the assisted" needs to be opened up. The "assistance" Black women receive when they walk into a hospital to give birth is, often, the assistance of being ignored. Black women in the U.S. die in childbirth at three to four times the rate of white women, a disparity that has nothing to do with biology and everything to do with how their pain is read, how their warnings are dismissed, how their bodies are touched. The "assistance" that undocumented women receive in pregnancy is, increasingly, the assistance of fear: surveillance at clinics, denial of care, the implicit threat that to seek prenatal services is to risk family separation. Naming all of this as "assistance" is technically correct under Emre's framework — yes, every reproductive act takes place inside a thick mesh of institutional involvement — but it strains the political utility of the term to call medical neglect and immigration enforcement merely different modes of assistance.

A more useful framing might be: all reproduction is conditioned. The conditions vary enormously, and the political project is not primarily to choose between natural and assisted but to transform the conditions so that anyone who wants to parent can do so safely. That project requires more than the redistribution of reproductive technologies. It requires Medicare for All, full immigrant protection, prison and police abolition, real investment in Black maternal health, restoration of tribal sovereignty over Native families, paid leave universal and generous, and a welfare state that supports rather than surveils.

Emre's essay gestures toward this in its final paragraph, and she is right that the political must catch up to the technological. I would put it more strongly: the political project required is large enough that the technological story, fascinating as it is, becomes a relatively small part of it. The horizon is not the universalization of egg freezing. It is the dismantlement of the systems that make so many forms of motherhood — including, and especially, the kinds that have nothing to do with clinics — unlivable.

The Embryos We Are Choosing

Merve Emre's essay is the rare piece of writing about reproductive technology that takes seriously both the intimacy and the politics of the procedures it describes. I learned from it, and I want to return the favor with a hard question. Among the technologies Emre catalogs — IUI, IVF, ICSI, oocyte cryopreservation — one is mentioned only in passing but does enormous moral work in the narrative. That is preimplantation genetic screening. B's IVF produces twenty-two eggs, eleven embryos, six PGS-tested embryos, and finally "only three turned out to be free of any chromosomal abnormalities." N and K's last retrieval yields five fertilized eggs, of which "only one was viable." This is the language of the clinic, and Emre adopts it without inflection. From a disability-studies perspective, that inflection — and its absence — is itself the story.

What does it mean for an embryo to be "viable"? Strictly, in PGS, it means screened for aneuploidies — chromosomal abnormalities, of which the best known and most commonly excluded is trisomy 21, Down syndrome. PGS is also increasingly used to screen for single-gene disorders: cystic fibrosis, Huntington's, sickle cell, BRCA mutations, sometimes deafness, sometimes dwarfism. Some clinics test for sex. Some, in countries with looser regulation, test for predispositions that shade into trait selection. The technology is improving rapidly and its use is rapidly expanding. The decisions it underwrites are decisions about what kinds of people will, and will not, come into existence.

The disability-rights critique of selective reproductive technologies is now several decades old and it has not lost its force. Marsha Saxton, Adrienne Asch, Rosemarie Garland-Thomson, Alison Kafer, and many others have argued that the routine prenatal screening regime — which now begins, in many clinics, before implantation rather than at twelve or twenty weeks — communicates a clear social message: that lives with these conditions are lives not worth bringing into being. The message is communicated not through anyone's bigotry but through the architecture of the procedure itself. The default of the testing protocol is to screen out. The default of the counseling is to recommend termination or non-implantation. The default of the funding, in many systems, is to pay for the test but not for the lifelong supports that would make a chosen disabled life livable. This is what Asch called the "expressive" problem: independent of any individual's intention, the practice expresses a social judgment.

Emre's essay does not engage this critique. It treats PGS as a kind of relief, a way of avoiding the "sudden and singular loss" of miscarriage in favor of "gradual attrition — the calculated paring down of her eggs and embryos after tests and screens." That phrase, "calculated paring down," is striking. It is presented as a tender, even feminist alternative to traumatic miscarriage. But it is also a description of selection — of deciding, in advance, which possible children will be permitted to develop. There is a politics there that deserves more than passing.

I do not raise this to moralize against IVF or against the women Emre profiles. I raise it because the essay's overall argument — that "the natural" is an ideological screen and that we should embrace "assisted reproduction [as] the preferred strategy by which we fight" — runs into real trouble at exactly this point. The fantasy of unconstrained reproductive choice that Emre rightly diagnoses includes, in its current technological form, the fantasy of choosing healthy children. And "healthy" is doing tremendous ideological work in that sentence. Healthy is a moving target. In the 1980s, the technology made it possible to screen out trisomies. In the 1990s, to screen out single-gene disorders associated with childhood death. In the 2000s, to screen for adult-onset and reduced-penetrance conditions. By 2018, increasing numbers of clinics test for traits whose disability status is contested even within the affected communities — deafness most famously, but also achondroplasia and others. Each expansion has been described, at the time, as a modest extension of existing practice. Cumulatively, they amount to a quiet eugenic infrastructure built inside the for-profit fertility industry, with little public deliberation.

The standard liberal response is that these are private choices and that the alternative — restricting them — would be coercive and patriarchal. I am sympathetic to that response in many cases. But the disability critique is not, in the main, a call for restriction. It is a call for honest social accounting. If we are building a society in which fewer and fewer children with Down syndrome are born, we should say so, and we should attend to what that means for the people with Down syndrome who do exist and the families who raise them. If the cost of being a person with cystic fibrosis is increasingly being treated as a moral problem to be solved upstream, by not bringing such people into being, then the people downstream — adults living with CF — are entitled to ask what status that confers on their lives.

Emre's essay actually contains a powerful counter to the trajectory I am describing, and she does not develop it. The high-risk obstetrician who tells K and N they "should not reproduce biologically" because of K's depression and weight is recognizable to any reader of the history of eugenics. It is the voice of medical authority telling a couple that their genetic future is not theirs to determine. K and N respond by reproducing anyway, "furious" and "motivated to act without their doctors' blessings." That is a profoundly disability-aware moment: the refusal of medical authority's right to sort who deserves to reproduce. But the same essay does not similarly trouble the moment when PGS is used, by N and K and by B, to sort embryos rather than parents. The sorting has moved one stage earlier and become, somehow, unobjectionable.

What I am asking for is not a different ending to anyone's IVF cycle. I am asking for a more honest reckoning with what the technology, taken at scale, does. The reproductive justice movement has long insisted that the right to parent disabled children with dignity is part of the right to reproduce. That insistence is in tension with the standard PGS protocol, and the tension is not resolved by calling the protocol "assisted."

The deeper philosophical point is this: when Emre argues that all reproduction is assisted and that we should make peace with the assistance, she is right at one level and missing something at another. The assistance reshapes the children who arrive. It does not merely help "the same" children be born under better conditions; it determines, in part, which children are born at all. A feminism that takes this seriously cannot simply celebrate the dethroning of the natural. It has to ask, at every stage, what is being chosen, by whom, in whose interest, with what consequences for the people the technology is now able to refuse.

Yes, And Further

Reading Merve Emre's essay, I kept wanting to say: yes, and further. Yes, all reproduction is assisted. Yes, the natural is an ideological screen. Yes, the manifesto tradition from Firestone through Haraway through Laboria Cuboniks has been right to insist that the body is not a destiny but a substrate. The essay's diagnosis is mostly correct. Where I want to push is on the gentleness of its conclusions. Emre has written what amounts to a careful, narratively rich argument for what xenofeminists have been saying more loudly: that the universalization of reproductive technology is a feminist project, that biology is not a defense, and that the work of dismantling forced kinship structures has barely begun. I think this position deserves a louder voice than Emre gives it.

Start with the manifesto tradition itself. Emre treats it with respectful skepticism: the manifestos universalize, she says, and what gets lost in their universalizing are the particular differences of particular bodies and particular care arrangements. This is true. But manifestos are not ethnographies; they are not supposed to be. The point of Firestone's call for ectogenesis, or Laboria Cuboniks's call for gender abolition through hormonal hacking, is not to describe how reproduction is currently distributed. It is to name a horizon against which the current distribution looks absurd. The narrative method Emre prefers is good at showing how particular women navigate the existing system. It is less good at imagining a different one. To get from the first to the second, you need both — and the manifesto's universalizing impulse is the form that imagines.

What does the horizon look like? It looks like a world in which gestation is one option among many, including ectogenesis at scale, including state-funded gestational labor compensated like other forms of skilled care work, including non-biological kin structures with full legal recognition, including hormonal interventions distributed as freely as eyeglasses. It looks like a world in which the question "whose womb?" is no more politically charged than "whose liver was the transplant from?" — a question of medical sourcing rather than essential identity. It looks like a world in which the trans woman banking sperm before estrogen, the cis lesbian couple using donor gametes, the single parent by IVF, the surrogate carrying a child for a same-sex couple, the gestational parent who is not the social parent, the chosen-family commune of co-parents — all of these are normal, in the sense that they are unremarkable, supported, paid for, and legally protected.

We are not in that world. The reason is not, principally, that the technology is not ready, although in many cases it isn't. The reason is that the political and economic structures that would universalize reproductive assistance do not exist, and the ideological structures that legitimize current arrangements are very strong. The "natural" is the most visible of those ideological structures, and Emre is right to attack it. But the natural is downstream of something else: the privatization of children. The reason every woman in Emre's essay has to fight her way through expensive clinics, borrow money from siblings, hide pregnancies from employers, and stake her parenting future on her ability to time her own retrievals is that having children, in the United States, is treated as a private consumption decision. The state's role in child-rearing is minimal and increasingly punitive. The community's role is residual. The kin network has been ground down to nuclear bits.

In this structure, the natural is doing very specific ideological work. It says: this is a private choice, and the responsibilities are private. The pain is yours, the cost is yours, the failure is yours. The child you produce is yours to fuck up, in Firestone's biting phrase. By making the reproduction look private and inevitable, the natural foreclosed the political question of how children get made and supported. To dismantle the natural is therefore to open that question — to make it visible that reproduction is a social process all the way down and that the social process can be organized differently.

This is why I think Emre's closing image — "the political to catch up to the technological, our behavior to catch up to our beliefs" — is too modest. The political does not need to "catch up." It needs to lead. The technology is already, in many cases, ahead of where collective imagination has caught up. Ectogenesis is not science fiction. Recent work on artificial wombs for premature lambs has demonstrated that gestation outside a human body is now a tractable engineering problem, not a metaphysical one. The relevant question is no longer whether ectogenesis is possible but who will own it, who will pay for it, who will be allowed to use it, whose bodies it will replace, whose labor it will continue to require. These are political questions, and they will be answered by political actors. If feminists do not show up for them, the answers will be supplied by venture capitalists, by religious conservatives, by the same insurance-and-clinic apparatus that currently extracts fortunes from infertile couples.

The xenofeminist slogan — "if nature is unjust, change nature" — captures the right orientation. But it has to be paired with a corresponding insistence: not just change nature, change ownership. The technologies of reproductive assistance are not neutral instruments waiting for better distribution. They are being built inside a particular political economy, and they carry that economy's marks. Egg freezing as a corporate perk; surrogacy as a globalized labor market with brown women's wombs at the bottom of the supply chain; gamete donation as a market commodity racially stratified by price; IVF as a tens-of-thousands-of-dollars purchase priced out of reach for most. The horizon of universal assisted reproduction is not the universalization of the existing for-profit clinic. It is its supersession.

Emre's narrative method is generous, and her interviewees do not deserve to be conscripted into someone else's manifesto. But I think the lesson her essay teaches, more starkly than she lets on, is that piecemeal access to a fragmented private system will not redeem reproductive politics. What is needed is the actual horizon: socialized reproduction, demystified gestation, recognized kin pluralism, dismantled property logic in children. The natural will lose its grip not when individual women buy their way around it, but when collective practice replaces it. That is what "all reproduction is assisted" should mean. Not that we have always quietly been using technology — we have — but that we have always been making, together, the conditions under which new humans appear in the world. The question is whether we do it consciously, justly, and at scale, or whether we keep pretending it is private.

The Children We Are Not Having

I want to ask a question Merve Emre raises near the end of her essay and then sets aside: why does anyone want to have children? Her instinct is that the question is fundamentally irrational, a matter of fantasy. I think this is too easy. The question is not irrational. It is, in 2018, a question with a different shape than it had a generation ago, and the new shape is part of the political context for everything she has written.

We live, as Emre and her interviewees do, in a moment of intersecting catastrophes. The latest IPCC reports project warming beyond two degrees within the lifetimes of children born this year. Insect biomass is collapsing in Europe. Coral bleaching events have happened in three of the last five years on the Great Barrier Reef. Atmospheric CO2 has been above 410 ppm since spring of this year. None of these facts appear in Emre's essay. Neither does the rising tide of conversation among prospective parents — particularly younger ones — about whether having children in the climate context is ethically defensible. The BirthStrike movement is beginning to organize publicly. Conceivable Future, the U.S. group on climate and parental decisions, has been hosting house parties for several years. Academic philosophers from Sarah Conly to Travis Rieder have begun to argue in print for procreative restraint as a climate response. This is a substantive political and ethical conversation, and a feminist essay about reproductive desire that does not engage it in 2018 is missing a piece of the picture.

I am not, to be clear, advocating antinatalism. The arguments of David Benatar and his fellow travelers — that it is always wrong to bring new people into existence — strike me as both philosophically thin and politically unhelpful, and they have a habit of arriving at conclusions about which populations should reduce that fall along disturbingly familiar lines. The climate-procreation conversation does not have to take that form. It can be more humble: a recognition that the carbon footprint of an additional Global North child is enormous, that the world the child will inhabit is in significant ways worse than the one her parents inhabit, and that these facts deserve a place in the moral phenomenology of choosing to reproduce.

Emre's essay has a very specific moral phenomenology, and it is worth naming. The dominant register is desire — reproductive desire, framed as irrational, intimate, and largely beyond political evaluation. The fantasy of a child is, in her telling, a fantasy each person constructs privately, and the political task is to lower the structural barriers to its fulfillment. This is a deeply individualist frame. It treats the desire as given, the obstacles as the problem, and the appropriate response as enabling. It does not ask whether the desire might be socially constructed and worth examining; it does not ask whether bringing a child into a particular world might be something that requires justification, not just facilitation.

Consider how different the essay would read with the climate context in view. S, freezing her eggs in San Francisco, is preserving the option of a child whose ecological inheritance will include sea-level encroachment on the very neighborhoods her employer is now displacing. B, planning her IVF transfer, is choosing the timing of a child whose adolescence will overlap with the projected exhaustion of multiple major aquifers. N and K, deciding on a fifth transfer, are doing so in a country whose current administration has withdrawn from the Paris Agreement, accelerated oil and gas leasing, and gutted the EPA. None of this is incidental to the question of whether and how to reproduce. It is part of the answer to the question Emre's friend asked. We want children, in part, because the cultural script tells us to want them. We continue to want them, even as the inherited conditions for raising them deteriorate, because the script is more powerful than the deterioration. The desire is not pre-political. It is conditioned, and the condition is intensifying.

I want to be careful with this argument. The disability-rights critique I value most warns against conditional reproduction — the idea that some lives are not worth living. The climate argument can slide into a similar trap if it treats future children as ruined goods. That is not what I am saying. What I am saying is that the question of whether to reproduce, and how many children to have, and at what carbon cost, is a real moral question that thoughtful people are increasingly asking, and feminism is poorly served by a framework that treats the desire to reproduce as the unexamined center of the conversation.

There is also a class dimension to this. The discussion of climate procreation has, fairly, been criticized for landing most heavily on women in the Global North while populations in the Global South — whose per-capita emissions are vastly lower and who are the front-line victims of warming — are subjected to a much older population-control discourse. Reproductive justice scholars have rightly worried about the slippage between "fewer children for climate reasons" and "fewer of those children." I take this concern seriously. But it cuts in the direction of asking the high-emitting populations to ask the question of themselves — which is precisely the populations whose stories Emre tells.

What would a climate-attentive reading of Emre's narratives look like? It would not, I think, deliver a verdict on S, B, or N and K. Their decisions are theirs, and the structural failures that shape those decisions are real and worth fighting. But it would notice that the essay's frame — assisted reproduction as feminist horizon — is operating on the assumption that more access to assisted reproduction is straightforwardly a good. That assumption is not climate-neutral. The fertility industry is energy-intensive. The standard pronatalist progressivism that increasingly characterizes American liberal feminism — paid leave, IVF coverage, more support for parents — is a politics built around the expansion of a particular form of life that, at scale, the planet may not be able to support.

I do not have a clean resolution. The honest answer is that there is a tragic structure here that Emre's essay does not name. The capacity for assisted reproduction is expanding at the same moment that the ecological case for restraint is hardening. Both are true. Both deserve voice. A feminism worthy of the moment should be capable of holding both: defending the right to reproduce, including by technological means, while also welcoming the conversations among women who are choosing otherwise — choosing fewer children, no children, adopted children, communal care of other people's children — for reasons that are not failure, not loss, not ideology, but a serious response to the world we are in.

Why does anyone want to have children? Maybe the better question is: in what world, with what others, with what obligations? Emre's essay puts the first half of that question. The second half is still waiting.

The Trans Reproductive Frontier

The most quietly radical moments in Merve Emre's essay are the ones featuring trans people: Lara, the trans woman in Joanne Spataro's New York Times piece, gradually tapering her estrogen to try to conceive a child with her cis partner; micha cárdenas, banking sperm before HRT, photographing her gametes through a fifty-dollar children's microscope, writing a poem-pregnancy as art. These figures sit at the edge of Emre's argument, and I think they should be at its center. The reason is not identity-political. It is that trans reproduction makes the structural argument of the essay — that the natural is an ideological screen, that all reproduction is assisted — undeniable in a way that other examples do not.

Consider what is involved, materially, in Lara and Joanne's project. Lara has been on estrogen and androgen blockers for some period before deciding to try to conceive. Her endocrine system has been intentionally restructured. To produce viable sperm again, she must reverse parts of that restructuring — taper the estrogen, accept the return of features she chose hormones partly to suppress, ride out the physical and psychological changes of partial detransition, and then conceive, and then either resume HRT or not. Every stage of this process is technologically mediated. Every stage is also chosen. There is no moment at which "the natural" can pretend to be the default. The conception is biological in the narrow sense — egg, sperm, uterus, the standard ingredients — but no part of it is unmediated, automatic, or pre-political.

This is what is so radical about Lara and Joanne's situation, and why it sparked the angry online reaction Emre describes. The "old-fashioned way" framing in the Times tweet was not an accident; it was an attempt to defuse what the story actually reveals. Trans reproduction makes visible the technological mediation of all sexed bodies. It makes visible the fact that endocrinology is not destiny, that gametes can be banked, that fertility can be paused and resumed, that the boundary between "natural" and "assisted" reproduction is not a biological line but a cultural one, drawn at certain culturally legible interventions and not others. Hormonal contraception, which restructures fertility in cis women for decades at a stretch, has been domesticated; HRT, which restructures fertility in trans women, has not. The difference is political, not biological. To watch the conversation rage about Lara and Joanne in 2018 is to watch the policing of that political line in real time.

cárdenas's bioart makes the same point with a different texture. She had been told she would be sterile. She was not. She bought a child's microscope and filmed her own sperm and put the video in a Facebook group for trans women banking gametes — a small underground network of self-knowledge that the medical establishment had refused them. The poem Emre quotes — "they told me I would be sterile, / the doctors and brochures, / that I couldn't do this, / what I'm doing" — names something specific. The medical apparatus that purports to manage trans bodies has consistently lied to trans people about what those bodies can do. The pre-transition counseling about fertility preservation has historically been inadequate or absent. The information about post-transition reproductive capacity has been wrong as often as it has been right. Trans people have built their own knowledge networks, against this apparatus, to find out what their bodies are. This is technologically assisted reproduction in the deepest sense: the technology of shared knowledge, accumulated against institutional indifference, that allows a person to know what reproductive options she has at all.

This frame — trans reproduction as paradigmatic rather than peripheral — has implications for how we read the rest of Emre's essay. It means that S, B, and N and K are not the central cases with cárdenas as a poetic afterthought; cárdenas is the central case and the others are recognizable variants. Each of these women is navigating a reproductive system that requires technological mediation to deliver what cultural ideology pretends is natural. Each is doing so in the context of an information environment partially structured by misinformation, neglect, or hostility. Each is leaning on networks — friends, partners, online communities, Facebook groups, queer TTC sisters, employer-sponsored LGBT associations — to compensate for the failures of mainstream medicine. The trans case is not exceptional. It is, in a sense, the unmasked version of everyone's case.

This has political implications. If trans reproduction is the paradigm rather than the periphery, then reproductive politics needs to be reorganized around what trans reproduction needs. That includes: routine fertility counseling and preservation as part of standard transition care, fully funded, without gatekeeping; legal recognition of all forms of parenthood that emerge from trans reproductive paths, including non-genetic and non-gestational parents; protection of trans people from family-court attempts to use transition as evidence of unfitness; expansion of insurance coverage for assisted reproductive technology to all sexed configurations, not just those that fit the infertility-diagnosis template; protection of trans youth from interventions, including hormonal interventions, that proceed without informed consent about fertility implications; and, not least, the basic safety conditions cárdenas names when she observes that for trans women of color the reproductive horizon includes the possibility of being killed before having children.

The last point deserves more weight than Emre gives it. cárdenas's line — "I could be dead anytime. There is a real sense of urgency" — names a reproductive issue that virtually no mainstream feminist literature treats as such. The murder rate of trans women, particularly Black and Latina trans women, in the United States in 2018 is a reproductive issue because it forecloses reproductive futures. The asylum cases at the border, where trans women are routinely detained in male facilities and assaulted, are reproductive issues. The healthcare denials that shorten trans lives are reproductive issues. To treat these as separate from reproductive politics — as belonging to "trans rights" or "anti-violence" but not to "reproductive justice" — is to misunderstand the categories. Reproductive justice in its founding articulation has always included the right to live long enough to parent, in conditions safe enough to parent. Trans women of color make that demand particularly legible.

What I am asking, then, is for the trans reproductive frontier to be read not as one more interesting case in the pluralism of reproductive arrangements, but as the case that exposes the underlying truth of the others. All reproduction is assisted; trans reproduction makes that fact unhideable. All reproductive bodies are technologically mediated; trans bodies have always been described as such, while cis bodies have been allowed to pretend otherwise. All reproductive desire takes place against structural conditions that variously enable or foreclose it; for trans people, especially trans women of color, those conditions include the elementary condition of staying alive.

Emre's essay does serious justice to this material. I want to take it one step further. The future of reproductive politics, if it is going to be honest, will be a trans future. Not in the sense that everyone will be trans, but in the sense that the reproductive imagination organized around fixed sex, given fertility, and unmediated biology will have been abandoned. Cárdenas is not at the margin of this picture. She is the picture.

The Global Womb

Merve Emre's essay is a careful tour through American assisted reproductive technology as it is experienced by relatively privileged American women. I admired its narrative texture and learned from its arguments. I want to register what is missing, because what is missing matters enormously to the politics the essay claims to be doing. The fertility industry the essay describes is global. The American clinic is one node in a transnational supply chain of gametes, embryos, hormones, gestational labor, and reproductive desire. Almost none of that chain appears in Emre's account.

Start with surrogacy. Commercial gestational surrogacy is, as Emre writes, increasingly available to American couples and individuals who can afford it. What she does not say is that the market for it has, over the past fifteen years, become aggressively global. India was, until its 2015–2018 regulatory closures, the largest single destination for international commercial surrogacy, with an industry estimated in the billions of dollars and clinics catering primarily to American, Israeli, and European clients. Thailand and Nepal hosted boom-and-bust markets that produced internationally notorious scandals — the Baby Gammy case in 2014, the post-earthquake evacuations from Nepal in 2015 — before regulatory crackdowns. As of 2018 the principal commercial destinations are Ukraine, Russia, Georgia, Mexico, and increasingly several African countries; clinics in Cambodia and Laos have absorbed displaced demand. The pattern is consistent: surrogacy markets cluster in countries where wages are low enough that the payment is significant for the surrogate, regulation is light enough to permit commerce, and the surrounding political economy generates a population of women willing to gestate strangers' children for money.

The labor conditions of these surrogates have been documented in detail by scholars including Amrita Pande, Kalindi Vora, and Sharmila Rudrappa. Pande's ethnography of Indian surrogacy hostels describes women confined to dormitories for the duration of pregnancy, subjected to managed diets and medical regimes, separated from their own children, and paid sums that, while transformative for them, represent a tiny fraction of what the commissioning parents pay the agency. The pregnancies are routinely managed via maximum-intervention protocols — multiple embryo transfers, scheduled cesareans — driven less by maternal health than by clinic efficiency. The relationship between commissioning parents and surrogates is often structured to minimize affective bonds and to forestall any post-birth claims; surrogates are typically poor, often illiterate in the contract language, and dependent on agencies for both wages and protection.

This is reproductive labor. It is also reproductive assistance. It is, in fact, exactly the kind of "assisted reproduction" that the techno-feminist horizon Emre gestures toward would have to address. The horizon of "more access to assisted reproduction" cannot be articulated honestly without addressing the question: assistance from whom, for whom, at what cost to whom? The egg freezing, IVF, and embryo screening Emre describes do not, in most of her cases, involve another woman's gestational labor. But these technologies coexist, within the same industry, with surrogacy and gamete sourcing arrangements that do. The American clinics that serve B and S and N and K share infrastructure, regulatory environment, and personnel with the agencies that broker Ukrainian surrogates and Spanish egg donors for American clients. The boundary between domestic and international ART is porous in both directions.

Gamete markets show the same pattern at smaller scale. The American egg market is structured by sharply differentiated prices: ova from young, Ivy-educated, white or East Asian donors command premiums of tens of thousands of dollars; ova from donors of color or with less educational capital are cheaper. Sperm banks operate similarly. The pricing structure is, by any reasonable definition, eugenic — not in the strict twentieth-century state sense, but in the consumer-market sense that wealthy buyers can purchase preferred traits and poorer donors can sell their gametes at lower margins. International gamete markets are more starkly stratified. Spanish ova are widely shipped because Spain's regulatory environment permits anonymous donation and high compensation; Eastern European ova are shipped because both the supply and the cultural-cosmetic preferences of Western European and American buyers align. None of this appears in Emre's account, but all of it is part of "assisted reproduction" as it actually exists.

I raise these facts not to oppose ART as such, and not to suggest that S, B, or N and K bear personal responsibility for the global structure of the industry that served them. I raise them because Emre's argument is structured around the claim that "the natural" is the principal obstacle to reproductive justice, and that the embrace of assisted reproduction would open a path toward solidarity. From the perspective of the global supply chain, the opposite seems closer to true. The current configuration of assisted reproduction is producing some of the sharpest reproductive inequalities in human history. A woman in San Francisco can purchase the gametes of an Eastern European donor she will never meet, the gestational services of an Indian or Ukrainian surrogate she will see only on video, and the embryonic screening of a Chicago lab — and assemble the resulting child into a family her American legal system will recognize. The constituent women in the chain — donor, surrogate, sometimes egg-vendor, sometimes wet nurse — have, by design, no comparable access. They will not assemble a child by purchasing gametes from a wealthier woman. They will not engage a surrogate from a more privileged country. The flow of reproductive material and reproductive labor is one-directional, structured by global income inequality, and rationalized through contracts.

The right response is not to ban these arrangements outright. Bans, where they have been tried, have generally pushed the trade to less regulated jurisdictions with worse conditions for the women involved. The right response is the same response that other globalized labor markets demand: real regulation of working conditions, durable rights for the workers, redistribution of the gains, and ultimately the elevation of source-country wages and protections so that the choice to surrogate is a real choice and not a coerced consequence of poverty. This is a labor agenda. It is also, necessarily, a reproductive-justice agenda, because the labor in question is reproductive.

The closing rhetorical move of Emre's essay — "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight" — does not, in this light, sound like a politics of solidarity. It sounds like the export of a particular consumer model. A feminism that wants to be global, rather than provincially American, has to ask whose assistance is assisting whom. The women Emre interviews are working hard, and their stories matter. But so do the women who carry the pregnancies they cannot, donate the eggs they did not have time to freeze in their twenties, and provide the cheap obstetric labor that makes the global price structure work. They have not yet been invited into the conversation. They are the ones the conversation has, so far, been about.

What the Old Words Were Doing

Merve Emre's essay aims to retire a word — "natural" — that she believes has done feminism more harm than good. The retirement is intended both as critique of conservative ideology and as opening for new reproductive arrangements. I want to make the case, as gently as I can, that the word was doing some work the new vocabulary will not do as well, and that the cost of retiring it is higher than Emre's essay suggests.

Begin with what the word actually marks. "Natural" in the reproductive context is not, despite Emre's framing, primarily an ideological gloss on women's suffering. It is a folk-anthropological term that marks continuity with the biological life of the species. It says: this body is a mammal, this child grew the way mammal children grow, this relationship is the relationship that has obtained between gestating bodies and their offspring across evolutionary time. The word is doing the work that "tradition" does in other domains — naming the shape of practice that obtains before any deliberate intervention. Like "tradition," "natural" is sometimes used to defend bad practices. Like "tradition," it is also sometimes used to mark something genuinely valuable that deliberate intervention may unbuild.

Emre's reading of the word as essentially reactionary depends on her selection of examples. The natural-birth Instagram blogger reciting Adrienne Rich; the prenatal supplement label promising Eden; the manufacturer of expensive birthing tubs — these are easy targets, and they deserve mockery. But they are not, in fact, the people for whom the word "natural" is doing serious work. The people I have in mind are: women who have given birth several times across several decades and who can tell you that some of the routine medical interventions they were subjected to were unnecessary, painful, and bad for them and their children; midwives in continuous traditions of practice who carry knowledge that the obstetrical profession spent the twentieth century actively suppressing; mothers in indigenous and immigrant communities whose embodied knowledge of pregnancy, labor, and lactation has been treated as superstition by clinicians who could not match it; and, frankly, ordinary women who notice that the institutional management of their bodies seems organized for institutional convenience rather than their own. When these people invoke "natural," they are not, mostly, signing on to the School of Great Experience. They are pushing back against a medical apparatus whose presumptions about their bodies have been wrong often enough to warrant skepticism.

The history of obstetrics in the twentieth century vindicates a great deal of this skepticism. Twilight sleep, the routine use of forceps, the over-resort to episiotomy, the dorsal lithotomy position adopted for surgeon convenience, the rates of unnecessary C-section, the historical exclusion of fathers and other support figures from delivery rooms, the management of birth as a sequence of timed interventions on a labor curve invented by a single physician with a small dataset — all of this was done in the name of modern medicine and over the objection of women who said something was wrong. The pushback against these practices was, in significant part, articulated in the vocabulary of the natural. That vocabulary was instrumentally productive. It built midwifery into hospitals, returned doulas to the standard birthing team, banned routine episiotomy in many institutions, and produced clinical research showing that several "naturalist" preferences — upright labor positions, intermittent rather than continuous fetal monitoring, skin-to-skin contact after birth, immediate breastfeeding — improved outcomes by the medical profession's own metrics.

Emre's essay treats "breast is best" as an example of natural ideology that "occludes the many individuals who carry or care for children." This is a specific and contested claim. The slogan was developed in response to a documented history of aggressive infant-formula marketing — most notoriously by Nestlé in the Global South — that contributed to infant deaths from contaminated water used to mix formula in conditions where breastfeeding would have been safer. The slogan emerged from a real public-health movement with real evidentiary support, and its appropriation by judgmental middle-class mommy culture in the United States is a distortion of its purpose, not its purpose. To collapse the slogan with the lifestyle culture it has been twisted into is to give the lifestyle culture the last word. A more discerning critique would distinguish the public-health insight from its bourgeois moralization.

The same holds for several other items on Emre's list. Fertility-tracking apps are sometimes promoted as natural alternatives to hormonal contraception, with all the moralizing baggage that implies. They are also, at the basic level, just information — information about a body that women have historically had limited access to, that the medical profession has historically managed on their behalf, and that they are now able to gather and interpret themselves. Hormonal accounts of maternal bonding can be deployed to coerce reluctant mothers; they can also be empirically correct accounts of postpartum neuroendocrinology, of considerable interest to women who experience the changes and want a name for them. The blanket dismissal of these accounts as ideology drops information that some women want.

What I am asking for is a sharper instrument than Emre's essay provides. The instrument should be able to distinguish: (a) the use of "natural" to coerce, shame, or exclude — bad, and to be opposed; (b) the use of "natural" to defend valuable embodied practice against institutional encroachment — defensible, and to be supported; (c) the use of "natural" as a marketing category attached to overpriced products — silly, and to be ignored; (d) the use of "natural" as an honest descriptor of continuity with mammalian and human-historical reproductive life — neither good nor bad in itself, but doing real semantic work that no other word in current English does as well.

Without that instrument, the call to retire the word lands as a call to abandon the second and fourth uses along with the first and third. The cost is that the political case against unnecessary medicalization, against the dismissal of embodied knowledge, and against the corporate capture of reproductive options loses one of its principal vocabularies. The case can be made in other words, of course. But the other words are not, yet, doing the work.

The deeper point is this. Emre's essay tilts, in spite of its careful narrative method, toward a particular orientation: the assumption that more technological mediation of reproduction is, on net, the direction of feminist progress. I am skeptical of that assumption, not because I am committed to nature-mysticism but because I have read too much medical history to believe that technological mediation is, by default, in patients' interests. It is sometimes in their interests. It is sometimes not. The discernment between the two requires exactly the kind of body-grounded skepticism that has, in the twentieth-century American context, often been expressed in the language of the natural. Retire the word and you are likely to retire the skepticism with it. That would be a loss.

What Narrative Can and Cannot Do

Merve Emre opens her essay with a methodological claim: "To appreciate all this — and to figure out what to do about it — we need narrative." The four stories that follow — S, B, N and K, micha cárdenas — are the essay's argumentative substance, and they are exquisitely told. I want to spend a response taking that methodological claim seriously, which means asking what narrative can do in reproductive politics, and what it cannot.

What narrative can do, demonstrably, is render the texture of reproductive technology as it is actually lived. Until you have read or written something like Emre's account of egg freezing, you do not understand the daily rhythm of the procedure: the 9 p.m. injections, the every-other-day ultrasounds, the prohibition on sex and exercise, the trigger shot scheduled exactly seventy-two hours before retrieval, the fear of the 1–2 p.m. phone call. The clinical literature does not convey this; the policy literature does not convey this; the manifesto literature, in its hortatory pitch, does not convey this. Narrative does. And once you have the texture, certain arguments become available that were not available before. You can see, for example, that the choice to freeze eggs is not a one-time decision but a month-long bodily project; that the OHSS risk B nearly fell into is not a footnote but a serious adverse event; that the indignities N and K encountered from the resident who could not find a cervix are not exceptional but structural; that the "DIY" creativity of cárdenas's bioart was made necessary by the failures of the medical literature she was given.

These are real gains. The argumentative work narrative does here is the work of grounding — taking abstractions like "assisted reproduction" or "reproductive choice" and showing what they consist of, hour by hour, in particular bodies and particular clinical encounters. The feminist tradition that Emre is working in — from Adrienne Rich's Of Woman Born through Rayna Rapp's ethnographies of amniocentesis through more recent work by Sarah Franklin and Charis Thompson — has long understood that the grounding work is essential. Reproductive politics conducted in pure manifesto register, without ethnographic substance, tends to misfire. The manifesto knows what it wants; it does not know what the thing it wants is actually like.

Now the harder question: what can narrative not do? It cannot, by itself, generate the political conclusions Emre draws in her closing pages. The conclusions — that we should make assisted reproduction the "preferred strategy" of feminist struggle, that we should close the gap between political and technological, that we should move from a politics of "the natural" to a politics of acknowledged assistance — do not, in fact, follow from the four narratives. They float above them. The narratives are compatible with those conclusions, but they are also compatible with several other conclusions, including some quite different ones.

Consider what other arguments S, B, N and K, and cárdenas could each support. S could equally well anchor a critique of corporate egg freezing as a privatization of social reproduction, in which a tech firm extracts ovarian labor in lieu of providing real parental support. B could equally well anchor a defense of "natural" pacing — her hyperfertile family, her healthy follicle count at forty, the obstetric establishment's insistence that she should accept IVF rather than continue with IUI — and a critique of the genetic-screening cascade that ended with her ranking three "viable" embryos by sex. N and K could anchor a defense of community-based, non-medical knowledge networks — the queer TTC Facebook group, the lesbian-specific subgroup — as the actual technology that made their parenthood possible, with the clinic as a frequently hostile environment they had to endure rather than as the site of liberation. cárdenas could anchor a critique of the entire fertility industry, since the central fact of her story is that she had to build her own knowledge with a fifty-dollar microscope because the medical establishment had lied to her and the Facebook network of trans women was the only source of accurate information she could find.

Each of these alternative readings is consistent with the narrative material. Emre selects one reading and frames the others, when she frames them at all, as partial views to be integrated into hers. This is not a critique of her method but a recognition that narrative under-determines its uses. The same story can be told in several political registers. The selection of register is an argumentative choice, and it should be defended argumentatively, not just narratively.

The choice Emre makes is, broadly, the assimilationist one: the technologies are here, the women are using them, the appropriate political program is to universalize access and dethrone the residual ideology of the natural. This is not an unreasonable choice. But it is a choice. The alternatives — defending the natural as anti-medicalization critique; defending community knowledge against clinic capture; suspecting the technology as a privatization of social reproduction; questioning the trajectory of selective reproduction — are also live, and they are also supportable from the same material.

I want to flag one specific place where I think the narrative work and the argumentative work part company. The essay's final paragraphs invoke Dorothy Roberts's reproductive justice framework and use it to call for "a non-discriminatory system of universal healthcare, paid parental leave, and protections for LGBT people and people with disabilities." Good. But the essay then concludes that "acknowledging the positive right to reproduce" could "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight." This is a non-sequitur. The positive right to reproduce, in Roberts's framework, is principally a right of women of color and poor women to have and raise children — children who are being taken from them by the foster system, killed by police, deported, denied healthcare, and shaped by environmental racism. Assisted reproduction, in the technological sense, is not the central instrument for vindicating that right. The central instruments are: childcare, housing, healthcare, immigration protection, police reform, environmental justice. Emre's conclusion fuses two different things — the technological "assisted" of IVF and gamete preservation, and the social "assisted" of community and state support — under one word and treats the political weight of the second as if it transferred to the first. It does not.

What I am suggesting is that the essay is two essays. One essay is a beautiful piece of reproductive ethnography that does what narrative does best: grounds abstractions in bodies and clinics. That essay is a real contribution. The other essay is a manifesto-in-disguise that draws conclusions narrative cannot fully support and that, on examination, smuggle in commitments the narrative material would not unanimously endorse. The two essays are intertwined, and the second is supported by the first in ways that warrant pulling them apart.

This is not a rejection of Emre's project. It is a reminder that the form of "narrative" she rightly champions does not, by itself, resolve the political questions it raises. To resolve them, we still need argument, and the argument is still being conducted — among feminists who agree that the natural is contested, the technology is here, and the politics of reproduction is unfinished, but who do not agree about which way the unfinishedness should be finished. I want to engage with Merve Emre's essay on its own philosophical terms, setting aside for the moment the political implications that others have emphasized. The question of whether "all reproduction is assisted" is, I think, more complicated than Emre allows.

Emre's argument is that technology mediates all reproduction: pregnancy tests, ultrasounds, prenatal screening, hospital births. These interventions are so ubiquitous that distinguishing "natural" from "assisted" reproduction makes little sense. Both involve technology; the difference is only which technologies and who controls them.

This argument is philosophically coherent, but it depends on a very broad concept of technology. If we define technology broadly enough—any tool or technique for accomplishing a goal—then almost all human activity is technological, and the distinction between natural and artificial dissolves entirely. But this conceptual move sacrifices precision for rhetorical effect.

A more rigorous analysis would distinguish between technologies that merely observe or monitor reproduction and technologies that fundamentally transform it. Pregnancy tests and ultrasounds are observational: they tell us about the state of a pregnancy without changing it. IVF and egg freezing are transformative: they alter the reproductive process itself, bypassing or modifying biological steps.

This distinction matters because the risks, costs, and ethical implications differ dramatically. Observational technologies are relatively low-risk and widely accessible, at least in wealthy countries. Transformative technologies involve significant medical intervention, substantial costs, and complex ethical questions about the status of embryos and the commodification of reproductive labor.

Emre's case studies focus almost exclusively on transformative technologies—egg freezing, IVF, IUI. The essay doesn't consider the more common forms of technological mediation that most pregnant people experience: the standard prenatal tests, the routine ultrasounds, the hospital-based births that are the norm in much of the world. These experiences are less dramatic but more universal.

The philosophical point is that "assisted reproduction" as a category includes both observational and transformative technologies, and we need to be precise about which we're discussing. Emre's essay conflates these different types, using examples of transformative technology to make claims about all technological mediation.

This imprecision creates rhetorical effects but analytical confusion. If "assisted reproduction" means any technologically mediated reproduction, then it includes virtually all reproduction in contemporary societies. But if it means specifically the transformative technologies of IVF and egg freezing, then it applies to a much smaller population with very different experiences.

Emre seems to want both moves simultaneously: universalizing the concept to show that "all reproduction is assisted" while focusing on specific populations whose experiences illustrate particular problems. This combination produces an essay that is intellectually stimulating but analytically unstable.

A more careful approach would specify which claims apply to which technologies and which populations. We might conclude that some forms of reproduction are more technologically mediated than others, that this variation has political implications, and that we should work toward equitable access to the technologies that people need. This conclusion would be less striking than "all reproduction is assisted" but more useful for political analysis.

We have become skilled at analyzing how ideology works, at exposing the hidden assumptions embedded in seemingly natural practices, at demonstrating the contingency of categories we had assumed were fixed. But we have become less skilled at connecting these analyses to collective action for social change.

The essay's case studies illustrate the problem. Emre presents vivid accounts of women navigating assisted reproduction, but she doesn't tell us what to do with these stories. Are they meant to generate empathy? To illustrate structural barriers? To provide material for political mobilization? The essay doesn't say, and the ending—calling for universal healthcare and paid leave—feels appended rather than integrated.

The invocation of Firestone is instructive here. The Dialectic of Sex was explicitly a manifesto—a call to action, a utopian vision of reproductive transformation. Firestone's argument was that reproductive technology could liberate women from the burden of biological reproduction and the social roles attached to it. She aimed to change the world, not just interpret it.

Emre's essay, by contrast, is primarily interpretive. It explains how reproduction is already mediated, how the natural is an ideology, how structural inequalities shape reproductive access. But it doesn't offer a vision of what reproduction might become if these inequalities were eliminated. It describes the present with precision but doesn't imagine the future.

This timidity may reflect the political moment. Firestone wrote during the heady days of second-wave feminism, when revolutionary change seemed possible. Emre writes in an era of reaction, when modest reforms are achievements. But the reproductive justice movement offers visions of transformation that go beyond current reforms—demands for universal access to reproductive technology, for the democratization of fertility treatment, for the recognition of diverse family forms.

Emre's essay could contribute to this movement if it connected its analytical insights to political demands. The case studies show that assisted reproduction is already here, already shaping reproductive life for millions of women.
