Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Lea Ypi's essay does something rare and valuable: it refuses the terms of debate that both the populist right and the center-left have settled into. The juxtaposition she draws out — Trump's chained deportees broadcast on social media alongside his "Platinum Card" auctioning citizenship for five million dollars — captures something essential about the present moment that neither the immigration-skeptical left nor the humanitarian liberal quite manages to name. The border is not a wall; it is a sieve calibrated by class. The cruelty visited on the destitute traveler and the welcome extended to the wealthy investor are not separate phenomena but two faces of the same regime. Any honest analysis of contemporary migration politics has to start there, and Ypi is right to insist on it.

I also think she is correct, against a certain tendency on the Wagenknecht-Starmer axis of European politics, that the "trade-off thesis" — solidarity for natives requires restriction for foreigners — rests on an idealized picture of the welfare state that the welfare state itself does not deliver. The actually existing British, German, or Italian state is not a Bernsteinian cooperative being undermined by foreign arrivals; it is an entity that has spent four decades transferring risk downward and rents upward, and whose redistributive capacities were hollowed out long before the small-boat crossings became politically salient. The migrant is not the agent of austerity. Anyone who has watched a hospital trust be sold to private equity, or a council estate demolished to free land for luxury flats, knows where the welfare state actually went.

Where I want to press on Ypi's argument is not at the diagnosis but at the inference she draws from it. From the (true) claim that the class character of border regimes is obscene, and the (also true) claim that capital benefits from rightless workers, she slides toward the conclusion that the proper progressive stance is to reject "the reduction of democracy to membership" tout court — to treat any account of bounded political community as already a concession to ethnonationalism. This is, I think, both philosophically too quick and politically self-defeating.

Consider the historical record Ypi herself invokes. The struggles she celebrates — workers' movements for universal suffrage, women's struggles for political rights, anticolonial independence, civil rights — were not struggles against the form of bounded citizenship. They were struggles to be included in it. The suffragettes did not want to abolish the franchise; they wanted to be inside it. Anticolonial movements did not demand the dissolution of political community into cosmopolitan flux; they demanded sovereign states of their own. The Indian or Algerian or Ghanaian who fought against empire was, in the most precise sense, demanding a border — a line that marked where one people would govern itself rather than be governed by another. Ypi's own beautiful memoir of growing up in Albania describes a country whose people fought, at terrible cost, for the right to constitute themselves as a free political community. To treat membership as such as the enemy is to misread the emancipatory tradition she wants to recover.

Bernstein's wager, which Ypi cites approvingly, was precisely that democratic citizenship could discipline capital because it bounded a demos that could act collectively. The mid-twentieth-century welfare states — the "rare and fortunate historical episodes" she mentions in passing — were not accidents. They depended on the capacity of a political community to tax its capital, conscript its young, and distribute its surplus, and that capacity in turn depended on a relatively settled answer to the question of who "we" were. One can dislike this fact; one cannot wish it away. The cosmopolitanism of Nathan the Wise is morally luminous, but Nathan's Jerusalem is not a polity that redistributes income through progressive taxation. The institutions we actually have for converting class struggle into social rights are national institutions, and they work, when they work, through the very membership Ypi treats as the obstacle.

This matters because Ypi's prescription — "fighting for an expansion of political representation beyond inherited citizenship," refusing to "separate problems of admission from problems of integration" — has, in the world as it is, a predictable consequence: it concedes the question of who decides to the right. The far right's electoral success in Europe and North America rests not principally on racism (though racism is present) but on a perception, widespread among voters who are not themselves racist, that mainstream parties have stopped treating the polity as something the polity controls. When Starmer's "island of strangers" rhetoric is the only alternative to a position that appears to refuse any account of legitimate border policy whatsoever, voters who would prefer neither are forced into a choice. Ypi acknowledges this in passing — "in a contest on ethnocentrism and immigrant scapegoating, the left will never win against the right" — but the conclusion she draws, that the left should therefore change the subject to class, underestimates how much the demand for democratic control over membership is itself a class demand. Working-class voters are not confused when they ask who lives here and on what terms; they are asking whether the political community is still theirs to shape.

A more powerful version of Ypi's argument would, I think, distinguish more sharply between the form of bounded citizenship and its current substance. The form — that there exists a political community with the right to determine its own composition and to extend rights to those it admits — is not dispensable; it is the precondition for any project that wants to "shift the costs of economic decisions from workers to capital." The substance — what kinds of admission, on what criteria, with what protections, against what background of international obligation — is precisely what is up for political contestation. On substance, almost everything Ypi says is right: golden visas are an obscenity; integration regimes that condemn people to permanent second-class status are cruel and corrosive; the EU's externalization of migration control to Libyan militias is indefensible; the structural sources of displacement (debt, austerity, climate, the wars she rightly names from the Balkans to Iraq to Libya) demand redress at source. A left that argued all of this while also taking seriously the demand for democratic regulation of admission — including against the wealthy, whose mobility is far more politically corrosive than the poor's — would not be conceding to the right. It would be doing what the right pretends to do and never does: reclaiming the polity from capital.

Ypi ends with the warning that "first they will come for irregular migrants, then for resident noncitizens, then for citizens whose names are Mohamed and Abdallah." She is right to issue it. The historical pattern is real, and on this point I would not soften her language by a syllable. But the answer to that pattern is not the dissolution of the demos into cosmopolitan abstraction. It is the construction of a demos that includes Mohamed and Abdallah as full members, that taxes the man with the Platinum Card, and that knows the difference. That is harder than either open borders or closed ones. It is also, I suspect, the only proposition that can actually win.

Lea Ypi’s incisive essay rightly names the real border crisis as a crisis of liberal democracy itself, not of migrant numbers. Her diagnosis of a global order in which class conflict, neoliberal restructuring, and the commodification of citizenship drive forced displacement is a necessary corrective to the culturalist hysteria dominating public debate. Ypi’s linking of the Washington Consensus’s wreckage, the selling of Golden Visas to the wealthy, and the criminalisation of poor migrants is an indictment that progressives ignore at their peril. Yet for all its analytical power, the essay’s prescriptive horizon—a class-first internationalism that rejects the membership frame entirely—underestimates the immediate political terrain the left actually inhabits and the moral weight that legitimate, bounded political communities retain even for those who deplore the current order.

The strength of Ypi’s argument lies in its refusal to treat migration as a problem of cultural maladaptation. She demonstrates that anti-immigrant sentiment rises even as numbers fall, revealing a political and economic malaise manipulated by elites. Her account of citizenship becoming a luxury commodity—Trump’s $5 million “Platinum Card” juxtaposed with shackled deportees—captures the obscene asymmetry of a system that turns human mobility into a class privilege. And she is correct that social-democratic parties that mimic nativism will never outbid the far right; Keir Starmer’s “island of strangers” rhetoric is as electorally shortsighted as it is morally bankrupt.

However, Ypi’s reconstruction of the Enlightenment ideal through Lessing’s Nathan the Wise, while moving, also reveals the limits of her approach. She celebrates Nathan’s forward-looking cosmopolitanism, his refusal to define himself by nation or creed. But note: Nathan returns home. He is no permanent nomad; his rootedness in Jerusalem, his particular relationships and obligations, are not incidental to his humanity. The play itself does not ask us to transcend all membership, only to refuse its descent into tribal supremacism. A democratic politics that simply dismisses the emotional and institutional reality of political membership—the fact that people understand their moral obligations as running first to those with whom they share a scheme of social cooperation—risks floating free of the very communities whose solidarity it needs. Ypi’s argument that the moral weight of the citizen–migrant distinction is “merely contingent” and parasitic on an arbitrary territorial association is philosophically bold, but it overlooks why, for many, that contingency has felt morally significant for generations: because democratic states, however imperfect, have been the primary arenas in which ordinary people have fought for and won rights, built public goods, and forged bonds of mutual responsibility. Dismissing that history as a capitalist ruse demobilises the very working-class agents Ypi wants to recruit.

A second difficulty is the article’s treatment of culture. Ypi is right that the “clash of civilisations” frame is a dangerous fiction, and that class cuts across national lines. Yet her insistence that all political conflicts be read as class conflicts between domestic and international elites and workers flattens an awkward reality. In many receiving societies, tensions over migration are not solely manufactured by capital; they also reflect genuine clashes over liberal norms—gender equality, religious freedom, sexual orientation—that cannot be reduced to economic grievance. When a social-democratic politician in Germany worries that parallel societies harbour illiberal attitudes towards women or LGBTQ people, the response cannot simply be that this is a distraction from class war. The working-class voters who express those anxieties are not only dupes of right-wing propaganda; they often live in the neighbourhoods where such tensions are most acutely felt. An effective left politics must offer a language that addresses both economic precarity and the defence of liberal democratic culture, without ceding the latter to chauvinists. Ypi’s framework, by framing any acknowledgment of cultural friction as a capitulation to the membership frame, leaves the left ill-equipped for this task.

The most pressing challenge, however, concerns electoral strategy and institutional reality. Ypi concedes that rejecting dominant exclusionary discourses “even at the price of a short-term loss” may be necessary. But the repeated short-term losses of left parties across Europe over the past decade have not been mere blips; they have delivered power to ethnonationalist governments that have devastated asylum systems, dismantled welfare states, and shredded labour protections far more aggressively than any cautious social democrat. Telling the left to abandon the electoral arena of parliamentary democracy and shift to “counter-hegemonic projects” beyond narrow cycles sounds principled, but it neglects the fact that state power—control over borders, labour law, public investment—is exercised now. Refusing to engage with border politics from inside the state means ceding that power entirely to the right. The alternative cannot be a purely outsidist politics of transnational worker solidarity which, while inspiring, remains fragmentary. The “Make Amazon Pay” campaign exemplifies coordination across borders, but it is a far cry from transforming the global migration regime, and its achievements depend precisely on the legal frameworks of the states where strikes occur. Without a strategy to win and wield state power, the left’s cosmopolitanism risks becoming, to borrow Craig Calhoun’s phrase, the class consciousness of frequent-travelling activists rather than a politics of the majority.

What might a more plausible left response look like? It would take Ypi’s class analysis seriously without jettisoning the democratic state as an irredeemable site of capitalist control. It would defend the right to asylum and legal pathways for migration while acknowledging that any viable welfare state requires some capacity to plan public services and housing, which in turn implies a managed migration system—one that is transparent, humane, and decoupled from the cruelty of deportation spectacle, but still a system. It would fight against the commodification of citizenship while recognising that political membership can, in better democratic conditions, serve as a tool for inclusion rather than exclusion. And it would invest massive resources in integration, in language training, in anti-discrimination enforcement, and in rebuilding public infrastructure so that higher immigration does not become a zero-sum competition for scarce social goods. This is not Ypi’s vision of a world where nobody is forced to leave their home; that remains a distant horizon. In the meantime, the left must govern the borders we have, while relentlessly attacking the global economic injustices that make those borders lethal.

Ypi’s essay performs an essential task by exposing the hypocrisy of liberal states that preach universality while erecting fortress walls for the poor and open gangplanks for the rich. Her call to see migration as a symptom of class war is a vital counterweight to nativist panic. Yet the left cannot simply stand on the right side of history and wait for the long run. A politics that insists on the total overcoming of the membership frame risks making the perfect the enemy of the good—and in that interval, the very vulnerable people she seeks to defend will continue to drown in the Adriatic, as Ben the Lame did, while liberal democracies drift further into oligarchy. The real border crisis is indeed a crisis of democracy, but the democratic repair must begin where people actually are: inside states, with their imperfect but real collective identities, their constrained but still-useable institutions, and their desperate need for a politics that offers both security and solidarity.

Lea Ypi’s sweeping essay offers a profound and necessary corrective to the dominant narratives surrounding global migration. By shifting the focus away from the fabricated culture wars of the right and the defensive, apologetic triangulations of the center-left, Ypi rightly identifies the "border crisis" not as a clash of civilizations, but as a symptom of a violently unequal global economic order. She is entirely correct that the same neoliberal consensus that demands the uninhibited movement of capital also produces the very crises—deindustrialization, debt traps, and the dismantling of the public sector—that force the most vulnerable people in the Global South to abandon their homes. Furthermore, her indictment of the gross hypocrisy of contemporary citizenship—where a desperately poor asylum seeker is chained and deported while a billionaire can purchase a "Platinum Card" from Donald Trump or a European "golden visa" for a few million dollars—is devastatingly accurate. We are witnessing the unabashed commodification of political belonging.

However, while Ypi’s diagnosis of the ravages of global capitalism is impeccable, her proposed prescription—a wholesale rejection of national membership in favor of borderless, transnational class solidarity—founders on the very political realities she urges us to confront. In her noble attempt to reclaim the cosmopolitan spirit of the Enlightenment, Ypi dismisses the bounded nation-state as little more than an instrument of capitalist exclusion. In doing so, she strips the left of the only institutional mechanism that has ever successfully constrained the excesses of capital: the democratic, territorial welfare state. Ypi argues that to restrict immigration is to pit vulnerable domestic workers against vulnerable foreign workers, thereby doing the bidding of ruling elites. But this framework ignores the deeply institutional, material, and democratic prerequisites of social democracy.

The central flaw in Ypi’s argument lies in her characterization of the left’s concern over migration as mere ideological capitulation. She targets politicians like Germany’s Sahra Wagenknecht, accusing them of idealizing the liberal state and falsely claiming a "trade-off" between openness to immigration and support for the welfare state. For Ypi, this trade-off is an illusion propagated by an oligarchic system looking to divide the working class. But the tension between porous borders and a robust welfare state is not a right-wing myth; it is an enduring institutional reality.

Welfare states are not magical wellsprings of infinite resources. They are historically contingent, politically fragile systems of mutual insurance that require deep civic solidarity, high levels of taxation, and a clearly defined pool of contributors and beneficiaries. Universal healthcare, public housing, and comprehensive unemployment benefits rely on a social contract in which citizens agree to pool their resources under the assumption of reciprocal obligation. When population flows are rapid, unmanaged, and asymmetric, they place acute, localized pressures on public services that have been scaled and funded for a specific demographic baseline. To acknowledge that sudden surges in population can strain municipal budgets, exacerbate severe housing shortages, and depress wages in the most precarious sectors of the labor market is not to succumb to xenophobia. It is to acknowledge the material reality of governing.

By dismissing these pressures as mere artifacts of elite manipulation, Ypi effectively abandons the working-class citizens whose neighborhoods and public services bear the immediate brunt of rapid demographic change. When progressives refuse to acknowledge the spatial and material limits of local infrastructure, they do not foster transnational class consciousness; they simply drive their traditional constituents into the arms of the resurgent far-right. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s rhetoric may be uninspiring to the cosmopolitan ear, but the fundamental electoral imperative remains: left-wing parties cannot build an egalitarian society if they are structurally locked out of state power by voters who feel their material anxieties are being derided as false consciousness.

Furthermore, Ypi’s vision fundamentally misconstrues the relationship between democracy and borders. She urges us to decouple the concept of democracy from "legally recognized membership," advocating instead for a political space of contestation where all those subjected to coercion have a voice. This is a beautiful normative ideal, but democracy, as a functional system of self-rule, requires boundaries. Without a defined "we," the concept of collective self-determination dissolves. To tax, to regulate, and to redistribute wealth requires an authoritative jurisdiction.

Historically, the capitalist class has always preferred a borderless world—not for the sake of human rights, but to escape regulation, taxation, and labor organizing. The nation-state, for all its historic crimes and exclusions, has served as the primary fortress within which the working class has been able to mobilize, vote, and rewrite the rules of the economy. The labor movement achieved the eight-hour workday, child labor laws, and workplace safety standards by capturing the legislative apparatus of the bounded state. Ypi asserts that employers favor "the movement of people without rights," which is true. But the way to ensure people have rights is not to dismantle the concept of membership; it is to strictly enforce labor standards within the state's jurisdiction while managing the influx of new entrants so that the regulatory capacity of the state is not overwhelmed.

Ypi points to transnational labor campaigns, such as the 2023-24 "Make Amazon Pay" strikes, as the blueprint for a new, borderless political solidarity. Such movements are indeed vital and inspiring. Cross-border union coordination is a crucial tool against multinational monopolies. Yet, we must be ruthlessly realistic about the limits of this approach. A coordinated strike can win a modest wage increase from a single employer, but a transnational union cannot levy a wealth tax, build public housing, mandate universal healthcare, or enforce environmental regulations. Only the coercive power of a democratic state can achieve these systemic transformations. By suggesting that the left should prioritize abstract transnational alliances over the defense and capture of national political institutions, Ypi asks the most vulnerable members of society to trade the tangible protections of the welfare state for the utopian promise of a global workers' revolution that has been perpetually delayed for a century and a half.

None of this is to say that Ypi is wrong about the grotesque nature of our current border regimes. The fact that the Global North extracts immense wealth from the Global South while violently repelling the very people displaced by that extraction is a moral atrocity. The systemic underdevelopment of poorer nations through structural adjustment programs, unfair trade regimes, and the looming specter of climate change—driven overwhelmingly by Western consumption—constitutes an ongoing crisis of justice. Ypi is entirely right to demand that we stop viewing migrants as invaders and start viewing them as casualties of a broken global order.

But how should a responsible, progressive politics respond to this reality? If the answer is not the false binary of either capitulating to right-wing nationalism or embracing open-border utopianism, what is the alternative?

A genuine left alternative must operate on two tracks: domestic realism and international radicalism. Domestically, progressive parties must unapologetically defend the integrity of the welfare state. This means defending managed migration systems that align the intake of newcomers with the state’s capacity to provide housing, healthcare, and education. It means decoupling migration policy from the demands of corporate lobbies that seek cheap, exploitable labor to undermine domestic bargaining power. It also means cracking down fiercely on the commodification of citizenship. The "golden visas" and "Platinum Cards" Ypi rightfully excoriates must be abolished. Citizenship must be a status of equal democratic participation, not a luxury asset traded on the global market. At the same time, the state must grant immediate, robust labor protections to all residents within its borders, regardless of legal status, to prevent employers from using irregular migrants as a tool to undercut native workers.

Internationally, however, the left must embrace the radicalism Ypi advocates. The solution to forced displacement is not to simply absorb the displaced into Western welfare states—a mathematical and political impossibility—but to stop generating the conditions that force people to flee in the first place. This requires a fundamental overhaul of foreign policy and international economic institutions. It means abandoning the neoliberal trade regimes that destroy local agriculture in the Global South. It means massive, unconditional debt forgiveness for developing nations so they can build their own sovereign welfare states. It means aggressive technology transfers and climate reparations to help vulnerable nations adapt to a warming world. It means stopping the arms exports and imperial proxy wars that destabilize entire regions.

In short, the left must fight for a world where people have the "right to stay," rather than merely treating the "right to move" as the highest expression of freedom. The goal of international solidarity should not be the abolition of borders, but the equalization of the conditions on either side of them.

Let us return to Lessing’s *Nathan the Wise*, as Ypi does in her conclusion. It is true that Nathan’s cosmopolitanism asks us to look to the future, to build a world based on universal reason rather than inherited tribalism. But the Enlightenment was also an era of immense practical state-building. The philosophers of the Enlightenment understood that liberty and equality could not exist in the ether; they had to be institutionalized. They required constitutions, laws, and republics capable of defending themselves against the arbitrary power of monarchs and oligarchs.

Today, the arbitrary power that threatens us is global capital. To resist it, we cannot simply rely on the moral superiority of a borderless ideal. We must wield the tools of democratic sovereignty. We must recognize that the national political community, while imperfect and historically exclusive, remains the most potent engine for social justice yet devised. To protect the universal values of freedom, equality, and solidarity, we must have the courage to govern the real world, rather than waiting for a perfect one. Defending the democratic state and its borders is not a betrayal of the migrant; it is the preservation of the only sanctuary capable of giving those Enlightenment values enduring, material life.

Lea Ypi is right to insist that what is usually called a “border crisis” is not, at bottom, a crisis caused by migrants. The spectacular images—the boats, the fences, the encampments, the deportation flights—invite us to mistake a symptom for a cause. People do not risk the Darién Gap, the Mediterranean, the Channel, the Sahara, or the Sonoran Desert because mobility is a lifestyle preference. They move because violence, poverty, repression, family separation, ecological stress, or sheer blocked possibility has made staying intolerable. And they arrive in states whose political classes have spent decades weakening public housing, labor protections, asylum administration, and social insurance, only to blame newcomers for the scarcity that follows. On this, Ypi’s argument is powerful and necessary.

She is also right that the class character of contemporary mobility is obscene. The world has become increasingly open to capital, credentials, and inherited advantage, while becoming increasingly punitive toward those who travel with nothing but need. The same states that criminalize the poor migrant court the investor migrant; the same governments that warn of “queue jumping” create premium queues for the rich. Golden visas, citizenship-by-investment programs, and fast-track residence for the highly skilled expose the fiction that restrictive migration policy is simply about preserving civic solidarity. Often it is about sorting human beings by market value.

But if Ypi’s essay clarifies what is morally corrupt in the current order, it risks understating a different problem: democratic border politics cannot be reconstructed by class analysis alone. Class is indispensable, but it is not exhaustive. The fact that anti-migrant politics is frequently manipulated by elites does not mean that all concerns about scale, pace, institutional capacity, or civic trust are mere mystification. Nor does the fact that borders are unjustly administered mean that borders as such have no democratic function. The challenge is to defend migrants without pretending that membership, consent, and public capacity are politically irrelevant.

A humane politics has to hold two truths together. First, affluent democracies have far greater obligations to migrants and refugees than they typically admit. Their wealth is entangled with colonial extraction, carbon emissions, military interventions, unequal trade, and international financial rules that have constrained poorer states’ choices. They also need migrants: demographically, economically, culturally, and morally. Second, even just societies would need institutions to regulate entry, allocate resources, protect labor standards, and sustain the confidence that makes redistribution possible. The alternative to cruel borders is not no governance; it is better governance.

This distinction matters because state capacity is not a right-wing fantasy. When asylum systems are backlogged for years, everyone suffers: refugees remain in limbo, receiving communities lose trust, and opportunistic politicians turn administrative failure into civilizational panic. When cities receive large numbers of newcomers without housing, school places, health services, language support, or work authorization, the result is not solidarity but competition under conditions of scarcity. That competition is not the fault of migrants, but neither is it imaginary. A low-wage citizen waiting for social housing may be wrong to blame a refugee family, but she is not wrong to notice that the state has failed both of them.

Ypi warns that the left should not accept the right’s framing. I agree. But there is a difference between accepting xenophobic premises and acknowledging institutional constraints. If progressives respond to every concern about migration levels as racism in disguise, they abandon the terrain on which democratic legitimacy is built. Voters can be misled, but they are not simply raw material for enlightenment by better theorists. Democratic politics requires persuasion, and persuasion begins by taking seriously the lived experience of people who feel that decisions affecting their neighborhoods, wages, schools, and public services are made elsewhere.

The stronger progressive claim is not that borders do not matter. It is that the current border regime produces disorder by design. In the United States, decades of congressional failure have created a system in which employers rely on unauthorized labor while politicians denounce the workers who supply it. In Europe, safe legal routes remain narrow, asylum processing is uneven, and responsibility is pushed onto border states or poorer neighbors. The 2024 EU Pact on Migration and Asylum promised order, but much of its energy went into accelerated procedures, detention-like border facilities, and externalization. Britain’s Rwanda scheme became a symbol of performative deterrence: expensive, legally contested, and morally degrading. These policies do not restore control; they dramatize control while deepening vulnerability.

The central labor-market point in Ypi’s essay is exactly right: employers do not simply want migrants; they want migrants with fewer rights. A worker tied to a single employer, afraid of deportation, excluded from welfare, or unable to unionize is easier to exploit. That exploitation then undercuts standards for everyone. The answer is not to exclude the worker but to remove the vulnerability: broad work authorization, strong wage enforcement, firewalls between labor inspection and immigration enforcement, portable visas, sectoral bargaining, and serious penalties for employers who use status as a weapon. If undocumented workers can report wage theft without risking removal, they become less useful as tools of discipline against citizen workers.

Still, the politics of solidarity cannot be reduced to shared material interest. People also ask who “we” are, what obligations we owe one another, and how common institutions can survive disagreement. These questions can be answered badly—with blood-and-soil nationalism, religious exclusion, or racial hierarchy. But they cannot be avoided. The postwar welfare states Ypi invokes were never purely class machines; they were also national projects, often built around narratives of shared sacrifice, public contribution, and mutual obligation. Their exclusions were real and often shameful. Yet their solidarities were real too. The task is not to pretend that democratic belonging is obsolete, but to widen and revise it.

There are examples of such widening. The reception of Ukrainians after Russia’s full-scale invasion showed that European states can move quickly when they choose: temporary protection, work rights, school access, and public sympathy were mobilized at scale. The contrast with Syrians, Afghans, Sudanese, Eritreans, or Palestinians revealed racial and geopolitical double standards. But it also demonstrated a practical lesson: when legal status is clear, work is permitted, and local authorities are supported, reception is less chaotic. Asylum claims should be decided quickly, fairly, and with legal representation—not through years of destitution followed by sudden removal, and not through sham “safe third country” arrangements. People without valid claims should not be brutalized, but a system that never reaches decisions will lose public support and empower authoritarians. Regularization should be available for long-settled residents, especially those with families and work histories. Housing, schools, clinics, translation services, and legal aid are not sentimental extras; they are the infrastructure of integration. If the public sees newcomers housed only by displacing the already insecure, the far right wins. If new arrivals are matched with visible investment that benefits existing residents too, solidarity becomes materially plausible.

Universities and employers should not be allowed to use international students and migrant workers as revenue streams or disposable labor. Rich states should expand climate finance, debt relief, public-health investment, and fair trade rules; they should stop exporting arms into conflicts while lamenting refugees from those conflicts; they should support regional refugee hosts, who continue to bear a disproportionate share of responsibility. A world in which the poor are immobilized by visa regimes, displaced by economic restructuring, then criminalized when they move is not a liberal world in any meaningful sense. They promise universal rights while rationing them by passport, wealth, and race; they promise control while producing illegality; they promise solidarity while starving the public realm.

The left should refuse the lie that migrants are the enemy. But it should also refuse the temptation to answer border cruelty with border abstraction. The humane alternative is neither fortress nor fantasy: it is a politics that expands legal mobility, protects refugees, empowers all workers, funds integration, democratizes membership, and confronts the global inequalities that make movement desperate. Such a politics would not abolish every border tomorrow. It would make borders less like instruments of class rule and more like accountable institutions constrained by justice. That may fall short of cosmopolitan redemption, but it would be a beginning worthy of Nathan’s wager on the future.

**In Defense of the Bounded Demos**

Lea Ypi has written a brilliant and frustrating essay. Brilliant because her diagnosis of how citizenship has been commodified at the top and weaponized at the bottom is unanswerable: the Trump Platinum Card and the chained deportation flights are indeed two faces of the same political economy, and any honest politics must reckon with that. Frustrating because she uses this real and outrageous asymmetry as a wedge to dislodge something quite different — the idea that democratic political community has, or could have, legitimate boundaries at all. The first argument is correct. The second does not follow, and adopting it would, I think, undermine the very emancipatory tradition Ypi claims to be defending.

Let me start where Ypi is strongest. The contrast between the investor visa and the deportation flight is not incidental. It tells us that the contemporary border is not really a wall around a community; it is a sorting mechanism for capital. The same state that cannot find a lawful pathway for a Salvadoran agricultural worker who has been picking American tomatoes for twelve years can find a very quick pathway for a Russian fertilizer magnate willing to write a check. Ypi is right that this is corrupting, that it exposes the "rule of law" rhetoric as ideology, and that no progressive politics can accept it. If her essay were simply a sustained polemic against the sale of citizenship and the criminalization of poverty at the border, I would sign it without revision.

But Ypi wants more than this. She wants to argue that the very distinction between members and non-members of a polity is "parasitic" on capital's prior interests — that to speak of the obligations a state owes its own citizens before those it owes outsiders is already to have surrendered to the class enemy. Here she goes too far, and her argument depends on an idealization of the alternative — transnational, class-based solidarity — that is at least as utopian as the liberal nationalism she critiques.

The bounded democratic community has a real and defensible point. It is the only political form we have ever found that can do three things at once: hold rulers accountable to the ruled, redistribute resources from the better-off to the worse-off, and sustain those redistributions across time through cycles of reciprocity that voters can verify. None of these functions operates well — perhaps none operates at all — once the demos becomes coextensive with humanity. Accountability requires identifiable rulers and identifiable ruled. Redistribution requires a fiscal community whose members trust that their contributions will be matched by others'. Reciprocity requires a horizon of expectation that today's contributor will be tomorrow's beneficiary, and vice versa. The historical record is fairly clear here: every robust welfare state on earth was built within the boundaries of a nation, and most were built on quite thick assumptions about shared fate.

Ypi knows this. Her response is to characterize such arguments as "an idealized account of the liberal democratic state." She points out, correctly, that real existing states have entrenched property hierarchies, excluded racial minorities, and recognized rights only formally. But the same could be said — and Ypi has elsewhere said it powerfully, in her writing on Albania — about real existing socialism, real existing internationalism, real existing anti-imperialism. The fact that an ideal has been imperfectly realized is not an argument against the ideal; it is, if anything, an argument for working out the conditions under which it might be better realized. And those conditions, historically, have involved binding particular peoples to particular institutions through which they could exercise collective authorship of their conditions of life.

Consider Ypi's own example: the great expansions of the franchise. Suffragists, trade unionists, civil-rights campaigners, anticolonial nationalists — she lists them as the heroes of an expansive citizenship. But the striking thing about all four movements is that they sought *inclusion into* a bounded political community, not its dissolution. The Indian National Congress wanted Indian self-government, not the abolition of the political. The British suffragettes wanted votes in British elections. The American civil-rights movement wanted full membership in the American republic. None of them imagined that the answer to their exclusion was to deny that bounded membership had any moral weight. They imagined that membership had to be made real — and they were, of course, right.

Ypi will reply that I am missing the asymmetry: yes, citizenship has been the prize of struggle, but the struggle has always been from below by the excluded, and treating today's excluded migrants as the equivalent of yesterday's disenfranchised workers requires us to dismantle, not defend, the citizen/non-citizen line. This is a serious argument and I want to take it seriously. But I think it equivocates between two very different categories of "excluded": those who live and work permanently inside a polity but are denied its rights (the appropriate analogue to the disenfranchised worker), and those who happen to live elsewhere and would like to come (a different category altogether). The first group has an overwhelmingly strong claim — they are subject to the state's coercion, they are part of its productive life, they should be inside the demos. The second group's claim is real but different: it concerns the global distribution of opportunity, which is a question about international justice, development, and the structure of world institutions, not about whether national democracies should exist.

Run them together — as Ypi tends to — and you produce a politics that cannot speak to either constituency well. To the long-resident undocumented worker, you say: your problem is the existence of borders, not your particular exclusion from rights inside one. To the would-be migrant in Lagos or Tegucigalpa, you say: the answer to global inequality is that you should be able to move to Europe or the United States, rather than that your country should be able to develop on terms that do not require you to leave. The first answer is too radical for the problem; the second is too modest. And politically, neither answer can sustain the cross-class coalitions on which any actually-existing left depends.

There is also a question Ypi underweights: what democracy requires from those who participate in it. Ypi treats integration tests, language requirements, and civic education with a fairly broad brush as obstacles imposed on the poor. Some of them are. But the underlying principle — that membership in a self-governing community involves some shared linguistic and civic infrastructure — is not a Trumpian invention. It is closer to a precondition for being able to argue at all. A society in which no common language can be assumed, in which civic traditions are not transmitted, in which the demos is in continual demographic flux that outpaces institutional adaptation, is not a society more conducive to working-class power. It is a society more conducive to the very oligarchy Ypi rightly fears, because oligarchy thrives wherever the demos cannot constitute itself as a collective interlocutor.

What Ypi's essay actually demonstrates, against its own framing, is that we need *more* democratic nationalism, not less — but a democratic nationalism cleansed of its ethnocultural pathologies. Pay full citizens fully; integrate long-resident migrants quickly and unconditionally; make asylum work; ban the sale of passports; tax capital where it accumulates; redistribute aggressively; cooperate internationally on the global structures that generate displacement. This is a recognizably social-democratic program. It is not the cosmopolitan deconstruction of citizenship. And it is, I would suggest, the only program that has ever delivered any portion of the freedom, equality, and solidarity Ypi correctly identifies as the unfinished business of the Enlightenment.

**Solidarity Is Thicker Than Ypi Thinks**

Lea Ypi's essay is animated by a generous moral vision and a sharp class analysis, but it depends on a sociological premise that I think is mistaken. The premise is that the obstacles to migrant inclusion in rich democracies are essentially ideological — products of "narrative," "hegemony," and a "lack of courage to think critically." Strip away the bad ideas, in this telling, and the conflicts between working-class natives and working-class migrants would be exposed as a confidence trick perpetrated by capital. I want to argue that this gets the causality wrong, and that the social-democratic concerns Ypi dismisses — the welfare-state-and-solidarity argument associated with figures like Wolfgang Streeck, David Goodhart, and yes, Sahra Wagenknecht — deserve a more serious hearing than she gives them.

Start with what is actually known about how welfare states function. A welfare state is not merely a redistributive mechanism; it is a long-running cooperative scheme in which citizens make sacrifices today (taxes, deferred consumption, compliance with regulations) in expectation of receiving benefits over the life cycle and across generations. Such schemes depend on what political scientists awkwardly call "fellow-feeling" or "diffuse reciprocity": the willingness of net contributors to keep contributing when they cannot directly observe whether the net beneficiaries are using the resources well, and the willingness of beneficiaries to acknowledge contributors' efforts. Decades of research — including by scholars sympathetic to immigration like Alberto Alesina, and by left-wing political economists like Wolfgang Streeck — suggest that the heterogeneity of the demos matters here. Not because there is anything wrong with diverse societies in principle, but because trust in shared institutions is built slowly and can be eroded quickly when people cease to recognize one another as participants in a common project.

This is not a "cultural" argument in the racist sense Ypi rightly excoriates. It is a structural argument about what makes generous welfare states politically sustainable. The Nordic states — the high-water mark of social-democratic achievement — were built on extraordinarily dense civic infrastructure, near-universal labor-market participation, strong unions, shared linguistic and educational backgrounds, and the resulting willingness of high earners to pay marginal tax rates that would be politically unthinkable in more heterogeneous polities. The point is not that diversity is bad; it is that the *speed* and *scale* of demographic change in many European societies in the 2010s and 2020s has run ahead of the institutional capacity to integrate new arrivals into the cooperative scheme — and that this lag has material consequences for the legitimacy of redistributive politics.

Ypi will say: but the right exploits these anxieties dishonestly, and the empirical record on whether migrants actually burden welfare states is mixed at best. Both true. But the question is not only what migrants empirically cost; it is whether voters perceive the system as fair. And fairness perceptions are sensitive to visible asymmetries — for example, between someone who has paid into a system for thirty years and someone who arrived last year, regardless of either party's underlying merit. Mocking these perceptions as false consciousness has been the social-democratic strategy for two decades. It has not worked. The Labour Party under Starmer, which Ypi correctly notes has adopted a depressing anti-migration register, did so precisely because its predecessors lost five elections in a row trying to make the cosmopolitan case Ypi prefers.

Here is where I think Ypi's analogy to past struggles for inclusion breaks down. The suffragettes, the labor movements, the civil-rights campaigners were demanding inclusion in a polity to which they already belonged in every meaningful sense: they were born there, raised there, worked there, paid taxes there, fought in its wars, were subject to its laws. The injustice of their exclusion was that the formal demos denied what the substantive demos already was. The case for migrant inclusion of long-resident workers — undocumented people who have lived in the U.S. or U.K. for decades, raised children, paid into the system — is exactly analogous, and progressives should fight for it tooth and nail. But the case for treating *prospective* migrants as if they were already members of the demos is a categorically different argument, and conflating the two is a rhetorical move, not a logical one.

What Ypi calls "the reduction of democracy to membership" is in fact one of democracy's foundational features. A demos that does not constitute itself — that does not say "we, these particular people, are going to govern ourselves together" — cannot democratically deliberate at all, because it has no stable referent for the "we" that does the deliberating. Migration policy, far from being a question on which the demos has no legitimate authority, is one of the questions on which a self-governing community most clearly does have authority, because it concerns the very composition of the community over time. To say that the demos cannot have meaningful views about who joins it is to say the demos is not really a self-governing community.

This is not a counsel for closed borders. There are many strong reasons — moral, economic, demographic — for relatively open immigration policies. But these reasons must be made within the framework of democratic self-government, not against it. A defensible center-left position would say: yes to large-scale legal migration, properly resourced; yes to a fast and fair asylum system; yes to a clear pathway to citizenship for long-resident undocumented people; yes to aggressive labor enforcement against employers who exploit migrant workers to undercut wages; no to the criminalization of poverty at the border; no to the commodification of citizenship at the top. But also: yes to integration policies that take language, civics, and shared institutions seriously, because these are the substrate of any solidaristic politics, and the demos has a legitimate interest in maintaining them.

The strongest section of Ypi's essay is her insistence that the rich and the poor face radically different border regimes. She is right that golden visas are a scandal and that they expose the hypocrisy of "rule of law" rhetoric. But the appropriate inference is not that all borders are illegitimate; it is that borders should not be a market. Citizenship should not be for sale, full stop. And the reason it should not be for sale is precisely because it is supposed to be a relationship of mutual obligation among co-citizens, not a commodity transaction. The argument *against* selling citizenship is parasitic on the very thick conception of bounded political community that Ypi elsewhere wants to dissolve.

Finally, a word about Lessing. Nathan the Wise is a beautiful text, but it is also, in its own way, an idealization. Nathan is a figure who can be cosmopolitan because he stands above the actual conflicts of medieval Jerusalem — wealthy, learned, indifferent to revenge. Most actual humans, including most actual migrants and most actual citizens, are not Nathans. They are embedded in particular communities, attached to particular places, dependent on particular institutions. A politics that treats this embeddedness as a problem to be overcome by sufficient critical reflection has misunderstood what politics is for. The Enlightenment Ypi rightly cherishes is not the dissolution of the particular into the universal; it is the patient, fallible construction of universalisms *through* particular institutions. Citizenship in a bounded, self-governing community is not the enemy of that project. It is, on the available evidence, its indispensable vehicle.

**View From the Border**

I write this from a position Lea Ypi does not occupy and most of her commentators will not occupy either: I have spent the past decade working in legal aid and accompaniment with migrants in detention and at the U.S. southern border. So forgive me if my response is less philosophical than the conversation Ypi has invited. I want to record what looks different from where I work, and what looks the same.

What looks the same — and Ypi is exactly right about this — is the structural pattern. The people I sit with in detention are almost without exception poor, dark-skinned, and from countries whose recent histories include U.S.-backed coups, U.S.-backed counterinsurgencies, U.S.-driven trade displacement, U.S.-funded drug wars, or U.S.-tolerated kleptocracies. The Honduran women I work with did not appear from nowhere. They came from a country whose 2009 coup was acquiesced to by the U.S. State Department, whose subsequent governments dismantled rural support programs under IMF pressure, and whose murder rate for women is among the highest in the world. The Guatemalans came from communities whose Indigenous economies were broken by NAFTA-era corn imports. Ypi's "broken order" is not a metaphor. It is the specific historical record of what was done to these people's countries, by my country, within living memory.

What looks the same: the obscenity of asymmetric mobility. I have watched a Honduran mother be denied bond because she could not document an address, while in the same week a Russian-Israeli businessman with a residence-by-investment portfolio was processed through customs in twenty minutes. Ypi's Platinum Card contrast is not a clever rhetorical move; it is the empirical reality of every day at every port of entry.

What looks the same: the use of irregular status as a wage-suppression tool. The construction sites, meatpacking plants, hotels, and farms of the United States depend structurally on workers who cannot file labor complaints, cannot unionize without exposure to ICE, cannot demand overtime, cannot leave abusive employers without losing their precarious foothold. When Tyson Foods's poultry plants are raided, the workers are deported; the executives are not even charged. The border, in this respect, functions less as a wall than as a sorting mechanism that determines who has rights inside the country and who does not. Ypi sees this clearly.

Now what looks different. The migrants I work with are not, on the whole, looking for the abolition of borders. They are looking for *a fair process*. They want to know whether they will be allowed to stay or not. They want their asylum claim heard by someone who will listen. They want, if they cannot stay, to be sent back with dignity rather than chained in a flight without their belongings. They want their children to be allowed to go to school. They want to be able to work without being extorted. Most of them want, eventually, to become citizens of the country they have come to, because they understand that citizenship is the only legal status that confers real protection.

This matters because Ypi's framework, taken to its logical end, can lead progressives to underinvest in the boring legal-procedural work that actually transforms migrants' lives. The Biden administration's asylum policies, the immigration court backlog, the legal-aid funding cuts, the work-permit timelines — these are the terrain on which actual migrants live or die. A politics that talks about "the commodification of citizenship" without also talking about whether the asylum office in San Antonio is staffed is a politics that the people I work with cannot use.

I also want to push back, gently, on the dismissal of the "trade-off" argument. Ypi is right that it is often offered in bad faith. But there is a version of it that I encounter regularly from working-class native-born U.S. citizens — including African-American workers in the South — that deserves a serious answer rather than a structural-analysis brush-off. The version is this: the same construction company that hires undocumented workers at $12 an hour was paying $20 an hour, with benefits, twenty years ago. The same nursing home that staffs itself with workers on H-2B visas was paying $18 an hour to local women a generation back. The undocumented worker is not the cause of this — Ypi is absolutely right that the cause is the employer's power and the state's refusal to enforce labor law — but the worker is the proximate experience of it. Telling that displaced native worker that her resentment is "false consciousness" is not going to build the cross-border solidarity Ypi rightly wants. Working-class voters notice this, and they have noticed it long enough that they no longer trust the people who do it. Ypi quotes this against the rich, but the line is just as cutting against her. The contemporary cosmopolitan-progressive position on migration is held overwhelmingly by people whose own jobs, neighborhoods, and children's schools are not in the front line of demographic change. It is held by people who can afford to live in the parts of London or New York or Berlin where high-skilled migration is a cultural amenity rather than a competition for housing. The British factory town that voted Leave in 2016 was not voting against Lessing; it was voting against thirty years of being told by people insulated from the consequences that its concerns were ignorant. The diagnosis was wrong then and it is wrong now.

Now to the substance. Ypi's argument has a sleight of hand at its core. She wants to deny that there is any meaningful distinction between "deserving" and "undeserving" migrants — the asylum seeker and the economic migrant, the political refugee and the chancer — on the grounds that such distinctions are tools of class power. But the distinction is not invented by capital; it is a moral distinction that ordinary people, including ordinary migrants, recognize and respect. The Albanian who fled Hoxha was a refugee. She assumes they have been manipulated. This is the standard cosmopolitan-progressive move, and it is not respectful of democracy. It is condescending toward it.

There is a deeper philosophical issue here. Ypi treats the bounded democratic state as essentially a vehicle for class power, useful or harmful depending on whose hands it is in. But the bounded democratic state is also the only form of political organization in which ordinary people have ever exercised meaningful authority over their conditions of life. The labor movements Ypi celebrates were built within national frameworks. The welfare states she presupposes were built within national frameworks. The civil-rights victories she cites were won within national frameworks. The transnational labor solidarity she gestures toward — the Make Amazon Pay campaign — is, by any honest assessment, marginal, episodic, and incapable of substituting for the national institutions on which working-class power has historically depended.

What Ypi proposes, stripped of its rhetorical adornment, is that working-class voters in rich countries should accept large-scale migration, accept the resulting pressure on wages and services, accept the rapid transformation of their neighborhoods, and accept that any objection on their part will be classified as ethnocentrism — all on the promise that one day, through cross-border labor organizing, a global working class will materialize. This is not a serious political program. It is a confession of faith.

Second, that the rich countries' welfare states were built on assumptions of cultural and institutional continuity that her preferred policy strains. Third, that the political coalition required to defend her position does not exist in any rich country, and the trend lines are getting worse, not better. Fourth, that the actual existing alternative to her position is not Trumpian fascism but rather a defensible center-left politics of bounded democratic solidarity — the politics of, say, Denmark's Social Democrats — which she lumps in with the right and dismisses.

I do not write any of this in defense of Trumpian cruelty, the Platinum Card, the chained deportation flights, or any of the other genuine outrages Ypi rightly identifies. Those are wrong, full stop. But the answer to those outrages is not the dissolution of bounded democratic membership. It is the strengthening of it, for everyone inside, on terms the demos can recognize as fair. That is the politics that has historically delivered what little freedom, equality, and solidarity actually exist in the world. It is the politics Ypi cannot bring herself to defend, and so we are left with her elegant essay and the steady advance of the very forces it laments.

Lea Ypi's essay is sweeping, eloquent, and at the level of historical and philosophical interpretation, I find much of it persuasive. What I want to do here is add the dimension her essay treats only glancingly: the empirical and demographic substrate of the migration debate, which is going to matter enormously over the next twenty-five years and which neither the cosmopolitan left nor the nationalist right has yet absorbed.

Italy, which Ypi mentions, has a total fertility rate of around 1.20. Spain is similar. Germany is around 1.35. South Korea, the leading edge, is at roughly 0.7 — meaning each generation is less than half the size of the one before. China's rate has collapsed below 1.1. The United States, long an outlier, is now at about 1.6 and falling. These are not cyclical fluctuations. They are structural and, on the available evidence, accelerating.

The implication for migration is straightforward and is essentially never stated in the political debate: the rich-world labor force is going to contract sharply over the next two decades, with or without migration. The pension systems, health systems, and tax bases that ordinary citizens depend on cannot be maintained at current ratios. The question is not whether there will be substantial migration into Europe, North America, and East Asia in coming decades; the question is whether it will be organized or chaotic, legal or illegal, integrated or marginalized. The political class that postures against migration is, materially, postponing decisions that the actuarial tables have already made.

This vindicates Ypi's broader argument in a way she does not fully exploit. The anti-migration politics of the European and American right is not even consistent with the economic interests of the constituencies that support it. The Italian pensioner voting for Meloni is voting against the workers whose contributions will pay her pension. The American retiree voting for Trump is voting against the workers whose payroll taxes fund Medicare. The German voter angry about asylum applications is angry about the very inflows that, if integrated into the labor market quickly, would be the only realistic path to maintaining the Bismarckian welfare system. The anti-migration position is, in this respect, a self-mutilation dressed up as self-defense.

But — and here is where I want to depart from Ypi — these demographic facts also impose constraints she does not discuss. Migration on the scale demography requires has to be organized. It needs functioning legal pathways, working integration infrastructure, language acquisition programs, housing capacity, and labor-market matching. Countries that have done this well (Canada at its best, parts of Germany during the 2015–16 Syrian intake, Australia in some periods) have invested billions in the unglamorous administrative work of receiving people. Countries that have done it badly have not. The political viability of the migration the demography requires depends on these capacities existing — and they have been systematically defunded across the rich world for forty years, in the name of the same neoliberalism Ypi rightly indicts.

Second empirical observation: climate. The IPCC's most recent estimates suggest that by 2050, climate-driven displacement could plausibly involve 150 to 200 million people. This is a guess, the bands are wide, but no serious estimate is below 100 million. The corridors are already visible: Sahel to North Africa to Europe; Central America to North America; Bangladesh to India; Pacific island states to Australia and New Zealand. Climate migration does not fit cleanly into existing legal categories. Climate refugees are not refugees under the 1951 Convention. Climate-driven economic migrants are not protected by anything. The current legal architecture is not just inadequate to the coming flows — it is barely conceptually equipped to think about them.

This is where Ypi's call to "build institutions fostering joint bargaining at both national and transnational levels" needs to become more concrete. The contemporary international order has almost no functional institutions for managing population flows. The Global Compact for Migration is non-binding and was rejected by several of the states that most matter. The UNHCR is chronically underfunded. There is no climate refugee regime. There is no functioning framework for "complementary pathways" between sending and receiving countries. The void here is enormous and the cost of not filling it will be paid first by the migrants themselves, in deaths at sea and in deserts, and second by the political legitimacy of receiving-state democracies as they convulse under unmanaged pressure.

Third observation, and one that should give comfort to Ypi: the empirical record on the labor-market and fiscal effects of migration is, after decades of study, fairly clear. The mainstream finding — synthesized in the U.S. National Academies' 2017 report, OECD analyses, and Bank of England work in the U.K. — is that migration's effects on native wages are small and on aggregate fiscal positions positive, especially over long horizons. There are distributional effects: low-end native workers and previous migrants tend to experience modest wage competition; the gains accrue disproportionately to higher-end workers and consumers. These distributional effects are real and matter politically. But the aggregate picture is not the picture of disaster that the right portrays.

This points to a policy direction that Ypi could endorse and that has more political traction than her cosmopolitan framing: aggressive transfers from the gains of migration to the workers who bear its distributional costs. If migrants raise the productivity and tax base of rich countries — and they do — then the political legitimacy of migration depends on the gains being shared. The 2010s welfare-trade-off argument associated with Wagenknecht and Streeck is wrong empirically (migrants do not, on net, drain welfare states; they fund them) but it points at a real political fact: voters need to see that they personally benefit from the system, not just that the system is in aggregate beneficial. A pro-migration politics is sustainable only inside a redistributive politics.

Fourth and final observation. Ypi rightly emphasizes that remittances now exceed foreign aid by a factor of three. This is one of the most important and least-discussed facts in development economics. Migration is, by an enormous margin, the most effective anti-poverty intervention humans have ever devised — far more effective than any aid program, any structural-adjustment package, any trade agreement. A Honduran working in Houston transfers more to her family than any aid agency could ever deliver. If we cared about global poverty as much as we say we do, we would design our migration regimes to maximize remittance flows and worker protections, not minimize them.

This is, I think, where Ypi's argument lands strongest. The rich world's anti-migration politics is not just morally questionable; it is, on the evidence, the largest active obstacle to global poverty reduction. That is the real border crisis. Ypi's essay points at it through the language of class and Enlightenment; the numbers point at it through demography, climate, and development economics. The two languages should be speaking to one another more than they currently do.
