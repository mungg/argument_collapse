Vanilla Claude Opus 4.7

Piscopo's essay performs an important act of comparative imagination, asking U.S. feminists to look outward—particularly to Latin America—for models of how to translate formal suffrage into substantive representation. The reframing of citizenship as "the right to elect and be elected" is rhetorically powerful, and the catalog of quota mechanisms is a useful corrective to American provincialism. But the essay underplays features of the U.S. constitutional order that make the prescription harder than it appears, and it too quickly dismisses the candidate-recruitment model whose limits it diagnoses.

Start with the institutional problem. Piscopo acknowledges in passing that U.S. parties do not control ballot access, then proceeds as though this were a quirk rather than the foundation of how American elections work. Latin American and European quotas operate through party lists—the law tells a party which candidates it must nominate, and voters then ratify the list. The U.S. uses single-member districts filled by primaries in which the voters, not the party, choose nominees. A statutory requirement that, say, 40 percent of nominees be women would either have to bind primary voters (almost certainly unconstitutional under the First Amendment associational reasoning of *Tashjian* and *California Democratic Party v. Jones*) or apply to general-election ballot composition in ways that cut across the geography of districts. The reforms Piscopo offers as translatable—lower filing fees, party board quotas, short-list requirements—are real, but they are precisely the small-bore measures unlikely to move the 24-percent needle she wants moved.

Second, the essay's treatment of constitutional culture is too brisk. The negative/positive rights distinction Piscopo draws is real, but the United States is not merely missing a positive-rights tradition—it has an active jurisprudence of skepticism toward race- and sex-conscious government action. *Adarand*, *Croson*, and the line of equal protection cases applying strict and intermediate scrutiny to classifications by race and sex would almost certainly capture any quota statute. Rubio-Marín's worry, which Piscopo notes but waves past, is the central one: in a constitutional order where affirmative action for African Americans hangs by a thread, it is implausible that legislatures or courts would carve out a more aggressive regime for women. Zackin's state-constitutional optimism is appealing, but the positive rights she catalogs (education, labor protections) don't classify individuals by ascriptive characteristics in the way quotas do.

Third, the dichotomy between "lean in" and "lean out" is overdrawn. The candidate-training and donor-network model has actually produced results: 2018 saw a substantial increase in women's representation, and the pipeline organizations Piscopo somewhat condescends to have built the infrastructure that made it possible. The performance premium she cites cuts two ways—yes, it indicates discrimination, but it also suggests that mobilization and recruitment work. Twenty-five percent of the Senate is dismal compared to Rwanda, but it is roughly double what it was fifteen years ago, achieved without quotas.

None of this is to defend American complacency. Piscopo is right that the framing of "women's right to be elected" as a structural political right, rather than an individual ambition problem, would deepen the conversation. She is right that other democracies have outpaced us. But the path from that diagnosis to actionable U.S. reform runs through constitutional and institutional terrain her essay treats as scenery rather than obstacle. A serious comparative agenda has to engage those obstacles directly.

Vanilla DeepSeek V4 Pro

Jennifer M. Piscopo’s sweeping comparative analysis offers a bracing challenge to American complacency on women’s political representation. She marshals a compelling history, showing how activists from Argentina to Rwanda reinterpreted the old formula of “elect and be elected” into a positive right, obligating states to actively ensure women’s presence in office. The result has been a wave of gender quota and parity laws that, for all their imperfections, have vaulted dozens of countries ahead of the United States. On this centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment, Piscopo is right to insist that the women’s vote alone is insufficient and that Americans should look outward for tools. Yet her account, precisely because it rests on the transformation of political rights from negative to positive, also obscures the immense institutional and legal hurdles that make direct translation to the U.S. system so fraught—and potentially counterproductive.

The central obstacle is structural. In the Latin American and European cases that Piscopo celebrates, gender quotas succeeded because political parties control candidate selection and ballot access, and because legal systems recognized group-based remedies for historic discrimination. Mexico’s electoral court, for instance, could rule that party autonomy must yield to constitutional gender equality, directly imposing candidate parity. But the United States operates a candidate-centered system of primary elections in which parties lack that gatekeeping power. Anyone can file to run under a party label, and courts have long treated ballot access as an individual right against state interference. The Supreme Court’s line from *Williams v. Rhodes* to *Lubin v. Panish* rests, as Piscopo notes, on negative logic: the government may not impose undue burdens, but it is under no obligation to engineer outcomes. Asking American courts to pivot from that tradition to a positive duty to guarantee candidacy for a group would be a constitutional earthquake—one that contradicts the entire architecture of U.S. election law.

Voluntary party quotas, which Piscopo floats as an alternative, face their own severe limits. The Democratic Party’s 50-50 rule for internal committees has not translated into candidate slates because primary voters, not party bosses, decide nominations. A party pledge to recruit or fund women candidates cannot override the preferences of a local electorate—or the well-documented tendency of party insiders to channel resources to perceived front-runners, who are often men. Sweden’s voluntary party quotas work within a proportional representation system where parties submit ordered lists; in U.S. single-member districts, the “quota” would have to be applied district by district, colliding with the zero-sum logic that Piscopo herself acknowledges. It is no accident that the dramatic gains in women’s representation worldwide have occurred overwhelmingly in list-proportional systems, which the United States lacks.

Piscopo acknowledges the racism that deformed American suffrage and the unique toxicity of affirmative action politics, but she underestimates how completely those legacies would poison any quota debate. Even modest race-conscious measures like the Voting Rights Act’s preclearance provisions have been gutted by the courts. A law setting aside candidate slots or steering public funds to women would face an immediate equal protection challenge, with opponents wielding the same “colorblind” logic that doomed many affirmative action programs. While Costa Rica’s court embraced “compensatory inequalities,” U.S. jurisprudence has moved aggressively in the opposite direction, culminating in decisions like *Shelby County v. Holder*. In that environment, framing women’s underrepresentation as a rights violation requiring positive remedies is more likely to provoke years of litigation than to produce elected bodies that look like the nation.

This does not mean Americans should resign themselves to the status quo. Piscopo’s core insight—that treating the problem as one of women’s individual ambition deficits lets governments off the hook—is essential. But the path forward may need to run through different terrain: electoral system reform. Multi-member districts with ranked-choice voting, for example, have been shown in jurisdictions from Ireland to several U.S. cities to create a “pipeline” effect for women and candidates of color without explicit quotas, because they lower the advantage of incumbency and encourage coalition-building. Such reforms were gaining steam by 2020, with Maine adopting ranked-choice voting and cities like New York preparing to do so. They are no magic bullet, but they attack the structural barriers—gatekeeping by party insiders, the incumbency lock—that quotas address overseas, while operating within the American negative-rights framework.

Piscopo’s essay performs a vital service by provincializing the U.S. experience and showing that our stagnation is a policy choice, not a natural state. Yet adopting the instruments that succeeded elsewhere requires adapting them to a political ecosystem that is far more fragmented, more individualistic, and more legally resistant to group entitlements. If the lesson of global feminism is that the impossible becomes possible when someone tries, then American activists would do well to experiment with the tools that fit their own broken machinery, rather than transplanting engines designed for a different vehicle. The battle for women’s representation in the twenty-first century will indeed be waged on the terrain of rights—but which rights, and through which institutions, remains an open question.

Jennifer M. Piscopo rightly identifies a glaring hypocrisy in American democracy: a century after the Nineteenth Amendment, the United States remains embarrassingly behind the rest of the world in electing women to national office. Her critique of the "mobilization approach"—the distinctly American reliance on candidate-training programs and donor networks to bridge the gender gap—is both sharp and necessary. As she notes, constantly telling women they simply need to "lean in" or overcome their own "ambition deficit" ignores the structural realities of patriarchy and lets the political system off the hook. However, while Piscopo’s diagnosis of the problem is flawless, her proposed remedy—importing the positive-rights framework of gender quotas and parity laws from Latin America and Europe—fundamentally misjudges the legal and cultural bedrock of the U.S. electoral system.

Piscopo builds a compelling historical narrative around "passive suffrage," or the right to be elected. She demonstrates how feminist activists in countries like Argentina, Mexico, and Costa Rica leveraged this concept to mandate gender quotas, successfully arguing that democracy requires descriptive representation and that the state must proactively guarantee women’s presence in government. High courts in these nations upheld these quotas by embracing the doctrine of "compensatory inequalities"—the idea that ending historical discrimination demands unequal treatment in the present.

The fatal flaw in applying this roadmap to the United States is that American jurisprudence is fiercely, perhaps insurmountably, hostile to the concept of compensatory inequality in the electoral sphere. The U.S. Constitution’s Equal Protection Clause is interpreted through a lens of individual, not group, rights. For decades, the Supreme Court has consistently struck down strict numerical quotas in educational affirmative action and employment. Any legislative attempt to mandate that a certain percentage of candidates or legislative seats be reserved for women would be immediately subjected to heightened judicial scrutiny.

In the current judicial climate of 2020, a government-enforced gender quota would almost certainly be struck down as a violation of the equal protection rights of male candidates. Piscopo briefly acknowledges that positive action has a "tortured" history in the U.S., but she leans on political scientist Emily Zackin to suggest that positive rights are not entirely alien to American state constitutions. Yet, while state constitutions may guarantee material goods like public education or workers' rights, federal equal protection supremacy means explicit electoral quotas would face a legal brick wall.

Does this mean American women are doomed to inch forward at a glacial pace, entirely dependent on organizations like EMILY’s List to fight the systemic headwinds? Not necessarily. If we cannot legally mandate gender parity through positive rights, we must instead dismantle the underlying electoral mechanics that passively enforce male dominance. The solution lies not in explicit gender quotas, but in structural electoral reform—specifically, abandoning the single-member, winner-take-all districts that define American politics.

The U.S. relies on a first-past-the-post voting system. In a single-member district, political parties and donors are heavily incentivized to coalesce around the candidate they perceive as the most "electable" or "safe." Despite data showing that women actually possess a "performance premium," the lingering biases of party gatekeepers—often the local boys' clubs Piscopo mentions—mean the "safe" choice is traditionally a white man. Women, and especially women of color, are routinely told to "wait their turn" because gatekeepers fear splitting the vote or backing a candidate who deviates from the historical norm.

Transitioning to multimember districts combined with Ranked-Choice Voting (RCV) would fundamentally alter this calculus, achieving the effects of a quota system without triggering its constitutional tripwires. In a multimember district where three or five representatives are elected simultaneously, political parties are heavily incentivized to field a diverse slate of candidates to appeal to the broadest possible coalition of voters. A party running a slate of five identical men would be politically punished by the electorate. Furthermore, RCV eliminates the "spoiler" effect. Women can run without being accused of dividing the base, and voters can rank their true preferences without fear of wasting their vote. Early data from jurisdictions that have adopted RCV, such as Maine and various municipal governments across the country, show it consistently results in more women and people of color running and winning.

Beyond how we cast ballots, we must also reform how we fund campaigns. Piscopo is entirely correct that the burden of raising money acts as a discriminatory barrier. The U.S. candidate-centered system requires individuals to build their own fundraising infrastructure from scratch. Implementing robust public campaign financing—such as democracy vouchers or small-dollar matching funds—would dilute the power of wealthy, predominantly male donor networks. Crucially, these reforms align perfectly with the American "negative rights" framework: rather than mandating an outcome, they remove the structural and financial barriers that impede women's right to candidacy.

Piscopo provides a vital service by reframing women’s political underrepresentation as a violation of democratic justice rather than a mere failure of female ambition. She proves that a democracy cannot claim to be fully representative when half its population is systemically locked out of the halls of power. But rather than fighting a losing constitutional battle over explicit quotas, American reformers should channel this righteous demand for justice into overhauling the machinery of our elections. By implementing ranked-choice voting, multimember districts, and public financing, the United States can create an electoral environment where parity is not an artificial mandate, but the natural outcome of a truly representative system.

Jennifer Piscopo is right that the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should not be treated as a victory lap. A political system in which women can vote but remain dramatically underrepresented in office has not fulfilled the democratic promise of suffrage. Her most important move is to reject the familiar American story that women’s underrepresentation is mainly a problem of confidence, ambition, or training. The question is not why individual women fail to “lean in” hard enough. It is why parties, donors, media, electoral rules, and voters continue to make women clear higher hurdles than men.

Still, importing the language of the “right to be elected” into U.S. politics requires more than adding gender-conscious candidate policies to the existing system. The American problem is not only that women are underrepresented; it is that representation itself is filtered through institutions unusually hostile to broad inclusion. Single-member districts, winner-take-all elections, privately financed campaigns, weak parties, strong incumbency advantages, and state control over voting rules all narrow the range of people who can plausibly run and win. Gender parity efforts will be most durable if they are tied to a larger democratizing agenda: voting rights, public financing, fair districting, party accountability, and electoral systems that make representation less zero-sum.

Quotas have worked elsewhere in part because parties control candidate lists. In the United States, by contrast, primaries are candidate-centered and decentralized. Party leaders cannot simply alternate women and men on a national list, as in many proportional representation systems. That does not make positive action impossible, but it does change the target. The most promising reforms are likely to be internal party rules and incentives: gender-balanced recruitment committees, transparent endorsement procedures, parity targets for open seats, public reporting on candidate support, and guaranteed party resources for women—especially women of color—who enter competitive primaries. Parties already shape the field long before voters see a ballot. They should be judged not by how warmly they praise women candidates after they win, but by whether they help create fair contests before anyone has declared.

Money is another place where the state can act without pretending that ambition is the main barrier. Running for office in the United States often requires access to wealthy networks, flexible employment, family resources, and tolerance for financial risk. These are not evenly distributed by gender or race. Small-donor matching systems, democracy vouchers, spending limits where constitutional, and public grants for qualifying candidates would not be women-only reforms, but they would reduce the premium placed on elite networks that have historically favored men. Campaign rules could also recognize caregiving as a legitimate campaign expense. A mother of young children should not have to treat childcare as a private obstacle unrelated to public service.

Piscopo is also right to emphasize harassment and sexist treatment as political barriers, not merely unpleasant background noise. Women in public life face threats, sexualized abuse, and scrutiny of appearance and family life that men rarely experience in the same way. This is especially acute for Black women, Muslim women, immigrant women, and other women whose candidacies provoke racialized and gendered backlash. Legal remedies must be crafted carefully to protect speech and press freedom, but parties, legislatures, and platforms can do far more: enforce codes of conduct, provide security resources, sanction members who traffic in misogynistic attacks, and treat online threats as threats to democratic participation rather than as the cost of doing politics.

At the same time, the category “women” cannot do all the work. Descriptive representation matters because social hierarchies matter, but American hierarchies are never only gendered. The Nineteenth Amendment did not enfranchise all women in practice; Black women in the South still faced terror, poll taxes, literacy tests, and white primaries, while Native women and many Asian immigrant women were excluded by citizenship law. A parity politics that celebrates women’s presence without confronting racial exclusion risks reproducing the old suffrage movement’s failures. If positive action is defended as a remedy for patriarchy, it must also be defended as a remedy for white supremacy. Otherwise the beneficiaries will be disproportionately women already closest to power.

The partisan dimension is unavoidable too. The surge of women candidates in 2018 was real, but it was heavily Democratic. Republican women remain strikingly scarce in Congress, and the reasons include both party recruitment patterns and ideological resistance to gender-conscious remedies. A national conversation about women’s representation therefore cannot rely only on feminist organizations aligned with one party. If half the political system treats parity as a partisan slogan, gains will be uneven and fragile. Voluntary party rules may be easier to imagine among Democrats, but democratic legitimacy requires pressure on both parties to recruit, fund, and support women candidates.

Finally, we should ask whether winner-take-all districts themselves are part of the problem. Countries with proportional representation often elect more women, not only because of quotas but because multi-member districts allow parties and voters to imagine representation as a team rather than a single heroic individual. American reformers should take seriously proposals for ranked-choice voting and multi-member districts. These would not guarantee gender parity, but they could weaken incumbency, reduce spoiler fears, and make it easier for voters to elect delegations that reflect the public’s diversity.

The right to be elected should become part of the American democratic vocabulary. But it should not be understood narrowly as a right of individual candidacy, nor even only as a demand for more women in the same offices under the same rules. It should mean that political institutions have an affirmative duty to remove structural barriers to equal power. Women do not need more exhortations to be brave. They need parties that stop telling them to wait, campaign systems that do not punish caregiving and poverty, election rules that broaden representation, and a democracy that recognizes exclusion from office as a public injury.

Piscopo's analysis illuminates a fundamental tension in American democracy: the distinction between formal equality and substantive representation. Her argument that the United States construes political rights negatively—focusing on what government cannot restrict rather than what it must actively promote—correctly identifies a structural obstacle to women's political advancement. Yet this framework, while analytically useful, may oversimplify both the American constitutional tradition and the challenges facing quota adoption.

The negative rights tradition Piscopo describes was not merely an oversight but a deliberate philosophical choice rooted in distrust of concentrated power. The Founders feared that positive rights might enable government overreach, and this suspicion has deep roots in American political culture. Recognizing this context does not invalidate Piscopo's critique, but it suggests that移植ing Latin American or European models of positive action requires more than policy diffusion—it demands a cultural and constitutional reckoning with assumptions that date to the Republic's founding.

Piscopo correctly notes that voluntary party quotas have succeeded in other democracies without government mandate, and she highlights the Democratic Party's gender balance requirements as evidence this approach could translate to the United States. However, she understates how uniquely American party structures complicate this strategy. Unlike European parliamentary systems where parties are centralized and ideologically cohesive, American parties are decentralized federations where national committees have limited leverage over state and local organizations. The very features that make U.S. parties permeable to new candidates also make coordinated reform difficult.

The intersectional dimension merits deeper exploration than Piscopo provides. She acknowledges Rubio-Marín's observation that American discussions of positive action cannot separate race and gender, yet this point deserves more weight. Black women face compounded barriers that white women do not, and any reform agenda must grapple with how quotas interact with existing disparities. Research on Latin American quota systems suggests they do not automatically benefit women of color; implementation matters enormously. Argentina and Mexico adopted quotas but initially failed to ensure minority women's inclusion, requiring subsequent reforms targeting intersectionality.

Piscopo's emphasis on the "right to be elected" offers a productive reframing, but practical politics will require coalition-building beyond feminist circles. The instrumental arguments she documents—that women's presence improves policy outcomes—may resonate more broadly than justice-based claims rooted in descriptive representation. For a country still debating whether structural barriers exist at all, this pragmatic framing might prove more persuasive than abstract rights language.

The centennial commemoration presents both opportunity and risk. Commemoration without transformation risks cementing the status quo as achievement rather than unfinished promise.

The Right Frame, the Wrong Ceiling

Jennifer Piscopo's essay is the most useful piece of writing on women's representation I've read in the suffrage centennial year, and I want to say so plainly before pushing her argument further than she takes it. Piscopo gives American feminists something we have desperately needed: a vocabulary for talking about representation as a political right rather than a personal aspiration. The "lean in and win" framework that dominates U.S. women-in-politics organizations, however well-intentioned, accepts the structural conditions of American electoral life and asks individual women to compensate for them. Piscopo's reframing — from active to passive suffrage, from negative to positive rights, from candidacy as freedom from interference to candidacy as guaranteed access — is not a rhetorical flourish. It is a complete repositioning of what equality demands.

What I want to suggest, however, is that the gender-quota model Piscopo champions is the floor of ambition, not the ceiling. If U.S. feminists adopt the "elect and be elected" framework, we should use it to ask for more than a Latin American–style quota law. Three points are worth making.

First, candidate quotas presuppose competitive party nominations as the choke point. In closed-list proportional representation systems, this is exactly right: parties compose the list, voters ratify the party. A 50/50 zipper requirement in such a system is mechanically effective. But the United States runs primarily single-member-district plurality elections with primaries that are open to anyone who can collect signatures. Piscopo acknowledges this and notes that the policy menu must adapt. I want to underline how serious the adaptation must be. A candidate quota in a U.S. primary would mean either that the party's central committee endorses two candidates, one of each sex (which gives party elites enormous new power), or that voters choose between sex-balanced finalists in a runoff (which restructures the whole nominating calendar). Neither is impossible, but neither is what gender quotas mean in Argentina or France. We need to be honest that translating "positive action" to American conditions probably requires translating the underlying electoral system as well. Multi-member districts and ranked-choice voting, both of which have been quietly gaining ground in U.S. localities, are natural partners to any serious gender-representation reform.

Second, Piscopo's essay focuses on legislatures, where descriptive representation has clear theoretical and empirical traction. But the American glass ceiling is not in Congress alone. It runs through governorships (we have nine women governors as of 2020), state attorneys general, mayoralties of large cities, and most pointedly the presidency. The right to be elected, taken seriously, has to grapple with executive offices, which by their nature cannot be quota-allocated. Some of what Piscopo recommends — party-board gender balance, parity on shortlists, public-funding incentives — is portable to executive contests. But the deeper work is cultural and institutional: dismantling the implicit assumption that the chief executive is male. The 2020 vice-presidential selection, in which Joe Biden has now chosen Kamala Harris, was made possible in part by an explicit pledge to nominate a woman. That pledge is the kind of positive commitment Piscopo's framework legitimates. We should generalize it.

Third — and this is where I want to part company with the cleaner versions of Piscopo's argument — descriptive representation is not the same as substantive representation, and the gender-quota literature has been honest about this. Argentina's quota produced more women in Congress; it did not produce, on its own, transformative gender policy. Mexico's quota expanded steadily, yet feminicide remains a crisis. Rwanda's reserved-seats system has put women in formal positions while the broader political order remains autocratic. None of this discredits the quota; it just disciplines our expectations. Numerical presence is necessary but not sufficient. Piscopo cites Anne Phillips, but Phillips herself, in The Politics of Presence, was careful to distinguish presence from policy outcomes. American advocates should not oversell quotas with the promise that more women in office mechanically produces better policy for women. That is the kind of instrumental argument Piscopo rightly worries about, because it conditions women's inclusion on their political utility.

The strongest argument, which Piscopo emphasizes and I want to amplify, is the justice argument. Women constitute roughly half the population. Their systematic underrepresentation in deliberative bodies is itself an injustice, regardless of whether their inclusion produces particular policy goods. If U.S. courts have been receptive to anything in the political-rights literature, it has been arguments grounded in equal protection. The Nineteenth Amendment's centennial is a moment to reread it not as the conclusion of a story but as a starting premise. The Amendment guaranteed that the right to vote shall not be abridged on account of sex. The interesting interpretive question — and one that current jurists have barely touched — is whether, a century later, persistent and dramatic underrepresentation in elected bodies itself constitutes an abridgment of that right's promise.

I would add one consideration Piscopo treats only briefly: the political violence problem. She mentions in passing that some countries now classify sexist harassment of women politicians as political violence. This is one of the most significant legal innovations of the past decade, pioneered by Bolivia and Mexico. In the United States, where the harassment of women officeholders — from local school board members to members of Congress — has intensified with the rise of social media and the partisan radicalization of the past five years, this is not a peripheral concern. Women cannot meaningfully exercise the right to be elected if exercising it routinely entails death threats, doxxing, and stalking. American law treats this as an individual safety question, if it treats it at all. The Latin American framework treats it as a structural violation of political rights. That alone would be a transformative import.

Piscopo ends with cautious optimism, citing Emily Zackin's argument that positive rights are familiar to American state constitutions even if absent from the federal one. I share that optimism, with one caveat. The most successful positive-rights mobilizations in U.S. state constitutional history — for education, for labor protections, for environmental rights — succeeded because they had committed coalitions willing to pursue a multi-decade strategy of litigation, lobbying, and ballot initiatives. The women's representation movement in the United States does not yet have that infrastructure. EMILY's List and its cousins are election-cycle organizations. The Latin American activists Piscopo describes spent twenty or thirty years building the legal and rhetorical groundwork for quotas. If U.S. feminists want what they have, they need to be prepared to build for the long haul, beyond any single candidate or any single election.

The centennial is a useful prompt, but the project Piscopo invites us into is generational. The question is whether American feminism, accustomed to thinking about representation in the language of individual ambition and electoral cycles, can shift to the slower, harder vocabulary of constitutional rights. It can. The Latin American example proves it. We just have to start.

Diversified Claude Opus 4.7

Quotas Are Not Enough, and Sometimes They Are the Wrong Question

Jennifer Piscopo writes with great moral clarity about a real problem — the United States has fewer women in its national legislature than most of the democratic world — and offers an elegant solution, gender quotas, with a deep history in Latin American constitutionalism and a track record of measurable effect. I want to register a friendly dissent from the left. The quota framework Piscopo recommends is not wrong, but it is partial, and the part it leaves out is the part that matters most.

Begin with what quotas actually do. They redistribute access to legislative seats among the kind of people who are already plausible candidates for legislative seats. In every country Piscopo discusses, the women who have entered office through quotas have been drawn disproportionately from the same socio-economic strata, the same urban professional class, the same educational pedigrees that produce male politicians. Argentina's quota gave us more women in Congress, but it did not give us more working-class women, more rural women, more Indigenous women, more women whose lives resemble the lives of the women most damaged by Argentine economic policy. Rwanda has 60 percent women in Parliament and a regime that arrests journalists. Mexico has gender parity in Congress and femicide rates that have climbed almost monotonically through the period of quota expansion. The descriptive numbers improve; the substantive lives of most women do not.

I do not say this to dismiss quotas. I say it because the language of "women's representation" tends to flatten what representation is for. Piscopo, to her credit, acknowledges the tension between descriptive and substantive representation. She quotes Anne Phillips, who, again to her credit, made the same point at length. But the essay's overall energy still pulls toward the assumption that getting women into office is the proximate goal, and that policy benefits will follow. The history of gender-quota implementation tells us this is true only in narrow domains — paternity leave, some reproductive health measures, sometimes domestic violence law — and only when the women who arrive in office already have feminist political commitments. Margaret Thatcher's election did not produce family-friendly policy. Conservative Latin American women who arrived under quotas have voted against abortion liberalization in every country that has debated it. The category "women in office" is not a political program.

This matters because the United States, more than most countries, is in the grip of a deep structural crisis that is not primarily about descriptive representation. Wages have stagnated for forty years. Health care is rationed by employment. Housing is unaffordable. The criminal legal system is racialized in ways that produce mass incarceration. Climate policy is paralyzed. Every one of these problems falls heavily on women, particularly working-class women and women of color. A Congress that was 50 percent women drawn from the same donor class that already populates Congress would not, I submit, change the trajectory of any of these crises. It would change who delivers the bad news.

What would? A serious answer would have to start with the structural features of American politics that Piscopo treats as background: the role of money, the candidate-centered nominating system, the geographic distortion of representation through the Senate and the Electoral College, the long-term decline of organized labor as a counterweight to capital. These are the conditions that make American politics responsive to the wealthy regardless of the gender of the wealthy. A quota system layered onto these conditions would produce — and in Latin America to some extent has produced — a more demographically diverse oligarchy.

There is a left tradition of thinking about representation that is older than the quota debate and that Piscopo's essay does not engage. It runs through trade union representation, through cooperative movements, through workers' councils, through the question of which interests have organized standing in the policymaking process. This tradition holds that the meaningful question is not whether the legislature mirrors the demographics of the population but whether the working majority of the population has organized capacity to shape outcomes. By that measure, the United States has gotten dramatically worse over the past half-century, regardless of who sits in office.

To be clear: I would vote for a gender-quota law tomorrow. I think Piscopo's reframing of representation as a political right rather than an individual ambition is correct and important. I think the dismantling of the "women need to lean in" narrative is overdue. But I want the quota debate to be conducted alongside, and not in place of, the debate about whose interests are actually organized in American politics. Otherwise we risk a politics in which the descriptive metrics improve while the substantive conditions deteriorate — and a generation of feminist energy is spent securing a presence in institutions whose decisions have already been made elsewhere.

There is one more thing worth saying. Piscopo concludes by noting that the impossible becomes possible if someone tries. True enough. But not everything that is possible is worth trying. The Latin American quota story is a real accomplishment, and Piscopo tells it well. It is also, viewed at the regional scale, a story in which formal political equality for women advanced significantly while economic inequality between women advanced even more. American feminists looking south for inspiration should look at the whole picture, not just the legislative graphs. The aspiration should be a politics in which working women — who are most women — actually shape decisions, not a politics in which the people who shape decisions happen, in proportionate numbers, to be women.

The Trouble With Group Rights

Jennifer Piscopo's essay is admirably clear about its premises, which makes it possible to disagree with it on the same terms. Her argument turns on three moves: a redefinition of political rights from negative to positive, a redefinition of representation from individual to group, and an embrace of quota-based "positive action" as the natural policy expression of those redefinitions. I think each of these moves is mistaken, and that the cumulative case for gender quotas in the United States is weaker than her sweeping international comparisons suggest.

Begin with negative versus positive rights. Piscopo treats the American constitutional tradition's preference for the former as a kind of historical accident, an artifact of Locke and Mill and the Bill of Rights that the rest of the world has thoughtfully moved beyond. This is a tendentious reading. The reason the American constitutional tradition is built on rights of noninterference is not provincialism. It is a considered judgment, repeatedly reaffirmed in our political theory and our constitutional law, that government power to grant positive entitlements is also government power to allocate burdens, and that such allocations require both robust justification and democratic consent. The Fifteenth and Nineteenth Amendments did not "merely" remove obstacles to voting. They imposed serious limits on what state legislatures could do, and they did so through a supermajoritarian amendment process that established broad social consent. Quota laws of the sort Piscopo recommends would impose meaningful constraints — on parties, on candidates, on voters — without anything like the same democratic warrant. That is a serious matter, not a paperwork detail.

Now turn to descriptive representation. Piscopo presents this concept as if its theoretical foundations were settled. They are not. The standard argument for descriptive representation — that legislatures should "mirror" the citizenry along socially salient lines — has at least three well-known problems that the essay does not engage. First, it has no principled stopping rule. Once we accept that women, as a socially constructed group, deserve proportional presence, we must explain why the same logic does not apply to every other socially constructed group: religious minorities, gay and lesbian citizens, disabled citizens, rural citizens, the working class, the elderly, the young, immigrants. Piscopo's nod toward "politically salient" characteristics is a real attempt at a stopping rule, but it is one that simply transfers the contested judgment to a different level. Who decides which groups are politically salient? In practice, the groups that get represented are the groups that organize most effectively. Quotas thus tend to entrench the political map of one moment as the political map of the future.

Second, descriptive representation treats group membership as politically meaningful in a way that classical liberalism explicitly rejects. The whole point of representative democracy, on a Madisonian view, is to refine and enlarge the public view by passing it through representatives chosen for judgment, not for demographic resemblance. A representative is supposed to represent constituents on the basis of interests articulated through deliberation, not on the basis of shared bodily characteristics. The slippage from "women are underrepresented" to "women cannot be represented except by women" is real, and it has consequences. It implies that a male representative cannot adequately represent his women constituents, which is empirically dubious and politically corrosive.

Third, and most importantly, the empirical claims about what descriptive representation accomplishes are weaker than Piscopo's essay suggests. Yes, there is research linking women in office to certain family-friendly policies. There is also research showing that the gender of legislators is dwarfed in explanatory power by their party affiliation. Republican women in the U.S. Congress vote like Republican men. Democratic women vote like Democratic men. If you want different policy outcomes for women, the most efficient path is to elect more Democrats, not to ensure that the Republicans you elect happen to be female. Piscopo gestures toward this complication when she warns against purely instrumental arguments, but she does not let it disturb the broader case.

I want to address one specific argument because it appears repeatedly in the quota literature: the claim that women candidates are systematically higher-quality than their male counterparts, citing Sarah Fulton and the Center for American Women in Politics. Piscopo treats this finding as evidence of discrimination. It can be read just as plausibly as evidence of selection: women who survive a more rigorous self-screening process are higher-quality because the screening is more rigorous, not because the electoral system is biased against them. Distinguishing these interpretations is hard, and the quota literature has generally not done so. If the quality premium reflects selection, then quotas — by lowering the implicit bar for entry — would lower the average quality of women officeholders, which is not the result advocates want.

There is also a normative concern that quota advocates rarely confront. A 30 or 50 percent reservation for women is, mechanically, a 70 or 50 percent ceiling for men. In a candidate field where women already have a quality advantage, the quota is asking voters to accept lower-quality male candidates over higher-quality female candidates in any race where the male is the quota-favored category. This is not a hypothetical. It happened in France after parity, where parties shuffled women into unwinnable seats and men into safe ones, while quality variation across both groups remained considerable. Quotas do not in fact guarantee that legislatures fill with the best-qualified members of each sex. They guarantee that they fill with the members of each sex whom the relevant nominating authority chooses to advance. That authority — usually a party committee — gains enormous power under a quota regime, and the historical record on party committees with enormous power is not encouraging.

Finally, Piscopo's enthusiasm for Latin American precedent should be tempered by the political context of those reforms. Argentina, Mexico, Costa Rica, and most of the other quota adopters had legislatures that were corrupt, party systems that were elite-captured, and political cultures in which constitutional change was a top-down affair conducted by political insiders. In those contexts, quotas were a relatively easy reform because the insiders deciding the question were not facing accountability mechanisms strong enough to constrain them. The United States, for all its dysfunctions, has a more decentralized, more contested, and more litigious political environment. Importing quotas here would mean fighting the reform through fifty state legislatures, the federal courts, and a deeply skeptical electorate, with no obvious constituency strong enough to win those fights.

None of this is to deny that women are underrepresented in U.S. political office. They are. Nor is it to deny that the underrepresentation matters. It does. But the path to addressing it does not run through quotas. It runs through the slower and less satisfying work of recruiting, training, and funding women candidates within the existing system — the work organizations like EMILY's List and She Should Run are already doing, and doing with measurable results. The 2018 cycle saw the largest single-cycle gain in women's congressional representation in American history. That is not the limit of what voluntary mobilization can accomplish. It is closer to the beginning.

Piscopo's framework asks us to remake American constitutional culture so that quotas become possible. The simpler path is to keep doing the work that has, over the past three decades, doubled women's congressional presence, and to trust that the same trajectory, accelerated by social change, will continue. That is not a counsel of complacency. It is a recognition that the American answer to the question Piscopo asks is unlikely to be the Argentine one, and need not be.

The Electoral System Is the Problem

Jennifer Piscopo's essay does an extraordinary service by lifting our gaze from the parochial American debate about whether women want to run for office and toward the comparative question of how other countries actually elect women in much larger numbers. But the essay glides past what I take to be the central obstacle to women's representation in the United States, and the central reason that the gender quota model so impressive in Latin America and Europe cannot simply be imported here. The obstacle is the American electoral system itself.

Consider the world's top performers on women's legislative representation: Rwanda, Cuba, Bolivia, the United Arab Emirates, Mexico, Nicaragua, Sweden, Grenada, Andorra, Costa Rica. Set aside the authoritarian outliers. The democracies on that list all share a structural feature: they use proportional representation or some form of multi-member-district electoral system. Of the fifty countries Piscopo cites that have reached the 30 percent threshold, the overwhelming majority use proportional representation. Single-member-district plurality systems — the kind used in the United States, the United Kingdom, and Canada — populate the bottom of the list, almost without exception, whether or not they have quota laws.

This is not a coincidence. In a single-member district, every nomination is a binary choice. The party picks a candidate; the voters pick a winner. Gender balance cannot be achieved within a district; it can only be achieved across districts, by aggregating many separate binary choices. This makes a quota law mechanically awkward. Should the law require parties to nominate women in exactly half their districts? If so, which half? The safe districts, where the nomination is effectively the election, or the unwinnable ones, where it does not matter? The French parity experience is the cautionary tale: parties complied with the letter of the law by running women in seats they did not expect to win, paid the modest financial penalties for noncompliance in seats they did expect to win, and ended up with a legislature substantially less gender-balanced than the law contemplated.

By contrast, in a proportional representation system with party lists, gender balance is a list-construction problem. A "zipper" rule — alternating men and women down the list — produces a legislature that mirrors the list. Quota laws work, in other words, where parties already have to make collective decisions about slates of candidates. They work poorly where each district produces its own discrete winner.

Piscopo acknowledges this in passing — "one quirk of the U.S. political system is that political parties do not control ballot access" — but treats it as a technical wrinkle. It is not. It is the central fact about why American women's representation has lagged. Even before we get to quotas, the United States has been an outlier on women's legislative representation for as long as comparative data has existed, and the gap has been larger than the difference between countries with and without quotas. The biggest single explanatory variable in cross-national women's representation is not whether a country has a quota law. It is whether the country has a proportional representation electoral system.

This suggests a different policy agenda from the one Piscopo recommends. The most efficient American path to gender-balanced legislatures is not to import gender quotas. It is to change the electoral system. Multi-member districts elected by ranked-choice voting — a reform that Fair Vote has been pushing for years and that has begun to gain traction in cities like Cambridge, Massachusetts, and in the state of Maine — would by itself, without any explicit gender provision, substantially raise women's congressional representation. The mechanism is well understood: in multi-member districts, parties have incentives to balance their slates demographically, because a slate that ignores half the electorate forfeits votes. The same incentive does not operate in single-member districts, where a party need only nominate a candidate plausibly preferred by the median voter.

Combined electoral-system and quota reforms would be even more powerful. But the electoral-system reform is the foundation. Layering a gender quota onto single-member-district plurality elections is, mechanically, an attempt to solve a coordination problem with a constraint that does not match the choice architecture. It can be done, but it is the hardest way to do it.

There is a second institutional point worth making. American political parties are not the closed organizations that European parties are. Anyone who can collect signatures can run in a party primary; the party has limited ability to anoint or block candidates. This is why the European model of party-controlled lists is so foreign to American practice, and why the suggestion in Piscopo's essay that the major U.S. parties could "voluntarily" adopt quotas is more complicated than it appears. The Democratic Party's gender-balance rules for convention delegates work because delegates are selected through a quasi-administrative process. They could not work the same way for congressional candidates, who are selected by voters in open primaries. The party can endorse, recruit, fundraise, and train. It cannot determine who appears on the general election ballot under its label.

This means that the most promising American reforms are likely to be ones that work with, rather than against, the structural features of the U.S. system. Public campaign financing with gender-balance incentives — bonus funds for parties whose nominees include some minimum proportion of women — would create real party incentives without violating the candidate-centered logic of American primaries. Filing-fee reductions and signature-requirement adjustments for women candidates, modeled on the indigent-candidate jurisprudence Piscopo cites, would lower entry barriers without dictating outcomes. Mandatory diversity reporting by parties at the recruitment stage would create the kind of accountability data that has driven corporate-board diversity reforms.

None of these are gender quotas in the strict sense, and none of them by themselves would put U.S. women's representation on par with Mexico's. But the combination of electoral-system reform and incentive-based gender provisions could plausibly do so, and could do so without requiring the kind of constitutional rewrite that quota laws would, in the U.S. context, almost certainly demand.

I think Piscopo is right that the American "lean in" frame is inadequate. I think she is right that representation should be understood as a right rather than a personal achievement. I think she is right that the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment is the occasion to ask harder questions. But the answer to those harder questions, for the United States, is more likely to involve ranked-choice voting and multi-member districts than to involve a 50 percent quota law modeled on Mexico. The Latin American story is inspiring. It is not, mechanically, our story to tell.

What Quotas Do and Don't Do

Jennifer Piscopo's lead essay marshals an impressive body of comparative evidence in support of gender quotas as the missing piece of the American suffrage story. As someone who has spent years reading the empirical literature she cites, I want to register a more cautious view of what the evidence actually shows.

The basic claim — that countries with gender quotas have more women in their legislatures than countries without them — is true. It is also somewhat trivially true: a law that requires X tends to produce X. The harder and more interesting questions are: (1) Does increased descriptive representation produce the substantive policy changes advocates promise? (2) Are the gains durable across political contexts? (3) Are there costs that should be weighed against the benefits? The honest answers, in 2020, are: sometimes, sometimes, and yes.

Start with substantive representation. The cleanest test is whether women legislators, holding other variables constant, behave differently from men legislators. The evidence here is mixed and contingent. Studies of Indian village councils with reserved seats for women find clear effects on local policy: more attention to water infrastructure, more reporting of crimes against women. Studies of Latin American legislatures find more bills introduced on women's issues but mixed effects on enactment. Studies of European parliaments find that the gender effect on roll-call voting is, in most cases, dwarfed by the party effect. Piscopo's essay cites the strong-effect studies and skims past the weak-effect studies. A balanced reading of the literature would say that gender of legislators matters, but conditionally — on party, on institutional context, on whether the women elected through quotas are feminist-aligned or simply female relatives of male party leaders.

The "female relatives" problem is not hypothetical. In several Latin American countries, the immediate effect of candidate quotas was to bring into politics women who served as proxies for male party bosses — wives, daughters, sisters, who held seats and voted as instructed. Mexican election scholars have documented the "Juanitas" phenomenon, in which women elected to comply with quota requirements resigned shortly after taking office to be replaced by male suplentes. Reforms have closed many of these loopholes, but the basic point stands: a quota guarantees the demographic composition of officeholders, not the substantive autonomy of those officeholders. In strongly patron-client party systems, descriptive representation can be achieved without much substantive change.

Now consider durability. The quota wave has been impressive, but it has not been monotonic. In some countries — Pakistan, Bangladesh — reserved-seat systems have coexisted with persistent gender hierarchy and have not generated the broader political integration of women that advocates hoped for. In others, including some Latin American cases, parity provisions have produced backlash effects: harassment of women candidates, the development of gendered political violence as a phenomenon serious enough to require its own legal category. Piscopo mentions political violence as evidence of how sophisticated other countries' frameworks have become. It is also evidence of how partial the quota victory has been: in country after country, women's nominal inclusion has been met with informal exclusion mechanisms that the quota laws did not anticipate.

Then there is the question of costs. I do not mean the costs to men, although those are real and worth honest acknowledgment in any quota debate. I mean the costs to the broader project of representative democracy. Quotas alter the principal-agent relationship between voters and candidates by inserting an additional principal — the party committee or electoral authority that enforces the quota — into the chain of accountability. A legislator who owes her seat to a quota allocation has different incentives from one who owes it entirely to her voters. This can be a feature, not a bug, in contexts where voters are themselves systematically biased. But it can also produce legislators whose primary loyalty is to the gatekeeping mechanism that put them there. Studies of French parity implementation have raised exactly this concern.

I do not raise these points to oppose gender quotas in principle. I support them, in contexts where the electoral system is amenable and the party system is strong enough to make implementation meaningful. I raise them because Piscopo's framing — quotas as the natural conclusion of a global movement for substantive equality — understates the empirical messiness and overpromises what the American adoption of quotas would deliver.

Concretely: if the United States adopted a 30 percent gender quota for congressional candidates tomorrow, the most likely effect would be a modest increase in women's representation, concentrated disproportionately in safe Democratic seats (because the Democratic primary electorate is already more receptive), and with relatively little change in the policy output of Congress (because the gender effect is dwarfed by the party effect, and party balance would not change). This is not nothing. But it is also not what the Latin American comparison promises.

A more honest case for American quotas would acknowledge these limitations and rest the argument on justice rather than instrumental gains. Piscopo nods toward this when she observes that justice-based arguments have actually fared better in courts than instrumental ones. I think this is the strongest part of her essay. The case for women's proportional presence in legislative bodies does not need to rest on what women will do once they are there. It rests on the simple proposition that a deliberative body that excludes half the population it claims to represent is not, in any meaningful sense, representative. That proposition is true regardless of whether women legislators vote differently from men.

If we want to make that argument in the United States, we should make it directly and not dress it up in instrumental promises that the empirical literature only partly supports. The case for women's representation as a matter of democratic legitimacy is strong. The case for women's representation as a guaranteed mechanism for better policy outcomes is weaker. Piscopo's essay tends to blend them. American advocates would do better to separate them, and to lead with the stronger argument.

Race Is Not a Footnote

Jennifer Piscopo's essay arrives at a moment when American politics is being rearranged, in real time, by a mass mobilization against police violence that has put racial justice at the center of national debate in a way it has not been for decades. It is striking, then, that her account of women's representation treats race as a complication to be acknowledged near the end rather than as a feature central to the whole story. I want to spend this response essay placing race back at the center, because I think doing so changes both the diagnosis and the prescription.

Piscopo's narrative arc goes like this: in the nineteenth century, Latin American constitutions defined citizenship in terms of "the right to elect and be elected"; twentieth-century suffragists in those countries used this language to demand both vote and office; late-twentieth-century activists revived the framework to push for gender quotas, with great success. The American story is a parallel one in which the same conceptual move was, for historical reasons, not made. Piscopo's prescription is to make it now.

The trouble is that the parallel is partial. Latin American gender-quota campaigns succeeded in part by treating "women" as a unified political category whose claims could be advanced without reference to the racial, class, and indigenous hierarchies that also structure those societies. Piscopo cites Ruth Rubio-Marín making essentially this observation, and acknowledges that the rhetorical separation of gender from race "worked elsewhere" but is harder in the United States, where "white supremacy and patriarchy are intertwined." She then moves on. I want to stay with this point, because I think it is the crux of the American question.

When American women first organized for suffrage in the nineteenth century, the movement was already racially fractured. The white-led wing, exemplified by Stanton and Anthony, made strategic decisions to exclude Black women and Black suffrage demands, sometimes explicitly. When the Nineteenth Amendment passed in 1920, southern Black women remained effectively disenfranchised through poll taxes, literacy tests, and white terror for another four and a half decades. Native women had to wait until 1924 for citizenship and longer for the franchise in many states. Asian American women's access to citizenship was constrained by immigration and naturalization law until the 1940s and beyond. The 1920 centennial is, accurately understood, the centennial of white women's enfranchisement; for many other American women, the more meaningful date is 1965, or even later.

This history matters for the quota debate because it determines which "women" American advocates are talking about when they talk about women's representation. The 2018 wave of Democratic women elected to Congress was a genuine moment of change, and it was disproportionately a moment of change for women of color. Ayanna Pressley, Ilhan Omar, Rashida Tlaib, Sharice Davids, Deb Haaland — these are the names that mattered. The "wave" was not a wave of generic women; it was a wave of specifically situated women whose campaigns were rooted in racial-justice and immigration-justice constituencies. The structural exclusions they were overcoming were not only gendered but racialized. The local party leaders Piscopo mentions, who told women aspirants to wait their turn, were not just sexist. They were operating within party machines whose racial assumptions about who can win are deeply entrenched.

This suggests that any positive-action framework for the United States must be intersectional from the start, not as an afterthought. A gender quota that does not specify racial composition would, in the U.S. context, likely produce a Congress whose women members were disproportionately white, because white women have greater access to the donor networks, party connections, and educational credentials that current candidate-development infrastructure relies on. We have evidence for this. State legislatures with the highest percentages of women are not, on average, the state legislatures with the highest percentages of women of color. The two dimensions of representation are partially independent, and a reform aimed at one without attention to the other can entrench inequality on the other.

There is a deeper point here too. The reason positive-action frameworks have struggled in the United States is not just that the constitutional tradition is built on negative rights. It is that the most visible American experiment with positive action — race-conscious affirmative action — has been under sustained legal and political attack for forty years, has been narrowed by the Supreme Court in case after case, and is now hanging by a thread. American jurisprudence has moved steadily toward a "colorblind" reading of the Fourteenth Amendment that treats any race-conscious remedy as constitutionally suspect. To then introduce a sex-conscious remedy — a gender quota — into this legal environment is to invite a doctrinal collision. Opponents will argue, with some legal force, that if race-conscious remedies require strict scrutiny and narrow tailoring, sex-conscious remedies require at least intermediate scrutiny, and that a sweeping quota law fails both. The Latin American countries Piscopo discusses do not face this jurisprudential challenge in the same form, because their constitutional traditions accept "compensatory inequalities" as a legitimate framework. American constitutional law does not, except in narrow remedial contexts.

This means that any serious American positive-action campaign for women's representation must either (a) limit its ambitions to measures that survive existing constitutional doctrine — voluntary party rules, public-financing incentives, recruitment programs — or (b) commit to the much larger project of changing American constitutional doctrine to accept positive group rights. The latter would require revisiting the trajectory of affirmative-action jurisprudence and, in effect, winning the political and legal fight for race-conscious remedies as well. You cannot, in the American context, secure sex-conscious positive action while race-conscious positive action is being dismantled. The two stand or fall together.

This is, in some ways, a forbidding prospect. But it is also clarifying. It tells us that the path to gender quotas in the United States runs through, not around, the politics of racial justice. And it tells us that white-led feminist organizations whose strategic instinct has been to keep the gender question separate from the race question — to make the quota debate easier by not entangling it with affirmative action — are misreading the political terrain.

The encouraging news is that the women-of-color-led feminist organizing of the past decade has already begun to chart this integrated path. Higher Heights, Latino Victory Project, EMILY's List's increasing attention to women of color candidates, the Black Women's Roundtable's representation work — these organizations are doing the kind of intersectional candidate development that an honest American positive-action framework requires. They have built the infrastructure that produced the 2018 wave and that will produce future waves. If Piscopo's reframing of representation as a political right is to take root in the United States, it will take root through their work, not through an imported Latin American template.

I want to close by underlining what I take from Piscopo's essay, even as I push back. She is right that the American obsession with women's individual ambition is inadequate. She is right that representation is properly understood as a political right rather than a personal achievement. She is right that the centennial moment invites a deeper conversation than the one we are having. I just want that conversation to begin from the recognition that American women have never been a single political category, that the suffrage history she narrates has been racially uneven from the start, and that the representation we should be fighting for is one that takes that unevenness seriously. Otherwise, we will end up with a more demographically diverse version of the same exclusion.

First, national legislation requiring parity for federal campaigns—perhaps by conditioning public financing on gender balance among major party candidates. Second, state-level reforms mandating that major parties provide gender-balanced slates for statewide offices. The enthusiasm for gender quotas evident throughout her analysis neglects significant empirical and philosophical problems with this approach. I do not dispute that women's legislative representation in the United States is shamefully low—indeed, the statistic placing the nation alongside Afghanistan should disturb anyone who values equal citizenship. But the remedy proposed requires examination beyond the largely uncritical acceptance evident in Piscopo's treatment.

Consider first the empirical question: do gender quotas actually deliver their promised benefits? Piscopo cites research showing that more women in office results in "more family-friendly policies, including paid daycare and paternity leave; more liberal reproductive health policies." But correlation is not causation. Women elected under quota systems often come from established political families, belong to the same parties as their male colleagues, and face identical institutional constraints. The evidence for quotas changing policy outcomes remains contested among political scientists, with careful scholars noting that selection effects and party differences often explain apparent gender differences.

More troubling is Piscopo's reliance on instrumental arguments—claims that women will make politics better. She acknowledges this harkens back to suffragist claims about women's "superior morality" but suggests modern evidence justifies the claim. It does not, or at least not clearly. There is no unified "women's perspective" on policy, and treating women's presence as inherently beneficial in ways men's presence is not reinforces the very essentialism that feminist politics should reject. If women candidates hold regressive views on reproductive rights or labor policy, should their presence still be celebrated?

The justice-based arguments fare better philosophically. The claim that systematic exclusion constitutes harm and that correction requires positive action has principled force. Yet even here, Piscopo understates the tension between group rights and individual autonomy. Her assertion that women constitute a "politically salient group" based on "politically salient shared characteristics" assumes consensus about which characteristics matter that does not exist. Some women reject feminist politics entirely. Others define themselves primarily through ethnicity, class, or profession. Forcing these diverse individuals into a unified category "women" may serve strategic political goals but risks the very essentialism that democratic pluralism should resist.

The U.S. system features candidate-centered elections, weak parties, primary systems, and federal structures that distribute electoral authority across fifty states. A federal quota law would face constitutional challenges based on the Elections Clause and state autonomy. Piscopo's analysis traces an elegant throughline from nineteenth-century suffrage movements to contemporary gender parity reforms, arguing that the "right to be elected" language in Ibero-American constitutions provided the normative foundation for quotas that the United States never developed. This is historically sophisticated, but it risks obscuring another important historical parallel: the decades-long struggle to apply voting rights protections to actual electoral participation, a struggle that reveals both the possibilities and limits of rights-based approaches to democratic inclusion.

The Fifteenth Amendment prohibited voting restrictions based on race in 1870; the Voting Rights Act finally enforced that prohibition in 1965. The Nineteenth Amendment prohibited voting restrictions based on sex in 1920; enforcement of women's voting rights has been ongoing ever since. These gaps between formal rights and effective access are not accidental. They reflect the deliberate strategies of political opponents who accepted formal exclusions while preserving substantive power through alternative mechanisms—poll taxes, literacy tests, party rules, harassment, and simple violence.

Piscopo briefly acknowledges how Jim Crow restrictions affected Black women even after the Nineteenth Amendment, but she understates how thoroughly this pattern repeated. The suffragists won the right to vote in 1920, yet women of color faced barriers that persisted for generations. Native women were not recognized as citizens until 1924. Black women in the South could not exercise their formal rights until the 1960s and still face systematic disenfranchisement through voter ID laws, polling place closures, and felon disenfranchisement. The lesson is clear: winning a legal right does not automatically translate into effective participation.

Gender quotas aim to prevent a similar pattern in women's representation by intervening at the candidate emergence stage rather than waiting for women to win elections. This is strategically sound. But the history of voting rights enforcement suggests that formal rules alone are insufficient. Implementation matters. Enforcement mechanisms matter. Political will matters. And in the United States, all three have been consistently lacking when it comes to women's full democratic incorporation.

The comparison with the civil rights movement's approach to voting rights is instructive. Brown v. Board established that segregation was unconstitutional in 1954, but the Voting Rights Act did not pass until 1964, and it was significantly weakened by Shelby County v. Holder in 2013. Throughout this period, advocates developed sophisticated strategies combining litigation, organizing, direct action, and legislative lobbying. This campaign would include litigation challenging ballot access rules that disadvantage women, organizing to pressure parties to adopt voluntary quotas, voter education about the importance of women's representation, coalition-building with other reform movements, and sustained attention to the cultural and psychological barriers she discusses. Quotas are a powerful tool but not a sufficient one.

Voting rights advocates measure success not by formal legal victories but by actual participation rates, voter access, and the responsiveness of elected officials to all communities. Gender parity advocates should adopt similarly rigorous metrics. Simply counting women in office is insufficient; we must ask which women, elected through what processes, representing which communities, accomplishing what substantive outcomes. Piscopo makes a strong case that the United States' conceptualization of political rights as negative—as prohibitions on government interference rather than affirmative guarantees of participation—explains much of the gap between American women's voting rights and women's representation. This is an insightful diagnosis, but diagnosing the problem is easier than prescribing solutions, and the essay's prescription—that the United States should adopt gender quotas comparable to those in Latin America and Europe—faces significant obstacles that deserve deeper examination.

The most fundamental obstacle is institutional. The United States operates under a first-past-the-post, single-member district electoral system with candidate-centered elections and relatively weak parties. This system has consequences: incumbency rates above ninety percent in House races, high costs of campaigns requiring personal fundraising, and winner-take-all outcomes that make minor parties irrelevant. Gender quotas are most effective in proportional representation systems where parties present ranked lists and voters choose among them; such systems naturally produce representation ratios roughly matching population ratios, and quotas accelerate this process. In the American system, parties cannot guarantee that nominating equal numbers of women and men will produce equal numbers of elected officials, because winners are determined by individual races in individual districts.

Consider the practical difficulties. Suppose Congress passed a law requiring major parties to field equal numbers of female and male candidates for House seats. How would this law be enforced? Primary elections are state-run, not party-run. Federal law could require the major parties to set nomination rules ensuring gender balance, but parties already resist federal regulation of their internal affairs. Even if achieved, the law's effect would be uncertain: a party might field equal numbers of female and male candidates while losing most female-leaning districts. The system would produce compliance without changing outcomes.

The Democratic Party's existing gender balance requirements for convention delegates and committee members offer a model, but these rules govern internal party organization rather than candidate nomination. They ensure that women have voice in party decisions but do not guarantee that women will be nominated for winnable seats. The gap between presence in party structures and election to legislative office remains substantial.

Piscopo suggests alternative policies that might avoid some obstacles: reducing filing fees for women candidates, allocating party funds for women's campaigns, requiring women on candidate short lists, and treating harassment as political violence. These proposals are more adaptable to American institutional contexts, but they also encounter resistance. Filing fee reductions would need to survive equal protection challenges arguing that differential treatment based on sex requires heightened scrutiny. Party fund allocations face obstacles from campaign finance law. Short-list requirements would require party rules that resist external mandates.

Perhaps the most realistic pathway involves state-level reforms. State governments control ballot access for state and local offices, and many states have proportional representation systems for state legislative seats. Gender balance requirements for state parties, combined with state-level public financing conditioned on gender balance, could produce significant change within state legislatures and provide models for eventual federal adoption. This approach would require sustained organizing across multiple states, but it avoids the constitutional obstacles facing federal mandates while building concrete evidence that gender balance produces positive outcomes.

The lesson from comparative politics is not that quotas automatically work but that political will matters. Countries that have succeeded in achieving gender parity did so through sustained advocacy, political entrepreneurship, and institutional learning. The United States could follow this path, but it requires recognizing that institutional constraints are real and that strategies must adapt to context rather than assuming that "trying" what worked elsewhere will produce similar results.

What strikes me most about Piscopo's essay is its implicit theory of change. The argument proceeds by identifying a legal-constitutional framework—positive rights to political participation—that enabled other countries to achieve gender parity, then suggests that the United States should adopt similar frameworks. This approach treats legal and constitutional reform as the primary mechanism for change, with attention to political mobilization as secondary support.

This framework is partially correct but ultimately incomplete. The history of women's rights in the United States demonstrates that legal change without cultural and structural transformation produces partial victories at best. The Nineteenth Amendment guaranteed women's voting rights in 1920; yet women's voting rates remained lower than men's for decades, only converging in recent elections. Brown v. Board desegregated schools in 1954; yet meaningful educational integration remained decades away and has since retreated. Roe v. Wade established abortion rights in 1973; yet access has been systematically narrowed through regulations, clinic closures, and state laws for fifty years.

Each of these examples involved legal victories followed by sustained political conflict over implementation. Gender parity faces similar dynamics: even if the United States adopted comprehensive quota legislation, the question would become whether such legislation could survive constitutional challenge, whether enforcement mechanisms would be adequate, and whether political opposition would find alternative methods of exclusion.

The evidence from other countries suggests both possibilities. France adopted gender parity requirements in 1999 and faced immediate legal challenges that courts ultimately rejected. Belgium implemented quotas that initially produced only modest representation increases until party-level compliance improved. Italy achieved gender balance requirements but circumvented them through a complex system of multiple candidates per district. In each case, formal legal rules were necessary but not sufficient; political culture, party organization, and sustained advocacy determined outcomes.

For the United States, this suggests that advocacy should pursue multiple channels simultaneously. Legal strategies might include challenging ballot access rules that create barriers to candidate entry, arguing that the Constitution's requirement of "republican government" implies representation proportionate to the population, and developing litigation that frames the lack of women's representation as a violation of equal protection. Structural strategies might include organizing within parties to change nomination processes, building coalitions for electoral system reform toward proportional representation, and developing robust support networks for women candidates that reduce barriers to entry.

But perhaps most important is cultural transformation. The narrative that "the problem for women is not winning, it is deciding to run" locates the barrier in women's psychology rather than in political structures. This framing, while partially accurate, risks obscuring how structures shape psychology. Research cited by Piscopo shows that women perceive themselves disadvantaged in every aspect of campaigning—this perception is rational response to structural disadvantage, not irrational self-doubt.

Changing this culture requires changing structures, but it also requires alternative narratives. Rather than exhorting women to lean in, advocates should normalize women's political ambition as simply political ambition, frame women's presence as correcting historical exclusion rather than as special privilege, and celebrate women's political achievements as achievements rather than as progress toward an ideal. Media coverage that treats women candidates as serious candidates rather than as novelty or aberration would help.

Piscopo's essay provides valuable conceptual tools for thinking about these challenges. The distinction between negative and positive rights clarifies why formal suffrage has proven insufficient. The international evidence demonstrates that alternatives exist and work. The analysis of descriptive versus substantive representation explains why mere presence matters for democratic legitimacy. But application of these insights to American conditions requires acknowledging that the United States is not Latin America, is not Europe, and is not any other country. The strategies that succeed here will be American strategies, developed by American advocates responding to American conditions, even as they draw on global lessons and models.
