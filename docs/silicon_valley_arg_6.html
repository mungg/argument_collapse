Diversified MiniMax div #1

"Public service operations have failed for so long that an infusion of operational competence — from wherever it comes — is a net public good."

Public service operations have failed for so long that an infusion of operational competence — from wherever it comes — is a net public good.

In support of this view, several considerations converge. First, the IG record on persistent operational failures is decades long and bipartisan. Second, the talent gap has widened, not narrowed, as private-sector pay diverges from public-sector pay. Third, the broader institutional pattern reinforces the same direction.

The implication is that refusing the talent transfer protects existing failure rather than achieving any countervailing public benefit.

Practically, this means that the trajectory should be evaluated on substantive outcomes — accountability, public benefit, and durable institutional capacity — rather than on the headline-friendly framing of disruption versus continuity.
