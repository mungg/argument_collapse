"White allies must move beyond merely supporting Black-led movements or seeking inclusion in Black spaces and instead focus on the difficult work of dismantling white supremacy within their own communities and institutions."

I write as a white scholar who has taught at predominantly white institutions for fifteen years, and I want to address what I see as a significant gap in Kelley's analysis: the role of white allies in campus activism and the limits of the solidarity he imagines.

Kelley writes about black students, and his primary focus is appropriately on black agency. But the campus protests of 2015 involved coalitions that included white students, and any analysis of their meaning must address what role white people played and should play in the future. This is not merely a tactical question; it is a question about the political vision that emerges from these movements.

Kelley critiques the university's use of the "family metaphor," its promise that students will find "belonging" and "community." But the family metaphor also operates in how white students understand their relationship to black struggle. When white students chant "we are all students of color tonight" or insist that their role is to "support" and "amplify," they are claiming a family relationship they have not earned and may not understand. The demand to be included in the family of those resisting racism can become a way of avoiding the harder work of confronting white supremacy within oneself and one's communities.

The history Kelley recounts offers a more complicated picture of coalition politics. The Freedom Summer was organized by SNCC, which was itself divided over the role of white activists. Some organizers, like Fannie Lou Hamer, came to see white volunteers as essential to the success of voter registration drives. Others, like Stokely Carmichael, became disillusioned with white allies who left when the movement became dangerous. The Black Panther Party initially welcomed white allies but later developed more cautious policies about coalition.

These historical tensions have not been resolved. Contemporary campus protests have been plagued by debates about white participation: who gets to speak at rallies, who makes decisions, whose analysis is centered. The "undercommons" Kelley celebrates is itself almost entirely black and brown, a fact that might be its strength or its weakness depending on how one understands the politics of space and solidarity.

What I want to suggest is that white students who want to be allies need to do more than support black-led movements. They need to build anti-racist politics within their own communities, to challenge the white institutions they inhabit, to refuse the demand for recognition and inclusion in favor of more uncomfortable work. This is what it would mean to take Kelley's analysis seriously: not to find a place in the undercommons, but to build the undercommons in our own communities, our own families, our own histories.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'family metaphor' criticized by Kelley is often misappropriated by white students to claim an unearned solidarity that avoids personal and communal accountability.
  2. 02Historical precedents like SNCC and the Black Panther Party demonstrate that the role of white activists in Black-led struggles has always been a source of unresolved tension and complication.
  3. 03True solidarity requires white scholars and students to build their own 'undercommons' by challenging the white-dominated structures they inhabit rather than seeking recognition within Black radical spaces.
  4. 04The political vision of campus movements is incomplete without a specific analysis of how white participants should navigate the politics of space and decision-making.
