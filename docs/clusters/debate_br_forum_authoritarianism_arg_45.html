"The Democratic Party should not abandon moderation, but rather refine it by aggressively disciplining the national party brand to appeal to mainstream voters and marginalize unpopular cultural positions."

The essay by Bonica and Grumbach is a useful provocation, but it mistakenly conflates “moderation” with “inauthenticity” and “popularism” with “pandering.” The result is a sweeping dismissal of a strategic tradition that, properly understood, remains the Democrats’ best hope—not just for winning elections but for building a durable anti-authoritarian coalition. The core error is the authors’ assumption that because the electoral benefit of moderation has diminished in the current environment, the entire enterprise of calibrating one’s message to mainstream opinion is misguided. That’s like saying that because an umbrella doesn’t work well in a hurricane, you should throw it away and pray for sunshine.

The authors’ empirical case is impressive in its sophistication but limited in its applicability. They demonstrate that the measurable effect of a candidate’s ideology on vote share, controlling for other factors, is negligible in the Trump era. But this finding is entirely consistent with a world in which moderation is still essential—it just means that moderation must happen at the party brand level, not the individual candidate level. Because elections are nationalized, no single candidate can easily outrun the party label. A moderate Democrat in a reddish district will be tied to Nancy Pelosi, AOC, and whatever the latest far-left social media outrage is, regardless of their personal voting record. The appropriate response is not to give up on appealing to the middle; it is to aggressively shape the party’s national image so that it no longer repels these middle voters. That requires party leaders, presidential nominees, and prominent voices to enforce a disciplined message that foregrounds popular economic policies and marginalizes unpopular cultural positions. When the authors say that moderates have been “exhausted” because they already dominate competitive districts, they miss the point: it’s not enough to have a moderate in the seat; the party must stop constantly supplying the opposition with ammunition that incinerates that moderate’s brand.

The proposed anti-corruption frame is an interesting idea, but it’s a message, not a strategy. An anti-corruption message can cut either way, as we saw in 2016 when Trump notoriously outflanked Clinton on the “rigged system” narrative. Without deep, visible changes in the party’s own behavior—not just rejecting PAC money but severing ties with the entire donor advisory apparatus that shapes policy—an anti-corruption frame will ring hollow. And even if it were credible, it’s not clear it would motivate the marginal voters Democrats need. The voters who stayed home in 2024 were disproportionately low-income, young, and people of color. Their failure to turn out likely had more to do with economic precarity and a sense that politics doesn’t deliver for them than with a burning concern for congressional insider trading. An anti-corruption message that doesn’t immediately and credibly promise a material improvement in their lives is just more noise.

Where the authors go seriously awry is in their treatment of turnout. They argue that progressive candidates slightly boost base turnout, and they imply that a mobilization strategy around anti-corruption could replicate that effect at scale. But the data they cite from tracking 120 million voters is fragile: it shows a small, aggregate effect that might be driven by specific sub-populations or contexts. Meanwhile, the real-world counterexamples are glaring. The 2020 Democratic primary, which featured a historically progressive field, produced a nominee in Joe Biden who explicitly ran as a moderate and a unifier—and he won, defeating an incumbent president. Yes, Biden benefited from extraordinary circumstances, but his victory was built on significant gains among suburban moderates and older voters, groups that a more aggressively progressive candidate might have alienated. The 2024 race, by contrast, saw Harris attempt a half-hearted pivot to the center after spending much of her political career being perceived (fairly or not) as a San Francisco progressive. The failure was not too much moderation; it was too little, too late, and too unconvincing.

The authors’ most revealing concession is that “nobody knows for certain” what will work. In that context, the prudent approach is not to abandon a strategy of broad appeal in favor of a gamble on anti-corruption populism; it is to diversify the party’s bets. Let a thousand flowers bloom, as the authors say, but that includes moderates. In districts where a moderate can credibly hold a seat—like the ones that flipped in 2018 and sustained the Democratic majority—it makes sense to run candidates who reflect their district’s cultural values. In safely blue seats, let progressives push the envelope and develop new arguments. The national party should focus on what unites: a robust economic agenda that raises wages, lowers costs, and invests in public goods, while leaving cultural debates to be fought at the local level where nuance is possible. This is not Cleon’s pandering; it’s the wisdom of a big-tent party that knows its coalition includes both the Bernie Sanders volunteer and the Joe Manchin voter.

The authors’ invocation of anti-authoritarian movements abroad actually undercuts their own argument. When democratic forces rallied against Viktor Orbán in Hungary, they didn’t abandon centrist voters to chase anti-corruption mobilizations. They built a broad coalition—including conservatives, liberals, and socialists—that was united by a commitment to democratic norms and European integration. In Poland, the Civic Coalition succeeded by mobilizing a massive turnout, yes, but that mobilization was fueled by a clear, positive alternative to PiS, not just by a negative anti-corruption message. They appealed to voters’ desire for a normal, competent, decent government. That is a form of moderation in temperament if not in policy. The lesson is not to discard the center but to build a coalition that makes the center possible again.

Ultimately, the Democrats’ problem is not that they have been too moderate; it’s that they have been perceived as extreme on cultural issues while being insufficiently bold on economic ones. That’s a branding problem, not an ideological one. The solution is to rebalance the public’s perception without abandoning the moral commitments that define the party. A candidate can support transgender rights while also believing that women’s sports should be protected. A candidate can call for an assault weapons ban while also respecting the rights of responsible gun owners. These aren’t contradictions; they are the balance of a pluralistic society. Politics is the art of the possible, and in a country as diverse as the United States, the possible necessarily lies somewhere near the middle. To reject that is to reject democracy itself.

  2. 02The proposed anti-corruption framework is an insufficient strategy because it lacks credibility without deep institutional changes and fails to address the material economic concerns of disengaged voters.
  3. 03Historical evidence, such as Joe Biden’s 2020 victory, suggests that broad appeal to suburban moderates and older voters remains a more viable path to victory than gambling on progressive mobilization.
  4. 04Successful anti-authoritarian movements abroad demonstrate that victory requires building broad, inclusive coalitions that include centrist and conservative elements rather than narrowing the focus to anti-establishment populism.
  5. 05The party's primary challenge is a branding problem where it is perceived as culturally extreme, which should be solved by balancing moral commitments with mainstream pluralistic values rather than moving further left.
