"Agnes Callard’s defense of eternal anger and revenge is flawed because it relies on a purely backward-looking logic that ignores the pragmatic and functional necessity of moving past anger to maintain human well-being and social order."

Agnes Callard’s essay, "The Reason to Be Angry Forever," provides a brilliant but ultimately flawed critique of the philosophical consensus regarding anger. She argues that the stoic eradication of anger and the sentimentalist purification of anger both fail because they ignore the stubborn rationality of grudges and revenge. Callard asserts that because a past wrong cannot be undone, the reason to be angry is permanent. She further argues that the mechanism by which we hold people accountable is inherently vengeful—making the offender’s "bad" our "good." Therefore, to engage in moral life is to be morally corrupted. While Callard's psychological description of anger's grip is masterful, her argument collapses because it relies on an impoverished, purely backward-looking conception of moral rationality that ignores the necessity of pragmatism and consequence in ethical life.

Callard's Argument for Grudges rests on a simple premise: a past injustice is an unchangeable historical fact; therefore, the reason for anger generated by that fact is eternal. Apologies and restitution do not alter the past, so they do not invalidate the anger.

From a strict, logical-positivist perspective, Callard is correct that the past cannot be changed. But moral rationality is not a mathematical exercise; it is a pragmatic tool for navigating human existence. A purely backward-looking rationality is useless for human beings who must live in the present and build the future. When we assess the rationality of an emotion or a policy, we must look at its function and its consequences.

The function of anger is to signal a breach of norms and to motivate a response that restores the moral equilibrium. Once that equilibrium is restored—through apology, restitution, and a credible commitment to future good behavior—the functional utility of anger is exhausted. To claim that it is "rational" to continue feeling the same level of anger is to sever rationality from utility. It is akin to a smoke detector continuing to blare days after the fire has been put out, simply because the fire *did* occur. A smoke detector that cannot be reset is not "perfectly correct"; it is broken.

Similarly, Callard's Argument for Revenge asserts that revenge is "perfectly rational, justified, and intelligible" because it imposes the offender's rule back upon them. She writes, "insofar as one acts from anger, one pursues what is good for oneself by doing what is bad for another."

But is this actually rational? Even Callard admits that extracting this revenge requires the victim to "remodel your psychological landscape into one devoted to regulating mine." The victim must bear significant psychological costs, obsessing over the offender and sacrificing their own peace of mind. A utilitarian or pragmatist would immediately point out that an action which decreases the overall well-being of the victim, inflicts pain on the offender, and fails to produce any tangible future benefit (since the past cannot be changed) is the very definition of irrationality.

Callard anticipates this by saying, "the point is not that, all things considered, one should take revenge. One may take other factors... into account." But this caveat undermines her entire thesis. If "other factors" (like one's own sanity, social cohesion, and future happiness) routinely outweigh the desire for revenge, then the "logic of anger" is not a supreme moral directive; it is merely a primitive psychological impulse that our broader, more comprehensive rationality must manage and suppress.

Callard's reliance on Foucault and Nietzsche to suggest that our social institutions (like prisons) are just sublimated vengeance is well-taken. But it actually proves the opposite of her point. The reason we outsource punishment to an impartial state apparatus is precisely because interpersonal revenge is so corrosive and irrational. We recognize that the victim’s raw, limitless anger cannot be the basis for a functioning society. The law, with its rules of evidence and proportional sentencing, is a pragmatic mechanism designed to put a definitive end to the cycle of violence that Girard warned about. By sublimating revenge into institutional justice, we create a system where victims do *not* have to spend their lives endlessly prosecuting their offenders. They are freed to move on.

Callard’s concluding story about her grandmother is a poignant illustration of what happens when one cannot move on. The grandmother's Holocaust trauma caused her to view a young German girl as an enemy. Callard concludes that her grandmother was "not innocent" and that this proves we cannot be good in a bad world.

But Callard's diagnosis is inverted. The grandmother’s prejudice was not a rational moral judgment; it was a heuristic failure caused by extreme trauma. She applied a defensive mechanism developed for Nazis to a child. This is deeply understandable, but it is not a model of moral rationality. It is a cautionary tale about the devastating, blinding consequences of perpetual anger.

Callard gives us a stark choice: be morally corrupted by eternal vengeance, or be morally corrupted by acquiescing to evil. But a pragmatic approach offers a third way: we use anger as a temporary tool to demand repair, and once that repair is achieved (or institutional justice is served), we rationally choose to let the anger go. Not because the past is changed, but because the future demands our attention.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moral rationality should be viewed as a pragmatic tool for navigating the future rather than a mathematical exercise in acknowledging unchangeable past facts.
  2. 02The functional utility of anger is to restore moral equilibrium; once restitution is made, continuing the anger is as irrational as a smoke detector that refuses to stop blaring after a fire is extinguished.
  3. 03Revenge is inherently irrational from a utilitarian perspective because it requires the victim to incur significant psychological costs and sacrifice their own peace of mind for no tangible future benefit.
  4. 04The existence of impartial legal systems proves that society recognizes interpersonal revenge as corrosive and seeks to replace it with a pragmatic mechanism that ends cycles of violence.
  5. 05Callard’s example of her grandmother’s prejudice illustrates a heuristic failure caused by trauma rather than a rational moral judgment that should serve as a model for human behavior.
  6. 06A pragmatic third way exists between vengeance and acquiescence: using anger as a temporary tool to demand repair and then rationally choosing to let it go for the sake of the future.
