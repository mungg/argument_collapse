"While Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' correctly identifies the need for sustainable living, its focus on the psychological fulfillment of the affluent North ignores the material debt and structural restitution owed to the global South."

Kate Soper’s “alternative hedonism” is a necessary but profoundly circumscribed intervention. She is right that the rich world’s model of the good life is a planetary disaster and that we must find ways to live that are both sustainable and genuinely pleasurable. But by addressing herself almost exclusively to the affluent societies of the global North, and by centering the question of pleasure, she unwittingly reproduces the very provincialism she warns against. The “we” of her essay — the overworked, overconsuming subjects who need to learn to enjoy slower, local, less material lives — is not the “we” of humanity. It is a privileged minority whose consumption patterns have been subsidized for centuries by the extraction of resources and labor from the global South. Any vision of an alternative hedonism must begin not with the question of what the rich will lose, but with what the poor are owed.

There is a bitter irony in urging the citizens of rich nations to rediscover the joys of gardening, bike-riding, and mending clothes, when billions of people in the developing world have never had the chance to leave behind a far more arduous version of that life. The peasant farmers, factory workers, and slum dwellers of the global South are not suffering from a surfeit of consumerist pleasure; they are struggling for basic dignities — clean water, reliable electricity, decent housing — that the West long ago took for granted. For them, the “alternative hedonism” Soper extols can sound disturbingly like the advice of a comfortable outsider to remain content with less. Nemonte Nenquimo’s powerful words, which Soper quotes, come not from a place of hedonist choice but from a history of violent dispossession. To deploy them as part of a plea for slower travel is a category mistake.

I do not believe Soper intends to impose a new austerity on the world’s poor. She explicitly notes that economic growth will be needed in the short term for the poorest nations and acknowledges the monstrous inequality of carbon emissions. Yet the architecture of her argument structurally sidelines the demands of global justice. The shift to a postconsumerist economy in the North will require not just a change in desires but a massive transfer of wealth and technology to the South to enable leapfrogging to clean energy and sustainable development. The “debt for climate” campaign she mentions is a start, but the scale of what is needed — reparations for climate loss and damage already locked in, funding for adaptation, and the relinquishment of the intellectual property that keeps green technology expensive — goes far beyond debt cancellation. It requires the rich world to accept that its own “good life” will have to contract in material terms not only for the sake of the planet but as a matter of restitution.

From a Southern vantage point, Soper’s focus on the pleasures of alternative consumption can seem almost decadent. When we are told that we should take pleasure in cooking from scratch or walking to the market because it is more “sensually engaging,” we hear the echo of a long colonial tradition in which the hardships imposed on the colonized were rebranded as spiritual values — simplicity, contentment, closeness to nature. The global poor do not need a new aesthetic of the good life; they need concrete, material improvements: solar panels, efficient cookstoves, reliable public transit, and, yes, the chance to enjoy some of the conveniences that Northerners are now being told to renounce. The worldwide production of over 100 billion items of clothing annually is obscene, but much of that production occurs in Bangladesh or Vietnam, where a garment worker’s livelihood depends on it. A sudden contraction of fast fashion without a just transition would immiserate millions.

This is not a brief for continuing business as usual. The climate crisis is already devastating the poorest nations, who have contributed least to it. The global economic order is a racket. But the way out must be led by those with the greatest responsibility and capacity — the affluent North — in a spirit not of discovering new pleasures but of discharging a debt of justice. That will involve consuming far less, but the framing should be one of solidarity and restitution, not hedonism. The joy that might come from living more simply on a healed planet is a byproduct, not the purpose.

Soper’s retrieval of utopian desire is admirable and needed. But until it is stitched into a truly internationalist framework that centers the demands of the global majority, her alternative hedonism will remain what it risks being: a beautiful dream for the few, paid for by the misery of the many.
