"The lead essay’s defense of an absolute right to protest fails to distinguish between uncomfortable political speech and targeted, coordinated acts of intimidation that create a discriminatory environment for Jewish and Israeli students."

Alex Gourevitch’s eloquent defense of the right to protest rests on a series of false equivalences and a profound moral blindness to the nature of what unfolded on American campuses over the past year. His essay frames the debate as one between a courageous, principled commitment to free expression and a panicky, illiberal retreat into safetyism. But to accept that frame is to ignore the real and documented harms experienced by Jewish and Israeli students in the name of “protest.” When large groups of demonstrators chant “Globalize the intifada” outside Hillel houses, when student activists post the names and photos of Jewish students on social media with captions urging their peers to “hold them accountable,” when a mob physically blocks Jewish students from entering a central campus building until they renounce their Zionism—these are not merely instances of uncomfortable speech. They are targeted, coordinated acts of intimidation designed to make a minority group feel unwelcome, afraid, and excluded. To call such conduct a “right to be hostile” is to dress up bullying in the robes of principle.

Gourevitch’s central move is to wave away the hostile environment justification by claiming that it rests on nothing more than subjective feelings. He quotes students who say they felt unsafe walking through protests and dismisses their testimony as insufficient to meet an objective standard of harassment. But he ignores a critical dimension: the chants, signs, and actions were not generically offensive political speech. They were directed at a specific, historically persecuted group in a context where that group has, in living memory, been the target of genocidal violence. When a protest explicitly celebrates the October 7 massacre—as some did—and calls for armed resistance against Zionist “settlers” around the world, it is reasonable for Jewish students to perceive a threat of violence, not merely a bad idea. The line between speech and conduct blurs when the speech is a direct incitement to harm.

Protesters have every right to criticize Israel’s government and its military campaigns. They do not have a right to turn a campus commons into a space where Jewish students are systematically excluded. Title VI of the Civil Rights Act does not require administrators to wait until someone is physically assaulted before acting; it requires them to ensure equal access to education. When a university allows an encampment to occupy the main thoroughfare for weeks, replete with checkpoints that require students to declare their political views before passing, it is failing in that duty. The fact that some universities overcorrected—that Trump’s intervention was a brute political shakedown—does not retroactively sanctify everything that happened before. Two wrongs do not make a right.

Gourevitch’s historical analogies also fail him. He cites the 1960s protests and the long tolerance for encampments against apartheid and for living wages. But those protests were not accompanied by an explicit rejection of the right of an entire national or ethnic group to exist, nor by the systematic singling out of members of that group for personalized harassment. The anti-apartheid movement targeted a foreign regime, not the white South African students on campus (however complex those dynamics). The Gaza protests, by contrast, often collapsed the distinction between the state of Israel and Jewish identity, leading to the exclusion of anyone suspected of Zionist sympathies from progressive spaces. That is not protest; it is a purge.

The most troubling part of Gourevitch’s essay is his deployment of the argument that “whatever right protesters claim for themselves is a right they are also claiming for their enemies.” He means this as a defense of even the most inflammatory speech, but it actually reveals the deep asymmetry. If the right to protest includes the right to target and intimidate a specific minority, then that right is not reciprocal. The Jewish students do not have a rival encampment demanding that Muslims be driven from campus; they are not calling for the annihilation of Palestinians. The power differential matters. And in the current climate, the call for an absolutist right to protest, without any guardrails against targeted harassment, becomes a license for the strong—those who can mobilize large numbers and dominate public space—to silence the weak.

None of this is to justify the Trump administration’s overreach, which is a transparent assault on academic independence. But that overreach does not absolve us of the responsibility to take seriously the claims of Jewish and Israeli students who say they could not safely access their education. A university that cannot protect its most vulnerable members from targeted harassment has already abandoned its mission. The solution is not to give everyone a free pass to scream whatever they want in whatever manner they choose; it is to enforce consistent, content-neutral rules that prohibit harassment, threats, and obstruction, and to adjudicate violations with real but proportionate sanctions. Gourevitch’s vision, for all its lofty rhetoric, would turn campuses into arenas of perpetual intimidation where only those with the thickest skins and the largest megaphones survive. That is not democracy; it is a war of all against all.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The 'hostile environment' standard is an objective legal necessity under Title VI to ensure equal access to education, rather than a mere reflection of subjective 'safetyism.'
  3. 03Historical analogies to the 1960s or anti-apartheid movements are invalid because those protests did not systematically target and exclude a specific ethnic or national group on campus.
  4. 04An absolutist right to hostile protest creates a power imbalance where large, aggressive groups can effectively silence and marginalize vulnerable minority populations.
  5. 05Universities must enforce content-neutral rules against harassment and obstruction to prevent the campus from becoming an arena of perpetual intimidation.
