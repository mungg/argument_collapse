"To preserve liberal democracy during future crises, the United States must implement structural and procedural reforms that constrain emergency executive power and institutionalize transparency, dissent, and cost-benefit analysis."

The most important sentence in Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee’s essay is not their conclusion that institutions failed. It is their observation that the pandemic tested “our commitments to liberal democracy.” COVID-19 was a public health emergency, but it was also a constitutional emergency in the broad sense: a test of how a free society governs under fear, uncertainty, and time pressure. By that standard, the record is mixed at best. We did some remarkable things, especially vaccine development. We also normalized forms of executive rule, expert deference, emergency spending, and speech management that deserve much more scrutiny than they have received.

The question now is not whether every governor, health official, superintendent, or university president should be condemned. Many were making decisions in fog. The question is whether we can build rules for the next fog. Liberal democracy cannot depend on leaders becoming wise under panic. It needs institutions that make panic less dangerous.

Emergency government tends to follow a pattern. At first, the public demands action. Executives move quickly. Legislatures defer. Courts hesitate. Experts gain authority. Agencies improvise. The press amplifies urgency. Costs that would ordinarily be debated are treated as distractions. Dissenters are told that now is not the time. Later, when the emergency recedes, institutions prefer to move on. No one wants to revisit frightened judgments; no profession wants to admit that its incentives may have distorted its perception.

That is why Macedo and Lee’s call for a postmortem is essential. But the postmortem should not be framed only as “Were NPIs effective?” It should ask how decisions were authorized, reviewed, communicated, and ended. Even a policy that might be justified for two weeks can become illegitimate if extended for months without fresh evidence and democratic approval.

The first reform should be strict sunset rules for emergency powers. In March 2020, governors and mayors had to act quickly. But long-lasting restrictions on movement, assembly, worship, business, and schooling should require legislative renewal at short intervals. Thirty days is a reasonable default; after that, the executive should have to present evidence, alternatives, costs, and exit criteria. If legislatures cannot deliberate during an emergency, then representative government is too fragile. Remote voting, emergency committees, and expedited procedures should be prepared in advance.

Second, emergency orders should be tiered by rights sensitivity. Not all interventions are constitutionally or morally equal. Publishing guidance, funding ventilation, expanding hospital capacity, distributing masks, or offering paid isolation support are different from closing churches, banning protests, shuttering schools, or prohibiting people from earning a living. The more a measure burdens fundamental liberties or children’s development, the heavier the evidentiary burden should be and the shorter the authorization period.

Religious liberty offers an example of what went wrong. Some restrictions on large indoor worship during severe outbreaks were defensible if comparable secular gatherings were treated similarly. But too often officials appeared to rank activities by cultural familiarity rather than neutral risk. When casinos, film productions, or favored commercial activities received more generous treatment than worship, constitutional distrust was predictable. A pandemic does not suspend the requirement of equal treatment. Indeed, emergencies are when equal treatment matters most.

Third, schools should receive special legal protection. We learned, at terrible cost, that school closure is not a routine public health measure. It is a massive intervention in children’s lives, family stability, educational equality, disability services, nutrition, mental health, and future earnings. Future pandemic plans should classify in-person schooling as essential infrastructure. Closures beyond short emergency periods should require specific findings: why less restrictive measures cannot work, how vulnerable staff and students will be accommodated, what services will be preserved, how learning loss will be addressed, and what measurable conditions will trigger reopening. The presumption should be: bars close before schools; adult entertainment yields before childhood development.

Fourth, public health agencies need a new ethics of communication. The old model assumed that consistent messaging produces compliance and that public disagreement among experts confuses people. There is truth in that concern. But enforced simplicity can become deception. During COVID-19, officials sometimes failed to distinguish evidence from aspiration, uncertainty from settled fact, and population-level advice from moral command. The result was not durable trust but brittle trust: strong for those already inclined to believe, shattered for those who noticed contradictions.

Agencies should adopt a “candor protocol” for emergencies. Every major recommendation should state: what we know; what we do not know; how confident we are; what values are involved; who is likely to bear the costs; when the guidance will be revisited; and what evidence would change it. This may sound cumbersome, but citizens can handle complexity. What they cannot forgive is being managed.

Fifth, scientific advisory bodies should include structured dissent. Courts have dissents; central banks publish minutes; intelligence agencies sometimes register confidence levels. Public health advice should do the same. If credible experts disagree about school closures, masking, vaccine mandates, or border restrictions, the disagreement should be summarized rather than hidden. The goal is not to paralyze action. It is to prevent officials from pretending that contested judgments are unanimous science. A minority report can be wrong and still useful; it forces the majority to answer hard questions.

Sixth, government should be sharply limited in its ability to pressure platforms over disputed scientific claims. There is a legitimate role for agencies in correcting false information and alerting companies to fraud, impersonation, or dangerous scams. But when officials lean on platforms to remove views because they conflict with current agency guidance, they risk converting private moderation into state-adjacent censorship. That risk is especially acute when guidance is changing. The lab-leak debate and shifting claims about masks, transmission, and vaccine effects show why no agency should be treated as an oracle. The better answer to most disputed claims is visible rebuttal, not disappearance.

Seventh, cost-benefit analysis should be institutionalized before the next crisis. This does not mean reducing every life to dollars or pretending that economics can settle moral questions. It means forcing decisionmakers to identify tradeoffs. How many children will lose services? How many cancer screenings will be delayed? Which workers will lose income? Which businesses will fail? Which communities will bear enforcement? What mental health harms are expected? What fiscal commitments are being made? In March 2020, uncertainty was unavoidable. By summer and fall, continued restrictions should have been accompanied by serious public accounting.

Eighth, federalism should be used as a learning system rather than a partisan sorting machine. One of the advantages of fifty states is experimentation. During COVID-19, however, policy differences became badges of tribal identity. Blue states often treated caution as virtue; red states often treated resistance as freedom. Federal agencies should have done more to compare outcomes rapidly and transparently, not to shame governors but to learn. If schools reopened safely in one set of conditions, others should have adapted. If a mitigation measure failed to show benefit, it should have been abandoned. Emergency federalism should generate evidence, not just conflict.

Macedo and Lee argue that stricter state policies did not clearly reduce mortality before vaccines. That claim should be examined seriously, though with humility. Ecological comparisons cannot settle all questions. Voluntary behavior, timing, density, age, climate, and prior exposure complicate inference. But the very fact that the evidence remains contested is a reason for procedural reform. When evidence is uncertain, coercive policies need more democratic scrutiny, not less.

The pandemic’s successes also teach institutional lessons. Operation Warp Speed worked because it set a clear goal, mobilized resources, tolerated financial risk, relied on scientific expertise, and focused on measurable outputs. It did not require closing debate about every other policy. The vaccine achievement should make us more pro-science, not less. But pro-science does not mean pro-technocracy. Science is most valuable when paired with transparency, competition, replication, and criticism.

The deepest danger now is that each faction learns only the lesson convenient to itself. Many progressives want to remember denialism, anti-mask theatrics, vaccine refusal, and attacks on health officials, while forgetting school closures, speech suppression, and elite insulation. Many conservatives want to remember closed churches, closed schools, and hypocritical officials, while forgetting mass death, hospital strain, reckless minimization, and the lifesaving effect of vaccines. A serious democratic memory must hold all of it.

How did we fare? Better than our worst moments, worse than our self-congratulation. We produced vaccines in record time and delivered emergency aid on an enormous scale. We also spent too freely without adequate safeguards, kept some children out of school far too long, tolerated arbitrary rules, allowed expert agencies to overclaim, and let public debate become morally policed. We were neither a tyranny nor a model of wisdom. We were a frightened democracy with weak guardrails.

The next pandemic will not look exactly like COVID-19. It may be deadlier for children, or less deadly overall, or spread differently, or evade vaccines longer. That uncertainty is precisely why the lesson cannot be a fixed policy script. The lesson must be constitutional: preserve deliberation, require evidence, protect liberties, prioritize the vulnerable, speak honestly, and make emergency power temporary.

Macedo and Lee are right that trust must be restored. But trust will not be restored by asking citizens to trust harder. It will be restored by designing institutions that deserve distrust less. That means rules before emergencies, candor during emergencies, and accountability after emergencies. A free society can act decisively against disease without forgetting that its citizens are not subjects to be managed. They are the reason the system exists.
