"While the Port Huron Statement’s ideal of participatory democracy remains morally significant, it is insufficient as a political strategy unless it directly confronts the structural power of capitalism and shifts focus from mere inclusion to economic ownership and control."

Tom Hayden’s essay is inspiring, but its very capaciousness reveals the weakness of “participatory democracy” as a politics. If liberals, radicals, libertarians, populists, Marxists, feminists, anarchists, progressive entrepreneurs, unions, NGOs, poets, musicians, parties, and even conservatives can all “easily fit in,” then perhaps the concept is no longer doing enough work. A banner broad enough to cover everyone may conceal the conflicts that matter most.

The Port Huron Statement spoke to alienation, bureaucracy, militarism, and the emptiness of Cold War liberalism. It helped give a generation a language of moral revolt. But fifty years later, the central fact of American life is not simply insufficient participation. It is capitalism’s domination of democracy. Wealth governs. Corporations shape law. Finance disciplines states. Workers produce more and receive less. Public goods are privatized. Education becomes debt. Health care becomes a market. Housing becomes speculation. Elections become auctions. In this context, participation without a theory of power risks becoming therapy.

Occupy Wall Street’s importance lies precisely in naming class power, even if it does so in the broad language of the 99 percent. For decades American politics has been organized around evasion: values, opportunity, competitiveness, innovation, responsibility, security. Occupy reintroduced inequality, exploitation, debt, and oligarchy into public speech. That is a real achievement. But the movement’s participatory forms, however admirable, do not by themselves answer the question of how to confront capital. General assemblies cannot socialize investment. Consensus process cannot regulate banks. Encampments cannot by themselves build worker power. At some point, the question is not whether people have voice, but whether they have control over resources.

Hayden’s praise for Barack Obama’s 2008 campaign as a manifestation of participatory democracy is especially troubling. The campaign mobilized millions, yes. But mobilization is not power. Small donors and volunteers helped elect a president who then governed within the constraints of Wall Street, the national security state, the insurance industry, and congressional conservatism. The banks were rescued; homeowners were not rescued on comparable terms. The public option disappeared. Afghanistan was escalated. Civil liberties abuses continued. The campaign’s grassroots energy became a managed apparatus. If this is participatory democracy, then the term has been emptied.

The lesson is not that Obama is uniquely perfidious. It is that electoral politics in a capitalist democracy is structurally constrained. Candidates need money, media access, expert validation, and party support. Once in office, they face bond markets, corporate lobbying, military bureaucracy, trade pressures, and the permanent threat of capital strike. Social movements can pressure them, but unless movements possess independent power rooted in workplaces and communities, they become adjuncts of the Democratic Party. They supply enthusiasm; others make policy.

The Port Huron tradition has often been strongest in moral critique and weakest in political economy. Its language of participation can make domination appear as exclusion from decision-making rather than as ownership and control. But the problem with a corporation is not only that workers lack a voice in management. It is that the enterprise is organized around profit for owners. The problem with universities is not only that students and faculty are excluded from governance. It is that education is being subordinated to market logic, debt financing, corporate research, and administrative expansion. The problem with climate policy is not only that citizens are insufficiently consulted. It is that fossil fuel capital has a material interest in burning carbon and the political power to defend that interest.

Participatory democracy can coexist with neoliberalism. Indeed, neoliberal institutions often adopt the language of participation: stakeholder meetings, community input, public-private partnerships, online feedback, choice, empowerment. Citizens are invited to comment on decisions already shaped by market imperatives. Nonprofits administer austerity with inclusive language. Universities hold listening sessions while raising tuition. Corporations crowdsource ideas while suppressing unions. Participation becomes a management technique.

That is why the old socialist question must return: who owns, who controls, who benefits? Without economic democracy, political participation is fragile. Workers need unions, but also more ambitious forms of control: cooperatives, public ownership, sectoral bargaining, pension fund activism, community land trusts, democratic planning for ecological transition, and constraints on capital mobility. Banks that are too big to fail are too powerful to be privately governed. Energy systems that determine the fate of the planet cannot be left to corporations whose legal duty is profit. Health care should not depend on employment or private insurance markets. Education should not require decades of debt.

Hayden’s coalition includes “progressive entrepreneurs,” a revealing phrase. There may be decent small business owners and socially minded innovators, but entrepreneurship cannot be the horizon of the left. The celebration of entrepreneurs often smuggles market ideology into progressive politics: creativity is privatized, risk is individualized, and structural inequality is recast as opportunity waiting to be seized. A democratic economy cannot be built on heroic founders any more than a democratic politics can be built on heroic candidates.

Nor can the left avoid conflict by invoking a wider sea with many currents. Some currents run against each other. Labor wants jobs; environmentalists may oppose carbon-intensive projects. Immigrant workers need legalization; some unions have feared wage competition. Nonprofits may depend on foundation funding tied to elite priorities. Libertarians may support civil liberties but oppose the redistribution and public provision necessary for substantive freedom. Conservatives may like local control until local majorities challenge property or privilege. Coalition is necessary, but it must be built through struggle, not sentimental pluralism.

To be fair, Hayden does not deny conflict. His own life has included serious battles against war, racism, and poverty. But his essay’s tone risks converting the Port Huron legacy into an all-purpose democratic humanism. The danger of such humanism is that it underestimates enemies. The civil rights movement did not win because segregationists were persuaded that participation was beautiful. It won because activists disrupted an economic and political order, forced federal intervention, and made the costs of maintaining Jim Crow too high. Labor did not win the weekend through dialogue. It won through strikes, organizing, socialist agitation, and fear among elites that worse would come if reform did not.

The same will be true now. Wall Street will not yield because citizens assemble transparently. Fossil fuel companies will not abandon reserves because climate activists speak movingly. Universities will not stop raising tuition because students testify. Employers will not grant dignity because workers ask to participate. Power concedes when counter-power is built.

Participatory democracy can be part of that counter-power if it is embedded in class organization. Democratic unions, tenant associations, debtor unions, militant community groups, socialist organizations, and disciplined coalitions can use participatory methods to develop members and choose strategy. But participation must serve organization, and organization must confront capital. Otherwise we mistake the experience of empowerment for the fact of power.

At fifty, the Port Huron Statement deserves respect, not reverence. Its moral seriousness is badly needed in a cynical age. Its insistence that ordinary people should shape history remains indispensable. But the next left must go beyond Port Huron. It must speak not only of voice but of ownership, not only of inclusion but of redistribution, not only of transparency but of control, not only of democracy as self-creation but of socialism as collective power over the conditions of life.

The question is not whether we all need participatory democracy. We do. The question is whether participatory democracy is willing to name capitalism as the system that most consistently defeats it.
