"While Merve Emre’s essay provides a rigorous cultural analysis of how reproduction is technologically mediated, it fails to bridge the gap between interpreting these social conditions and proposing concrete political actions or a transformative future vision."

Merve Emre's essay is a model of intellectually rigorous cultural analysis, but it raises questions about the relationship between cultural criticism and political action that deserve more attention. The essay's method—detailed case studies connected to theoretical frameworks—illuminates the mediated character of reproduction, but it doesn't point toward clear political interventions.

This limitation is not unique to Emre's essay; it's a characteristic feature of much contemporary cultural criticism. We have become skilled at analyzing how ideology works, at exposing the hidden assumptions embedded in seemingly natural practices, at demonstrating the contingency of categories we had assumed were fixed. But we have become less skilled at connecting these analyses to collective action for social change.

The essay's case studies illustrate the problem. Emre presents vivid accounts of women navigating assisted reproduction, but she doesn't tell us what to do with these stories. Are they meant to generate empathy? To illustrate structural barriers? To provide material for political mobilization? The essay doesn't say, and the ending—calling for universal healthcare and paid leave—feels appended rather than integrated.

The invocation of Firestone is instructive here. The Dialectic of Sex was explicitly a manifesto—a call to action, a utopian vision of reproductive transformation. Firestone's argument was that reproductive technology could liberate women from the burden of biological reproduction and the social roles attached to it. She aimed to change the world, not just interpret it.

Emre's essay, by contrast, is primarily interpretive. It explains how reproduction is already mediated, how the natural is an ideology, how structural inequalities shape reproductive access. But it doesn't offer a vision of what reproduction might become if these inequalities were eliminated. It describes the present with precision but doesn't imagine the future.

This timidity may reflect the political moment. Firestone wrote during the heady days of second-wave feminism, when revolutionary change seemed possible. Emre writes in an era of reaction, when modest reforms are achievements. But the reproductive justice movement offers visions of transformation that go beyond current reforms—demands for universal access to reproductive technology, for the democratization of fertility treatment, for the recognition of diverse family forms.

Emre's essay could contribute to this movement if it connected its analytical insights to political demands. The case studies show that assisted reproduction is already here, already shaping reproductive life for millions of women. The question is not whether to use reproductive technology but how to make it accessible, safe, and democratic.

This political vision would require engaging with practical questions that the essay avoids: How do we pay for universal reproductive healthcare? How do we regulate the fertility industry? How do we protect surrogates and egg donors from exploitation? How do we ensure that LGBTQ+ families have equal access to reproductive technology? These are not philosophical questions; they're political ones that require collective action.

The essay's final paragraph gestures toward this political vision—"until the natural no longer looms so large, no longer nestles comfortably into our language"—but it remains at the level of language rather than politics. Changing how we talk about reproduction is valuable, but it's not sufficient for changing how reproduction works.

Emre's essay is a significant intellectual achievement that deserves wide readership. But intellectual achievement alone won't change the material conditions of reproductive life. For that, we need political mobilization around the structural demands that the essay mentions but doesn't prioritize.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s detailed case studies serve as effective cultural criticism but lack a clear connection to collective mobilization or social change.
  2. 02Unlike Shulamith Firestone’s manifesto, which sought to revolutionize reproductive roles, Emre’s work remains primarily interpretive and descriptive of the present.
  3. 03The essay avoids essential practical and political questions regarding the regulation, funding, and democratization of the fertility industry.
  4. 04The concluding call for policy reforms like universal healthcare feels like an afterthought rather than an integrated component of the essay's analytical framework.
  5. 05Changing the language and discourse surrounding reproduction is an insufficient substitute for the political mobilization required to change material reproductive conditions.

"Merve Emre’s reliance on individual narratives fails to provide a cohesive political or economic analysis of reproductive technology, ultimately offering critique without proposing actionable solutions."

The most thought-provoking aspect of Merve Emre's essay is its implicit challenge to how feminist theory approaches reproduction. Emre criticizes manifestos for their universalizing tendencies, their failure to capture the specificity of individual reproductive experiences. But her own alternative—narrative—is equally problematic in different ways.

The case studies that occupy the essay's center are vivid and particular, but they don't add up to an argument. S's egg freezing experience, B's IVF debt, N and K's lesbian conception: these are compelling individually but don't cohere into a unified thesis. We learn that assisted reproduction is complicated, that structural inequalities shape access, that the natural is an ideology. But we don't learn what follows from these observations.

This methodological problem reflects a broader challenge in feminist cultural studies: the turn to narrative as a substitute for political analysis. Emre quotes Dorothy Roberts's reproductive justice framework but doesn't integrate it with her case studies. The result is a series of stories that illustrate structural injustice without analyzing its sources or proposing remedies.

The case study method also obscures the political economy of reproductive technology. Emre mentions that tech companies offer egg freezing as a perk, that B has to borrow money for IVF, that N and K rely on family financing. But she doesn't analyze how these arrangements serve particular interests—keeping workers productive, extracting profits from desperate patients, treating reproduction as an individual rather than collective responsibility.

A more rigorous political economy would ask: Who profits from assisted reproduction? Who bears its costs? How do corporate practices shape reproductive technology development and access? These questions matter because reproductive technology is not just a philosophical puzzle but a multi-billion dollar industry with significant political influence.

Emre's essay doesn't engage with this industry dimension. She describes medical procedures with precision but doesn't examine the pharmaceutical companies, clinics, and tech firms that drive innovation in reproductive technology. The result is an oddly decontextualized account of experiences that are fundamentally shaped by commercial forces.

The essay's invocation of Firestone is also underdeveloped. Firestone's manifesto was explicitly political—she argued for revolution, for the abolition of gender roles, for radical transformation of society. Emre's essay, by contrast, remains at the level of critique without proposing alternatives. The "manifesto tradition" she traces—from Firestone through Haraway to Laboria Cuboniks—offered utopian visions of reproductive transformation. Emre's essay offers only more critique.

This is a missed opportunity. If Emre genuinely believes that "all reproduction is assisted," she should argue for what this means for reproductive politics. Should assisted reproduction be publicly funded? Should it be regulated to prevent exploitation? Should we be working toward universal access as a matter of reproductive justice? These questions deserve direct engagement rather than oblique suggestion.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The use of specific case studies provides vivid personal detail but lacks a unified thesis or a clear path forward for reproductive politics.
  2. 02The essay prioritizes personal storytelling over a necessary analysis of the political economy and the corporate interests that drive the multi-billion dollar reproductive technology industry.
  3. 03By focusing on narrative specificity, the author fails to integrate Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework into a structural analysis of the sources of reproductive inequality.
  4. 04The response retreats from the radical, visionary tradition of feminist manifestos like Shulamith Firestone’s, opting for decontextualized critique rather than advocating for specific policy changes like universal access or public funding.

"While Merve Emre correctly identifies that the natural/artificial binary in reproduction is an ideological myth, her analysis lacks the necessary emotional weight and radical political advocacy required to transform this insight into a movement for universal reproductive justice."

Merve Emre's essay offers a genuinely original intervention in feminist debates about reproduction and technology. Her core insight—that the natural/artificial binary is ideological rather than descriptive—deserves wide circulation, though I want to extend her argument in a more utopian direction.

The essay's case studies are meticulously detailed, and their power lies in their specificity. S's egg freezing experience captures the rhythm of medicalized reproduction: nightly injections, daily clinic visits, the anxiety of waiting. B's IVF journey shows the class dimensions of assisted reproduction more clearly than any statistics could. N and K's lesbian conception story reveals the heteronormativity embedded in medical practice—from residents who assume they're friends to forms that can't imagine their family structure.

But Emre's treatment of these stories is oddly affectless. She describes the physical and emotional experiences with clinical precision but doesn't always convey their weight. B's miscarriage is recounted matter-of-factly, though the experience was clearly devastating. N and K's repeated failures are presented with almost bureaucratic detachment.

This restraint may be deliberate—Emre wants to show that these experiences are "normal," that medicalized reproduction need not be tragic—but it risks sanitizing the genuine suffering that assisted reproduction often involves. The hormonal swings, the surgical procedures, the uncertainty, the loss: these are not neutral experiences that happen to be technologically mediated. They are often profoundly difficult, and we shouldn't pretend otherwise.

At the same time, Emre's philosophical framing risks missing what is genuinely hopeful about reproductive technology. cárdenas's poem, which the essay quotes, shows the creative and political potential of individuals taking reproductive technology into their own hands, making it on their own terms. This DIY approach to reproduction offers a glimpse of what reproductive technology might become if it were truly democratized.

Emre quotes cárdenas's observation about "the privilege of cis-hetero reproduction," but she doesn't develop the political implications of this insight. If cis-hetero reproduction is privileged, then reproductive technology should be redistributed—made accessible to all who need it, regardless of income, sexuality, gender, or relationship status.

This redistribution would require political mobilization around reproductive justice demands: universal healthcare that covers fertility treatment, regulations that protect LGBTQ+ families, protections for surrogates and egg donors, and investments in childcare and family support. Some of these demands appear in Emre's conclusion, but they feel obligatory rather than integral to her argument.

I want to suggest that Emre's "all reproduction is assisted" thesis could be the foundation for a more radical reproductive politics. If all reproduction involves technology, then reproductive choice depends on access to technology—access that should be universal rather than market-mediated. This is Firestone's insight, updated for the present moment.

The essay's limitation is that it doesn't take this political step. It describes the mediated character of reproduction but doesn't argue for what we should do about it. Perhaps this reticence reflects the essay's origin as a literary-cultural analysis rather than a political manifesto. But the material demands of reproductive justice are urgent, and they deserve more direct advocacy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The clinical and detached tone of the essay risks sanitizing the genuine physical and emotional suffering inherent in medicalized reproductive processes.
  2. 02The essay fails to fully develop the utopian potential of 'DIY' reproductive technology as a means of democratizing and reclaiming reproduction from heteronormative medical structures.
  3. 03The 'all reproduction is assisted' thesis should serve as a foundation for demanding that reproductive technologies be treated as universal rights rather than market-mediated privileges.
  4. 04The specific policy demands for reproductive justice, such as universal healthcare and LGBTQ+ protections, feel like an afterthought rather than the central focus the material requires.
