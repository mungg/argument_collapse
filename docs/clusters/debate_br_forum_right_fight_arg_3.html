Diversified Gemini

"Daniel Richman’s proposal to rely on state and local law enforcement as a check on federal counterterrorism overreach is dangerously flawed because it ignores the historical and contemporary reality of local police abuses and the tendency of local majoritarian politics to prioritize security over minority rights."

Daniel Richman’s “The Right Fight” offers a comforting, if highly theoretical, vision of how to balance the demands of domestic intelligence gathering with the preservation of civil liberties in the post-9/11 world. His central thesis—that incorporating state and local law enforcement into the federal counterterrorism network will naturally temper federal overreach because local police depend on community trust—is an elegant piece of institutional design. By introducing the friction of local political accountability into the secretive machinery of federal intelligence, Richman hopes to achieve a systemic equilibrium: a counterterrorism apparatus that is both effective at finding terrorists and respectful of the diverse communities in which they might hide.

However, this optimistic scenario rests on a fundamentally flawed and ahistorical understanding of local policing in the United States. To believe that local police departments will serve as the natural guardians of civil liberties against federal encroachment requires us to ignore the actual history of local police abuses, the realities of racial profiling, and the specific political dynamics that govern mayoral and police elections in American cities. Decentralization does not equal accountability; more often than not, it simply multiplies the vectors of abuse and subjects vulnerable minorities to the whims of local majoritarian fear.

Richman correctly points out that the federal government’s overreach during the COINTELPRO era was a disaster for civil liberties. But he fails to fully grapple with the fact that local police were not merely passive dupes in those abuses; they were enthusiastic participants and, in many cases, independent innovators of political suppression. The "Red Squads" of the mid-twentieth century—special intelligence units established in major cities like Chicago, New York, and Los Angeles—operated with virtual impunity for decades. They harassed civil rights leaders, infiltrated anti-war groups, and compiled massive dossiers on local citizens engaged in constitutionally protected First Amendment activities. These units were deeply embedded in the local political structure, often used by mayors and local power brokers to monitor political rivals and suppress dissent. The abuses of local intelligence units were only curtailed by intense federal intervention and hard-won consent decrees in the 1970s and 1980s. To suggest that we should now entrust these same institutions with vast new intelligence-gathering responsibilities, under the assumption that their local nature makes them inherently more accountable, is to read history backward.

Even if we accept Richman’s premise that modern "community policing" has transformed the culture of local law enforcement since the 1960s, the contemporary evidence still does not support his optimism. Throughout the 1990s and into the present decade, local police departments across the country have been plagued by scandals involving racial profiling and excessive force. The phrase "Driving While Black" was not coined in response to federal law enforcement; it describes the routine, day-to-day practices of state troopers and local beat cops. The Rampart scandal in Los Angeles and the horrific abuses of Abner Louima and Amadou Diallo in New York City demonstrated that local police departments are perfectly capable of alienating the communities they police, often with the tacit approval of a broader electorate that prioritizes aggressive "law and order" tactics over the civil rights of minorities.

This brings us to the crux of Richman’s argument: electoral accountability. Richman argues that because mayors and county executives are elected, they will restrain police chiefs from adopting counterterrorism tactics that alienate local communities. "Scrutiny of police performance by the local electorate," he writes, will keep the police accountable. But this assumes that "the electorate" is a monolithic entity with a unified interest in protecting the civil liberties of immigrants and religious minorities. In reality, the political economy of fear dictates otherwise.

In the wake of September 11, the majority of voters in most American cities are deeply fearful of another attack. If a local police chief institutes aggressive surveillance of mosques, or conducts sweeping interviews of young Arab men, the political backlash from the targeted minority community may be far outweighed by the political rewards from the anxious majority. We have already seen this dynamic at play in the broad public support for the USA PATRIOT Act and the profiling of Middle Eastern men at airports. Local politicians are highly responsive to the demands of their constituents, but when those constituents demand safety at the expense of someone else’s liberty, local democratic accountability becomes a mechanism for oppression, not a safeguard against it.

Richman relies heavily on the idea that police need community cooperation to solve everyday crimes, which will prevent them from totally alienating immigrant populations. While it is true that police chiefs express concern about the chilling effect of enforcing civil immigration laws, this pragmatic concern only goes so far. When the mandate shifts from solving burglaries to preventing catastrophic terrorist attacks, the calculus changes. The pressure from federal authorities, the media, and the local populace to "do something" about terrorism will easily override the slow, painstaking work of community trust-building. We are already seeing local police forces eagerly accepting federal homeland security funds to purchase military-grade equipment and advanced surveillance technology. The temptation to use these new toys to aggressively monitor "suspicious" populations is overwhelming, regardless of the long-term cost to community relations.

Furthermore, Richman’s structural solution ignores the essential role of the federal courts and the federal legislature in defining and protecting constitutional rights. He dismisses judicial and legislative constraints as ineffective, arguing that the low-visibility decisions of counterterrorism are "simply not amenable to effective judicial or legislative constraint or oversight." Therefore, he concludes, "the primary sources of accountability and due moderation will have to come from institutional structure." This is a dangerous abdication of the rule of law. If our intelligence-gathering apparatus has become so vast and secretive that it can no longer be constrained by the courts or by Congress, the solution is not to throw up our hands and hope that the competing interests of local and federal bureaucrats will magically produce a just outcome. The solution is to demand robust judicial review, to repeal the most intrusive elements of the PATRIOT Act, and to insist on stringent oversight mechanisms.

Institutional friction is arbitrary. It depends entirely on the personalities of the local police chief, the mayor, and the local FBI Special Agent in Charge. In a city with a liberal mayor and a strong immigrant advocacy network, perhaps the police will push back against federal overreach. But in a more conservative jurisdiction, or a city where anti-immigrant sentiment runs high, local police may well become the willing shock troops of a domestic intelligence network that is far more invasive and unaccountable than anything the FBI could build on its own.

Relying on state and local governments to protect civil liberties in the War on Terror is a gamble we cannot afford to take. The protection of fundamental rights against the apparatus of national security requires clear, enforceable legal boundaries, not just the unpredictable push-and-pull of intergovernmental relations. Daniel Richman is right that state and local police will inevitably be part of the post-9/11 landscape, but we must view their involvement with deep skepticism and subject it to the highest level of legal scrutiny, rather than resting our hopes on the false promise of local accountability.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Local police departments have a long history of independent political suppression and civil rights abuses, such as the 'Red Squads,' proving they are not inherent guardians of liberty.
  2. 02The theory of local electoral accountability fails because anxious majorities often reward politicians for aggressive policing that targets vulnerable minority groups.
  3. 03The pragmatic need for community cooperation to solve local crimes is easily overridden by the intense political and federal pressure to prioritize catastrophic terror prevention.
  4. 04Relying on institutional friction between levels of government is an arbitrary safeguard that varies by jurisdiction and cannot replace robust judicial and legislative oversight.
  5. 05Decentralizing intelligence gathering may actually increase the risk of abuse by multiplying the number of agencies operating with advanced surveillance technology and minimal transparency.
