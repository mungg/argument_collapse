Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada archive should be judged as a significant work of imaginative fiction and literary world-building rather than being dismissed as a sociological hoax or a failure of authenticity."

Marjorie Perloff is one of our sharpest readers of poetry, and her unmasking of the cultural forces at play in the Araki Yasusada hoax is largely persuasive. She is right about the Orientalism, right about the guilt-ridden multiculturalism of the editors, and right about the historical impossibilities within the text. But in her rush to treat the Yasusada archive as a sociological symptom and a clever critique of the "New Sentence," Perloff entirely loses sight of the most important thing: the poems themselves.

Read "Telescope with Urn" or "Mad Daughter and Big-Bang" again. Divorced from the scandal of their authorship, these are extraordinary pieces of writing. Perloff dismisses their emotional impact by attributing it to "residual guilt" and "Orientalist" programming. She suggests that because we know Yasusada is a fiction, the pathos of a father discovering the "severed head of my mad daughter" is somehow nullified, reduced to a "surreal, oblique image" that "makes no demand on us."

This is a startlingly impoverished view of how literature works. When we read Shakespeare, do we dismiss the grief of King Lear because Shakespeare never actually had three daughters who betrayed him on the heath? When we read a great historical novel, do we refuse to be moved because the author did not personally experience the Napoleonic Wars?

The Yasusada archive is not a mere "hoax"; it is a sprawling, multi-genre epistolary novel written in verse. Kent Johnson has created a character of immense depth, tragedy, and aesthetic sophistication. The fact that the text is presented as a "found" archive, complete with fake footnotes, letters, and English assignments, places it in a rich tradition of literary world-building, akin to Nabokov’s *Pale Fire* or Borges’s *Ficciones*.

Perloff complains that Yasusada spares us the hard questions about geopolitical responsibility that actual war memoirs provoke. But fiction has a different job than a war memoir. Yasusada is an exploration of grief, the limits of language, and the surreal nightmare of surviving the end of the world. The anachronisms Perloff points out—the scuba divers, the premature reading of Barthes—need not be seen merely as "clues" left to expose gullible editors. Within the logic of the artwork, they can be read as the distortions of a mind shattered by trauma, a poetic consciousness where time and history have collapsed into the singularity of the "Big-Bang."

The editors who initially praised the work—Ron Silliman, Bradford Morrow—were not just duped by identity politics; they were responding to genuinely powerful writing. Their subsequent embarrassment, and the critical rush to dismiss the poems as mere pastiche, is a tragic failure of nerve. We should stop prosecuting Kent Johnson for impersonation and start reading Araki Yasusada as one of the most compelling and tragic fictional characters in contemporary American literature. The urn may be imaginary, but the art is real.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The aesthetic and emotional power of the poems remains valid regardless of the author's actual identity or background.
  2. 02Perloff’s dismissal of the work's pathos based on its fictional nature ignores the fundamental way that literature and persona-based art function.
  3. 03The archive is a sophisticated multi-genre epistolary novel that belongs to a rich literary tradition of invented worlds, similar to the works of Nabokov or Borges.
  4. 04The historical anachronisms in the text can be interpreted as artistic representations of a mind shattered by trauma rather than just clues to a deception.
  5. 05The initial positive reception of the work by editors was a legitimate response to high-quality writing rather than a mere byproduct of identity politics.
Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada archive should be celebrated as a masterwork of heteronymy that actualizes the postmodern 'death of the author' rather than being dismissed as a fraudulent hoax or reduced to the sociological motivations of a single creator."

Marjorie Perloff is entirely correct that the Araki Yasusada archive is a brilliant achievement, but she remains trapped in the very paradigm of "authorship" she claims the hoax disrupts. While she meticulously dismantles the naive, identitarian longing that led editors to embrace Yasusada, she ultimately seeks to unmask the "real" author—Kent Johnson—and evaluate his sociological motivations. In doing so, we miss the opportunity to embrace Yasusada for what he truly is: not a hoax, but a masterwork of heteronymy that perfectly actualizes the postmodern condition.

Since Roland Barthes and Michel Foucault announced the death of the author decades ago, the literary academy has paid extensive lip service to the idea that texts are produced by cultural discourses rather than autonomous, sovereign geniuses. Yet, as the Yasusada affair reveals, the moment a text actually enacts this decentering, the literary establishment recoils in sheer terror. Arthur Vogelsang of *American Poetry Review* calling the submission a "criminal act" is the desperate cry of a gatekeeper realizing the gates have melted. What exactly is the crime? The disruption of a biological copyright? The violation of a marketing category?

We should look not to Ossian, as Perloff suggests, but to Fernando Pessoa. The great Portuguese modernist did not merely write under pseudonyms; he created entire lives, philosophies, and distinct poetic styles for his heteronyms (Alberto Caeiro, Ricardo Reis, Álvaro de Campos). Yasusada is our contemporary Caeiro, complete with a network of translators, friends, and correspondents (Tosa Motokiyu, Okura Kyojin). The Yasusada archive is a decentered, collaborative text. In fact, Kent Johnson denies being the sole author, attributing the work to the pseudonym Tosa Motokiyu. Why do we insist on reducing this rich, polyphonic creation to a single Midwestern professor's careerist prank?

Perloff notes the anachronisms—scuba divers, Barthes in the 1960s—and assumes they are winks to the reader, intentional clues left by Johnson. But what if they are exactly what they appear to be: the necessary fractures in a text that is translating itself across multiple subjectivities? Yasusada is a textual node where the trauma of the twentieth century, the American avant-garde, and the mythos of Japan intersect. The "errors" are the friction of that intersection.

When we read a novel, we do not demand that the author have lived the events described. Why does poetry remain shackled to this naive autobiographical fallacy? The outrage over Yasusada's "inauthenticity" stems from the poetry world's lingering romanticism, its desperate need for the poet to be a saintly sufferer rather than a skilled manipulator of signs. Yasusada’s poems are stunning, and their beauty is not diminished by the lack of a corresponding biological body. The author is dead; long live Araki Yasusada.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The literary establishment's hostile reaction to the archive reveals a hypocritical attachment to biological authorship and marketing categories despite decades of academic lip service to the idea that texts are products of cultural discourse.
  2. 02Yasusada is best understood through the lens of Fernando Pessoa’s heteronyms—distinct, fully realized poetic identities—rather than as a simple pseudonym or a careerist prank.
  3. 03The anachronisms and 'errors' in the text are not just clues for the reader but represent the necessary friction and fractures of a text translating itself across multiple subjectivities and historical intersections.
  4. 04The outrage over the work's 'inauthenticity' exposes a lingering, naive romanticism in the poetry world that demands poets be 'saintly sufferers' rather than skilled manipulators of language.
  5. 05The aesthetic value and beauty of the poems are independent of the author's biological identity or personal history.
