"The comparison between Kent Johnson’s Yasusada hoax and Ezra Pound’s modernist translations is a flawed analogy that elides the fundamental ethical and aesthetic differences between transparent literary homage and deceptive impersonation."

**Pound, Propertius, and the Limits of an Analogy**

Marjorie Perloff returns several times in her essay to Ezra Pound, and the analogy bears scrutiny. She compares Yasusada's anachronisms—scubadivers, crematoria, capitalist investors among the chrysanthemums—to Pound's frigidaire in *Homage to Sextus Propertius*. She compares the layered linguistic registers of the Yasusada renga to Pound's *Electra*, where Greek tragedy meets the American vernacular. She concludes that Johnson, like Pound, is performing a kind of deliberate temporal overlay, "problematizing representation and tone." The analogy is suggestive, but I think it ultimately obscures more than it illuminates, and the differences between Pound and Johnson are worth spelling out.

Pound, in *Homage to Sextus Propertius*, signed his own name. He titled the work "Homage," not "Translations of." When the classicists attacked him for the frigidaire and for what they called his mistranslations of the Latin, Pound responded with the famous letter to the editor stating that he was not translating Propertius but performing an act of dialogue with him across two thousand years. The work's effect depended on the reader's awareness that two voices were present: the Latin elegist and the modernist American, layered into a palimpsest. Take away the awareness of Pound, and the *Homage* loses its constitutive irony. The frigidaire is funny because Pound has put it there.

Yasusada works differently. The Yasusada presentation does not invite the reader to perceive two voices. It invites the reader to perceive one voice—an obscure Hiroshima poet, recently deceased, whose notebooks have at last reached the West. The anachronisms function, in Perloff's reading, as clues that this single voice is in fact a fiction, but the clues are designed to be discovered only by readers careful enough to verify them, and—as the publication history makes plain—most readers, including very sophisticated readers, do not discover them. The work's effect on its actual audience is dependent on the audience not noticing what Perloff says they were "meant" to notice. This is a very different aesthetic situation from Pound's.

Perloff acknowledges the difference but folds it into the structural argument: "Pound was already famous when he wrote his Latin 'translation' and so he could afford to be Ezra Pound, whereas the unknown Kent Johnson, writing in what is an increasingly glutted and cutthroat poetry market, had no such alternative." This is curiously sympathetic to Johnson—as though impersonating a Hiroshima survivor were a regrettable but understandable career adaptation by an unknown poet competing for limited journal space. I want to push back. Many unknown poets in the current market do not impersonate Hiroshima survivors. Most of them, in fact, simply submit work under their own names and accept that this is harder. The Macpherson route is not the only route available to the obscure. It is a particular route, with particular ethical baggage.

The deeper trouble with the Pound analogy is that it imports Pound's authority into a work that has not earned it. Pound's *Homage* and his *Cathay* are situated within a body of openly signed work in which the poet's procedures are visible and accountable. We know how Pound used Fenollosa; we can compare *Cathay* against the Fenollosa notebooks Hugh Kenner reproduces; we can argue with Pound's choices and locate them within his larger project. Yasusada is not situated. The "translators" are themselves pseudonyms. The supposed source notebooks do not exist. We have no body of work against which to triangulate Johnson's choices, because we do not even know with certainty that Johnson is the author—Perloff has to use the qualifier "or whoever the 'real' Yasusada turns out to be." The reader has nothing to grasp except the surface texts and a swirl of attribution claims, each one less verifiable than the last.

I do not say this to insist that work must be signed by traceable persons to count as literature. Pessoa's heteronyms are signed by Pessoa; "Pierre Menard" is signed by Borges. The question is what kind of literary contract the work asks the reader to enter into. Pound, Pessoa, Borges—each of these writers asks the reader to perceive the apparatus of attribution as part of the work. Yasusada asks the reader, at least at first, not to perceive it. That asymmetry is what makes the case ethically distinctive, and the Pound analogy elides it.

There is one further consideration. *Homage to Sextus Propertius* makes free with a Roman elegist twenty centuries gone. The temporal distance affords an enormous license. No living person reads Pound's frigidaire and feels that their suffering has been appropriated, because Propertius's suffering, whatever it was, has been absorbed into a literary tradition for which Pound was a legitimate inheritor. The Hiroshima dead are not so distant. The survivors are still alive. The political and historical questions surrounding nuclear warfare are not yet settled. To overlay anachronistic registers onto a Hiroshima narrative is not analogous to overlaying them onto Propertius, because the historical material is not analogous. To pretend otherwise—to invoke Pound's example as legitimating Yasusada—is to flatten an important difference.

Perloff is by no means a naive partisan of Johnson, and her essay raises questions about the American poetry establishment that need to be raised. But I think the Pound analogy works in Johnson's favor more than it ought to, conferring a modernist legitimacy on what is really a postmodernist provocation of a quite different and quite more troubling kind. We can defend Pound's frigidaire without thereby defending Yasusada's scubadivers. The two cases are not the same, and we lose something important when we treat them as if they were.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike Pound, who signed his own name and invited readers to perceive a dialogue between two voices, Johnson’s work relies on the audience's ignorance of the author's identity to achieve its effect.
  2. 02The Pound analogy improperly grants modernist authority to a project that lacks a visible, accountable authorial body of work or verifiable source material.
  3. 03Impersonating a Hiroshima survivor carries unique ethical baggage that cannot be equated to a career adaptation or compared to the appropriation of ancient Roman figures whose suffering is distant and settled.
  4. 04The Yasusada hoax functions as a postmodern provocation rather than a modernist palimpsest because it initially asks the reader to accept the fiction as reality rather than as an artistic apparatus.
  5. 05Invoking Pound to legitimate Johnson flattens the distinction between a poet submitting work under their own name and one who exploits historical tragedy through a deceptive persona.
