The Relational Heart of Anger

Agnes Callard’s analysis of anger, for all its brilliance, suffers from a deep individualism. She treats moral life as a confrontation between two isolated selves—the wrongdoer and the victim—locked in a zero-sum struggle over value. Feminist philosophers have long argued that such a picture misses the fundamentally relational character of human existence. Once we see ourselves as embedded in networks of care and interdependence, anger reveals itself not as an inherently corrupting force but as a signal for relational repair. Callard’s tragic conclusions arise not from the nature of anger but from the impoverished moral ontology she assumes.

Consider the Argument for Grudges. Callard insists that apology and restitution do not alter the fact of the wrong and therefore cannot erase the reason for anger. But this treats moral facts as inert, free-floating propositions detached from the ongoing relationship between persons. In a relational model, an apology is not a mere attempt to change the past but a transformative act that reshapes the meaning of the past within the present relationship. When I apologize sincerely and you accept that apology, we are not undoing what happened; we are renegotiating the terms of our connection going forward. Your anger was, in large part, a reaction to a rupture in our bond, a betrayal of trust. My apology constitutes a recommitment to that bond, and your acceptance signals that the rupture is no longer a defining feature of our interaction. To hold on to the same intensity of anger after such an exchange is not to honor an eternal moral truth but to refuse the possibility of renewed relationship.

Callard might respond that the moral fact remains, and that is enough for eternal anger. But anger is not a mathematical theorem; it is a human emotion that arises within a context of expectation, trust, and shared life. A feminist perspective emphasizes that our moral identities are constituted in and through our relationships. Wrongdoing damages those relationships, but repair can restore them—not perfectly, but sufficiently. If anger is the appropriate response to the damage, then the appropriate dissolution of anger is the appropriate response to repair. To insist otherwise is to freeze the relationship at the moment of injury, foreclosing the very future that morality is meant to protect.

The Argument for Revenge is even more troubling from a care ethics standpoint. Callard portrays revenge as the mechanism of moral accountability, turning the wrongdoer’s evil logic against her. But this adversarial model of accountability ignores the alternative of holding someone accountable with the aim of bringing them back into right relationship. Accountability, in this view, is not about inflicting proportional suffering but about communicating the wrong in a way that invites understanding, repentance, and eventually reconciliation. This may involve protest, the setting of boundaries, and even temporary separation. But its end is restorative, not retaliatory. Callard’s “uncanny intimacy” of anger is indeed a sign of connection, but she reads it as necessarily hostile. In reality, that intimacy can be the very ground on which transformation occurs. When we care about someone, their wrongdoing wounds us deeply, but our anger can also be an expression of that care—a demand that they be better, for the sake of the relationship. The vengeful impulse is not the only or primary expression of anger; it is often a secondary reaction when the offender refuses to listen.

Callard’s own story of her grandmother and the German girl inadvertently supports this relational reading. She says her grandmother’s anger was a rational response to the Holocaust, but the outcome was a severed connection—not just with the perpetrators but with an innocent child who had no part in the atrocity. The grandmother’s anger, however historically grounded, failed to distinguish between the source of harm and the possibility of new relationship. In relational terms, that anger became destructive precisely because it was allowed to override the complex web of human connection in which even enemies’ grandchildren are, in some sense, neighbors. Callard herself, as a child, instinctively reached across the fence, recognizing a relational impulse that her grandmother’s logic suppressed. The tragedy of the situation is not that anger is essentially corrupting but that trauma can so narrow one’s relational vision that it becomes impossible to see the other as anything but a threat.

If anger is not inherently vengeful, then the dichotomy between corruption and acquiescence collapses. One can protest a wrong without desiring the infinite suffering of the wrongdoer. One can demand change without clinging eternally to the wrong. This is not a philosophical fiction; it is a daily practice for those who live in close relationships where forgiveness, boundaries, and accountability coexist. It is not easy, but it is real. Teaching children to say “I’m sorry” and “I forgive you” is not training them in a fantasy but in the skills of relational repair. The fact that such repair sometimes fails, that some wrongs are too deep for reconciliation, does not mean the project is hollow.

Ultimately, Callard has shown the dark side of a morality built on the model of autonomous individuals in combat. But that is not the only morality on offer. A care-based framework sees anger as a danger signal demanding a restoration of just relationship, and it equips us with practices—apology, forgiveness, and above all, the willingness to stay in connection—that can transmute anger into something that neither corrupts nor acquiesces. The world is not divided into saints and the corrupt; it is filled with people constantly reweaving the threads of connection, one apology at a time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moral facts are not inert propositions but are embedded in relationships, meaning that sincere apology and restitution can transform the meaning of a past wrong and provide a rational basis for ending anger.
  2. 02Anger functions as a signal for relational repair rather than a mathematical theorem of eternal grievance, and its appropriate dissolution follows the restoration of trust.
  3. 03Accountability should be viewed as a restorative process aimed at bringing a wrongdoer back into right relationship rather than an adversarial mechanism for inflicting retaliatory suffering.
  4. 04The vengeful impulse is a secondary reaction to a refusal to listen rather than the primary or necessary expression of anger.
  5. 05A care-based ethics framework collapses Callard's dichotomy between corruption and acquiescence by offering a middle path of protest, boundaries, and forgiveness.
