"Selective engagement is a superior grand strategy to offshore balancing because it maintains the forward deployments and alliance commitments necessary to prevent great-power conflict and regional instability."

Stephen Walt’s critique of the Bush administration’s foreign policy is trenchant, and his call for a more restrained use of American military force is a healthy corrective to the delusions of global hegemony. His proposal for offshore balancing, however, is an overcorrection that misreads the sources of global stability and the requirements of American leadership. Selective engagement—the strategy that Walt associates with George H.W. Bush and Bill Clinton—remains the superior framework, precisely because it sustains the forward deployments and alliance commitments that have kept the peace among great powers for six decades while avoiding both crusading utopianism and reckless disengagement. Walt’s bill of particulars against selective engagement is thin, and his argument that it failed because it was not selective enough actually points toward reform, not abandonment.

The central defect of offshore balancing is its assumption that local powers can reliably uphold regional balances without an on-the-ground American presence. Walt places great faith in the tendency of regional states to worry more about each other than about the United States. But that dynamic, in Europe and Asia, is itself a product of America’s forward presence, not a natural state of affairs that would persist in its absence. NATO’s integrated command structure and the U.S. military footprint in Europe were explicitly designed to dampen the security competition that had produced two world wars. Those conditions have not vanished just because the Soviet threat disappeared; they have been suspended by the American security guarantee. Remove that guarantee, and Europe’s historical rivalries could reemerge, not in the form of a German march to war, but as a disorderly scramble for influence in Eastern Europe, the Caucasus, or beyond. Walt’s confidence that a European Union with a barely coherent foreign policy can handle even modest security challenges ignores the EU’s miserable performance in the Balkans throughout the 1990s, when only American-led intervention produced a settlement. The same is true in Asia, where America’s hub-and-spokes alliance system has allowed Japan, South Korea, and others to forego the sort of competitive armament that would almost certainly erupt if they believed the United States was no longer the ultimate guarantor. A strategy that tells Tokyo and Seoul that they can rely on American help “only when absolutely necessary” will be read in those capitals as a signal to acquire their own nuclear deterrents and to hedge aggressively against China, setting off a destabilizing arms race that the United States would eventually have to pacify at much greater cost.

Walt’s treatment of the Persian Gulf also illustrates the weakness of his approach. He is right that the United States should not attempt to occupy or transform the region, but offshore balancing as he describes it—shifting weight as needed to prevent any one state from dominating—is essentially a policy of intermittent war. In a region where the primary oil producers are separated by shallow waters and deep animosities, a purely over-the-horizon presence invites the very aggression it is meant to deter. Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait in 1990 was made possible, in part, by the ambiguity of American commitments after the Iran–Iraq War. It is true that a permanent garrison in Saudi Arabia helped fuel the rise of al Qaeda, but the right lesson is not to abandon forward basing altogether; it is to diversify basing arrangements and lighten the footprint, as the Bush administration has already begun to do with new access agreements in Qatar, Kuwait, and elsewhere. The point of selective engagement is to maintain a visible, credible capability that makes aggression unattractive, while using diplomacy, sanctions, and multilateral pressure to manage proliferation threats. That mix is precisely what the Clinton administration attempted, with successes in shutting down Libya’s WMD programs and containing both Iran and Iraq—however imperfectly—without a catastrophic ground invasion.

Moreover, Walt’s proposal to end the unconditional U.S. security guarantee to Israel and to link support to withdrawal from the occupied territories, while appealing to many critics of the occupation, misunderstands the psychology of the conflict. Israel will not take existential risks for a settlement unless it is confident that the United States remains an unshakable defender of its security. That is why the Clinton parameters, which Walt cites approvingly, were offered in the context of a deep strategic embrace, not as a threat. Making U.S. support conditional would embolden rejectionists on all sides and signal to both Israel and its adversaries that the United States is an unreliable ally, thus undermining the very trust needed for painful concessions. A more effective policy would pair an unshakable commitment to Israel’s security with sustained, presidential-level diplomacy that presses both parties toward a final-status agreement, while simultaneously pushing Arab allies to normalize relations and support Palestinian institution-building. That is the essence of selective engagement: it uses the full weight of American commitment to create a framework in which both sides can take risks, rather than creating a vacuum that others will fill.

Walt is right that the United States cannot run vast areas of the world by force. But selective engagement does not aim to do so; it aims to create an environment in which the likeliest sources of great-power war and catastrophic proliferation are continuously managed through a combination of alliance discipline, forward deterrence, and multilateral institutions. The Bush administration’s mistake was not the global deployment of American power per se, but the contempt with which it treated the very alliances and institutions that give that power legitimacy. A strategy that reaffirms the centrality of NATO and the Asian alliances, invests in the United Nations and non-proliferation regimes, and uses force only as a last resort—the path Bush’s father pursued—is neither global hegemony nor a withdrawal to the water’s edge. It is the strategy of a power that understands that its own security is inseparable from the stability of the core regions of the world, and that leadership requires presence, not just power projection from a distance. That presence, exercised with restraint and in partnership, has been the foundation of the Pax Americana. It deserves to be preserved, not dismantled.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Offshore balancing incorrectly assumes that regional powers can maintain stability on their own, whereas historical evidence suggests that peace in Europe and Asia is a direct result of the American security guarantee.
  2. 02Withdrawing to an over-the-horizon posture in Asia would likely trigger a destabilizing arms race and nuclear proliferation as allies like Japan and South Korea seek to hedge against China.
  3. 03An intermittent, offshore presence in the Persian Gulf invites aggression by creating ambiguity regarding American commitments, whereas selective engagement deters conflict through a visible and credible military capability.
  4. 04Conditioning the security relationship with Israel would undermine the trust necessary for peace negotiations and signal to global adversaries that the United States is an unreliable partner.
  5. 05The failures of recent years stem from the Bush administration’s unilateralism and contempt for international institutions, not from the inherent flaws of a selective engagement strategy that emphasizes alliance discipline and multilateralism.
