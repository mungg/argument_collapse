"To be adequate to the present, a justice framework rooted in the history of slavery must be expanded into a project of environmental justice that addresses the racialized instrumentalization of nature."

Walter Johnson’s essay ends by linking slavery, racial capitalism, and the instrumentalization of nature. That final point deserves to be moved closer to the center. The plantation was not only a labor regime and racial order; it was an ecological project. It transformed land, water, plants, animals, bodies, and climates of risk. If justice rooted in the history of slavery is to be adequate to the present, it must become environmental justice.

Johnson is right to resist versions of the “Anthropocene” that treat humanity as a single geological actor. The human species did not collectively decide to clear Indigenous lands, build plantations, transport Africans, exhaust soils, burn coal, extract oil, and distribute toxins along racial lines. Some humans commanded; others were coerced. Some accumulated wealth; others accumulated disease, displacement, and death. To speak of “humanity” as the agent of planetary crisis can obscure the histories of empire, slavery, capitalism, and race that produced unequal responsibility and unequal vulnerability.

The plantation is a key site for understanding this. Cotton cultivation in the American South depended on Native dispossession, enslaved labor, global finance, and ecological transformation. Forests were cleared. Soils were depleted. Rivers became commercial arteries. Monoculture spread. The demand for new land intensified expansion westward, which meant further Indigenous removal and further extension of slavery. The plantation did not merely occupy nature; it reorganized nature for commodity production. It treated land as an input, enslaved people as capital, and ecological exhaustion as a reason to move on.

This pattern was not limited to cotton. Sugar plantations in the Caribbean remade islands through deforestation, soil exhaustion, brutal labor discipline, and deadly disease environments. Colonial extraction of rubber, ivory, timber, minerals, and later oil similarly joined racial domination to ecological violence. Du Bois’s image of piano keys and billiard balls carrying the hidden blood of elephants and African labor is an environmental history in miniature. Metropolitan pleasure was assembled from distant suffering, animal death, and colonial terror. Consumers encountered polished surfaces; the violence remained elsewhere.

That “elsewhere” is one of racial capitalism’s central technologies. Distance makes extraction morally manageable. The investor does not see the mine. The consumer does not see the plantation. The city does not see the poisoned watershed. The suburban household does not see the refinery community. The wealthy nation does not see the island losing shoreline or the drought-stricken farmer. Racial categories help organize this distance by marking some communities as naturally suited for danger, heat, waste, and sacrifice.

Environmental racism in the United States is therefore not a recent aberration. It is part of a long history in which black, Indigenous, Latino, Asian, and poor white communities have been differentially exposed to harm. Slavery itself was an environmental health regime: heat, malnutrition, disease, overwork, polluted water, inadequate shelter, and violence structured bodily life. After emancipation, sharecropping, convict leasing, industrial labor, segregated housing, and urban pollution continued to distribute environmental risk. Today toxic dumps, highways, petrochemical corridors, lead exposure, inadequate infrastructure, and climate vulnerability are mapped through histories of race and class.

The lead crisis in Flint, Michigan, still unfolding as Johnson writes, is a stark example. A majority-black, economically battered city was exposed to poisoned water through decisions made under austerity governance. Residents’ complaints were dismissed. Children’s bodies became evidence only after official denial. Flint is not slavery, but it belongs to the afterlife of a political order in which some communities are treated as expendable. The question “who must drink the poison?” is inseparable from “whose lives are discounted?” and “who controls public resources?”

Climate change intensifies these questions. Rising seas, storms, heat waves, droughts, fires, and food instability will not strike evenly. Poor communities and formerly colonized regions face disproportionate danger despite contributing least to the atmospheric accumulation produced by industrial capitalism. Within the United States, communities already burdened by segregation, inadequate infrastructure, and health disparities will be less able to adapt. A human-rights framework can say that all persons have a right to life, health, and security. That is important. But Johnson’s framework asks the deeper historical question: how did the wealth that produced the emissions accumulate, and why are the costs distributed as they are?

The term “racial capitalism” is useful because it prevents environmentalism from becoming a politics of generic stewardship. Calls to “save the planet” can sound noble while leaving intact the social relations that made the planet profitable to destroy. If the problem is humanity as such, then the solution may be technocratic management: carbon markets, geoengineering, efficiency, conservation, population control. Some of these tools may have roles, but none addresses the racialized history of extraction. Worse, population control has often targeted the reproduction of poor and racialized women rather than the consumption of wealthy societies.

A justice rooted in slavery would ask different environmental questions. Who owns the land? Who decides what is grown, mined, burned, transported, dumped, and protected? Who labors in dangerous conditions? Whose neighborhoods lack trees, grocery stores, clinics, and clean water? Whose sacred sites are destroyed? Whose children breathe asthma-inducing air? Who profits from fossil fuels? Who pays for transition? Who has the power to say no?

Johnson’s emphasis on reproduction is relevant here. Environmental harm is reproductive harm. Lead damages children’s development. Air pollution affects pregnancy and asthma. Toxic chemicals alter endocrine systems. Climate disasters separate families and disrupt care. Food insecurity shapes bodies across generations. If slavery subordinated black reproduction to property, environmental racism continues to undermine the capacity of communities to sustain life. Reparations must therefore include ecological repair: clean water, soil remediation, safe housing, health care, renewable infrastructure, and community control.

This also means that labor and environmental politics must be joined rather than opposed. Workers in fossil fuel industries, industrial agriculture, logistics, and manufacturing often depend on jobs that expose them and others to harm. A just transition cannot simply shut down industries and abandon workers. Nor can labor justice be used as an excuse to poison communities or accelerate climate crisis. The history of racial capitalism teaches that capital thrives by dividing workers from communities, jobs from health, and present wages from future survival. A reparative environmental politics would guarantee employment, retraining, income, and democratic planning while rapidly reducing ecological harm.

Indigenous sovereignty is central to such a politics. Many of the most important environmental struggles are also struggles over treaty rights, sacred land, water protection, and consent. Standing Rock has reminded the country that pipelines are not merely infrastructure; they are assertions about whose land and water can be risked. Indigenous movements have long articulated a relation to land that challenges the property-centered worldview of racial capitalism. This does not mean romanticizing Native peoples as ecological symbols. It means respecting Native nations as political actors whose sovereignty is indispensable to environmental justice.

Johnson’s critique of “dehumanization” can also be extended to nature. Just as slavery did not simply fail to recognize enslaved people’s humanity but exploited their human capacities, capitalism does not simply “de-naturalize” nature. It recognizes the capacities of soil, rivers, forests, animals, and fossilized life in order to instrumentalize them. Cotton grows in particular climates. Rivers carry goods. Coal burns. Oil stores ancient sunlight. The system knows these capacities intimately, scientifically, financially. The problem is not ignorance of nature but domination of it under the sign of profit.

Yet we should be careful. Human beings are not the same as rivers, and the language of slavery should not be casually applied to nonhuman nature in ways that blur moral distinctions. Enslaved people were persons with claims, voices, and histories. Still, the histories are linked because the same imperial and capitalist projects treated both racialized people and land as sources of value to be extracted without reciprocal obligation. “Dominion” over nature and domination over people grew together.

What would environmental reparations look like? They would begin with historical accounting: mapping the relationship between slavery, segregation, industrial siting, land theft, and environmental exposure. They would require massive investment in communities harmed by pollution and disinvestment. They would include replacing lead pipes, cleaning contaminated land, funding public health, relocating communities when necessary and desired, supporting black farmers and Indigenous land recovery, protecting workers, and democratizing energy. They would require climate policy that recognizes historical responsibility, both within the United States and globally. They would treat clean water and air as owed, not gifted.

They would also require changing consumption in affluent communities. Racial capitalism has allowed some people to experience the world as convenience: cheap food, cheap clothing, cheap energy, cheap delivery, cheap waste disposal. The cheapness is an illusion produced by unpaid costs borne elsewhere. Slavery was one form of this illusion: cotton appeared as a commodity, not as stolen life. Today a plastic package, a smartphone, a gallon of gasoline, or a winter strawberry may likewise hide labor and ecological violence. Justice requires making those hidden relations politically visible.

The present moment is not encouraging. The new administration seems committed to fossil fuel expansion, deregulation, and hostility toward climate science. Environmental protections are likely to be portrayed as elitist obstacles to jobs, while vulnerable communities are asked to bear more risk. This makes Johnson’s framework all the more important. Environmentalism cannot succeed if it speaks only in the language of polar bears, atmospheric parts per million, or wilderness. It must speak in the language of race, labor, health, land, and repair.

To remake the world is to remake our relation to the earth and to one another. Slavery teaches that prosperity built on extraction is not innocent prosperity. It teaches that distance is a moral technology. It teaches that bodies remember political economy. It teaches that land, labor, race, and reproduction cannot be separated. An environmental justice movement adequate to the age of climate crisis must begin there: not with “humanity” as an undifferentiated species, but with the histories that made some humans masters of extraction and others its raw material.
