"While the framework of racial capitalism correctly identifies the historical entanglement of slavery and economic development, a viable labor politics must distinguish between the profits of capital and the 'wages of whiteness' to build cross-racial working-class solidarity."

Walter Johnson’s essay offers a necessary rebuke to those who would separate race from capitalism or slavery from modernity. It is impossible to understand American economic development without slavery, and impossible to understand American racism without political economy. The plantation was not a moral accident alongside the market. It was one of the market’s engines. The cotton kingdom, Northern finance, British industry, Indian removal, and federal power belonged to a single history.

Yet I want to raise a concern from the standpoint of labor politics. Johnson rightly emphasizes the “wages of whiteness” and the ways white workers have been incorporated into racial capitalism. But if this insight hardens into a view of white workers primarily as beneficiaries of racial domination, it may leave us with a morally satisfying analysis and a politically inadequate strategy. The challenge is to tell the truth about whiteness without surrendering the possibility of cross-racial working-class politics.

Du Bois’s concept of the wages of whiteness remains indispensable. White workers in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries often accepted symbolic and material advantages over black workers in exchange for loyalty to a racial order that also limited their own economic power. They claimed public respect, access to certain jobs, political standing, and the status of citizenship. Employers exploited these divisions. White workers could be poor, indebted, and harshly treated, yet still defend segregation or exclusion because whiteness offered a comparative advantage and a sense of superiority. This pattern has not vanished. The current political moment, with its fusion of economic grievance and racial resentment, makes Du Bois feel painfully contemporary.

Still, one must be precise. The wages of whiteness were real, but they were not the same as capital’s profits. A white tenant farmer did not benefit from slavery or segregation in the same way a planter, banker, railroad executive, or industrialist did. A white textile worker in Manchester or Massachusetts may have consumed cotton produced by enslaved labor and may have embraced imperial or racist assumptions, but he also sold his labor under conditions he did not control. A white coal miner could claim racial privilege and die from black lung. A white factory worker could support exclusionary unions and be crushed by the employer’s speedup. To say this is not to excuse racism. It is to distinguish between hierarchy among workers and domination by capital.

The distinction matters because racial capitalism survives partly by persuading workers that their conflicts with one another are more important than their common conflict with those who own and govern. Johnson knows this; indeed his essay says race and class are functionally intertwined. But the rhetoric of racial capitalism can sometimes be heard as saying that white workers are simply junior partners in domination. Some are. Many have acted that way. But a politics that cannot imagine them otherwise will leave the field open to demagogues who promise racial restoration in place of economic democracy.

This is not an abstract worry. In the 2016 election, Donald Trump appealed to white workers with a story of stolen greatness. The villains in that story were immigrants, Muslims, black protesters, Chinese workers, Mexican workers, bureaucrats, and cosmopolitan elites. The story was false in many of its details and dangerous in its implications. But it had power because many communities really have experienced deindustrialization, wage stagnation, opioid addiction, declining life expectancy, union collapse, and social abandonment. If the left responds by saying only that these communities are racist, it will be correct about part of the problem and useless about the rest.

A better response begins where Johnson begins: with history. White workers’ economic insecurity has often been managed through racial entitlement. After emancipation, the possibility of an interracial democracy of labor was violently defeated in the South. Populist and labor movements periodically crossed racial lines, but they were undermined by terror, disenfranchisement, employer tactics, and white workers’ own commitments to supremacy. New Deal labor protections excluded many black workers through compromises with Southern Democrats. Postwar public policy subsidized white homeownership and suburbanization while black communities faced redlining, segregation, and predatory extraction. Deindustrialization then hit black workers first and hardest, before spreading to many white communities that had once been shielded by racial advantage and union power.

This history suggests that white workers are neither innocent nor omnipotent. They have been agents of racism, beneficiaries of racism, victims of capitalism, and potential participants in a multiracial democracy. The proportions vary by time, place, and institution. A serious labor politics must speak to all of these truths.

Johnson’s critique of human rights is useful in this regard. Formal equality in the labor market is not enough. If workers are legally equal in their right to accept poverty wages, unsafe conditions, and precarious employment, then political emancipation remains thin. The right not to be discriminated against by an employer matters enormously, but so does the right to a union, the right to health care, the right to housing, the right to education, the right to a clean environment, and the right to democratic control over economic life. A society that gives everyone the equal right to be dominated by capital has not achieved justice.

But here I would put more emphasis than Johnson does on universal social-democratic institutions, provided they are designed with racial justice at their core. Reparations and targeted remedies are necessary because the injuries are specific. But universal programs can also undermine racial capitalism when they reduce the material insecurity that demagogues racialize. Universal health care, strong labor law, full employment, public jobs, childcare, housing guarantees, debt relief, and free higher education would disproportionately benefit black and brown communities because need is unequally distributed. Yet they could also create shared interests across racial lines.

The danger, of course, is that universal programs in the United States have often been administered in racially unequal ways. Social Security initially excluded agricultural and domestic workers. The GI Bill was filtered through segregated local institutions. Federal housing policy built white wealth while excluding black families. Universalism without anti-racist enforcement becomes white universalism. But targeted racial justice without broad economic transformation can become too narrow, leaving the underlying class structure intact. We need both: race-conscious universalism and reparative particularism.

Consider labor unions. Historically, unions have been both vehicles of solidarity and instruments of exclusion. Some defended white job monopolies; others organized across race and challenged segregation. The lesson is not that unions are inherently emancipatory or inherently racist. It is that institutions matter. A revitalized labor movement must be explicitly anti-racist, committed to organizing the lowest-paid sectors, including domestic, care, agricultural, logistics, retail, and service workers. It must defend immigrant workers. It must treat police unions differently from unions of workers subject to state violence. It must understand that black workers’ fight against discrimination is a labor fight, not a distraction from one.

Johnson’s warning about writing slavery without enslaved people applies to writing labor history without workers. Political economy can become bloodless if it describes systems without attending to lived experience. But labor politics can also become sentimental if it imagines “the working class” as a single subject waiting to be unified. The working class is divided by race, gender, citizenship, region, and sector. Some workers police, imprison, deport, or supervise other workers. Some workers’ wages are paid by the extraction of rent from poorer workers. Some workers identify upward with capital or sideways with whiteness. Solidarity is not automatic. It is built through struggle, institutions, and political education.

The history of slavery gives us both warning and hope. During the Civil War and Reconstruction, enslaved people initiated a general strike against slavery by fleeing plantations, joining Union lines, withholding labor, serving as soldiers, and demanding land and schools. Their action transformed a war for Union into a war for emancipation. Poor white Southerners did not join them in sufficient numbers, and the resulting democracy was overthrown. But the episode shows that labor struggle can be world-making when it confronts property, race, and state power together.

A contemporary equivalent would not look like nineteenth-century Reconstruction, but it would share some features: redistribution of wealth and power; protection of voting rights; demilitarization of policing; investment in communities long exploited; union rights; public employment; land and housing justice; and a politics that refuses to trade the safety of immigrants, Muslims, black people, or Native nations for promises of white jobs. It would tell white workers the truth: racism has offered you status in place of power, and the bargain has helped destroy the very solidarities that could improve your lives. It would tell black and brown workers another truth: your struggles are not “identity politics” but central to any meaningful economic democracy.

Johnson’s concept of racial capitalism helps because it prevents us from separating the economic from the racial. But we should be careful not to make the term so total that it obscures strategic possibilities. If white workers are nothing but bearers of whiteness, there is no labor politics worth having. If class is invoked to minimize racism, there is no justice worth having. The task is to build a politics in which white workers abandon whiteness not as an act of charity but as a condition of their own liberation, and in which the leadership of those most harmed by racial capitalism reshapes the meaning of class struggle.

Human rights language alone cannot do this. Nor can Marxist language alone. Nor can moral condemnation alone. We need a reconstruction of institutions: unions, parties, schools, churches, community organizations, cooperatives, public agencies, and movements capable of sustaining solidarity against the pressures that break it. The Movement for Black Lives has helped clarify the agenda by linking policing, prisons, education, housing, labor, gender, sexuality, and reparations. Labor movements should learn from that breadth rather than retreating to a narrow wage politics.

Johnson ends with Du Bois’s sense of historical opportunity. The present moment is dangerous, but danger is not destiny. Racial capitalism has always depended on dividing those it exploits and differentially rewarding some for participating in the subordination of others. A labor politics adequate to our time must break that bargain. It must say that the wages of whiteness are not worth the world they require. It must offer, in their place, the material and democratic wages of solidarity.
