"The essay supports Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism, arguing that the idealization of maternal devotion often enables entitlement and reinforces gender inequality rather than fostering social responsibility."

Hirschmann's essay arrives at a crucial moment in feminist thought, when the celebration of care has begun to feel less like liberation and more like a subtle recoding of older feminine obligations. Her central insight—that feminist theory can be co-opted to serve ends directly opposed to those for which it was intended—deserves serious attention from anyone who cares about gender equality.

The story of "Anthony" crystallizes the problem with devastating precision. Here was a young man about to enter the high-pressure world of investment banking, a profession where ethical standards matter enormously for society. Yet his mother's response to documented academic cheating was to protect him from consequences and attack the professor who had the audacity to enforce standards. This is not love; it is the worst kind of indulgence, teaching that one's connections and position can insulate one from accountability. That such behavior occurs in families where mothers have devoted themselves entirely to their children's success suggests that pure devotion, without any commitment to broader values, produces not good citizens but entitled ones.

What strikes me most about Hirschmann's analysis is her willingness to name a truth that many feminists have been reluctant to acknowledge: the individualist turn in care feminism has failed. Despite decades of argument that men would embrace care once they understood its importance, the data from the National Science Foundation study shows women still doing two to three times more childcare and housework. This is not because men are naturally indifferent to their children or families. It is because the existing structure of work, law, and social expectation gives men no incentive to change. When women argue for public recognition of care work while simultaneously performing that work themselves, they are simply subsidizing male privilege with their own labor.

Hirschmann's point about "tough love" is not a retreat from feminist principles but their fulfillment. True care for one's children includes preparing them to live in a world where they must earn respect through achievement, where they must learn to share responsibilities with partners, where they must understand that their choices affect other people. The mother who enables her son to cheat is not showing care; she is showing a kind of selfishness dressed up as devotion. The mother who insists that her husband share childcare, even when he resists, is doing something far more loving—both for her children and for her marriage.

I also appreciate Hirschmann's willingness to acknowledge that family-friendly policies have backfired in practice. Parental leave designed to help women often advantages men instead. Maternity leave helps women take time off but does nothing to challenge the assumption that care is women's work. These findings are uncomfortable because they suggest that feminist victories in the policy arena have been more apparent than real. Changing the law is not the same as changing culture, and perhaps the lesson is that feminist energy should be directed at the more stubborn problem of individual behavior within relationships.

The conclusion—that women need to care a little less—is provocative but, I think, correct. Not because caring less means abandoning children or treating them with indifference. Rather, it means refusing to let care work consume all of one's identity and energy, leaving no resources for the political fight that remains. It means recognizing that individual choices, however rational they seem in the context of one's own family, have collective consequences that can perpetuate or challenge gender hierarchy. And it means accepting that liberation, for women as for all people, requires risk—the risk of disappointing spouses, of disappointing children, of standing alone against cultural expectation.

Hirschmann offers not a blueprint but a challenge: to think honestly about what our theories of care actually produce in the world, and to be willing to revise them when they fail. That is the mark of serious intellectual work.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Maternal indulgence that protects children from accountability, as seen in the case of academic cheating, produces entitled individuals rather than the good citizens care theory envisions.
  2. 02The individualist turn in care feminism has failed to change the sexual division of labor because it provides men with no structural incentive to share the burdens of domestic work.
  3. 03Public policies like parental leave often backfire by subsidizing male privilege and entrenching the assumption that care is primarily a woman's responsibility.
  4. 04True care requires 'tough love' and a commitment to broader social values, including the refusal to let care work consume a woman's entire identity.
  5. 05Women must 'care a little less' to preserve the personal and political resources necessary to challenge the gender hierarchies that individual domestic choices often perpetuate.
