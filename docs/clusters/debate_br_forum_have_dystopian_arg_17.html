"Redirecting AI toward socially beneficial outcomes requires dismantling or strictly regulating the market concentration of dominant tech firms whose business models inherently favor harmful practices like data extraction and automation."

Daron Acemoglu asks us to redirect AI. I agree, but the redirection cannot be achieved without confronting the market structure of the digital economy. The central fact about AI in 2021 is not simply that researchers favor automation or that governments subsidize capital. It is that the most important AI capabilities are concentrated inside a handful of dominant firms whose business models depend on data extraction, platform control, and scale. If we do not break or discipline that concentration, appeals to social purpose will be overwhelmed.

The list is familiar: Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, and, globally, Alibaba, Tencent, and Baidu. These firms differ in products and culture, but they share structural advantages. They control vast datasets, cloud infrastructure, user networks, advertising markets, app ecosystems, e-commerce platforms, and talent pipelines. They can buy promising startups before they become threats. They can subsidize expansion in one market with profits from another. They can shape academic research through grants, fellowships, consulting, and hiring. They can influence regulation through lobbying and public relations. They do not merely participate in AI markets; they organize them.

This concentration helps explain why AI develops in socially troubling directions. Advertising platforms want prediction and influence. E-commerce platforms want logistics optimization, pricing power, and labor discipline. Cloud companies want enterprise automation. Social media platforms want engagement. These incentives do not naturally produce AI that empowers teachers, nurses, local governments, small firms, or workers. They produce AI that reinforces the platform’s central position.

Consider Facebook. Its AI systems are not built primarily to improve democratic discourse. They are built to rank content, target ads, detect some forms of abuse, and keep users engaged. Even when the company invests heavily in safety, the underlying model depends on attention and behavioral data. The same machinery that allows small businesses to find customers allows political operatives to microtarget voters and misinformation to spread rapidly. The problem is not a lack of ethical awareness among engineers. It is the business model.

Consider Amazon. AI improves warehousing, recommendation, cloud services, voice assistants, and delivery. Some of this is genuinely useful. But Amazon’s scale allows it to set the terms for sellers, workers, and even cities competing for investment. Algorithmic management in warehouses can raise productivity while increasing injury risk and stress. Marketplace data can be used to identify successful third-party products. Cloud dominance gives Amazon a central role in the infrastructure of countless firms. The issue is not whether a particular algorithm is fair; it is whether one company should have so much power over commerce, labor, and infrastructure.

Antitrust therefore belongs at the center of AI policy. Acemoglu mentions concentration, but his remedies emphasize funding, norms, and democratic oversight. Those are necessary. Yet a concentrated industry can absorb or deflect them. Large firms can comply with complex regulations more easily than startups, turning rules into barriers to entry. They can fund “AI for good” projects while preserving harmful core businesses. They can hire ethicists and publish principles while lobbying against enforceable limits. They can shape standards in ways that protect incumbents.

A serious response requires structural separation and competition policy. Platforms that operate marketplaces should not compete on those marketplaces using privileged data. Firms that control essential digital infrastructure should face nondiscrimination obligations. Dominant platforms should be restricted from acquiring nascent competitors. Interoperability and data portability should allow users and businesses to leave without losing their networks. App stores should not be able to impose arbitrary rents on dependent developers. Digital advertising markets should be investigated for conflicts of interest and opacity.

Breaking up companies is not a magic solution. Smaller firms can also misuse AI, exploit workers, or invade privacy. But concentration magnifies harm and reduces democratic leverage. When a few platforms mediate speech, commerce, and information, individual choice becomes weak. “Use another service” is not meaningful when all one’s friends, customers, suppliers, or documents are locked into the incumbent. Competition can create space for alternative business models: subscription services, cooperatives, public-interest platforms, privacy-preserving tools, and sector-specific AI built around user needs rather than advertising scale.

Data concentration is especially important. AI systems improve with data, and dominant platforms collect data because they are dominant. This feedback loop can entrench power. Some propose treating data as labor and paying users for it. That may have limited value, but it risks legitimizing extraction. A better approach is to limit collection, require interoperability, and create data trusts or public data sets governed for specific purposes. Health, transportation, climate, and education data can support innovation, but they should not simply become proprietary assets of whoever collected them first.

Cloud computing is another underappreciated bottleneck. Modern AI requires enormous computational resources. If a small number of cloud providers control access to computing infrastructure, they shape who can innovate. Public investment in academic and nonprofit computing resources would reduce dependence on corporate platforms. Universities should not have to partner with tech giants simply to train advanced models. Public-interest AI requires public infrastructure.

Acemoglu’s proposal to shift research norms is valuable but must be insulated from corporate capture. Many AI researchers move between universities and companies because that is where the data, compute, salaries, and prestige are. Government funding can counterbalance this, but only if it is large, sustained, and pluralistic. Public grants should support projects that dominant firms would not pursue: labor-enhancing tools for small businesses, privacy-preserving systems, public health applications, interpretable models for government benefits, assistive technologies, and AI for underrepresented languages. Funding should also support social scientists, legal scholars, historians, and community organizations—not only computer scientists.

The labor question also intersects with competition. Monopsony power allows firms to suppress wages and impose bad conditions. In local labor markets dominated by a few employers, workers have limited exit options. Platform companies can use algorithmic systems to manage large workforces while avoiding traditional employment obligations. Antitrust law has too often focused narrowly on consumer prices, missing harms to workers, suppliers, privacy, and innovation. A modern competition policy should consider whether AI-enabled business models reduce labor bargaining power.

The democracy question likewise runs through concentration. Facebook and Google dominate digital advertising. YouTube shapes video politics. Twitter, though smaller, has outsized influence among journalists and politicians. Amazon Web Services hosts critical infrastructure. Apple and Google control mobile app distribution. When private firms become gatekeepers of public discourse, democratic accountability becomes indirect and improvised. Conservatives accuse platforms of censorship; liberals accuse them of enabling extremism; civil libertarians worry about both state pressure and corporate power. The common problem is that too few institutions have too much control.

Regulating content decisions is hard and dangerous. But reducing platform dominance would help. A more interoperable social media ecosystem could allow communities to choose different moderation rules without losing access to broader networks. Users could select ranking algorithms. Researchers could study harms without depending on company permission. Political advertising could be made more transparent. The goal should not be a government ministry of truth; it should be a less centralized information environment.

International competition complicates the picture. Some argue that breaking up American tech giants would weaken the United States against China. This argument should be treated skeptically. Monopolies often wrap themselves in the flag. But democratic resilience is itself a strategic asset. A society whose information systems are dominated by unaccountable firms is vulnerable. Moreover, competition has historically been a source of American innovation. The breakup of AT&T did not end telecommunications innovation; it helped open space for new technologies. Strong antitrust can coexist with public investment in strategic capabilities.

Acemoglu is right that the market will not automatically choose the best AI path. But “the market” is not a neutral collection of firms. It is structured by law. For forty years, American law allowed digital platforms to consolidate power with little interference. That choice shaped AI. A different legal regime can shape it differently.

AI’s future will not be decided only in research labs. It will be decided in antitrust agencies, procurement offices, labor boards, courts, standards bodies, and legislatures. If we want AI that serves human beings rather than dominant platforms, we must reduce the power of the platforms. Otherwise, we will keep asking the same companies that profit from surveillance, automation, and dependency to lead us out of the problems they helped create.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current trajectory of AI is dictated by the business models of a handful of dominant firms that prioritize advertising engagement, labor discipline, and platform control over human empowerment.
  2. 02Antitrust and competition policy must be the central tools for AI reform because concentrated industries can easily absorb or bypass changes to research norms and funding.
  3. 03Structural separation is necessary to prevent platforms from using their dominance in infrastructure and data to stifle competitors and exploit dependent users or workers.
  4. 04Public investment in independent cloud computing and data infrastructure is essential to reduce the academic and commercial reliance on corporate giants for AI development.
  5. 05The feedback loop of data concentration must be broken through interoperability requirements and the creation of public data trusts to prevent entrenched power.
  6. 06A less centralized digital ecosystem is the most effective way to protect democratic discourse from the opaque and unaccountable content moderation of a few gatekeepers.
  7. 07National security arguments against breaking up tech giants are often self-serving justifications for monopolies that ignore the fact that competition and democratic resilience are strategic assets.
