"The success of a multiracial, left-wing populism depends less on perfecting a rhetorical message and more on building durable, worker-led organizations that can translate movement energy into institutional power."

The debate over populism often floats at a high altitude, circling around “the people,” “the elite,” sovereignty, charisma, pluralism, and other large abstractions. Adom Getachew usefully brings it down to earth by beginning not with a theory but with organizations: the Chicago Teachers Union, SEIU Local 73, United Working Families, the Working Families Party, city council campaigns, strikes, and local leaders who emerged from concrete struggles. That is where any serious discussion of left populism should begin. The question is not whether the word can be purified. The question is whether there are institutions capable of making a multiracial working-class majority act like a political force.

At the moment, the answer is: not yet, but there are signs of what such institutions might look like.

The Sanders campaign revealed both the possibility and the insufficiency of message-driven left politics. In two presidential cycles Sanders helped transform the policy agenda of the Democratic Party. He showed that millions of Americans, especially younger people, were ready for a direct attack on billionaire power and a universalist program of health care, education, jobs, and climate action. He proved that a campaign could raise enormous sums from small donors and compete without relying on the traditional donor class. He brought democratic socialism out of the margins and made it part of ordinary political argument.

But his campaign also revealed that enthusiasm is not organization, and that polling support for progressive policies is not the same as a governing coalition. In many states, turnout among young voters did not rise enough to offset weaknesses elsewhere. Union support was divided. Older voters, especially older black voters, often preferred Joe Biden. Many working-class voters were not reached by the campaign or did not trust it. The Democratic Party’s elected officials and allied groups consolidated against Sanders when he appeared threatening, and the left lacked the institutional strength to withstand that consolidation.

This should not lead to despair. It should lead to a more practical understanding of what building power requires. A “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” will not be conjured by a presidential candidate, however skilled or principled. It has to be built through durable organizations rooted in workplaces, neighborhoods, schools, churches, tenant associations, immigrant communities, and local political fights. It needs candidates, but also canvassers, shop stewards, ward organizations, strike committees, dues-paying members, political education, legal capacity, data infrastructure, candidate recruitment, and relationships that exist between election cycles.

The Chicago story matters because it shows how this can work. The CTU’s 2012 strike changed more than a contract. It changed the common sense of the city. It challenged the idea that school reform meant closing schools, expanding charters, and blaming teachers. It connected classroom conditions to neighborhood inequality. It made austerity visible as a political choice. The 2019 strike deepened that model by joining teachers with support staff and bargaining over the broader conditions of public education. The union did not simply say “the people versus the elite.” It built the capacity to make that claim credible.

Local electoral victories followed because there was an organized base to support them. Candidates associated with labor and socialist politics did not emerge from nowhere. They were connected to campaigns against school closures, fights for affordable housing, struggles over policing, and ward-level organizing. Jeanette Taylor’s history in the hunger strike to save Dyett High School is important because it shows the difference between a candidate who adopts movement language and a candidate produced by a movement. The first may be useful. The second is transformative.

The Working Families Party has long understood this terrain. Its greatest promise lies in acting as connective tissue between unions, community organizations, and progressive candidates. In states where fusion voting is possible, it can use ballot lines to discipline major-party candidates and express independent power. Where fusion is not available, it faces a harder task: operating inside and outside the Democratic Party at the same time. That ambiguity can be frustrating, but it reflects the actual structure of American politics. Third-party purity usually produces marginality; simple absorption into the Democratic Party produces demobilization. The challenge is to maintain an independent base while contesting Democratic primaries and general elections strategically.

The pandemic raises the stakes of this organizational question. We are living through a sudden and massive economic shutdown, with unemployment claims reaching levels that would have seemed almost unimaginable weeks ago. Workers are being divided into those who can work from home, those who have lost income, and those who must risk infection to keep society running. The last group is now called essential, but the label often masks powerlessness. Many essential workers lack paid sick leave, health insurance, protective equipment, hazard pay, or a meaningful voice at work. Many are women, immigrants, black, Latino, and low-wage.

If left populism is to mean anything in this moment, it should mean organizing essential workers into power. Applause, yard signs, and corporate advertisements thanking heroes are not enough. Heroism can become a substitute for rights. A nurse without protective equipment does not need flattery; she needs masks, staffing, paid leave, and bargaining power. A grocery clerk does not need to be told he is brave; he needs hazard pay and the ability to refuse unsafe work without being fired. A delivery driver does not need an app notification calling him essential; he needs employee protections, health care, and control over his conditions.

This is where labor law and workplace organization become central. The decline of unions has left workers exposed not only economically but politically. Unions are not perfect. They can be bureaucratic, conservative, exclusionary, and self-protective. But there is no substitute for organized worker power. Without it, the working class appears in politics mainly as an object of appeal: voters to be persuaded, consumers to be relieved, victims to be assisted. With it, workers can set demands, enforce safety, and shape public policy.

The immediate demands are clear: an enforceable emergency workplace safety standard; guaranteed paid sick leave; universal health coverage at least for the duration of the crisis, and preferably beyond; hazard pay for frontline workers; protection for whistleblowers and strikers; income support for the unemployed; relief for renters and homeowners; inclusion of undocumented workers in assistance; and aid to state and local governments to prevent austerity. But demands without organization remain wishes. The left’s task is to turn emergency grievances into lasting institutions.

That requires paying attention to where organizing is already happening. Workers at warehouses, grocery stores, hospitals, transit systems, fast-food restaurants, and delivery companies are protesting unsafe conditions. Nurses are speaking publicly about shortages. Tenants are discussing rent strikes. Mutual aid groups are delivering food and medicine. Prison abolitionists are demanding releases to prevent outbreaks. Immigrant rights groups are opposing detention and deportation. These are not all the same kind of organization, and they will not automatically converge. Political work means connecting them without flattening their differences.

A practical left populism should therefore be less obsessed with the perfect message and more concerned with the pipeline from crisis action to durable membership. When a worker signs a petition for protective equipment, who follows up? When tenants join a group chat about rent, how do they become part of a tenant union? When volunteers deliver groceries through mutual aid, how do they learn about municipal budgets, food systems, and public health? When people donate to a bail fund, how are they brought into campaigns against pretrial detention? Moments of crisis create openings, but openings close unless organizations capture the energy.

The electoral calendar complicates everything. Donald Trump must be defeated. His administration has combined incompetence, cruelty, and xenophobia in ways that have worsened the crisis. The attacks on immigrants, the scapegoating of China in terms that fuel anti-Asian racism, the resistance to oversight, the chaotic communication, and the preference for corporate relief over worker power all make a second term dangerous. Yet the presumptive Democratic nominee, Joe Biden, does not represent the left’s aspirations. His instinct is restoration, not transformation. His foreign policy rhetoric toward China has already shown how easily Democrats can echo nationalist themes.

The left should not respond by retreating into purity. Nor should it dissolve itself into Biden’s campaign. It should pursue a dual strategy: defeat Trump while building independent power to pressure a Biden administration from day one if he wins. That means extracting commitments where possible, but more importantly electing down-ballot progressives, strengthening unions, organizing issue campaigns, and preparing for fights over recovery legislation. The next administration, whoever leads it, will face decisions about bailouts, austerity, health care, climate investment, labor rights, and state budgets. Organized forces will shape those decisions; disorganized opinion will not.

The danger after the immediate health crisis is a new austerity. State and local governments are already facing collapsing revenues and rising needs. Without massive federal aid, public workers will be laid off, schools and transit systems will be cut, health services will suffer, and cities will be told to tighten belts after corporations have been rescued. We have seen this story before after the financial crisis: emergency spending followed by deficit panic, with public workers and poor communities made to pay. A left populist response must be ready to say: no austerity, no layoffs, no privatization, no sacrifice by those who have already sacrificed.

Here the old question “how will you pay for it?” has indeed been weakened. Congress has shown that when elites perceive an emergency, trillions can be mobilized. But it would be a mistake to think this automatically helps the left. Money creation and public spending can stabilize the powerful as well as liberate the vulnerable. The issue is not only the size of government action but its direction and control. Are we rescuing payrolls or shareholders? Hospitals or private equity? Tenants or landlords? State budgets or tax avoiders? Workers or executives? A populism with institutional grounding can make these distributive conflicts visible.

Still, we should be careful with the language of “the elite.” Some of the people we most need in this crisis are experts: epidemiologists, nurses, public health administrators, logistics specialists, local officials, and scientists. The enemy is not expertise. It is unaccountable power and profit-driven decision-making. A worker-centered politics should respect knowledge while democratizing its use. Public health measures must be explained, debated, and implemented fairly. Stay-at-home orders are easier for professionals than for low-wage workers; social distancing is different in a large house than in a crowded apartment or prison cell. Expertise without equity breeds distrust. Populism without expertise breeds disaster.

A similar precision is needed on race. A multiracial working-class alignment cannot be built by avoiding racial conflict for fear of alienating white workers. Nor can it be built by treating white workers as uniquely central to victory. The pandemic’s racial disparities must be at the center of the left’s account. Black death rates in cities like Chicago and Milwaukee reveal the accumulated violence of segregation, environmental racism, unequal health care, and labor market stratification. Latino and immigrant workers face exposure, exclusion from relief, and immigration enforcement. Asian Americans face racist scapegoating. Indigenous communities face chronic underinvestment in health infrastructure. These are not side issues. They are working-class issues.

At the same time, the politics must be relational rather than merely accusatory. It must show how racism has been used to divide workers and justify underinvestment in public goods, while also insisting on targeted repair. White workers do not benefit from living in a country without universal health care or paid leave, but many have been taught to fear programs associated with racial others. Organizing means breaking that story through shared struggle and honest confrontation. The CTU model offers one path: fight for universal public goods in ways that foreground the communities most harmed by their denial.

The international dimension is also practical, not decorative. Supply chains, migration, disease, and climate do not stop at borders. If the United States hoards medical equipment, maintains sanctions that restrict medical access, or deports people during a pandemic, it worsens global suffering and ultimately undermines public health. Left politics must reject the nationalist frame that pits American workers against Chinese workers, migrants, or the global poor. The antagonists are not foreign peoples; they are governments and corporations that prioritize power, secrecy, profit, and domination over human life. That includes our own.

Can such a politics be called populism? Perhaps. It appeals to the many against the few, to workers against concentrated wealth, to public goods against private greed. But its success will depend less on the label than on the organizational form. Populism without organization becomes mood. Organization without a broad moral narrative becomes interest-group bargaining. The left needs both: a story about a society run by and for those who sustain it, and institutions capable of making that story real.

The pandemic has given us a cruel lesson in dependence. We depend on one another, and we depend most on people who have been least respected. A politics built from that lesson would not merely thank essential workers; it would make them powerful. It would not merely mourn racial disparities; it would dismantle the conditions that produce them. It would not merely criticize Trump; it would build the capacity to govern differently. It would not merely wait for the next Bernie Sanders; it would produce thousands of organizers and candidates from the struggles already underway.

Reclaiming populism, if the phrase is to be useful, means reclaiming the institutions of popular power. That work is slower than a presidential campaign and less glamorous than a viral slogan. But it is the only path from righteous anger to durable change.
