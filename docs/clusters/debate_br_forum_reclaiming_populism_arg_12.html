"To be effective, a reclaimed left populism must move beyond domestic coalition-building and grapple with the structural international constraints and tensions inherent in the global nation-state system."

Adom Getachew's essay performs a valuable service by insisting on the international dimensions of the questions we in the United States are too inclined to treat as domestic. The fall of Evo Morales's government in Bolivia and the defeat of Jeremy Corbyn's Labour Party in the United Kingdom are not mere background context for American concerns. They are data points in a broader analysis of the possibilities and limits of left populism, and we ignore them at our peril. Getachew is right to call for thinking "comparatively and internationally," and in this response I want to pursue that suggestion in a direction that the forum's essays do not fully explore: the relationship between left populism and the international order that constrains it.

The context matters. For all the talk of globalization and the erosion of national sovereignty, the international system remains stubbornly organized around the nation-state as the primary unit of political authority. International law, international institutions, and international norms all presuppose a world of more or less sovereign states that manage their own affairs and relate to each other through diplomatic and economic channels. This architecture has never been neutral—it has always advantaged powerful states and the interests associated with them—but it has created certain possibilities for progressive politics that are now under severe pressure.

The COVID-19 pandemic has made this pressure visible. As Getachew notes, the competition for scarce medical resources has placed poor and middle-income countries at a profound disadvantage. The Trump administration's refusal to lift sanctions that impede countries like Iran, Venezuela, and Cuba from accessing medical supplies is a grotesque manifestation of this dynamic. But the problem is not only—or even primarily—the malevolence of any particular administration. It is the structural logic of a system in which each state is responsible primarily for its own citizens, in which international cooperation is voluntary and easily disrupted, and in which the enforcement of existing obligations (including humanitarian obligations) depends on political will that cannot be assumed.

What does this mean for left populism? The populism that Getachew and her collaborators seek to reclaim is, by definition, oriented toward "the people" as a political subject. But "the people" is an internally contentious category in the international context. Whose people? The citizens of a particular nation-state? The global working class? The subaltern majority of the world's population? These are not rhetorical questions. They have concrete policy implications that the essays in this forum do not adequately address.

Consider the Working Families Party's effort to build "a left-led, multiracial working class alignment." This is a genuinely ambitious and important project. But its multiracial character is defined, almost inevitably, in relation to the American racial order. It does not automatically extend to solidarity with workers in other countries who may be competing with American workers for jobs or whose labor standards may be undercut by international trade regimes. The tension between domestic coalition-building and international solidarity is not unique to the American context, but it is acute in the United States, where the legacy of nationalism and nativism creates constant pressure to define "the people" in exclusionary terms.

The Morales case is instructive here. MAS's government in Bolivia achieved remarkable things: poverty reduction, indigenous recognition, economic growth. But it was also dependent on commodities exports that connected it to global markets in ways that ultimately destabilized its social coalition. The protests that preceded Morales's ouster were not simply a white supremacist reaction to indigenous empowerment, as some commentators have suggested; they reflected genuine disagreements about governance, about the relationship between state and society, and about how to navigate a global economic system that constrains even the most progressive governments. The lesson is not that left populism cannot succeed internationally but that it must grapple seriously with the international dimensions of its project.

What would a left populism capable of engaging international dimensions actually look like? I want to suggest three elements that seem to me essential. First, a serious engagement with the question of international economic justice—not the vague solidarity rhetoric that usually passes for internationalism but a concrete analysis of how the international economic order can be reformed to serve human needs rather than corporate profits. Second, a commitment to migrant rights and the free movement of people that goes beyond rhetoric about "sanctuary cities" to demand systemic change in immigration regimes that serve primarily to discipline labor and maintain racialized hierarchies. Third, a rethinking of sovereignty that recognizes the ways that national boundaries have always served to exclude and exploit as well as to protect and empower.

None of this is easy, and I do not pretend that these suggestions constitute a complete program. But the alternative is a left populism that remains trapped within the boundaries of the nation-state, that builds coalitions at home while ignoring or underestimating the global structures that constrain them, and that is ultimately incapable of addressing the transnational challenges—pandemics, climate change, financial crises—that will define the coming decades. Getachew and her collaborators are right that the long-term political implications of the COVID-19 crisis remain to be seen. But one thing seems clear: the left that emerges from this crisis will be measured not only by what it achieves domestically but by its capacity to think and act internationally.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current international order is structurally biased toward powerful states, creating a logic where national self-interest routinely undermines global humanitarian cooperation and resource distribution.
  2. 02There is an inherent tension between building a domestic multiracial working-class alignment and maintaining international solidarity with workers abroad who are often positioned as competitors.
  3. 03The failure of left populist movements like MAS in Bolivia demonstrates that even successful domestic programs are vulnerable to destabilization by global market dependencies.
  4. 04A viable internationalist populism requires concrete policies for global economic justice, systemic reform of immigration regimes, and a rethinking of national sovereignty.
  5. 05A left populism confined to national boundaries will remain incapable of addressing inherently transnational crises such as pandemics, climate change, and financial instability.
