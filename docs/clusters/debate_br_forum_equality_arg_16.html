"While the focus on outcomes is necessary to move beyond the flaws of 'equal opportunity,' the goal should be 'relational equality'—securing the material and social conditions for all to stand as equals—rather than the potentially paternalistic and impossible goal of 'equal flourishing.'"

Christine Sypnowich is right that “equal opportunity” often functions as a moral solvent: pour it over entrenched hierarchy and the hard edges of inequality seem to disappear. If the poor had a fair shot, if the labor market were color-blind, if schools were adequately open, then the fact that some live in precarity while others accumulate dynastic wealth can be redescribed as the residue of choice. This is politically convenient and philosophically misleading. In societies where family wealth buys neighborhood, school, safety, confidence, social networks, internships, test preparation, legal help, and time, opportunity cannot be cleanly separated from outcome. The child’s “opportunity” is made out of the parents’ outcomes.

But I hesitate before Sypnowich’s proposed replacement: equality of flourishing. The hesitation is not because outcomes do not matter. They matter enormously. It is because “equal flourishing” is too ambitious in one sense and not political enough in another. It risks asking public institutions to equalize what they cannot and should not fully control, while leaving somewhat underdescribed the more concrete egalitarian aim: to build social relations and institutions in which no one is dominated, abandoned, humiliated, or forced to sell their life chances cheaply.

The strongest part of Sypnowich’s argument is her attack on the choice/circumstance distinction. Contemporary inequality is not a track meet in which runners arrive with different shoes but the same map. It is a system that produces capacities, expectations, and desires. A young person who “chooses” not to apply to university because no one in her family has done so, because debt seems terrifying, because her school has few counselors, and because paid work is immediately necessary has not simply revealed a preference. She has adapted to a horizon. Likewise, “bad choices” about health, work, money, or relationships are often made under stress, scarcity, trauma, and social isolation. Behavioral economics has confirmed what socialists and social workers have long known: scarcity taxes attention and narrows planning. To treat the resulting inequalities as morally deserved is cruel.

Sypnowich is also right that neutrality is often fictitious. The liberal state already promotes forms of life: homeownership through tax policy, employment through welfare rules, marriage through law, consumption through infrastructure, fossil fuel use through subsidy and design, and education through compulsion. Markets are not neutral forums in which autonomous selves disclose their values. They are engines for shaping wants, attaching status to commodities, and rewarding activities whose social value may be dubious. A society that leaves children’s cultural formation to advertising, streaming platforms, unequal families, and local tax bases is not respecting freedom; it is privatizing influence.

Still, the language of flourishing should make egalitarians cautious. Flourishing is plural, partly subjective, and partly tragic. Some people flourish in solitude; others in dense association. Some find meaning in ambition, others in care, devotion, craft, worship, play, or contemplation. Disability rights movements have rightly challenged outside judgments about which lives are “less flourishing.” Feminists have shown how ideals of the good life can smuggle in gendered expectations about family, dependency, and service. Religious and cultural minorities have reason to fear public authorities that confidently rank ways of living. Even when coercion is disavowed, the state’s power to “incentivize” and “educate” can become a power to normalize.

The problem is not that public judgment can be avoided. It cannot. The problem is how to discipline it. Appeals to flourishing need democratic, rights-protecting, and anti-domination constraints. We should be especially wary when elites define the valuable pursuits of others. Music education is a benign example, and a persuasive one: compulsory schooling should introduce children to arts, sciences, physical activity, civic life, and practical skills they might not otherwise encounter. But the same rationale could be used less benignly—to stigmatize working-class leisure, pressure women into approved forms of “empowerment,” or treat some communities as culturally deficient. A politics of flourishing must therefore be participatory, not merely perfectionist. People must have power in defining the conditions of their own flourishing.

For that reason, I would put relational equality at the center. The question is not whether everyone achieves the same level of well-being, an impossible and perhaps undesirable goal. It is whether our institutions secure for all the material and social bases of standing as equals. That includes outcomes: income, wealth, housing, health, education, leisure, safety, political voice, and access to care. But the point of equalizing them is not to produce identical lives. It is to prevent caste, dependency, and domination.

This emphasis yields a demanding political program. It supports universal health care, including mental health care; strong unions and sectoral bargaining; a generous child allowance; paid leave; public childcare and eldercare; social housing; desegregated and well-funded schools; free or low-cost higher and vocational education; disability supports; public transit; progressive taxation of income, inheritance, and wealth; and democratic constraints on corporate power. It also supports public goods that are not merely redistributive in the narrow sense: libraries, parks, arts institutions, community centers, safe streets, and time away from paid labor. These policies care about outcomes, not just access. Their success is measured by whether people are actually housed, healthy, educated, secure, and able to participate.

Such a program also avoids one weakness of opportunity rhetoric: its obsession with mobility. A society should not ask whether a few children born poor can become rich; it should ask why being born poor is so damaging in the first place. The point is not to diversify the elite while leaving the hierarchy intact. It is to compress the hierarchy and make the lower rungs decent, powerful, and secure. Equal opportunity can coexist with a brutal competition for scarce goods. Egalitarianism should reduce the brutality and the scarcity.

Sypnowich’s socialist conclusion is therefore compelling if socialism means democratic control over the economic conditions that shape our lives, decommodification of basic needs, and solidaristic provision beyond market desert. But socialism should not promise equal flourishing in too total a sense. It should promise something at once more modest and more radical: that no one’s life prospects will depend on parental wealth, market vulnerability, racial status, gendered subordination, disability, or sheer abandonment; that all will have the resources and standing to help shape the common world; and that inequalities will be judged by what they do to people’s real lives, not by whether they can be laundered through the language of choice.

Equal opportunity is not enough. But the alternative is not a society that guarantees that all flourish equally. It is a society that takes responsibility for the conditions under which flourishing becomes broadly and genuinely possible—and that refuses to excuse misery because, somewhere along the way, a formal gate was open.

  2. 02The state is never truly neutral, as it already promotes specific ways of life through policy, and leaving cultural formation to the market is a form of privatized influence rather than true freedom.
  3. 03A focus on 'flourishing' risks being overly ambitious and potentially oppressive if it allows elites to define and incentivize what constitutes a valuable life for others.
  4. 04Egalitarianism should prioritize 'relational equality' to prevent caste, dependency, and domination, ensuring everyone has the material basis to participate in society without being forced to sell their life chances cheaply.
  5. 05The political focus must shift from social mobility—which merely diversifies the elite—to compressing social hierarchies and making all positions in society decent, powerful, and secure.
