"The federal government should dramatically reduce the number of official holidays to maintain religious neutrality and minimize economic costs."

The question of which holidays the federal government should recognize starts from a faulty premise: that the government needs to be in the business of sanctifying anyone’s cultural or religious calendar at all. The answer is not to add more holidays until every group gets a turn; it is to dramatically pare back the official list so that the state stops picking favorites and stops mandating when the nation’s workers must collectively take a break.

Federal holidays impose real costs. Every mandated closure of government offices ripples through the economy in lost productivity, delayed services and extra administrative burdens for businesses that coordinate with public agencies. The Office of Personnel Management itself has noted that a single federal holiday can cost hundreds of millions of dollars in direct payroll outlays alone, not counting the broader economic drag. Adding more holidays, however well‑intentioned, would only compound that expense at a time when fiscal restraint is desperately needed.

But the economic argument is only the surface layer. The deeper problem is the immense symbolic power that comes with a state‑sanctioned holiday. When the government declares the last Monday in May a holiday for Memorial Day, it makes a statement about the virtue of military sacrifice that commands broad, though not universal, assent. When it closes its doors for Christmas, it implicitly endorses a religious tradition that tens of millions of Americans do not share—and that many actively reject. No amount of talk about “secularized” Christmas trees and Santa Claus erases the fact that the holiday remains, at its core, a celebration of the birth of Jesus Christ, and that government recognition of it constitutes a preference for Christianity over other faiths and over nonbelief. If we are serious about religious neutrality, the government should exit the holiday‑endorsement business almost entirely.

A lean holiday calendar would retain only the most essential civic commemorations: Independence Day, to mark the founding of the republic; perhaps New Year’s Day, as a purely secular marker of the calendar change; and possibly a day of national thanksgiving that is stripped of its specific religious trappings and reframed as a harvest festival or a day of general gratitude. Everything else—Christmas, Easter, Passover, Mawlid, Diwali, the lunar new year—belongs in the private sphere, where individuals and private employers can observe whatever they wish according to their own consciences. Shifting to a minimalist federal calendar would be the truest expression of religious freedom and the most honest acknowledgment that in a diverse society, the government has no business deciding which holy days or cultural festivals get the official stamp of approval. We don’t need more holidays; we need fewer, and better reasons for the ones we keep.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Federal holidays impose significant economic costs through lost productivity, delayed services, and massive payroll outlays.

  3. 03The government should only recognize essential civic commemorations, leaving religious and cultural celebrations to the private sphere.
  4. 04A minimalist federal calendar better respects the diversity of American society by not forcing the state to choose which cultural festivals deserve official approval.
