"While Acemoglu’s diagnosis of AI’s trajectory is compelling, his proposed solutions are incomplete because they fail to account for how intense geopolitical competition and the global diffusion of technology limit a single nation's ability to redirect AI development."

Acemoglu concludes by noting that international coordination is necessary for his proposed AI redirection. This observation deserves much more attention than the essay gives it.

The geopolitical context shapes the feasible policy space. The U.S. and China are engaged in strategic competition across multiple domains, and AI is central to this contest. Chinese investment in AI research exceeds $70 billion annually; Chinese firms dominate computer vision and have major positions in natural language processing. Military applications of AI have become a national security priority for both powers.

In this environment, can democratic nations coordinate to redirect AI? The OECD AI Principles represent a step, but they're voluntary and don't bind China. Export controls on AI chips have been imposed, but these constrain legitimate research along with military applications. Any serious effort to redirect AI globally would require binding agreements, which seem politically impossible given current tensions.

This suggests Acemoglu's framework is incomplete. His diagnosis of AI's trajectory as a domestic policy failure (driven by weak institutions, captured regulators, and misaligned incentives) is compelling. But his proposed solutions assume a degree of national autonomy that may not exist in a world of strategic competition.

Consider the surveillance technology concern. Acemoglu rightly criticizes facial recognition and social media manipulation. But these technologies are actively developed by Chinese firms and deployed by authoritarian governments seeking to strengthen their grip on power. Democratic nations can't redirect global AI while these applications continue unabated.

The essay's climate analogy breaks down here too. Carbon emissions cross borders, but the energy transition can proceed nationally. Germany can decarbonize its electricity grid regardless of Chinese coal policy. AI development is fundamentally different because knowledge diffuses across borders, researchers collaborate internationally, and dual-use applications are the norm.

What does this mean for policy? It suggests that national redirection alone is insufficient. Any credible strategy must address the international dimension through export controls on surveillance technologies, investment screening for AI-related acquisitions, and perhaps international agreements limiting certain applications. The current trend toward decoupling complicates cooperation but may be necessary to prevent the most dangerous applications from proliferating.

Acemoglu's suggestion that international coordination requires "at least a modicum" of cooperation understates the challenge. Effective coordination requires binding commitments, verification mechanisms, and enforcement capacity. None of these currently exist for AI governance.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The strategic and military competition between the U.S. and China makes voluntary or domestic-only AI redirection politically and practically unfeasible.
  2. 02The climate change analogy is flawed because, unlike national energy transitions, AI knowledge and dual-use applications diffuse across borders, making national autonomy in this sector impossible.
  3. 03Effective redirection of AI requires binding international agreements, verification mechanisms, and enforcement capacity, all of which are currently non-existent in the global governance landscape.
  4. 04A credible strategy must move beyond domestic norms and include aggressive international policy tools such as export controls and investment screening to counter the proliferation of dangerous AI applications by authoritarian regimes.
