The European Lesson: Wealth Taxes Failed There, and They Will Fail Here

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman address the European experience with wealth taxes only to dismiss it. They claim that concerns about wealth taxation failing in other countries are exaggerated and that the United States can succeed where others have struggled. This is wishful thinking. France, Germany, Sweden, and other European nations have tried wealth taxes and largely abandoned it. Their experience offers crucial lessons we should heed before repeating their mistakes.

France provides the most instructive case. The French wealth tax, or Impôt de Solidarité sur la Fortune, existed from 1988 to 2017. For nearly three decades, France attempted to tax the accumulated assets of its wealthiest citizens. The results were disastrous. Thousands of wealthy French residents left the country, taking their tax revenue with them. A 2016 report by the French Senate estimated that the wealth tax had driven away over 12,000 millionaires between 2000 and 2010, costing the government approximately 50 billion euros in lost revenue. When France finally abolished the tax, it was replaced by a higher property tax that hit the middle class more than the wealthy.

Germany repealed its wealth tax in 1997 after similar problems. Sweden's wealth tax contributed to an exodus of capital and entrepreneurs before its abolition in 2007. In each case, the pattern was the same: the wealth tax raised less revenue than projected, drove away mobile capital and the wealthy who held it, and imposed disproportionate compliance burdens on those who remained.

What went wrong? Several things. First, the wealthy proved far more mobile than policymakers anticipated. Unlike income, which must be earned where work is performed, wealth can be relocated. Bank accounts can be moved, companies can be restructured, residences can be changed. The European experience demonstrates that the superrich can and do flee higher taxes.

Second, compliance costs proved crushing. Administering a wealth tax requires annual valuations of diverse assets—private companies, art collections, real estate holdings, financial instruments. Each category presents unique valuation challenges, and each creates opportunities for disputes and litigation. The IRS is already underfunded and overstretched; asking it to conduct thousands of complex annual valuations is unrealistic.

Third, exemptions and carve-outs proliferated. European wealth taxes were riddled with exceptions for business assets, agricultural property, and other favored categories. The result was a tax that hit diversified investors but spared those whose wealth was tied up in farms, family businesses, and other politically connected interests. The authors propose a clean tax with few exemptions, but their proposal ignores the political economy of tax-making.

The United States has significant advantages over European nations in administering taxes: a large domestic market, the dollar as the world's reserve currency, and the citizen-based tax system that taxes Americans on worldwide income regardless of residence. But these advantages are offset by the extraordinary mobility of the ultra-wealthy and the sophistication of their advisors. When Warren Buffett can structure his affairs to pay 0.058 percent of his income in taxes, we should not assume that a wealth tax will be easily collected.

I agree with Saez and Zucman that the current system is inequitable. But the solution is not to import a failed European policy. Instead, we should focus on comprehensive income tax reform: eliminating the preferential rates on capital gains and dividends that allow the wealthy to shelter income, strengthening the estate tax with better enforcement and lower exemptions, and actually funding the IRS to audit high-net-worth taxpayers. These approaches work within our existing framework and do not require the administrative miracles that a wealth tax would demand.
