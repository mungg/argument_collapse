"While the concentration of extreme wealth is a serious threat to democracy, a federal wealth tax is a flawed solution that should be abandoned in favor of reforming existing income and estate tax structures."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman rightly identify the extreme concentration of wealth in the United States as a profound threat to our social fabric and our democratic institutions. The staggering statistics they present—where the richest 400 Americans pay a lower effective tax rate than the working class—should be a source of national shame. They are entirely correct that our current tax system has been engineered, over decades of lobbying and legislative erosion, to protect dynastic wealth and allow the superrich to accumulate vast fortunes virtually tax-free. However, their proposed solution—a sweeping federal wealth tax—is a perilous distraction. It is a policy that is constitutionally suspect, administratively unworkable, and ultimately unnecessary if we are willing to fix the perfectly adequate tax tools we already possess.

The fundamental problem with the U.S. tax system is not that it lacks a wealth tax; the problem is that it has deliberately exempted the primary form of income enjoyed by the superrich: unrealized capital gains. As Saez and Zucman note, billionaires like Jeff Bezos and Warren Buffett do not receive massive paychecks subject to the income tax. Their wealth grows as the stock in their companies appreciates. Under current law, this appreciation is not taxed until the asset is sold. And, crucially, if the asset is held until death, the "step-up in basis" loophole wipes out the capital gains tax liability entirely. This "buy, borrow, die" strategy is the engine of modern dynastic wealth. The wealthy borrow against their rising portfolios to fund lavish lifestyles, paying minimal interest, and pass the assets to their heirs tax-free.

Rather than inventing an entirely new tax regime that requires the annual valuation of all global assets held by the wealthy, we could directly attack this engine of inequality by reforming the income tax. A mark-to-market tax system for the ultra-wealthy would treat the annual appreciation of publicly traded assets as ordinary income. If Jeff Bezos’s Amazon stock goes up by $10 billion in a given year, he would owe tax on that $10 billion gain, whether he sells the stock or not. For illiquid assets, we could impose a deferral charge—a tax assessed when the asset is finally sold or transferred, with an added interest penalty to account for the years the tax was deferred. Coupled with the immediate repeal of the step-up in basis, these reforms would effectively end the tax-free compounding of vast fortunes.

Furthermore, Saez and Zucman dismiss the estate tax too quickly. It is true that the current estate tax is "moribund," but that is a political choice, not an economic inevitability. The Republican tax cuts of 2017 doubled the estate tax exemption to roughly $22 million for a married couple, and the proliferation of grantor retained annuity trusts (GRATs) and valuation discounts has turned the tax into a voluntary levy for those with good estate planners. But we know how to fix this. We can lower the exemption threshold back to $3.5 million, raise the top rate to 50 or 60 percent, and close the trust loopholes that allow billionaires to pass on their wealth tax-free.

Pursuing a wealth tax instead of these proven reforms invites a catastrophic legal and political risk. The United States Constitution explicitly states in Article I, Section 9, that "No Capitation, or other direct, Tax shall be laid, unless in Proportion to the Census." While the Sixteenth Amendment exempted the income tax from this apportionment requirement, a wealth tax is widely considered a direct tax. If a President Sanders or Warren were to pass a wealth tax, it would face an immediate legal challenge. Given the current composition of the federal judiciary and the Supreme Court, there is a very high probability that the wealth tax would be struck down as unconstitutional.

Imagine the scenario: Democrats spend immense political capital passing a wealth tax, ignoring reforms to the income and estate taxes. The law is immediately enjoined by the courts. After three or four years of furious litigation, the Supreme Court rules it unconstitutional. The political moment has passed, the administration has lost its signature legislative achievement, and the billionaires have enjoyed another half-decade of untaxed wealth accumulation. Why risk the entire progressive economic agenda on a constitutional gamble when mark-to-market taxation and robust estate tax enforcement achieve the exact same goals within the established bounds of the Sixteenth Amendment?

Administratively, the wealth tax proposed by Saez and Zucman is a bureaucratic nightmare. The authors breezily suggest that valuing private companies, art collections, and complex offshore trusts is merely a matter of "political will." This is a vast underestimation of the ingenuity and resources of the tax avoidance industry. The IRS has been systematically defunded over the last decade; it has lost thousands of its most experienced auditors. Rebuilding the agency to enforce existing laws will take years. Tasking a hollowed-out IRS with conducting annual valuations of every closely held business, private equity partnership, and Cayman Islands trust held by the ultra-wealthy is a recipe for endless litigation and administrative gridlock.

To solve the valuation problem, Saez and Zucman propose a radical "pay-in-kind" system, where wealthy individuals can hand over shares of their private companies to the government, which would then auction them off. This is a startlingly naive proposal. The government would suddenly become a massive clearinghouse for minority stakes in closely held family businesses. Who will buy a 2 percent, non-voting stake in a private agribusiness like Cargill at a fair market price? Nobody. Such minority stakes are inherently illiquid and subject to massive marketability discounts. The only willing buyers would be the existing majority shareholders—the very same billionaires—who would buy the shares back from the government at a steep discount, effectively slashing their own tax bills and making a mockery of the system.

Saez and Zucman are brilliant economists, and their documentation of the inequality crisis is indispensable. But we do not need to invent a new, legally dubious, and administratively chaotic tax system to solve this crisis. We just need the courage to make the wealthy pay the taxes that are already on the books. Tax capital gains as ordinary income. Eliminate the step-up in basis. Ban GRATs and dynasty trusts. Restore the corporate tax rate. Fund the IRS to pursue offshore tax evasion. These are the pragmatic, constitutional, and highly effective tools available to us right now. Let us use them, rather than chasing the phantom of a wealth tax.
