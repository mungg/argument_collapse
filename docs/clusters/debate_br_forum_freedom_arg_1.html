Diversified DeepSeek

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the imperial roots of the American right's version of freedom, his proposed solution of long-term institution-building underestimates the power of cultural inertia and the urgent need for a simultaneous, aggressive narrative struggle."

Aziz Rana’s diagnosis of the American right’s appropriation of freedom is as rich as it is disheartening. His historical argument—that settler colonialism and imperial power have embedded zero-sum, racialized, and individualistic notions of liberty deep in the culture—explains a great deal. And his core prescription, that the left needs not mere rhetorical repackaging but a long-term project of institution-building that makes solidaristic self-rule tangible, is in many ways a welcome corrective to the shallow “message discipline” advice so common in liberal circles. Yet for all its sophistication, the essay underestimates the sheer weight of cultural inertia and the limits of institutional transformation in a society where the material base has changed but the underlying cultural commitments remain stubbornly resilient. The danger is that a strategy focused on gradually reshaping everyday institutions will be too slow and too weak to compete with the right’s cultural machinery.

Rana’s historical narrative hinges on a crucial insight: the settler version of freedom felt real, not illusory, to millions of white Americans because they enjoyed genuine material benefits—land, independence, upward mobility. When those material underpinnings crumbled, as they did in the early twentieth century, a more solidaristic freedom had a chance. The implication is that today’s precarity and imperial overreach are again creating a structural opening. But the material conditions of the early twenty-first century are profoundly different from those of the 1930s, and these differences undercut the parallel. The settler experience left behind a cultural DNA that is not simply a superstructure dependent on a material base; it has evolved into a deeply held worldview that is actively reproduced by an immense right-wing media ecosystem, a well-funded network of think tanks, and a political party increasingly organized around ethnonationalist grievance. Even if some working-class white Americans now sense that corporate interests no longer serve them, that does not mean they will embrace a multiracial, solidaristic conception of freedom. In fact, the current moment suggests that many are doubling down on the exclusionary, zero-sum version—not because it delivers material prosperity, but because it offers a sense of status, identity, and belonging in a changing world.

Moreover, Rana’s emphasis on institution-building, while essential, has a chicken-and-egg problem. He argues that the left must create workplaces, neighborhoods, and schools where people actually experience collective self-rule, so that the word “freedom” becomes organically linked to mutual respect and common concern. But building such institutions requires political power, resources, and mass participation—precisely the things that are profoundly difficult to achieve when the dominant culture still valorizes individualistic freedom and suspicion of collective action. Union organizing, for instance, faces not only hostile labor law but a workforce that has been marinated in a culture telling them that unions are coercive, that they demand dues for nothing, and that the admirable worker is the plucky independent contractor. A bill like the PRO Act could help, but even if it passed (a tall order given the filibuster and a hostile judiciary), it would take a generation to rebuild union density to levels that genuinely shift workplace culture. In the meantime, the right continues to wield freedom talk to dismantle regulations, cut taxes for the wealthy, and restrict voting rights.

Rana is aware of this difficulty. He acknowledges that activists today operate on a terrain “dramatically altered by neoliberal ascendance” and that there are “vanishingly few sites” where collective agency is a meaningful reality. But he seems to hold out hope that by building those sites—through electoral reform, labor law changes, and immigration policy—the cultural associations can be changed over time. The problem is the timeline. The right’s narrative machine works on an hourly cycle of outrage and reinforcement. Its “fragility,” as Robin D. G. Kelley puts it, is real, but fragility can be a powerful mobilizing force when it is endlessly stoked by demagogues who promise a return to a past of clear hierarchies and national strength. The left’s slow, institutional counter-project is no match for this in the short run, and losing the short run consistently means losing the capacity to build those institutions in the first place.

This is not an argument for abandoning the fight for structural change. It is an argument for recognizing that the cultural war over the meaning of freedom must be fought on multiple fronts simultaneously, including the front of narrative and symbolism. Rana rightly pushes back against the notion that this is merely a matter of communications strategy, but he risks overcorrecting. Yes, institutions are crucial, but people also live inside a torrent of stories, images, and arguments. The civil rights movement did not just build new legal frameworks; it staged dramatic, televised confrontations that reframed freedom in the public imagination. The labor movement of the 1930s was not just about the Wagner Act; it was about sit-down strikes and mass demonstrations that enacted solidarity in a visible, compelling way. Today’s left needs to find its own forms of symbolic action that disrupt the right’s framing of freedom, even as it works the long game of institution-building.

Perhaps what is needed is a more dialectical approach: using narrative interventions to open political space for institutional changes, which then feed back into a new common sense about freedom. For instance, the recent labor upsurge among Starbucks baristas and Amazon warehouse workers is not just about winning contracts; it is already shifting the cultural conversation about who a worker is and what dignity at work means. Those struggles are themselves communicative acts that challenge the individualistic myth. Rana’s analysis could be enriched by acknowledging that narrative work and institution-building are not sequential but intertwined, each enabling the other.

In the end, I share Rana’s commitment to a freedom grounded in solidarity and self-rule, and I admire his refusal to cede the language of liberty to a nativist right. But the path forward must account for the fact that American culture has been shaped by empire for so long that its reflexes are incredibly deep. We cannot simply wait for institutions to reshape consciousness; we must wage a deliberate struggle over meaning in the very arena where the right has dominated—the realm of public discourse and popular culture. Only by engaging in that messy, symbolic contest, alongside the patient construction of democratic workplaces and inclusive communities, can the left hope to reclaim a word that has lately been twisted into a cudgel. Otherwise, we will find ourselves, a generation from now, with a few admirable worker cooperatives and a political landscape still defined by exclusion and domination.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the need for institutional restructuring to reclaim freedom from the right, his proposal is incomplete because it underestimates the necessity of a parallel cultural and rhetorical campaign to provide a visceral, emotionally resonant alternative to the right's narrative."

Aziz Rana offers a powerful and deeply researched account of how the American right came to dominate the language of freedom. His tracing of a zero-sum, exclusionary liberty—rooted in settler colonialism and later reinforced by global empire—is a vital corrective to the simplistic notion that the left simply needs better slogans. Yet for all its historical insight, the essay’s prescription falls short in one crucial respect: it understates the work of cultural and rhetorical reclamation that must accompany institutional change. Rana is right to say this is not just a messaging problem, but the battle over freedom will not be won by new workplaces and voting reforms alone. The right’s hold on the idea persists not only because people lack experience with democratic self-rule, but because they are immersed in a cultural narrative that frames freedom in ways that feel emotionally urgent and morally clear. The left needs to contest that narrative directly, with stories, symbols, and a visceral alternative vision—not just material restructuring.

Rana’s historical scaffolding is convincing. He shows how settler expansion made the experience of individual self-mastery seem not just possible but natural for white Americans, while the state’s coercive power was always a sword pointed outward. This, he argues, created a durable cultural reflex: internal government restraint is tyranny, external force is liberty’s bulwark. The imperial decades after World War II, with the nation’s global economic primacy, gave this reflex a second life by linking domestic prosperity to individualized market entitlements rather than collective provision. The result is a freedom talk that mates rugged individualism with nativist violence, and that—as he notes—is increasingly fragile because it can no longer deliver the material independence it promises.

But Rana’s own characterization of the right’s current weakness may be too sanguine. He calls Trumpism and its “worn-out tropes” fragile, noting that its base increasingly sees corporate interests as divorced from the public good. Yet in the immediate present, polling and political energy tell a more ambiguous story. The freedom to refuse vaccines, to carry guns without permits, to speak without “woke” censure—these continue to mobilize millions. The right’s freedom narrative may be contradictory and often hypocritical, but it is not yet hollow to its adherents. It provides a clear antagonist (the state, the left, the outsider) and a tangible sense of personal agency, even if that agency is frequently performative. The left cannot count on its imminent collapse; it must offer something with equal emotional purchase.

Rana’s corrective is institution-building: sites where ordinary people experience collective self-rule—unions, reformed workplaces, democratic political structures—will organically foster a solidaristic freedom. And because empire has always fed exclusionary liberty, he insists we must also adopt an anti-imperial foreign policy and reimagine immigration as a site of shared freedom. These are worthy, necessary goals. But the idea that changing the everyday experience of work and governance will naturally produce a new common sense about freedom underestimates the autonomy of culture. After all, many labor unions in the mid-twentieth century were themselves complicit in racial exclusion, and even today, strong union identity does not always translate into inclusive solidarity on immigration or foreign policy. Furthermore, the cultural space is filled with media, political entertainment, and religious narratives that constantly re-inscribe the old individualist frame. Institution-building is a generational project; without a parallel effort to reclaim the moral imagination, its fruits may be co-opted or contained by the very narratives we wish to displace.

The deeper challenge is this: the left’s inclusive freedom can sound abstract—as Rana admits—precisely because it lacks a compelling cultural story. The right cluttered the public square with “freedom fries” and “Don’t Tread on Me” for a reason: they connected the idea to immediate gut feelings of defiance, ownership, and identity. The left needs its own resonant symbols and rhetorical frames that make solidaristic freedom feel like a direct answer to real fears. Consider the lived experience of precarity: crushing medical debt, algorithmic surveillance at work, unaffordable housing—these feel like profound unfreedom. They are experienced not merely as missed solidarity but as a direct assault on one’s ability to direct the basic conditions of life. The left can and should speak of freedom in these registers: freedom from wage slavery, freedom from being one illness away from bankruptcy, freedom from the arbitrary whims of a landlord or a scheduling algorithm. This is not a substitute for institutional change, but it gives the project a felt urgency that transcends abstract appeals to self-rule.

The civil rights movement, which Rana rightly invokes, succeeded in transforming the national conversation not only through organizing and institution-building but through an unforgettable moral and rhetorical campaign. King’s “I Have a Dream” speech did not merely describe a legislative agenda; it painted a world in which freedom became a shared, almost tactile experience of dignity. The Freedom Budget, for all its policy specificity, was an attempt to give material heft to that vision. Today’s left needs a similar narrative audacity—one that does not shy away from calling its project freedom, and does so with images and language that can compete with the right’s martial and nostalgic tropes.

This points to another tension in Rana’s argument. He insists that the left’s domestic freedom project requires an anti-imperial reorientation of foreign policy. While the historical linkage is real, tying the reclamation of freedom at home so directly to a wholesale rejection of American global primacy risks turning a broad, popular appeal into an ideological checklist that many ordinary people will find distant and disorienting. Most Americans, including many who are open to stronger unions and a fairer economy, do not immediately connect their own sense of unfreedom to the defense budget or the legacy of colonialism. The left can articulate a freedom vision that prioritizes economic security, community control, and racial justice without making geopolitical transformation a prerequisite. The goal should be to fracture the old settler equation from within, showing that a good life is possible without external violence, not to demand that foreign policy conversion precede domestic hope.

Rana’s essay closes by refusing to abandon freedom talk to the right, and that is exactly the right instinct. But reclaiming freedom is as much a cultural struggle as an institutional one. The right’s version persists not because most Americans actually enjoy robust self-rule, but because the story speaks to a deep desire for agency in a world that feels out of control. The left can answer that desire not only by building spaces of collective agency—though that is essential—but by telling a new story in which freedom looks like a secure, dignified life, unburdened by both corporate domination and atomized isolation. Institution-building will take decades; a compelling freedom narrative can begin to shift the ground now, making those reforms feel like the natural outcome of a widely shared moral vision rather than the top-down projects of policy wonks. Only when the words “freedom” and “solidarity” are as instinctively paired in the American mind as “freedom” and “individual” have been will the left’s project truly take root.

Diversified MiniMax

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies that the collapse of historical material conditions for individualistic freedom creates an opening for a solidaristic alternative, structural institutional changes must be accompanied by a deliberate reimagining of the cultural and intellectual 'grammar' of freedom."

Aziz Rana's essay achieves something rare in contemporary political discourse: it takes history seriously as a way of illuminating present possibilities. Too often, American political debate treats the past as a source of inspirational quotes rather than analytical leverage. Rana does the harder work of tracing how specific historical structures—settler colonialism, imperial projection, racial exclusion—shaped the very categories through which Americans understand themselves and their possibilities.

The argument that settler expansion was constitutive of white freedom is particularly important. This isn't just about land theft as an injustice in itself; it's about how the availability of frontiers created a material and psychological condition in which individual self-rule seemed achievable. When you could always move west when conditions became untenable, dependency on collective structures seemed less necessary. This helps explain why American workers have historically been ambivalent about the welfare state: they could imagine, however illusionarily, that they might not need it.

I want to emphasize one implication of this analysis that Rana touches on but doesn't fully develop: the way the right's current "freedom" politics depends on nostalgic fantasy rather than material reality. There is no frontier anymore. There are no new lands to colonize. The global economy does not offer easy exits from domestic conditions. This means the individualistic freedom the right invokes is not merely morally impoverished; it's substantively unavailable to most Americans. They know this, or sense it, even if they don't articulate it in these terms.

This is the opening Rana identifies, and I think he's right that it represents a genuine political opportunity. But it's important to understand why previous openings—the early twentieth century, the New Deal, the Great Society, even the 1960s—didn't fully transform the cultural grammar of freedom. The answer, I suspect, is that structural changes alone cannot rework deep habits of imagination. The institutions Rana calls for are necessary but not sufficient. We also need intellectual and cultural work that reimagines what freedom could mean.

Rana's essay is a contribution to that work. More please.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The historical failure of previous progressive eras to permanently transform the American understanding of liberty suggests that structural shifts alone are insufficient to rework deep-seated habits of the imagination.
  3. 03Rana’s analysis provides essential analytical leverage by showing how specific historical structures like settler colonialism and imperial projection shaped modern American identity.
  4. 04To successfully reclaim freedom, the left must engage in intellectual and cultural work that defines what freedom means in a world where individualistic self-rule is substantively unavailable to most people.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the structural roots of the right's capture of 'freedom,' his focus on long-term institution-building overlooks the immediate power of narrative and the American left's own robust, historical tradition of using freedom talk to drive social change."

Aziz Rana’s essay marshals an impressive historical arsenal to argue that the right’s capture of freedom talk is rooted in the material realities of settler colonialism and empire, and that reclaiming it requires institution-building, not just rhetorical strategy. It is a compelling narrative, but it rests on a historical simplification that ultimately constrains its political imagination. The story Rana tells is one of long-term structural determination, in which the settler-imperial experience consistently produced a zero-sum, exclusionary freedom, with only brief windows of inversion. But the actual history of the American left is a history of robust, enduring, and creative appropriation of the language of freedom—not as a fragile anomaly, but as a central thread of American radicalism. By underplaying this tradition, Rana risks reinforcing the very pessimism among left-liberals that he hopes to counter, and he proposes a laborious institutional detour when what the moment demands is a more confident and accessible revival of a freedom talk that the left once deployed with world-changing effect.

The freedom tradition on the American left is not limited to the interwar period Rana highlights. It stretches from the abolitionist cry of “Am I Not a Woman and a Sister?” through the post-Civil War labor slogan “Eight hours for work, eight hours for rest, eight hours for what we will,” to the Industrial Workers of the World’s vision of “building a new world in the shell of the old.” None of these were merely defensive; they were affirmative, everyday articulations of freedom as the capacity to control one’s time, body, and labor. The civil rights movement’s “freedom songs” and the feminist chant “my body, my choice” are not footnotes; they are the most resonant examples of freedom talk in American memory. When Bernie Sanders, a democratic socialist, rallies millions with the phrase “freedom is not a choice between the party of the oligarchs and the party of the plutocrats,” he is tapping into a deep left vein, not conjuring a new one. Rana himself quotes Jefferson Cowie’s steelworker son and acknowledges that the mid-century labor movement delivered a genuine experience of liberation. But he treats this as a product of empire’s rents rather than as a political accomplishment won by decades of struggle—a framing that diminishes the agency of the movements that made it happen.

The problem is that Rana’s structural determinism leads him to underestimate the power of narrative and movement-building in shifting cultural meanings. He is right that institutions are crucial, but the civil rights movement did not wait for institutional change before it changed the meaning of freedom in American life; it used direct action, speeches, and moral confrontation to redefine freedom in the public mind, which then created the political pressure for institutional reforms like the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act. The movement’s “freedom rides” and “freedom schools” were themselves institution-building, but they were also deeply symbolic actions designed to dramatize a new vision of freedom before the law had caught up. The lesson is not that narrative work is futile without prior institutional transformation, but that institutions and narratives co-evolve, often with narrative leading the way.

The left’s current predicament, then, is not that it lacks the institutional base to make a solidaristic freedom resonate. It is that it has, for decades, ceded the very act of telling a compelling freedom story. Rana’s call for union-building, electoral reform, and anti-imperial policy is important. But these are long-term projects that, in the absence of a powerful and emotionally gripping freedom narrative, will struggle to attract the mass support needed to enact them. People need a story that connects their immediate struggles to a larger vision of liberation, and that story cannot wait for the slow accumulation of institutional change. It must be told now, in public, with passion and clarity.

The right’s current dominance of freedom talk is not, as Rana implies, an inevitable cultural reflex of empire. It is the product of a deliberate, well-funded, five-decade project by conservative intellectuals and political strategists who understood that language matters. The left once had a comparable infrastructure: a labor press, radical newspapers, folk music, and a network of schools and camps that inculcated an alternative vision. The neoliberal era dismantled much of that, but the digital age has created new possibilities for narrative dissemination. Movements like #BlackLivesMatter and the Fight for $15 have shown that it is possible, with relatively few resources, to reframe public debate around freedom from state violence and economic precarity. These are not yet the “organic” cultural presence Rana wants, but they are more immediate and scalable than the generation-long task of rebuilding union density.

Rana’s anti-imperialism is also, in this context, an unnecessarily heavy cudgel for a domestic narrative project. He is right that an honest account of American freedom must reckon with empire, but the primary audience for a left freedom narrative is not a historical tribunal; it is the millions of Americans who feel unfree in their daily lives. Connecting their unfreedom to the Pentagon budget is a complex analytical move that even the most engaged activists struggle to hold in their heads while also worrying about rent and healthcare. The left’s history teaches a more inductive method: start with the concrete unfreedom people experience—the boss who schedules you for 39 hours to avoid benefits, the medical debt that dictates your life choices—and build outward toward a systemic critique. That is how the Freedom Budget framed its demands: not as a philosophical treatise on empire, but as a practical plan to secure the material preconditions of liberty for all, which then implied a challenge to militarism because the money had to come from somewhere.

None of this is to dismiss Rana’s central challenge. Institution-building is vital. But the best institution-builder is a movement that has already captured the public’s moral imagination, and that requires a powerful story. The left should not shy away from waging a full-throated battle for the word “freedom” precisely because it has the superior claim. The right offers a freedom that is, as Rana brilliantly shows, hollowed out for all but a few. The left can offer a freedom that is richer: the freedom to have a say in the decisions that affect your life, to live without the constant fear of destitution, to love whom you love, to move without being treated as a criminal. That is a story Americans are ready to hear, and it need not wait for the revolution. The left’s own history demonstrates that when it speaks of freedom, it speaks in a tongue people understand. The task is not to invent a new language, but to rediscover the one that cadres once sang on picket lines and that Ella Baker whispered in church basements. That language is not dead; it is merely waiting to be spoken again.
