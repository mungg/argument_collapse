"Reclaiming freedom for the left requires more than better messaging; it necessitates building democratic institutions that allow people to experience collective agency and self-rule in their daily lives."

Aziz Rana is right to insist that the left’s problem with freedom is not primarily semantic. The word has not drifted rightward because Democrats forgot to hire better consultants or because progressive candidates failed to say “freedom” enough times in stump speeches. It has drifted because many Americans experience the institutions around them—workplaces, schools, courts, hospitals, landlords, police departments, immigration offices—not as sites of democratic agency but as places where decisions are made elsewhere. Under those conditions, the right’s promise of freedom as release from constraint, from taxes, from regulation, from unfamiliar neighbors, has an intuitive appeal. It may not deliver self-rule, but it gives a name to resentment.

The strongest part of Rana’s essay is his insistence that a democratic meaning of freedom must be lived before it can be believed. That claim should become a starting point for left politics. Too often progressive freedom talk has the cadence of a moral lecture: you are not really free unless others are free too; you are not really free unless institutions are democratic; you are not really free unless your private choices are embedded in public obligations. All true. But truth is not the same as political force. If people have no ordinary experience of collective power, solidarity can sound like another demand placed on already burdened lives.

The old labor movement understood this better than many contemporary progressives do. A union was not merely a redistributive instrument, though it was that. It was also a school of freedom. In a shop where workers could elect stewards, file grievances, bargain over schedules, and strike, freedom meant something more than being left alone. It meant having a say. It meant that the foreman’s whim could be contested. It meant that wage workers, who were formally free to quit but practically dependent on a paycheck, could exercise power together. No messaging operation can substitute for that kind of experience.

This is why recent labor activity matters beyond the contract gains themselves. The strikes and organizing drives of the last few years—among teachers, nurses, autoworkers, graduate workers, warehouse workers, baristas, hotel workers, and others—have been important not only because they sought higher wages or better staffing ratios. They have reopened a basic question: who should govern the workplace? When autoworkers challenge a two-tier wage system, they are not just asking for money. They are rejecting a structure that tells younger workers they are permanently less entitled to security. When nurses demand safe staffing, they are not merely bargaining for working conditions. They are asserting that those who care for patients should have authority over the conditions of care. These are freedom claims, whether or not every striker uses that word.

But Rana’s account also asks us to see why such claims have been difficult to sustain in the United States. His history of settler freedom is especially useful because it explains a durable emotional pattern: the belief that government coercion is tyranny when applied to insiders but legitimate when directed against outsiders. One can see this pattern in nineteenth-century Indian removal, in slavery and Jim Crow, in anti-Mexican violence, in the policing of Black neighborhoods, in immigration raids, and in today’s fantasies of mass deportation. The same people who denounce public health rules as despotism often celebrate the state when it cages migrants or clears homeless encampments. That is not a contradiction. It is a theory of freedom organized around membership and domination.

Still, if the left is to build a more democratic freedom, it must avoid two temptations. The first is to speak as if ordinary Americans who are drawn to right-wing freedom are simply dupes of empire and racism. Some are deeply invested in racial hierarchy, and that must be said plainly. But many also respond to a real loss of control: plant closures, medical debt, unaffordable housing, algorithmic management, decaying public services, and an electoral system that often feels immune to popular pressure. A left freedom politics should be unsparing about the reactionary channeling of that loss, but it should not deny the loss itself.

The second temptation is to treat institution building as a slow substitute for immediate political conflict. Rana rightly says there is no shortcut around unions, voting rights, and democratic workplaces. Yet institution building requires state power. Workers cannot simply will a new labor regime into existence while employers enjoy weak penalties for union busting. Tenants cannot build durable associations when eviction is swift and housing is scarce. Parents cannot participate meaningfully in schools that are starved of resources. The left needs both bottom-up organizing and legislative confrontation: labor law reform, sectoral bargaining experiments, just-cause eviction protections, public housing, universal health care, child care, and voting rights. These are not technocratic add-ons. They are the scaffolding of self-rule.

Rana’s foreign policy argument is also essential. The United States cannot indefinitely practice domination abroad and democracy at home without the former contaminating the latter. Militarized habits travel. So do racial stories about dangerous populations needing discipline. So do budgetary priorities. When public money flows easily to weapons but grudgingly to schools, hospitals, transit, and climate adaptation, citizens learn what kind of security counts. The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq did not make Americans freer; they helped normalize secrecy, surveillance, executive unilateralism, anti-Muslim suspicion, and a politics in which sacrifice was demanded of soldiers and civilians abroad while the public at home was asked mainly to shop.

The hard question is how to make anti-imperial freedom tangible to people who do not experience foreign policy as part of their daily lives. One answer is budgetary: every unnecessary weapons system is a school not repaired, a lead pipe not replaced, a clinic not funded. But the deeper answer is moral and institutional. A country that accepts limits abroad may become more capable of democracy at home because it must give up the fantasy that security comes from command. It must learn negotiation, interdependence, and restraint. Those are democratic virtues.

The left should reclaim freedom, but not by pretending the word is innocent. Rana’s essay shows that American freedom has often been built through unfreedom. The point is not to purify the term by assertion. It is to change the social relations that give it content. Freedom should mean not fearing a boss’s retaliation, not rationing insulin, not being trapped in an abusive relationship by poverty, not watching police treat your neighborhood as occupied territory, not having your family separated by an immigration bureaucracy, not seeing your future consumed by climate disaster, not being told that democracy ends at the factory gate or the border.

Such a freedom will not be won by slogans. But slogans may matter once backed by institutions. “Freedom” on the right has power because it condenses a world of practices: guns, property, policing, borders, markets, and suspicion of the state. A democratic freedom must condense another world: unions, public goods, equal citizenship, demilitarization, racial justice, reproductive autonomy, and shared power. The task is not merely to say that this freedom is better. It is to make enough people live it that the word begins to sound different in their mouths.

"While reclaiming freedom from the right requires building institutions of collective solidarity, a successful left project must also protect individual agency by checking arbitrary power within both private and public institutions."

Aziz Rana is right that the American language of freedom has been warped by conquest, racial exclusion, and empire—and right, too, that the left should not surrender the word to those who define it as tax cuts, gun maximalism, and the right to dominate weaker people. But reclaiming freedom requires more than substituting solidarity for individualism. It also requires taking seriously why many Americans hear progressive invocations of collective power as potential threats to their own agency. A democratic left freedom must be capacious enough to include not only freedom through shared institutions, but freedom from arbitrary power wherever it appears: in markets, in police departments, in employers, in borders, but also in bureaucracies, parties, schools, unions, and states.

Rana’s central historical claim is compelling. The United States has repeatedly offered real freedoms to some by denying them to others. White settlers experienced landownership as independence because Indigenous nations were dispossessed. White workers could imagine themselves as self-made because racialized labor and imperial markets subsidized their security. Cold War prosperity helped many Americans identify private employment, employer-based benefits, and consumer choice with freedom, even as U.S. power constrained self-determination abroad. This history helps explain why the right’s freedom talk has often meant a shield for insiders and a sword for outsiders.

Yet the same history also warns against making “collective self-rule” do too much work on its own. Majorities can dominate. Solidarities can exclude. Institutions built in the name of the people can become insulated from the people. The New Deal, which Rana rightly treats as a moment when freedom and democracy were joined more robustly, also entrenched racial exclusions in agricultural and domestic labor, accommodated segregationist power, and built welfare and labor regimes that often depended on male breadwinner assumptions. Industrial unions were schools of democracy for millions, but some were also hostile to women, Black workers, immigrants, and internal dissent. None of this refutes Rana’s argument. It deepens it: freedom requires institutions of collective power, but also rights, checks, contestation, and exit.

That point matters because the contemporary left’s difficulty with freedom is not only institutional weakness. It is also a credibility problem about coercion. Many Americans experience unfreedom through employers, landlords, medical debt, student loans, algorithmic scheduling, and the fear of losing health insurance with a job. But they also experience unfreedom when public systems seem opaque, punitive, or contemptuous. A parent fighting a school bureaucracy for a disabled child’s services, a small contractor navigating licensing rules written for larger firms, a tenant trapped in a public housing authority’s indifference, or a dissident student worried about ideological conformity may not think “solidarity” captures the remedy they need. They may want voice, but they also want limits on power.

The right exploits these anxieties cynically, often redirecting anger away from corporate domination and toward migrants, teachers, trans people, or racial justice protesters. But the anxieties are not all manufactured. During the pandemic, for example, Americans saw both the necessity of collective action and the costs of institutional mistrust. Public health measures saved lives; they also revealed how unevenly burdens were distributed, how poorly authorities sometimes communicated uncertainty, and how little say many families and workers felt they had in decisions that transformed daily life. A left freedom politics that treats all resistance to public authority as selfish libertarianism will fail. The better answer is to democratize authority, make it accountable, and be honest about tradeoffs.

This is why messaging cannot be so neatly separated from institution building. Rana is surely right that slogans alone cannot create lived experiences of collective agency. But political language is one of the ways people decide which institutions are worth building and which sacrifices are legitimate. The civil rights movement did not merely construct organizations; it named the country’s contradictions in biblical, constitutional, and democratic terms. Labor movements did not only bargain; they taught workers to see the eight-hour day, the weekend, and workplace safety as freedom rather than favors. Feminists changed law and workplaces partly by changing the meaning of privacy, equality, bodily autonomy, and dependence. Words do not substitute for power, but they organize perception. They make some injuries intolerable and some futures imaginable.

A persuasive left account of freedom should therefore speak in several registers at once. It should insist that nobody is free if illness means bankruptcy, if a boss can fire workers for organizing, if police can kill with impunity, if pregnancy is compelled by the state, or if citizenship status makes a person permanently exploitable. It should also insist that people are not free when decisions affecting their neighborhoods, schools, and workplaces are made by distant authorities they cannot influence. And it should defend civil liberties without embarrassment: speech, association, due process, religious liberty, privacy, and protection against arbitrary punishment are not bourgeois distractions from material justice. They are tools the powerless have repeatedly used to organize, dissent, and survive.

Rana’s anti-imperial emphasis is indispensable, especially after Iraq and Afghanistan, and amid the horror in Gaza after October 7. A country habituated to treating other peoples’ self-rule as secondary will struggle to cultivate democratic humility at home. The scale of U.S. military spending also plainly crowds out social investment. Still, an anti-imperial freedom politics must avoid becoming a reflexive inversion of Washington’s preferences. Ukrainians resisting Russian invasion, Taiwanese people facing coercion from Beijing, Iranian women confronting theocratic repression, and Palestinians demanding equal dignity all speak languages of freedom. The question is not whether American power is good or bad in the abstract. It is how to support self-determination without domination, how to use restraint without indifference, and how to distinguish solidarity from geopolitical campism.

The most promising part of Rana’s essay is his call to rebuild everyday sites of agency. Unions matter not only because they raise wages, but because they let people practice democracy where they spend much of their lives. Tenant unions, cooperatives, community land trusts, participatory budgeting, public banking, school boards that are genuinely representative, and immigrant worker centers can all make freedom tangible. So can universal programs—health care, child care, paid leave, housing supports—that reduce dependence on employers and intimate partners. The point is not to replace individual freedom with collective freedom, but to show that secure individuality often depends on collective provision.

The left should reclaim freedom, then, but not by denying the American fear of domination. It should vindicate that fear and redirect it. The problem is not that Americans care too much about being pushed around. The problem is that our politics has taught many to see domination only when government acts, and not when capital, inherited hierarchy, racial violence, or imperial power does. A better freedom would make people less governable by bosses, bigots, monopolies, police, creditors, and unaccountable officials alike. It would be solidaristic because domination is shared, and pluralist because power is dangerous even when exercised in the name of justice. That freedom is harder to build than to brand. But it is also harder for the right to counterfeit.

"To reclaim freedom as a solidaristic practice, the left must look beyond national policy and messaging to revitalize the 'middle layer' of civic life—the local associations and institutions where Americans can practically experience and learn collective agency."

Aziz Rana is right that freedom cannot be reclaimed by messaging alone. Where I would push his argument is toward the middle layer of American life: the associations, congregations, cooperatives, clubs, libraries, mutual aid societies, parent groups, neighborhood councils, ethnic organizations, and local institutions that sit between the isolated individual and the national state. If Americans lack an everyday experience of collective agency, the problem is not only weak unions or antidemocratic national structures. It is also the thinning of civic life.

Freedom as self-rule requires practice. People do not become democratic citizens only by voting every few years, nor by consuming national political media, nor by expressing opinions online. They become citizens by running meetings, arguing over budgets, caring for shared spaces, electing officers, organizing food drives, bargaining with city agencies, creating rituals, teaching children, welcoming newcomers, and resolving conflicts with people they cannot simply block. These mundane activities are not distractions from politics. They are the texture of democratic freedom.

Rana’s account of settler freedom emphasizes land, empire, race, and markets. That history is essential. But another part of the American story is associational abundance. Many excluded communities built their own institutions because dominant institutions denied them equal membership. Black churches, mutual aid societies, women’s clubs, immigrant lodges, labor halls, settlement houses, freedom schools, and cooperative ventures did more than provide services. They cultivated leadership and solidarity. They made freedom visible in daily life. The civil rights movement did not emerge from moral insight alone; it emerged from dense networks of churches, colleges, local NAACP branches, unions, youth groups, and family ties.

The erosion of such institutions helps explain why freedom now so often appears as personal autonomy or resentment. If your main experiences of collective life are bureaucracy, employment discipline, consumer platforms, and national partisan conflict, then collective power will feel either remote or threatening. If the homeowners’ association is the most immediate form of local governance, freedom may come to mean keeping others out. If online spaces replace physical associations, political identity may become performance without responsibility. If congregations decline and unions shrink, people lose places where they can practice solidarity across difference.

This civic erosion has many causes: suburbanization, long work hours, television and digital entertainment, economic precarity, professionalized nonprofits, distrust of institutions, geographic mobility, and the privatization of leisure. Neoliberalism matters, as Rana says, but the remedy cannot be only national policy. We need civic reconstruction.

What would that look like? First, public libraries should be understood as freedom institutions. They provide access to knowledge, meeting space, internet, children’s programming, job assistance, and a rare noncommercial public environment. Attacks on libraries and librarians are attacks on democratic life. A country serious about freedom would fund libraries as civic infrastructure and defend them against censorship.

Second, schools should be community institutions, not merely testing sites or culture-war battlegrounds. Public schools are among the few places where families of different backgrounds encounter one another through a shared institution. They can teach democratic habits through student councils, debate, service learning, parent participation, and curriculum that treats history honestly. But schools cannot do this if they are underfunded, segregated, or governed by panic. The fight over education should not be framed only as parental freedom versus bureaucratic authority. It should ask how schools can become places of shared self-rule while protecting vulnerable students from majoritarian abuse.

Third, local journalism matters. Without reliable information about city councils, school boards, courts, employers, landlords, police, and developers, citizens cannot govern. The collapse of local news has nationalized attention and empowered rumor. People know every outrage in Washington but not who is shaping zoning decisions in their town. Public or nonprofit support for local journalism should be part of a freedom agenda.

Fourth, cooperatives and mutual aid deserve more than romantic praise. Worker cooperatives, credit unions, food co-ops, community land trusts, and mutual aid networks can give people direct experience with economic democracy. They are not substitutes for unions or welfare states; they are complements. A community land trust, for example, can protect housing affordability while giving residents a say. A credit union can embed finance in local accountability. These institutions make interdependence practical.

Fifth, religious and moral communities should not be written off. Many progressives are understandably wary of conservative religion’s role in restricting abortion, LGBTQ rights, and education. But religious institutions have also powered abolition, civil rights, sanctuary work, labor organizing, and antiwar activism. A left freedom politics that cannot speak to congregational life will miss millions of Americans who learn solidarity through faith. The goal should not be to sacralize politics but to welcome institutions that cultivate service, discipline, and moral imagination while firmly defending pluralism and equal rights.

Sixth, national service in a democratic, nonmilitary form deserves reconsideration. Not compulsory militarism, and not a cheap labor scheme, but well-paid opportunities for young people and others to work on climate resilience, elder care, tutoring, public health, conservation, and infrastructure. Such programs could mix people across region and class while producing tangible public goods. Freedom is strengthened when citizens can point to something they built together.

Rana might respond that all this risks nostalgia for civil society while ignoring structural power. That is a fair warning. Civic associations can be exclusionary, parochial, racist, patriarchal, or captured by local elites. The small town is not automatically democratic. Neighborhood control can become NIMBYism. Parent groups can become vehicles for censorship. Churches can abuse authority. Nonprofits can depoliticize struggle. Associations need rights, equality norms, and broader public frameworks.

But the answer to bad civil society is better civil society, not reliance on national politics alone. The left sometimes speaks as if power exists only in the state and the economy. Yet culture is produced in repeated association. Trust is built through doing. People who have successfully organized a tenant association or restored a park may be more open to unions, public goods, and democratic reform because they have felt collective agency in their hands.

Rana’s anti-imperial point also has a civic dimension. Empire weakens local democracy by directing imagination outward toward enemies and upward toward commanders. It teaches citizens to identify with national power rather than local responsibility. A less militarized country could invest not only in social programs but in civic capacity: disaster preparedness, climate adaptation, public health corps, refugee welcome networks, and city-to-city international partnerships based on reciprocity rather than dominance.

The right has been effective at building freedom institutions of its own: gun clubs, homeschooling networks, churches, legal organizations, talk radio audiences, business associations, and local activist groups. Progressives sometimes mock these spaces, but they help produce a lived meaning of freedom. If the left wants a different freedom, it needs places where people can belong before they agree on everything, act before they master the theory, and experience obligation as empowering rather than punitive.

Freedom is learned in public. Not only in the grand public of Congress and courts, but in the small publics where voices shake, votes are counted, coffee is poured, minutes are taken, and neighbors decide what to do next. Rana’s call for institution building is right. Let us make sure we build at human scale too.

"To reclaim the concept of freedom from the right, the left must move beyond messaging and focus on the workplace as the primary site where people learn and practice collective self-rule through participatory labor organizing."

I read Aziz Rana’s essay less as a theory of political language than as a challenge to organizers. If freedom is going to mean collective self-rule, where exactly will people learn it? Not in a staff memo. Not in a social media campaign. Not in a candidate’s slogan. They will learn it, if they learn it at all, in places where they take risks with others and win something they could not win alone.

That is why the workplace must be central. Rana says this clearly, but it deserves even more emphasis. For most people, work is where unfreedom is most ordinary. The boss changes the schedule. The app changes the route. The customer is always right. The algorithm measures bathroom breaks. The company announces layoffs after stock buybacks. The hospital understaffs the floor and calls it efficiency. The school district demands miracles from teachers and gives them crowded classrooms. The university relies on adjuncts and graduate workers while speaking the language of enlightenment. America tells workers they are free because they can quit. But quitting without savings, health insurance, child care, or another job is often just a different name for desperation.

A union campaign changes the meaning of freedom because it changes the posture of the worker. People who have been trained to keep their heads down begin to talk. They map the workplace. They identify leaders. They test one another’s trust. They confront fear. They discover that the boss’s authority is not natural, even if it is legal. They may lose. Many do. But even losing can teach the first lesson of democratic life: the rules were written by someone, and they can be rewritten.

Rana is right that the right’s freedom language has been durable because it has been embedded in institutions and material experience. White settlers did not merely hear speeches about independence; many received land, legal status, and protection. Midcentury workers did not merely hear about opportunity; many experienced rising wages, homeownership, employer benefits, and a sense of household security, even though those gains were racially and gender uneven and tied to American power abroad. If the left wants people to associate freedom with solidarity, it must create comparable experiences. A good union contract is a better argument than a thousand op-eds.

But we should be concrete about the obstacles. The American labor law regime is designed to exhaust workers. Employers can hold captive-audience meetings, delay elections, retaliate with minimal penalties, misclassify workers, close stores, hire union-busting consultants, and drag out first contracts. The formal right to organize exists alongside an everyday right of employers to intimidate. This legal reality teaches workers a cynical lesson about democracy: you may have rights on paper, but power decides whether they matter.

That is why labor law reform is not a procedural concern; it is a freedom demand. Card check, meaningful penalties for retaliation, first-contract arbitration, sectoral bargaining, protection for secondary activity, and a real right to strike would alter the terrain on which Americans encounter authority. So would repealing or overriding “right to work” laws, which are brilliantly named instruments of anti-solidarity. They tell workers that freedom means enjoying the benefits of collective bargaining while refusing to support the collective institution that makes those benefits possible. It is the consumer model of citizenship brought into the shop floor.

Yet labor must also examine itself. If unions are to be schools of freedom, they cannot simply be service providers. Too many workers experience unions, when they have them, as distant offices that handle grievances and negotiate contracts behind closed doors. That model may deliver gains, but it does not build democratic capacity. A freedom-centered unionism must be participatory: open bargaining when possible, elected bargaining committees, contract campaigns, strike preparation, political education, member-to-member organizing, and real attention to divisions of race, gender, immigration status, and job classification. Workers become free together by practicing power together.

The most promising labor struggles today often point in this direction. Teachers have linked their working conditions to students’ learning conditions. Nurses have made staffing a patient-care issue. Logistics and warehouse workers have exposed the bodily cost of speedup. Autoworkers have challenged tiers and insisted that the transition to electric vehicles not become an excuse to degrade labor standards. Service workers have turned small shops into public symbols of dignity. These fights broaden freedom beyond wages without floating away from material life.

Still, I want to complicate Rana’s contrast between individualistic and solidaristic freedom. In organizing, people often join collective struggle for very personal reasons. They want weekends with their children. They want a knee injury to stop getting worse. They want to tell a supervisor no. They want enough money to move out, leave a bad relationship, pay rent, or go to school. Solidarity does not erase individual desire; it gives it a vehicle. Rana’s anti-imperial argument also has consequences for labor. American unions have not always been on the right side of empire. It means opposing trade regimes that protect capital mobility while treating labor rights as secondary. It means supporting migrant workers as workers, not as threats. It means refusing to let bosses use borders to divide the shop floor.

Immigration is a test. In many industries—agriculture, construction, food processing, domestic work, hospitality, delivery—immigrant labor sustains daily life. When workers lack legal status, employers gain a weapon. Deportability is a labor discipline system. A freedom politics must therefore include legalization, protection against retaliation, labor standards enforcement regardless of status, and paths to citizenship. But unions must also speak honestly to native-born workers who fear wage competition. The answer is not to dismiss them as xenophobes. The answer is to organize standards high enough that no worker can be used to undercut another.

The same applies to public goods. Universal health care would be one of the greatest pro-labor reforms imaginable because it would weaken employer control. Housing policy matters because workers cannot strike if they are one missed paycheck from eviction. Child care matters because meetings and picket lines are not accessible if parents are unsupported. Transportation matters because time is part of freedom. A labor politics that confines itself to the National Labor Relations Act will miss the ecosystem of dependency.

There is no substitute for the terrifying moment when workers go public and learn who stands with them. Freedom is not a brand. It is a capacity built through conflict.

If the left wants to reclaim freedom, it should start by asking every institution a simple question: who has a say here? In most workplaces, the answer is still: not the people doing the work. Until that changes, the right will continue to sell counterfeit freedom to people who know they are not free. The labor movement’s task is to make the genuine article visible, practical, and contagious.

Begin with the obvious. The single most powerful invocation of freedom in American politics today is the post-Dobbs reproductive freedom movement. The vocabulary that worked was explicitly the vocabulary of freedom: "freedom to decide," "freedom from government interference in private medical decisions," "the freedom to make our own choices." This was not a niche or marginal use of freedom-language. It was the most successful left invocation of freedom in American politics for at least a generation.

Rana's framework has trouble accounting for this. The reproductive freedom argument that has worked electorally is not primarily solidaristic; it is, in important ways, individualistic. It claims the right of the individual woman, in consultation with her doctor and her conscience, to make decisions about her own body without state intrusion. This is closer to the negative-liberty tradition Rana criticizes than to the positive-liberty, collective-self-rule tradition he champions. And it has worked precisely because it speaks the deep American vocabulary of bodily autonomy and freedom from government intrusion — a vocabulary that Rana attributes to settler individualism but that has now been turned, devastatingly, against the religious right.

This suggests that the right's monopoly on individualist freedom-talk is more fragile, and more recoverable, than Rana's structural account implies. The same vocabulary that the right deployed against the welfare state can be deployed against the religious right's attempts to legislate against women's bodies. The individualist tradition is not inherently right-coded; it depends on what the relevant threats to individual freedom are and who can credibly name them.

A second feminist complication. Rana's emphasis on the workplace as the central site for reconstituting freedom-as-self-rule reflects a long-standing labor tradition that has, historically, undervalued reproductive and care labor. The household, the daycare, the nursing home, the school — these are workplaces too, and they are workplaces where women, disproportionately women of color and immigrant women, perform the labor that makes all other economic activity possible. A workplace-centered freedom project that does not address care work, that does not include domestic workers and home health aides in its vision of labor democracy, that does not grapple with the unequal distribution of unpaid household labor, will reproduce some of the same exclusions that the settler tradition produced.

This is not a small point. The National Domestic Workers Alliance and related organizations have done extraordinary work in recent years to extend labor protections to workers historically excluded from the Wagner Act. The Care Economy organizing that Ai-jen Poo and others have led explicitly frames care work as a freedom issue — both for the workers who perform it and for the families who depend on it. None of this appears in Rana's essay. It should.

A third complication. The American history of women's movements is itself a contested terrain that does not map onto Rana's settler-imperial frame in any simple way. The early women's rights movement was deeply entangled with abolition (Stanton, Anthony, Mott emerged from abolitionist circles), and also with the racism that followed the Fifteenth Amendment's enfranchisement of Black men but not women. First-wave feminism was largely a project of white middle-class women, and its achievements (suffrage, professional access, property rights) were structured by class and racial exclusions. Second-wave feminism emerged partly out of women's experiences within New Left and civil rights organizations, where male leadership often replicated the very hierarchies the movements ostensibly opposed. Third-wave and intersectional feminism have grappled with the limits of universal claims about "women" and "women's freedom" in ways that have produced both productive complication and persistent fragmentation.

This history suggests that "reclaiming freedom" for the left will require more than building unions and demilitarizing foreign policy. It will require grappling with the specifically gendered structures of unfreedom: the ongoing crisis of intimate partner violence, the failure of the legal system to address sexual assault, the persistent wage gap, the rollback of reproductive rights, the targeting of trans youth, the affordability crisis in childcare, the impossibility of work-family balance for most working women. Each of these is a freedom issue. Each requires institutional transformation. None of them are captured by Rana's framework as he has presented it.

A fourth complication. The international dimension of Rana's argument — that domestic freedom rests on global conditions, and that anti-imperialism is essential to any project of domestic emancipation — looks different through a feminist lens. International feminist movements have long pointed out that American foreign policy is not just imperially destructive in general but is destructive in specifically gendered ways. The structural adjustment programs imposed by the IMF on Global South countries have disproportionately harmed women, who absorb the costs of cuts to public services through unpaid care labor. The migration patterns Rana mentions are themselves gendered, with women migrating into domestic and care work in wealthy countries while their own children are raised by other women in poorer ones (the "global care chains" Arlie Hochschild and others have documented). A feminist anti-imperialism would be richer than the largely race- and class-coded version Rana offers.

None of this is to dismiss Rana's essay. Its analytical resources are real, and many of its institutional recommendations are ones I support. But the omission of gender is not a small gap to be filled in by adding a sentence or two about reproductive rights. It is a structural absence that, if addressed, would alter both the diagnosis and the prescription. The freedom we most urgently need to reclaim may be the freedom currently being denied to women, queer people, trans youth, and care workers — and reclaiming it may require vocabularies and strategies that Rana's framework, as presently constituted, cannot adequately provide.

Aziz Rana's essay offers one of the most ambitious recent attempts to explain why "freedom" sounds American only when it sounds conservative — and what it would take to change that. His core claim is structural rather than rhetorical: the language of freedom in the United States has been bent toward a zero-sum, racialized, individualistic key because the material conditions of American life, from settler conquest to postwar hegemony, made that key seem credible to its beneficiaries. This is a powerful frame, and it usefully cuts against the easy liberal hope that the problem is mostly one of Democratic messaging. But I think Rana asks a single causal thread — empire — to bear more weight than it can support, and in doing so he understates both the institutional peculiarities of American politics and the genuine purchase of rhetorical struggle. A reconstructed left politics of freedom needs the institutional program he sketches, but also needs to be honest about what else is doing the work.

Start with the comparative puzzle. If imperial expansion produces individualistic, zero-sum accounts of liberty at home, we ought to find some version of the American syndrome in other modern empires. Britain ruled a quarter of the globe; France pursued an extraordinarily violent colonial project from Algeria to Indochina; the Netherlands, Belgium, and Portugal all extracted on a scale that decisively shaped their political economies. Yet each of those societies developed, in the twentieth century, a robust social-democratic vocabulary in which freedom and collective provision are not antonyms. The British defend the NHS in the language of liberty. French *laïcité* and the French welfare state were built by people perfectly comfortable describing public power as emancipatory. If empire reliably produced the American syndrome, the European postwar settlement should look more like ours. It doesn't. So something distinctively American is doing the heavy lifting, and empire is at most one ingredient.

A more candid story would have to add the constitutional inheritance. The Madisonian design — federalism, separation of powers, a Senate that overrepresents land, a judiciary with extraordinary policy reach, and weak, undisciplined parties — multiplies veto points and makes positive collective action expensive. The institutions that in Europe forged solidarity (programmatic mass parties, parliamentary majorities able to legislate sweeping settlements, encompassing union federations bargaining at the sectoral level) either never developed here or were dismantled early. In a system where it is much easier to block than to build, "freedom from" naturally outcompetes "freedom to," and the political economy of negative liberty thrives. Rana gestures at this when he calls for political reform — voting rights, depoliticized districting — but in his framing these are downstream remedies for an imperial pathology, when they are arguably upstream causes in their own right. The settler experience and the constitutional design reinforce each other, but they are not identical, and conflating them obscures what reform actually has to do.

The Piketty citation illustrates the cost of leaning too hard on empire. Rana writes that nineteenth-century American wealth was less concentrated than European wealth and that "the answer is fairly evident": the frontier. This is plausible but underdetermined. The relative absence of an entailed aristocracy, the early dismantling of primogeniture, partible inheritance, mass primary education, and an enormous flow of European immigrants who were poor on arrival are all part of why the U.S. wealth distribution looked the way it did. Land mattered, conquest mattered, slavery mattered horribly — but so did legal and demographic features that have no direct lineage in settler violence. Reducing "free white capitalism" to expropriation flattens a more textured story and, more importantly, makes the institutional remedies seem like a smaller lift than they are.

I want to push hardest, though, on Rana's near-dismissal of messaging. He says reclaiming freedom "is not chiefly about communication strategy or political messaging. The two New Deal generations were not only an institutional accomplishment; they were a rhetorical accomplishment, and the rhetoric did not merely follow the institutions but helped call them into being. Roosevelt's Four Freedoms speech in January 1941 enumerated a concept of freedom — freedom from want, freedom from fear — that reorganized the conceptual field. It made Social Security and the GI Bill and full-employment policy intelligible as freedom rather than as freedom's opposite. King's "I Have a Dream" and "Beyond Vietnam" did similar work. To say that this rhetoric mattered only because it was anchored in unions and churches and Black freedom institutions is true; to say that the institutions would have done the cultural work without the rhetoric is not. The Biden administration's recent and largely unsuccessful attempts to talk about industrial policy, abortion, and unions in the register of freedom show what happens when you try the rhetoric without the institutional anchor. Rana is right that the anchor is missing. But the rhetorical project is not a sideshow; it is one of the things you have to do well while you are rebuilding the anchor.

Where the essay is most persuasive is in its claim that 2023 is an unusually open moment. Here Rana could even be more optimistic than he allows himself to be. The UAW's Stand-Up Strike this fall, conducted in explicitly moral language about dignity and a fair life, won concessions thought impossible a year ago. Starbucks Workers United, the Amazon Labor Union, and the writers' and actors' strikes have made the language of collective self-rule legible to a generation that, on Rana's account, should have no experiential vocabulary for it. The post-Dobbs referenda in Kansas, Kentucky, Michigan, and Ohio show majorities mobilizing under the banner of freedom-as-bodily-self-rule, in red states, against a right that controls the formal "freedom" vocabulary. These are not yet a movement on the scale Rana wants, but they are not nothing, and they show that the institutional path he recommends is not merely hypothetical. People are walking it.

That makes the closing prescriptions more urgent, not less. The Protecting the Right to Organize Act, sectoral bargaining, automatic voter registration, an immigration framework that decriminalizes entry and recognizes the international economic causes of migration, a serious rebalancing of the security state against domestic provision — these are the right items on the list. I would add to them constitutional reform that Rana underplays: ending the filibuster, reforming or expanding the Court, ending partisan gerrymandering through federal standards, and admitting D.C. and (on its terms) Puerto Rico. Without those, the institutional ecosystem he wants cannot be sustained against a counter-majoritarian system that will keep producing minority rule.

Rana's deeper insight is that freedom is always defined against some specific form of domination people actually experience. The question for the left, then, is whether it can name today's dominations — at work, at the doctor's office, at the border, in the algorithmic dark — clearly enough that the answer sounds like liberation rather than like another set of obligations. The institutional work is the precondition. The naming is the work itself.

Messaging Is Not a Sideshow

Aziz Rana wants to convince us that the right's monopoly over "freedom talk" cannot be undone by communications strategy, that what is needed instead is a long march through institutions — unions, workplaces, voting systems, immigration regimes — that would make solidaristic freedom an everyday felt experience rather than a rhetorical claim. He is half right. But the half he gets wrong matters, and dismissing the work of language and persuasion as superficial threatens the very project he wants to build.

Begin with the historical record Rana invokes. The New Deal coalition did not assemble itself out of pre-existing institutional facts. Franklin Roosevelt's "Four Freedoms" speech in 1941 — freedom of speech, freedom of worship, freedom from want, freedom from fear — was not a description of conditions Americans already inhabited. It was a rhetorical act that named aspirations and gave a generation of Americans a vocabulary to demand them. The Wagner Act, Social Security, and the GI Bill came after, not before, the discursive reframing. The same is true of the civil rights movement Rana rightly celebrates. The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom did not emerge from already-thriving institutions of interracial solidarity; it summoned those institutions into being through extraordinary acts of public language. King's "I Have a Dream" was speech, not infrastructure. The Freedom Rides were performances, not policies.

Rana's framing — institution-building versus messaging — is a false dichotomy. Institutions do not build themselves, and they do not sustain themselves without stories that explain why they matter. The Protecting the Right to Organize Act he mentions has been stalled in the Senate for years not because workers fail to experience workplace domination, but because the political coalition needed to overcome a filibuster has not been assembled, and assembling it requires persuading marginal voters in marginal states that union freedom is their freedom too. That is a communications problem as much as an organizing one.

There is also a strategic question about audience. Rana writes as if the relevant task is to reach the disaffected working class through transformed daily experience. But American politics is decided at the margins, and at those margins are voters who have been told for forty years that "freedom" means low taxes, gun rights, and getting government off their backs. These voters do not work at sites of imminent unionization. Many of them are retired, or self-employed, or work in small businesses where collective bargaining is structurally impossible. For them, the only available route to a different account of freedom is rhetorical — through churches, talk radio, social media, family conversation, and yes, the speeches of politicians they may or may not vote for. Conceding the language to the right while waiting for institutional transformation to do the persuasive work amounts to unilateral disarmament.

Consider what Democratic politicians have actually done when they take freedom language seriously. Elizabeth Warren's stump speeches in 2019–2020 routinely framed antitrust, financial regulation, and child care as freedom issues. Bernie Sanders has done the same with Medicare for All. More recently, Vice President Harris has framed reproductive rights explicitly in the vocabulary of freedom, and Democratic candidates in 2022 successfully ran ads accusing Republicans of stripping women of their liberty. These were communications choices, made by political professionals, and they moved votes. The 2022 midterms — historically anomalous for a first-term president's party — suggest that the rhetorical battlefield is not lost; it is being fought on, with some success.

Rana might respond that such gains are fragile precisely because they are not anchored in transformed daily experience. Fair enough. But the inverse is also true: transformed daily experience without a vocabulary to interpret it produces, historically, not solidarity but resentment. The white steelworker quoted in Jefferson Cowie's Stayin' Alive whom Rana cites — "if what we lived through in the 1950s was not liberation, then liberation never happens" — inhabited a deeply unionized institutional environment. That experience did not automatically produce solidaristic freedom; it produced, by the late 1970s, Reagan Democrats. What made the difference was the story workers told themselves about what their unions and their prosperity meant, and the right won that story.

There is also a deeper philosophical point about how political identities form. Rana's model assumes a kind of materialist sequencing: change the institutions, and consciousness will follow. But the past half-century of political science suggests something more recursive. People interpret their material conditions through pre-existing narrative frames, and they often interpret identical conditions in opposite ways depending on which frame is culturally available. The same factory closure becomes, for one worker, evidence that capitalism has betrayed them; for another, evidence that immigrants and welfare queens are stealing what is rightfully theirs. The institutions did not determine the interpretation. The language did, working in conjunction with media ecosystems, religious affiliations, and partisan signals.

None of this is to dismiss Rana's institutional program. He is correct that a freedom politics without union density, voting rights, and demilitarized foreign policy is a freedom politics of pure exhortation. The PRO Act matters. So does Puerto Rican self-determination, so does decriminalizing border crossing, so does cutting the Pentagon budget. I would happily sign onto every concrete reform he names. The disagreement is about sequencing and emphasis. Rana treats persuasion as downstream; I think it is constitutive. The institutions he wants to build will not be built by people who lack a shared vocabulary for why they matter, and that vocabulary has to be spoken aloud, repeatedly, by candidates and journalists and organizers, before institutional change becomes politically achievable.

There is one more thing to say about the right's appropriation of freedom. Rana attributes it to structural conditions — settler expansion, then global primacy — that emboldened a zero-sum, racialized account. This is partly true but incomplete. The right's freedom talk also reflects deliberate, generational investment by conservative intellectuals, foundations, think tanks, and media operations. From the Mont Pelerin Society to the Federalist Society to talk radio to Fox News to the manufactured grievance machinery of the Tea Party and Trump, the right built an apparatus designed precisely to seize the language of liberty. That apparatus is institutional, yes, but its primary product is rhetoric. The left does not lack institutions in any absolute sense; it lacks an equivalently coordinated rhetorical apparatus committed to a different vision of freedom. Building that apparatus is messaging work, and it is urgent.

Reclaiming freedom, then, requires both the long institutional project Rana describes and a more aggressive, more immediate rhetorical struggle. To say "this is a matter of institution building, not communication strategy" is to set up a contrast that the historical record does not support. The civil rights movement, the New Deal, the suffragists, the abolitionists — all of them built institutions and spoke a language of freedom simultaneously, each reinforcing the other. We should do the same, and we should start now, in the speeches of the next campaign, not after the institutional revolution has already happened.

Recovering the Republican Tradition

Aziz Rana's essay would be strengthened by more explicit engagement with a particular philosophical tradition that has been quietly enjoying a revival in Anglo-American political theory over the past three decades: civic republicanism, in the sense developed by Quentin Skinner, Philip Pettit, and (in slightly different registers) Michael Sandel, Iseult Honohan, and Frank Lovett. Rana gestures toward this tradition without quite naming it. His core distinction — between freedom as "not being subject to someone else's arbitrary whims" and freedom as "experiencing independence and control over the basic conditions of one's life" — sounds very much like Pettit's distinction between freedom as non-domination and freedom as non-interference. Naming the tradition explicitly would, I think, strengthen Rana's argument and connect it to a body of philosophical work that gives it sharper edges.

The central republican insight is that liberty is undermined not only by actual interference but by exposure to the arbitrary will of another, whether or not that will is exercised against you. A slave whose master happens to be kind is not free; he is dominated, because the master could at any moment exercise his power differently, and the slave has no institutional recourse. Skinner's recovery of this conception in works like Liberty Before Liberalism (1998) showed that the neo-Roman tradition of liberty as non-domination was central to early modern republican thought and was systematically displaced by Hobbes and his successors, who narrowed liberty to non-interference. Pettit's Republicanism (1997) and subsequent work developed this into a contemporary political theory whose policy implications — strong democratic accountability, contestatory institutions, anti-corruption measures, protection against unaccountable private power — overlap substantially with Rana's program.

Why does this matter for Rana's argument? Because the republican tradition offers a vocabulary of freedom that is both indigenous to the American constitutional inheritance and explicitly committed to the institutional and structural commitments Rana wants to advance. American revolutionaries did not think of themselves as Lockean individualists; they thought of themselves as inheritors of a republican tradition stretching from Rome through Machiavelli, Harrington, and the English commonwealthmen. Their concept of liberty was self-government — the capacity of a free people to make their own laws, free from the domination of foreign or domestic tyranny. The transformation of this tradition into the negative-liberty individualism of nineteenth and twentieth century liberalism was a historical accomplishment, not a logical inevitability. Recovering the older meaning is not a foreign import; it is a return to the American republic's own founding self-understanding.

This recovery does work that Rana's settler-imperial framework alone cannot do. It allows the left to make the case for democratic and labor reforms in vocabulary that is recognizable to constitutionally-minded Americans, including conservatives. The argument for labor democracy, for instance, becomes: workers exposed to arbitrary employer power are dominated, even when individual employers happen to be benign. Liberty requires institutional protection against this domination, which is what unions provide. This is a freedom argument, not just a fairness argument, and it draws on resources older and deeper than the welfare state liberalism that the right has spent fifty years discrediting.

Similarly with antitrust. Concentrated corporate power dominates consumers, workers, suppliers, and communities even when particular firms behave reasonably. The republican tradition gives us vocabulary for naming this — the dispersal of power as a condition of liberty — that is much harder for opponents to dismiss as socialism. Senator Elizabeth Warren has occasionally drawn on this tradition; Tim Wu has written explicitly about the antimonopoly tradition as a freedom tradition. There is more here for Rana's project than he has yet drawn upon.

Republican freedom also bears specifically on Rana's foreign policy concerns. The classical republican tradition held that imperial overreach corrupts republican liberty at home — the very thesis Rana wants to defend. Polybius, Sallust, Machiavelli, Montesquieu, and the American antifederalists all worried about how empire abroad produces tyranny at home. This is not a left-wing thesis; it is a deeply conservative one, in the older sense of conserving the institutions of free self-government. Some of the most prescient critics of post-9/11 American foreign policy — Andrew Bacevich, for instance — have written explicitly in this republican tradition. Rana's anti-imperialism would gain coalition partners by being framed in this vocabulary.

Now for a friendly criticism. Rana sometimes blurs the distinction between two different things that republican theory carefully separates: the elimination of domination (a defensible and powerful ideal) and the achievement of full collective self-rule (a more ambitious and more contestable one). Pettit's contemporary republicanism has been careful to argue that non-domination does not require continuous active participation by all citizens in all decisions. It requires institutional structures — separation of powers, judicial review, contestatory procedures, transparency, accountable representation — that protect citizens against arbitrary power without demanding that they spend their lives in political meetings. This is a more politically viable program than the "continuous exercise in participation" Rana sometimes seems to call for, and it answers some of the libertarian objections to positive-liberty traditions.

The distinction matters because many Americans, given a choice, do not want to participate continuously in collective self-government, and a freedom politics that demands they do so will struggle to find them. What they do want is to live in a society where they are not at the mercy of arbitrary power — bosses who can fire them at will, landlords who can evict them on whim, insurance companies that can deny claims for opaque reasons, police who can stop them without cause, governments that can surveil them without restraint. A republican freedom politics oriented to dismantling these dominations is widely appealing across the political spectrum. A republican freedom politics that requires citizens to become full-time participants in collective decision-making is narrower in appeal.

Rana could have it both ways by drawing the distinction more carefully. The institutional reforms he wants — labor law modernization, voting reform, demilitarization, immigration reform — can be framed as reductions in domination without requiring continuous participatory exercise of self-rule. This makes the program more politically accessible without abandoning its emancipatory core. It also distinguishes the program from older socialist visions that fared poorly precisely because they seemed to require a citizen subject more politically engaged than most actually want to be.

Finally, the republican tradition has resources for thinking about the relationship between freedom and virtue that Rana mostly avoids. Classical republican thought held that liberty depends on civic virtue — on citizens disposed to common good rather than private interest, capable of self-restraint, attentive to the institutions that make their freedom possible. This is unfashionable language in contemporary leftist discourse, which prefers structural explanations. But it captures something true: institutions do not run themselves, and a republic populated by citizens uninterested in the conditions of their own freedom will lose that freedom regardless of its institutional design. The right has had some success in claiming the language of virtue and responsibility; the left has largely surrendered it. Recovering republican vocabulary would help the left contest this ground too.

Rana's essay is excellent. Adding civic republicanism to its analytical toolkit would make it better still.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the structural roots of the right's capture of 'freedom,' his focus on long-term institution-building overlooks the immediate power of narrative and the American left's own robust, historical tradition of using freedom talk to drive social change."

Aziz Rana’s essay marshals an impressive historical arsenal to argue that the right’s capture of freedom talk is rooted in the material realities of settler colonialism and empire, and that reclaiming it requires institution-building, not just rhetorical strategy. It is a compelling narrative, but it rests on a historical simplification that ultimately constrains its political imagination. The story Rana tells is one of long-term structural determination, in which the settler-imperial experience consistently produced a zero-sum, exclusionary freedom, with only brief windows of inversion. But the actual history of the American left is a history of robust, enduring, and creative appropriation of the language of freedom—not as a fragile anomaly, but as a central thread of American radicalism. By underplaying this tradition, Rana risks reinforcing the very pessimism among left-liberals that he hopes to counter, and he proposes a laborious institutional detour when what the moment demands is a more confident and accessible revival of a freedom talk that the left once deployed with world-changing effect.

The freedom tradition on the American left is not limited to the interwar period Rana highlights. It stretches from the abolitionist cry of “Am I Not a Woman and a Sister?” through the post-Civil War labor slogan “Eight hours for work, eight hours for rest, eight hours for what we will,” to the Industrial Workers of the World’s vision of “building a new world in the shell of the old.” None of these were merely defensive; they were affirmative, everyday articulations of freedom as the capacity to control one’s time, body, and labor. The civil rights movement’s “freedom songs” and the feminist chant “my body, my choice” are not footnotes; they are the most resonant examples of freedom talk in American memory. When Bernie Sanders, a democratic socialist, rallies millions with the phrase “freedom is not a choice between the party of the oligarchs and the party of the plutocrats,” he is tapping into a deep left vein, not conjuring a new one. Rana himself quotes Jefferson Cowie’s steelworker son and acknowledges that the mid-century labor movement delivered a genuine experience of liberation. But he treats this as a product of empire’s rents rather than as a political accomplishment won by decades of struggle—a framing that diminishes the agency of the movements that made it happen.

The problem is that Rana’s structural determinism leads him to underestimate the power of narrative and movement-building in shifting cultural meanings. He is right that institutions are crucial, but the civil rights movement did not wait for institutional change before it changed the meaning of freedom in American life; it used direct action, speeches, and moral confrontation to redefine freedom in the public mind, which then created the political pressure for institutional reforms like the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act. The movement’s “freedom rides” and “freedom schools” were themselves institution-building, but they were also deeply symbolic actions designed to dramatize a new vision of freedom before the law had caught up. The lesson is not that narrative work is futile without prior institutional transformation, but that institutions and narratives co-evolve, often with narrative leading the way.

The left’s current predicament, then, is not that it lacks the institutional base to make a solidaristic freedom resonate. It is that it has, for decades, ceded the very act of telling a compelling freedom story. Rana’s call for union-building, electoral reform, and anti-imperial policy is important. But these are long-term projects that, in the absence of a powerful and emotionally gripping freedom narrative, will struggle to attract the mass support needed to enact them. People need a story that connects their immediate struggles to a larger vision of liberation, and that story cannot wait for the slow accumulation of institutional change. It must be told now, in public, with passion and clarity.

The right’s current dominance of freedom talk is not, as Rana implies, an inevitable cultural reflex of empire. It is the product of a deliberate, well-funded, five-decade project by conservative intellectuals and political strategists who understood that language matters. The left once had a comparable infrastructure: a labor press, radical newspapers, folk music, and a network of schools and camps that inculcated an alternative vision. The neoliberal era dismantled much of that, but the digital age has created new possibilities for narrative dissemination. Movements like #BlackLivesMatter and the Fight for $15 have shown that it is possible, with relatively few resources, to reframe public debate around freedom from state violence and economic precarity. These are not yet the “organic” cultural presence Rana wants, but they are more immediate and scalable than the generation-long task of rebuilding union density.

Rana’s anti-imperialism is also, in this context, an unnecessarily heavy cudgel for a domestic narrative project. He is right that an honest account of American freedom must reckon with empire, but the primary audience for a left freedom narrative is not a historical tribunal; it is the millions of Americans who feel unfree in their daily lives. Connecting their unfreedom to the Pentagon budget is a complex analytical move that even the most engaged activists struggle to hold in their heads while also worrying about rent and healthcare. The left’s history teaches a more inductive method: start with the concrete unfreedom people experience—the boss who schedules you for 39 hours to avoid benefits, the medical debt that dictates your life choices—and build outward toward a systemic critique. That is how the Freedom Budget framed its demands: not as a philosophical treatise on empire, but as a practical plan to secure the material preconditions of liberty for all, which then implied a challenge to militarism because the money had to come from somewhere.

None of this is to dismiss Rana’s central challenge. Institution-building is vital. But the best institution-builder is a movement that has already captured the public’s moral imagination, and that requires a powerful story. The left should not shy away from waging a full-throated battle for the word “freedom” precisely because it has the superior claim. The right offers a freedom that is, as Rana brilliantly shows, hollowed out for all but a few. The left can offer a freedom that is richer: the freedom to have a say in the decisions that affect your life, to live without the constant fear of destitution, to love whom you love, to move without being treated as a criminal. That is a story Americans are ready to hear, and it need not wait for the revolution. The left’s own history demonstrates that when it speaks of freedom, it speaks in a tongue people understand. The task is not to invent a new language, but to rediscover the one that cadres once sang on picket lines and that Ella Baker whispered in church basements. That language is not dead; it is merely waiting to be spoken again.
