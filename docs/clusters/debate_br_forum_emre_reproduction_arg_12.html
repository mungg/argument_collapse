"While the lead essay correctly critiques the coercive ideology of 'natural' reproduction, feminism should not entirely abandon the concept of nature, but rather reclaim it as a language of embodiment and humility that resists the consumerist fantasy of total technological control."

I share the lead essay’s impatience with the cult of the natural. Too many women have been told that pain is ennobling, exhaustion is maternal, breastfeeding is moral, and childbirth without intervention is the measure of authentic womanhood. These claims deserve feminist suspicion. Yet I want to defend a word that the essay often treats as irredeemable: nature. Not the nature invoked to shame women or exclude queer families, but nature as a language of embodiment, limitation, gratitude, and relationship. Feminism should criticize naturalism without surrendering the insight that bodies matter and that not every limit is an enemy.

There is a difference between saying “women are naturally destined to bear children” and saying “pregnancy is a bodily experience that some people find meaningful.” The first is coercive ideology. The second is a description of human variety. Some women experience pregnancy as alien invasion; others experience it as wonder; many experience it as both. Some find childbirth traumatic; others find it empowering; many find it painful, boring, frightening, and beautiful in alternating waves. A feminist politics should make room for all of these accounts. It should not replace compulsory reverence for pregnancy with compulsory disenchantment.

The lead essay quotes Firestone’s joke about childbirth as “shitting a pumpkin,” and the joke lands because it punctures sentimental nonsense. But there is a danger in allowing anti-romanticism to become its own orthodoxy. If a woman says that giving birth changed her sense of her body, or that breastfeeding was a joy, or that she wanted to conceive through sex with her husband because that mattered to her, she is not necessarily a dupe of patriarchy. She may be speaking from an experience as real as S’s injections or B’s miscarriage. The feminist problem is not that some people value embodied reproduction. The problem is that their valuation has been universalized and weaponized.

The essay is most persuasive when it argues that “natural” reproduction is never unassisted. Marriage law, hospitals, prenatal care, family money, and social recognition all assist it. But from the fact that all reproduction is assisted, it does not follow that all modes of reproduction are experientially or morally identical. The distinctions may not justify hierarchy, but they still matter. Gestating a child is different from contributing genetic material. Breastfeeding is different from bottle-feeding. Adoption is different from birth. IVF is different from intercourse. Surrogacy is different from pregnancy for oneself. These differences should not become rankings of legitimacy, but neither should they disappear under a single theory of mediation.

Why insist on this? Because human beings often make meaning through limits and givenness. A child is not a product we design but a person we receive. Even in unassisted conception, parents discover rather than control who arrives. Assisted reproduction can intensify the fantasy of control: select the best embryo, time the transfer, screen for abnormalities, preserve options, optimize the future. Of course much of this control is illusory, and people undergoing IVF know uncertainty intimately. Still, a culture already prone to consumer choice may turn reproductive technology into another domain of management. The language of nature, at its best, can remind us that children exceed our plans.

This is not a conservative argument against ART. Infertility is a real grief. Queer and trans people should not be denied family because their bodies do not fit heterosexual assumptions. Single people can be devoted parents. Medical help in conception is not an affront to creation, whether one thinks in secular or religious terms. Most religious traditions that value family have also found ways to accept medicine. Glasses assist sight; insulin assists metabolism; incubators assist premature infants. Assistance is not hubris simply because it is technological.

But technology should be governed by humility. The question is not only “Can this help someone have the child they desire?” It is also “What habits of heart does this encourage?” Does it teach us to welcome children unconditionally, or to evaluate them as successes and failures? Does it honor the bodies of egg donors and surrogates, or use them? Does it make room for disability, or treat disability as a preventable defect? Does it strengthen communities of care, or intensify private striving? Does it liberate women from forced roles, or ask them to undergo ever more procedures to satisfy unchanged social expectations?

Here I find the lead essay’s critique of corporate egg freezing especially important but perhaps understated. A company that pays for egg freezing may be doing something useful for particular employees. But if the same company does not provide generous parental leave, humane hours, and childcare, its assistance carries a message: adapt your fertility to our timetable. This is not liberation from nature; it is submission to another master. Natural limits can oppress, but so can institutions that refuse to recognize limits.

A humane reproductive politics would affirm three freedoms together: the freedom to accept bodily processes, the freedom to alter them, and the freedom to refuse them. A woman who wants an epidural should receive one without shame. A woman who wants an unmedicated birth should not be mocked as a mystic. A person who wants IVF should have fair access. A person who wants to stop fertility treatment should not be told she has given up. A trans person should receive fertility counseling without assumptions. A mother who cannot breastfeed should be supported; a mother who loves breastfeeding should not be told her pleasure is false consciousness.

This pluralism requires better social supports. Many appeals to nature become oppressive because the work attached to “natural” practices is unsupported. Breastfeeding on demand is nearly impossible without paid leave, workplace accommodations, and help at home. Natural childbirth is dangerous without emergency medical care nearby. Parenting according to a child’s rhythms is a luxury without flexible work. If society truly valued embodied care, it would not leave caregivers isolated. It would provide time, money, and community. The fact that it does not suggests that much rhetoric of nature is cheap praise for costly labor.

The lead essay’s narratives show people caught between desire and politics. I would add that they are also caught between control and surrender. Reproduction always involves both. The most technologically assisted pregnancy still depends on implantation, growth, birth, and a child’s unknowable future. The least technologically assisted pregnancy still depends on social recognition, nutrition, shelter, and care. We should not pretend that technology abolishes vulnerability, nor that nature guarantees wisdom.

The religious traditions I know best speak of children as gifts. That language can be misused to deny abortion, stigmatize infertility treatment, or demand maternal sacrifice. But it can also resist commodification. A gift is not earned, optimized, or possessed absolutely. A gift creates obligation. To call a child a gift is to say that parents and communities receive someone whose dignity does not depend on fulfilling adult fantasies. Secular feminism need not adopt this theology, but it might find a parallel in the insistence that children are subjects, not projects.

So yes: all reproduction is assisted. Let us expose the invisible supports that make some families seem natural and others artificial. Let us end discrimination against queer, trans, single, disabled, and infertile people. Let us provide health care, leave, and legal recognition. Let us reject the sanctification of women’s pain. But let us also preserve a language for the body as more than raw material for choice. The answer to coercive nature is not limitless mastery. It is reverent freedom: freedom to work with our bodies, freedom to seek help when they do not do what we need, and freedom to welcome children without pretending we made them alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Feminist politics should accommodate a pluralism of reproductive experiences, acknowledging that embodied processes like pregnancy or breastfeeding can be personally meaningful without being patriarchally mandated.
  2. 02The fact that all reproduction is assisted does not mean all methods are experientially identical, and these distinctions matter for how individuals make meaning of their lives.
  3. 03The language of nature serves as a necessary reminder that children are independent subjects to be received as gifts rather than products to be optimized through technological management.
  4. 04Reproductive technologies like corporate egg freezing can become new forms of oppression when they force individuals to adapt their biology to capitalist demands rather than providing genuine social support.
  5. 05A humane reproductive politics must simultaneously defend the freedom to accept bodily processes, the freedom to alter them, and the freedom to refuse them.
  6. 06The focus should shift from achieving limitless mastery over the body to creating social structures—like universal healthcare and paid leave—that support the labor of care regardless of how a child is conceived.
