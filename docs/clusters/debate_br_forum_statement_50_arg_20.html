"While Tom Hayden celebrates the domestic successes of participatory democracy, he ignores the catastrophic failure of the movement to challenge the permanent national security state and the expansion of unaccountable executive war powers."

In commemorating the fiftieth anniversary of the Port Huron Statement, Tom Hayden offers an elegant, sweeping vision of participatory democracy, drawing a straight line from the civil rights and anti-war movements of the 1960s to the protests of today. His formulation of an "inside-outside" strategy—where street-level activism pushes pragmatic politicians to enact lasting change—is a familiar and comforting narrative for American progressives. However, while Hayden rightfully celebrates domestic victories like the Americans with Disabilities Act and the rise of the undocumented youth movement, his essay displays a glaring and catastrophic silence on the issue that originally catalyzed much of the New Left’s radicalism: American imperialism and the permanent national security state.

When the Students for a Democratic Society drafted the Port Huron Statement in 1962, they were writing in the shadow of the Cold War, the nuclear arms race, and a military-industrial complex that was beginning to exert a stranglehold over American society. Their call for participatory democracy was not just about domestic equality; it was a desperate plea to democratize foreign policy, to wrest control of the mechanisms of life and death away from unaccountable generals, intelligence agencies, and defense contractors.

Today, looking at the political landscape of April 2012, one must ask: where is the participatory democracy in our foreign policy?

Hayden highlights the "historic election of 2008" and the upswell of youth commitment to Barack Obama as a triumph of participatory democracy. Yet, this same administration has overseen an unprecedented expansion of executive war powers, completely insulated from democratic oversight or public participation. The Obama administration currently operates a global, covert drone assassination program, utilizing "kill lists" to execute individuals—including American citizens—without indictment, trial, or due process. This is the absolute antithesis of participatory democracy; it is the ultimate expression of an imperial presidency acting in total secrecy.

Furthermore, the United States remains bogged down in a decade-long war in Afghanistan, Guantanamo Bay remains open despite clear campaign promises to close it, and the President recently signed the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA), which contains provisions allowing for the indefinite military detention of individuals without trial.

How can we celebrate a "greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history" when the machinery of war and surveillance operates entirely beyond the reach of the electorate? Hayden vaguely mentions "efforts to extend and deepen public participation in foreign policy," but provides no examples, likely because there are none to be found. The anti-war movement, which was a massive "outside" force during the Bush administration, has largely demobilized, sedated by the presence of a Democrat in the White House. The "inside-outside" strategy has proven to be a fatal trap for anti-imperialists, as partisan loyalty has trumped principled opposition to the war machine.

Hayden asserts that the establishment embraces reforms when they realize it is better to concede on issues than allow the popular surge to become radical. But the national security establishment makes no such concessions. It is remarkably impervious to street protests. Millions marched globally in 2003 against the invasion of Iraq, arguably the largest instance of participatory democracy in human history, and the war was launched anyway, based on fabricated intelligence. The military-industrial complex that Port Huron warned us about has evolved into a bipartisan consensus that views public input on foreign policy as an irrelevant nuisance.

Even the Occupy Movement, which Hayden praises, focused almost entirely on domestic economic inequality, largely ignoring the trillions of dollars diverted from domestic needs into the black hole of the global war on terror. We cannot have a functioning participatory democracy at home while maintaining a sprawling empire abroad. The two are mutually exclusive. The militarization of our foreign policy inevitably bleeds into the militarization of our domestic police forces, as witnessed by the armored vehicles and paramilitary tactics used to crush the very Occupy camps Hayden celebrates.

The true, unfinished legacy of the Port Huron Statement is not the pursuit of consensus in neighborhood assemblies or the proliferation of independent media, as vital as those things are. Its most urgent, yet neglected, mandate is the dismantling of the permanent war economy. If participatory democracy is to mean anything in the twenty-first century, it must confront the bipartisan consensus that sustains perpetual war.

It is easy to celebrate the courage of those who went to jail to resist the Vietnam War in hindsight. It is much harder to apply that same critical lens to the power structures of the present, especially when they are commanded by politicians we helped elect. If we are to honor the spirit of the 1960s, we must stop pretending that domestic reforms alone equate to a functioning democracy, and we must boldly challenge the empire that operates in our name, using our tax dollars, entirely without our consent.
