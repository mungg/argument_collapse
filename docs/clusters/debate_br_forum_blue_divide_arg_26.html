"The Democratic Party's economic shift under Biden was driven less by internal progressive evolution than by a post-neoliberal turn necessitated by global security imperatives and the collapse of the globalization consensus."

Hacker and Pierson have written a powerful account of the Democratic Party’s economic turn, but their explanation is too domesticated. They treat the Biden agenda mainly as a product of intra-party evolution: progressive pressure, the lessons of the Great Recession, the shock of COVID-19, the rise of policy entrepreneurs, and the need to hold together a U-shaped metropolitan coalition. All of that matters. But it leaves out one of the central facts of the period: the return of great-power competition and the collapse of the old globalization consensus.

The Democrats did not rediscover industrial policy in a vacuum. They rediscovered it in a world in which the United States had become anxious about China’s technological rise, semiconductor dependence, fragile supply chains, deindustrialization, energy security, and the geopolitical consequences of letting strategic production move offshore. The political economy of the Biden years cannot be understood without that shift. The CHIPS and Science Act, the domestic content rules, the battery supply chain provisions, the emphasis on manufacturing, the more skeptical approach to trade, and the fusion of climate policy with industrial strategy all reflect a world in which national security and economic policy have merged.

This does not refute Hacker and Pierson’s thesis, but it changes its meaning. The Biden agenda was not simply a leftward economic turn. It was also a nationalist turn, or at least a post-neoliberal turn shaped by security imperatives. That distinction matters because security politics can make policies possible that egalitarian politics alone cannot.

For decades, many Democrats accepted the broad premises of liberal globalization: free trade, financial integration, efficiency, consumer welfare, and the idea that distributional harms could be compensated after the fact. The compensation rarely arrived, and the political backlash did. But the decisive break with that model did not come only because progressives won arguments inside the party. It came because elites across the political spectrum began to see the old model as strategically dangerous. The pandemic exposed supply-chain fragility. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine underscored the geopolitical importance of energy and industrial capacity. China’s rise made semiconductors, artificial intelligence, telecommunications, batteries, and critical minerals matters of national power.

In that context, “industrial policy” stopped sounding like a left-wing fantasy and started sounding like prudence. The same suburban professionals who might have balked at old-style redistribution could support public investment in chips, clean energy, infrastructure, and scientific research because these programs could be framed as competitiveness, resilience, and national renewal. The politics of “make stuff in America again” are not identical to the politics of “tax the rich to fund social rights.” The former can attract support from business, labor, security hawks, climate advocates, and regional development interests. The latter depends more directly on class conflict.

This distinction helps explain why the surviving pieces of the Biden agenda look the way they do. The expanded child tax credit expired. Paid leave died. Free community college died. Much of the care agenda died. But climate industrial policy, infrastructure, semiconductor subsidies, and research spending survived. That pattern is not random. Programs that could be integrated into investment, competitiveness, and supply-chain resilience had a better chance than programs that more straightforwardly expanded the welfare state. The Inflation Reduction Act is an enormous climate law, but it is also an industrial policy law. Its structure relies heavily on tax credits, private investment incentives, domestic production, and place-based manufacturing. It is more Hamilton than Beveridge.

Hacker and Pierson acknowledge that much of Build Back Better failed, but they still treat the near miss as evidence of a broad Democratic transformation. I would put more weight on the distinction between what Democrats proposed and what the governing system accepted. The party’s aspirations may have moved left, but the enacted settlement was more selective: green investment, infrastructure, chips, modest health care subsidies, some drug pricing reforms, and limited corporate taxation. That is significant, but it is not the same as a durable social-democratic breakthrough.

The difference matters for the party’s future. If the Democrats’ economic turn depends heavily on national security and climate investment, it may be more durable in some areas and weaker in others. The politics of clean energy manufacturing in Georgia, Arizona, Michigan, and Ohio may outlast a narrow Democratic trifecta because those investments create local stakeholders, business constituencies, and jobs. Republican officials may denounce the laws while welcoming the factories. That is how industrial policy can build power: by creating material facts on the ground.

But the politics of care, child poverty, and income support remain far more vulnerable. They lack the same alliance with national security. They are easier to attack as spending. They are more dependent on federal transfers. They also require a more direct confrontation with gender and class hierarchies. The United States has long tolerated underpaid care work because the costs are hidden inside families and imposed on women, immigrants, and workers of color. Rebuilding the care economy would require not merely a subsidy but a moral and political revaluation of labor that the market has treated as cheap. That is a harder coalition to assemble than one built around semiconductor fabs and electric vehicle batteries.

The China omission also matters because it complicates the authors’ account of affluent suburban voters. Many high-income Democratic voters work in sectors deeply tied to global integration: technology, finance, consulting, higher education, medicine, law, and multinational business services. Yet many are also national-security liberals, climate liberals, or institutionalists who now accept a more active state. Their support for Bidenomics may rest less on redistributive solidarity than on a belief that the United States needs competent public investment to compete and survive. That is still a meaningful change from the Clinton era, but it is not necessarily a left-populist one.

Indeed, the Biden administration’s economic program contains several strands that sit uneasily together. There is a Sanders-Warren strand focused on inequality, labor power, antitrust, and social rights. There is a climate strand focused on decarbonization. There is a national-development strand focused on infrastructure, manufacturing, and regional revival. There is a China-competition strand focused on strategic industries. There is also a technocratic growth strand that sees public investment as a way to crowd in private capital. These factions overlapped in 2021 and 2022, but they are not the same. Their alliance may be powerful; it may also fracture.

Consider trade. Biden has largely maintained Trump’s break with the old free-trade consensus, though with different rhetoric and alliances. That shift appeals to some union voters and industrial communities. It also reflects concern about China. But protection and domestic content rules can raise costs, anger trading partners, and complicate climate goals if they slow deployment. A purely egalitarian analysis might celebrate the return of manufacturing jobs; a climate analysis might worry about bottlenecks; a geopolitical analysis might prioritize secure supply chains; a consumer-welfare analysis might object to higher prices. The “new blue” agenda is not one thing.

Inflation exposed another fault line. Hacker and Pierson note that Manchin invoked inflation to shrink the agenda. They are right that inflation became a convenient weapon against social spending, and many Build Back Better provisions would not have been major near-term inflation drivers. But inflation was not imaginary. By 2021 and 2022, voters were experiencing sharp price increases in food, energy, housing, and other essentials. The politics of abundance collided with the politics of scarcity. A party promising activist government had to show not only that it could distribute money, but that it could deliver capacity: homes, power lines, child care slots, heat pumps, chips, ports, workers, and permits.

This is where the domestic and geopolitical stories converge. The United States has spent decades underinvesting in public capacity while relying on global markets and private actors to deliver abundance. Reversing that is not just a matter of passing spending bills. It requires state competence. It requires procurement reform, permitting capacity, trained workforces, regional planning, and institutions that can coordinate investment. If Democrats promise industrial transformation and then cannot build transmission lines, housing, transit, or clean energy projects quickly enough, the new economic agenda will disappoint.

The authors are right to resist the lazy claim that Democrats care only about culture. The Biden years plainly disprove that. But to understand the party’s transformation, we need a broader frame: not just redistribution within metro America, but the reorganization of American capitalism under conditions of climate crisis, geopolitical rivalry, and supply-chain insecurity. The Democratic Party moved left partly because progressives gained influence. It moved toward industrial policy because the world changed.

The future of the new blue divide will depend on which strand dominates. If the agenda becomes primarily a subsidy program for favored industries, it may produce jobs and emissions reductions but leave inequality and care work largely untouched. If it becomes a serious social-democratic project, it must find a way to revive the child tax credit, paid leave, child care, housing reform, and labor organization. If it becomes a national-security project, it may attract bipartisan rhetorical support while narrowing the moral horizon.

Biden’s economic turn is real. But its deepest source may be less the triumph of the Brahmin left over neoliberalism than the end of the world in which neoliberalism seemed plausible. China, COVID-19, climate change, and war have forced American elites to rediscover the state. The question is whether that rediscovered state will be used merely to compete—or also to care.
