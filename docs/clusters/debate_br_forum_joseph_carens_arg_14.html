"Amnesty for long-settled unauthorized migrants should be framed as a tool for proactive civic integration and assimilation rather than a moral right based solely on the passage of time."

Joseph Carens makes a powerful humanitarian case, but he does not fully confront the cultural dimension of immigration. In polite circles, any mention of culture quickly invites accusations of nativism. That is unfortunate, because liberal democracies are not merely labor markets or rights-protecting machines. They are communities with languages, civic habits, public memories, and shared institutions. If amnesty is to be defended successfully, its advocates must explain how legalization will strengthen, rather than weaken, civic integration.

I support a conditional amnesty for long-settled unauthorized migrants. But I support it because it can promote assimilation, not because social membership automatically emerges from residence alone. Living in a place matters, but membership also involves learning how to participate in its civic life. A person may work, pay taxes, and raise children here while remaining linguistically isolated, politically alienated, or dependent on ethnic enclaves. That is not a reason to deport him after many years. It is a reason to take integration seriously.

The United States has historically been more successful at incorporating immigrants than many European countries. Birthright citizenship, public schools, a flexible national identity, and a strong labor market have all helped. The children and grandchildren of immigrants usually become English-speaking Americans with hyphenated identities. This is one reason the American debate should not imitate European panic about immigrant difference. Still, success is not automatic. It depends on institutions that encourage common citizenship.

Carens’s essay emphasizes time, but time can produce parallel lives as well as shared ones. If unauthorized status keeps people afraid of public institutions, they may avoid police, schools beyond the minimum, banks, courts, and civic organizations. Legalization can help break that isolation. A person who no longer fears deportation is more likely to report crime, invest in education, improve English, start a business, and participate openly. In that sense amnesty is not a retreat from assimilation; it is a precondition for it.

Yet legalization should come with expectations. English acquisition should be encouraged, not as a weapon against immigrants but as an instrument of equality. Without English, workers are easier to exploit, parents struggle to engage schools, and citizens cannot deliberate together. Civics education should be part of the path to permanent residence. Applicants should learn basic constitutional principles, rights, responsibilities, and the history of American immigration, including its failures. This should not be a humiliating test designed to exclude. It should be an invitation into shared citizenship.

Critics of amnesty often say that unauthorized migrants have shown disrespect for American law. Sometimes this charge is made hypocritically by people who gladly benefit from immigrant labor. But the rule-of-law concern is not meaningless. A legalization program should therefore be public and orderly. Applicants should register, undergo background checks, pay reasonable fines or fees scaled to income, settle tax obligations where possible, and demonstrate a commitment to lawful conduct. These requirements would not erase the past, but they would mark the transition from shadow residence to civic membership.

Carens worries, reasonably, that too much discretion will invite discrimination. But a purely time-based rule may fail to reassure the public that membership is more than endurance. The answer is not broad bureaucratic judgment about whether someone “belongs.” The answer is clear, objective requirements: years of residence, no serious criminal convictions, basic English progress over time, tax compliance, and civic instruction. Such a system can be firm without being arbitrary.

Family unity should be central. Immigrant integration happens through families and schools more than through speeches. When parents are unauthorized, children—often U.S. citizens—learn fear instead of confidence. Legalization would allow parents to engage more fully with teachers, health providers, and local institutions. It would also reduce the formation of a hereditary underclass in which children inherit the insecurity of their parents even when citizenship law formally protects some of them.

The cultural argument also cuts against mass deportation. Removing millions of people who have lived here for years would not defend American identity. It would brutalize it. Door-to-door enforcement, workplace raids, family separation, and detention camps would teach citizens to accept cruelty as the price of purity. A nation confident in its civic culture should not fear incorporating those who have already built lives here.

At the same time, future immigration policy must be credible. Repeated amnesties without functioning legal channels and enforcement would undermine public trust. The 1986 experience looms large because Americans were promised that legalization would be followed by control. That promise was not kept. Any new amnesty must be paired with a realistic system for future workers, secure identification, employer accountability, and humane border enforcement. Cultural confidence depends partly on the belief that democratic decisions matter.

Carens is correct that many unauthorized migrants had no lawful path. If our economy needs workers, we should create legal routes that do not require illegality as the first step toward belonging. Otherwise we teach newcomers that American law is something to navigate around rather than participate in. A well-designed immigration system would invite people openly, protect them while here, and offer citizenship to those who settle permanently.

The goal should be neither exclusion nor multicultural fragmentation, but civic incorporation. Amnesty can serve that goal if it is framed honestly: not as forgiveness without expectations, and not as punishment disguised as mercy, but as a process by which people already living among us become fully visible members of the republic.

Carens gives us the moral foundation: time and rootedness matter. I would add the civic foundation: membership must be cultivated. Legalization should be accompanied by English learning, civics, labor rights, family stability, and public investment in integration. Done this way, amnesty would not dilute American citizenship. It would renew the old democratic promise that people from elsewhere can become, in fact and in law, part of “we the people.”

  6. 06Amnesty must be paired with credible future enforcement and functional legal immigration channels to maintain the democratic integrity of the immigration system.
