"The primary obstacle to Democratic success is not candidate positioning or messaging, but a structural disadvantage in the media and information environment that systematically favors the right."

What the Authors Get Right About the Information Environment, and What They Miss

The strongest part of Bonica and Grumbach's essay is the argument that elections have nationalized in ways that make traditional candidate positioning largely irrelevant. They are right that AOC and Jared Golden rise and fall with the same partisan tides, that voters increasingly choose Team Red or Team Blue rather than evaluating individual candidates, and that the "all politics is local" frame is no longer accurate. This is well-documented in political science and worth taking seriously.

But the authors do not follow through on the implications of their own argument. If elections are now national referenda mediated by an information environment that overwhelms individual candidate signals, then the central strategic question is not what candidates should do but how the information environment itself can be shaped. And on this question—the central question—the essay has very little to say.

Consider the actual mechanism by which "national tides" reach voters. Voters do not receive raw partisan information directly from Washington. They receive it through specific media channels: cable news, social media platforms, podcasts, local television (declining but still significant in some markets), partisan websites, and increasingly, algorithmic content streams curated by platforms whose incentives have nothing to do with civic deliberation. The character of these channels has changed dramatically over the last decade in ways that systematically advantage the right.

The right has built a media ecosystem—Fox News, the broader Sinclair empire, the major right-wing podcast network anchored by Rogan, Carlson, and the Daily Wire ecosystem, the political talk-radio infrastructure that has been operating since the 1990s, the relatively coordinated activist Twitter/X presence amplified by the platform's owner—that the left has nothing approaching. The Democratic-aligned media environment is fractured, undercapitalized, and structurally less able to coordinate national messaging. MSNBC reaches a fraction of Fox's audience. There is no left-wing equivalent of the Daily Wire. The most successful left-leaning podcasts (Pod Save America, Chapo, Klein's show) have audiences orders of magnitude smaller than Rogan's.

This asymmetry has electoral consequences that swamp anything the authors discuss. When the authors note that messaging is "drowned out by partisan tides," what they are describing is the operation of a media environment that systematically delivers right-wing framings to a much larger audience than left-wing framings reach. The 2024 election was decided in part because, in the closing months, the right-wing media ecosystem could coordinate sustained attacks on Harris on a small number of themes (the border, "she/her" pronoun videos, inflation), while no equivalent coordinated attack on Trump existed in the media diet of low-engagement voters.

The authors' anti-corruption frame, however appealing on the merits, runs into this structural problem immediately. A Democratic anti-corruption message has to travel through a media environment in which (a) the right-wing ecosystem will reframe it as a Democratic attack on the working class, the police, traditional values, etc., and (b) the centrist mainstream media will sanewash both sides. Even if Democrats had a perfect anti-corruption messenger and a perfect anti-corruption agenda, the message would not reach the voters who need it without a media infrastructure capable of carrying it.

What follows? Two things. First, Democrats and Democratic-aligned philanthropists should be investing in media infrastructure at a scale far beyond anything currently contemplated. This is not glamorous, it has long time horizons, and it does not produce wins in the next election cycle, but it is the most important structural intervention available. The right understood this in the 1980s and built the institutions over decades. The left has been comparatively absent, partly because of a misplaced faith that mainstream institutions would carry the necessary information, and partly because liberal donors prefer to fund issue advocacy with measurable outputs.

Second, the most strategically important Democratic figures in the next decade may not be politicians at all. They may be the people who build durable left-leaning media platforms—not the boutique podcasts that talk to the converted, but the genuinely cross-pressured platforms that can reach voters who are not already Democrats. This is the model of Joe Rogan, who began as a comedian and ended up shaping the information diet of millions of low-engagement male voters. There is no Democratic equivalent. Producing one is a more important strategic priority than any messaging shift.

The authors gesture at this when they discuss authenticity and the "tells it like it is" appeal, but they treat it as a feature of candidate positioning rather than as a feature of the platform on which candidates appear. The platform shapes what authenticity looks like. Trump succeeded in part because he came up through media (The Apprentice, Twitter, cable news appearances) and was fluent in its forms. Most Democratic politicians come up through politics and are not fluent. This is not fixable by repositioning; it requires a different recruitment model and a different media infrastructure.

I would push the authors' argument in a more structural direction. Yes, the median voter theorem is dead. Yes, moderation has lost its premium. But these are downstream effects of a deeper change: the information environment in which voters form opinions has been restructured in ways that systematically advantage one side. Until Democrats grapple with this at the level of infrastructure rather than at the level of messaging, no strategic pivot—popularist, populist, or otherwise—will produce sustained gains.

Anti-corruption framing is fine. Recruiting working-class candidates is fine. Mobilizing the base is fine. But the binding constraint on Democratic success is upstream of all of these, in the architecture of how political information moves through American society. That is where the next major Democratic project must be located if the authors' larger ambition—defending democracy against an authoritarian movement—is to be plausible at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The nationalization of politics means that individual candidate signals are overwhelmed by a media ecosystem that delivers right-wing framings to a much larger audience.
  2. 02The right has built a coordinated, well-capitalized media infrastructure—spanning cable news, podcasts, and social media—that the left has failed to match.
  3. 03Strategic pivots like anti-corruption framing will fail unless there is a media infrastructure capable of preventing the message from being reframed or suppressed by opponents.
  4. 04Democratic-aligned donors must shift focus from short-term issue advocacy to long-term investment in durable, cross-pressured media platforms.
  5. 05The party needs a new recruitment model that prioritizes figures who are fluent in modern media forms rather than traditional political backgrounds.
