The Hidden Statism in Rana's "Freedom"

Aziz Rana presents his alternative vision as a more capacious, inclusive understanding of freedom — one that joins liberty to solidarity, democracy, and shared responsibility. It sounds attractive. But beneath the rhetoric is a familiar substitution: the replacement of the individual liberty tradition with collective political authority, dressed up in liberatory language. Anyone who values freedom in its older, more rigorous sense — the absence of coercion, the security of one's person, property, and conscience — should read Rana's essay carefully, because it is precisely the kind of conceptual maneuver that erodes liberty in the name of expanding it.

Consider Rana's working definition: "Above all, to be free was to enjoy a robust experience of self-rule — an experience that combined a mastery over one's own life and a central role in shaping the direction of collective arrangements and institutions." Notice what has happened here. Two distinct ideas have been silently fused. "Mastery over one's own life" is the classical liberal idea — call it autonomy or independence. "A central role in shaping collective arrangements" is something different: political participation, or democratic citizenship. These are both goods, but they are not the same good, and conflating them produces conceptual havoc.

Isaiah Berlin warned against exactly this conflation in his "Two Concepts of Liberty." Negative liberty — being unobstructed by others — and positive liberty — being self-directed, or participating in collective direction — are different things, and the second can be, and historically has been, used to override the first. If "real freedom" requires my participation in shaping collective decisions, then someone who simply wishes to be left alone — to mind his shop, raise his children, attend his church, write his novel — is not, on Rana's account, truly free. He must be made free, drawn into participation, integrated into democratic institutions, taught to experience solidarity. This is freedom with a quotation mark around it, and historically, the projects that have pursued it have not been gentle.

Rana would deny this implication, and he would point to workplace democracy and voting rights as benign examples of his vision. But examine the workplace case. Rana proposes that workers must "enjoy shared control over the decisions that shape the workplace," "regardless of their wealth or status." The word "must" is doing significant work. In Rana's world, the worker who would rather just do his job and go home — who has no interest in attending union meetings, voting on shop-floor governance, or codetermining production schedules — is to be enrolled in this collective freedom whether he wants it or not. The owner who built the business and risked her capital must surrender authority to a collective. This may or may not be a good policy. It is not, however, freedom in any sense the word has historically carried. It is the redistribution of authority.

The same conceptual slippage runs through Rana's treatment of public goods. He laments that in the United States, "jobs, pensions, and health insurance were all private entitlements for workers to negotiate from employers, not public goods entrenched through collective action and government provision." But notice the move: the absence of state provision is presented as a deficit of freedom. By this logic, the freer society is the one with more comprehensive government programs. Yet the citizen of such a society faces higher taxes, more regulation, more dependence on bureaucratic institutions for the necessities of life, and less practical ability to exit, dissent, or arrange his affairs differently. Whatever else one calls this, calling it freedom requires the kind of redefinition that emptied the word of meaning in twentieth-century totalitarian discourse.

Rana is too sophisticated to be confused with a totalitarian, of course. His project is a democratic-socialist one. But the conceptual machinery he deploys has been used, and continues to be used, to justify expansions of state power against which the classical liberal tradition has always cautioned. When Rousseau wrote that citizens might be "forced to be free," he was articulating the same move: the genuine freedom of the individual is identified with the general will, and the general will is identified with the institutional decisions of the collective. There is no recourse against the collective in the name of freedom, because the collective is freedom.

Consider next Rana's history. He traces American individualism to settler expansion and imperial extraction. This is a partial truth made into a totalizing claim. Yes, American agrarian independence depended on land taken from Native peoples. But the principles by which settlers understood their freedom — the common law tradition of property rights, due process, freedom of conscience, jury trial, habeas corpus — were not produced by the conquest. They were imported from England, where there were no Indians to displace. Locke's Two Treatises is, among other things, a defense of property and limited government written against an absolutist monarch on a small island. The reduction of liberal individualism to "settler freedom" is a genetic fallacy: the historical context of an idea's adoption does not exhaust its meaning or validity.

Worse, the genealogy obscures the actual function of individual rights in protecting the marginalized. Frederick Douglass appealed to the Constitution. Ida B. Wells appealed to the right to due process. The civil rights movement won its great victories through litigation under the Fourteenth Amendment and through claims of equal individual liberty. The freedom that mattered most to those at the bottom was not the collective self-rule of a majority that had oppressed them, but the constitutional protection of individuals against majoritarian abuse. Rana mentions King and Hamer, but flattens their thought into a generic communitarianism. King invoked the Declaration and the Constitution — documents of individual rights — as the unfulfilled promissory note America owed.

There is, finally, a practical objection. Rana wants to build institutions that make collective self-rule the everyday experience of Americans. But the historical record of large-scale projects to remake everyday life around collective participation is grim. Even the gentlest versions — the European corporatist arrangements he implicitly admires — have produced sclerotic economies and increasingly censorious public spheres. Real human beings, given a choice, often prefer to be left alone with their families and their projects rather than to attend yet another meeting in which they are invited to codetermine something. This preference is not false consciousness. It is the recognition that the unexamined life may not be worth living, but the over-politicized life is exhausting and corrosive.

Reclaim freedom by all means. But reclaim it as what it actually is: the right of individuals, in their persons and properties and consciences, to be free from coercion, including the coercion of well-meaning collectives. The rest is politics, and politics, however necessary, is not the same as liberty.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rana conflates the classical liberal concept of individual autonomy with democratic participation, falsely suggesting that one cannot be free without engaging in collective political decision-making.
  2. 02The vision of 'shared control' in the workplace and society necessitates coercion, as it forces individuals into collective arrangements and redistributes authority away from private owners and independent workers.
  3. 03Defining government provision of social goods as 'freedom' is a conceptual error that ignores the resulting increase in bureaucratic dependence, taxation, and regulation.
  4. 04Rana’s historical analysis commits a genetic fallacy by reducing the universal principles of individual rights to mere byproducts of settler colonialism and imperial extraction.
  5. 05The essay ignores the vital role that individual constitutional rights—rather than majoritarian collective rule—have played in protecting marginalized groups from oppression.
  6. 06The project of politicizing everyday life through mandatory collective participation ignores the human preference for private life and historically leads to sclerotic, over-regulated societies.
