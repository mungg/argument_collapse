"While Robert Pollin’s goal of prioritizing full employment is correct, his specific proposal to fund it by drastically cutting military spending is politically and economically unrealistic."

Robert Pollin’s "Back to Full Employment" provides a sobering reminder of the devastating human toll of the Great Recession. His core thesis—that employment must return to the center of economic policymaking—is unassailable. Yet, when transitioning from diagnosis to prescription, Pollin’s essay suffers from a profound lack of political realism. By hinging his entire strategy on a massive defunding of the American military, Pollin guarantees that his proposal will remain an academic thought experiment rather than a workable policy.

Pollin casually suggests transferring 25 percent of total military spending—roughly $165 billion annually—into education and green energy. He frames this as simply returning the military to "pre-Iraq and Afghanistan levels." Writing in early 2011, this ignores the immense complexities of global security and the actual mechanics of defense drawdowns. The United States is currently engaged in a massive, delicate counterinsurgency in Afghanistan, with over 100,000 troops deployed. We are in the midst of a precarious transition in Iraq. A sudden 25 percent reduction in the Pentagon budget is not merely an accounting trick; it would mean the immediate discharge of hundreds of thousands of active-duty service members, civilian contractors, and defense manufacturing workers.

Ironically, Pollin’s attempt to solve the unemployment crisis would begin by firing hundreds of thousands of people. The defense sector is one of the few remaining areas of American manufacturing that provides highly skilled, unionized, middle-class jobs. The aerospace engineers in Washington State, the shipbuilders in Virginia, and the auto-workers manufacturing armored vehicles in Michigan would all face devastating layoffs. Pollin claims his new green jobs will absorb these workers, but the transition would be brutal, localized, and economically destabilizing in the short term.

Furthermore, this proposal fundamentally misunderstands the political economy of the United States. Defense spending enjoys broad bipartisan support precisely because it functions as an implicit, politically acceptable jobs program across almost every congressional district. To tie the desperately needed goal of full employment to a radical, politically toxic demand to gut the military is to doom the entire enterprise.

If advocates for working people want to see a massive investment in clean energy and education, they must decouple it from pacifist pipe dreams. We can afford to finance clean energy infrastructure through progressive taxation or by issuing long-term bonds at currently rock-bottom interest rates, without needing to cannibalize the defense budget. By insisting on this specific $330 billion transfer, Pollin has allowed his ideological preferences to overshadow the pragmatic necessity of building a broad, passable coalition for job creation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A sudden 25 percent reduction in defense spending would cause immediate mass unemployment among service members, civilian contractors, and manufacturing workers.
  2. 02The proposal would devastate the defense sector, which is one of the few remaining sources of high-skilled, unionized, middle-class manufacturing jobs in the United States.
  3. 03Tying a full-employment agenda to radical military cuts is politically toxic because defense spending functions as a bipartisan jobs program across most congressional districts.
  4. 04Alternative funding mechanisms, such as progressive taxation or long-term bonds, could finance job-creating investments without the need to cannibalize the defense budget.
