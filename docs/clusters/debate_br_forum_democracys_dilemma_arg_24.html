"While Farrell and Schneier provide a useful structural framework for democratic information vulnerabilities, their analysis is overly Americentric and fails to account for the complex international dynamics and comparative experiences essential to a universal solution."

Farrell and Schneier have written a valuable essay that deserves credit for treating democratic information vulnerabilities as a structural problem rather than merely a matter of bad actors. Their framework of Democracy's Dilemma offers genuine analytical purchase. But their analysis suffers from a significant blind spot: it is overwhelmingly focused on the American case while presenting its conclusions in universal terms. A framework that does not adequately grapple with the international dimensions of democratic information security risks offering solutions that address symptoms while ignoring causes, and that may be not just incomplete but actively counterproductive when applied in different contexts.

The authors are certainly aware that foreign actors can destabilize democracies through information manipulation—they discuss Russian operations at length. But they treat this primarily as a threat to American democracy rather than as one instance of a broader dynamic. The framework they propose—"attack surface" analysis, "threat models" applied to democratic systems—emerges from American experiences and American vulnerabilities. Applying these frameworks globally requires attention to how different political systems face different versions of the Democracy's Dilemma and how international dynamics interact with domestic vulnerabilities in ways that are not reducible to any single national context.

Consider first the problem of comparative vulnerability. The authors implicitly treat democratic resilience as a property of well-functioning democracies that are under attack from external adversaries. But many democracies are not well-functioning in ways that their own citizens recognize. When citizens of Hungary, Poland, or Turkey view their own systems as already deeply compromised, the threat model looks different. Information manipulation by foreign actors may be less significant than manipulation by domestic elites who control major media outlets, who have reshaped institutions to entrench their power, and who have created information environments that serve their interests. The American case—with its strong traditions of federalism, independent journalism, and constitutional constraints—may be more resilient to certain kinds of manipulation than political systems with weaker institutional traditions.

The international dimension also involves competitive dynamics among democracies. The authors note that authoritarian states "can do so by repurposing the same tools of confusion and disarray that they have developed to shore up their own domestic security." This is true, but it understates the extent to which democracies themselves have developed information manipulation capabilities. American intelligence agencies have long engaged in influence operations abroad; other democracies do so as well. The Framework Convention on Cyber Security and the Tallinn Manual address some of these issues, but there is no international consensus on what constitutes legitimate versus illegitimate information operations by democracies against each other. A truly comprehensive response to Democracy's Dilemma would need to address these competitive dynamics among democracies as well as the threat from authoritarian states.

There is also the question of how democratic information vulnerabilities interact with broader geopolitical competition. The authors note that "democracies will become more vulnerable to manipulation by foreign authoritarian governments" if they fail to address domestic weaknesses. This is correct but incomplete. It suggests that the path to resilience is internal reform that makes democracies more capable of resisting external manipulation. But an alternative reading might suggest that authoritarian states invest in information manipulation against democracies precisely because they fear democratic influence on their own populations. Russian information operations in the United States may be aimed not primarily at affecting American policy but at demonstrating that democracies are no better than autocracies—that all political systems are corrupt, that all elections are rigged, that there is no meaningful difference between political orders. If this reading is correct, then the path to resilience may involve not just technical fixes but demonstrations of democratic effectiveness that undermine the authoritarian narrative.

The authors call for a "democratic counterpart to the political technologists of authoritarian and semi-authoritarian regimes." This is a valuable aspiration, but it requires clarity about what such an institution would do and how it would relate to existing international institutions. Would it be national or international? How would it relate to existing international law governing information operations? How would it balance transparency with operational security? These are not technical questions but political ones that will require international negotiation and agreement.

Finally, the authors' treatment of international examples is surprisingly thin given the richness of comparative experience. Taiwan has developed significant capabilities for detecting and countering disinformation. Singapore has taken a different approach involving government-led fact-checking and platform regulation. The European Union has developed frameworks for platform accountability and election security. Each of these approaches offers lessons for American policy, but the authors engage with none of them. A genuinely comprehensive framework would need to draw on this comparative experience and specify what conditions make different approaches more or less effective.

The authors are right that democracies face serious information security challenges and that these challenges require systematic responses. But their analysis would be strengthened by fuller engagement with international dimensions—not just the threat from foreign adversaries but the broader ecology of democratic information systems in a interconnected world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' threat models do not translate well to democracies with weaker institutional traditions where domestic elite manipulation is a more significant factor than foreign interference.
  2. 02The framework ignores the competitive information operations that democracies conduct against one another, which complicates the distinction between legitimate and illegitimate influence.
  3. 03Authoritarian information attacks are often a geopolitical response to the fear of democratic influence, meaning resilience requires demonstrating democratic effectiveness rather than just implementing technical institutional fixes.
  4. 04The proposal for 'democratic political technologists' lacks a necessary international dimension regarding how such roles would navigate international law and cross-border cooperation.
  5. 05The essay fails to incorporate successful comparative strategies from other nations, such as Taiwan's disinformation detection or the European Union's platform accountability frameworks.
