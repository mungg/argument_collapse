"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies a broader range of innovation models, his framework remains flawed because it prioritizes global market competitiveness over the foundational social and democratic needs of a community."

Dan Breznitz wants to rescue innovation from Silicon Valley. That is a worthy project. But perhaps we should also rescue prosperity from innovation.

The lead essay argues that communities have been misled by a narrow model: venture-backed start-ups, high-tech novelty, financial exits, and the dream of becoming the next Silicon Valley. In its place Breznitz offers a broader understanding of innovation across multiple stages of production. Cities and regions, he argues, can build inclusive prosperity by finding the right place in global value chains: design, prototyping, component improvement, production engineering, assembly, and continuous upgrading. This is a much better account of how economies actually work. It honors forms of skill and learning that the start-up world ignores.

And yet the essay still accepts a premise that deserves more scrutiny: that local well-being should be organized around competitive innovation-led growth. What if that premise is part of the problem?

The places Breznitz describes are constantly asked to adapt to global demand. They must identify niches, build capacities, upgrade skills, and remain alert to shifting markets. There is no permanent security. A community survives by becoming useful to global production networks. This may be realistic, but it is also a bleak foundation for democratic life. It means that the dignity of a town depends on whether it can find a defensible market position. Those that succeed become case studies. Those that fail become Cobalt.

Of course communities need economic activity. People need jobs, tax bases, and opportunities. But the language of innovation can crowd out other ways of thinking about what places are for. A town is not a firm. A city is not a product. A region is not a portfolio. The purpose of local life is not to maximize competitiveness but to allow people to live decently together: to care for children and elders, educate the young, repair homes, maintain parks, provide food, support art, protect health, and sustain relationships. Much of this work is not export-oriented. Much of it does not scale. Much of it is not “innovative” except in the ordinary human sense of solving problems with the resources at hand.

The pandemic should have made this obvious. When COVID-19 arrived, the workers most essential to daily survival were not primarily venture-backed engineers or advanced manufacturing specialists. They were nurses, home health aides, teachers, grocery clerks, delivery drivers, farmworkers, cleaners, public health staff, child care workers, and neighbors checking on neighbors. Some relied on sophisticated technologies, but the core of their work was care, maintenance, and social reproduction. These sectors are often low-paid not because they lack social value, but because our economy rewards market power more than human need.

An inclusive prosperity agenda that begins with innovation risks missing this. It may create good jobs in certain industries while leaving care workers underpaid, renters insecure, bus systems unreliable, and public health departments understaffed. A region could become a world-class hub for battery components and still fail its elderly residents. It could build a thriving design cluster and still have homeless encampments under the freeway. It could produce export success and still leave single parents without child care.

Breznitz might reply that innovation-based growth supplies the tax base for these social goods. Sometimes it does. But in the United States, growth often fails to translate into collective provision. Silicon Valley itself is the proof. Immense wealth exists alongside housing crisis, long commutes, food insecurity, and public dysfunction. The problem is not only the wrong kind of innovation; it is the weakness of social claims on private wealth. Without strong taxation, public institutions, labor power, and democratic planning, even successful local growth can bypass basic needs.

There is also an ecological question. The essay is written in the shadow of COVID-19, but also in the age of climate crisis. Growth through endless production, upgrading, consumption, and global competition cannot be assumed benign. Some innovation is essential for decarbonization and adaptation. But many innovations accelerate throughput, planned obsolescence, extraction, and waste. A cheaper smartphone, a faster delivery system, a more flexible manufacturing process—these may increase convenience and profits while deepening environmental burdens. The question should not be “How can this community innovate?” but “What kinds of economic activity are compatible with a livable planet and a decent society?”

Bicycles, one of Breznitz’s central examples, are interesting in this respect. Better bikes can support low-carbon mobility and public health. But a bicycle economy oriented around constant product differentiation, carbon fiber upgrades, and globalized consumption is not the same as an economy oriented around safe streets, repair shops, affordable transportation, and everyday use. The social value of the bicycle lies less in the sophistication of its components than in whether people can safely ride to school, work, and the grocery store. That requires urban design, public investment, maintenance, and political choices about cars. The innovation that matters most may be a protected bike lane, not a lighter frame.

The same distinction applies elsewhere. Food systems need less glamorous innovation and more regional resilience, soil health, fair wages, and reduced waste. Housing needs new materials and construction methods, yes, but even more it needs land policy, tenant protections, public housing, and an end to exclusionary zoning. Health care needs medical innovation, but also primary care, staffing, trust, and universal access. Education needs technology only insofar as it supports teachers and students; it does not need another wave of platforms promising disruption. In each case, the central issue is not whether innovation occurs but whether human needs govern it.

A better frame might be the “foundational economy”: the sectors that make everyday life possible. These include utilities, care, housing, food distribution, health, education, transit, sanitation, and local public administration. They are often less mobile than high-tech firms and less glamorous than export clusters. They also employ large numbers of people across skill levels. Improving them can create jobs, reduce inequality, and make places more livable. Some improvements will involve technology and process innovation. Others will involve higher staffing ratios, better wages, public ownership, or simpler rules. The point is to begin from needs rather than from markets.

This does not mean rejecting Breznitz’s argument. On the contrary, his broader definition of innovation can be useful if turned toward maintenance and care. Continuous improvement is not only for bicycle gears and semiconductors. It is also for elder care scheduling, school meals, heat-wave response, building retrofits, bus reliability, water systems, and public libraries. But once we move in this direction, the distinction between innovation policy and social policy begins to dissolve. The goal is not local competitiveness; it is collective capability.

Communities should still build on their strengths. A region with machine tool expertise should not ignore it. A city with medical device firms should support them. A rural area with agricultural knowledge should develop it. But these strategies should be subordinate to a broader democratic plan for well-being. Too often economic development agencies ask how to attract and grow firms, then assume the public will benefit. They should ask first what residents need: affordable homes, clean air, good schools, dignified care, reliable mobility, meaningful work, cultural life, and security. Firms and innovation strategies should be evaluated by how well they serve those ends.

This also changes how we think about “left behind” places. The phrase implies that every community must catch up to a growth frontier defined elsewhere. But some places may not want to become hubs. They may want stability, beauty, affordability, ecological restoration, and care for an aging population. That is not failure. It is only failure in an economic imagination that recognizes value mainly when it is tradable, scalable, and innovative.

The Silicon Valley myth is damaging because it tells communities to chase a narrow dream. Breznitz offers a wider and more realistic set of dreams. But perhaps the deeper freedom is to dream less competitively. A good society would not force every town to justify itself through global market success. It would guarantee the material foundations of life and then allow places to cultivate their own forms of usefulness, creativity, and belonging.

Innovation has a role in that society. But it should be a servant, not a god.
