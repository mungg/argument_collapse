"The broad, multi-stage understanding of innovation proposed by Dan Breznitz is essential for a successful and inclusive climate transition, as decarbonization requires systemic transformation rather than just high-tech inventions."

The strongest reason to accept Dan Breznitz’s argument is not nostalgia for manufacturing, nor irritation with Silicon Valley’s self-regard. It is climate change. Decarbonization will demand precisely the broad understanding of innovation that Breznitz defends: not only invention, not only venture-backed start-ups, not only the first dazzling product, but the patient work of redesigning systems, improving components, scaling production, lowering costs, training workers, and deploying technologies everywhere.

If we think about climate through the Silicon Valley lens, we will get the transition wrong. We will imagine that the central task is to fund brilliant founders until they produce breakthrough technologies that magically diffuse through the economy. Breakthroughs matter, of course. Better batteries, zero-carbon fuels, low-carbon cement and steel, advanced geothermal, grid management software, and carbon removal may all prove important. But the climate problem is not mainly a shortage of clever ideas. It is a problem of transformation at civilization scale. Electricity, transportation, buildings, agriculture, industry, land use, and finance must change together. That requires innovation in every stage of production and every layer of governance.

Breznitz’s distinction between invention and innovation is therefore indispensable. Solar photovoltaics, wind turbines, lithium-ion batteries, and heat pumps are not new in the sense of having appeared yesterday. Their climate significance comes from decades of improvement: higher efficiency, better manufacturing, lower costs, more reliable installation, more sophisticated financing, better grid integration, and wider adoption. The most important climate technologies may be those that become boring. A heat pump in every suitable building, a reliable electric bus fleet, a standardized retrofit process for old homes, a cheaper transformer, a smarter inverter, a better window, a more efficient industrial motor—these are not the stuff of heroic start-up mythology. They are the substance of decarbonization.

This is where local prosperity and climate policy can reinforce each other. Many of the tasks required for a low-carbon transition are place-based. Buildings must be retrofitted where they stand. Transit systems must be built and maintained locally. Electric vehicle charging networks require electricians, planners, construction workers, software, and utilities. The grid needs transmission lines, substations, control systems, storage, and maintenance crews. Ports must be modernized. Water systems must adapt to droughts and floods. These activities can support skilled work across a wide range of education levels. They can also generate local learning: better methods for retrofitting row houses in Philadelphia, cooling schools in Phoenix, hardening coastal infrastructure in Louisiana, or electrifying buses in Chicago.

But the climate transition will not automatically produce inclusive prosperity. Without policy, it may reproduce the very dynamics Breznitz criticizes. Venture capital will fund software platforms and speculative technologies with high exit potential. Large firms will capture subsidies. Production may be offshore. Installation work may be subcontracted to low-wage, nonunion employers. Wealthy households may receive tax credits for electric cars and rooftop solar while renters and low-income communities face pollution, heat, and high energy bills. A green Silicon Valley is still Silicon Valley if it privatizes gains and socializes costs.

That is why Breznitz’s emphasis on public leadership is crucial. Climate innovation must be mission-oriented. The state cannot merely correct market failures at the margins; it must shape markets. It must create demand, coordinate standards, finance infrastructure, support research, and ensure that benefits are broadly shared. Operation Warp Speed, which Breznitz cites in relation to vaccine vials, offers one model of urgent public-private coordination. The climate equivalent would involve procurement, loans, grants, technical assistance, domestic capacity requirements where appropriate, and clear performance targets. Government should not simply hope that green markets emerge. It should build them.

Public procurement is especially powerful. Federal, state, and local governments buy vehicles, construct buildings, operate schools and hospitals, maintain military bases, fund transit, and regulate utilities. If they commit to buying low-carbon materials, electric buses, efficient appliances, clean backup power, and high-performance building systems, they can create stable demand for firms willing to invest. Procurement can also require labor standards, apprenticeship programs, domestic content in strategic sectors, and community benefits. This is how innovation policy becomes industrial policy and social policy at once.

Breznitz’s four stages help clarify the work ahead. Stage one novelty is needed for technologies not yet commercially mature: long-duration storage, clean industrial processes, low-carbon aviation fuels, perhaps advanced nuclear or carbon removal. These require research funding, demonstration projects, and tolerance for failure. Stage two design and production engineering will matter enormously in adapting technologies to real-world use. Retrofitting a building stock as varied as America’s is a design and logistics challenge, not simply a product challenge. Stage three second-generation innovation is where costs fall and reliability improves. Batteries, electrolyzers, heat pumps, and grid components all need continuous improvement. Stage four production and assembly will determine whether clean technologies can be manufactured and deployed at the required scale.

The United States has weaknesses at every stage, but especially in the later ones. It remains strong in research universities, software, finance, and entrepreneurial formation. It is weaker in manufacturing ecosystems, vocational training, supplier networks, and patient industrial finance. That imbalance is dangerous for climate policy. If American firms invent clean technologies but rely almost entirely on foreign production, the country may lose jobs and political support for decarbonization. Worse, supply bottlenecks could slow deployment. The point is not to retreat into autarky; global cooperation is essential. But resilience requires domestic and allied capacity in critical sectors.

The semiconductor debate is a warning. Only belatedly has the United States realized how much fabrication capacity and process knowledge it allowed to migrate elsewhere. A similar pattern in batteries, grid equipment, electrolyzers, or critical minerals processing could constrain the clean transition. If public officials are serious about climate, they must care about supply chains before shortages become crises. That means mapping capacities, investing in community colleges and technical training, supporting small and medium-size manufacturers, and coordinating with allies.

Yet climate policy also complicates local development strategies. Not every community should chase the same green industries. The phrase “green jobs” can become as vague and overused as “innovation.” A coal region, an auto region, a port city, a farm county, and a university town have different assets and needs. Some may specialize in battery components, others in grid services, offshore wind logistics, mass timber, building retrofits, regenerative agriculture, or water management. Breznitz is right that communities must choose based on real capabilities and global demand.

The just transition requires special attention to fossil fuel communities. It is not enough to promise that clean energy will create more jobs in aggregate. A laid-off refinery worker in Louisiana cannot pay a mortgage with a national employment statistic. Regions dependent on coal, oil, and gas need targeted investment, wage insurance, pension protection, reclamation jobs, infrastructure, and real participation in planning. Some may develop new industrial roles; others may need long-term public support. Climate policy will fail politically and morally if it treats these communities as collateral damage.

Environmental justice must be equally central. Many low-income communities and communities of color have borne the costs of the fossil economy: refineries, highways, ports, power plants, warehouses, and polluted land. Green industrial policy should not simply place new facilities in the same overburdened neighborhoods without consent and safeguards. Inclusive prosperity means cleaner air, lower energy bills, safer housing, and community ownership opportunities, not just jobs somewhere in the region.

Breznitz’s critique of Silicon Valley also helps us see the danger of climate hype. There is already a growing industry of green techno-optimism: founders promising moonshot solutions, investors chasing the next Tesla, politicians posing beside prototypes. Some of this will be useful. Much will be theater. The climate clock does not allow us to confuse announcements with deployment. A start-up that raises a large round is not the same as a factory that produces reliable equipment, a workforce that can install it, or a utility that can integrate it.

The low-carbon transition will be won or lost in the unglamorous middle: permitting, standards, workforce development, supply chains, maintenance, financing, and public trust. That is exactly the territory Breznitz invites us to take seriously. Innovation is collective, cumulative, and institutional. It is not a lightning bolt from a founder’s mind.

If we apply that lesson to climate, we can build an economy that is cleaner and more broadly prosperous. If we ignore it, we may get a handful of green billionaires, fragile supply chains, uneven deployment, and a backlash from communities left behind. The planet cannot afford the Silicon Valley theory of change. We need an innovation politics big enough for the atmosphere, and democratic enough for the people who must live through the transition.
