In Defense of the Manchin View

It has become fashionable, in the precincts where Hacker and Pierson write and where I sometimes write, to treat Joe Manchin as a villain — the man who stood between America and the largest expansion of the welfare state in two generations. Hacker and Pierson are too sophisticated to put it in those terms, but the structure of their argument carries the same moral charge. The 2021 agenda was a near miss. Manchin and Sinema killed it. The density tax conspired against the metropolitan party. Better luck next time.

I want to offer a contrary reading. Manchin was substantially right on the merits, and the Democratic Party's failure to engage seriously with what he was telling it was a significant strategic error whose consequences are still being felt. The Hacker-Pierson essay, by treating Manchin as an obstacle rather than as a serious interlocutor with a defensible position, misses the most important political economy story of the Biden years.

Start with what Manchin actually said. He said, in early 2022, that the country was facing an inflation problem, that injecting an additional $2 trillion of demand into an already-stretched economy would make it worse, and that programs designed to be temporary should not be made permanent through accounting fictions. Each of these claims was empirically defensible at the time. The administration's own economists were divided. Larry Summers, much maligned in the Hacker-Pierson account, had been warning since early 2021 that the American Rescue Plan was too large for the output gap and would generate inflation. By the time the Build Back Better debate was underway, his predictions had been broadly vindicated. CPI was running above 7 percent. Real wages were falling. The political effects on the Democratic majority were severe.

Hacker and Pierson treat all of this as essentially background noise — they mention inflation only as the justification Manchin invoked, with a faint implication that the justification was opportunistic. But inflation was the central fact of the 2021-2022 economy and is the central fact of the political climate facing Democrats in 2024. It is the reason Biden's approval on the economy is poor despite low unemployment. It is the reason voters of all races and classes report dissatisfaction with their economic situation. And it is, plausibly, partly the product of policy choices that the authors of the essay defend.

Consider the counterfactual. Had the full BBB passed in something like its initial form, with all the demand-side stimulus front-loaded, what would the inflation rate have been in 2022 and 2023? It is hard to say precisely, but the direction is clear. The labor market was already tight. Supply chains were already strained. Additional fiscal stimulus would have pushed against capacity constraints and produced more inflation, not less. The Inflation Reduction Act was rebranded specifically because the political team understood that the public was no longer in the mood for further demand stimulus, and the resulting bill was deliberately less stimulative than what it replaced. The compromise that emerged was, in important respects, the bill that should have been pursued from the beginning.

The deeper point Manchin was making — and that the Democratic political establishment was unable to hear — is that the political economy of an inflationary environment is fundamentally different from the political economy of the secular stagnation era. From roughly 2010 to 2020, the binding macroeconomic constraint was deficient demand. The standard progressive case for more spending was strong. After 2021, the binding constraint shifted to supply, and the standard progressive case had to shift with it. Some progressive economists understood this — there was useful work on "supply-side progressivism" from people like Ezra Klein, the Niskanen Center, and Roosevelt itself. But the party's political machinery did not adjust. It continued to push demand-side programs in an environment that no longer rewarded them.

Hacker and Pierson hint at this when they note that "few if any of [the Democrats'] initiatives seem to have built power through policy." But they treat this as a puzzle to be explained by media noise or insufficient organizing. The more parsimonious explanation is that voters experienced the totality of the Biden agenda as part of an inflationary episode that hurt them, and they apportioned credit and blame accordingly. The CTC extension delivered checks; inflation simultaneously eroded the purchasing power of those checks and of every other income source. The net experience was not gratitude. It was anxiety.

There is a related point about the size and shape of the agenda. A more disciplined Democratic Party, taking Manchin's concerns seriously rather than treating him as an obstacle, would have designed the 2021 package differently. It would have made the popular elements — the CTC expansion, the ACA subsidy enhancement, child care subsidies — permanent, while paring back the elements that were either inflationary or politically marginal. It would have prioritized the climate provisions, which were genuinely transformative and had a long-tail rather than near-term demand effect. It would have funded fewer ambitions more durably. Instead, the party tried to do everything at once, with sunsets engineered to make the price tag look smaller, and ended up with neither permanence nor magnitude.

The Hacker-Pierson essay's framing — bold agenda, narrow miss, structural obstacles — obscures the real lesson. The lesson is that the Democratic Party, having spent the entire Obama era debating whether the stimulus was big enough, swung to the opposite error in 2021 and has had to live with the political consequences. Manchin was the only senior Democrat with sufficient independence to say so. He was vilified for saying so. He was eventually proven roughly correct.

This has implications for what "next time" should look like. Hacker and Pierson urge that the 2021 agenda be ready to revive when the political window reopens. I would urge instead that the next agenda be substantially redesigned. Make fewer things permanent, but actually permanent. Choose carefully which constituencies you want to build. Pay attention to the macroeconomic environment and adjust accordingly. Stop treating moderation as betrayal. The Brahmin left moderates Hacker and Pierson disparage — the Clinton Democrats, the Obama Democrats, the Manchins of the current party — were not wrong about everything. They were right that a center-left party in a polarized country with a tilted institutional playing field has to be disciplined about both the substance and the politics of what it promises. The Biden agenda was insufficiently disciplined on both fronts, and the price has been a presidency that, despite real accomplishments, struggles to defend its record to the voters who supposedly benefited from it.

That is not a story of structural obstacles. It is a story of strategic choices that could have been made differently.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Joe Manchin’s opposition was empirically defensible because the proposed $2 trillion in additional demand would have exacerbated the central political and economic problem of the Biden era: high inflation.
  2. 02The Democratic leadership failed to recognize that the economic environment had shifted from a period of deficient demand to one of supply constraints, requiring a different policy approach than the one pursued.
  3. 03The party’s attempt to fund too many temporary programs through 'accounting fictions' and sunsets resulted in an agenda that lacked both permanence and political efficacy.
  4. 04Voters did not reward the administration's policies because the benefits of initiatives like the Child Tax Credit were effectively neutralized by the erosion of purchasing power caused by inflation.
  5. 05Future Democratic agendas must be more disciplined, prioritizing fewer, permanent programs and adjusting to the macroeconomic environment rather than treating moderation as a betrayal.
