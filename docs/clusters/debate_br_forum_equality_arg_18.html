Is Equal Opportunity Enough? · Cluster 18 of 41

"Instead of pursuing the insufficient ideal of equal opportunity or the overly intrusive ideal of equal flourishing, egalitarians should advocate for democratic sufficiency, which ensures everyone has the material and institutional thresholds necessary for a decent life while limiting the power of the wealthy to dominate others."

Christine Sypnowich is right about the politics but less convincing about the philosophy. Equal opportunity, as used by contemporary politicians, is plainly insufficient. It permits leaders to deplore barriers while leaving people poor, sick, unhoused, indebted, and exhausted. But the alternative should not be equality of flourishing. A better public philosophy for egalitarians is democratic sufficiency: everyone should have enough of the central conditions of a decent life, no one should have so much power as to dominate others, and inequalities beyond that should be judged pragmatically by their effects.

This may sound less inspiring than “equal flourishing,” but it has advantages. It is more administrable, more pluralistic, and more politically plausible. It also gets us closer to the urgent injustices Sypnowich rightly identifies. The problem in the United States in 2023 is not that some people have slightly more flourishing than others. It is that millions lack affordable health care, stable housing, safe neighborhoods, decent wages, child care, and retirement security, while a small minority accumulates wealth capable of buying political influence and dynastic privilege. The immediate task is to build floors and lower ceilings, not to equalize the whole range of human fulfillment.

Opportunity rhetoric fails because it is too compatible with bad outcomes. But outcome egalitarianism can fail by choosing the wrong outcome. Flourishing is too broad. It includes objective goods such as health and education, subjective goods such as happiness and life satisfaction, relational goods such as friendship and respect, and cultural goods such as beauty and meaning. These are real. Yet they cannot be equalized in the way income, school funding, or health coverage can be distributed. Some people are temperamentally melancholic. Some choose demanding vocations. Some experience grief. Some prefer solitude. Some make sacrifices for children, art, faith, or political causes. Not all unequal flourishing is a problem justice can solve.

Sypnowich acknowledges this, but the acknowledgement weakens the guiding ideal. If equal flourishing does not mean actually equal flourishing, but rather serious public concern for whether people live well, then much of the disagreement disappears. Liberal egalitarians, social democrats, capability theorists, and relational egalitarians can all endorse that concern. If, however, equal flourishing is meant to direct policy toward equalizing quality of life, then it is too ambitious and potentially intrusive.

A sufficientarian approach begins elsewhere. It asks: what must everyone securely have to live as a free and equal member of society? The answer includes income, health care, housing, nutrition, education, safety, mobility, legal rights, political voice, digital access, and time. It also includes protection against domination by employers, landlords, creditors, abusive partners, police, and bureaucracies. These are not mere opportunities. They are thresholds of decent social membership.

The threshold must be generous, not minimal. “Enough” does not mean bare survival. It means enough to appear in public without shame, to refuse abusive work, to care for family, to participate in politics, to recover from illness, to develop talents, to enjoy leisure, and to plan beyond the next emergency. A society that provides only a subsistence floor while tolerating mass insecurity has not met sufficiency. A decent minimum in a rich country should rise with social standards and productive capacity.

This framework yields a clear policy agenda. First, universal health care. Illness should not mean bankruptcy, job lock, or dependence on employer benevolence. Second, child allowances and child care. Children are the clearest case against choice-based accounts of desert, and families cannot flourish when care is treated as a private luxury. Third, housing policy that treats shelter as infrastructure: more supply where needed, social housing, tenant protections, and assistance for those at risk of homelessness. Fourth, labor market reform: higher minimum wages, wage subsidies where appropriate, predictable schedules, paid leave, union rights, and enforcement against wage theft. Fifth, education from early childhood through vocational and higher education that does not saddle young adults with life-distorting debt. Sixth, public goods: transit, libraries, parks, broadband, community health, and environmental protection.

This is not opportunity in the thin sense. It is outcome-oriented because it asks whether people actually have health coverage, housing, income, schooling, and time. If take-up is low, the policy has failed. If a benefit is so stigmatized that eligible people avoid it, the policy has failed. If schools remain segregated by class and race, the policy has failed. If child care exists only on paper because it is unaffordable, the policy has failed. We should measure results relentlessly.

But the results measured should be institutional and material thresholds, not equal flourishing as a whole. This distinction preserves room for pluralism. Government can know whether a person has insurance; it cannot know with comparable legitimacy whether she has achieved a flourishing relationship to music, friendship, spirituality, ambition, or community. Government can reduce loneliness by funding public spaces and supporting care; it cannot guarantee companionship. Government can make arts education universal; it should not treat adults’ unequal aesthetic engagement as an injustice in itself.

The sufficientarian view also has a different relationship to responsibility. It does not require elaborate judgments about whether disadvantage was chosen. Everyone gets health care. Every child gets support. Everyone has access to housing assistance, food security, and basic income protection. Universalism avoids much of the cruelty of means-tested moral inspection. At the same time, sufficiency can preserve expectations of contribution where reasonable. Able adults may be expected to work, train, care, study, or serve in some socially useful way, not as a condition of human worth but as part of reciprocity. The key is that failure to meet expectations should not result in destitution or civic exclusion.

Sypnowich is right that luck egalitarianism can become a machine for blaming the poor. But a politics that never speaks of responsibility risks losing public trust and misunderstanding human agency. Most citizens believe, reasonably, that choices matter. Egalitarians should not answer by denying this. They should say: yes, choices matter, but no one’s ordinary failures justify homelessness, untreated illness, hunger, or humiliation; and many so-called choices are made under conditions that a just society would never have permitted. Sufficiency can hold both thoughts.

What about inequality above the threshold? Here Sypnowich’s argument has bite. If some people enjoy extreme wealth while others merely have enough, formal sufficiency may coexist with plutocracy, status hierarchy, and resentment. Therefore sufficiency must be paired with limits on domination. Very high concentrations of wealth should be taxed, especially inherited wealth, capital gains, land rents, and monopoly profits. Campaign finance should be regulated. Public goods should be funded in ways that reduce class segregation. Executive compensation and financial extraction deserve scrutiny. The reason is not envy or a desire to equalize happiness. It is that extreme inequality undermines democracy and social cohesion.

Still, not all inequalities require correction. If some earn more by doing difficult work, innovating, taking risks, or accepting unpleasant responsibilities, those inequalities may be acceptable if the social floor is high and political power is protected from wealth. The relevant questions are empirical and moral: Does the inequality improve or damage the lives of the least advantaged? Does it buy domination? Does it entrench class across generations? Does it undermine equal respect? Does it reflect exploitation or legitimate reward? These questions are more useful than asking whether flourishing is equal.

Sypnowich’s essay is also right to emphasize non-material goods, but here policy should be indirect. Loneliness, alienation, mental distress, and cultural deprivation have social causes. Long work hours, car-dependent sprawl, unaffordable cities, school closures, social media, precarious employment, and the decline of local institutions all matter. Government can respond by creating spaces and time for association: parks, libraries, community centers, transit, shorter workweeks, support for clubs and arts, mental health care, and family leave. But the humility of indirectness is essential. Friendship and meaning cannot be delivered like cash transfers.

The political appeal of equal opportunity is that it promises fairness without threatening freedom. Its defect is that it often leaves people alone with deprivation. The appeal of equal flourishing is that it refuses this abandonment. Its defect is that it asks politics to take responsibility for too much. Democratic sufficiency occupies the middle ground: demanding enough to condemn our current inequalities, modest enough to respect plural lives.

Equal opportunity is not enough. Equal flourishing is not the right public standard. The aim should be a society in which everyone has the secure conditions of dignity, no one is forced into servility or desperation, and inequalities are permitted only when they are compatible with democratic equality. That would already be a revolution in the rich capitalist countries Sypnowich has in mind. We should not let the perfect equality of flourishing distract from the urgent work of building a high floor, strong public goods, and real limits on private power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Equal flourishing is an impractical and potentially illiberal standard because the state cannot legitimately measure or equalize subjective, pluralistic outcomes like happiness, friendship, or aesthetic fulfillment.
  2. 02A sufficientarian approach provides a clearer, more administrable policy agenda by focusing on universal access to concrete goods like healthcare, housing, and education rather than abstract quality-of-life metrics.
  3. 03By establishing a generous social floor, society can respect individual agency and responsibility without allowing personal failures to result in destitution or civic exclusion.
  4. 04Egalitarianism must pair sufficiency with 'lowering the ceiling' through taxation and regulation to prevent extreme wealth from undermining democratic equality and social cohesion.
  5. 05While the state should address non-material harms like loneliness and alienation, it should do so indirectly by providing public goods and leisure time rather than attempting to equalize flourishing directly.
