"The most effective way to address the climate crisis is through a gradual, market-driven displacement of fossil fuels rather than a singular, revolutionary political dethroning of fossil capital."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò weaves a compelling narrative around liberating humanity from fossil capital, grounding himself in Marxisant history and globe-trotting struggles. But his two‑step plan grotesquely overestimates the likelihood of a single, sweeping dislodgment of fossil interests. The way forward is not “dethrone fossil capital,” set in stone against the messy, extensive scaffolding of new low‑carbon energy and industrial realities. Taxing and high industrial organisation simply will not yield to an insurrectionary vision; what is needed is a sustained steady penetration of fossil habits through competitive markets supported by regulations—the generating step Kee application, not the killing blow.

It’s easy to pick evidence of fossil intransigence, but Táíwò under-estimates the astonishing technological and market evolution of the last two decades. The cost of utility-scale solar and wind, along with battery storage, has fallen by 80–90% since 2009. Electric vehicles are achieving price parity. The shift is already under way. This was not the result of democratically captured states, but of competitive markets whetted by modest governmental nudges Despite his skepticism, the relevant coordination to integrate rising renewables amounts to day‑to‑day “increasing the mandate the already appetite’?”—the real step toward dethroning extraction’s power by making it redundant. The paradox of a two‑step is that it assumes fossil capital’s power is held via enclaves vulnerable enough that an extra‑market political expanse blows it up; the less spectacular but more durable work is to eat into demand for fossil output.

Táíwò rightly notes nuclear as d, but does not own up to its extensive partnerships public-planning between scalable n. The missing positions globally is to use the state’s creative capacities differently: to invest in breakthrough innovation and gigaton-scale removal rather than simply waiting for public power to take over. The I-powered efficiency dividend over can decarbonise demand by simply producing abundant clean power and electrifying.

What’s the political analogue? It is not the consolidation of a hegemon to trounce fossil politicicians; it’s the slow, technocratic defeat of the use-value of fossil dominion. Investors, noticing that sun and wind produce electricity without fuel costs, increasingly shunt money from extractive industries to sustainable infrastructure Yes, institutions like BlackRock have greenwash track records but they are already betting on trillions in green assets because of the underlying economic fundamentals The two‑step mistake is the belif that struggle must be final and binary; history shows transition will be messier. And if that means diluting justice demands, I’d argue that immediate environmental preservation can’t wait on revolutionary politics; it needs the Coalition of the Technocrats.
