"The proposed 'two-step' strategy is insufficient because it focuses narrowly on dethroning fossil capital rather than addressing the underlying systemic drive of capital accumulation and the imperative for economic growth."

Táíwò’s lead essay is a timely admonition to face the elephant in the room that unless we defeat fossil capital, no progressive politics stands a chance. His historical excavtion of the “two-step” is instructive, and his call to clarity about primary contradictions is a welcome rap on the knuckles of those who confuse moral parity with strategic sequencing. Yet his argument’ foundation, as well as its terminus, lead directly into a dead end because it fundamentally misidentify the enemy and thus the nature of the necessary delinking.

For Táíwò, the first step is “dethrone fossil capital, This is a profoundly insufficient articulation of the problem. Fossil capital is not anomalous, it is not a rogue fraction It is a concentration and expression of the self-expansionary drive of capital as such The environmental crisis is driven not by one part of capital run amok but by the inherent tendency of a system compelled to constant growth and accumulation. By singling out fossil capital, Táíwò makes a fetish of one commodity while ignoring that under any post–fossil state-led “public” energy system, capital’s logic—growth, accumulation, and exploitation—would quickly attach itself to the new green commodities. It is the system of capital accumulation that requires dethroning, not merely one epochal fuel.

Samir Amin’s concept of delinking, which Táíwò invotes, was precisely a call to delink from the world capitalist system, not just from one faction of it. Amin’s delinking was about subordinated countries disentangling from the global division of labor to build self-reliant, internally focused economies—a move that implies a break with accumulation for accumulation’s sake. Adapting this to a planetary “delink the world economy from fossil fuels” misses the original’s anti-systemic thrust. By limiting the delinking to fuel sources, Táíwò implicitly retains the overarching architecture of growth and commodity production that will inevitably devour any finite resources

Consider Norway’s sovereign wealth fund, which Táíwò acknowledges is not exemped from environmental harm despite its progressive imago. Why? Because the fund is organically tied to the government’s need to manage an oil-based economy. Even if Norway shifted to 100% renewables and became post–, it would still accelerate resource extraction for export to maintain living standards—offshore fishng, mining of rare earths for turbines, construction of ever-more battery banks The underlying driver of ecological overshoot is the compulsion of unending economic growth A new green public sector under state capitalism will inevitably bump against planetary boundaries and either stalling to death or intensify extractivism This is what the degrowth camp has long recognised.

A truly transformative two-step must be a delinking from the growth imperative and from capital itself. This means a first step that is not merely about dethroning fossil fuel companies but about delinking social reproduction and well-being from insatiable accumulation That will need state power, yes, but of a kind radically different from the capacities Táíwò envisions. A degrowth state plan would reduce energy and material throughput, but its primary work would be redistributive: a job guarantee, reduced working time without loss of income, and the decommodification of housing, transport, and agriculture. Such a programme, built around universal basic services, is the only foundation for a genuinely egalitarian phase-out of not just fossil extraction but also of wasteful production.

Temporarily the defeat of fossil capital must be embedded in a broader systemic intervation, otherwise coal’s boots will simply be replaced by lithium boots. The two-step for our generation is: first, build a political coalition capable of delinking well-being from economic growth; then, within that framework, decarbonize. Without that first step, the immensity of the required socialization and redistribution of wealth to delink energy from ecological harm will never be achieved—private or public capital will resist, as Táíwò fears. But it’s not just fossil capital that will fight back; any capital will. The two-step is not wrong in form, but its content must be degrowth.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fossil capital is not a rogue fraction of the economy but a concentrated expression of capital’s inherent self-expansionary logic, which will simply migrate to green commodities if the system itself is not challenged.
  2. 02The concept of 'delinking' must be applied to the global capitalist system of growth and commodity production rather than just to fuel sources to avoid simply replacing fossil fuel extraction with other forms of destructive extractivism.
  3. 03A state-led energy transition that retains the growth imperative will inevitably hit planetary boundaries and fail to resolve the ecological crisis.
  4. 04The first strategic step must be to delink social well-being from economic growth through degrowth policies like universal basic services and decommodification to create the necessary framework for decarbonization.
  5. 05Any form of capital, not just fossil capital, will resist the redistribution of wealth required for a just transition, making an anti-capitalist degrowth framework a strategic necessity.
