"The perceived failure of non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) in the United States was not due to the tools themselves being unscientific, but rather a result of the country's fractured political leadership, lack of state capacity, and eroded social solidarity."

In "How Did We Fare on COVID-19?", Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee mount a sweeping attack on the use of non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) during the pandemic, concluding that there is a "dearth of evidence" that policies like lockdowns, mask mandates, and business closures actually saved lives. They point to the United States' domestic experience—specifically the lack of a clear mortality divergence between restrictive Democratic states and permissive Republican states prior to the vaccine rollout—as proof that the mitigation strategy was a costly, unscientific failure. This argument, while emotionally resonant in a country exhausted by years of disruption, is dangerously parochial. By constraining their analysis almost entirely to the deeply flawed, hyper-polarized American context, Macedo and Lee draw the wrong conclusions about the efficacy of NPIs, blaming the tools for the failures of the carpenters.

To understand why NPIs appeared to fail in the United States, one must look beyond our borders. If we zoom out to the global stage, the assertion that NPIs do not work collapses. Consider the experiences of countries like Australia, New Zealand, South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan in the pre-vaccine era of 2020 and 2021. These nations utilized the exact same toolkit that Macedo and Lee disparage—border closures, strict quarantine, aggressive contact tracing, mask mandates, and targeted lockdowns. The result? They maintained mortality rates that were a tiny fraction of the American death toll while often experiencing less severe long-term economic disruption because they were able to crush community transmission and return to domestic normalcy faster.

Why did NPIs work in Seoul and Sydney, but supposedly fail in Los Angeles and Miami? The answer is not that the virus behaves differently in different hemispheres, nor is it that the science of viral transmission is flawed. The answer lies in state capacity, social solidarity, and political leadership.

The American implementation of NPIs was fatally compromised from the very beginning by a fractured, decentralized system of government and a toxic political culture. Unlike nations with robust federal health authorities that coordinated national responses, the United States left pandemic management to fifty different governors, resulting in a chaotic patchwork of policies. A lockdown in Illinois is rendered largely ineffective if the bars are open in neighboring Indiana and interstate travel remains unrestricted. We did not execute a comprehensive strategy of viral suppression; we executed a porous, half-hearted mimicry of one.

Furthermore, NPIs require a high degree of public compliance to achieve epidemiological impact. They are behavioral tools, not magical shields. In the United States, basic public health measures were almost immediately weaponized as markers of partisan identity. Egged on by a President who openly flouted his own administration's guidelines and a media ecosystem that thrives on outrage, a significant portion of the American public actively resisted mitigation efforts. Mask-wearing became a culture war; defying gathering limits became a distorted expression of liberty. Macedo and Lee use Oxford’s stringency index to measure state policies, but policy on paper is meaningless without compliance in practice. A mandate that half the population aggressively ignores will naturally fail to produce the desired epidemiological results, but this failure indicates a breakdown in civic cohesion, not a flaw in the underlying epidemiological theory.

Macedo and Lee also dramatically understate the cost of inaction. They rightly highlight the severe harms of school closures, delayed medical care, and economic dislocation. These costs were agonizing and real. However, they analyze these costs in a vacuum, failing to seriously grapple with the counterfactual: what would the United States have looked like in 2020 without any NPIs?

Before the advent of vaccines, allowing a novel, highly contagious virus with a significant hospitalization rate to rip unchecked through a population of 330 million people would have resulted in catastrophic mass casualty events. We saw glimpses of this in New York in the spring of 2020 and in the Dakotas in the winter of 2020. Hospitals do not simply get busy; they collapse. When health systems are overrun, the fatality rate of the virus skyrockets because patients cannot receive oxygen or basic care, and the mortality rate for everything else—heart attacks, trauma, strokes—also spikes. The economic and social devastation of millions of deaths, millions more disabled by long-term post-viral illness, and a completely shattered healthcare infrastructure would have dwarfed the pain of the lockdowns.

The tragedy of the American COVID-19 response is not that we tried to use NPIs; it is that we lacked the societal discipline, the institutional trust, and the competent leadership required to use them effectively. Macedo and Lee’s retrospective is vital because it highlights exactly how disjointed and costly our specific national failure was. But their ultimate conclusion—that we should abandon these public health tools because they didn't work smoothly in the fractured American landscape—is an invitation to future disaster.

If we learn from the pandemic that we should simply surrender to the next novel pathogen because mitigation is too hard, we are condemning ourselves to unimaginable suffering. The true lesson of COVID-19 is that public health cannot succeed in a society tearing itself apart. We must rebuild our civic infrastructure, demand coherent federal leadership in times of crisis, and foster a culture of mutual obligation. Blaming the concept of quarantine and distancing for our profound political dysfunction is a comforting illusion, but it is one that will leave us entirely defenseless when the next pandemic arrives.
