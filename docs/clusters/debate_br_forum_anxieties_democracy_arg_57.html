"The essay argues that representative democracy is an obsolete 18th-century model and should be replaced by direct democracy facilitated by modern technology."

Ira Katznelson mounts a passionate defense of representative democracy against what he sees as rising threats from populism, deliberation, and direct plebiscites. He argues that only a centralized legislature can properly manage conflict and produce legitimate, contingent policy. But in his swift dismissal of direct democracy—which he reduces to the California referendum system where voters "watch others argue, and then they choose"—Katznelson betrays a 20th-century bias that ignores the technological realities and democratic potential of the 21st century.

Katznelson argues that representative democracy is necessary because, in large, complex societies, direct influence is "elusive." This was undeniably true in 1787, when information traveled at the speed of a horse, and it was mostly true in 1938 when E.M. Forster gave democracy his "two cheers." In those eras, electing a few representatives to travel to a central capital to read legislation and debate on behalf of the masses was the only logistical way to run a large republic.

But today, we live in an era of instantaneous global communication, decentralized networks, and unprecedented access to information. The logistical barriers to direct democratic participation have evaporated, yet we cling to an institutional model designed for the agrarian age.

Katznelson accurately identifies the failures of the modern legislature: it is captured by moneyed interests, distorted by partisan gerrymandering, and increasingly incapable of addressing long-term challenges. Yet his solution is to simply hope that we somehow regain a lost "civic virtue" or "background agreement." This is wishful thinking. The legislature is failing because the principal-agent problem at the heart of representation has become insurmountable. The representatives are no longer acting as agents of the people; they are agents of the donor class.

Direct democracy is not a dangerous circumvention of legitimate government; it is the necessary evolution of it. Katznelson criticizes plebiscites by claiming they lack deliberation. But this is a failure of current implementation, not the concept. We now have the technological architecture to create platforms for mass deliberation, secure voting, and liquid democracy (where citizens can vote directly on issues they care about, and temporarily delegate their vote to trusted experts on issues they don't).

Instead of relying on 535 out-of-touch politicians to understand the complexities of technology policy, climate change, or healthcare, we could harness the collective intelligence of the populace. Open-source software development and wiki-based knowledge creation have proven that massive, decentralized networks of humans can collaboratively solve complex problems and create robust rulesets without centralized, representative hierarchies.

When citizens feel that their voice does not matter, they retreat into apathy or destructive populism. By giving citizens direct, meaningful power over legislation, we force them to take responsibility for the outcomes. We foster the very civic education and engagement that Katznelson desires.

The scaffolding around the Capitol may indeed signify a need for reconstruction, but we should not simply rebuild the 18th-century structure. Just as the printing press enabled the shift from monarchy to representative democracy by allowing the spread of enlightenment ideals, the internet enables the shift from representative democracy to direct democracy. Katznelson’s fear of a "de-liberalization" is backwards. Bypassing a corrupt, gridlocked legislature to allow direct citizen lawmaking is not illiberalism; it is the ultimate fulfillment of the democratic promise.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Modern legislatures suffer from an insurmountable principal-agent problem where representatives serve the donor class rather than the citizenry.
  3. 03Technological architecture now allows for mass deliberation and 'liquid democracy,' which can harness collective intelligence more effectively than a small group of politicians.
  4. 04Direct participation in lawmaking fosters civic education and responsibility, providing a better solution to political apathy than traditional representative institutions.
  5. 05Direct citizen lawmaking is the ultimate fulfillment of democratic ideals rather than a threat to liberal values.
