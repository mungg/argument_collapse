The Reproductive Hinge: What Johnson Gestures Toward and Does Not Quite Hold

Walter Johnson writes, near the heart of his essay, that "racial capitalism swung on a reproductive hinge." It is a striking phrase, and the paragraph it introduces — invoking Deborah Gray White on sexual violation, reproductive invigilation, and natal alienation as "elementary aspects of slavery, and thus of racial capitalism" — is one of the essay's most important. I want to praise this gesture and then to argue that Johnson does not quite carry through on what it implies. The reproductive analytic is mentioned, given a sentence or two, and then largely set aside. Among the six "principal virtues" of his proposed framework, gender appears as the fourth item, presented as if it were one ingredient in a recipe. But the reproductive hinge is not one ingredient. It is the hinge. And the moment we take it seriously, the architecture of the argument has to be rebuilt.

Let me first say what Johnson gets right, because it is a great deal. The political economy of the antebellum United States, after the closing of the international slave trade in 1808, was uniquely dependent on enslaved women's reproductive capacity. The expansion of cotton across the Deep South — the very expansion that built Manchester and Manhattan in the way Johnson describes — would have been impossible without the natural increase of the enslaved population. The internal slave trade that moved people from the Upper South to the cotton frontier was, at its base, a trade in the children of enslaved women. The legal doctrine of *partus sequitur ventrem* — that the status of the child follows that of the mother — quietly performed an enormous amount of economic work: it converted reproductive labor into capital accumulation. Johnson, by citing DeBow's proposal to reopen the Atlantic trade as an alternative to natural increase, registers this dynamic. But he does not develop it.

What does developing it look like? It looks, first, like taking seriously that the racial-capitalist accumulation Johnson describes is not analogous to gendered exploitation; it is constitutively gendered exploitation. The category of the slave was, from the eighteenth century forward, structured around a specifically feminine reproductive function that was simultaneously extracted (as labor power), commodified (as the production of new slaves), and rendered invisible (as "natural" increase). Hortense Spillers, working in a register Johnson does not quite enter, has argued that under slavery the very categories of "mother" and "father" are denied to the enslaved in ways that produce a distinctive kinship grammar — what she famously calls the "American grammar." The point is not merely that enslaved women were exploited as women; it is that the gendered structure of slavery generated a set of categories that have continued to structure black social life and its representation long after formal emancipation.

If we take this seriously, several of Johnson's framings need adjustment.

Consider his critique of the "dehumanization" thesis. Johnson is right that slavery depended on the human capacities of enslaved people — labor, sentience, sociality. But the reproductive analytic suggests that what slavery did was not dehumanize so much as ungender. Spillers's argument is that the violence of slavery produced a category — the "flesh," as distinct from the "body" — that lay outside the gendered hierarchies of the bourgeois social order, available for forms of violation and use that the bourgeois subject was supposed to be protected from. The enslaved woman was not simply a worker who happened to be female; she occupied a position that was neither the wage-laborer of orthodox Marxism nor the domestic woman of bourgeois ideology. She was something else, and that something else is what racial capitalism required. Johnson's argument that we should "use the history of slavery as the source rather than the subject of knowledge" applies with particular force here: a political economy reconstructed from the standpoint of the enslaved woman would look quite different from one reconstructed from the standpoint of the male field hand.

Consider, next, Johnson's account of the relationship between capital and labor. He observes, correctly, that the enslaved were both capital and labor at once — that their double economic aspect could not be separated. But the case is even stranger when we add reproduction. The enslaved woman was capital, labor, and the means of producing new capital-laborers. Her body was a productive site, a workplace, and a commodity, all at once and frequently in violent simultaneity. No category of orthodox political economy — neither Adam Smith's nor Marx's — can hold this configuration. To name this we need a vocabulary that thinks production and reproduction together, and that thinks both as racially structured. The work of black feminist scholars — Angela Davis in *Women, Race and Class*, Hazel Carby, Saidiya Hartman, Jennifer Morgan's *Laboring Women* — has been developing this vocabulary for decades. Johnson cites a few of these scholars, but the engagement is more nominal than substantive.

Consider, finally, Johnson's program for justice. The fourth of his principal virtues is that an account of justice rooted in racial capitalism would be "attentive to questions of gender and sexuality, on the ways that reproductive invigilation and natal alienation . . . were core features of the human wrongs of slavery." Yes. But what does this mean concretely? If reparations are to take the form Johnson gestures toward, they must reckon with harms that are not easily monetized: the destruction of kinship relations, the long history of medical experimentation on black women's reproductive bodies, the ongoing structures of differential maternal mortality, the criminalization of black motherhood, the carceral targeting of black families. These are not addenda to a reparations program. They are the spine of one. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson cites includes provisions that move in this direction — demands around reproductive justice, around the freeing of incarcerated black mothers, around the family policing system. The framework Johnson articulates needs to internalize these as central rather than as derivative.

There is one further consequence of the reproductive analytic that I want to draw out, because Johnson does not. The sixth of his principal virtues is the relationship between the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature. He frames this primarily in terms of resource extraction — the elephants whose teeth become billiard balls, the land whose fertility is mined out by the cotton crop. But the deepest connection between racial capitalism and ecological domination runs through reproduction. The plantation was an experiment in industrialized biological production — of cotton plants, of livestock, of human beings. The techniques of selective breeding, the optimization of yield, the management of bodies for productive output: these were developed on the plantation before they were generalized across modern agriculture and modern industry. Donna Haraway and others have begun to trace the lines from plantation biopower to contemporary techniques of biological management. The "instrumentalization of nature" Johnson invokes is not parallel to the instrumentalization of slaves; it grew out of it, and the connecting tissue is the reproductive management of bodies, human and otherwise.

I have been pressing Johnson, but I want to close by acknowledging that his essay is genuinely opening a door. The phrase "reproductive hinge" alone is worth a great deal. What I am urging is that the door be walked through with greater willingness to let the destination be reshaped by what is found on the other side. The tradition Johnson invokes — Du Bois, Robinson, James, Williams, Rodney, Davis — is in fact richer on these questions than his essay quite registers. Angela Davis's work in particular has long insisted that the analysis of capitalism cannot proceed without the analysis of reproduction, and that the racial structure of capitalism is inseparable from the gendered structure of reproductive labor. Johnson cites Davis, but more as a name in a list than as a presence in the argument.

The stakes are not merely academic. The form an account of justice takes determines what kinds of redress it makes thinkable. An account rooted in the labor of field hands tends toward redress in the currency of labor — jobs, wages, working conditions. An account rooted in the reproductive labor of enslaved women, and in the ongoing afterlives of natal alienation, tends toward redress in the currency of kinship, bodily autonomy, and the social conditions of mothering and care. We need both. But the second has been chronically subordinated to the first, in the scholarship and in the politics. If Johnson's essay is to live up to its own promise — if the history of slavery is really to be the source rather than the subject of knowledge — then the reproductive hinge has to be more than a phrase.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The expansion of the antebellum economy was not merely supported by reproduction but was constitutively dependent on the legal and economic conversion of enslaved women's reproductive labor into capital accumulation.
  2. 02Slavery functioned less through 'dehumanization' and more through a process of 'ungendering' that placed enslaved women in a category of 'flesh' outside the protected hierarchies of bourgeois social order.
  3. 03The dual status of the enslaved as capital and labor is further complicated by reproduction, as the enslaved woman functioned simultaneously as capital, a workplace, and a producer of new capital-laborers.
  4. 04A justice framework rooted in the history of slavery must move beyond labor-based redress to prioritize the repair of kinship, bodily autonomy, and the social conditions of care.
  5. 05The instrumentalization of nature in racial capitalism is directly linked to the plantation's role as an early experiment in the industrialized biological management and reproductive optimization of both human and non-human life.
