Diversified DeepSeek

"While the economics of the past deserved its loss of credibility due to its detachment from reality, the field is currently undergoing a vital transformation into an empirical, humble, and essential science."

We are having the wrong conversation. Asking whether economists are overrated is like asking whether doctors are overrated in the era before germ theory. The answer is that a vital craft is in the midst of a painful but exhilarating transformation, and we should be cheering it on, not writing its obituary. The economics of the recent past was indeed overrated—a discipline drunk on mathematical elegance and detached from the messy reality of human behavior. But the economics that is emerging right now is an empirically grounded, humble, and deeply useful science that we underrate at our peril.

The old guard deserved its crisis of faith. For decades, the field’s internal reward system prized theoretical cleverness over real-world verification. A beautiful proof about general equilibrium was valued more highly than a historically grounded study of how a bank run actually unfolds. The models were filled with hyper-rational agents who never made cognitive errors, possessed perfect information, and inhabited markets that miraculously cleared. When the real world, populated as it is by panicked, short-sighted, and wonderfully irrational creatures, failed to cooperate, the models offered no warning.

But the reformation is well underway, driven by a new empiricism. The revolution in behavioral economics, led by figures like Kahneman and Thaler, has reintroduced actual human psychology into the core of the discipline. The rise of massive data sets and clever natural experiments, exemplified by the work of economists like Raj Chetty, is allowing researchers to trace the causal effects of policies—from tax credits to neighborhood environments—with a precision that was unimaginable a generation ago. This new breed of economist does not offer confident pronouncements from on high. She offers careful, partial, evidence-based insights into specific mechanisms. She is the first to tell you that a universal theory is impossible, but a careful analysis of a specific labor market friction is within reach. That kind of economics is not overrated; it is an essential antidote to ideology. The public’s cynicism is healthy, but we must not let it harden into a blanket dismissal of a field that is finally learning to walk on the ground.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The traditional economic guard failed because it prioritized mathematical elegance and theoretical proofs over real-world verification and historical context.
  2. 02Old economic models were fundamentally flawed because they relied on the assumption of hyper-rational agents rather than accounting for irrational human behavior.
  3. 03The rise of behavioral economics has successfully integrated actual human psychology back into the core of the discipline.
  4. 04Modern economists are using massive data sets and natural experiments to provide precise, evidence-based insights into the causal effects of specific policies.
  5. 05The new generation of economists has replaced overconfident universal theories with a more modest and useful focus on specific market mechanisms.
Diversified Gemini

"Economists are not overrated because the field has undergone an empirical revolution that provides data-driven, practical solutions to real-world problems."

The narrative that economics is a failed, overrated discipline is stuck in the past. Critics who point to the missed forecasts of the Great Recession are aiming their fire at a version of macroeconomics that is rapidly being eclipsed. We are currently living through an empirical revolution in economics, and rather than stripping the profession of its authority, we should be leaning into the incredible, data-driven insights it is beginning to produce.

Historically, much of economics was constrained by a lack of data, forcing scholars to rely on elegant but simplistic theoretical models. Today, however, the availability of massive administrative datasets and the computing power to analyze them has transformed economics into a deeply empirical, highly practical science. The field is no longer dominated by theorists debating abstract models of the business cycle. It is dominated by applied microeconomists who are solving granular, real-world problems.

Look at the recent work of scholars like Raj Chetty, who has used anonymized tax data to map social mobility in the United States down to the specific county, revealing exactly which neighborhoods help lift children out of poverty and which trap them in it. Or look at the work of Esther Duflo and the Poverty Action Lab at MIT, who have pioneered the use of randomized controlled trials—the gold standard of medical research—to figure out exactly which anti-poverty interventions work in the developing world, saving billions of dollars and millions of lives.

This is not the work of out-of-touch ivory tower forecasters. This is the work of mechanics rolling up their sleeves and figuring out how the engine actually runs. When policymakers face questions about how to structure teacher tenure, how to optimize Medicaid expansion, or how to design a tax credit that actually encourages workforce participation, the new empirical economists are the only ones providing answers based on hard evidence rather than ideological priors.

If economists are quoted frequently in the press, it is because they are uniquely equipped to parse the vast amounts of data that define the modern information age. Far from being overrated, the new breed of empirical economists is finally delivering on the long-promised potential of the social sciences: to replace guesswork and dogma with evidence and rigorous testing. We need their voices more than ever.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The profession has shifted from abstract theoretical modeling to a deeply empirical science powered by massive datasets and high computing power.
  2. 02Modern economists are successfully addressing granular social issues, such as mapping social mobility and testing the efficacy of anti-poverty interventions.
  3. 03Economists provide evidence-based answers to complex policy questions that would otherwise be decided by ideological dogma or guesswork.
  4. 04The frequent presence of economists in public discourse is justified by their unique ability to analyze and interpret the vast amounts of data defining the modern information age.
