"College students are a decisive constituency whose votes matter because they are numerous and strategically concentrated enough to swing outcomes in a closely divided country."

College students’ votes matter because elections are not won by abstractions; they are won by margins. In a closely divided country, a constituency does not need to be a majority to be decisive. It needs to be large enough, concentrated enough in competitive states, and motivated enough to change the outcome. College students can meet that test.

The clearest recent example is 2008 and, to a lesser extent, 2012. Barack Obama’s advantage among younger voters was not a decorative feature of his coalition; it helped him win states that Republicans had every reason to contest. Young voters made up roughly one-fifth of the electorate in 2012, and Obama won them by a wide margin. In close states, that is the difference between governing and giving a concession speech.

The same dynamic is visible in this year’s Democratic primaries. Senator Bernie Sanders’s huge support among younger voters has not guaranteed him the nomination, but it has changed the conversation. Free public college, student debt, campaign finance, inequality and climate change are being discussed with more urgency because young voters and campus organizers have demanded it. Even where they do not control the final outcome, they alter the incentives of candidates who want to assemble a winning coalition.

There is also a multiplier effect. College campuses are unusually dense organizing environments. A student can register a roommate, drag a friend to a caucus, staff a phone bank, circulate a petition, or host a debate-watching party without leaving a few blocks. That makes civic engagement at universities more efficient than in many other settings.

But none of this means college students matter automatically. A rally is not a ballot. A trending hashtag is not turnout. Politicians respond to people who vote consistently, especially in primaries, midterms and local elections. Seniors have power not because they are morally more deserving but because they show up. If students vote only in presidential elections when a charismatic candidate excites them, they will be treated as an unreliable bonus rather than a core constituency.

The answer, then, is yes — but conditionally. College students matter when they register, when they learn the rules in the state where they live, when they vote down the ballot, and when they keep voting after graduation. They are not the whole electorate, and they cannot substitute for broader coalitions. But in a polarized era, they are far too numerous and strategically placed to dismiss.
