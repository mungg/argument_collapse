"While sharing the goal of a multiracial democracy, the author argues that populism is an inherently flawed political form that risks undermining the very institutions, pluralism, and democratic norms necessary for achieving egalitarian justice."

I share Adom Getachew’s desire for a more egalitarian and multiracial democracy. I am less convinced that “populism” is the language or political form best suited to achieve it. The problem is not simply that the term has been unfairly maligned by centrist commentators who prefer technocratic governance to popular mobilization. That has surely happened. The problem is that populism, even in its left variants, carries recurring temptations that are not accidental: the temptation to imagine a unified people where there is disagreement; to reduce institutional mediation to obstruction; to treat opponents as enemies of democracy rather than participants in it; and to confuse moral clarity with political simplification.

Getachew is right that all democratic politics involves appeals to the people. No election can proceed without claims about public interest, majorities, constituencies, and representation. But it does not follow that every politician is a populist in any meaningful sense. If every democratic appeal is populist, then the word does little analytical work. If, however, populism means something more specific—a politics that divides society between a virtuous people and a corrupt elite, and claims special legitimacy for those who speak in the people’s name—then its dangers are more than reputational. They are built into the form.

The United States in April 2020 does not suffer from too much pluralism, too much institutional restraint, or too much respect for expertise. It suffers from minority rule, partisan polarization, racial injustice, economic inequality, and a president who has shown contempt for truth and constitutional limits. The COVID-19 crisis has made these failures painfully visible. But it has also shown why institutions and expertise matter. Public health agencies, hospitals, state unemployment systems, scientific researchers, inspectors general, local governments, journalists, courts, and legislatures are not romantic objects. Many are flawed; some are deeply unjust. Yet the response to a pandemic depends on competence, accountability, and institutional trust. A politics that intensifies suspicion of mediation and elevates popular will as the answer to every problem can make democratic governance harder rather than easier.

To be clear, the status quo before the pandemic was indefensible. Millions lacked health insurance or had insurance so precarious that illness could mean financial ruin. Workers were expected to appear at their jobs sick because paid leave was treated as a perk rather than a public necessity. Prisons and jails were overcrowded. Black Americans faced disproportionate exposure to chronic disease, environmental harm, medical neglect, and now death from COVID-19. Immigrants lived under threat of detention and deportation. None of these facts should be minimized in the name of institutional moderation.

But the alternative to left populism is not complacency. It is a democratic egalitarianism that combines social-democratic policy, racial justice, constitutional safeguards, administrative competence, and pluralist coalition-building. Such a politics can be passionate without being populist. It can mobilize majorities without claiming that one movement uniquely embodies “the people.” It can confront oligarchy without collapsing every disagreement into a battle between the people and their enemies.

One of the risks in current debates is that “populism” becomes a flattering self-description for any politics that is more redistributive than neoliberalism. But the achievements Getachew highlights—the Chicago Teachers Union strikes, the demand for nurses and librarians in schools, the organizing of education workers and support staff, the election of local left candidates—do not require the category of populism to be admired. They can be understood as labor democracy, social movement unionism, municipal progressivism, or public goods politics. Indeed, what is most impressive about the CTU is not that it spoke as “the people” against “the elite,” but that it made specific, institutionally grounded demands: smaller classes, more staff, better pay, improved school conditions. It engaged parents, neighborhoods, and public officials in a fight over the concrete meaning of public education.

That is a model worth emulating. But it is not obvious that the broader populist frame improves it. Teachers’ unions succeed when they persuade parents and communities that their interests are linked, not when they imply that dissenters are outside the people. A strike is powerful because it is organized, accountable, and negotiable. It has leaders, procedures, demands, and endpoints. Populist politics often borrows the moral energy of such struggles while loosening their discipline.

The Sanders campaign presents a similar ambiguity. Its great contribution has been to expand the horizon of American political debate. Medicare for All, tuition-free public college, student debt cancellation, a Green New Deal, and an aggressive critique of billionaire power are now part of mainstream Democratic argument in a way they were not a decade ago. The campaign mobilized young people, normalized democratic socialism for many, and correctly insisted that economic inequality is a political choice. These are substantial achievements.

Yet Sanders’s defeat also reveals the limits of a populist theory of majority formation. Many voters who would benefit from his policies did not support him. Older black voters in particular were often more loyal to Joe Biden, not because they were duped by elites, but because they made judgments shaped by memory, risk, party attachment, and the overriding desire to defeat Donald Trump. Some voters preferred incremental reform; some doubted the feasibility of Sanders’s agenda; some trusted Biden because of his association with Barack Obama; some feared that “socialism” would be an electoral liability. One may disagree with these judgments, but a democratic politics must take them seriously.

The populist habit is to interpret such resistance as evidence of establishment manipulation: the media manufactured consent, party elites closed ranks, voters were scared by corporate interests. These things may all contain elements of truth. But they are insufficient. Voters are not clay. They have reasons, identities, loyalties, fears, and priorities. A politics that claims to speak for the working class must not treat actual working-class disagreement as false consciousness.

A pluralist egalitarian politics begins from the fact that there is no single people waiting to be assembled behind the correct antagonism. There are many publics, many injuries, many forms of exploitation, and many legitimate disagreements about means and ends. Democracy is not only the rule of majorities; it is a system for living with disagreement among equals. That requires rights, institutions, parties, courts, independent media, federalism, civil society, and norms of restraint. These are not substitutes for justice, but neither are they mere obstacles to it.

The challenge is especially acute in a multiracial society. Getachew and the forum participants are rightly concerned with the persistence of white supremacy. But this concern should make us more cautious about populism, not less. In American history, appeals to “the people” have often depended on racial exclusion. The people were white farmers, white workers, white taxpayers, white homeowners, white citizens. Black claims were cast as special pleading, immigrant claims as foreign intrusion, indigenous claims as obstacles to national development. Even when economic populists denounced banks, railroads, monopolists, and distant elites, they frequently compromised with racial hierarchy.

A “multiracial populism” seeks to reverse this inheritance, but the adjective does a lot of work. If the people are constituted through an antagonism against elites, what prevents some members of the coalition from identifying racial minorities, immigrants, experts, or urban residents as the real beneficiaries of elite favoritism? Right-wing populism has succeeded precisely by fusing anti-elite rhetoric with racial resentment. It tells white voters that liberal elites look down on them while giving undeserved advantages to minorities and immigrants. That story is false and poisonous, but it is emotionally potent. Left populism must either fight it directly, thereby risking the loss of some voters it hopes to win, or mute racial conflict in the name of class unity, thereby betraying its multiracial promise.

There are left answers to this dilemma, but they require careful coalition politics more than populist rhetoric. They require showing how universal programs can reduce racial inequality without pretending that universalism alone is enough. They require targeted remedies for communities harmed by discrimination, alongside broad social insurance. They require police and prison reform, voting rights, fair housing, environmental justice, immigrant rights, and health equity. They require language that is honest about racism without implying that all white working-class people are irredeemable. This is difficult democratic work. It cannot be solved by invoking “the people.”

The pandemic likewise calls for more institutional imagination than populism usually supplies. We need massive public investment, but also capable administration. We need universal health coverage, but also supply chains, testing regimes, epidemiological surveillance with privacy safeguards, hospital coordination, and clear public communication. We need emergency economic relief, but also unemployment offices that can process claims, small-business support that does not simply reward the well-connected, and oversight to prevent corruption. The slogan “people over profits” captures an essential moral truth. It does not tell us how to govern.

This matters because distrust is now one of America’s gravest civic diseases. Some distrust is earned. The federal government has lied about wars, tolerated racial injustice, bailed out banks while abandoning homeowners, and allowed corporate influence to corrode policy. But generalized distrust is combustible. It can lead people to dismiss public health guidance, reject vaccination, believe conspiracy theories, or see every institution as part of a hostile plot. Trump’s politics thrives in that atmosphere. A responsible left should distinguish between warranted criticism of power and indiscriminate anti-institutionalism.

The international dimension strengthens this argument. Getachew condemns the Trump administration’s immigration restrictions, deportations, sanctions, and anti-Chinese rhetoric, and rightly criticizes Biden’s willingness to echo confrontation with China in a campaign ad. The pandemic demands cooperation, not nationalist scapegoating. Yet populism is often weakest here because its basic grammar is domestic: our people versus their elites, our workers versus foreign competitors, our sovereignty versus global constraint. Left populists can and do articulate internationalist versions, but the fit is uneasy. If the emotional center of politics is a bounded people, international obligations may appear secondary when scarcity intensifies.

A pluralist social democracy can say more forthrightly that democratic states have duties to citizens and duties to noncitizens. It can defend national welfare states while supporting refugee rights, global public health, debt relief, and an end to sanctions that impede medical access. It need not pretend that all claims are identical. It can acknowledge that elected governments are specially accountable to their own residents while insisting that human rights and global interdependence limit nationalist self-regard. That balance is hard, but it is more honest than stretching populism until it means international solidarity too.

The strongest argument for reclaiming populism is strategic. If right-wing forces are successfully constituting “the people” in exclusionary terms, must the left not contest the same terrain? If ordinary citizens feel abandoned by technocratic liberalism, does egalitarian politics not need a language of conflict and belonging? I accept much of this. Politics is not a seminar; it requires symbols, emotions, enemies, and collective identification. The left cannot win with policy white papers alone. It must speak to anger, dignity, and betrayal.

But there is a difference between popular politics and populism. A popular politics seeks majority support for justice while respecting the legitimacy of opposition and the necessity of institutions. A populist politics tends to identify one movement with the people as such. The distinction is not always sharp in practice, but it is worth preserving. We should reclaim popular sovereignty, not populism; mass democracy, not the fantasy of a unitary people; social citizenship, not charismatic anti-elitism.

What would this look like now? It would mean pushing the Democratic Party toward a far more ambitious emergency response: direct cash payments, expanded unemployment insurance, paid leave, health coverage for the uninsured, protection for frontline workers, aid to states and cities, support for hospitals, and safeguards against corporate profiteering. It would mean demanding racial data on COVID-19 infections and deaths, targeted resources for black and Latino communities, decarceration where public health requires it, and protection for immigrants regardless of status. It would mean strengthening unions and worker voice, including for gig and care workers. It would mean preparing for an election under pandemic conditions through vote-by-mail, safe polling places, and protection against voter suppression.

It would also mean rebuilding trust in democratic government. That requires not only boldness but competence. If progressives win power and administer programs badly, the right will use failure to discredit the public sector for another generation. Universal programs must work. Agencies must be funded and staffed. Expertise must be democratized, not denigrated. Public health officials should be accountable, but their knowledge should not be treated as just another elite opinion. The administrative state can be a vehicle of domination; it can also be a vehicle of social rights. The left should fight to make it the latter.

There is a legitimate worry that this language sounds too restrained for the scale of the crisis. But restraint about populism need not mean restraint about justice. One can demand transformative policy while rejecting the notion that politics is a battle between the authentic people and illegitimate enemies. One can be radical about material equality and liberal about civil freedom. One can seek majority rule while protecting minorities. One can distrust concentrated wealth while valuing courts, journalism, science, and constitutional process.

The future after COVID-19 will not be determined automatically. Getachew is right: the crisis may deepen the rightward trend as easily as it may strengthen progressive causes. That is precisely why the form of progressive politics matters. If the left responds with its own simplified moral dualism, it may win some battles while corroding the democratic culture it hopes to redeem. If it responds with technocratic caution, it will fail to meet the moment. The task is to build a democratic egalitarianism that is militant about human needs, clear about racial injustice, serious about institutions, and humble about representation.

Populism gets a bad rap partly because elites use the term to dismiss challenges from below. But it also gets a bad rap because the claim to speak for “the people” has too often licensed exclusion, contempt, and institutional vandalism. We do not need to reclaim that inheritance. We need to build something better: a popular, pluralist, multiracial social democracy capable of governing in crisis and preserving the democratic conditions under which disagreement can continue.
