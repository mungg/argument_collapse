"The United States' political paralysis is caused by a polarized political culture and party system that rewards obstruction, rather than the constitutional structure of checks and balances itself."

The Veto Points Are Not the Problem

Lisa Miller's essay is a bracing read, and I share its frustration with the spectacle of Democratic leaders treating "checks and balances" as a magical incantation against Trumpism. But her diagnosis confuses a symptom for the disease. The United States is not a uniquely sclerotic polity because James Madison gave us bicameralism, an independent judiciary, and federalism. It is sclerotic because we have built, on top of those constitutional bones, a political culture and a party system that systematically rewards obstruction and punishes governing. Tear out the veto points and you will not get the responsive social democracy Miller envisions. You will get something closer to what Viktor Orbán built in Hungary—a parliamentary majority unchecked by counter-majoritarian institutions, governing in the name of "the people" while methodically dismantling the press, the courts, and the opposition.

Consider Miller's central comparative claim: that the United States is alone among wealthy democracies in lacking universal health care, and that this anomaly is explained by our excess of veto points. The intuition is plausible. But Canada, which Miller implicitly contrasts with the U.S., has a Westminster system layered onto a federal structure with strong provincial autonomy over health policy. Germany, often celebrated for Bismarckian universal coverage, has a federal structure, a powerful Bundesrat that operates as a second chamber with real veto authority, and a Constitutional Court whose activism makes John Roberts look restrained. Switzerland has more veto points than the United States by almost any measure—a collegial executive, mandatory referenda, deep cantonal federalism—and yet provides universal coverage. The proliferation of veto points is not destiny.

What distinguishes the United States is not that our institutions allow blocking; it is that one of our two major parties has, for roughly half a century, been organized around the proposition that the federal government should not provide universal health care, and has been willing to use every available institutional lever to enforce that proposition. The veto points are the weapons, not the cause. Change the institutional architecture and a determined ideological minority will find new weapons. Eliminate the filibuster and they will gerrymander harder. Expand the House and they will weaponize state legislatures. The fundamental problem is asymmetric polarization combined with a media ecosystem that rewards intransigence.

Miller's prescription—mass mobilization through the Democratic Party to enact constitutional reforms that would reduce veto opportunities—is internally contradictory in a way she does not adequately confront. If our system is as elite-captured and obstruction-prone as she claims, how does she imagine passing a constitutional amendment requiring three-fourths of state legislatures? She gestures at history: women's suffrage took forty-two years. But the amendments that actually reduced veto points are vanishingly few. The Seventeenth Amendment is perhaps the only clear example, and its long-run effects on Senate accountability are contested. The structural reforms Miller wants—eliminating the Electoral College, neutering the Senate, ending judicial life tenure—are precisely the sort of changes that the existing structure makes impossible. So her program is not a program; it is a wish.

There is a deeper issue. Miller assumes that the public interest is reasonably well-defined and that obstruction is what prevents government from serving it. This is the recurring fantasy of majoritarian reformers: that beneath the institutional brambles lies a coherent popular will waiting to be liberated. But polling consistently shows that Americans hold contradictory preferences. They want lower taxes and more services. They want universal health coverage and to keep their employer-sponsored insurance. They want secure borders and pathways to citizenship. They want fewer regulations and more consumer protections. The job of democratic politics is not to "release" some pre-existing majority preference but to forge actual coalitions through deliberation, negotiation, and compromise. Veto points—at their best—force that work to happen.

Miller is right that lobbying is most effective when aimed at blocking change. But it does not follow that change is therefore good and blocking bad. Some changes are bad. The Reconstruction Amendments were enacted by a Congress that had effectively expelled the South; the result was a generation of genuine progress followed by violent reaction. The PATRIOT Act passed quickly through a panicked Congress. The Iraq War authorization sailed through with bipartisan supermajorities. Veto points slow down both good change and bad change. The relevant question is whether, on average, the changes that get blocked are worse than the changes that get through. That is a deeply uncertain empirical question, and Miller's confidence that more action would mean better action is unwarranted.

Finally, the essay's treatment of Trump is curiously evasive. Miller acknowledges that Trump is "advancing rank corruption and kleptocracy" and that he is bypassing Congress through executive orders precisely because his agenda is unpopular. But this is an argument for checks and balances, not against them. Right now, in May 2025, the only institutions that have meaningfully slowed the administration's worst impulses are federal courts—the very institutions Miller dismisses as elite, anti-democratic, and reactionary. District judges blocking unconstitutional executive orders, appellate courts pausing illegal deportations, state attorneys general filing suits to protect federal funding: these are checks and balances doing exactly what Schumer and Clinton invoked them to do. They are imperfect, often slow, sometimes captured. But they exist, and they bind.

Miller's response would presumably be that we should not rely on them, that mass mobilization is the only sustainable answer. Fine. But "and also" is different from "instead of." The lesson of comparative authoritarian backsliding is that countries with multiple, redundant veto institutions are harder to capture than countries with streamlined majoritarian systems. Hungary fell in a decade because Fidesz could rewrite the constitution with a parliamentary supermajority. Poland's PiS faced more resistance because Polish institutions, though weaker than ours, still had some independent footholds. The friction Miller decries is precisely what makes authoritarian consolidation difficult in the United States—even as we watch an administration that is testing every limit.

The honest case for Miller's position would be: yes, dismantling checks and balances increases the risk of authoritarianism, but the cost of paralysis is so high that the gamble is worth taking. I do not find that argument persuasive, but at least it is coherent. The argument that reducing veto points will simultaneously empower progressive policy and constrain authoritarian elites requires a faith in the alignment of "the people" with the author's politics that the past decade should have shaken in anyone paying attention. The people elected Trump. Twice. A system that more efficiently translates majoritarian preferences into policy is a system that more efficiently translates Trumpism into law.

That is not a reason to celebrate gridlock. It is a reason to be careful about which locks we pick.

"The current paralysis of American democracy is not an inherent defect of the Madisonian constitutional structure itself, but rather a result of contingent political choices and statutory rules that can be reformed without the daunting task of constitutional amendment."

The Madisonian System Is Not the Author's Real Enemy

Lisa Miller has written a smart and pugnacious essay, and I find myself agreeing with much of its diagnosis even as I think its account of American constitutionalism is historically tendentious in ways that matter. The essay treats the Madisonian system—separation of powers, federalism, judicial review, bicameralism—as an originalist project of elite restraint that has functioned, by design, to obstruct popular government. This is too simple. The actual American constitutional order has always been a contested terrain on which different visions of democracy have struggled, and the version of constitutionalism Miller decries is itself a relatively recent and specifically conservative construction.

Consider the institutions Miller treats as the core of the problem. The filibuster, which she rightly identifies as a major obstruction to popular legislation, is not in the Constitution. It is a Senate rule, codified in its modern form in the twentieth century, that could be eliminated tomorrow by majority vote. Lifetime judicial tenure is constitutional, but the size of the Supreme Court is statutory and has been changed multiple times. Judicial review itself is not in the constitutional text—it is a doctrine the Court asserted in Marbury v. Madison and that has fluctuated in scope ever since. The Electoral College is constitutional but its functioning is shaped by state-level winner-take-all rules that are themselves not required by the Constitution. The "checks and balances" Miller decries are partly Madisonian inheritance, partly nineteenth-century innovation, and partly twentieth-century accretion.

This matters because Miller's polemic conflates the genuinely problematic features of contemporary American government with the constitutional structure itself, and in doing so makes reform seem more daunting than it actually is. We do not need a constitutional amendment to abolish the filibuster. We do not need a constitutional amendment to expand the Supreme Court, regulate its jurisdiction, or impose term limits through statute on the lower federal bench. We do not need a constitutional amendment to expand the House of Representatives, which is held at 435 only by an act of Congress passed in 1929. We do not need a constitutional amendment to enact the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact, which would effectively neutralize the Electoral College once enough states sign on. The constitutional structure is more reformable than Miller's framing suggests, but the reform agenda is largely statutory and political, not constitutional.

This connects to a deeper historical point. Miller treats the Framers as architects of elite restraint, citing the familiar critique that the Constitution was designed by propertied men to protect their property against democratic majorities. This is the Charles Beard reading of American constitutionalism, and it has a long tradition. But it is not the whole story. The Federalist papers themselves contain extended arguments for energetic government—Hamilton's vision in particular was of a strong national state capable of governing a commercial republic. The Anti-Federalists, whose worries Miller's essay sometimes echoes, were not advocates for centralized democratic majoritarianism; they were advocates for localism and small-republic government of exactly the kind Miller criticizes when she talks about federalism's tendency to entrench local hierarchies.

The actual constitutional tradition is more contested than either the "elite restraint" reading or the "democratic majoritarianism" reading allows. Madison himself, by the 1790s, was already arguing for stronger popular control of government and a more vigorous Republican party as a check on Hamiltonian elitism. The Reconstruction Amendments rewrote the Constitution in fundamentally majoritarian and nationalist directions, creating federal authority over civil rights and citizenship that the original Constitution lacked. The progressive amendments of the early twentieth century—direct election of senators, income tax, women's suffrage—pushed the structure further toward popular government. The constitutional order Miller decries is the result of a long political struggle in which democratic forces have repeatedly won and lost ground.

This suggests a different framing of the present crisis than Miller offers. The problem is not that the Constitution as such is anti-democratic, nor that "checks and balances" as such are anti-democratic. The problem is that the specific configuration of American institutions in the early twenty-first century has been captured by a coalition that uses constitutional and quasi-constitutional mechanisms to entrench minority rule. Senate malapportionment was always a feature, but it has become more politically consequential as the geographic distribution of partisan support has shifted. Gerrymandering has always existed, but new technologies have made it vastly more effective. Campaign finance was always influenced by wealth, but Citizens United and the rise of dark money have changed the scale. The filibuster was always available, but the norm of using it sparingly has been abandoned.

In other words, the present sclerosis is not a destiny inherited from 1787; it is a contingent political achievement of the contemporary American right, built on top of constitutional structures that are more flexible than they appear. The corollary is that reversing this achievement is not necessarily a multi-decade constitutional project. Some of it could be done quickly with a determined congressional majority. Filibuster reform plus statehood for D.C. and Puerto Rico plus statutory protection of voting rights plus an expanded Supreme Court plus a return to historical norms of executive restraint would dramatically change the operation of American government within a single congressional session.

The obstacle to this program is not the Constitution; it is the absence of a stable congressional majority willing to enact it. Building such a majority requires the kind of mass politics Miller calls for, and on that she is exactly right. But the framing of "the system itself is the problem" risks pointing political energy at the wrong target. The system has been worse in the past and has been reformed before, and within the existing constitutional framework. What we need is not to overthrow Madisonianism but to recapture it for democratic purposes.

A final note. Miller writes that "nearly all of the substantive amendments to the Constitution since the Eleventh in 1795 have made it more inclusive, more democratic, or sought to strengthen the national government and make it more effective." This is true, and it is a more hopeful observation than the rest of her essay suggests. The constitutional tradition is not just a story of elite restraint frustrating popular majorities. It is also a story of those majorities periodically breaking through and reshaping the structure. The Reconstruction Amendments. The progressive amendments. The expansion of civil rights enforcement under the Fourteenth Amendment. The constitutional tradition Miller decries also contains the resources for its own reform.

The challenge is not to escape Madison. It is to remember that Madison's own tradition is more capacious than the version his contemporary defenders want to enforce.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Many of the most significant obstacles to popular government, such as the filibuster and the fixed size of the House and Supreme Court, are statutory or procedural rather than constitutional mandates.
  2. 02The essay's critique relies on a historically narrow 'elite restraint' interpretation of the Constitution that ignores competing traditions of energetic government and successful democratic expansions.
  3. 03Framing the constitutional system itself as the primary enemy risks misdirecting political energy away from achievable legislative reforms that could be enacted by a determined congressional majority.
  4. 04The current crisis of minority rule is a modern political achievement of the American right rather than an inevitable destiny inherited from the Framers.
  5. 05The American constitutional tradition has historically proven flexible enough to be recaptured and reshaped by mass majorities to serve democratic purposes.

The Comparative Picture Is More Mixed Than Miller Admits

I want to take seriously Lisa Miller's central empirical claim—that American checks and balances are a comparative anomaly that explains America's distinctive policy failures—and push back on it. Her case rests heavily on the universal health care example, but the comparative political science of veto points and policy outcomes is considerably more contested than her essay suggests, and the implications for institutional reform are more ambiguous.

Let me start with the health care case, because it is the centerpiece of her argument. Miller asserts that the United States is "the only one [among wealthy democracies] that lacks national, universal health coverage" and that this is explained by "the unusually complex array of veto opportunities that opponents of health care policy reforms can access." But this elides the actual variation among wealthy democracies in how they organize health care. Germany has a multi-payer system grounded in occupational sickness funds. The Netherlands has a regulated private insurance market with mandatory coverage. Switzerland has a regulated private insurance market with mandatory coverage, layered onto strong cantonal federalism and frequent referenda that act as additional veto points. Canada has a single-payer system administered at the provincial level. France has a hybrid system. The United Kingdom has socialized medicine through the NHS.

These systems were established at different times under different political configurations. Switzerland passed its current universal health insurance law in 1994, under a system with arguably more veto points than the United States, including direct democracy through referenda. The relevant variable is not just the number of veto points but the structure of political competition, the strength of organized labor, the timing of welfare state development, and the role of religious and confessional parties in social policy. Esping-Andersen's classic typology of welfare state regimes maps onto institutional structure only loosely.

The U.S. case is overdetermined in ways Miller does not fully acknowledge. Yes, veto points matter. But so does the absence of a major social democratic party with strong labor union backing—a structural feature of American politics that long predates the contemporary configuration of veto points and that reflects deep features of American class structure, racial politics, and immigration history. The early-twentieth-century failure of European-style social democracy in the United States is not principally an institutional story; it is a story about why the American working class never developed the organizational and ideological coherence to demand European-style welfare provision. The Selig Perlman and Werner Sombart literature on American exceptionalism, whatever its limits, points to causal factors that operate independently of constitutional design.

This matters because it implies that institutional reform alone will not produce European-style social democratic policy outcomes. Eliminate the filibuster, expand the Supreme Court, abolish the Electoral College, and you will still have a country with a weak labor movement, deep racial divisions, a fragmented working class, and an ideological tradition skeptical of state authority. These factors shape what congressional majorities want to do, not just what they are institutionally permitted to do. Comparative scholarship by people like Kathleen Thelen and Paul Pierson on welfare state development suggests that institutional configurations interact with class formation, party systems, and policy legacies in ways that resist simple causal claims.

A second issue with Miller's comparative argument is selection bias in which policies she considers. She lists policies that supermajorities of Americans purportedly support but that the system has blocked: minimum wage increases, universal health care, paid family leave, immigration reform, background checks. Set aside the fact that polling on most of these issues is sensitive to question wording and that "majority support in the abstract" often collapses when specific provisions are debated. The deeper issue is that this list excludes policies the American system has produced that comparative systems have struggled with. The United States has a more aggressive antitrust tradition than most European countries (or did, until recently). The United States has stronger constitutional protections for free speech than any other democracy. The United States has more robust judicial enforcement of civil liberties against police and prosecutorial overreach in some respects. The United States produces world-leading research universities, scientific innovation, and cultural production at scale.

These are not unmitigated goods, and the costs of the American model are real. But a comparative analysis that focuses only on the policies the American system fails to produce, while ignoring the policies it produces that other systems do not, is going to overstate the relative dysfunction of American institutions. Every institutional configuration has tradeoffs. The Westminster system produces more decisive government but also more abrupt policy reversals when governments change. Continental European systems produce more durable welfare states but also more entrenched insider-outsider divides in labor markets and more difficulty integrating immigrants. The American system produces fragmented and inadequate social provision but also significant pluralism, innovation, and protection of dissent.

A third concern is Miller's treatment of public opinion. She repeatedly invokes the fact that majorities of Americans support various policies that the system fails to enact. The implication is that a more responsive system would translate these majority preferences into law. But the relationship between mass public opinion and policy is more complex than the median voter theorem suggests. As Larry Bartels, Martin Gilens, and Benjamin Page have documented, policy outcomes correlate more strongly with the preferences of wealthy citizens than with the preferences of median citizens. Miller cites this literature approvingly. But she does not engage with its more uncomfortable implication: even in democracies with fewer veto points than the United States, policy generally tracks elite preferences rather than median preferences. The Gilens-Page finding, while contested, suggests that the bias toward economic elites is a fairly general feature of contemporary representative democracy, not a peculiar feature of the American veto-point system.

If this is right, then reducing American veto points might modestly improve responsiveness to median preferences but would not fundamentally alter the elite bias of policymaking. Achieving that would require more than institutional reform; it would require shifting the underlying distribution of political resources, which means stronger unions, more equal campaign finance, better protections for political organizing, and so on. Miller acknowledges this in places but does not let it disturb her central argument that institutional reform is the key.

I do not mean to defend the status quo. The Senate is poorly designed for the contemporary political coalition. The Electoral College is indefensible. The filibuster is an embarrassment. These features should be reformed. But the comparative case for treating American institutions as uniquely responsible for American policy failures is weaker than Miller suggests, and the implicit promise that institutional reform will produce European-style social democratic outcomes is overstated. The fight for institutional reform should proceed, but with humility about how much it can accomplish on its own.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure to achieve universal health care in the U.S. is driven more by the absence of a strong social democratic party and labor movement than by the number of constitutional veto points.
  2. 02Focusing only on the social welfare policies the U.S. fails to produce ignores the unique policy successes, such as robust free speech and antitrust traditions, that the American institutional structure has historically enabled.
  3. 03The bias of policy toward elite preferences is a general feature of modern representative democracies and is not uniquely caused by the American system of checks and balances.
  4. 04Institutional reforms like abolishing the filibuster or the Electoral College will not automatically result in social democratic outcomes without addressing underlying issues like racial divisions and a fragmented working class.
  5. 05Comparative examples, such as Switzerland, demonstrate that countries with high numbers of veto points and decentralized structures can still successfully implement universal health care.

Parties, Not Constitutions

The most useful frame for thinking about the current American crisis is not the constitutional design Miller critiques, but the structure of the party system that operates within and around it. Miller acknowledges parties at various points—she ends her essay calling for revitalized partisan majoritarianism through the Democratic Party—but her analysis treats party behavior as largely derivative of institutional structure. I think this gets the causation backwards. American institutions look the way they do today because of how American parties have evolved, and reforming the institutions without reforming the parties will accomplish less than her essay implies.

Here is the basic point. The same constitutional structure that Miller diagnoses as paralyzed today functioned relatively effectively in earlier eras. The mid-twentieth century saw an enormous expansion of federal authority, the creation of the modern welfare state, civil rights legislation, and major regulatory reforms—all under the same Madisonian framework. What changed was not the framework; what changed was the structure of the parties operating within it.

Three features of the contemporary party system explain most of what Miller attributes to constitutional design.

First, the parties are unusually ideologically sorted. Through most of American history, both major parties were heterogeneous coalitions containing significant ideological diversity. Northern liberal Republicans and conservative Southern Democrats were normal categories. This heterogeneity meant that cross-party coalitions on specific issues were routine, and that the obstructive potential of veto points was diluted by the difficulty of organizing solid party blocs against legislation. Today's parties are ideologically homogeneous to a degree unprecedented in American history. The most conservative Democrat is more liberal than the most liberal Republican, with virtually no exceptions. This means that veto points get used at maximum effectiveness, with party leaders able to enforce discipline against the opposition party's program almost regardless of content. Miller's diagnosis of veto points as obstacles is correct, but veto points were always there. What changed is the willingness and ability of parties to use them in unified opposition.

Second, the parties are nationalized in their funding, communication, and political incentives in ways that disconnect them from local conditions. A Republican senator from Maine and a Republican senator from Texas now face essentially the same media environment, donor base, and electoral pressures from the national party apparatus. This nationalization eliminates the cross-pressures that historically forced moderation. A Republican senator who might want to support a popular bipartisan bill on prescription drug pricing faces immediate pressure from national party media, donors, and primary challengers in ways that were less true thirty years ago. The institutional structure has not changed; the party operating environment has.

Third, the parties have become asymmetrically polarized in ways that distinguish the American case from most other democracies. The Republican Party in particular has moved further right than the Democratic Party has moved left, and has become more willing to use procedural and constitutional hardball to advance its agenda. The transformation of the GOP from a coalition that contained both Nelson Rockefeller and Barry Goldwater to a coalition unified around Trumpian populism is the most consequential political development of the past half-century, and it is not principally explained by constitutional structure. It is explained by deeper changes in the social bases of American politics, the structure of conservative media, and the strategic choices of party elites going back to Newt Gingrich and earlier.

This party-centered framing has important implications for Miller's argument.

First, it suggests that institutional reform alone will not solve the obstruction problem. Eliminate the filibuster, and ideologically homogeneous parties will simply produce sharper policy reversals between unified governments, as occurred in Wisconsin and North Carolina when polarized state governments oscillated between full Republican and full Democratic control. The result might be more legislation, but not necessarily better legislation or more durable legislation. Eliminate the Electoral College, and parties will adjust their strategies to maximize popular vote totals, which might or might not improve policy outcomes depending on the resulting incentive structure.

Second, it suggests that the actual reform agenda should focus on party structure as much as constitutional structure. Reforming campaign finance to weaken the role of national party committees and large donors. Reforming primary elections to weaken the dominance of ideological activists in candidate selection—through ranked-choice voting, open primaries, or other mechanisms. Reforming political media to weaken the polarization-incentivizing structure of partisan outlets. Reforming the relationship between parties and Congress to restore more independent judgment by individual members. These are not the high-profile constitutional reforms Miller emphasizes, but they may matter more in practice.

Third, it suggests that the Democratic Party Miller pins her hopes on has its own contributions to the crisis to grapple with. The asymmetry of polarization is real, and the Republican Party has been the more destabilizing actor. But the Democratic Party's evolution into a coalition dominated by college-educated professionals, its abandonment of organizational depth in working-class communities, its substitution of advocacy organization mobilization for genuine mass membership, and its difficulty in adapting to a media environment that no longer rewards expertise—these are all party problems that institutional reform cannot solve. A revitalized Democratic Party capable of leading the mass democratic politics Miller envisions would have to look quite different from the actual Democratic Party of 2025.

Finally, the party-centered framing helps explain why the most worrying feature of the current Trump administration is not the policy substance but the attack on democratic norms. The Madisonian institutional structure assumed that ambition would counteract ambition—that legislators would defend congressional prerogatives against executive encroachment, that party leaders would defend institutional integrity against members' short-term partisan interests, and so on. This assumption broke down because contemporary parties are organized around partisan loyalty in ways that override institutional loyalty. Congressional Republicans do not defend congressional prerogatives against Trump's executive overreach because they identify primarily as Republicans rather than as members of Congress. This is a party pathology, not an institutional one.

What follows? Not that Miller's reform agenda is wrong, but that it is incomplete in a specific way. The constitutional reforms she lists are valuable but will not, by themselves, restore the kind of cross-pressuring, coalition-building, and institutional loyalty that made the system function in earlier eras. That requires changes to the party system itself: in its membership structure, its funding, its media environment, its primary rules, and its relationship to civil society. These changes are harder to articulate as a reform program because they involve cultural and organizational evolution as much as formal rule changes. But they are at least as important as constitutional amendments to the long-run health of American democracy.

The Framers' system worked, when it worked, because parties balanced ideological commitment with institutional and local diversity. It has stopped working because that balance has collapsed. The recovery of American democracy will require rebuilding it.

  3. 03Asymmetric polarization, particularly the Republican Party's shift toward procedural hardball and populism, is driven by social and media changes rather than institutional structure.
  4. 04Institutional reforms like eliminating the filibuster or the Electoral College will fail to solve obstructionism if the underlying party structures remain polarized and ideologically rigid.
  5. 05A meaningful reform agenda must prioritize changes to party-related mechanisms, such as campaign finance, primary election rules, and media incentives, over high-profile constitutional amendments.
