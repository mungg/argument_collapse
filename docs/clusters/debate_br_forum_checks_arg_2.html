"The United States must transition from a veto-based constitutionalism that enables elite obstruction to an accountability-based constitutionalism that empowers majorities to govern while preserving essential democratic safeguards."

The argument over checks and balances often proceeds as if Americans must choose between two caricatures. On one side is the civics-textbook view: the Framers designed an almost providential machine that protects liberty by making action difficult. On the other is an increasingly common democratic impatience: veto points are mainly tools of oligarchy, so the cure is majoritarian power. Lisa Miller’s essay is a sophisticated version of the second view, and it usefully demolishes the first. But the better answer lies in neither reverence nor rejection. The United States needs a shift from veto-based constitutionalism to accountability-based constitutionalism.

Miller is persuasive that the current system often checks democratic publics more effectively than it checks elites. The examples are familiar because they are persistent: health care, labor law, gun background checks, voting rights, climate policy, immigration, family leave, minimum wage increases. On many of these issues, there has been broad and durable public support for action, but legislation dies in committees, in the Senate, in litigation, in state implementation, or in a haze of procedural excuses. This is not an accident. A system with multiple separately elected institutions, two legislative chambers, judicial review, federalism, staggered elections, and supermajority practices gives opponents of change many chances to win even after losing the broader argument.

That structure favors those who can afford patience. A family without health insurance cannot wait indefinitely. A worker facing wage theft cannot wait. A community facing polluted water cannot wait. But a trade association, a monopolist, a billionaire-funded advocacy network, or a professional lobby can wait. Delay is often victory. American institutions convert time into a weapon, and the wealthy usually have more of it.

Still, the answer cannot be to collapse constitutionalism into simple majority rule. The problem with checks and balances is not that they check power. The problem is that they check the wrong powers in the wrong ways and often evade responsibility. A Senate filibuster that allows forty-one senators to block voting rights legislation is not equivalent to a court order requiring the executive to provide due process before deportation. A committee bottleneck that hides legislators from accountability is not equivalent to an inspector general exposing corruption. State autonomy used to suppress votes is not equivalent to state officials refusing to participate in unlawful federal coercion. The category “check” includes both democratic safeguards and democratic sabotage.

An accountability-based constitutionalism would begin from three principles.

First, elections must produce governing responsibility. If a party wins power on a platform, it should generally be able to enact ordinary legislation, subject to constitutional rights and later electoral judgment. This means the filibuster should not be allowed to operate as a routine supermajority requirement. It means the House should be enlarged so representation is less distorted. It means gerrymandering should be restricted by national standards. It means voting access should not depend on the partisan incentives of state officials. It also means the Electoral College should be replaced, or at least neutralized, by a system that makes the presidency reflect the national popular choice.

Second, rights must protect persons, not entrench privilege. American constitutional law has too often treated property, contract, corporate spending, and gun ownership as robustly judicially protected while treating voting, welfare, labor organizing, and equal access to public goods as politically optional. A democratic Constitution should protect speech, conscience, bodily liberty, due process, and equal citizenship. But it should not constitutionalize plutocracy. Campaign finance doctrine that disables Congress from limiting the political power of wealth is not neutral; it is a choice to empower one kind of minority. A jurisprudence that is skeptical of labor regulation but deferential to concentrated economic power is not liberty-preserving; it is class politics by other means.

Third, checks should clarify accountability rather than obscure it. Many American institutions do the opposite. When legislation fails, citizens often cannot tell whether the culprit was party leadership, a committee chair, a filibuster threat, a parliamentarian’s ruling, a donor revolt, a court precedent, or a state’s refusal to implement. When policy is made by executive order because Congress is gridlocked, responsibility shifts again. When courts decide major social questions, elections seem less meaningful. The result is not moderation but mystification. A healthier system would make it easier to know who did what and to reward or punish them.

Miller’s essay is especially important because it links institutional dysfunction to authoritarian temptation. Authoritarianism does not arrive only because voters love cruelty or because demagogues are charismatic. It also grows when ordinary politics seems incapable of solving ordinary problems. When citizens believe elected officials cannot deliver health care, housing, safety, jobs, or fairness, they become receptive to someone who promises to smash constraints. Trump’s appeal has always depended in part on this contrast: corrupt, stalled, hypocritical “Washington” versus decisive personal will. The tragedy is that his decisiveness is aimed not at democratizing power but at concentrating it in himself and his allies.

That is why reformers must be careful. If they say only that checks and balances are the problem, they may unintentionally validate the authoritarian critique of constraint itself. Trump also hates checks, but for the opposite reason Miller does. He does not want democratic government to act on behalf of ordinary people; he wants executive power freed from law, expertise, oversight, and opposition. The democratic reform project must therefore be explicit: weaken vetoes that obstruct majority-backed legislation, strengthen checks that prevent arbitrary or corrupt rule.

This distinction matters for federalism. Miller is right that state power has a grim history, especially in the defense of slavery, Jim Crow, and racial inequality. She is also right that state variation produces cruel inequalities in health care, education, voting, criminal justice, and labor standards. But federalism is not only a pro- or anti-democratic thing; it is a terrain of struggle. In the present crisis, state courts, attorneys general, election administrators, and governors may slow authoritarian consolidation. In other moments, they may obstruct national rights. The right principle is not “states’ rights” or “national power” in the abstract. It is equal citizenship and democratic self-government. Where national standards are needed to secure those values, national power should prevail. Where state institutions protect rights against federal abuse or innovate above a national floor, they should be welcomed.

The same mixed view applies to courts. Miller rightly rejects the liberal habit of treating courts as reliable engines of progress. The Warren Court period encouraged a misleading memory. Much of the Court’s history has been anti-labor, anti-egalitarian, and deferential to hierarchy. The current Court has intensified distrust through decisions on voting rights, campaign finance, reproductive rights, administrative power, and guns. But the answer is not to despise judicial review as such. In 2025, courts remain essential to enforcing due process, preserving the freedom of the press, checking unlawful executive action, and protecting targeted minorities. The democratic demand should be for courts that are less supreme in policymaking but firm in legality: less willing to invent deregulatory constitutional doctrines, more willing to insist that government obey law.

Citizens’ assemblies, which Miller mentions, are a promising addition because they address a real gap in American democracy: the distance between organized publics and policymaking. Properly designed, deliberative bodies can bring ordinary people into agenda-setting, reduce elite monopoly over reform, and create recommendations that are harder to dismiss as partisan messaging. But they should supplement, not replace, electoral democracy. The danger of many “democratic innovations” is that they become consultative rituals while real power remains elsewhere. If citizens’ assemblies are to matter, they need institutional hooks: mandatory congressional hearings, public responses, ballot pathways, or agenda rights.

The ultimate question is not whether America needs checks and balances. Every democracy needs ways to prevent domination. The question is whether the people can use government to check concentrated power in all its forms: public and private, national and local, majoritarian and minoritarian, bureaucratic and plutocratic. Miller’s essay rightly insists that private power has escaped constitutional scrutiny for too long. But the best response is not to abandon the language of checking power; it is to democratize it.

A reformed American system would let electoral majorities govern, protect vulnerable minorities, prevent executive lawlessness, reduce the political power of wealth, guarantee equal voting rights, and make institutions legible enough for citizens to hold leaders responsible. That is a high bar. But it is also the standard by which a constitutional democracy should be judged. The dead end is not checks and balances themselves. The dead end is a version of them that confuses paralysis with freedom and minority rule with liberty.

"While the lead essay correctly identifies that America’s complex system of veto points fuels public disillusionment and authoritarianism, the immediate priority must be a staged strategy that uses existing institutional checks to defend democracy in the short term while building the political power necessary for long-term constitutional reconstruction."

Lisa Miller is correct about the disease but too optimistic about the treatment. The United States has too many veto points, too little democratic accountability, and a constitutional mythology that often confuses paralysis with liberty. But in May 2025, calls for constitutional transformation face a brutal practical problem: the very institutions that need reform are the ones that must approve it, and the political coalition for deep reconstruction does not yet exist. That does not make Miller’s argument wrong. It means the immediate task is to connect long-term constitutional ambition to short-term democratic defense.

The lead essay’s critique of checks and balances is strongest as an explanation for public disillusionment. Americans routinely vote for change and receive stalemate. They are told to elect better representatives, then discover that representatives are trapped by the Senate, the filibuster, courts, party factions, donors, committees, federalism, and procedural rules almost no normal person understands. They are told courts will protect rights, then watch courts withdraw rights or disable regulation. They are told states are laboratories, then see basic welfare depend on ZIP code. The system’s complexity may delight constitutional lawyers, but for citizens it often looks like a scam.

This matters because democratic failure creates authoritarian opportunity. Trump’s second administration thrives on the claim that regular government is a rigged game and that only forceful executive action can break it. His answer is not democratic responsiveness; it is personal domination, patronage, revenge, and spectacle. But his critique lands because normal politics has so often failed to deliver. If defenders of democracy offer only a return to pre-Trump normalcy, they will lose the argument before it begins.

Still, the path from diagnosis to reform is narrow. Abolishing the Electoral College requires constitutional amendment or a highly uncertain interstate workaround. Reducing the Senate’s power would run into the Constitution’s equal suffrage guarantee for states. Supreme Court term limits may require either constitutional amendment or carefully designed statute. Campaign finance reform confronts entrenched doctrine. National voting rights legislation must pass through Congress and survive courts. Even filibuster reform requires a Senate majority willing to act. Miller knows this, but the essay’s invocation of past movements can understate the difference between moral necessity and available power.

The nineteenth and twentieth century democratizing struggles she cites were long, conflictual, and contingent. Abolition required civil war. Reconstruction was partially overthrown. Women’s suffrage took generations. The New Deal depended on economic catastrophe, overwhelming electoral victories, and a very different party system. Civil rights required mass disruption, Cold War pressures, urban uprisings, legal strategy, partisan realignment, and extraordinary courage. These examples should inspire, but they should also discipline us. Constitutional change is not an agenda item; it is the outcome of a transformed political order.

So what should be done now?

First, defend legality without pretending legality is enough. Courts, inspectors general, civil servants, state attorneys general, journalists, universities, bar associations, and civic groups all have roles in resisting unlawful executive action. If the administration defies court orders, corruptly directs contracts, punishes speech, or denies due process, institutional resistance is essential. Miller is right that “checks and balances” should not be the whole democratic message. But in a confrontation with executive lawlessness, checks are not decorative. They buy time, impose costs, expose facts, and preserve arenas for opposition.

Second, make Congress matter again. One reason presidents govern unilaterally is that Congress has surrendered authority. Emergency powers, war powers, appropriations, confirmations, oversight, and agency delegations all need renewal. Members of Congress should be forced to vote on major questions rather than hide behind procedural fog. If Republicans support Trump’s actions, they should own them publicly. If Democrats oppose them, they should offer legislation that clarifies the stakes. Accountability begins with recorded responsibility.

Third, prioritize democracy reforms that can be explained in one sentence. “Every citizen should be able to vote easily and have that vote count equally.” “Politicians should not choose their voters.” “The candidate with the most votes should win.” “No president should be above the law.” “Billionaires should not be able to buy elections.” “Judges should not hold power for life without enforceable ethics.” Reform movements fail when they sound like seminars. They succeed when institutional design is tied to felt unfairness.

Fourth, pair process reforms with material commitments. Miller is exactly right that democracy cannot be defended as procedure alone. A voting rights bill attached rhetorically to nothing but institutional repair will not mobilize enough people. It must be connected to wages, health care, housing, schools, child care, debt, safety, and freedom from corruption. People should understand that the same veto points that block voting rights also block lower drug prices, labor rights, disaster response, and clean water. Institutional reform must be made concrete: the rules are why you cannot get what you voted for.

Fifth, build state and local capacity without romanticizing it. In the short term, states and cities are indispensable. They can protect election administration, sue the federal government, maintain benefits, defend immigrants, preserve reproductive access, enact labor standards, and demonstrate governing competence. But localism should not become an excuse for national abdication. The message should be: we will protect people wherever we have power, and we will fight for national floors so protection does not depend on geography.

Sixth, reduce dependence on courts even while using them. Liberal politics became too court-centered in the late twentieth century, and Miller is right that this weakened mass politics. Litigation is necessary in 2025, but it cannot be the strategy. Court victories can be narrow, temporary, and demobilizing if citizens assume lawyers will save them. Every major lawsuit against authoritarian overreach should be accompanied by organizing, public education, congressional pressure, state action, and electoral work. Legal defense must become political offense.

Seventh, prepare for reform windows. Constitutional and institutional change often seems impossible until suddenly it is not. Crises open windows; organized movements decide what passes through them. Reformers should have ready proposals: filibuster abolition, House expansion, national voting standards, independent redistricting, public financing, Supreme Court term limits, enforceable ethics, emergency-power reform, anti-corruption statutes, and protections for civil service independence. Even if these cannot pass today, they can structure political demands and reveal who benefits from the status quo.

The most difficult challenge is coalition. Miller sees the Democratic Party as the most plausible vehicle for mass democratic politics, and she is probably right. But the party is an uneasy alliance of professionals, union households, racial minorities, young progressives, suburban moderates, nonprofit networks, and disaffected anti-Trump conservatives. It cannot become a majoritarian reform party merely by adopting better white papers. It needs a language that joins anti-authoritarianism to anti-oligarchy, patriotism to pluralism, and competence to solidarity.

That language should avoid two traps. The first is institutional nostalgia: “Save our democracy” cannot mean “restore the old system that many people experienced as unresponsive.” The second is anti-institutional romanticism: “The people versus the system” cannot mean giving presidents, parties, or movements a blank check. The right message is that Americans deserve a government they can actually hold accountable: strong enough to solve problems, constrained enough to prevent abuse, and fair enough that every vote and every person counts.

Miller’s essay is valuable because it forces readers to confront a truth many elites prefer to avoid: American democracy did not become vulnerable only because Trump arrived. It became vulnerable because too many people concluded that constitutional democracy was a machine for disappointment. But if that is true, the response must be staged. In the short run, defend every lawful barrier against authoritarian consolidation. In the medium run, win elections with a program that materially improves lives. In the long run, change the constitutional rules that make minority rule and elite veto so durable.

The dead end is not the idea of checks and balances. The dead end is believing that inherited checks will automatically produce democratic balance. They will not. Balance must be built through power, organization, reform, and delivery. The task now is to survive the immediate threat without losing sight of the deeper reconstruction that survival is meant to make possible.

"The American system of checks and balances must be reformed because it has devolved into a mechanism of institutional fragmentation that empowers elite minorities to obstruct the democratic will and protect private power."

Lisa Miller’s central claim is both bracing and right: the American cult of checks and balances has become an obstacle to diagnosing the crisis it is supposed to solve. The problem is not that restraint is worthless. It is that Americans have been taught to mistake obstruction for liberty, institutional fragmentation for democracy, and elite veto power for constitutional wisdom. In a moment when Donald Trump’s second administration is testing the limits of lawful government, it is tempting to reach for the most familiar civic catechism. But Miller is correct that simply chanting “checks and balances” cannot explain why the system has so often failed to deliver what majorities want, why it has empowered organized minorities, or why a demagogue can plausibly present himself as the only person able to “break” a broken order.

The deepest insight in Miller’s essay is that inaction is not neutral. American political argument usually treats state power as the thing that must be checked, while private power is treated as background scenery. But for most people, domination is not experienced only as the knock on the door from a government agent. It is also the landlord who can raise rent beyond reach, the employer who can fire workers for organizing, the insurance company that denies care, the lender who traps families in debt, the fossil fuel company that poisons a community, the platform monopoly that controls markets and speech. A democratic government that cannot act against such concentrated private power is not protecting freedom. It is abandoning the public to forms of coercion that are less constitutionally visible but no less real.

This is why the standard defense of veto points as consensus-forcing is so unconvincing. Consensus is admirable when the parties to the negotiation have roughly equal capacity to participate and when refusal to bargain carries costs. But American institutions often allow the most organized and best-funded interests to avoid bargaining altogether. They do not have to persuade a national majority; they need only capture a committee chair, threaten a swing senator, finance a litigation strategy, or wait for courts to narrow what Congress has enacted. Miller’s health care example is apt because it reveals a pattern, not an exception. The United States did not fail to achieve universal coverage because Americans uniquely hate doctors, taxes, solidarity, or bureaucracy. It failed because opponents repeatedly found points of leverage inside a system designed to make national action extraordinarily difficult.

That design also scrambles accountability. In parliamentary systems, parties that win governing majorities can usually enact a program and then face voters on the results. In the United States, a party can win the presidency, House, and Senate and still be unable to govern because of the filibuster, malapportionment, internal committee chokepoints, hostile courts, federal implementation gaps, and a lobbying apparatus built for delay. Voters then conclude, reasonably enough, that politicians never keep promises. The resulting cynicism is fuel for authoritarian politics. When democratic government cannot deliver, the strongman’s promise to cut through procedure begins to sound less like a threat and more like a solution.

Miller is also right to challenge the sentimental language of minority protection. “Minority” is one of the most morally overworked words in American constitutional discussion. Sometimes it means vulnerable groups excluded from full citizenship: Black Americans under Jim Crow, religious minorities, immigrants, LGBT people, dissidents, the poor. But sometimes it means billionaires, monopolists, organized professional lobbies, segregationist committee barons, or states that want to evade national standards. A political system cannot be judged democratic merely because it protects minorities in the abstract. We have to ask which minorities, against what harms, and by what institutional means.

The American system has too often protected powerful minorities against democratic accountability while leaving vulnerable minorities exposed. Federalism protected slavery and segregation long before it protected sanctuary cities or state climate policy. The Senate empowered slave states, then Jim Crow states, and now sparsely populated states in ways that distort national majorities. Courts have sometimes defended equal citizenship, but they have also struck down labor protections, narrowed voting rights, weakened campaign finance regulation, and empowered gun-rights claims over public safety legislation. There is no institutional innocence here. Every check has a social constituency. Every veto point is used by someone.

Yet the remedy cannot be an equally simple cult of majoritarianism. Miller does not call for unchecked presidential power, but her critics will surely accuse her of opening that door. The answer is to distinguish democratic capacity from executive unilateralism. A government capable of acting is not the same thing as a president capable of commanding. In fact, the growth of executive unilateralism is partly a symptom of legislative incapacity. When Congress cannot legislate, presidents govern through emergency powers, executive orders, administrative waivers, prosecutorial discretion, and creative interpretations of old statutes. Then courts become the battlefield of every policy dispute. The result is not energetic democracy but unstable, personalized rule.

A democratic reform agenda should therefore strengthen Congress and the electorate, not merely weaken constraints. Abolishing or radically limiting the Senate filibuster is an obvious start. Expanding the House would make representation less distorted and reduce the absurd size of congressional districts. National standards for voting access, independent redistricting, campaign finance regulation, statehood for disenfranchised Americans where appropriate, and an end to the Electoral College would all move the system closer to equal political voice. Supreme Court reform—whether term limits, ethics rules, jurisdictional limits in some areas, or expansion—should be discussed not as revenge but as democratic maintenance. Lifetime judicial supremacy over a modern regulatory state is not a sacred principle; it is an institutional choice.

But Miller’s most important prescription is not a checklist of reforms. It is a politics. Institutional reform never happens because clever people publish institutional reform proposals. It happens when organized publics make the existing order feel less legitimate than the alternative. The labor movement, abolitionism, Reconstruction, women’s suffrage, the New Deal, and civil rights did not succeed by revering the constitutional order as it stood. They succeeded by forcing Americans to see that the Constitution’s democratic promises were being betrayed by its institutional arrangements and by those who controlled them.

This is why appeals to “normalcy” are so inadequate after Trump’s return to power. Normalcy was not working for millions of people. Normalcy meant unaffordable health care, stagnant wages for many workers, a housing crisis, deindustrialized regions, grotesque inequality, endless fundraising, and a political class that often seemed more comfortable explaining why change was impossible than fighting to make it happen. Trump’s answer is corrupt and authoritarian: smash the administrative state where it protects the public, preserve or expand state power where it punishes enemies, and use resentment to enrich allies. But one reason that answer has purchase is that liberal constitutionalism has too often sounded like a defense of procedures that produce no material improvement.

The democratic answer must be different: not “trust the institutions,” but “make institutions answer to the people.” That means pairing constitutional reform with a program that people can feel in daily life: universal health care, stronger labor rights, child care, housing construction and tenant protections, climate investment, antitrust enforcement, and a tax system that curbs oligarchy. If democracy is experienced only as voting in a system that cannot deliver, it will lose. If democracy is experienced as collective power that disciplines private domination and improves life, it can be defended.

Miller’s essay is a warning against nostalgia. Checks and balances have not “served this republic well for centuries” if the measure is equal citizenship, responsiveness, or protection against oligarchy. They have sometimes prevented abuse, but they have also entrenched abuse. The task is not to abolish every restraint on power; it is to build a democracy in which the strongest restraints fall on domination, corruption, and minority rule, while the people’s capacity to govern in the public interest is enlarged. That is not a rejection of constitutionalism. It is the only constitutionalism worth saving.

"While the American system of checks and balances often functions as a tool for elite obstruction, a truly emancipatory politics must reform these institutions to distinguish between anti-democratic privilege and the essential protections required to safeguard vulnerable minorities from majoritarian cruelty."

The most important question raised by Lisa Miller’s essay is not whether checks and balances have “served the republic well.” For many Americans, especially Black Americans, Indigenous peoples, immigrants, workers, women, and sexual minorities, the answer has always been: sometimes, partially, and often far too late. The more urgent question is how oppressed groups should think about institutions that have alternately abandoned and protected them. Miller is right that American veto points have frequently empowered elite minorities against egalitarian majorities. But a politics of emancipation must remain wary of both elite obstruction and majoritarian cruelty.

The history of race in the United States confirms Miller’s critique in devastating detail. The Constitution’s original compromises protected slavery not only through explicit clauses but through institutional design. The Senate, Electoral College, federalism, and supermajoritarian amendment process all gave slaveholding power room to expand and defend itself. After the Civil War, Reconstruction briefly offered a different constitutional vision: national citizenship, equal protection, voting rights, and federal authority against state oppression. But courts narrowed those promises, Congress retreated, Northern white commitment faded, and Southern white elites used violence and law to reestablish domination. For nearly a century, “checks and balances” did not protect Black citizens from tyranny. They protected the tyrannies of states, sheriffs, mobs, employers, landlords, and party machines.

The Senate filibuster’s role in blocking anti-lynching legislation and civil rights measures is not an unfortunate footnote. It is central to the moral evaluation of American institutions. So is the Supreme Court’s long record of indifference or hostility to Reconstruction’s egalitarian promise. When national majorities or near-majorities favored action, institutional vetoes allowed an intense white minority to prevail. Miller’s argument that veto points often empower powerful minorities is plainly correct.

Yet racial history also complicates any simple faith in majority rule. White supremacy in America has never been only an elite project. It has been organized by elites, to be sure, but it has also relied on mass participation: voters who supported segregationists, juries that acquitted murderers, homeowners who defended exclusion, workers who rejected interracial solidarity, parents who resisted school integration, and ordinary citizens who accepted or demanded punitive policing. Racial domination was not imposed solely from smoke-filled rooms. It lived in popular institutions as well as elite ones.

That matters because vulnerable minorities cannot afford institutional romanticism of any kind. Courts have failed them; courts have saved them. Congress has ignored them; Congress has protected them. States have oppressed them; states have sometimes expanded rights. Presidents have enforced civil rights; presidents have betrayed them. Local democracy has produced grassroots empowerment; local democracy has also produced sundown towns, segregated schools, discriminatory zoning, and abusive policing. The lesson is not that one institution is the true friend of justice. The lesson is that freedom requires power in multiple places, organized movements capable of using that power, and rights that do not vanish when a hostile majority wins.

Miller’s essay is especially persuasive when she criticizes the abstract language of “minority rights.” In American discourse, “minority” can mean a historically subordinated community seeking equal citizenship, or it can mean a handful of billionaires seeking to prevent taxation. Treating these as conceptually similar is absurd. The minority that wants to vote without intimidation is not the same as the minority that wants to buy elections. The minority that needs protection from discrimination is not the same as the industry lobby that wants immunity from regulation. A democratic constitutionalism must distinguish between anti-subordination rights and anti-democratic privilege.

But this distinction must be built into institutions, not left to partisan goodwill. If the same majority that can enact universal health care can also restrict religious practice, censor dissent, expand surveillance, or target immigrants, then vulnerable groups need enforceable rights. The problem with the current system is not that it protects rights too strongly. It is that it often protects the wrong rights and treats material inequality as constitutionally irrelevant. A tenant facing eviction, a pregnant woman denied care, a worker fired for organizing, a trans teenager targeted by state law, a Black voter packed out of influence, a migrant denied a hearing—these are constitutional injuries in any morally serious sense, even when doctrine does not recognize them as such.

Miller calls for mass politics, and she is right. Every major advance in equal citizenship has required disruptive organizing: abolitionist agitation, slave resistance, Black conventions, Reconstruction mobilization, labor organizing, women’s suffrage campaigns, civil rights boycotts, sit-ins, freedom rides, farmworker struggles, disability activism, queer liberation, immigrant rights marches, and more. Courts did not produce justice from above; they responded, when they responded, to movements that changed the political world. Law follows power as well as principle.

But mass politics must be cross-racial and anti-caste, not merely majoritarian. There is a difference between building a majority that includes and empowers subordinated groups and invoking “the majority” as if numbers alone confer justice. The New Deal is an instructive example. It built social citizenship for many workers, but it also compromised with Southern segregationists and excluded or disadvantaged many Black and brown workers through occupational categories and local administration. Majoritarian reform can reproduce hierarchy if the coalition’s terms are set by those willing to sacrifice the vulnerable for victory.

The same warning applies today. A Democratic Party seeking broad working-class appeal may be tempted to downplay racial justice, immigrant rights, trans rights, or criminal justice reform in the name of economic populism. That would be a mistake both morally and strategically. Economic injustice and status hierarchy are intertwined. Health care, wages, housing, schools, policing, environmental exposure, voting rights, and bodily autonomy are experienced through race, gender, geography, and citizenship status. A politics of ordinary people that treats minority rights as a distraction will fracture the very majority it needs.

Miller is right that Americans are less divided on many equality questions than our institutions make them appear. But we should be careful. Public opinion is often broad but shallow; support for equality in principle can weaken when remedies become specific. Many people support “fairness” but oppose school integration, housing density, reparative policies, asylum rights, or trans participation when framed by fear. This is where leadership and organizing matter. Majorities are not found objects; they are built. The content of majority politics depends on the stories movements tell and the conflicts they are willing to wage.

What institutional reforms follow from an anti-subordination perspective? First, voting rights must be foundational. Without equal political power, every other right is precarious. Congress should establish national voting standards, restore and expand protections against racial discrimination in election rules, ban partisan gerrymandering, and protect election administration from sabotage. Second, the Senate’s minority-rule features must be confronted. The filibuster has no democratic legitimacy when used to block civil rights, labor rights, or basic governance. Third, courts need reform, but not abolition of judicial rights enforcement. Term limits, ethics rules, and jurisdictional clarity can reduce judicial oligarchy while preserving courts as forums for rights claims. Fourth, social rights should enter constitutional politics: health care, education, housing, and labor rights are not separate from equal citizenship.

Fifth, federalism should be restructured around equality. States should be free to expand rights, not contract them. National floors should protect voting, nondiscrimination, reproductive freedom, labor standards, environmental justice, and due process. State constitutions can be powerful tools, but no state should be allowed to turn local control into local caste rule.

Finally, citizens’ assemblies and participatory institutions should be designed with anti-domination safeguards. Random selection alone does not overcome inequality. Participants need translation, child care, compensation, accessibility, protection from harassment, and agenda-setting power. Marginalized communities must not be tokenized in deliberations whose outcomes elites can ignore.

Miller’s essay performs a necessary service by puncturing the complacency that says the system will save us. It will not. But neither will “the people” save us unless the people are organized around a vision of equal freedom. The American constitutional order has been transformed before when movements forced the country to choose between inherited structures and democratic promises. That is the choice again. The goal should not be fewer checks in general. It should be checks against domination: against oligarchs, against presidents, against courts that entrench hierarchy, against states that deny citizenship, and against majorities that would purchase unity by abandoning the vulnerable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The history of racial subjugation in the United States demonstrates that institutional veto points like the filibuster and federalism have historically protected local tyrannies rather than individual liberties.
  2. 02Racial domination has frequently relied on mass participation and popular support, suggesting that simple majoritarianism is insufficient to guarantee justice for marginalized groups.
  3. 03Vulnerable groups require power distributed across multiple institutions and enforceable rights because no single branch of government has been a consistent ally of justice.
  4. 04A democratic constitutionalism must differentiate between the 'minority rights' of the wealthy to obstruct progress and the 'anti-subordination rights' of historically oppressed communities.
  5. 05Mass politics must be intentionally cross-racial and anti-caste to avoid the historical failures of majoritarian movements that sacrificed the interests of the most vulnerable for broader economic gains.
  6. 06Institutional reforms, such as national voting standards and Senate restructuring, should be designed specifically as 'checks against domination' to prevent both elite oligarchy and the infringement of civil liberties by hostile majorities.
