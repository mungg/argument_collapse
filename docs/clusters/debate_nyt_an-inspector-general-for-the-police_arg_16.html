"The New York Police Department should have an independent inspector general to ensure that police policies are fair, transparent, and constitutionally sound without relying on slow and expensive litigation."

The argument for an NYPD inspector general begins with a simple democratic principle: people should not have to sue their own city to find out whether they are being policed fairly. Lawsuits over stop-and-frisk and surveillance of Muslim communities have forced facts into public view, but litigation is a poor substitute for ordinary accountability. It is expensive, slow and available only after the damage has been done.

For many New Yorkers, stop-and-frisk is not an abstraction. It means being ordered against a wall on the way to school, being asked to empty pockets in front of neighbors, being treated as a suspect in the city one calls home. The constitutional rule may turn on reasonable suspicion, but the civic injury is broader. When a whole class of people comes to expect police encounters as a rite of passage, the city has a fairness problem even before a judge rules on liability.

The Muslim surveillance issue reveals an even deeper danger. After Sept. 11, fear made extraordinary measures politically easy. But religious liberty is tested precisely when officials say the stakes are high. If police map restaurants, mosques, student groups or ordinary social life without evidence of wrongdoing, they are not merely gathering information. They are telling a community that its identity is enough to invite suspicion.

An inspector general would give communities a forum that does not require them to prove individual injury one lawsuit at a time. It could ask whether the department’s policies comply with the Constitution, whether they are supported by evidence and whether they distribute burdens in a way the city is prepared to defend openly. That last point matters. Public safety is a public good, but its costs are not always publicly shared. Too often they fall on neighborhoods with the least political power.

Opponents say police need flexibility. They do. But flexibility is not secrecy plus deference. Doctors, prosecutors, judges, schools and intelligence agencies all operate under forms of oversight because professional judgment can go wrong. Police judgment is no different, and police power is more immediate than most.

The NYPD deserves credit for reducing crime and confronting real threats. That record does not exempt it from scrutiny. In fact, a powerful and celebrated department needs independent oversight more, because success can harden into impunity. An inspector general would not guarantee justice. But it would make fairness a standing obligation of the department, not an occasional concession extracted by scandal or court order. For a democratic city, that is a modest demand.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Litigation is an inadequate substitute for proactive accountability because it only addresses grievances after damage has been done and is inaccessible to many.
  2. 02Widespread stop-and-frisk practices cause significant civic injury by treating entire classes of people as suspects, creating a fairness problem that exists regardless of legal liability.
  3. 03Surveillance of Muslim communities based on identity rather than evidence of wrongdoing undermines religious liberty and signals that certain identities are inherently suspicious.
  4. 04An inspector general would provide a necessary forum for marginalized communities to challenge the disproportionate burden of policing policies that are often hidden from public scrutiny.
  5. 05Independent oversight is a standard democratic practice for powerful institutions and does not inherently prevent the police from maintaining the flexibility needed to ensure public safety.
  6. 06The NYPD’s success in reducing crime does not exempt it from scrutiny; rather, its power necessitates oversight to prevent professional success from turning into impunity.
