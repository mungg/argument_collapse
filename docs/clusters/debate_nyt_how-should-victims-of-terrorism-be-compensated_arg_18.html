"The federal government has a unique moral and legal obligation to compensate victims of terrorism because such attacks are acts of war against the state rather than ordinary crimes."

To understand how victims of mass violence should be compensated, we must clearly distinguish between ordinary crime and terrorism. A local mass shooting, no matter how devastating, is a failure of local law enforcement and community mental health resources. But terrorism—whether foreign or domestic—is fundamentally different. It is an act of war and a direct assault on the American state. Because of this, the federal government has a unique, binding moral obligation to compensate victims of terrorism.

When terrorists placed bombs at the finish line of the Boston Marathon, or flew planes into the World Trade Center, their primary target was not the specific individuals who were killed or maimed. The target was the United States of America. The victims were collateral damage in a political strike against our nation’s values, economy, and foreign policy.

The most basic premise of the social contract is that the state holds a monopoly on the legitimate use of force, and in exchange, it protects its citizens from foreign and political enemies. When a terrorist attack occurs on American soil, it represents a profound failure of the federal government to uphold its end of that contract. The intelligence community, the military, and federal law enforcement failed to detect and neutralize a threat to the republic.

Therefore, compensating victims of terrorism should not be left to the whims of private GoFundMe campaigns, local charities, or the lengthy delays of civil litigation. It is a debt owed by the state. Congress should establish a permanent mechanism strictly for the victims of designated acts of terrorism, acknowledging that these individuals suffered because the government failed to protect the nation from its political enemies. While local mass shootings demand civic compassion and better social services, terrorism requires state accountability. Treating terrorism victims as casualties of a war fought on our own soil is the only appropriate framework for ensuring they receive the support they deserve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Terrorism victims are targeted as symbolic representatives of the United States rather than as specific individuals, making them collateral damage in a political strike against the nation.
  2. 02A terrorist attack represents a failure of the federal government to uphold its social contract to protect citizens from foreign and political enemies.
  3. 03Compensating victims of terrorism is a debt owed by the state for its failure to detect and neutralize threats to the republic.
  4. 04Relying on private donations or civil litigation is insufficient and inappropriate for addressing the state accountability required by acts of terrorism.
