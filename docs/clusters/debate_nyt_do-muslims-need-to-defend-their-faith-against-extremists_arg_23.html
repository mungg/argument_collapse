"Muslims must defend their faith by moving beyond simple denunciations of extremism to actively reforming the theological interpretations and political environments that allow extremist ideologies to take root."

Muslims should defend Islam against extremists, but not with the easy formula that extremism has “nothing to do with Islam.” That sentence may be emotionally understandable, especially when Islam is being slandered wholesale. But it is intellectually evasive. The Islamic State does not recruit by saying, “Come abandon Islam.” It recruits by offering a brutal interpretation of Islamic history, law and identity. If Muslims do not examine how that interpretation is constructed, they will only denounce the symptom.

ISIS is selective, ignorant and grotesque. Its declaration of a caliphate has no legitimate consensus behind it. Its mass killing of civilians, enslavement of women, persecution of minorities and excommunication of other Muslims violate basic moral principles. Yet some of the raw material it exploits is real: medieval rulings about apostasy, hierarchy between Muslims and non-Muslims, triumphalist fantasies about empire, and a habit in some circles of treating Jews, Shiites, secularists or dissenters as enemies of God. These ideas did not begin with ISIS, even if ISIS weaponizes them in especially monstrous form.

That means the response must be deeper than public relations. Muslim scholars and lay leaders should defend freedom of conscience, not merely condemn violence. They should say clearly that no state should execute someone for changing religion or criticizing a cleric. They should reject sectarian incitement whether it comes from a jihadist video, a Gulf-funded preacher, an Iranian-backed militia, or a schoolbook. They should defend equal citizenship for Christians, Yazidis, Shiites, Sunnis, atheists and women — not as a concession to the West, but as a requirement of justice.

This will be uncomfortable because it requires confronting Muslim governments as well as non-state extremists. Saudi Arabia’s export of intolerant doctrines, Pakistan’s blasphemy politics, Egypt’s repression, Iran’s sectarian adventurism, and the brutality of Assad all help create an atmosphere in which sacred language is attached to domination. Western hypocrisy and military blunders matter too, but they cannot be used to avoid internal criticism.

The great Islamic civilizations were not built by frightened literalists with Twitter accounts. They were built by jurists, poets, philosophers, merchants, mystics, scientists and ordinary believers negotiating faith with life. Muslims can draw on that richness, but only if they stop pretending that every problem is an outside smear.

Defending Islam today requires moral courage: not only saying “ISIS is wrong,” but explaining which inherited assumptions made ISIS sound plausible to some — and then reforming them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Claiming that extremism has nothing to do with Islam is an intellectually evasive tactic that fails to address how militants exploit specific historical and legal interpretations of the faith.
  2. 02Muslim leaders must explicitly reject and reform medieval rulings regarding apostasy, sectarianism, and religious hierarchy that serve as the raw material for extremist propaganda.
  3. 03The defense of Islam requires a commitment to universal justice, including the protection of freedom of conscience and equal citizenship for all religious minorities and dissenters.
  4. 04Muslims must confront the role of state governments and intolerant doctrines within the Muslim world rather than blaming external factors or Western hypocrisy for internal crises.
  5. 05Reclaiming the faith involves drawing on the intellectual richness of Islamic history to challenge literalist interpretations and reform inherited assumptions that make extremism seem plausible.
