"While Buddy Roemer correctly identifies the moral crisis of legal corruption in American politics, his proposed solutions are too narrow because they focus on campaign finance mechanics rather than the broader systemic distribution of power and economic inequality."

Buddy Roemer is right that American democracy is being bent by concentrated wealth. But his proposed cure is too modest for the disease he describes.

The strength of his essay lies in its moral clarity. He understands that corruption is not confined to criminal bribery. The scandal is legal. It is the daily conversion of wealth into access, access into influence, and influence into policy. It is the tax preference that never dies, the regulator who knows where the lucrative jobs are, the committee member who spends more time with industry lobbyists than with unemployed constituents, the senator who denounces Wall Street on television and then quietly attends a fundraiser hosted by financiers. Roemer’s phrase “campaign welfare” is apt: private interests subsidize political careers and receive public benefits in return.

His examples are well chosen. The financial crisis should have shattered the bipartisan deference to Wall Street. Instead, after emergency rescues on a scale almost no ordinary citizen can comprehend, the largest institutions remain intact and profitable. Dodd-Frank is not nothing; it contains valuable provisions and may yet create stronger oversight if regulators are brave and properly funded. But the central fact remains: the firms that helped crash the economy still have the power to shape the rules under which they operate. That is political inequality in its most concrete form.

Still, Roemer’s frame narrows the problem by focusing almost entirely on campaign contributions and PACs. The deeper issue is not merely the financing of campaigns. It is the distribution of power in American society. Campaign money is one channel through which inequality speaks, but it is not the only one. Wealth also buys think tanks, academic respectability, media campaigns, lobbying staffs, litigation strategies, trade associations, and the promise of future employment. It shapes which ideas are considered “serious.” It determines who can afford to wait outside a congressional office and who is working two jobs. It affects whose suffering becomes a crisis and whose suffering becomes background noise.

This is why a $100 contribution limit, while admirable as a personal pledge, cannot be the centerpiece of democratic renewal. Roemer says he wants a president to represent 100 percent of Americans, not merely the 1 percent who give. But the 99 percent who do not give are not absent from politics because they lack a convenient payment mechanism. They are absent because they are busy, alienated, economically insecure, skeptical that politics can change their lives, and often deliberately discouraged from participating. The Internet helps, but it does not erase class. A small-donor model may broaden participation among the already engaged while leaving the poorest citizens unheard.

Nor is it enough to say, as Roemer does, that no citizen should be forced to support political speech with which he disagrees. That principle sounds fair, but it can become a barrier to public financing robust enough to matter. We require citizens to fund courts even when they dislike particular decisions, schools even when they disagree with a curriculum, and wars even when they oppose them. Democracy requires public goods, and a functioning electoral system is a public good. If we believe elections are the machinery of self-government, then public financing should not be treated as a suspect subsidy to politicians. It should be treated as democratic infrastructure.

The Fair Elections approach Roemer criticizes deserves more respect. Public grants and matching funds can free candidates from dependence on large donors while giving challengers a fighting chance. Yes, the design matters. Thresholds should prevent frivolous campaigns without protecting incumbents. Funds should be sufficient but not lavish. Participation should be voluntary under current constitutional doctrine. But the principle is sound: candidates who demonstrate public support through many small contributions should receive public resources to communicate with voters.

Roemer is also too accommodating toward Citizens United. Of course reformers must operate under existing Supreme Court doctrine. But they should also contest it. The decision’s formal equation of corporate independent spending with ordinary political speech ignores political reality. Corporations are not just associations of citizens; they are state-created entities with special privileges, perpetual life, limited liability, and economic purposes distinct from democratic citizenship. To say that ExxonMobil or Goldman Sachs may spend from treasury funds to influence elections on the same constitutional footing as a citizen speaking in a town hall is not neutrality. It is a judicial choice that magnifies existing power.

At minimum, Congress should require full disclosure of independent spending and the true sources behind it. Shareholders should have a say before corporate money is used politically. Contractors receiving federal funds should be barred from election spending aimed at the officials who oversee those contracts. Coordination rules should be tightened. But we should also be willing to say that the Constitution may need to be amended if the Court continues to prevent democratic regulation of concentrated political money.

The same broader ambition is needed in banking. Roemer rightly calls for reconsidering Glass-Steagall and confronting too big to fail. But the problem is not only that banks give campaign money. It is that our economy has become dangerously financialized. Too much talent, profit, and political attention have flowed into complex financial activity that extracts value rather than creates it. Restoring democracy requires stronger capital requirements, simpler banks, a credible resolution regime, protection for consumers, and a willingness to break up institutions whose failure would threaten the economy. If a bank is too big to fail, it is too big to govern democratically.

Finally, reform must include labor and civic organization. Ordinary citizens need countervailing institutions. The decline of unions, local parties, membership organizations, and civic associations has left many Americans isolated while corporations remain organized. A democracy of atomized small donors is better than a democracy of billionaires, but it is still thin. People need durable institutions through which they can learn, deliberate, bargain, and hold officials accountable.

Roemer’s essay is a welcome insurgent cry, especially from a Republican who refuses to defend Wall Street privilege. But the republic does not need only leaders “free to lead.” It needs citizens with enough power to be heard after Election Day. Campaign finance reform is indispensable, but if it is separated from the larger fight against economic and political inequality, it will disappoint. The goal should not be cleaner fundraising alone. It should be democracy strong enough to discipline wealth.
