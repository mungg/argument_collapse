"The moral case for amnesty should be based on a shifting moral calculus regarding the costs of enforcement rather than the idea that the state's legitimate authority to control its borders diminishes over time."

Carens makes an important contribution by emphasizing that immigration violations are administrative offenses, not crimes, and by drawing the analogy to traffic violations. But this analogy, while useful, conceals more than it reveals.

The relevant difference is not the severity of the offense but its relationship to state authority. Traffic violations harm individuals directly—speeding drivers may injure pedestrians, for example. Immigration violations, by contrast, are defined by the state itself and concern an individual's relationship to political authority rather than to other individuals. This means that the comparison to statutes of limitations is weaker than Carens suggests: we waive prosecution for old crimes because the state's interest in punishment diminishes over time and the harm to the individual of perpetual liability is disproportionate; but the state's interest in controlling territory and membership does not necessarily diminish in the same way.

Consider: if an undocumented immigrant has been present for twenty years, the state's territorial interest in controlling who may reside within its borders remains exactly what it was on day one. The migrant may have become a community member, but the community is still defined by the state's legal boundaries. What has changed is not the state's interest but our moral assessment of the costs of enforcement.

This is actually a stronger argument for amnesty than Carens's, because it does not rely on the fiction that time somehow diminishes the state's legitimate authority. The state retains full authority; what changes is the moral calculus of when and how to exercise it. Deportation may still be legal, but it may be wrong—or at least wrong to prioritize over the integration of long-term residents.

This framing also clarifies the proper remedy: not an automatic right to regularization, but a strong presumption against deportation that leaves room for individualized exceptions in cases of fraud, criminality, or national security concerns. Carens's automatic entitlement may be too rigid; a structured discretion that takes social membership seriously without creating an absolute right may better serve both the migrants' interests and the state's legitimate authority.
