Diversified Gemini

"The Left should abandon the attempt to reclaim the language of 'freedom' and instead focus on the unvarnished pursuit of collective power and material redistribution."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" provides a necessary corrective to the historical amnesia that often characterizes American political discourse. By tracing the right-wing capture of "freedom" back to the structural realities of settler colonialism, imperial expansion, and racial capitalism, Rana forces us to confront the ugly material basis of American liberty. He correctly identifies that the classical liberal conception of freedom—defined as self-mastery, individualism, and property ownership—was subsidized by the expropriation of Indigenous land and the exploitation of Black labor. However, while Rana’s historical materialism is sharp, his political conclusion—that the Left must now reclaim and redefine the language of "freedom"—is deeply misguided.

Rana envisions a massive political project to construct a new, "solidaristic" freedom, achieved through institution-building, workplace democracy, and a radical overhaul of U.S. foreign and immigration policy. But in clinging to the rhetoric of freedom, Rana traps the Left in a bourgeois conceptual framework that is fundamentally ill-suited to the demands of a socialist project. The Left should not be trying to reclaim freedom; it should be focusing on the unvarnished pursuit of *power* and the material redistribution of resources. "Freedom" is not a neutral vessel waiting to be filled with progressive values; it is the ideological architecture of capitalism, and attempting to repurpose it is a strategic dead end.

To understand why the Left must abandon the discourse of freedom, we must recognize its fundamental nature. As Karl Marx pointed out, the concept of individual freedom arose in tandem with the development of the capitalist mode of production. The "free" worker was free in a double sense: free to sell their labor power to the highest bidder, and "free" from the ownership of the means of production—meaning they had no choice but to sell their labor to survive. The entire ideological edifice of liberal freedom—freedom of contract, freedom of speech, freedom of movement—functions to facilitate the smooth operation of capital accumulation and to obscure the underlying reality of class exploitation.

Rana acknowledges this to some extent, noting that the "zero-sum" account of freedom won out in America. But he treats this as a contingent historical tragedy, a wrong turn facilitated by the frontier and empire. He believes there is a latent, "inclusive" version of freedom—glimpsed in the Civil Rights Movement and the early labor movement—that can be excavated and generalized. This is a dangerous illusion. The moments of working-class and anti-racist victory Rana cites did not succeed because they won a debate over the definition of freedom; they succeeded because they amassed sufficient counter-power to force concessions from capital and the state.

When the Congress of Industrial Organizations (CIO) organized millions of workers in the 1930s, they weren't primarily engaged in a philosophical project of redefining liberty; they were engaged in brutal, physical class warfare. They occupied factories, fought Pinkerton guards, and paralyzed production. The rhetoric of freedom may have been used as a mobilizing tool, but the actual substance of the movement was the assertion of collective power. By centering the *concept* of freedom, Rana elevates the ideological superstructure above the material base, suggesting that if we simply build the right institutions to foster the right "experiences" of solidarity, a new cultural narrative will emerge.

This puts the cart before the horse. You cannot build the institutions of workplace democracy—like those envisioned in the PRO Act—without first defeating the entrenched power of the capitalist class. And capitalists do not care about your definition of freedom. They care about their profit margins. To defeat them requires a language and a strategy of confrontation, discipline, and class solidarity, not a semantic struggle over a word that is genetically coded for individualism.

Furthermore, Rana’s attempt to stretch the concept of freedom to encompass anti-imperialism and open borders reveals the incoherence of the project. He argues that domestic freedom requires ending U.S. military hegemony and establishing "presumptive admission for all" migrants. While internationalism and opposition to imperial war are essential components of a left-wing program, framing these as prerequisites for "freedom" dilutes the concept to the point of meaninglessness. If freedom means everything—from unionizing a Starbucks to dismantling the Pentagon to abolishing border controls—it means nothing.

This conceptual overstretch also ignores the immediate material interests of the working class in the core capitalist countries. While it is true in a long-term, theoretical sense that the global working class shares a common interest in defeating capital, in the short term, workers within a nation-state often rely on the very structures of empire and bordered economies that Rana wishes to dismantle. The mid-century American middle class, which Rana rightly notes benefited from imperial rents, is a prime example. Telling an autoworker in Ohio that their true "freedom" requires liquidating the borders and defunding the military is not a winning political strategy; it is a rapid route to alienation. The Left must build solidarity based on shared material struggles, not on a requirement that workers adopt an abstract, globalist conception of liberty.

The right-wing dominance of "freedom" rhetoric is not a messaging failure by Democrats; it is a reflection of the fact that the word accurately describes the right-wing project: the freedom of capital from regulation, the freedom of the wealthy from taxation, and the freedom of the individual to act without regard for the collective good. The Right owns the word because the word belongs to them. It is the perfect linguistic encapsulation of bourgeois ideology.

Instead of fighting a losing battle on the enemy's terrain, the Left must articulate its own values, centered squarely on material security, equality, and collective power. We should talk about *guarantees*, not freedoms. A jobs guarantee. Guaranteed housing. Universal healthcare. We should talk about *power*—the power of workers to control production, the power of tenants to control their housing, the power of the public to dictate the direction of investment.

Rana is entirely correct that the left's current malaise is structural, stemming from the neoliberal erosion of working-class institutions. But the solution to this is the arduous, unglamorous work of organizing, striking, and building militant working-class formations. This work does not require a new theory of freedom; it requires a clear-eyed understanding of class conflict. Let the Right have "freedom." The Left should demand the world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The concept of freedom is not a neutral value but is the ideological architecture of capitalism, designed to facilitate capital accumulation and obscure class exploitation.
  2. 02Historical progress for the working class has been achieved through the exercise of material counter-power and class warfare rather than through winning philosophical debates over the definition of liberty.
  3. 03Centering a political project on the rhetoric of freedom mistakenly prioritizes ideological superstructure over the necessary material task of defeating the entrenched power of the capitalist class.
  4. 04Attempting to redefine freedom to include anti-imperialism and open borders results in a conceptual overstretch that alienates the domestic working class from their immediate material interests.
  5. 05The Left should replace the bourgeois discourse of 'freedom' with a platform centered on material guarantees, such as universal healthcare and housing, and the direct control of production.
