Diversified Gemini

"While Soper correctly identifies the failures of consumer culture, her reliance on shifting individual desires is idealistic; achieving a post-growth society requires a focus on transforming the built environment and institutional architecture of choice."

Kate Soper’s "For an Alternative Hedonism" correctly identifies the core contradiction of modern affluent societies: we are destroying the ecological foundations of life on Earth to sustain a mode of consumption that does not even make us happy. Her call to re-evaluate what constitutes the "good life" is a vital intellectual exercise. Yet, in relying so heavily on the concept of "structures of feeling" and a shift in consumer desire, Soper risks falling into a frustrating idealism. She diagnoses a structural, infrastructural, and architectural problem, but proposes what is fundamentally a cultural and psychological solution. To achieve the post-growth society Soper envisions, we must look past individual desires and focus on the built environment and the institutional architecture of choice.

Soper writes eloquently about the miseries of car culture—the toxic fumes, the traffic deaths, the spatial monopoly, the destruction of wildlife—and contrasts this with the "balm of solitude and silence" offered by walking and biking. She notes that if we reverse these trends, we could "readily provide for safe transit for pedestrians and cyclists." But how do we reverse these trends? The persistence of car culture in countries like the United States is not primarily the result of a cultural failure of imagination or a collective psychological addiction to speed. It is the result of decades of deliberate, state-sponsored infrastructural choices and corporate lobbying.

Since the mid-20th century, zoning laws, highway funding structures, and urban planning models have made the private automobile a mandatory prosthesis for survival in most North American communities. People do not drive because they reject the "pleasures of ambling and gossiping"; they drive because their cities lack sidewalks, because public transit has been systematically defunded, and because Euclidean zoning laws have separated residential areas from commercial centers by miles of un-walkable sprawl. You cannot choose "alternative hedonism" when the physical geography of your life mandates carbon-intensive consumption.

The same logic applies to Soper’s critique of fast fashion and the "convenience of Walmart and Amazon." The reliance on these hyper-exploitative, high-waste systems is deeply tied to the spatial and temporal realities of modern labor. When workers face long commutes, erratic scheduling, and stagnant wages, the "convenience" of cheap, delivered goods is not a frivolous luxury; it is a vital coping mechanism.

Therefore, a political project aimed at achieving a low-carbon, high-welfare society must be ruthlessly focused on institutional and infrastructural transformation, rather than moral or aesthetic persuasion. We do not need to wait for a critical mass of citizens to develop a new "structure of feeling" toward post-consumerism. We need a muscular, state-led industrial policy that forces a transition in the physical environment.

This means abolishing single-family zoning to allow for dense, walkable, mixed-use neighborhoods. It means reallocating the vast sums of public money currently spent on highway expansion toward the construction of high-speed rail and comprehensive urban light rail networks. It means aggressive government regulation to ban planned obsolescence, requiring manufacturers to produce durable, repairable goods. It means implementing extended producer responsibility (EPR) laws, forcing the corporations that create toxic e-waste and plastic packaging to pay for its full lifecycle costs, thereby making the disposable economy economically unviable.

Soper suggests that "concerned consumers... must lead the way in this rethinking." But the historical record shows that massive shifts in public behavior usually follow, rather than precede, shifts in infrastructure. When Paris Mayor Anne Hidalgo aggressively removed car lanes and expanded bicycle infrastructure, cycling rates exploded. The citizens of Paris did not suddenly undergo a spiritual conversion to alternative hedonism; their environment changed to make cycling safe, efficient, and pleasant. Behavior follows design.

Furthermore, Soper’s wariness of the "state" and "experts" dictating happiness misses the point that our current happiness is already being aggressively dictated by a highly organized system of corporate power and captured regulatory agencies. To dismantle this system requires utilizing the coercive power of the democratic state. We must tax the advertising industry into oblivion to stop the artificial stimulation of demand. We must regulate the algorithms of surveillance capitalism that Soper rightfully decries.

Soper has beautifully described the destination: a slower, fairer, and more joyful society. But her map for getting there relies too much on the nebulous hope that people will simply realize they want it. Changing what people want is a long, slow, and uncertain cultural project. Changing what people can actually do by redesigning their physical and economic reality is a tangible political project. Let us build the infrastructure of alternative hedonism, and the desires will follow.

  2. 02Reliance on the convenience of high-waste systems like Amazon is a vital coping mechanism for workers trapped in the spatial and temporal realities of modern labor.
  3. 03Political efforts should prioritize muscular, state-led industrial policies—such as abolishing single-family zoning and banning planned obsolescence—over moral or aesthetic persuasion.
  4. 04Historical evidence suggests that massive shifts in public behavior follow changes in physical infrastructure and design rather than preceding them.
  5. 05Dismantling the current system of corporate-dictated happiness requires utilizing the coercive power of the democratic state to regulate advertising and surveillance capitalism.
