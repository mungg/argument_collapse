"The question of abortion access should be settled through the democratic legislative process rather than judicial decree because it involves a moral disagreement among reasonable citizens that requires democratic legitimacy."

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s article makes a sophisticated argument for why the state should not restrict abortion, but it conspicuously does not make the case for why courts, rather than legislatures, should have the final say. In fact, she asserts that “there can be no principled objection to the settlement of the issue by the judicial rather than the legislative branch” because fundamental liberties are at stake. But this simply begs the question. Whether abortion is a fundamental liberty is precisely what democratic majorities disagree about. Thomson’s own reasoning unwittingly provides a powerful argument for letting the people decide.

Consider her central claim: that restricting abortion on the ground that the fetus has a right to life from conception is illegitimate because the constrained—women who reject that premise—are not unreasonable in doing so. This is a claim about the reasonableness of moral pluralism. If that is true, it follows that the issue is, in John Rawls’s terms, a matter on which reasonable people can differ. In a liberal democracy, such issues are paradigmatically suited for legislative deliberation, where competing reasonable views can be weighed, compromises forged, and legitimacy secured through the consent of the governed. By contrast, when a court imposes one side’s view on the whole nation, it short-circuits that deliberative process and provokes the very rage and violence Thomson deplores. The backlash against Roe v. Wade is not just about the substance of abortion; it is about the loss of democratic self-rule. Many citizens rightly perceive that a moral issue of profound importance was taken out of their hands.

Thomson dismisses this concern by citing a study suggesting that legislative action would have resulted in less access to abortion than we currently enjoy. But that is exactly the point: in a democracy, the level of access should reflect the considered moral judgment of the people, not the preferences of judges or philosophers. If the majority of Americans, after full debate, came to believe that late-term abortions should be restricted, or that parental consent laws are appropriate, or that public funding should not be available, those judgments deserve respect. The Court’s usurpation has poisoned abortion politics, turning every election into a referendum on judicial appointments and making political compromise nearly impossible.

Thomson’s analogy to Bossuet—“I have the right to persecute you because I am right and you are wrong”—is revealing. In a democracy, a legislative majority that enacts a law does not “persecute”; it governs on the basis of a collective, if contested, moral vision. The law always involves the imposition of force. The question is whether that imposition has democratic legitimacy. Thomson’s alternative is that the constrained must themselves accept the reason for the constraint. By that standard, virtually no law could survive, since there will always be some who reject its premise. The proper standard is that the reason be one that can be publicly advanced in a democracy, even if some reject it. The claim that human life begins at conception is such a reason, whether or not one finds it compelling.

Finally, Thomson’s assertion that the abortion right is necessary for women’s equality is a powerful substantive argument—but it is an argument that should be made to fellow citizens, not decreed by nine justices. If it is as persuasive as she believes, it will carry the day in the legislative arena. The path to lasting social peace on abortion is not through judicial fiat, but through a democratic consensus that respects the deeply held convictions on all sides. That process will be messy and imperfect, but it is the only one consistent with a free society.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Thomson’s own premise of moral pluralism suggests that abortion is a matter on which reasonable people differ, making it paradigmatically suited for legislative deliberation rather than a single judicial imposition.
  2. 02The judicial 'usurpation' of the abortion issue has poisoned national politics and provoked social instability by depriving citizens of their right to democratic self-rule on a profound moral question.
  3. 03In a democracy, the level of abortion access should reflect the collective moral judgment of the people through their elected representatives, even if that results in more restrictions than a court would allow.
  4. 04Thomson’s requirement that the constrained must accept the reason for a law is an impossible standard that would invalidate almost all legislation; democratic legitimacy only requires that reasons be publicly defensible.
  5. 05Substantive arguments regarding women's equality are powerful but should be used to persuade fellow citizens in the political arena to build a lasting social consensus.
