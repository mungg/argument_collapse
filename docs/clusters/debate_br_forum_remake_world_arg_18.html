"While Johnson’s framework of racial capitalism correctly centers slavery in political economy, it must be expanded to recognize settler colonialism as a distinct system focused on the elimination of Indigenous sovereignty rather than just the exploitation of labor."

Walter Johnson’s essay powerfully insists that slavery must be placed at the center of modern political economy. But from an Indigenous perspective, the essay’s own framework remains incomplete. Johnson does mention Native dispossession, especially the expropriation of Creek, Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole lands and the foundational role of Johnson v. McIntosh in U.S. property law. That acknowledgment matters. Yet Indigenous history appears mostly as the land base on which racial capitalism expanded. If we are to remake the world, Native nations cannot be treated as the ground beneath another story.

The United States was built through slavery and settler colonialism together. These systems intersected, reinforced each other, and sometimes operated through the same institutions, but they are not identical. Slavery sought to extract labor from people rendered property. Settler colonialism sought to acquire land and eliminate Indigenous political authority. Slavery commodified persons; settler colonialism converted Native homelands into alienable property under U.S. sovereignty. Slavery relied on the reproduction of enslaved labor; settler colonialism often sought the disappearance, absorption, removal, or containment of Indigenous peoples. Both were racial. Both were capitalist in important respects. But each has its own structure and its own demands for justice.

This distinction matters because reparations for slavery and decolonization are related but not interchangeable. A reparative program that redistributes wealth without confronting land and sovereignty may leave the settler state intact. A politics that treats Native people only as another dispossessed racial minority can erase the nation-to-nation relationships, treaty obligations, and territorial claims at the heart of Indigenous justice. Conversely, a politics of tribal sovereignty that ignores anti-blackness and the history of slavery, including slavery within some Native nations, would also be morally inadequate. The task is not to rank harms, but to understand their interdependence without collapsing them.

Johnson v. McIntosh is indeed a crucial starting point. In 1823, the Supreme Court declared that Native nations held a right of occupancy but not full title against the United States. The doctrine of discovery placed ultimate dominion in European and then American sovereigns. This decision did not merely facilitate private property; it announced a metaphysics of settler power. Native peoples could live on land until the United States decided otherwise, but they could not possess the same kind of title as those who claimed discovery and conquest. The law translated invasion into ownership.

That legal structure remains with us. Much of the United States rests on treaties broken, lands seized, allotments imposed, resources extracted, and jurisdiction curtailed. The cotton kingdom expanded through Indian removal. Railroads crossed Native lands. Mining, timber, dams, oil, and pipelines have repeatedly treated Indigenous territories as sacrifice zones. Boarding schools attacked language and kinship. Federal policy oscillated among removal, allotment, assimilation, termination, and limited self-determination, but the underlying question has remained: will Native nations be allowed to exist as peoples with land, law, and political authority?

Johnson’s framework of racial capitalism helps explain the economic motives and racial ideologies behind these processes. But settler colonialism is not reducible to labor exploitation. In some cases, Native labor was exploited directly. In others, Native presence was treated as an obstacle to be removed so that land could be made productive by enslaved Africans, white settlers, railroads, or corporations. The “resource” capitalism wanted was not always labor; often it was land, water, timber, minerals, routes, and jurisdiction. A theory that begins with slavery may not fully capture a system whose logic is replacement.

This is why the language of “dehumanization,” which Johnson critiques, has a different resonance in Indigenous history. Native peoples were sometimes depicted as savages, beasts, children, or vanishing remnants. But they were also romanticized, treaty-making partners when convenient, military enemies when resistant, wards when contained, and citizens when assimilation demanded it. The settler state did not simply deny Native humanity. It denied Native sovereignty. It could recognize Indians as human individuals while destroying Indigenous peoples as political communities. Liberal rights, in fact, were often used as weapons of assimilation. The allotment era promised individual property and citizenship while breaking communal land bases. Here Johnson’s skepticism toward rights is especially pertinent. Rights detached from collective self-determination can become instruments of conquest.

What would justice rooted in both slavery and settler colonialism require? First, it would require land. Not only symbolic acknowledgments before public events, not only museum exhibits, not only diversity programs, but land return and land governance. This need not mean the expulsion of all non-Native people. It does mean restoring territories where possible, expanding tribal jurisdiction, honoring treaty rights, co-managing resources on terms set by Native nations, and preventing further extraction without free, prior, and informed consent. Land is not merely property. It is the material basis of peoplehood.

Second, it would require sovereignty. Native nations are not interest groups. They are political communities with inherent powers that predate the United States. Justice must include respect for tribal courts, lawmaking authority, citizenship rules, cultural protection, and economic self-determination. The federal government’s trust responsibility should not be a paternalistic excuse for control, but a binding obligation arising from dispossession.

Third, it would require environmental repair. Johnson’s essay rightly connects racial capitalism to the instrumentalization of nature. Indigenous communities have long known that domination over people and domination over land are inseparable. The poisoning of water, destruction of sacred sites, removal of forests, depletion of fisheries, and contamination from mining are not merely environmental harms; they are attacks on culture, subsistence, health, and spiritual life. The recent resistance at Standing Rock has made visible to many Americans what Native communities have long confronted: infrastructure praised as development often requires that Indigenous lands and waters bear disproportionate risk.

Fourth, justice would require confronting anti-blackness within Indigenous and settler histories alike. The relationship between Native nations and African Americans is complex. There have been alliances, intermarriages, shared communities, and mutual aid. There have also been Native slaveholders, exclusion of freedpeople from tribal citizenship, and participation in anti-black structures. The Five Tribes, whose removal opened land for cotton slavery and who themselves had varied relations to slavery, force us to reject simple binaries of colonizer and colonized. A decolonial politics must be anti-racist, including anti-anti-blackness, or it will reproduce domination under another name.

Fifth, reparations for slavery must be designed in ways that do not deepen settler claims. For example, proposals involving land redistribution must ask: whose land? Public lands are often Native lands held by the federal government through conquest or treaty violation. Urban land also has Indigenous histories. Housing justice, community investment, and black land claims are vital, but they should be pursued with awareness of Indigenous sovereignty. This is not an argument against reparations. It is an argument for reparations and decolonization to be thought together.

Johnson’s essay is valuable because it refuses innocence. The wealth of the United States was not created in a neutral market and later marred by prejudice. It was made through coercion, expropriation, racial law, and violence. But “racial capitalism” should not become a container into which all histories are poured until their distinct shapes disappear. Native dispossession is not simply one example of racial capitalism. It is the founding condition of the settler polity within which racial capitalism developed in North America.

The phrase “Global South” also needs care. Indigenous nations within the borders of the United States are often treated as internal colonies, but they are not simply domestic minorities. Nor do they fit neatly into North/South geography. Some reservations contain conditions associated with the poorest regions of the world, but they exist inside one of the richest states on earth. This internal colonial relation complicates any map that imagines exploitation as something that happens over there while metropoles sit over here. The colony is also here.

A justice adequate to the present must therefore be multi-centered. If we begin only from European human rights, we miss slavery, colonialism, and land. If we begin only from slavery, we may miss sovereignty and settler replacement. If we begin only from class, we miss race and nation. If we begin only from identity, we miss political economy. The challenge is to hold these histories together without forcing them into a single hierarchy.

The current moment makes this urgent. A new administration has taken power amid intensified nationalism, border militarization, hostility to Muslims and immigrants, and promises to expand fossil fuel extraction. Native nations will face renewed pressure on land and water. Black communities will face intensified policing and backlash against movements for racial justice. Immigrants will face raids and exclusion. These struggles are connected, but connection should be built through respect, not absorption.

To remake the world, we must ask different questions from the ones the settler state asks. Not merely: how can individuals be included in American prosperity? But: what is America’s prosperity built on? Not merely: how can minorities gain rights? But: what nations, lands, and peoples did the United States claim the power to define as minorities? Not merely: how can the harms of the past be remembered? But: what legal structures from that past continue to organize ownership and sovereignty?

Johnson’s essay opens a necessary door by placing slavery and racial capitalism at the center of justice. We should walk through it carrying treaties, maps, oral histories, land claims, and the names of Native nations. The world cannot be remade if the land remains stolen and the theft remains the unspoken premise of every reform.
