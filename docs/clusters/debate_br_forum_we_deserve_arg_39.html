Diversified Gemini

"While Evgeny Morozov correctly identifies the philosophical and political limitations of AI, his critique is incomplete because it overlooks the technology's role as a tool for labor exploitation and the necessity of class struggle in democratizing it."

Evgeny Morozov’s compelling essay, “The AI We Deserve,” deftly unpacks the historical baggage of artificial intelligence, exposing its roots in Cold War teleology and bureaucratic optimization. By juxtaposing this “instrumental reason” with the eolithic “ecological reason” of Hans Otto Storm, Morozov provides a rich vocabulary for understanding the limits of generative AI. He rightly notes that the technology, while currently facilitating a kind of playful bricolage, is ultimately captured by the neoliberal “Efficiency Lobby,” which constrains its emancipatory potential. His turn to Latin American traditions of “normative planning” and participatory computing as models for a democratic AI is inspiring. Yet, while Morozov successfully deconstructs the epistemology of AI and its corporate ownership, his analysis misses a crucial dimension of the AI debate: the profound impact of generative AI on the global division of labor and the structural power of the working class.

Morozov’s vision of “ecological reason” centers on the individual or the citizen—the language learner, the flâneur, the participant in a budgeting meeting—engaged in a process of creative discovery and political deliberation. What is conspicuously absent from this utopian horizon is the worker. By framing the problem of AI primarily as a crisis of *reason* and *imagination*, Morozov risks abstracting the technology away from the contested terrain of the workplace, where AI’s most immediate and devastating impacts are currently unfolding.

The “Efficiency Lobby” that Morozov critiques is not simply a philosophical orientation toward problem-solving; it is a material project of capital accumulation predicated on the deskilling and discipline of labor. From its inception, the automation of cognitive tasks has been driven by management’s desire to break the power of skilled professionals and clerical workers, making them easily replaceable. Generative AI represents the latest, and perhaps most aggressive, phase of this project. When a hospital uses AI to summarize patient records, or a media company uses LLMs to generate copy, the primary goal is rarely to free workers to engage in “ecological reason.” The goal is to speed up the pace of work, lower wages, and eventually reduce headcount.

Morozov argues that AI’s focus on goal-attainment limits its potential. But for the worker on the factory floor, in the Amazon warehouse, or at the digital content farm, the problem is not that AI is too focused on goals; the problem is that the AI is being used as a mechanism of algorithmic management to squeeze every last drop of productivity from their bodies and minds. The technology is deployed to monitor keystrokes, analyze facial expressions during Zoom calls, and dynamically adjust quotas. In this context, calling for a shift to “ecological reason” feels like an intellectual luxury that fails to address the brutal reality of digital Taylorism.

Even when Morozov references the Latin American experiments, such as Salvador Allende’s Project Cybersyn, he frames them primarily as exercises in cognitive and political expansion—tools for “deliberation” and “imagination.” But the primary objective of Cybersyn was deeply material and rooted in labor power. It was an attempt to maintain industrial production in the face of violent sabotage by the capitalist class and the CIA (the trucker strikes). It was about securing the economic survival of the working class, not just facilitating “problem posing.” The tragedy of Cybersyn was not just that it was politically defeated, but that it was a desperate defensive measure by a besieged proletariat.

If we are to articulate a political project for the “AI we deserve,” it cannot merely be a project of reimagining how we use AI in the public sphere or in our personal lives. It must begin with building counter-power at the point of production. The real alternative to the neoliberal AI paradigm is not found in the abstract musings of cybernetic philosophers, but in the concrete demands of organized labor.

Over the past two years, the most significant democratic interventions into the trajectory of generative AI have not come from state regulators or open-source advocates, but from striking workers. The Writers Guild of America (WGA) and the Screen Actors Guild (SAG-AFTRA) strikes of 2023 were historic not just for their duration, but for their direct confrontation with the “Efficiency Lobby’s” AI agenda. The writers and actors did not ask for AI to be made more “eolithic.” They demanded, and won, strict contractual limits on how studios could use generative AI, ensuring that the technology could not be used to replace human labor or scrape their likenesses without consent and compensation.

This is what democratizing AI looks like in practice. It requires strong unions capable of forcing capital to negotiate the terms of technological adoption. A solidarity-based technological agenda must include demands for algorithmic transparency in hiring and firing, the right to disconnect from AI-driven management systems, and a guaranteed share of the productivity gains generated by automation (perhaps in the form of a shorter workweek).

Morozov is absolutely right that we must untether AI from its market-driven development model and stop treating human beings as “fleshy thermostats.” But this untethering will not happen simply by demonstrating the value of public-sector prototypes or by cultivating a new *Weltanschauung*. It will only happen through class struggle. The “Humanity Lobby” will only succeed if it is anchored in the labor movement, using the collective power of workers to halt the deployment of AI as a weapon of exploitation, and fighting to redesign it as a tool that genuinely lightens the load of human toil.
