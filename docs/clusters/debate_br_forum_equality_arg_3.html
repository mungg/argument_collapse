"While the lead essay correctly identifies the limitations of superficial 'equal opportunity' rhetoric, the proposed alternative of 'equal flourishing' should be rejected because it risks undermining liberal values like pluralism, individual autonomy, and the distinction between justice and the good life."

Christine Sypnowich’s essay is an eloquent challenge to a complacent political slogan. She is plainly right that “equal opportunity” is often used to evade questions about inequality of condition. When politicians praise opportunity while tolerating child poverty, racialized wealth gaps, unaffordable housing, and radically unequal schools, suspicion is warranted. But Sypnowich’s conclusion—that egalitarians should replace opportunity with equal flourishing as their guiding ideal—goes too far. It risks discarding precisely the liberal insights that make egalitarian politics humane: respect for pluralism, skepticism about concentrated authority, and a distinction between justice and the whole of the good life.

The problem is not that liberal egalitarianism cares only about formal opportunity. The best liberal egalitarian theories were developed in large part to show why formal opportunity is inadequate. Rawls’s fair equality of opportunity, for example, is not the thin idea that anyone may apply for a job. It demands that those with similar talents and ambitions have similar prospects regardless of class background. The difference principle then requires that social and economic inequalities be arranged to benefit the least advantaged. That is a profoundly demanding view. It would condemn the actual United States, Britain, and Canada not because they have failed to equalize flourishing, but because they permit class, race, wealth, and inheritance to structure life chances on a massive scale.

Likewise, the capability approach is not indifferent to outcomes. Sen and Nussbaum shifted attention away from income alone toward what people are actually able to do and be: to avoid premature death, to be educated, to participate, to be secure, to appear in public without shame. Calling these “opportunities” does not make them trivial. A capability is not a lottery ticket; it is a real freedom. If a person has no genuine ability to be nourished, housed, educated, or politically active, then capability theorists do not shrug and say the gate was open. They say justice has failed.

Sypnowich is strongest when criticizing luck egalitarianism’s harsher formulations. A political morality that asks whether the imprudent poor deserve their suffering can become morally ugly. The line between choice and circumstance is blurry, and social disadvantage does shape preferences, habits, and risks. But one need not embrace equal flourishing to reject punitive responsibility. Rawls did not make aid to the badly off depend on proving that their choices were blameless. Elizabeth Anderson’s democratic equality, though critical of luck egalitarianism, remains broadly liberal in its concern with equal standing rather than equal welfare. There is ample room within liberal egalitarianism for unconditional social insurance, universal public goods, and opposition to humiliating means tests.

The more serious difficulty with Sypnowich’s view concerns pluralism. She acknowledges that flourishing comes in many forms and that democratic debate should determine what counts as valuable. But this assurance does not fully answer the liberal worry. In modern societies citizens disagree deeply and reasonably about religion, family, work, art, pleasure, risk, discipline, community, and the meaning of success. Some think a flourishing life is centered on faith; others on artistic creation, entrepreneurship, public service, contemplation, parenting, scholarship, sport, or friendship. Some value intense achievement; others value tranquility. Some willingly accept hardship for a vocation. Some reject the very language of flourishing as too therapeutic or perfectionist.

A state committed to equal opportunity need not be indifferent to all values. Public education, public health, environmental protection, and civic equality all rest on judgments about human interests. But there remains an important difference between securing the conditions under which people can pursue their own lives and seeking equality in how well those lives go. The first is difficult but politically intelligible. The second invites either vagueness or intrusion. If government is responsible for equal flourishing, what should it do when some groups are less happy, less healthy, less socially connected, or less culturally engaged, despite having robust resources and rights? Should it redesign their communities, reshape their preferences, pressure them into activities, or treat their choices as evidence of false consciousness?

Sypnowich’s music example reveals the tension. I agree that public schools should expose children to music and the arts. Such exposure expands imagination and combats class exclusion. But the reason to support music education is not that the state knows children will flourish more musically than otherwise, nor that unequal adult engagement with music is itself an injustice. The reason is that education should furnish citizens with a broad repertoire of understanding and experience. After that, some people may never touch an instrument again, and there is no egalitarian failure in that. A life without music may be poorer by some standards, but justice should hesitate before making that judgment politically central.

There is also a measurement problem. Sypnowich says we care about inequality because of its effects on people, and that if the supposedly unequal are doing equally well in quality of life, inequality loses its sting. But flourishing is not like income or voting rights. It is multidimensional, partly subjective, partly objective, and often incommensurable. Consider two people: one a low-paid monk living communally, one a highly paid surgeon working long hours, one a parent caring for disabled children, one a solitary poet with precarious income but deep vocation. Who is flourishing more? If they differ, does justice require equalization? By what metric? To ask government to equalize flourishing is to ask it to compare goods that citizens themselves may refuse to rank in a common scale.

Liberal neutrality is often caricatured as the fantasy that the state can avoid shaping values. Of course it cannot. Laws and institutions always influence conduct. But neutrality, properly understood, is not the absence of all value judgment. It is a discipline of justification. The state should justify coercive laws and basic institutions by reasons that citizens with diverse conceptions of the good can reasonably accept. It may support education because citizens need capacities for agency and participation; health care because bodily security is a general precondition of pursuing many ends; income support because destitution undermines independence; anti-discrimination law because caste hierarchy denies equal citizenship. It should be more cautious about declaring which lives are flourishing and arranging policy to equalize them.

Sypnowich worries that if democratic institutions do not shape values, markets will. This is a powerful point. Market societies do not leave desire untouched; they commercialize attention, status, body image, sex, childhood, and leisure. But the answer is not to abandon liberal constraints. It is to democratize and regulate the background conditions of choice: limit monopoly power, restrict manipulative advertising to children, fund noncommercial spaces, protect public broadcasting, support libraries and parks, reduce working hours, and strengthen unions. These policies expand citizens’ effective freedom without requiring the state to assess whether each person’s life is equally flourishing.

The distinction matters politically. Egalitarianism already faces suspicion that it wants to level, supervise, or homogenize. Sypnowich insists that equal flourishing need not entail leveling down or coercion, and I believe her. But ideals have consequences beyond their best formulations. A politics organized around equal flourishing could too easily license busybody governance, moralized public health, or cultural favoritism. It may empower educated elites to define “valuable pursuits” in ways that mirror their own tastes. Even democratic deliberation is not immune to majority prejudice or class-coded judgments about how others ought to live.

A better egalitarian ideal is not mere opportunity in the thin political sense, but equal citizenship under fair institutions. This includes strong social rights: universal health care, child allowances, decent housing, high-quality schools, disability support, public transportation, a living wage, collective bargaining, progressive taxation, and inheritance taxation. It includes anti-domination: protection from abusive employers, landlords, creditors, police, and intimate violence. It includes democratic inclusion and civic respect. Such a society would produce much more equal outcomes than ours. It would not be indifferent to whether people can actually live decent lives. But it would stop short of making equal flourishing the direct object of justice.

One might say this is merely Sypnowich’s view in different words. Perhaps. If “equal flourishing” means that institutions should secure for all the robust conditions of a dignified life, then liberal egalitarians can agree. But if it means that justice demands equalization of how well people’s lives actually go, then disagreement remains. Justice should be ambitious about the social bases of agency and status; it should be modest about judging completed lives.

Equal opportunity, in its political-slogan form, is not enough. On that Sypnowich is right. But the remedy is not to demote opportunity. It is to recover its radical meaning and embed it in a broader liberal egalitarian account of equal citizenship. People should not merely have a formal chance to flourish. They should have the resources, rights, capabilities, and social standing that make self-authorship possible. Whether they flourish equally thereafter is a matter for families, associations, cultures, friendships, faiths, and individuals themselves—not for justice alone.

"While the state should ensure fair chances and basic needs, it should not pursue a policy of equal flourishing because such an aim oversteps the rightful limits of politics and undermines individual liberty and moral agency."

Christine Sypnowich offers a serious argument for a more ambitious egalitarianism than the familiar politics of equal opportunity. Her essay should make defenders of market liberal societies uncomfortable, especially when they use opportunity as a substitute for confronting inherited poverty, failing schools, family breakdown, addiction, and deindustrialized communities. Yet her proposed alternative—an egalitarian politics aimed at equal flourishing—would entrust too much to politics and too little to liberty, civil society, and the moral agency of persons.

The ordinary language of equal opportunity contains more wisdom than Sypnowich allows. It reflects a modest but important judgment: society should remove artificial and unjust barriers, but it should not promise to make lives come out equally well. People differ in ability, ambition, temperament, discipline, risk tolerance, family attachments, religious convictions, and preferences. They also make choices that matter. Some save, some spend; some study, some do not; some marry, divorce, move, start businesses, care for relatives, take dangerous jobs, or pursue uncertain vocations. A free society cannot insulate everyone from the consequences of these differences without becoming deeply intrusive.

Sypnowich is right that choices are shaped by circumstances. No serious person denies that family, neighborhood, culture, and economic conditions influence conduct. But influence is not erasure. If we treat bad choices primarily as social products, we risk denying people the dignity of agency. The young man who avoids crime in a violent neighborhood, the single mother who pursues training while raising children, the recovering addict who rebuilds his life, the immigrant who works long hours to establish a business—all testify that circumstance constrains but does not determine. Public policy should help people exercise responsibility, not dissolve responsibility into sociology.

The distinction between choice and circumstance is imperfect, but many distinctions necessary to law and morality are imperfect. We still distinguish accident from intention, negligence from malice, need from fraud, disability from unwillingness, childhood from adulthood. A humane society should avoid cruelty toward the imprudent, but it need not say that justice requires equal outcomes regardless of conduct. If someone repeatedly refuses work, abandons obligations, or dissipates resources, fellow citizens may owe emergency aid and opportunities for restoration. They do not owe the same claim as to someone struck by illness, disability, or economic displacement through no fault of his own. To deny this is not compassion; it is moral flattening.

Sypnowich’s critique of market influence also needs balance. Markets shape preferences, sometimes badly. Advertising can be manipulative; consumer culture can be vulgar; profit can crowd out nobler goods. But markets also protect pluralism by decentralizing decision-making. They allow people to pursue countless ends without asking permission from officials or majorities. They reward attentiveness to what others want, not always perfectly but often more flexibly than bureaucracies. They create wealth that funds education, health care, culture, and charity. A society suspicious of markets should be equally suspicious of the state, which has its own temptations: conformity, patronage, ideological control, incompetence, and coercion.

The language of flourishing heightens these dangers. Who decides what counts as flourishing? Sypnowich says democratic debate should do so and that coercion should be avoided. But democratic majorities are not automatically wise or gentle. They can be moralistic, class-biased, nationalistic, secularizing, religiously coercive, or simply fashionable. A public authority that “encourages people to see the value of one choice over another” may sound benign when the choice is music lessons rather than television. It sounds less benign when the choice concerns family structure, religious schooling, sexual norms, diet, work ethic, or patriotic education. Once the state is charged with equalizing flourishing, little in principle limits its reach.

The example of compulsory arts and music education is revealing. I support arts education. But not because the state should engineer equal flourishing. It should provide a basic education that transmits literacy, numeracy, history, science, civic knowledge, and some exposure to culture. Families and communities should do much of the rest. Some children will become musicians; others will prefer mechanics, athletics, worship, entrepreneurship, hunting, coding, gardening, or caring for siblings. A society that treats low participation in officially valued pursuits as a problem for egalitarian correction risks confusing refinement with justice.

Sypnowich also places great weight on solidarity. There is much to admire here. A decent society cannot be a mere collection of self-seeking individuals. Families, congregations, unions, clubs, neighborhoods, charities, and local associations are indispensable schools of mutual aid. But solidarity generated by political redistribution is thin compared with solidarity practiced in concrete communities. When care is routed primarily through the state, citizens can become taxpayers and clients rather than neighbors. The challenge is to strengthen mediating institutions, not to imagine that national policy can manufacture fellow feeling.

This point is especially important for non-material goods. Sypnowich observes that loneliness, alienation, and lack of love can diminish flourishing. True. But these are precisely the goods least amenable to political equalization. Government can support public safety, parks, community centers, and family-friendly labor policy. It can avoid policies that uproot communities unnecessarily. But it cannot distribute friendship. It cannot guarantee love. It cannot make people use their freedom well. A politics that acknowledges these limits is not callous; it is realistic about the human condition.

Where, then, should public policy go? First, formal equal opportunity remains essential: anti-discrimination law, equal civil rights, fair access to public institutions, and the rule of law. No one should be excluded from work, education, housing, or political participation because of race, sex, religion, disability, or other irrelevant status. Second, children deserve special concern. They do not choose their parents, neighborhoods, or schools. We should invest in early childhood health, safe streets, effective schools, vocational pathways, and support for families. Equal opportunity for children is much more demanding than a slogan.

Third, society should maintain a safety net that prevents destitution and offers second chances. This includes health care access, disability support, unemployment insurance, addiction treatment, mental health care, and basic income support tied where appropriate to work, training, or caregiving. The point should be restoration and independence, not permanent tutelage. Fourth, we should reduce barriers to work and family formation: housing supply, occupational licensing reform, wage subsidies for low-income workers, apprenticeships, and policies that make it easier to raise children. These are opportunity policies, but they are not trivial.

Fifth, we should remember that equality is not the only social good. Prosperity, liberty, excellence, community, tradition, innovation, and responsibility also matter. A society obsessed with equalizing flourishing may sacrifice dynamism and personal freedom. Inequalities are not automatically injustices. A doctor may earn more than a clerk; an entrepreneur may become wealthy by creating a valued product; an artist may accept poverty for independence; a parent may leave paid work for family life. Some inequalities reflect exploitation or unfair advantage and should be corrected. Others reflect diversity in human aims and contributions.

Sypnowich worries that meritocracy legitimates hierarchy. It can. A smug meritocracy that treats market winners as morally superior is repellent. But the answer is not to deny merit. Competence matters. We want bridges designed by skilled engineers, surgeries performed by trained physicians, classrooms led by capable teachers, and public offices staffed by people with judgment. Fair selection for roles is a moral achievement, especially when compared with nepotism, corruption, caste, or ideological loyalty. What we need is a humble meritocracy: one that rewards skill where relevant but refuses to treat worldly success as a measure of human worth.

The best society will not be one in which everyone flourishes equally. It will be one in which people are free and equipped to pursue the good as they understand it, protected from severe deprivation, bound by duties to family and neighbor, and governed by institutions that are fair but limited. Politics can provide guardrails and foundations; it cannot complete the human soul.

Equal opportunity is not enough if it means merely opening a door while ignoring whether children arrive hungry, frightened, or illiterate. But opportunity, broadly understood, remains the proper public ideal because it respects both justice and limits. The state should secure fair chances, basic needs, and civil equality. It should not assume responsibility for equal flourishing. A humane society must care about outcomes, but it must also know which outcomes lie beyond the rightful reach of politics.
