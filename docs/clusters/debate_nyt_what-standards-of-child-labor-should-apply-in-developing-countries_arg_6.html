Diversified Claude

"Western corporations should be required to apply universal child labor standards to their overseas supply chains, with the financial burden of compliance falling on the corporations rather than the families or suppliers."

Yes — Western child labor standards, or something very close to them, should apply to the overseas operations of Western corporations. The argument that poor families need their children's wages is real, but it has been used for two centuries to defend practices we now universally condemn, and it cannot bear the moral weight placed on it.

Start with what is actually being defended. When Apple, H&M, Nestlé, or Walmart contract production in Asia or West Africa, they are not subsistence farmers making impossible choices. They are among the most profitable enterprises in human history, sourcing from suppliers whose margins are squeezed precisely because the buyers demand it. The "child labor keeps families afloat" argument conflates two very different situations: a rural household sending a twelve-year-old to help in the family field, and a global supply chain in which a multinational captures most of the value while a child sews its shirts for pennies. The first may be unavoidable in the short run; the second is a choice made in boardrooms in Bentonville and Cupertino.

The economic argument against bans is also weaker than its proponents claim. The classic counterexample — Bangladeshi girls allegedly pushed into prostitution after the 1993 Harkin Bill scare — has been repeated so often it functions as folklore, but the underlying study covered a small sample and the worst outcomes occurred precisely because firms dumped workers without transition support. The lesson is not "don't enforce standards"; it is "enforce them with remediation": pay the family the lost wage, enroll the child in school, audit the next tier of suppliers. Several apparel brands have piloted exactly this and the costs are trivial relative to corporate revenues.

There is also a hard moral floor that cultural relativism cannot cross. A nine-year-old breathing tannery chemicals in Hazaribagh, or hauling cocoa sacks in Côte d'Ivoire under threat of violence, is not exercising a culturally specific livelihood strategy. She is being harmed in ways that will shorten her life and foreclose her future. Calling this "their standards, not ours" is a sophisticated form of looking away.

The honest position is that universal minimum standards — no work under fourteen, no hazardous work under eighteen, schooling guaranteed — should bind any company selling into Western markets, with the costs of compliance borne primarily by the buyers, not the suppliers. Anything less outsources our ethics along with our manufacturing.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic necessity of child labor is often a result of global corporations squeezing supplier margins rather than an unavoidable reality of subsistence living.
  2. 02The negative consequences of child labor bans can be mitigated through remediation strategies, such as replacing lost wages and funding education, which are affordable for major multinationals.
  3. 03Cultural relativism cannot justify the physical harm and loss of future opportunities inflicted on children working in hazardous conditions.
  4. 04Universal standards regarding minimum age and hazardous work should be a mandatory condition for any company selling products in Western markets.
