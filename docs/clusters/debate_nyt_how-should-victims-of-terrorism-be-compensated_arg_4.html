"The U.S. government should provide immediate interim compensation to victims of mass violence while preserving their right to pursue civil litigation against responsible parties to ensure accountability and transparency."

Compensation for terrorism should begin with responsibility, not sympathy. The first source should be the perpetrators and those who materially enabled them. The second should be negligent institutions. Only after that should government and charity fill the gaps.

It is tempting after a mass attack to move immediately to collective compassion. But if compensation is detached from fault, it can obscure the hard questions: Were warnings missed? Did a company ignore security obligations? Did an organization provide funds, training or weapons? Were existing laws enforced? Civil litigation is not merely a way to transfer money. It is a discovery machine. It can put documents under oath, compel testimony and expose failures that official reports sometimes soften.

That is why broad compensation funds can be troubling when they require victims to waive lawsuits. The Sept. 11 fund made sense in part because Congress feared that litigation could destroy the airlines and deepen a national economic emergency. But that bargain should not become routine. If a future bombing occurs after gross security failures, or if a mass shooting is aided by illegal sales or reckless conduct, victims should not be nudged into surrendering claims for the sake of administrative tidiness.

Of course, tort law alone cannot do the job. Many terrorists die in their attacks. Many criminals have no assets. Even meritorious lawsuits take years. That is why an interim public fund is reasonable, but it should be structured as an advance, not a replacement for accountability. Government can pay immediate medical, funeral and income-support costs and then seek reimbursement where liable parties are later identified. Victims should retain the right to sue unless they knowingly accept a separate settlement.

Private donations also have a role, especially for needs that courts and statutes do not measure well: travel for relatives, home modifications, counseling, community healing. But charitable funds should not be used by officials as an excuse to underinvest in victim services or by defendants as a shield against liability.

The public conversation often treats lawsuits as greedy and compensation funds as noble. That is too simple. Litigation can be painful and imperfect, but it is one of the few tools ordinary citizens have to demand answers from powerful institutions. A just system should offer immediate help without closing the courthouse door.

Victims deserve compassion. They also deserve the truth, and sometimes the truth only comes with a subpoena.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Civil litigation serves as a vital discovery tool that exposes institutional failures and security lapses that official reports might overlook.
  2. 02Broad compensation funds should not require victims to waive their right to sue, as doing so can shield negligent entities from liability for the sake of administrative convenience.
  3. 03Government assistance should be structured as an advance for immediate needs, with the state seeking reimbursement from liable parties once they are identified.
  4. 04Private donations should supplement legal and government compensation by addressing specific needs like community healing and counseling that courts do not typically measure.
  5. 05Prioritizing fault-based compensation ensures that the financial burden falls first on perpetrators and negligent institutions rather than solely on taxpayers or donors.

"The U.S. government should provide immediate interim assistance to victims of terrorism without requiring them to waive their right to pursue civil litigation against responsible parties."

Compensation after terrorism should not be allowed to bury accountability. That is the danger in turning every national tragedy into a special fund administered by a trusted public figure. Checks may arrive faster, but questions can disappear.

The Sept. 11 fund helped many families and avoided years of litigation. It also required victims who accepted payment to give up lawsuits against the airlines and other potential defendants. In the emergency of 2001, that bargain was politically understandable. As a general rule, it is troubling. When public money replaces litigation, institutions that may have failed get peace without discovery, testimony or reform.

Civil justice is not merely a lottery for plaintiffs’ lawyers. Lawsuits can uncover ignored warnings, inadequate security, defective procedures, negligent entrustment and official complacency. They create records. They force powerful institutions to answer questions under oath. They can deter future misconduct in a way that a charitable distribution cannot.

Of course, lawsuits alone are not enough. Many attackers are dead, imprisoned or penniless. Some victims have no realistic defendant to sue. Litigation can take years, and bills arrive immediately. For that reason, government should provide interim assistance: funeral expenses, emergency cash, medical and psychological care, wage replacement and help for dependents. But this should be assistance, not hush money. No victim should have to waive the right to sue in order to obtain basic help.

If later litigation identifies responsible parties, the government can recover some of its payments. If terrorist assets, criminal restitution or insurance proceeds are available, they should be used. If a foreign state sponsored an attack and reachable assets exist, victims should have access to them. The principle is simple: wrongdoers and negligent parties should pay before taxpayers do.

Private donations also play a role, but charity should not be mistaken for justice. A donation may ease hardship; it does not explain why an attack succeeded or who ignored risks.

A compensation system should be built around two promises. First, victims will not be abandoned while courts do their slow work. Second, public sympathy will not be used to purchase immunity for the powerful. Money matters, but truth matters too.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Relying solely on public compensation funds can bury institutional accountability by preventing the discovery and testimony that occur during lawsuits.
  2. 02Civil litigation serves a vital public interest by uncovering negligence, creating official records of failure, and deterring future misconduct.
  3. 03Government assistance should function as a temporary safety net for immediate needs rather than a final settlement that grants immunity to powerful institutions.
  4. 04Financial responsibility for compensation should prioritize wrongdoers, negligent parties, and seized terrorist assets over taxpayer funding.
  5. 05Private charity is an insufficient substitute for justice because it does not investigate why an attack succeeded or who was at fault.
