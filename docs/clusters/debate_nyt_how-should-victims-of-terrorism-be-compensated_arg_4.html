"The U.S. should adopt a mandatory insurance model funded by industries tied to mass violence risks to provide guaranteed, immediate compensation for victims."

Rather than rely on fickle donations or taxpayer bailouts, we should fund victim compensation through a mandatory insurance model that places the burden on the industries and institutions most directly tied to the risk of mass violence. Just as workers’ compensation spreads the cost of workplace injuries across employers, a “mass casualty security surcharge” could be levied on airlines, concert venues, stadiums, firearms manufacturers, and private security firms. These entities profit by gathering crowds or equipping individuals, and they are best positioned to invest in safety. A pre‑funded insurance pool would ensure that money is available immediately after an attack, without waiting for Congress to act or donors to open their wallets.

This approach has several advantages. First, it internalizes the cost of prevention. Airlines that skimp on screening and gun companies that enable straw purchases would pay higher premiums, just as reckless drivers pay more for auto insurance. That creates a market incentive to improve safety rather than externalize the cost onto society. Second, it depoliticizes compensation. The fund’s rules—who qualifies, what benefits are paid—would be set in advance by regulators and actuaries, not by public grief or political maneuvering after each tragedy. Third, it retains a connection between the harm and those who, even if not legally at fault, are part of the ecosystem of risk. When a bomb goes off at a marathon, the race organizer’s insurer would be on the hook, not some anonymous taxpayer in Idaho.

Private donations could still flow to victims for additional support, but the baseline of medical care, lost wages, and funeral expenses would be guaranteed by the insurance system. Lawsuits would remain available for cases of gross negligence, ensuring that the availability of compensation does not become a shield for wrongdoing. This model rejects both the callousness of “let them sue” and the irresponsibility of “let the government pay.” It is a pragmatic, market‑oriented solution that aligns incentives while honoring our collective obligation to those caught in the crossfire of our open, crowded, and sometimes violent society.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A pre-funded insurance pool ensures that financial aid is available immediately without relying on the delays of congressional action or the inconsistency of private donations.
  2. 02The model creates market incentives for safety by requiring higher premiums from entities that fail to implement rigorous security or prevention measures.
  3. 03Predetermined rules set by regulators and actuaries would depoliticize the compensation process, ensuring fairness regardless of public sentiment or political climate.
  4. 04The insurance model shifts the financial burden from the general taxpayer to the specific industries and institutions that profit from the ecosystem of risk.
  5. 05This system provides a guaranteed baseline of support while still allowing for private donations and legal action in cases of gross negligence.
