**What Gilens Misses About Majoritarianism**

Martin Gilens's research is valuable, and his evidence that policy tracks affluent preferences more closely than poor preferences is hard to dismiss. But Gilens frames his findings within a particular theory of democracy—a roughly majoritarian one in which the legitimacy of policy turns on its correspondence to citizen preferences—and that framing obscures as much as it reveals. Before we lament representational inequality, we should ask whether equal representation of preferences is actually what democratic theory recommends or what American institutions are designed to produce.

Start with what Gilens's own data show but he passes over quickly. Many of the policy reversals that occurred during the period he studies were not popular at the time but are now broadly accepted: civil rights legislation, immigration reform, the loosening of restrictions on stem cell research, expanded protections against discrimination. Gilens notes that the 1965 immigration reform was opposed by 90 percent of poor Americans and three-quarters of middle-class and affluent ones. By his criterion, this was a profound failure of democratic responsiveness. By almost any other criterion, it was one of the great achievements of postwar American governance. The same is true of a great deal of civil rights policy, which moved ahead of public opinion and helped pull opinion forward.

This is the classic Madisonian observation: pure responsiveness to popular preference is not a virtue but a danger. The American constitutional order was deliberately designed to filter, slow, and sometimes frustrate majority will, particularly when that will threatens minority rights or reflects passion rather than considered judgment. Gilens nods to this concern in a paragraph near the end ("minority rights are important too, and majorities are sometimes shortsighted or misguided") but does not let it disturb his framework. It should disturb it more.

There is also a problem with treating survey responses as the gold standard of "what the public wants." Survey questions present policy proposals stripped of context, tradeoffs, and budgetary implications. Respondents often hold contradictory preferences—they want lower taxes and more spending, more services and smaller government. Their views shift dramatically based on question wording and the salience of different considerations. When Gilens finds that policy fails to match poll responses, the failure may sometimes lie not with policymakers but with the polls' ability to capture preferences that could plausibly guide actual governance.

Consider Gilens's example of trade policy. He treats the move toward freer trade as evidence of policymakers ignoring the poor and middle class in favor of the affluent. But the public's apparent protectionism in polls coexists with persistent consumer behavior that has rewarded cheaper imported goods, and with the broad bipartisan judgment among economists, foreign policy professionals, and labor economists alike that the overall welfare effects of liberalized trade are positive even when distributional effects are uneven. A policymaker who decides that aggregate welfare considerations and expert judgment should outweigh diffuse poll opposition is not necessarily failing democracy; she may be exercising the kind of considered judgment that representative (as opposed to direct) democracy is designed to enable.

Now, none of this is a blanket defense of how American politics actually works. Gilens is right that the influence of large donors raises serious concerns, that voter turnout disparities are troubling, and that organized economic power can distort policy in ways that go well beyond legitimate expert judgment. The growing concentration of wealth at the top is a real political-economic problem, and the explosion of money in elections post–Citizens United deserves the worry Gilens directs at it.

But the diagnostic frame matters. If we believe the problem is that policy fails to match poll majorities, we will be drawn toward more populist, plebiscitary reforms—national referenda, term limits, weakening of "elite" institutions. If we believe the problem is more specifically the disproportionate role of concentrated wealth in shaping elite deliberation, we will be drawn toward campaign finance reform, anti-corruption measures, and strengthening countervailing institutions like unions. These are different reform agendas, and Gilens's framing pushes us toward the first when the second is probably what his evidence actually supports.

American democracy has serious problems. But the problem is not that the public doesn't get its way often enough. It is that some channels of influence have grown too powerful relative to others, distorting the deliberation through which representative government is supposed to operate. That is a narrower diagnosis than Gilens offers, but a more accurate one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Many landmark policy achievements, such as civil rights and immigration reform, were historically unpopular and would have failed under a strictly majoritarian system.
  2. 02Public opinion surveys are unreliable measures of democratic will because they fail to account for the complex trade-offs and budgetary realities inherent in actual governance.
  3. 03Representative democracy is intended to allow policymakers to exercise considered judgment and prioritize aggregate welfare over diffuse or contradictory public preferences.
  4. 04Framing the problem as a lack of majoritarian responsiveness incorrectly encourages populist reforms rather than targeted measures to address the specific distorting influence of concentrated wealth.
