"The responding essay argues that the left should reclaim freedom by disentangling the liberal tradition of individual autonomy from reactionary ideology, rather than rejecting individualism as a byproduct of settler colonialism and empire."

Aziz Rana’s essay is an ambitious attempt to explain the American right’s stranglehold on the language of freedom and to offer a path forward for the left. His historical linkage of settler colonialism and empire to the dominant, individualistic conception of liberty is compelling, and his call for institution-building over mere messaging is a necessary pushback against shallow political strategy. Yet there is a recurrent elision in his argument that weakens its force: he repeatedly conflates the liberal, market-oriented vision of freedom with the overtly racist and xenophobic strains he rightly condemns, and in doing so, he paints with too broad a brush. The result is a framework that, while insightful about the roots of America’s worst pathologies, risks alienating the very people—ordinary Americans who value both personal independence and a fair society—whose support is essential for any durable left-of-center majority. A more productive approach would disentangle the liberal freedom tradition from its reactionary capture and argue for a reformed social contract that keeps the best of individualism while firmly embedding it in a framework of shared responsibility.

Rana traces the zero-sum account of freedom to the settler experience, where land and independence were won through expropriation and where government intervention was seen as a threat to white self-rule. No one can deny the deep historical truth of this narrative. However, he then draws a direct line from that history to the “market fundamentalism” and “bellicosity” of the contemporary right, and seems to imply that any association of freedom with individual initiative, limited government, and market mechanisms is tainted by this lineage. This is where the argument overreaches. There is a robust liberal tradition—represented by thinkers from John Stuart Mill to John Rawls to Amartya Sen—that values individual liberty not as a zero-sum struggle but as a universal good, grounding it in a concern for human dignity and a recognition that people need both autonomy and a secure social floor to genuinely direct their own lives. This tradition has been a powerful force in American history, from the anti-Federalist concerns with centralized power to the Progressive Era and the New Deal, which married individual security with a recognition of collective bargaining. Franklin Roosevelt’s “Four Freedoms,” including freedom from want and freedom from fear, explicitly framed freedom as something the state must help guarantee, not merely refrain from abridging. That vision is not a historically anomalous blip; it is an American tradition, however imperfectly realized.

Rana might respond that this liberal tradition, too, has been compromised by empire and has served as a cover for global dominance. That is a fair critique, but it does not justify jettisoning the liberal vocabulary of individual rights and personal agency. On the contrary, the most effective responses to the right’s nativist freedom have often come from liberals who argued that real freedom requires active government: freedom from medical bankruptcy through universal healthcare, freedom to start a business without being chained to a job for health insurance, freedom to send children to good schools regardless of zip code. That is a language that can resonate with a broad swath of Americans who feel that the current system denies them the independence and self-mastery they crave—not because they are duped by settler ideology, but because they reasonably understand that being at the mercy of an employer with no alternatives is not freedom.

Rana’s prescription, centered on workplace democracy and a dramatic rollback of the American global security state, is admirable in its radicalism. But it also seems disconnected from the constituencies it needs to persuade. He acknowledges that for many Americans, solidaristic freedom can “seem theoretical and abstract,” and he is right. The solution, however, is not to double down on long-term institutional transformation that may feel remote from kitchen-table concerns. It is to reconnect freedom to tangible, immediate improvements. The fight for a higher minimum wage, paid family leave, affordable childcare, and stronger collective bargaining rights can all be framed not as a repudiation of individualism but as its precondition: no one is truly free if they can’t afford to leave a bad job, care for a sick child, or retire with dignity. This is the language that proved effective for Bernie Sanders, whose 2016 and 2020 campaigns rallied millions around a notion of economic freedom that was strongly individual in its focus—the freedom of every person to have healthcare, education, and a decent living—yet grounded in solidaristic social provision.

Rana’s anti-imperial stance, while morally consistent, may further limit the appeal of his vision. He is correct that U.S. foreign policy has often undermined self-rule abroad and wasted resources at home. But the average American does not experience the world in these terms, and framing the domestic freedom project as dependent on a fundamental rethinking of America’s global role risks entangling necessary domestic reforms in a highly divisive foreign policy debate. It is possible to argue for divesting from endless wars and corporate militarism without tying that argument to a sweeping critique of American empire and an embrace of presumptive immigration admission that leaves the boundaries of political community undefined. In an era of intense polarization, the left needs a narrative of freedom that builds majorities, not one that insists on a comprehensive ideological conversion.

This is not an argument for timidity or for abandoning the critique of imperial overreach. It is an argument for strategic prioritization and for taking seriously the genuine appeal of a certain kind of market-oriented freedom that, whatever its historical origins, is not experienced by most people as a form of domination. The task is to show that market fundamentalism actually destroys the very independence it promises—while the right’s brand of freedom has become a hollow evocation of power over others, as Rana himself notes. But the alternative must be presented not as a communalistic rejection of self-reliance, but as a richer, fuller independence: one that recognizes that in a complex society, the conditions of our own freedom are always bound up with the freedom of others. This is a harder argument to make than simply castigating liberal individualism as a product of empire, but it is one that stands a better chance of winning. Reclaiming freedom will require building new institutions, as Rana insists. But it will also require telling a story about freedom that makes room for the deeply held desire to be the author of one’s own life, while showing that such authorship is impossible in a society of radical inequality and insecurity.
