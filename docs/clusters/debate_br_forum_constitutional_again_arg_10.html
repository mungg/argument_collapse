Diversified Gemini

"The progressive strategy of reclaiming the "democracy-of-opportunity" constitutional tradition is a strategic miscalculation that prioritizes legalistic interpretation over the necessary building of working-class power."

In “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again,” Fishkin and his co-authors present a sweeping, erudite argument for a revival of progressive constitutionalism. They urge the left to abandon its defensive crouch and aggressively articulate a “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, insisting that the Constitution requires the dismantling of oligarchy and the broad distribution of wealth and power. This is an intellectually thrilling project, but from the perspective of the working-class left, it represents a profound strategic miscalculation. By urging social movements to channel their energy into constitutional interpretation, the authors ask us to rely on the master’s tools to dismantle the master’s house.

The fundamental flaw in this progressive constitutionalist project is its idealization of the U.S. Constitution itself. The authors treat the Constitution as a neutral, or even latently progressive, charter that has simply been hijacked by right-wing jurists. But historical materialism tells a different story. The Constitution was drafted in 1787 by a coalition of northern merchants and southern enslavers specifically to curb the democratic, redistributive impulses that had erupted in the states following the Revolutionary War. Rebellions by indebted farmers, like Shays’ Rebellion, terrified the elite. The Constitution they produced—with its checks and balances, its staggered elections, its unelected judiciary, and its explicit protections for property and slavery—was designed to insulate elite wealth from popular majorities.

To argue today that this same document *compels* the government to break up monopolies, empower labor unions, and distribute wealth is to engage in a profound historical fiction. It requires ignoring the plain text, the original intent of the framers, and the overwhelming majority of the document’s historical application. When conservatives point out that the Constitution protects private property and limits federal intervention, they are not merely inventing a jurisprudence out of whole cloth; they are reading the document largely as it was intended to be read by its aristocratic authors.

The authors argue that we must reclaim the First Amendment to protect labor organizing, pointing out that in the early twentieth century, some lawmakers argued that the First Amendment protected picketing and strikes. But this legalistic framing entirely misunderstands how labor actually won its rights. The National Labor Relations Act of 1935 was not passed because progressive lawyers finally formulated the correct constitutional argument. It was passed because the American working class made the country ungovernable. Millions of workers engaged in sit-down strikes, wildcat strikes, and violent confrontations with state police and Pinkerton guards. The Wagner Act was a concession granted by a terrified capitalist class to prevent a full-blown revolution.

When the labor movement later became institutionalized and reliant on lawyers to litigate its rights in court, it lost its militancy. The Taft-Hartley Act of 1947, which the authors rightly identify as a devastating blow to labor, was the beginning of a decades-long process of dragging labor struggles out of the streets and into the courtrooms. Asking the modern labor movement to focus on constitutional arguments about the First Amendment merely repeats this error. Workers do not need a constitutional theory of the First Amendment to recognize that they are being exploited; they need the organizational capacity to stop production. If a union decides to engage in an illegal secondary boycott to support fellow workers, their defense should not be a complex constitutional theory of countervailing power; their defense should be that their solidarity is stronger than the state's injunctions.

Furthermore, the authors’ call to “reforge the link between racial justice and political economy” through a constitutional lens glosses over the deeply exclusionary nature of the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition they champion. The Jacksonian era, which the authors cite as an origin point for this tradition, was defined by an anti-monopoly populism that was explicitly rooted in white supremacy. The Jacksonian expansion of opportunity for white working-class men was directly predicated on the genocide and removal of Indigenous peoples and the expansion of chattel slavery. Similarly, the New Deal’s political economy was built on a series of racial compromises that excluded domestic and agricultural workers—predominantly Black—from labor and social security protections.

A genuine movement for multiracial working-class power cannot build upon a tradition so deeply compromised by racial capitalism. We need a political vision that is explicitly internationalist, anti-racist, and anti-capitalist, not one that attempts to shoehorn these radical demands into the cramped, nationalist confines of American constitutionalism. When the Movement for Black Lives demands reparations or the defunding of the police, they are not making a claim about the hidden meaning of the Constitution; they are challenging the legitimacy of the historical constitutional order itself.

The authors worry that abandoning constitutional arguments “cedes power to courts.” But this assumes that courts only listen to legal arguments. In reality, courts are deeply sensitive to power. The Supreme Court did not uphold the New Deal in the famous “switch in time that saved nine” because they were persuaded by a new theory of the Commerce Clause; they did so because FDR had just won a massive landslide election, mass movements were demanding radical change, and the President was actively threatening to pack the Court. Power yields to power, not to better syllogisms.

If progressives spend their time crafting elegant constitutional arguments for a wealth tax or universal healthcare, they are misallocating precious resources. The right wing understands this. While the Federalist Society certainly cultivated constitutional theories, those theories were backed by billions of dollars in dark money, ruthless partisan hardball in the Senate, and a mobilized, reactionary base. The conservative legal movement is merely the ideological vanguard of a reactionary class project.

The left must build a countervailing class project. This means revitalizing the labor movement through aggressive organizing, building independent working-class political institutions, and utilizing strikes, protests, and direct action. When the working class has sufficient power, the legal and constitutional justifications for their demands will inevitably follow. The laws, including the Constitution, are ultimately just codifications of the balance of class forces at any given moment.

If Congress passes a wealth tax, and the reactionary Supreme Court strikes it down, the left’s response should not be to file a better amicus brief citing Brandeis and the democracy-of-opportunity tradition. The response should be to delegitimize the Court, ignore its ruling, or restructure it entirely. We must teach people that the Constitution is not a sacred covenant that dictates our economic destiny, but a historical artifact that must be overcome when it stands in the way of human flourishing.

Fishkin and his colleagues are right that the left has abandoned the field of political economy for too long, relying on technocrats to manage capitalism’s contradictions. But the solution to technocratic liberalism is not constitutional liberalism; it is democratic socialism. We must speak to the material needs of the people, not to the ghosts of the founding fathers. The future belongs to those who build power, not those who can best interpret an archaic text. Let us leave the Constitution to the historians, and let us build a movement capable of winning the world we need.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. Constitution is not a neutral or progressive document, but was intentionally designed by elites to protect private property and insulate wealth from democratic majorities.
  2. 02Labor rights and social reforms are historically won through militant mass action and making the country ungovernable, not through the formulation of superior legal theories.
  3. 03The 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is fundamentally compromised by its historical roots in white supremacy, indigenous genocide, and racial exclusions.
  4. 04Courts respond to shifts in the balance of class power and political threats rather than the intellectual merit of constitutional syllogisms.
  5. 05Focusing on constitutional interpretation misallocates resources that should be used to build independent working-class institutions and the capacity for direct action.
  6. 06When the Constitution obstructs necessary social progress, the left should seek to delegitimize or overcome the document rather than attempting to re-interpret it.
