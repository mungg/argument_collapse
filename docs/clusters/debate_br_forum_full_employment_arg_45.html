"While national macroeconomic shifts toward education and clean energy are necessary for full employment, they must be complemented by a regional strategy that rebuilds local civic machinery and financial institutions."

Robert Pollin’s essay is framed at the scale of national policy: federal stimulus, military spending, fossil-fuel investment, clean energy, education, financial regulation, and macroeconomic management. That scale is necessary. A recession this severe cannot be solved by neighborhood initiative alone. Yet the full-employment debate also needs a local and regional dimension. Jobs are not experienced as national aggregates. They exist in places, and the geography of unemployment matters.

A national unemployment rate above 9 percent conceals enormous variation. Some metropolitan areas with universities, hospitals, government employment, and diversified industries are struggling but intact. Others—especially manufacturing towns, rural areas, and communities tied to construction—face depression-like conditions. Even within prosperous regions, unemployment is concentrated by race, age, education, and neighborhood. A policy that creates millions of jobs in the aggregate may still leave particular places behind. Full employment must therefore mean more than a national target; it must mean that every region has a plausible economic future.

Pollin’s proposed shift toward education and clean energy is promising precisely because much of the work can be locally rooted. Schools are everywhere. Buildings needing energy retrofits are everywhere. Public transit, water systems, parks, and community colleges are local assets. But converting national spending into local employment requires institutions that can plan, coordinate, and deliver. The United States has allowed many of those institutions to decay. Local governments are laying off workers. Regional planning agencies are weak. Community development finance is undercapitalized. Workforce boards vary widely in quality. Public procurement often favors large incumbents rather than local firms. If we want full employment, we need to rebuild the civic machinery of development.

Small businesses deserve more attention in this agenda. They are often invoked sentimentally by politicians who then do little for them. In the current recession, many small firms face weak demand, tight credit, falling property values, and customers who are themselves anxious. A clean-energy or education program that flows only through large contractors, national chains, or distant manufacturers will miss an opportunity. Retrofitting homes and schools could support local construction firms. Local food procurement could support regional agriculture. Community health and elder-care services could employ neighborhood residents. But this requires procurement rules that break projects into accessible sizes, technical assistance for small firms, prompt payment, and credit channels that reach beyond the largest banks.

Pollin calls for financial regulation that channels credit toward productive investment. At the local level, this means strengthening community banks, credit unions, community development financial institutions, and public or quasi-public lending programs. The financial crisis revealed the danger of a system in which credit decisions are increasingly detached from local knowledge. Mortgage brokers originated loans they would never hold; securities were sliced and sold; communities were left with foreclosures. A full-employment economy needs finance that knows the borrower, the business district, the farm, the contractor, and the local market. Not all local lending is wise, and not all large-bank lending is predatory. But relationship finance has a role that securitized finance cannot replace.

Workforce development must also become more practical. Too often retraining is offered as a political consolation prize after jobs disappear. Workers are told to acquire new skills without assurance that jobs will exist at the end. A better model links training to actual projects and employers. If a region is weatherizing public buildings, community colleges and unions can train workers for that work. If hospitals need technicians, training programs should be designed with hospitals. If public transit is expanding, apprenticeships should prepare mechanics, drivers, and maintenance workers. Training without demand is frustrating; demand without training produces bottlenecks. Full employment requires both.

There is an important democratic point here. National programs can feel remote, especially when people distrust government. Local projects are visible. A repaired school roof, a reopened library branch, a restored park, a functioning bus line, a crew insulating homes on a winter morning—these make public action tangible. They also create accountability. Citizens can see whether money is being spent well. Workers can point to what they built. That matters for sustaining political support.

Pollin is right that full employment will challenge entrenched power. But the coalition for it cannot be built solely in Washington. Mayors, county officials, school boards, unions, churches, community colleges, neighborhood organizations, small manufacturers, environmental groups, and local bankers all have pieces of the answer. In some places they already collaborate; in others they barely speak. Federal policy should reward regional partnerships that produce credible job plans, especially in high-unemployment areas.

This does not mean substituting localism for macroeconomics. Localities cannot deficit-spend their way out of recession. Balanced-budget rules force states and cities to cut precisely when needs rise. Federal aid is essential. Nor can every town reinvent itself through heroic planning. Some places face structural decline that requires migration as well as redevelopment. People should be supported if they move to opportunity. But mobility is not a complete answer. Homes are underwater; families are rooted; communities have value. A humane economy does not casually tell whole towns to vanish.

Full employment also requires attention to job quality at the local level. Desperate communities often subsidize any employer willing to arrive, regardless of wages or stability. This can lead to a race to the bottom among cities and states. Federal standards can help: prevailing wages on publicly funded projects, local hiring goals consistent with fairness and efficiency, apprenticeship requirements, and clawbacks when subsidized firms fail to deliver promised jobs. Public money should not finance poverty-wage employment or corporate blackmail.

Pollin’s clean-energy emphasis could become the basis for a new kind of regional development. The industrial Midwest could produce transit equipment, wind components, efficient appliances, and advanced materials. The Great Plains could generate wind power. The Sun Belt could expand solar installation and manufacturing. Older cities could become laboratories for building retrofits and transit-oriented development. Rural areas could benefit from conservation, sustainable agriculture, and biomass where appropriate. But none of this will happen automatically. Markets alone will not coordinate the transition at the speed required; federal programs alone will not know enough about local assets. The missing layer is regional strategy.

The full-employment question, then, is not only “How many jobs per million dollars?” It is “Where? For whom? Under whose control? With what lasting institutions?” If public investment creates temporary jobs but leaves no local capacity behind, it will disappoint. If it strengthens schools, colleges, firms, unions, infrastructure, and civic networks, it can alter a region’s trajectory.

Pollin has given us a necessary national vision. To make it real, we should bring it down to earth: to the school district deciding whether to lay off teachers, the contractor who could hire three more workers if retrofit financing existed, the community college designing a weatherization course, the credit union considering a loan to a machine shop, the bus route that connects a low-income neighborhood to jobs. Full employment is a macroeconomic goal, but it is lived block by block.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01National unemployment aggregates conceal geographic and demographic disparities that require place-based economic planning to ensure no region is left behind.
  2. 02The transition to a full-employment economy requires rebuilding the 'civic machinery' of development, including local planning agencies, procurement rules that favor small businesses, and community development finance.
  3. 03Financial regulation must prioritize 'relationship finance' through community banks and credit unions to ensure credit reaches local contractors, farms, and small businesses.
  4. 04Workforce development should be integrated with specific local infrastructure projects and employer needs rather than offered as a generic or reactive consolation prize.
  5. 05Localizing job creation makes public action tangible and visible, which is essential for building and sustaining the democratic accountability and political support needed for a national full-employment agenda.
  6. 06Federal policy should include job-quality standards and regional partnerships to prevent a 'race to the bottom' where desperate localities subsidize low-wage or unstable employment.
