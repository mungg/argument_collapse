"Peter Singer’s utilitarian definition of 'the most good' is flawed because it prioritizes biological survival at the expense of the arts and culture, which are essential for a meaningful human existence and the development of moral empathy."

Peter Singer’s “The Logic of Effective Altruism” asks us to radically rethink our charitable obligations. By insisting that we must evaluate our philanthropy based on where it will do the "most good," Singer challenges the deeply entrenched habit of giving based on proximity, loyalty, or emotional resonance. While his critique of "warm glow" giving is incisive, Singer’s narrow definition of "the most good" creates an unnecessary tension between global moral imperatives and the cultivation of a rich, complex human civilization.

To illustrate his point, Singer targets the arts, explicitly stating that donating to opera houses and museums—such as David Geffen’s $100 million gift to Lincoln Center—is not doing the most good. He argues that in a world with extreme poverty, funding the arts is a secondary concern, a luxury we can only afford once existential suffering has been eradicated. This utilitarian calculus is deeply flawed because it misunderstands the role that art, culture, and beauty play in sustaining the very moral imagination that makes altruism possible.

Singer treats human survival as the ultimate and only metric of value. A life saved from malaria is a data point of unquestionable good. And indeed, it is. But what are we saving humans *for*? A society that directs all of its surplus resources solely toward biological survival and physical health, while starving its cultural institutions, would be a spiritually barren one. The arts are not mere entertainment; they are the arenas where we explore meaning, empathy, and our shared humanity.

When we engage with a powerful piece of theater, a transcendent symphony, or a profound work of literature, our capacity for empathy is expanded. The arts force us to confront perspectives outside our own, breaking down the boundaries of the ego. It is highly arguable that the very moral awakenings that lead young people like Matt Wage to care about distant strangers are downstream products of a culture enriched by the humanities and the arts. If we defund our cultural institutions in the name of global utility, we risk eroding the cultural soil from which movements like effective altruism grow.

Furthermore, Singer’s dichotomy ignores the fact that human beings need more than just the absence of suffering; they need beauty, joy, and inspiration. Even in the most impoverished communities on earth, people create music, decorate their homes, and tell stories. The desire for art is not a wealthy indulgence; it is a fundamental human need. To declare that funding civilization's highest artistic achievements is morally sub-optimal is to reduce the human experience to a ledger of caloric intake and disease prevention.

We do not have to choose between saving lives and nourishing the human spirit. David Geffen’s gift to Lincoln Center ensures that generations of people will have access to transformative artistic experiences. While that $100 million could have bought millions of bed nets, the impact of art reverberates across centuries in ways that defy RCTs and cost-benefit analysis. A truly effective altruism must recognize that alleviating physical suffering is a baseline requirement, but cultivating a world filled with beauty, art, and intellectual inquiry is what makes that survival meaningful.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Singer’s focus on physical health and survival as the sole metrics of value ignores the spiritual and intellectual needs that make human life worth living.
  2. 02The arts and humanities are not mere luxuries but are the primary tools for expanding the moral imagination and empathy required for altruism to exist.
  3. 03A society that directs all resources toward survival while starving cultural institutions risks becoming spiritually barren and eroding the cultural foundations of movements like effective altruism.
  4. 04The desire for beauty and artistic expression is a fundamental human need present in all communities, not just a wealthy indulgence.
  5. 05The long-term, transformative impact of cultural institutions on human civilization provides a form of value that cannot be captured by simple cost-benefit analyses or randomized controlled trials.
