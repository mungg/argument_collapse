"While the moral logic of effective altruism is sound, the movement must shift its focus from intimidating ideals of optimization to practical, sustainable habits and social norms that allow ordinary people to participate."

Peter Singer’s essay is morally bracing, but its greatest challenge may be practical rather than philosophical. Many readers will agree with him by the end and change nothing. They will concede that a dollar can do more good preventing malaria than renovating an elite concert hall. They will accept that distant lives matter. They may even admire the young effective altruists who give away large portions of their income. Then they will close the browser, answer email, pay bills, worry about their children, and continue as before.

This is not simple hypocrisy. It is a predictable feature of human moral psychology. Singer knows this; indeed, effective altruism exists partly because our instincts are biased toward the near and visible. But the movement’s rhetoric can make the psychological problem worse. “Do the most good you can” is inspiring for a small group of highly analytical and morally ambitious people. For many others, it sounds like an accusation they cannot possibly satisfy. If the fully ethical life requires maximizing good, then ordinary life begins in failure. Some will respond with heroic commitment. More will respond with avoidance.

Singer tries to soften the demandingness. Effective altruists are not saints, he says. They leave time for family, relaxation, and personal projects. They do not wallow in guilt. Good. But the underlying scale remains intimidating: minimally acceptable ethical life on one end, fully ethical life on the other, with most of us uncomfortably low. The risk is that people who might give 5 or 10 percent decide that, since they are not going to reorganize their lives around maximum impact, the whole thing is not for them.

Movements need ladders, not cliffs. One reason the old tithe has had staying power is that it gives people a concrete standard. Ten percent is demanding but imaginable. It can become a habit rather than a new moral crisis every month. Effective altruism has similar tools—the Giving What We Can pledge, for example—but Singer’s essay emphasizes the open-ended ideal more than the psychology of sustainable commitment. If we want millions rather than thousands to give effectively, we should make the first steps clear, honorable, and socially supported.

A practical ethic of effective giving might begin with three rules. First, give something significant every year, preferably a fixed percentage of income. For affluent households, 10 percent is a reasonable benchmark, with higher percentages for the very rich. Second, make at least half of that giving evidence-based and directed toward high-impact work benefiting those in greatest need. Third, review the evidence periodically and be willing to change recipients. Such rules will not satisfy the pure maximizer, but they could move vastly more money than a movement built around exceptional exemplars.

The same applies to career choice. The story of Matt Wage is dramatic, but it may unintentionally narrow the imagination. Most people will not become arbitrage traders donating six figures. Many should not. Some will do more good as nurses, teachers, public defenders, organizers, researchers, civil servants, engineers, or parents raising morally serious children. Others will stay in ordinary jobs and give steadily. Effective altruism should celebrate a wider range of roles. Otherwise it may look like a movement for elite graduates deciding between Oxford and Wall Street.

Class matters here. Singer writes mainly to those with spare resources, and rightly so. But the language of optimization can travel badly. A young professional burdened by student loans, a caregiver supporting relatives, or a worker in an unstable labor market may hear the same command as a millionaire: do more, give more, justify every comfort. Moral exhortation must distinguish between luxury and ordinary security. A family saving for medical expenses or childcare is not morally equivalent to a donor putting his name on a concert hall.

There is also a danger of private substitution for public responsibility. If effective altruism becomes the conscientious person’s main outlet, it may drain energy from citizenship. Donations to malaria prevention are admirable, but so are votes, taxes, and political advocacy for foreign aid, climate action, public health, refugee protection, and fairer economic rules. A person who gives effectively while ignoring politics may still leave large levers untouched. A practical movement should integrate donation with civic engagement: give well, vote well, organize well, and scrutinize the institutions through which wealth is made.

Singer’s critique of “warm glow” giving is deserved, but warmth can be harnessed. People maintain commitments when they are embedded in communities, stories, rituals, and visible norms. Effective altruism’s spreadsheets need social practices: giving circles, campus groups, workplace pledges, annual reviews, public but non-boastful commitments, and narratives of lives improved. The goal should not be to eliminate emotion but to attach emotion to better objects. Let people feel proud that they funded malaria prevention, cash transfers, or animal welfare reforms. Moral motivation is not dirtier because it feels good.

In the end, I agree with Singer’s central claim: affluent people have strong obligations to use substantial resources to reduce suffering and save lives, and they should care intensely about whether their efforts work. The question is how to turn that claim into durable behavior at scale. Heroic examples are useful, but norms are more useful. A society in which a few people give half their income is admirable. A society in which millions give 10 percent intelligently would be transformative.

Effective altruism should therefore become less like an honors seminar in moral demandingness and more like a public-health campaign for generosity: clear standards, easy defaults, credible information, social reinforcement, and room for people to improve over time. Singer has supplied the moral logic. The next task is building the habits and institutions that allow ordinary people to live by it.
