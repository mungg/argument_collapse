"The United States' failure to manage the COVID-19 pandemic was primarily a failure of political leadership and execution rather than a flaw in the epistemic paradigm of preparedness or a lack of intellectual humility."

It Was Politics, Not Epistemology

Sheila Jasanoff writes from the heights of science and technology studies, and the view from there is bracing. Her essay is one of the most thoughtful diagnoses of pandemic policy I have read. But I think her chosen target — the epistemic culture of "preparedness" — is the wrong one. The United States did not fail to control COVID-19 because its planners were insufficiently humble about uncertainty. It failed because, in January through April of 2020, when control was still possible, the country was led by an administration that actively denied the threat, suppressed information, fired or sidelined experts, and politicized basic protective measures. Replace that political fact with any one of half a dozen alternative histories — a Hillary Clinton administration, a Mitt Romney administration, even a normal-functioning Republican administration of the George W. Bush vintage — and we are talking about a very different pandemic. The frameworks would have been the same. The outcomes would not.

I do not say this to absolve the preparedness paradigm of its real shortcomings. Jasanoff is correct that the Global Health Security Index gave too little weight to political variables, that the Obama playbook assumed a level of inter-branch cooperation that no longer exists, and that "Ten Weeks to Crush the Curve" addressed a country that does not really resemble the one we live in. These are fair criticisms of expert documents that were written, in part, as if Donald Trump did not exist. But the criticism that the documents were apolitical is not the same as the criticism that preparedness as such is the wrong paradigm. The former calls for better preparedness; the latter calls for a different intellectual posture altogether. I think the former is correct and the latter is not.

Consider what actually happened in the first eight weeks of the pandemic. In January 2020, the National Security Council's pandemic preparedness directorate had been dissolved by John Bolton in 2018, removing the very coordination structure the playbook assumed. The president was briefed repeatedly on the threat from intelligence agencies and chose to dismiss it publicly, telling Bob Woodward that he wanted to "play it down." The CDC, the world's most sophisticated public health agency on paper, produced a defective test kit and then, for institutional and regulatory reasons, refused for weeks to authorize alternative tests from academic or commercial labs. The White House Coronavirus Task Force was reorganized around Jared Kushner, whose private-sector "shadow task force" undermined coherent federal procurement of PPE and ventilators. Governors were told to fend for themselves. Then, having lost the chance at suppression, the administration pivoted to a strategy of denial, theater, and culture war that turned masks into partisan symbols and undermined the eventual vaccine rollout among its own voters.

None of this was an epistemic failure. The relevant knowledge was sitting in the documents Jasanoff dissects. Anthony Fauci knew it. Robert Redfield knew it. Career officials at HHS and FEMA and the State Department knew it. What was missing was not humility; it was a functioning political executive willing to act on what was known. To attribute the catastrophe primarily to overconfident prediction is to give the political actors who actually drove the disaster a kind of intellectual alibi. They are recast as casualties of a flawed paradigm rather than as agents of choice.

The comparative evidence is instructive. Germany, which Jasanoff notes was ranked 14th in the GHSI yet outperformed the U.S. and U.K., did not deploy any radically different epistemic framework. It used essentially the same playbook of testing, tracing, isolation, and graduated restrictions that the WHO and the GHSI recommended. What Germany had was something the United States in 2020 did not: a competent head of government who took the threat seriously, deferred publicly to scientific advisors, and maintained at least the minimal political consensus required to sustain protective measures across the federal states. That is a political fact, not an epistemic one. South Korea, Taiwan, Vietnam, Australia, and New Zealand similarly succeeded not by inventing a new science of humility but by combining standard preparedness with competent political execution and, crucially, public trust earned by transparent communication.

The places that failed — the U.S., the U.K. under Boris Johnson, Brazil under Bolsonaro, India under Modi during the Delta wave — share a different profile. They were governed by populist nationalists who treated expertise as adversary and the pandemic as a public-relations problem. The pattern is striking enough that it ought to be the focus of any serious post-mortem. Yet "technologies of humility" do not really address it. What is the humility-based remedy for a head of state who refuses to listen? More citizen participation? Better stakeholder mapping? These prescriptions will not bite where the bite is most needed.

Jasanoff might respond that humility, as she defines it, is precisely about asking who is left out, who is hurt, whose voices are unheard — and that an inclusive process might generate the political legitimacy to make preparedness work. I am sympathetic to that ambition. But the historical record on participatory technology assessment is mixed at best. Deliberative exercises do good when they are coupled to actual decision authority and when the surrounding political culture has not already pre-sorted citizens into mutually hostile epistemic camps. In a country where 40 percent of the population now consumes news selected to confirm that the pandemic is a hoax engineered by Anthony Fauci and Bill Gates, an additional layer of participatory humility is not going to produce consensus. It may produce more polished documentation of the dissensus.

There is one place where I think humility, in Jasanoff's sense, would have helped: communication. The CDC's and WHO's early statements on masks, on asymptomatic transmission, and on surface contamination were issued with too much confidence and revised in ways that damaged credibility. A more candid acknowledgment, from the start, that "we are operating with incomplete data and our recommendations may change as we learn more" would have served public trust better than the false certainties followed by embarrassed reversals. Here the indictment of expert overconfidence has real bite. Public health authorities should learn to talk in confidence intervals, to flag what they do not know, to update without shame. That is humility worth cultivating.

But this is a refinement within preparedness, not a replacement of it. The architecture of pandemic response — surveillance, testing, tracing, treatment, vaccines, communication — does not change. What changes is the tone with which experts engage the public and the seriousness with which planners build political coalitions before the crisis hits. Those are valuable adjustments. They do not amount to a new paradigm, and elevating them to one risks distracting us from the unglamorous, profoundly political work of repairing the institutions and the democratic culture without which no paradigm — humble or heroic — can succeed.

The next pandemic will test, again, whether the United States can govern itself. That is the question Jasanoff's essay too gently sidesteps. Humility is a virtue. So is courage. So is competence. The country needs all three, and it needs them embodied in functioning political institutions. No reform of the expert imagination, however welcome, can substitute for that.

In Defense of Preparedness

Sheila Jasanoff's case against preparedness is gracefully made, but I think it gets the diagnosis wrong, and the remedy still more so. The United States did not lose three-quarters of a million people because its planners were too confident, too quantitative, or too Promethean. It lost them because, when the moment came, it discarded the playbook, ignored the experts, defunded the institutions, and elected a president who suggested injecting bleach. The problem was not that we prepared too aggressively. It was that we prepared on paper and then refused to execute. Substituting "humility" for preparedness as our governing ideal will not fix what went wrong; it will guarantee that the next time goes worse.

Let us begin with the comparison Jasanoff draws but does not quite confront. She notes that the United States and the United Kingdom topped the Global Health Security Index and then performed terribly, while less-ranked countries did better. She presents this as evidence that the GHSI's framework was misconceived — too checklist-driven, too blind to politics, too confident in the orderly cooperation of citizens and governments. But this is not the only inference available. An equally plausible reading is that the GHSI accurately measured capacity, and that the United States threw its capacity away. Capacity is necessary but not sufficient. A nation can have the world's best ventilator stockpile and still squander it; that does not prove that stockpiles are useless. South Korea, ranked ninth in the GHSI, executed brilliantly in early 2020 by using exactly the kind of tools the Index measures: rapid testing infrastructure, contact tracing capacity, transparent risk communication. Taiwan, Singapore, and Germany also drew on serious preparedness investments. The Index's predictive failure for the U.S. tells us something about American political dysfunction, not about the inadequacy of preparedness as a paradigm.

Jasanoff's critique of "the Fineberg plan" makes the same move. She points out that June 6, 2020, came and went without crushing the curve, and concludes that Fineberg's wartime mobilization rhetoric "presupposed [a] United States that never was." But Fineberg's six steps — unified command, testing, isolation, supply chains, public mobilization, equity — were not crazy. They were essentially what New Zealand and Australia executed, with results that should embarrass us. The fact that the United States could not muster a unified command is a fact about American political institutions in 2020, not a fact about the limits of preparedness thinking. To blame the manual because the country refused to read it is a curious form of analysis.

The deeper problem with Jasanoff's "technologies of humility" is that, as a positive program, they remain vague almost to the point of vanishing. We are told that humility means asking "who might get hurt, what happened when we tried before, whose perceptions were systematically ignored, and what protections are in place if we again guess wrong." These are excellent questions. They are also questions that good preparedness planning has been asking for decades. The Obama playbook explicitly flagged humanitarian impacts, political stability, and public concern. Pandemic exercises like Crimson Contagion in 2019 surfaced exactly the kind of federalism and supply-chain problems that humility advocates say were ignored. The After Action reports for H1N1 and Ebola took seriously the problem of distrust in marginalized communities. The intellectual content of "humility" is largely already inside the preparedness paradigm, even if it was honored more in the breach.

Where humility goes beyond preparedness is in its suspicion of action under uncertainty — and this is precisely where I think it courts danger. Cass Sunstein's old critique of the precautionary principle was not the caricature Jasanoff implies. His point was that "precaution" tends to be invoked asymmetrically: against new technologies, never against the harms of inaction. Imagine if a humility-driven FDA had subjected the mRNA vaccines to the kind of slow, consultative, citizen-participatory review process that Jasanoff's framework seems to recommend. We would still be debating Pfizer's emergency use authorization while another million people died. The vaccines were the single greatest policy success of the pandemic. They were the product not of humility but of audacious, predictive, capability-feeds-ambition science — Operation Warp Speed, massive parallel manufacturing bets, a willingness to compress timelines by acting in advance of full knowledge. That is preparedness at its best.

Jasanoff is right that the same was not true at every stage. The CDC's testing fiasco in February and March 2020, the on-again-off-again mask guidance, the FDA's slowness to authorize at-home rapid tests — these were real failures. But none of them was caused by overconfidence in prediction. They were caused by institutional caution, by an excess of bureaucratic conservatism, by a regulatory culture too humble to act without textbook-quality evidence. When the FDA dragged its feet on rapid tests because their sensitivity was lower than PCR, that was humility — humility about a technology's imperfections — that cost thousands of lives. When public health authorities refused to recommend masks in March 2020 because randomized trial data was thin, that too was humility. Sometimes the failure of expert institutions is precisely that they are too tentative, too hedged, too unwilling to make confident probabilistic bets under uncertainty.

There is also a sleight of hand in Jasanoff's invocation of "the unknown unknowns." Of course we should worry about surprises. But how does one prepare for what one cannot imagine? The honest answer is: by building robust, redundant capacity that can be repurposed against threats whose shape is not yet known. That is what preparedness, properly understood, is for. A well-staffed CDC, a domestic manufacturing base for PPE, a strategic national stockpile that is actually stocked, an interoperable public health data infrastructure, a trained reserve of contact tracers — these are not blueprints for fighting the last war. They are general-purpose institutional capabilities. They can be deployed against influenza, coronavirus, or something we have not yet named. The right response to unknown unknowns is not to plan less but to build more flexibly.

Finally, a word about the politics of humility. Jasanoff casts humility as the ethical alternative to the militaristic "can-do" posture of preparedness. But humility has its own moral dangers. It can shade into fatalism, into a willingness to accept harms as the price of acknowledging our limits. It can serve as alibi for inaction — exactly the charge Sunstein levels against precaution. And it can encourage a kind of expert defeatism that the public, faced with real and frightening threats, will not tolerate. People want their leaders to know what to do. The genius of the Coast Guard's "always ready" is not that it denies the possibility of defeat; it is that it commits the institution to act anyway. A democratic public can accept that its experts sometimes fail. It will not long tolerate experts who treat failure as a quasi-virtue.

The lesson of COVID-19 is not that we prepared too much. It is that we prepared abstractly, then governed terribly. The remedy is more preparedness, executed seriously, by states that fund the institutions they will need. Humility is a fine personal virtue. As a public-policy paradigm, it is no substitute for capacity, commitment, and the courage to act.

Whose Humility? A View from Outside the West

There is something faintly ironic about an essay urging humility that arrives, like so much of our pandemic commentary, framed almost entirely by the American experience. Sheila Jasanoff's analysis is acute, and her ethical instincts are admirable. But its frame of reference is consistently the United States and, to a lesser extent, the United Kingdom and Western Europe. The countries that actually performed well during the first eighteen months of COVID-19 — Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, Singapore, Thailand, New Zealand, Australia, Mongolia, Cuba — appear in her essay either glancingly or not at all. This is not a quibble. It bears directly on what we should conclude about preparedness, prediction, and humility, because the successful cases do not really fit Jasanoff's story.

What did the successful responders share? Not, on the whole, a more humble epistemic culture. Taiwan and South Korea, in particular, are technocratic societies whose pandemic responses leaned heavily on the most ambitious tools of prediction and control — digital contact tracing, large-scale testing programs, real-time data dashboards, integrated databases linking health insurance records to immigration data. These are, in Jasanoff's vocabulary, Promethean technologies. They worked. Taiwan's success was not built on doubt but on a kind of disciplined confidence: a central command structure under Audrey Tang and the CDC, an aggressive willingness to act on early signals from Wuhan, and a public that largely complied with restrictions. If anything, what these states demonstrated was that competent execution of relatively standard preparedness frameworks, combined with high social trust and serious state capacity, produces dramatically better outcomes than the same frameworks applied to a polarized, low-trust polity like the United States.

This matters because Alexandre White, whom Jasanoff briefly quotes, points to a deeper issue: the failure of Western pandemic policy was "rooted in the West's self-styled superiority to the rest of the world, especially when it comes to disease." The West congratulated itself on its preparedness indices while ignoring how much there was to learn from countries that had been through SARS, MERS, and avian influenza in living memory. East Asian polities did not need to manufacture humility through philosophical reflection; they had been humbled, repeatedly, by recent outbreaks, and they had built institutional muscle memory accordingly. Taiwanese officials had run pandemic exercises since 2004. Korean public health law had been revised after MERS in 2015 to permit rapid contact tracing and mandatory isolation. These are not the fruits of "humility as a stance"; they are the fruits of recent painful experience metabolized into administrative capacity.

This suggests an important amendment to Jasanoff's framework. The opposite of hubristic preparedness is not necessarily a humble stance toward uncertainty. It may be a more capable, more experienced state. The countries that did best were not the ones that thought more cautiously about what they did not know. They were the ones that had recently been bitten and had built systems accordingly. Their epistemic posture is closer to "we have done this before; we know what to do; do it now" than to the philosophical reflectiveness Jasanoff recommends. That is not arrogance; it is hard-won institutional learning.

Moreover, several of the successful responders relied on degrees of state intrusion that would sit uneasily with Jasanoff's humility framework. Singapore used a national tracing app with mandatory check-ins. China sealed entire cities. Australia, a liberal democracy, deployed extraordinarily strict border controls and internal movement restrictions. Vietnam locked down aggressively and enforced quarantines with state apparatus. These responses were neither participatory in the deliberative-democratic sense Jasanoff favors, nor humble in their assertion of state authority over individual movement. They were, by Western liberal lights, illiberal. They were also extraordinarily effective at saving lives. This raises an uncomfortable question for Jasanoff's framework: is "humility" defined in such a way that the most lethal policies (those of the U.S. and U.K.) are coded as Promethean, while equally aggressive but more effective policies (those of Vietnam or China) are also coded as Promethean, leaving only the philosophical posture of certain Western technocrats as the alternative? If so, the framework is doing more aesthetic than analytical work.

I want to be fair to Jasanoff. Her deepest concern is not really about state capacity or political execution. It is about whose voices are heard, whose perspectives shape the framing of problems, and how the harms of policy fall on the marginalized. Those are crucial concerns, and the technologies of humility she sketches — citizen engagement, attention to historical injustice, refusal to dismiss "unscientific" anxieties — speak to them. The history of public health is, after all, also a history of coercion against the poor, against immigrants, against colonized peoples. The 1918 quarantines in San Francisco fell hardest on Chinatown. Cholera responses in the colonial 19th century were instruments of empire. Modern HIV policy in many countries criminalized the very communities it claimed to protect. Sensitivity to these patterns is not optional. It is part of what any decent pandemic policy must include.

But there is a tension here that the essay does not resolve. The countries that did best during COVID-19 generally did not arrive at their effective policies by foregrounding the voices of the marginalized. They arrived through executive authority exercised by relatively insulated technocracies, often with limited public deliberation, often with practices that the marginalized themselves did not endorse. The cultural homogeneity of some of these polities, and the centralized authority of others, allowed actions that no participatory process in a polarized society could have produced. The American failure, by contrast, was not principally a failure to listen to marginalized communities. It was a failure to maintain even the minimum political consensus required for the state to act coherently at all. In such a setting, more participation might deepen, rather than resolve, the impasse.

What, then, would I retain from Jasanoff's essay? Three things. First, the critique of overconfident, militaristic preparedness language. The "war on the virus" framing was rhetorically counterproductive and analytically misleading. Second, the insistence that vulnerability is socially produced and predictable, and that any responsible plan must ask in advance who will bear the brunt of failure. Third, the call for genuine engagement with the populations whose distrust of expertise has its own historical reasons. These are important correctives, and they belong in any future preparedness framework.

What I would reject is the implication that the right paradigm is "humility" as such, opposed to prediction and preparedness. The successful pandemic responders combined ambitious prediction, robust preparedness, decisive execution, and — only secondarily — sensitivity to political and social texture. The failed responders had the first two on paper and lacked the third entirely. The lesson is not to abandon Prometheus for a more reflective deity. It is to staff the institutions, train the personnel, fund the surveillance systems, and build the public trust that allow Promethean tools to be used responsibly. Humility is a useful supplement. It is not, on the evidence of the past two years, the missing pillar.

Public policy after the pandemic should certainly include more reflection. But it should include even more capacity. The latter, not the former, is what separated the countries that buried tens of thousands from the countries that did not.
