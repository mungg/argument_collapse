"Doping should remain banned in the Olympics to preserve the ideal of fair competition, but it should be permitted in separate, professional 'enhanced' leagues for consenting adults under strict medical monitoring and disclosure."

The question should not be answered with one rule for all sports. “Sport” includes the Olympics, professional cycling, bodybuilding exhibitions, football, horse racing, recreational leagues and televised spectacles whose main purpose is entertainment. It is not obvious that every one of these activities must express the same ideal of the unenhanced body.

For the Olympics, doping should remain banned. The Games claim to represent nations, youth aspiration and a universal standard of fair competition. If Olympic medals become contests in pharmacology, the damage will extend far beyond the athletes who choose to use drugs. Publicly funded sports systems and ambitious children would feel the pressure. A race walker using EPO is not simply making a private medical choice; he is attacking the premise on which the event is staged.

But outside that setting, there is room for pluralism. If adult professionals in a particular sport want an openly enhanced league, with medical monitoring, disclosure, and no pretense that records are comparable to tested competition, why should that be forbidden? We already tolerate dangerous sports. Boxing damages brains. Alpine skiing breaks bodies. American football accepts enormous collision risks. The mere fact that an activity is dangerous cannot be the entire argument against it. The relevant questions are whether participants are adults, whether risks are disclosed, whether coercion is minimized, and whether spectators understand what they are watching.

An “open” category would also expose a truth the current system hides: many fans are fascinated by the outer limits of performance, even when they suspect those limits are chemically assisted. Better to separate tested competition from enhanced competition than to maintain one official category clouded by permanent suspicion.

This solution would be messy. Records would fragment. Sponsors might prefer the more spectacular version. Young athletes would need strong protection from pressure to enter enhanced pipelines. Yet the current system is messy too: selective enforcement, rumors after every extraordinary performance, and punishments that sometimes fall more heavily on the careless than the most systematic cheaters.

So doping should not be allowed in the Olympics as we know them. But sport is large enough to contain different bargains. Preserve a rigorously tested arena for those who want it, and allow adults, under strict conditions, to create an honest enhanced arena rather than a dishonest hidden one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Olympics represent a universal standard of fair competition and youth aspiration that would be undermined if medals became contests in pharmacology.
  2. 02Allowing doping in the Olympics would create systemic pressure on publicly funded sports programs and children to use performance-enhancing drugs.
  3. 03The inherent danger of doping is not a sufficient reason for a total ban, as sports already tolerate significant physical risks like those found in boxing or football.
  4. 04Creating an open, enhanced category would provide transparency for fans who are interested in the limits of human performance while removing the cloud of suspicion from tested sports.
  5. 05A dual system would be more honest than the current system of selective enforcement and constant rumors regarding extraordinary athletic achievements.
