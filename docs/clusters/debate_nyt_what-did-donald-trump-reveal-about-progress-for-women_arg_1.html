The reaction to Donald Trump’s 2005 comments reveals both how far women have come and how conditional that progress remains.

The evidence of progress is real. A generation ago, a major-party presidential nominee heard boasting about grabbing women’s genitals and forcing himself on them might well have been dismissed as merely vulgar or “private.” In 2016, the tape produced a national backlash, not just among feminists or Democrats but among Republican officials, conservative commentators, religious leaders and ordinary voters. Many people immediately understood that the issue was not “bad language” but the casual description of sexual assault. That matters. It shows that feminist ideas have entered the mainstream: consent is now part of ordinary political vocabulary, and public men are expected, at least in principle, to answer for predatory behavior.

The response of women themselves also shows progress. Many have refused to treat Trump’s remarks as shocking in the sense of unfamiliar. Instead, they have connected them to daily experience: harassment at work, intimidation on the street, unwanted touching, professional penalties for objecting. Social media has filled with women explaining that Trump’s words sounded less like an aberration than like the private script behind familiar public behavior. That willingness to speak openly is a form of power. It reflects decades of organizing against sexual harassment, domestic violence and rape myths, and it suggests that women are less willing to absorb humiliation silently.

But the scandal also exposes the limits of that progress. The speed with which some defenders reached for “locker room talk” was instructive. That phrase tried to shrink a boast about assault into a matter of etiquette, as though the only problem was profanity. It asked the public to protect male bonding from scrutiny, even when that bonding took the form of treating women’s bodies as trophies available to powerful men. The defense was not just an excuse for Trump; it was a reminder that many people still regard misogyny as normal male behavior, regrettable perhaps, but not disqualifying.

Equally revealing was the language of some Republican denunciations. A number of politicians explained their outrage by invoking wives, daughters and mothers. That response is understandable, but it is also incomplete. Women should not need to be imagined as someone’s female relative to deserve bodily autonomy. The “as a father of daughters” formulation can humanize the issue, but it can also reinforce the idea that women’s dignity is mediated through men’s protective roles rather than rooted in women’s equal citizenship.

The episode also shows why representation alone is not enough, even as Hillary Clinton’s candidacy is historically significant. A woman may be close to the presidency, and yet the campaign has still been saturated with gendered insults, double standards about ambition and likability, and tolerance for a male opponent who repeatedly demeans women’s appearance and worth. Progress can produce backlash. Indeed, Trump’s appeal has often depended on nostalgia for hierarchies that many Americans feel slipping away.

So the reaction to the tape is not a simple story of enlightenment or regression. It is a measurement of a society in transition. Many Americans now recognize sexual entitlement as an abuse of power. Many still minimize it when the powerful man is on their side. That contradiction is the point. Trump did not create American sexism, but he made it unusually audible—and forced the country to decide whether hearing it clearly is enough.

Women now have language, platforms, and at least some institutional allies to name predation when they see it. What they still lack is the assurance that naming it will reliably cost the predator anything.
