"The federal tax code should be permanently reformed to eliminate the marriage penalty by ensuring all tax brackets and credit phase-outs for married couples are exactly double those for single filers."

Marriage is the foundational institution of civil society, providing unmatched stability for adults, children, and communities. Yet, astonishingly, our federal tax code treats it as a taxable offense for millions of dual-income Americans. If two professionals decide to commit to one another in matrimony, the IRS often steps in to slap them with a "marriage penalty." This isn't just a mixed message; it is a profoundly anti-family policy that demands immediate, permanent correction through the full expansion of tax brackets for married couples.

The marriage penalty occurs because the tax brackets for married couples filing jointly are not uniformly double the size of the brackets for single filers. While Congress took steps a decade ago to alleviate this burden for lower-income brackets—ensuring that the 10 and 15 percent brackets for couples are exactly double those for singles—they failed to extend this logic up the income ladder. Consequently, middle-class and upper-middle-class couples who marry often find themselves pushed into a higher tax bracket than they would face if they simply cohabited.

Why should the government financially reward couples who live together out of wedlock, while penalizing those who make a legal and moral commitment to marriage? Public policy should, at the very least, remain neutral toward marriage, and ideally, it should encourage it. It is absurd that an institution that reduces reliance on the social safety net, fosters generational wealth, and improves outcomes for children is treated by the IRS as an opportunity to extract more revenue.

The solution is straightforward: full income splitting. Every single tax bracket, all the way to the top marginal rate, as well as all phase-outs for credits and deductions, should be exactly twice as large for married couples as they are for single filers. If the 25 percent bracket kicks in at a certain income level for a single person, it should kick in at exactly double that amount for a married couple.

Critics will argue this would cost federal revenue, but we should not balance the budget on the backs of married couples. Eradicating the marriage penalty entirely would send a clear, unified message: America values the institution of marriage and refuses to financially punish those who enter into it. It is time to align our tax code with our social values.
