Diversified Gemini

"Evgeny Morozov’s proposal for a democratic, public-spirited AI fails to account for the inherent ecological and material costs of generative AI, which are fundamentally incompatible with a truly sustainable and democratic society."

Evgeny Morozov’s elegant foray into the history of AI and his appeal for “ecological reason” offers a much-needed philosophical intervention into a discourse dominated by engineering metrics and venture capitalist hype. By recovering the concept of “eolithism”—the open-ended, purposeless play that leads to discovery—Morozov challenges the stifling instrumentalism of the “Efficiency Lobby.” However, his analysis suffers from a profound blind spot regarding the material realities of technological production. In his search for a more democratic, public-spirited AI, Morozov focuses almost entirely on the cognitive and ideological dimensions of technology, neglecting the devastating ecological and extractive material footprint of generative AI. True “ecological reason” must be grounded in actual ecology, not just a metaphor for cognitive playfulness. When we look at the physical reality of large language models, the utopian project of repurposing them for socialist or solidarity-based ends collapses under the weight of carbon, water, and exploited labor.

Morozov posits that the core problem with today’s generative AI is that it “embodies the ethos” of the market and treats users as consumers. He laments the carbon emissions generated by his API calls to OpenAI, but he treats this as a feature of the *corporate model* rather than a fundamental characteristic of the technology itself. He suggests that if we could untether these tools from market-driven development, we could redirect their “eolithic potential” toward public infrastructure.

This assumes that generative AI is a neutral vessel that can simply be reassigned to a new master. But the scale of compute required to train and run foundation models dictates a centralization of resources that is inherently anti-democratic and ecologically catastrophic. Generative AI is not a stone sitting on a beach waiting to be picked up by a wandering flâneur. It is a massive, continent-spanning industrial apparatus. Training a single cutting-edge LLM requires tens of thousands of specialized GPUs running at full power for months, consuming the electricity equivalent of a small town and evaporating millions of gallons of fresh water for cooling data centers.

The “stonefield” from which this technology is extracted is not a metaphorical space of idle curiosity; it is the literal cobalt mines of the Democratic Republic of Congo, the lithium flats of the Andean plateau, and the sprawling, energy-devouring server farms in Iowa and Arizona. To advocate for the socialized, public use of such a profoundly resource-intensive technology is to advocate for the democratization of ecological destruction. A publicly owned, democratically overseen machine that boils the oceans is still boiling the oceans.

Furthermore, the intelligence of generative AI is not just built on silicon and electricity; it is built on the mass expropriation of human labor. The models are trained on the scraped collective output of humanity—copyrighted books, personal blogs, artwork, and code—without consent or compensation. More insidiously, the models are fine-tuned and made “safe” through the grueling, traumatic labor of clickworkers in the Global South, who are paid pennies to filter out toxic, violent, and abusive content. This is the bedrock of the AI that Morozov wants to reclaim for the “Humanity Lobby.”

When Morozov brings up the Latin American cybernetic experiments of the 1970s, such as Salvador Allende’s Project Cybersyn, he does so to prove that technology can be aligned with socialist, emancipatory goals. But Cybersyn was a low-bandwidth system operating on a handful of telex machines and a single mainframe computer. It was a technology of communication and statistical coordination, not a technology of mass stochastic parroting. To compare the infrastructural requirements of Cybersyn to those of modern generative AI is a category error. Cybersyn could plausibly be run by a worker’s cooperative; a trillion-parameter LLM requires a planetary-scale supply chain that is fundamentally incompatible with ecological limits and localized democratic control.

If we take the concept of “ecological reason” seriously—meaning a mode of thinking that respects the interactive relationship between ourselves and our environments—we must conclude that generative AI, in its current form, is a dead end. We cannot “re-purpose” a technology whose very existence is predicated on unlimited extraction and infinite energy.

A truly ecological approach to AI would not seek to build public-sector versions of ChatGPT. Instead, it would ask whether we need these massive, omnivorous models at all. It would reject the premise that every technological capability must be realized simply because it is possible. If the goal is to enhance human coordination, creativity, and public deliberation, there are far simpler, less energy-intensive, and more transparent software tools available. We can build statistical modeling tools for participatory budgeting, as Morozov mentions, using basic algorithms that can run on a standard laptop without contributing to climate collapse.

Morozov is right that we need a radical political project to break ties with the Efficiency Lobby. But that project must also break ties with the Promethean fantasy that technology can liberate us from material constraints. The “AI we deserve” might very well be no AI at all, or at least a drastically scaled-down, localized, and materially transparent version of it. We do not need a socialist ChatGPT. We need an economy and a technological infrastructure that respects the carrying capacity of the Earth. Until the critics of the Efficiency Lobby reckon with the physical supply chains of compute, their utopian visions will remain trapped in the very modernist, extractive paradigm they claim to oppose.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Generative AI is not a neutral tool that can be repurposed for socialist ends because its massive compute requirements necessitate a centralization of power and resources.
  2. 02The physical infrastructure of AI—from mineral extraction to energy-intensive data centers—represents a democratization of ecological destruction rather than a path to liberation.
  3. 03The intelligence of large language models is built upon the mass expropriation of human intellectual property and the exploitation of low-wage labor in the Global South.
  4. 04Comparing modern generative AI to historical examples like Project Cybersyn is a category error because the latter did not require a planetary-scale, extractive supply chain to function.
  5. 05A true 'ecological reason' would reject resource-heavy foundation models in favor of simpler, transparent, and localized software tools that respect the Earth's carrying capacity.
