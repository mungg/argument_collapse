"The Deep South remains exceptionally conservative because its white political identity is rooted in a historical resistance to racial integration and federal intervention."

The most direct answer is that “Southern conservatism” today is largely white Southern conservatism, and it cannot be separated from the region’s racial history. That does not mean every Romney voter in Alabama or Mississippi is consciously voting on race, or that racial resentment is absent in Ohio or Pennsylvania. But the Deep South is the part of the country where slavery, Jim Crow, one-party white rule and resistance to civil rights most thoroughly organized politics, churches, schools, housing and social status. Those arrangements did not disappear when “New South” governors began recruiting factories and speaking the language of moderation.

The old Democratic South was already conservative. What changed after the civil rights era was party labeling. As national Democrats became identified with civil rights, voting rights, school desegregation and later affirmative action, many white Southerners moved toward the Republican Party. The GOP’s language was usually not explicit segregationism; it was “states’ rights,” “law and order,” opposition to “welfare,” hostility to federal judges and suspicion of Washington. But those issues had a regional accent.

This helps explain why economic class alone does not predict the Southern white working-class vote. Many white working-class Southerners benefit from Social Security, Medicare, veterans’ benefits, disability insurance, public schools and community colleges. Yet “government” is often imagined not as those programs but as an outside force redistributing status and resources to undeserving others. Race and class are intertwined: government aid is viewed differently when the presumed beneficiary is “people like us” than when it is racialized.

President Obama intensifies this pattern, not because he invented it, but because he embodies the demographic and cultural changes that many white conservatives fear: a multiracial coalition, urban liberalism, secularism, and a federal government willing to intervene in health care and civil rights. The Deep South’s conservatism endures because its white political identity was built in opposition to those very things.

The “post-racial New South” was always more aspiration than fact. Atlanta, Charlotte and Northern Virginia may symbolize transformation, but much of the Deep South still carries an older political memory. Until interracial politics becomes normal rather than exceptional, the region’s white working class will remain unusually conservative.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Southern conservatism is inextricably linked to a history of slavery, Jim Crow, and the defense of white social status.
  2. 02The shift of white Southerners to the Republican Party was driven by the national Democratic Party's association with civil rights and federal desegregation efforts.
  3. 03White working-class Southerners often oppose government programs because they perceive federal aid as a tool for redistributing resources to racialized 'others' rather than benefiting themselves.
  4. 04President Obama's leadership reinforces Southern conservatism by embodying the multiracial and secular cultural shifts that the region's traditional political identity was built to oppose.
  5. 05The concept of a 'post-racial New South' is largely aspirational, as much of the region continues to be governed by an older political memory of racial division.
