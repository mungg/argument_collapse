The Dead End of Checks and Balances · Cluster 13 of 25

"While the American system of checks and balances currently suffers from institutional paralysis and democratic defects, it remains an indispensable tool for preventing authoritarian rule and must be refined rather than abandoned."

Lisa Miller offers a powerful indictment of American institutional paralysis, but her essay presses a sound complaint into an unsound conclusion. It is true that American government contains too many veto points. It is true that the Senate is malapportioned, that the filibuster has often been abused, that courts have sometimes protected wealth and hierarchy, and that well-funded interests can exploit fragmentation to defeat popular legislation. But it does not follow that the language of checks and balances is a dead end. In May 2025, with a president openly contemptuous of legal limits and an administration willing to test whether courts, Congress, civil servants, states, universities, firms, and the press can be intimidated into submission, checks and balances are not a civic superstition. They are among the few tools available to prevent democratic politics from being converted into personal rule.

Miller’s strongest point is that American institutions often frustrate democratic responsiveness. But she underestimates the distinction between ordinary policy frustration and authoritarian emergency. These problems are related, but they are not the same. A system that makes it too hard to enact universal health care is defective. A system that prevents a president from jailing enemies, defying court orders, weaponizing prosecution, punishing speech, or using federal power for private enrichment is indispensable. The fact that the same constitutional vocabulary is invoked in both settings does not make the vocabulary useless.

The key issue is not whether checks and balances are good or bad in the abstract. It is what kinds of checks, operating against what kinds of power, under what democratic conditions. Miller recognizes this in passing, but the thrust of the essay risks blurring the matter. She writes that American checks constrain the public more than elites. Often they do. Yet the Trump administration’s early conduct in its second term illustrates the opposite danger: the capture of executive power by a faction that claims a mandate to override law itself. When courts order the government to respect due process, when inspectors general resist retaliation, when states refuse to assist unlawful federal schemes, when Congress investigates corruption, when the press exposes self-dealing, these are not elite vetoes against democracy. They are the infrastructure of republican freedom.

There is a reason authoritarians everywhere attack independent courts, professional bureaucracies, local autonomy, legislative oversight, universities, civil society, and the press. They do not do so because those institutions are always democratic in their internal structure or always wise in their judgments. They do so because plural centers of power make domination harder. A democratic majority can make law; a would-be autocrat seeks to monopolize interpretation, enforcement, information, and coercion. Checks and balances, broadly understood, prevent that monopoly.

Miller argues that majorities are less dangerous than elites. In contemporary America, that is often right. The most severe democratic erosion in recent years has indeed come from organized elites: party leaders who indulged election denial, donors who funded radical legal movements, media entrepreneurs who monetized resentment, judges who sometimes dressed partisan commitments in constitutional robes, and politicians who learned that institutional sabotage carries few penalties. But majorities, too, can be dangerous, especially when they are temporary, inflamed, misinformed, or organized around exclusion. American history is not only the story of elite minorities blocking egalitarian majorities. It is also the story of popular racism, nativism, anti-Catholicism, anti-communist hysteria, homophobia, and punitive moral panics. Jim Crow was maintained by elites, but it also drew on mass white participation. Japanese American incarceration was not merely the work of a few oligarchs. The persecution of sexual minorities was not simply imposed from above. Democratic politics needs both popular energy and principled limits.

The difficulty is that vulnerable minorities do not always know in advance whether national majorities, state governments, courts, or administrative agencies will be their best protection. During the civil rights era, federal power was essential against state oppression. During other periods, federal courts have been hostile and local governments have innovated. State autonomy has been used to preserve slavery and segregation; it has also been used to protect abortion access after Dobbs, defend environmental standards, expand Medicaid, legalize same-sex marriage before national recognition, and resist federal overreach. The lesson is not that federalism is noble. It is that dispersed authority can be morally mixed and politically useful.

Miller’s reform agenda includes many worthy proposals: voting rights legislation, an end to partisan gerrymandering, reform of life tenure for judges, a larger House, campaign finance reform, and perhaps changes to the Electoral College. But the essay does not sufficiently confront the problem of concentrated national power. If the United States had a simpler majoritarian system in January 2025, what exactly would happen when an authoritarian faction won unified control? The same institutional streamlining that enables a social democratic program can also enable mass deportation without due process, politicized prosecution, attacks on independent media, and the rapid transformation of civil service into patronage. Parliamentary democracies can act more decisively, but they are not immune to democratic backsliding. The question is how to build a state that can act for public purposes without making capture catastrophic.

One answer is to separate policy vetoes from legality checks. The filibuster, for example, is not a necessary safeguard against tyranny; it is a supermajority rule that often prevents ordinary lawmaking. It should be abolished or sharply narrowed. The Senate’s malapportionment is indefensible as a democratic principle, though very difficult to change. The House should be expanded. Voting rights should be nationalized to ensure equal citizenship. These reforms would improve responsiveness without eliminating constitutional restraints.

But legality checks are different. Courts must be obeyed. Presidents must be subject to judicial process and congressional oversight. Civil servants must not be forced to violate law. Inspectors general and whistleblowers need protection. The Justice Department must not become a personal law firm for the president. Congress must reclaim war powers, emergency powers, appropriations authority, and confirmation seriousness. These are checks and balances in the best sense: not devices to stop popular legislation, but barriers against arbitrary rule.

Miller is right that Democrats should not merely call for restraint while offering no positive agenda. A politics of “no” cannot defeat authoritarian populism. People need housing, health care, wages, safety, dignity, and a future. But a positive agenda and constitutional restraint are complements, not substitutes. Social democracy requires the rule of law. Universal programs cannot survive if presidents can impound funds at will, punish states for disloyalty, replace neutral administration with cronies, and ignore judicial judgments. A capable state must also be a law-bound state.

There is another danger in Miller’s framing: it may deepen public cynicism about the very institutions that must be defended in the short run. If citizens hear that checks and balances are mostly a fraud used by elites, some will conclude that Trump is simply more honest about power. That would be disastrous. The appropriate message is more demanding: our constitutional system contains both democratic defects and anti-authoritarian resources. We must reform the former while mobilizing the latter.

The American tradition is not reducible to Madisonian veto points. It also includes constitutional amendments that expanded citizenship, Reconstruction’s promise of national protection for equal rights, Progressive reforms, the New Deal’s recognition that economic security is a condition of freedom, and the civil rights movement’s insistence that law must answer to justice. Checks and balances can be part of that tradition if understood not as paralysis but as anti-domination: no president above law, no legislature above rights, no court above democratic criticism, no state above equal citizenship, no corporation above public regulation.

Miller’s impatience with civic mythology is valuable. The Constitution should not be worshiped, and institutional failure should not be romanticized. But in the spring of 2025, the answer to an abusive executive is not to retire the language of checks and balances. It is to reclaim and refine it. We need fewer veto points against democratic legislation and stronger barriers against lawless domination. We need a government easier for majorities to use and harder for authoritarians to capture. That is not the dead end of checks and balances. It is their democratic reconstruction.
