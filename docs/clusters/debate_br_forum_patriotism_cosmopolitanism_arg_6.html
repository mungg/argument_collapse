"Martha Nussbaum’s cosmopolitanism is flawed because it reduces Rabindranath Tagore’s complex, context-bound thought into a decontextualized Stoic allegory that ignores the necessity of local rootedness and the power dynamics of anti-colonial struggle."

Martha Nussbaum's invocation of Rabindranath Tagore as an ally in her campaign for cosmopolitan education is both welcome and deeply problematic. It is welcome because Tagore remains one of the most powerful voices against aggressive nationalism, and his novel *The Home and the World* is a work of profound moral insight. But it is problematic because Nussbaum’s reading of Tagore flattens his rich, ambivalent, and context-bound thought into a schematic fable that confirms her own philosophical commitments. If we are to learn from Tagore—and I believe we should—we must first rescue him from being reduced to a Stoic in a dhoti.

Nussbaum presents the conflict in the novel as a straightforward opposition between the cosmopolitan landlord Nikhil and the passionate nationalist Sandip, with the young wife Bimala caught between them. In her account, Nikhil speaks for universal moral reason, while Sandip represents the dangerous idolatry of the nation. The novel’s tragedy is that cosmopolitanism fails to grip the imagination, and so nationalism triumphs. This is not so much an inaccurate reading as a partial one, and it omits the very elements of the story that make it a specifically Indian, anti-colonial text rather than a timeless philosophical allegory.

First, the Swadeshi movement was not merely an outburst of "ethnocentric particularism." It was a campaign of economic resistance against British colonial rule, organized around the boycott of foreign cloth and the revival of domestic production. Tagore himself had been an early supporter of Swadeshi, and he withdrew his support not because he rejected the ideal of national self-determination, but because the movement, in the Bengal of 1905, had begun to exploit communal divisions between Hindus and Muslims—a division that would eventually lead to the partition of the province and, decades later, the horrors of 1947. When Nikhil says, "I am willing to serve my country; but my worship I reserve for Right which is far greater than my country," he is criticizing the methods and spirit of a particular nationalist agitation, not the very idea of loving one's country. Nussbaum quotes this line approvingly but strips it of its context. Nikhil is a landowner whose class interests and personal temperament lead him to oppose the boycott, which disrupted the economic relations on which his estate depended. This does not disqualify his views, but it complicates any attempt to read him as a pure moral hero.

Second, Nussbaum entirely ignores the positive content of Tagore's own cosmopolitanism. Tagore did not arrive at his world citizenship through a study of Seneca and Epictetus. His internationalism was rooted in a long tradition of syncretic Indian thought, from the Buddha's message of universal compassion to the medieval Bhakti poets who sang of a God beyond caste and creed, to the nineteenth-century reforms of the Brahmo Samaj that sought to purify Hinduism of superstition and sectarianism. The university Tagore founded at Santiniketan, whose name Vishva-Bharati means "the world's learning," was not a school for detached citizens of the world in the Stoic mold. It was an experiment in bringing together the best of Eastern and Western traditions, with a particular commitment to reviving Indian art, music, and village self-governance. Its pedagogy was grounded in the soil of rural Bengal, in a direct engagement with nature and local community. This was a rooted cosmopolitanism, one that knew that to love the world, one must first love a particular place and its people. The tragedy Nussbaum observes at Santiniketan today—the rise of Hindu fundamentalism and the marginalization of the university—is real, but the remedy cannot be to import more Diogenes. It must be to recover Tagore's own synthesis of local rootedness and universal aspiration.

Third, the very ambiguity of the novel's ending should caution against any triumphalist cosmopolitanism. Bimala, now widowed, tells her story as an act of confession and self-understanding. She comes to see that Nikhil was right about Sandip's dishonesty, but she does not thereby become a converted cosmopolitan. Her world has collapsed; her home is a shell; her husband is dead. The reader is left not with a philosophical victory but with a devastating sense of loss. Tagore himself, writing in the aftermath of the First World War and the failure of Wilsonian internationalism, was increasingly pessimistic about the prospects for a peaceful global order. His cosmopolitanism was always tinged with a tragic awareness of its own fragility.

Nussbaum’s use of the Stoic tradition to frame her argument, while intellectually stimulating, reinforces this problem of decontextualization. The image of the world citizen who throws off the "props of habit and local boundaries" carries a whiff of imperial Rome. When Marcus Aurelius recommends that we "enter into that person's mind," he is advising a Roman emperor how to govern Parthians and Sarmatians. This is admirable advice, but it is also an instrument of rule. Tagore's cosmopolitanism, by contrast, was the vision of a subject people striving to imagine a world beyond colonial domination. To equate the two is to miss the profound political difference between a universalism wielded by the powerful and a universalism claimed by the powerless as a weapon of critique.

This is not to reject cosmopolitan education. It is to insist that we build it on a broader, more diverse, and more historically self-aware foundation. Instead of beginning with Diogenes's witticism, we might begin with the efforts of Asian and African delegates at the Bandung Conference of 1955 to forge a common front against colonialism. Instead of the Stoic circles, we might study the international networks of abolitionists, suffragists, and trade unionists who actually bridged distance and difference. And instead of reading Tagore only for the sentences that echo our own preconceptions, we might let his contradictions and complexities trouble us. A cosmopolitanism that cannot face its own local origins, its own implication in power, is not yet ready to claim the world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Nussbaum strips Tagore’s characters of their historical and economic context, failing to see that Nikhil’s stance was a critique of specific nationalist methods rather than a rejection of national service.
  2. 02Tagore’s internationalism was a 'rooted cosmopolitanism' derived from syncretic Indian traditions and local engagement rather than the detached, abstract reason of Stoic philosophy.
  3. 03The tragic ending of 'The Home and the World' reflects Tagore’s pessimism and the fragility of his ideals rather than a clear-cut philosophical victory for cosmopolitanism.
  4. 04Nussbaum fails to distinguish between the universalism of the powerful, such as Marcus Aurelius’s imperial perspective, and the universalism of the powerless used as a tool for anti-colonial critique.
  5. 05A truly effective cosmopolitan education should be built on diverse, historically self-aware foundations and actual internationalist movements rather than abstract ancient Greek anecdotes.
