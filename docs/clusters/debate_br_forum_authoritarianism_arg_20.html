"To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must move beyond simple anti-corruption rhetoric and directly confront the alliance between capital and hierarchy by democratizing economic power."

Bonica and Grumbach rightly argue that Democrats cannot defeat authoritarianism by moderating their way into respectability. But the lead essay could go further in naming the social force behind the crisis: authoritarianism in the United States is not merely a style of politics or a threat to institutions. It is a project for protecting hierarchy and capital from democratic accountability. Its corruption is not incidental. It is constitutive.

The temptation in liberal analysis is to separate “democracy issues” from “economic issues.” On one side are elections, courts, norms, and the rule of law. On the other are wages, prices, health care, housing, taxes, and unions. Bonica and Grumbach reject this division by proposing anti-corruption as a bridge. That is a good start. But the bridge should be stronger: democracy is the means by which ordinary people can contest economic domination. When economic power becomes too concentrated, it seeks to insulate itself from democracy. Authoritarian politics is one method of insulation.

This is visible in the alliance between Trumpism and plutocracy. Trump’s rhetoric is anti-elite; his governing coalitions empower the rich, deregulators, fossil fuel interests, union busters, private equity, tax cutters, and ideological judges hostile to the administrative state. The movement’s mass appeal comes from resentment and identity, but its policy payoff flows upward. That combination is not accidental. Scapegoating supplies emotional compensation while the wealthy receive material compensation. The people are given enemies; the donors are given law.

Moderation fails because it refuses to confront this structure. A moderate Democrat may promise competent management, bipartisan gestures, and incremental relief. But if voters believe the economy is rigged, managerial liberalism sounds like maintenance of the rigging. Worse, when Democrats court corporate donors and celebrate fiscal restraint while inequality persists, they appear to be junior partners in the same order. Authoritarians then pose as the only alternative to a corrupt consensus.

The answer is not simply “move left” on a one-dimensional spectrum. It is to democratize power. That means labor power, tenant power, consumer power, public power, and political power. It means making the economy less dependent on the preferences of billionaires and less vulnerable to blackmail by mobile capital. It means treating unions as democratic institutions, not interest groups. It means public options where private gatekeepers exploit necessity. It means antitrust enforcement that understands monopoly as political power. It means taxes that reduce oligarchy, not merely raise revenue. It means campaign finance and lobbying reforms that prevent wealth from converting too easily into rule.

Bonica and Grumbach’s anti-corruption frame should therefore be class-conscious. Congressional stock trading is outrageous not only because it looks bad, but because it symbolizes a legislature more attuned to asset owners than workers. Supreme Court gifts are outrageous not only because of ethics forms, but because they reveal a judiciary socially embedded with the billionaire class. Corporate PACs are a problem not only because money influences votes, but because they structure politicians’ sense of whose approval matters. Corruption is the everyday merging of public authority and private wealth.

A class-conscious democracy strategy could also address the right’s appeal to working-class voters. Many voters drawn to Trump are not yearning for free-market orthodoxy. They want protection, recognition, and revenge against forces they cannot control. The right gives them cultural revenge while leaving economic domination intact. Democrats should offer democratic protection instead: strong unions, industrial policy tied to labor standards, universal benefits, rural investment, debt relief, affordable housing, and public enforcement against corporate abuse. The message should be: you do not need a strongman to fight for you; you need power of your own.

This requires breaking with donor liberalism. The Democratic Party cannot convincingly run against oligarchy while treating billionaires as enlightened partners whose interests must be delicately managed. There will always be wealthy individuals who support democracy, and in a crisis one should not reject allies foolishly. But there is a difference between accepting support and allowing donor comfort to define the agenda. If the party’s economic program is constrained by what private equity, Silicon Valley, Wall Street, crypto investors, or real estate interests will tolerate, anti-corruption rhetoric will ring hollow.

The authors call for rejecting corporate PAC money and deceptive fundraising practices. Good. Add to that: refuse union-busting consultants and vendors; bar party leaders from becoming lobbyists immediately after leaving office; deny committee influence to members who trade individual stocks; democratize convention and committee processes; support public financing; and make labor and community organizations, not donor networks, the core of candidate recruitment. These reforms would not purify politics, but they would signal a shift in power.

Critics will warn that this agenda is too left-wing. But many of its elements are popular because they address obvious unfairness. Voters may distrust “big government,” but they also hate being cheated by insurers, landlords, banks, employers, and monopolies. The question is whether Democrats can speak in a language of freedom rather than bureaucracy. Freedom is not only freedom from government; it is freedom from domination by bosses, creditors, monopolists, abusive spouses, bigoted officials, and presidents who place themselves above law. A democratic left should reclaim freedom.

Race and gender must be central to this class politics, not appended awkwardly. Economic domination in America has always been racialized and gendered. Care work is underpaid because women’s labor is devalued. Black wealth has been suppressed through exclusion, predation, and state violence. Immigrant workers are exploited through legal precarity. Trans people and disabled people face employment and housing discrimination. A class politics that ignores these realities will become nostalgic and exclusionary. A rights politics that ignores material power will become thin and elite. The task is to join them.

Bonica and Grumbach are also right that turnout depends on belief. People participate when they think politics can change the balance of power. Incremental policy lists do not create that belief. Conflict does. Democrats must be willing to identify opponents: not ordinary Republicans as irredeemable enemies, but the powerful actors who profit from minority rule and social division. Name the corporations, donors, judges, lobbyists, media owners, and politicians who benefit. Then offer people a way to fight them collectively.

There is danger in this. Class politics can be demagogic if it abandons truth or pluralism. But liberal fear of conflict has left the field to worse demagogues. Democracy is not the absence of conflict; it is the organization of conflict under equal rules. Authoritarianism arises when conflict is channeled into obedience to a leader and hatred of vulnerable groups. Democratic class politics channels conflict upward, toward accountable power.

The lead essay ends with a call to fight hard and smart. Fighting smart means recognizing that authoritarianism is not defeated by defending the pre-Trump status quo. That status quo produced too much inequality, too much impunity, and too little democratic control. If democracy means a return to a system where donors write the menu and voters choose between small variations, many people will shrug as it erodes. If democracy means the chance to govern the economy in the public interest, it can inspire.

Democrats should stop asking how to appear moderate enough to be trusted by a rigged system. They should ask how to become trustworthy enough to unrig it. That requires a confrontation with oligarchy. Anything less will leave authoritarianism’s material foundations intact.
