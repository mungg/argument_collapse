"The moral case for regularizing irregular migrants must be grounded in global justice and the structural responsibility wealthy nations bear for creating the conditions that drive migration, rather than just the passage of time."

The most striking feature of Carens's essay is its almost complete neglect of the global context in which irregular migration occurs. Carens treats illegal border crossing as analogous to traffic violations, but this domestic comparison obscures the structural violence of the international system that generates migration in the first place.

Consider the economic logic. When American companies use their market power to depress commodity prices, they undermine livelihoods in Mexico and Central America. When trade policies imposed by wealthy nations destroy agricultural sectors in developing countries, they create refugees who will eventually become economic migrants. When the drug war creates violence that makes life untenable in Central American cities, the people fleeing have no legal means to reach safety in the United States.

In this context, the distinction between "voluntary" and "forced" migration becomes highly suspect. Miguel Sanchez did not choose to be born in a country where his earnings would be a fraction of what he could earn in the United States. He did not choose the trade policies that depressed corn prices in Mexico. He did not choose the decades of intervention that have destabilized the region. And yet Carens's argument proceeds as if Sanchez's migration were a free choice that happened to violate the law.

If wealthy democracies are partly responsible for the conditions that drive irregular migration, then they bear some responsibility for addressing the problem—including by regularizing those who have migrated as a result. This is not charity but justice: acknowledgment that the border regime does not exist in a moral vacuum but in a world shaped by historical and ongoing injustices that the wealthy nations have done much to create.

Carens's complicity argument gestures toward this conclusion but does not develop it fully. A fully developed account would need to examine how trade, monetary policy, and foreign intervention create the conditions for irregular migration, and how regularization fits into a broader agenda of global justice.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The domestic analogy of immigration violations to traffic offenses obscures the structural violence and economic imbalances of the international system.
  2. 02Wealthy nations' trade policies, market power, and foreign interventions often destroy livelihoods and destabilize regions, making migration a matter of necessity rather than a free choice.
  3. 03The distinction between voluntary and forced migration is suspect when economic and political conditions in home countries are shaped by the actions of the destination countries.
  4. 04Regularizing migrants is an act of justice and restitution for historical and ongoing global injustices rather than a matter of humanitarian charity or social membership.
