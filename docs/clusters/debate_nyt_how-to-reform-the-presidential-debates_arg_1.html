"To prioritize substantive policy discussion over spectacle, presidential debates should be restructured to reward detailed explanation and serious governing approaches rather than soundbites and conflict."

The problem with the 2015 Republican debates is not simply that there are too many candidates. It is that the format rewards interruption, insult and memorized one-liners while punishing explanation. A serious debate cannot be built around 60-second answers to questions designed to provoke conflict.

The first reform should be to separate debates by subject. One night should be devoted to foreign policy, another to taxes and the budget, another to health care, immigration and executive power. Candidates should know the broad topic in advance, but not the exact questions. That would make it harder to survive on slogans and easier for voters to compare actual governing approaches.

Second, time should be redistributed. Each candidate should get several longer answers over the course of the evening, rather than constant lightning-round exchanges. A moderator could ask a candidate to explain a proposal, then give another candidate two minutes to challenge it, followed by a brief rebuttal. This would preserve conflict but make it substantive.

Third, eligibility rules should not rely so heavily on national polling months before anyone votes. Polls at this stage measure name recognition as much as support. If the field is too large for one stage, candidates could be divided randomly into two equal debates, rather than a “main event” and an undercard that reinforces media judgments.

Finally, moderators should ask fewer “gotcha” questions and more follow-ups. If a candidate promises large tax cuts and a balanced budget, the next question should be: what spending would you cut? If a candidate opposes a foreign agreement, what is the alternative? Debates will never be seminars, but they can be designed to reward seriousness instead of spectacle.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Debates should be organized by specific policy subjects to prevent candidates from relying on slogans and allow for direct comparisons of their platforms.
  2. 02The format should shift from lightning-round exchanges to longer response times that allow for structured challenges and rebuttals of specific proposals.
  3. 03Eligibility criteria should move away from national polling to avoid reinforcing media-driven name recognition and marginalizing lesser-known candidates.
  4. 04Moderators should replace provocative 'gotcha' questions with rigorous follow-up questions that force candidates to explain the mechanics of their promises.

"To foster substantive policy discussion, presidential debates must be redesigned to prioritize depth, expertise, and extended response times over the current format's focus on soundbites and large candidate fields."

The current debate format rewards rehearsed zingers and punishes nuance. When ten candidates share a stage and each gets sixty seconds to address tax policy, the only rational strategy is to deliver a memorable slogan and move on. If we want substance, we have to redesign incentives, not just plead for civility.

First, shrink the field by topic, not by polling. Instead of two undercard-versus-primetime tiers based on horse-race numbers, hold a series of smaller debates organized around policy domains—one night on foreign policy with six candidates, another on economic policy with a different six. Candidates with genuine expertise in an area get oxygen; voters get depth. CNN's three-hour cattle calls have shown that crowding everyone onto one stage produces noise, not information.

Second, replace the journalist-as-gladiator with a panel of subject-matter experts who can follow up. When Hugh Hewitt asked about the Quds Force or moderators press on Dodd-Frank, the value comes from the follow-up, not the gotcha. A retired general, an economist, and a constitutional scholar asking sequential questions—and empowered to say "you didn't answer"—would do more than any rule change to surface actual positions.

Third, lengthen response times and eliminate the buzzer arms race. Give candidates three uninterrupted minutes to lay out a position, then two minutes of cross-examination from one rival of their choosing. The Lincoln-Douglas comparison is overused, but the principle holds: you cannot explain an Iran policy in thirty seconds, and pretending otherwise teaches voters that you shouldn't have to.

Fourth, require candidates to engage with specifics before the cameras roll. The Commission on Presidential Debates—or the parties, for primaries—could publish a short list of factual premises (current deficit figures, treaty obligations, Supreme Court holdings) that moderators may reference, so candidates cannot simply invent their own reality without being called on it in real time.

None of this will eliminate the theatrical element; nor should it. But theater and substance are not opposites. The Kennedy-Nixon debates were riveting precisely because the stakes felt real. Restore the time, the expertise, and the follow-up, and substance returns on its own.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Debates should be organized around specific policy domains with smaller groups of candidates to allow for deeper exploration of complex topics.
  2. 02Moderation should be handled by panels of subject-matter experts who are empowered to ask sequential follow-up questions and demand direct answers.
  3. 03Response times should be significantly lengthened to allow candidates to explain nuanced positions without the pressure of short time limits.
  4. 04Candidates should be required to engage with a pre-established list of factual premises to prevent them from misrepresenting data or reality during the debate.

"To transform presidential debates from political theater into substantive deliberation, the format must be structurally reformed to prioritize depth and policy focus over entertainment value."

The 2016 Republican primary debates have delivered record ratings, but they’ve largely failed to elevate public understanding. To make these forums substantive rather than mere political theater, we need structural reforms that reward depth over zingers.

First, the number of participants must be cut. A stage with ten or more candidates precludes sustained discussion; answers become sound-bite sprints. Networks should set a higher polling threshold—say, five percent nationally—to winnow the field to serious contenders. For those left out, a separate “undercard” debate can still provide a platform, but the main event must allow time for genuine exchange.

Second, the format should resemble a focused policy discussion, not a game show. Instead of rapid-fire questions on a dozen topics, each debate could be devoted to a single issue area—foreign policy, the economy, health care—allowing candidates to articulate and defend their positions over 90 minutes. The Lincoln-Douglas model, where two leading candidates interrogate each other on a narrow subject, could be adapted for a smaller group of four or five, with moderators stepping back to let the contenders engage directly.

Finally, the audience’s role needs taming. Applause lines are the enemy of nuance. A studio audience instructed to remain silent, or limited to light applause only at the close, would reduce the incentive to play to the crowd. Combined with moderators empowered to press for specifics and follow up on evasions, these changes could transform the debates into an exercise in democratic deliberation rather than a reality-TV spectacle.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The number of participants on the main stage should be reduced through higher polling thresholds to allow for more sustained and meaningful discussion.
  2. 02Debates should be organized around single issue areas to encourage candidates to articulate and defend detailed policy positions.
  3. 03The influence of the live audience should be minimized to discourage candidates from relying on applause lines and sound bites.
  4. 04Moderators should be empowered to use follow-up questions to press candidates for specific details and prevent them from evading difficult topics.

"To foster substantive policy discussion, presidential debates must undergo structural reforms that prioritize depth and expertise over theatrical sound bites."

The presidential debate has always struggled to balance spectacle with substance, and the current format amplifies this tension rather than resolving it. To genuinely promote substantive policy discussion, we need structural changes that reframe what candidates are being asked to do.

First, the Commission on Presidential Debates should abolish its time-limited format entirely. Sixty-second sound bites leave no room for nuance. Instead, candidates should face extended questioning on single issues—five or ten minutes per topic—followed by genuine back-and-forth dialogue. This rewards depth over quick wit.

Second, panels of credentialed policy experts should replace journalists as questioners. Reporters are trained to pursue conflict and sound bites; policy specialists would ask the kind of technical questions that expose actual differences between candidates. The moderator's role should shift from managing the show to facilitating substantive exchange.

Third, we need to separate the theatrical elements from the substantive ones. The traditional debate—combining town halls, opening statements, and closing remarks with policy questioning—dilutes everything. What if we had a two-hour policy segment with no audience, no opening statements, and no closing platitudes? The candidates would hate it. Americans would learn more.

Critics who argue for "more circus" misunderstand the fundamental purpose. These events are the rare moments when voters can directly assess judgment and reasoning, not just prepared lines. The question isn't whether to make debates entertaining—it's whether we care more about engagement than comprehension.

"To transform presidential debates from shallow spectacles into substantive policy discussions, organizers must implement structural reforms that prioritize depth, expertise, and order."

The current 2016 Republican primary debates have devolved into a predictable spectacle. When networks cram ten or eleven candidates onto a single stage, the format inherently favors the loudest voices and the most inflammatory soundbites. To survive, candidates must pivot away from nuanced policy and deliver rehearsed zingers. If we want to rescue the debates from this circus-like atmosphere and return them to their substantive purpose, three major structural reforms are desperately needed.

First, we must abandon the "main stage" and "undercard" structure in favor of smaller, randomized groups. National polls this far out from the primaries are notoriously volatile and often have margins of error that make sorting candidates into tiers statistically meaningless. Instead, organizers should randomly divide a crowded field into two distinct, smaller debates, both broadcast in prime time on consecutive nights. Fewer candidates on stage means more time per candidate, allowing for complete thoughts rather than thirty-second rhetorical sprints.

Second, we need to rethink who is asking the questions. Currently, television anchors treat debates like reality shows, explicitly framing questions to bait candidates into attacking one another for viral moments. To elevate the discourse, networks should invite subject-matter experts—such as economists, former military leaders, or healthcare scholars—to moderate. Furthermore, instead of bouncing between five different topics in ten minutes, the format should feature dedicated, uninterrupted twenty-minute blocks focused on a single issue. This would force candidates to demonstrate a deep understanding of policy rather than relying on a quick pivot to their stump speech.

Finally, debate organizers must enforce time limits with strict technological controls. The simplest and most effective reform is the mute button. When a candidate’s allotted time expires, their microphone should be cut off. This would immediately end the shouting matches and strategic cross-talk that currently plague the broadcasts.

Presidential debates should be a public service designed to inform voters, not a gladiator match engineered to maximize cable television ratings. By reducing the number of candidates on stage at one time, utilizing expert moderators for deep-dive topics, and strictly enforcing time limits, we can ensure that substance finally outshines the spectacle.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Crowded fields should be randomly divided into smaller groups across multiple nights to allow each candidate more time for nuanced responses.
  2. 02Subject-matter experts should replace television anchors as moderators to ensure questions focus on policy rather than conflict.
  3. 03Debate formats should feature dedicated, uninterrupted time blocks focused on single issues to prevent candidates from using rehearsed stump speeches.
  4. 04Organizers should use technological controls, such as a mute button, to strictly enforce time limits and prevent candidates from interrupting one another.

"To make presidential debates more substantive, the format must be redesigned to prioritize depth of policy knowledge and detailed explanation over theatricality and soundbites."

The easiest way to make presidential debates more substantive is to stop pretending that every question can be answered in 60 seconds. The modern televised debate has inherited the rhythm of cable news: interruption, accusation, counter-accusation, pivot. Candidates learn, rationally, that a memorable jab is worth more than a careful answer. If the rules reward compression and theatricality, we should not be surprised when candidates perform rather than explain.

A better format would be a series of single-topic debates. One night on foreign policy, one on the economy, one on health care and entitlements, one on constitutional powers and governance. Each debate should have fewer questions, longer answer periods and guaranteed follow-up time. A candidate who says, “I will repeal Obamacare” or “I will destroy ISIS” should immediately be asked: with what legislation, what coalition, what cost, what risk, and what timetable?

Moderators should be chosen less for television celebrity than for subject-matter competence. A foreign policy debate moderated by journalists who know the Middle East and nuclear deterrence would be more useful than one moderated by anchors mainly looking for conflict. Likewise, an economic debate should include someone able to challenge fuzzy arithmetic in real time.

The crowded Republican field makes this harder, but not impossible. Networks could divide candidates randomly into equal groups rather than using poll rankings to create a “varsity” and “junior varsity” debate. That would reduce the perverse incentive to chase early name recognition and would give viewers a better chance to compare candidates in smaller settings.

Finally, debates should include written commitments before the event. Candidates could submit short policy memos on the evening’s topic, posted publicly in advance. The debate would then test those proposals rather than invite candidates to recite slogans. Viewers could see not only who speaks well, but who has thought seriously about governing.

Debates will never become graduate seminars, nor should they. Presidents must communicate under pressure. But the current format tests combativeness more than judgment. If we want substance, we have to design events in which substance is the path of least resistance.

  3. 03Candidates should be divided into equal groups randomly rather than by poll rankings to eliminate the incentive for early-stage grandstanding.
  4. 04Candidates should be required to submit written policy memos before the debate to serve as the basis for rigorous questioning during the event.

"To foster substantive policy discussion, presidential debates must be restructured to prioritize depth and serious exchange over entertainment and soundbites."

The crowded Republican stage this cycle has exposed the absurdity of trying to conduct a serious conversation with ten or eleven candidates and ninety seconds per answer. The arithmetic alone defeats substance: in a two-hour debate, after commercials and moderator preambles, each candidate gets perhaps eight or nine minutes of total speaking time. You cannot explain how to reform the tax code in eight minutes while also attacking your rivals and delivering your zingers.

A few concrete reforms would help. First, raise the threshold for participation, and do so honestly. If the polling cutoff produces ten candidates, the threshold is too low. Five is a workable number for genuine exchange.

Second, lengthen response times dramatically. Two minutes minimum for an answer, with one-minute rebuttals. Fewer questions, deeper answers.

Third, organize debates by subject. Devote an entire two-hour debate to economic policy. Another to foreign affairs. Another to social issues. This forces candidates to prepare seriously on each domain rather than dabble across all of them.

Fourth, eliminate the live studio audience, or at least require it to remain silent. The applause lines and boos turn the debate into a pep rally, rewarding the candidate who can land a clean attack rather than the one who can think through a hard problem.

None of these are novel suggestions, and none would require an act of Congress. They would, however, require the parties and the networks to want substance more than they want ratings. That is the real obstacle. The debate format is a symptom; the disease is the incentive structure of a campaign-industrial complex that treats a presidential election as a contest of memorable moments.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The number of participants should be limited to a maximum of five candidates to allow for genuine dialogue.
  2. 02Response and rebuttal times should be significantly lengthened to allow candidates to explain complex policy positions.
  3. 03Debates should be organized by specific subject matter to force candidates to demonstrate deep expertise in individual policy domains.
  4. 04Live studio audiences should be eliminated or silenced to prevent the event from devolving into a performance-driven pep rally.
  5. 05The underlying incentive structure that prioritizes television ratings over political substance must be addressed by parties and networks.

"Presidential debates should be restructured to focus on policy tradeoffs and the consequences of governing rather than allowing candidates to rely on vague slogans and positions."

Presidential debates should be reformed around tradeoffs. Nearly every candidate is against waste, for growth, for security, for opportunity and for the middle class. That language tells voters almost nothing. Governing is choice under constraint. A useful debate would force candidates to identify what they would sacrifice.

For example: If you cut income tax rates, which deductions go away? If you increase defense spending, what domestic spending is reduced or what taxes rise? If you oppose the Iran nuclear agreement, what is your alternative if allies will not restore sanctions? If you promise to preserve Social Security and Medicare unchanged, what level of debt or taxation are you prepared to accept? If you support mass deportation, how many people per year, at what cost, and with what due process?

These questions need not be hostile. They are the ordinary questions of governing. The problem with many debates is that they ask about positions rather than consequences. Candidates can answer positions with slogans. Consequences require thought.

I would require each debate segment to include a “tradeoff round.” Candidates would receive a policy goal and a constraint. For instance: “Design an immigration policy that reduces illegal immigration but does not expand the federal enforcement budget by more than a specified amount.” Or: “Propose a tax plan that does not increase the deficit under conventional scoring.” The numbers could be broad, but the discipline would matter.

Some will object that this is too complicated for television. But voters routinely make tradeoffs in their own lives: mortgages, insurance, tuition, retirement, job choices. They understand that promises without costs are suspect. What they rarely get is a political format that respects that intelligence.

Debates should also include post-debate scorecards prepared by nonpartisan analysts, noting whether candidates answered the tradeoff questions and whether their claims were plausible. Such scorecards should not declare winners. They should illuminate evasions.

The presidency is not an essay contest, but neither is it a talk show. A candidate who cannot explain tradeoffs on a debate stage is unlikely to be honest about them in office. The first reform is to ask questions that make fantasy harder.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Debate questions should force candidates to identify what specific sacrifices or costs they would accept to achieve their stated policy goals.
  2. 02Each debate segment should include a dedicated 'tradeoff round' where candidates must design policies within specific budgetary or logistical constraints.
  3. 03Voters possess the intelligence to understand complex tradeoffs because they navigate similar financial and personal choices in their daily lives.
  4. 04Nonpartisan analysts should provide post-debate scorecards to evaluate whether candidates actually addressed the tradeoffs or engaged in evasion.
  5. 05A candidate's ability to explain tradeoffs on stage serves as a necessary test of their honesty and preparedness for the actual constraints of the presidency.

The town hall format, first popularized in 1992, demonstrated that direct voter interaction produces more organic and substantive exchanges than panel-style questioning. The Commission on Presidential Debates should mandate that at least two of the general election debates follow this format, allowing candidates to address concerns posed by ordinary citizens rather than media professionals whose questions sometimes veer toward gotcha journalism.

Candidates should receive identical question banks in advance, enabling them to prepare substantive responses rather than scrambling for ad hoc reactions. This does not eliminate spontaneity—candidates still choose how to address the question and can be challenged by follow-ups. The pre-announced topics would simply replace chaos with the possibility of depth.

The real obstacle is not candidate willingness to discuss substance, but format design that makes substance nearly impossible. Elected officials spend years mastering policy detail; a reformed structure would give them room to demonstrate that mastery.

"To make presidential debates more substantive and democratically purposeful, the format must be redesigned to prioritize deep policy engagement, factual accountability, and direct voter input."

The presidential debate format has drifted so far from democratic purpose that reform requires reconceiving what these events are for. We should stop pretending the debates are primarily informational and acknowledge that they serve a legitimating function—signaling to the public that the democratic transition of power is proceeding normally. Once we accept this function, we can design debates that actually serve democratic needs.

First, debates should be broken into multiple segments, each focused on a single policy domain. Rather than jumping between foreign policy and healthcare in a single hour, candidates should have extended discussions of each topic. This would allow viewers to compare positions systematically and reduce the whiplash that makes substantive engagement difficult.

Second, candidates should be provided with statistical briefings in advance and required to justify claims with reference to specific data. When candidates make false or misleading claims, the fact-check should happen immediately, not three days later when the claim has already been broadcast millions of times. A panel of nonpartisan experts should be visible on screen during the debate, offering immediate assessment of factual claims.

Third, the debates should include a mechanism for voter input. Either through live polling or through questions submitted via digital platforms, ordinary citizens should be able to shape the agenda and question selection. This is not radical—it simply acknowledges that debates exist to serve voters, not networks' ratings needs or candidates' strategic calculations.

A democracy should expect more from its most prominent political rituals than the current debates provide. These reforms would begin to align debate structure with democratic purpose.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Debates should be divided into multiple segments focused on single policy domains to allow for systematic comparison and extended discussion.
  2. 02Candidates should be required to use specific data to justify claims, with a panel of nonpartisan experts providing immediate on-screen fact-checking.
  3. 03The debate agenda and question selection should be shaped by ordinary citizens through live polling or digital platforms to ensure the event serves voters rather than networks.

"Meaningful debate reform requires addressing the underlying commercial and political structures that prioritize entertainment and gatekeeping over substantive policy discourse."

Those calling for debate reform often make the same mistake: they assume the current format accidentally produces bad debates. The reality is more troubling—the format is functioning exactly as designed. The Commission on Presidential Debates, the networks, and the campaigns all have interests that align against substantive discourse.

Networks want ratings. Entertainment drives ratings. A two-hour block featuring policy discussion between two aging politicians offers less dramatic potential than conflict, interruption, and memorable moments that become next-day social media content. The networks have learned this lesson well, and their moderators are selected, at least implicitly, with entertainment value in mind.

The CPD's gatekeeping role similarly serves established political interests. Minor candidates who might offer genuinely different perspectives are excluded by thresholds designed to favor those with existing name recognition and fundraising capacity. The threshold requirements—15% in polling designated by the CPD—effectively freeze the field.

Reformers should acknowledge that meaningful change requires confronting these structural interests directly. Options include: pushing for federal funding of debates with format requirements that prioritize substance; creating parallel debate opportunities organized by civic organizations that operate outside commercial incentives; or developing alternative formats like written responses, extended policy papers, or online deliberative sessions that could supplement or eventually replace the televised spectacle.

The current debates are a product of political and commercial calculations that have little to do with helping voters make informed choices. Until reformers recognize this, proposed technical changes to question structure or timing will remain cosmetic.

  1. 01Television networks prioritize conflict and viral moments over policy discussion because entertainment drives the ratings they seek.
  2. 02The Commission on Presidential Debates uses polling thresholds to exclude minor candidates who might offer diverse perspectives, thereby protecting established political interests.
  3. 03Technical changes to debate timing or question structure are merely cosmetic and fail to address the misaligned incentives of the organizers.
  4. 04Effective reform should involve federal funding with strict format requirements or the creation of parallel debates hosted by non-commercial civic organizations.
  5. 05Alternative formats such as written responses and extended policy papers should be developed to supplement or replace the traditional televised spectacle.

"Presidential debates should be reformed into a more demanding format that prioritizes policy competence and complexity over entertainment to better serve democratic responsibilities."

Those lamenting the lack of substance in presidential debates should recognize that debate reform is not merely a technical question but a philosophical one about what democracy requires of its citizens and leaders. The current format implicitly assumes voters want entertainment and candidates want to provide it—this assumption deserves challenge.

A more demanding format would assume that citizens can handle complexity and that candidates should be expected to demonstrate policy competence. This would mean questions that require specific numerical responses, candidate statements that commit them to particular positions rather than general philosophies, and follow-up questions that probe the reasoning behind stated positions.

The objection that such a format would reduce viewership ignores evidence that audiences hunger for substantive content when it is offered. The 2012 Republican primary featured extended policy discussions on healthcare that generated significant viewer interest. The 2008 Democratic primary debates were notable for their substantive engagement with healthcare, financial regulation, and foreign policy.

What voters actually want, when given the choice, is often different from what they are given. Networks and campaigns have concluded that audiences prefer conflict and simplicity, but this may be a self-fulfilling prophecy—audiences may have adapted to low-quality content because higher-quality content has not been offered.

Reformers should design debate formats for the audience they want to create, not the audience that currently exists. Over time, expectations would shift, and candidates would adapt. The alternative is continuing to provide content that citizens increasingly recognize as inadequate to their democratic responsibilities.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The debate format should require candidates to provide specific numerical responses and commit to particular policy positions rather than general philosophies.
  2. 02Moderators should utilize follow-up questions to probe the underlying reasoning behind a candidate's stated positions.
  3. 03Historical evidence from past primary debates suggests that audiences are genuinely interested in substantive policy discussions when they are provided.
  4. 04The current focus on conflict and simplicity is a self-fulfilling prophecy that underestimates the audience's capacity for high-quality content.
  5. 05Designing debates for a more serious audience will eventually shift public expectations and force candidates to adapt their behavior.
