"The essay argues that Brandon M. Terry’s attempt to revitalize Martin Luther King Jr.’s legacy fails to recognize that King’s patriarchal, charismatic leadership model is structurally incompatible with modern intersectional and democratic organizing."

In his thoughtful essay "MLK Now," Brandon M. Terry seeks to revitalize Martin Luther King, Jr.’s legacy for contemporary activists, pushing past the sanitized holiday mythology to reveal a sophisticated theorist of racism, direct action, and ethics. Anticipating the critiques of contemporary feminists and organizers within the Movement for Black Lives (M4BL), Terry acknowledges King’s profound failures regarding gender. He admits that King’s essentialist views on women and the family represent a severe moral failure, and he proposes a seemingly straightforward fix: "Any retrieval of King’s legacy has to amend his triple evils to include a fourth: sexism."

Unfortunately, this additive approach—simply tacking sexism onto militarism, poverty, and racism—fails to grapple with how patriarchal assumptions structurally defined King's model of political action. The feminist critique of King is not just about his personal "blindness" to gendered dynamics or his failure to elevate women; it is fundamentally about the limits of the charismatic, top-down leadership model he embodied. For activists in 2018, moving past King is not an act of "penance," as Terry suggests, but a strategic and philosophical necessity.

The Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC), which King led, was explicitly organized around the authority of black male clergy. This was not merely a cultural relic of the time; it was a deliberate organizational strategy that sidelined the very democratic, grassroots power Terry praises. Ella Baker, one of the most brilliant organizers of the 20th century, left the SCLC precisely because she recognized that the charismatic, messianic leadership model centered on King was inherently limiting. Baker understood that strong people do not need strong leaders; they need organizational structures that empower them locally. She nurtured the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) to operate horizontally, prioritizing group-centered leadership over leader-centered groups.

When contemporary movements like M4BL look to ancestors like Audre Lorde, Assata Shakur, or Ella Baker instead of King, they are rejecting the patriarchal structure of the Civil Rights Movement. M4BL’s deliberate choice to be "leader-full" rather than leader-led is a direct response to the vulnerability of the King model. When a movement relies on a single, charismatic male figure, it is highly susceptible to decapitation by state violence (as tragically happened to King) and to the marginalization of the queer, trans, and female activists who invariably do the invisible labor of organizing.

Terry writes eloquently about King’s vision of mass civil disobedience, noting its "magnificent universal quality" that transcends exclusions of gender and age. But historically, the spectacle of the mass march often relied on women as logistical background workers—printing flyers, organizing carpools, sustaining boycotts—while men held the microphones and negotiated with power. Jo Ann Robinson and the Women's Political Council made the Montgomery Bus Boycott possible, but King became its face. A politics of direct action that does not interrogate who takes the stage and who sweeps it is fundamentally flawed.

Moreover, Terry’s defense of King’s "politics of character" and his emphasis on personal standards of excellence, while carefully distinguished from conservative "respectability politics," still carries the residue of patriarchal oversight. The demand for disciplined comportment and the policing of anger often falls disproportionately on marginalized women, who are expected to bear the brunt of systemic violence with superhuman grace. The "righteous anger" that King believed must be transmuted into love is a luxury that black women are frequently denied the right to express without being pathologized.

To truly honor King’s intellectual legacy, we must be willing to dismantle the operational architecture of his movement. We cannot simply extract his theories of structural racism and *agape* love while ignoring that they were packaged in a model of male pastoral authority that is fundamentally incompatible with modern intersectional organizing. Terry’s essay provides a brilliant mapping of King’s mind, but it ultimately underestimates the necessity of feminist organizational critique. The future of protest will not look like a King-led march; it will look like the decentralized, queer-inclusive, and feminist-led mutual aid and direct-action networks that are already charting a path beyond the shadow of the mountaintop.
