"The crisis of American democracy is fundamentally a crisis of labor, and any movement for reform must focus on restoring worker power, security, and democratic voice in the workplace."

The lead essay’s question—whether America is entering another moment when ideals and institutions visibly diverge—should be answered from the standpoint of work. The crisis of American democracy is, at bottom, a crisis of labor: not only the absence of jobs, but the degradation of work, the weakening of worker voice, and the severing of productivity from security.

For much of the postwar period, millions of Americans experienced economic growth through rising wages, benefits, and a plausible expectation that their children might do better. That arrangement was never universal; it often excluded women, black workers, agricultural and domestic workers, and many immigrants. But it established an important democratic principle: economic growth should be broadly shared. Over the last generation that principle has eroded. Productivity continued to rise, but wages for many workers stagnated. Health care and pensions shifted from stable promises to individual risks. Union density fell. Manufacturing employment declined. Service work expanded, often with low pay and irregular schedules. Then came the Great Recession, turning insecurity into emergency.

Occupy Wall Street has rightly focused attention on finance, but it should not let Wall Street become a symbol so large that it obscures the workplace. The slogan “we are the 99 percent” will have lasting force only if it becomes “we are workers,” including those who are unemployed, underemployed, unpaid caregivers, students preparing for work, and retirees whose security depends on past labor. A democracy in which most people spend much of their waking lives under authoritarian management cannot be fully democratic.

The attack on public-sector unions this year in Wisconsin and elsewhere revealed how central labor remains. Governors presented teachers, nurses, firefighters, and clerks as privileged burdens on taxpayers, rather than as workers who had retained some of the security that private-sector workers had lost. The politics of resentment was turned downward: instead of asking why so many private workers lack pensions and bargaining rights, citizens were encouraged to ask why public workers have them. This is the logic of austerity. It equalizes by subtraction.

A serious movement for the future must reject that logic. The answer to declining benefits is not to strip benefits from the remaining workers who have them. It is to rebuild institutions of security for all. That means defending collective bargaining, but also updating labor law for a changed economy. The National Labor Relations Act was built around a workplace model that fits poorly with subcontracting, franchising, temp work, and dispersed service employment. Employers can intimidate organizing drives, delay recognition, and treat penalties as business costs. If workers have no practical right to organize, then the formal right is hollow.

Labor law reform should be central to Occupy’s agenda. Card check may be politically stalled, but the underlying issue remains: workers need a fair path to representation. They also need protection against retaliation, sectoral approaches where workplace-by-workplace bargaining is ineffective, and ways for immigrant workers to assert rights without fear of deportation. Wage theft enforcement, paid sick days, predictable scheduling, and a higher minimum wage are not secondary reforms; they are democracy at work.

Jobs policy is equally urgent. It is morally indefensible to accept mass unemployment while hiding behind deficit rhetoric. People want to work. Communities need schools repaired, transit expanded, homes weatherized, parks maintained, and care work performed. A public jobs program would not be charity. It would be a recognition that the market has failed to provide sufficient employment and that the public has useful work to do. The Works Progress Administration is not a perfect model for today, but it reminds us that government can employ people directly when private demand collapses.

The lead essay’s language of participation also demands a broader conception of economic democracy. Worker cooperatives, employee ownership, and seats for workers in corporate governance should be part of the discussion. Germany’s system is not easily imported, but its stronger traditions of worker representation show that modern capitalism need not treat employees as disposable inputs. American firms ask workers for loyalty, flexibility, and sacrifice; workers should have voice in decisions that determine their lives.

Unions, for their part, must learn from Occupy. Too often organized labor has appeared defensive, bureaucratic, and tied to a shrinking base. Occupy brings moral clarity, youthful energy, and a willingness to challenge the financialization of the economy. Labor brings resources, experience, and a direct connection to millions of households. The alliance between the two is not automatic, but it is necessary. Without workers’ organizations, Occupy risks remaining expressive. Without new movements, labor risks managing decline.

The movement should also be careful with the category “middle class.” Politicians love the phrase because it avoids the language of class conflict. But a politics centered only on restoring the middle class may neglect the poor and stigmatize low-wage workers as people who have failed to arrive. The goal should not be to rescue a status category; it should be to guarantee dignity, voice, and security to all who labor.

Are we in a reform moment? We could be. But reform will not come from moral appeal alone. It will come when workers regain the capacity to disrupt, bargain, and govern. Wall Street’s power rests partly on the weakness of labor. Reversing inequality therefore requires more than taxing the rich after the fact. It requires changing how income and power are distributed before profits are booked.

Occupy the future by organizing the workplace, defending the unemployed, and making work democratic. A society that honors labor only in speeches while denying workers power is not living up to its principles. It is living off the people it refuses to hear.
