**Beyond the Principles Talk**

The Boston Review's lead essay is too tame. It frames Occupy as an effort to bring American institutions into alignment with American principles—a fundamentally reformist project within the existing political and economic order. I understand the editorial caution behind this framing, and I recognize that it will make the ensuing series more accessible to a broad readership. But it understates what is at stake, and it risks domesticating a movement whose most important contribution is to make visible what cannot be reformed away.

The basic problem is this. The forms of inequality, dispossession, and political capture that have provoked the Occupy movement are not deviations from American capitalism. They are expressions of it. The financial sector did not become dominant in the U.S. economy by accident; it did so because the productive economy stopped delivering adequate returns on capital and capital reorganized itself toward speculation and rent. Wages did not stagnate because policymakers forgot to be more generous; they stagnated because the destruction of organized labor, the offshoring of production, and the disciplining of workers through unemployment were systematic, decades-long projects in service of restoring profitability. The political class did not become responsive to wealthy donors because politicians forgot their ideals; it did so because the structural dependence of campaigns on private money makes responsiveness inevitable.

To address these conditions through "principles" is to mistake symptoms for causes and morality for politics. The American principles invoked by the lead essay—opportunity, voice, participation—have coexisted comfortably with slavery, conquest, Jim Crow, and several centuries of working-class immiseration. Principles do not change material conditions. Organized power does, and organized power requires more than moral clarity; it requires structural analysis and structural ambition.

What would a more honest framing look like? It would name capitalism as the system under examination, not as a backdrop. It would observe that the problems Occupy is responding to are global, not specifically American, and that they have arisen in capitalist democracies across the world over the same period. It would notice that the most ambitious responses to comparable crises in the twentieth century—the New Deal, postwar European social democracy—were achieved through the threat of more radical alternatives, and that the slow rollback of those achievements over the past forty years has coincided with the absence of any such threat.

I do not mean to say that Occupy should adopt a single ideological position—Marxist, anarchist, social-democratic, or otherwise. The movement's diversity is a strength, and the policy menu I would personally favor is contested even among those who share my general orientation. What I do mean to say is that the conversation has to be capacious enough to include radical analysis, and the Boston Review's framing essay does not signal that it will be.

A few specific points that I think a more ambitious framing would foreground.

First, the relationship between political democracy and economic democracy. Liberal political theory has long held that political equality (one person, one vote) can coexist with economic inequality (a free market in property and labor). The history of the past several decades suggests that this is increasingly untenable—that severe economic inequality progressively corrodes political equality, and that no amount of campaign finance reform can fully insulate democratic institutions from the influence of concentrated wealth. The implication is that political democracy requires some form of economic democracy: meaningful worker voice in firms, public ownership or strong regulation of natural monopolies and the financial sector, robust public provision of essential goods, and a vastly more egalitarian distribution of income and wealth than the current order produces.

Second, the question of growth. Mainstream policy debate, including most of the inequality discussion, assumes that the goal is to restore robust GDP growth and to distribute its fruits more fairly. But growth itself has costs—ecological costs above all—that are increasingly hard to ignore. A serious engagement with our current predicament has to ask whether continued exponential growth in a finite biosphere is feasible or desirable, and what an economy oriented to sufficiency and ecological stability rather than accumulation might look like. Occupy has not foregrounded climate, but it should, because the same political-economic structures that generate runaway inequality also generate runaway emissions, and the same elites benefit from both.

Third, the question of property. Much of American political tradition treats private property as an absolute, beyond the reach of democratic deliberation. But the specific configuration of property rights in any society is the result of political choices, and those choices can be revisited. Reasserting democratic authority over property—through progressive taxation, through breaking up monopolies, through expanding worker ownership, through reclaiming public goods that have been privatized—is essential to any serious egalitarian project.

I do not expect a Boston Review forum to adopt the full menu I am sketching. But I would urge the editors and contributors to widen the analytic aperture beyond "principles versus practices." The crisis is real, the opening is real, and the moment will not last. To use it well, we need to be willing to ask not only whether American institutions live up to American ideals, but whether the ideals themselves, in their dominant interpretations, are sufficient to the world we actually need to build.
