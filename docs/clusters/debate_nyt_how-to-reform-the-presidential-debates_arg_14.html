"To improve the substance of presidential debates, the format should be restructured to tether candidate rhetoric to a public, standardized dossier of written policy commitments."

No format will make presidential debates deeply substantive unless voters demand substance. Candidates behave as they do because campaigns are contests for attention. A detailed answer about Medicare reimbursement or the Syrian civil war rarely travels as far as a punch line. The problem is not merely the debate stage; it is the political culture surrounding it.

Still, formats can help. My preferred reform is to connect debates to a public “candidate dossier.” Before appearing, each campaign would have to submit answers to a standardized questionnaire on major issues: taxes, spending, immigration, health care, climate, abortion, guns, judicial appointments, foreign policy and executive power. These answers would be posted online, limited in length, and updated after each debate.

The debate itself would then begin from those written answers. “Governor, in your questionnaire you say your tax plan will increase growth enough to avoid adding to the deficit. Which independent estimate supports that?” “Senator, you say you would use military force against ISIS without a new authorization from Congress. What is your legal basis?” “Businessman, you say you will deport millions. What agency capacity and cost does that require?”

This approach has two advantages. First, it reduces the ability to hide behind generalities. Second, it gives citizens who want more detail a place to go. Not every voter will read the dossiers, but journalists, civic groups and rival campaigns will. That matters. Debate claims would be connected to a record.

We should also allow candidates to ask each other questions, but only after submitting them in broad topical categories to the moderator. This would prevent pure ambush while encouraging genuine contrast.

The danger in debate reform is designing for the fantasy voter: the citizen who watches two hours on monetary policy and then downloads spreadsheets. Most people are busy. They need clear, accessible events. The task is not to eliminate rhetoric but to tether rhetoric to commitments. A debate should be the visible tip of a larger structure of disclosure. Without that structure, even the best moderator is left trying to force substance out of candidates trained to escape it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lack of substance in debates is primarily a product of a political culture that rewards attention-seeking punchlines over detailed policy answers.
  2. 02Requiring candidates to submit a standardized questionnaire on major issues before debating would force them to move beyond generalities and provide a record for fact-checking.
  3. 03Basing moderator questions on candidates' written dossiers would hold them accountable to specific claims regarding the feasibility and legality of their plans.
  4. 04Providing a public dossier serves as a resource for journalists and civic groups to analyze details that cannot be fully explored during a live broadcast.
  5. 05Allowing candidates to ask each other pre-categorized questions would encourage genuine contrast between their platforms while preventing unproductive ambushes.
