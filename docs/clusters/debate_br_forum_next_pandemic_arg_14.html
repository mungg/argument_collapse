"While Sheila Jasanoff’s call for humility is valuable, it must be applied more rigorously to restrain the moral overconfidence and coercive power of public health authorities who often bypassed democratic deliberation and liberty in their pandemic response."

Sheila Jasanoff’s call for humility is welcome, but it should be turned more sharply toward the authorities who claimed the right to manage social life during the pandemic. The lesson of COVID-19 is not only that experts failed to anticipate political resistance, inequality, or distrust. It is also that public health power, once mobilized under conditions of fear, can become overconfident in its own moral authority. Humility should restrain not just prediction but compulsion.

The pandemic created real emergencies. COVID-19 has killed hundreds of thousands in the United States and millions worldwide. Hospitals have been strained; health workers exhausted; families bereaved. No serious response can deny the need for collective action. But collective action in a liberal democracy must still answer to liberty, pluralism, and proportionality. Too often during the pandemic, officials and commentators treated these concerns as annoyances rather than as central features of democratic governance.

Jasanoff criticizes preparedness for its heroic, command-oriented posture. She is right. But that posture did not disappear when experts acknowledged uncertainty. It reappeared in the assumption that public health agencies should decide, and everyone else should comply. Governors issued emergency orders for months on end. Schools closed, reopened, and closed again with limited democratic deliberation. Churches, restaurants, gyms, and small businesses faced sweeping rules that sometimes seemed arbitrary or inconsistently applied. Outdoor activities were restricted even when evidence of outdoor transmission was low. Beaches and playgrounds were closed in some places while crowded indoor workplaces continued. Families were told not to gather while political protests or other favored activities were described more sympathetically by some public health voices. Such inconsistencies damaged legitimacy.

A genuinely humble policy would have admitted from the beginning that pandemic restrictions impose harms as well as prevent them. It would not have sorted the world into “science” on one side and selfishness on the other. The decision to close a school is not purely scientific. Science can inform estimates of transmission, child risk, teacher risk, and community spread. It cannot by itself decide how to weigh learning loss, child mental health, special education needs, parents’ ability to work, domestic violence, nutrition, and children’s social development against infection risk. Those are political and moral judgments. They require democratic accountability.

By late 2021, the costs of prolonged school disruption are impossible to ignore. Children from affluent families often had private space, broadband, tutors, and parents able to work remotely. Poor children often did not. Children with disabilities lost services. Many adolescents suffered isolation and mental distress. The burden fell heavily on mothers, especially single mothers and low-wage workers. Jasanoff rightly emphasizes unequal impacts, but that insight should apply to mitigation measures as well as to the virus. Some policies adopted to protect public health widened inequality.

The same is true of lockdowns and business closures. Professionals moved to Zoom. Many small businesses lost revenue or closed. Workers in hospitality, entertainment, personal services, and retail faced layoffs or exposure. Emergency aid helped, but unevenly and temporarily. A laptop class could speak of “staying home” as civic virtue while delivery drivers, warehouse workers, nurses, cleaners, farmworkers, and grocery clerks kept the country running. A humble politics would be cautious about mandates whose burdens are borne by those with the least voice.

This does not mean restrictions were never justified. Early in 2020, when hospitals faced catastrophic overload and little was known, temporary emergency measures made sense. But emergency powers require constant justification. They should be specific, time-limited, evidence-responsive, and subject to legislative oversight. Too often, pandemic governance relied on executive discretion and expert guidance without enough transparent debate about tradeoffs. That fed backlash. Some of the backlash was irresponsible and conspiratorial. But some reflected a legitimate objection: citizens were being governed by decrees whose rationale shifted and whose costs were not evenly shared.

Mask mandates illustrate the complexity. Masks are low-cost compared with closures and can reduce transmission, especially indoors. Requirements in crowded indoor spaces were often reasonable. But outdoor mask mandates, masking very young children for long periods, or treating masks as symbols of moral worth rather than tools of risk reduction undermined public trust. A humble approach would distinguish high-risk from low-risk settings and would retire measures when evidence or circumstances changed. It would not cling to visible rituals because they reassure the already convinced.

Vaccination raises still harder questions. The vaccines have been remarkably effective at preventing severe disease and death. Encouraging vaccination is clearly justified. Requirements for health care workers or nursing home staff may be defensible because they protect vulnerable people in institutional settings. But broad mandates, especially when enforced through employment, deserve more scrutiny than they often receive. By late 2021, vaccines reduce severe outcomes substantially, but breakthrough infections occur, and Delta has complicated earlier hopes that vaccination would nearly stop transmission. The case for vaccination remains strong, but the case for coercion is not identical to the case for vaccination.

Public officials should recognize that bodily autonomy is not a fringe concern. People may refuse vaccines for bad reasons, but the right to make medical decisions cannot depend on whether officials approve of one’s reasoning. Mandates can sometimes be justified, but only after considering alternatives: paid time off, easy access, transparent data, physician counseling, targeted outreach, and protection against medical costs. When governments move too quickly to compulsion, they may harden resistance and confirm fears that experts disdain ordinary people.

Here Jasanoff’s discussion of vaccine hesitancy is valuable. She argues that fears should not simply be dismissed as ignorance. But the implication should be more radical: if distrust is rooted partly in powerlessness, adding more coercion may deepen the problem. A government that wants trust must behave in trustworthy ways. That includes admitting mistakes, compensating injuries when they occur, avoiding censorship except in the narrowest cases, and separating public health communication from partisan messaging.

Misinformation has been a serious problem throughout the pandemic. False claims about miracle cures, vaccine infertility, microchips, or mass death from vaccination have harmed people. Yet the fight against misinformation also requires humility. Scientific understanding changes. Claims once dismissed can later become plausible in modified form; claims once asserted confidently can weaken. The lab-leak hypothesis, for example, was at times treated as xenophobic fantasy, though by 2021 many reasonable observers regarded it as unresolved. Early statements about masks changed. Guidance on aerosols evolved. If platforms and authorities suppress too aggressively, they risk confusing error with dissent and dissent with danger. A democratic society needs open argument, especially during uncertainty.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” emphasize citizen participation. That is exactly right, but participation must be more than consultation with communities to improve compliance. Citizens should not be treated as instruments for implementing expert goals. They should help decide the goals. What level of risk is acceptable? Which institutions matter most to keep open? How should we protect the elderly without isolating them cruelly? How should schools balance child development and staff safety? What obligations do vaccinated and unvaccinated people owe one another? These are not questions experts can settle alone.

One reason the pandemic became so bitter in the United States is that both sides often abandoned humility. Some opponents of public health measures denied reality, minimized death, harassed officials, and turned masks into tyranny. But some supporters of restrictions implied that liberty was selfish, that dissent was anti-science, and that experts should rule until danger passed. Both impulses are dangerous. The first refuses solidarity; the second forgets self-government.

A better pandemic politics would combine scientific seriousness with constitutional modesty. It would prefer persuasion to coercion, targeted measures to blanket rules, ventilation and rapid testing to broad shutdowns, protection of nursing homes and high-risk workplaces to symbolic restrictions, and legislative debate to indefinite emergency rule. It would publish the evidence behind policies and the metrics for ending them. It would compensate those asked to bear special burdens. It would treat children’s development, religious exercise, livelihood, and mental health as real goods, not secondary inconveniences.

Preparedness remains necessary. We need stockpiles, tests, genomic surveillance, hospital capacity, vaccine research, and better data. But preparedness for a free society must include safeguards against overreach. The question is not only “Were we ready to fight the virus?” It is also “Were we ready to preserve democratic habits while fighting it?” On that measure, the United States struggled badly.

Humility after pandemic should mean remembering that no institution sees the whole. Public health experts see population risk; parents see children; workers see paychecks and exposure; business owners see survival; nurses see hospital reality; clergy see spiritual needs; civil libertarians see precedents; the elderly see both danger and loneliness. Policy must hear all of them.

The next crisis will again tempt leaders to declare that necessity has settled every argument. It will not have. Even emergency government must govern citizens, not subjects. Humility is therefore not only an ethic of better planning. It is a discipline of democratic restraint.
