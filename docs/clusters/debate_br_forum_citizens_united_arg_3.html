"While institutional corruption is a valid concern, the post-Citizens United challenge should focus on reforming corporate governance and internal accountability to ensure political spending is legitimately authorized by shareholders and stakeholders."

The debate over Citizens United has too often proceeded as if “corporation” named a single political creature: huge, profit-seeking, faceless, and conservative. Lawrence Lessig avoids the crudest version of that mistake, but his argument still treats corporate political spending primarily as a source of institutional corruption. From the perspective of corporate law and governance, the picture is more complicated. Corporations are not citizens, but they are associations of citizens, employees, managers, investors, and legal obligations. The post-Citizens United challenge is to regulate the internal legitimacy of corporate political spending as much as its external effects on candidates.

The majority opinion’s great rhetorical advantage is that Citizens United itself was a nonprofit advocacy corporation. If Congress can bar that entity from using treasury funds to distribute a film about Hillary Clinton, then the law reaches far beyond Exxon or Pfizer. It reaches incorporated groups formed precisely to speak. Many civil society organizations use the corporate form because it is convenient, durable, and legally necessary. The First Amendment would be impoverished if government could silence them during election season.

But the dissent’s strongest point, and Lessig’s, is that large business corporations are different in important respects from voluntary political associations. A business corporation accumulates wealth through market transactions, limited liability, favorable tax and entity rules, and investment decisions made for economic rather than political reasons. Its shareholders may have diverse and conflicting political views. Its employees may disagree strongly with management. Its customers may be unaware that profits from their purchases will support electoral messages. When managers spend corporate treasury funds on politics, whose speech is it?

The standard answer is that it is the corporation’s speech, authorized by management under state corporate law. But that answer begs the democratic question. Corporate law gives managers broad discretion to use assets in ways they believe advance the firm’s interests. Political spending may be justified as protecting the corporation from harmful regulation or promoting favorable policy. Yet shareholders invest for financial return, not necessarily to subsidize a CEO’s political preferences. The risk is not only corruption of Congress; it is agency cost inside the firm. Managers may spend other people’s money to pursue political goals that are only loosely related to shareholder value or that entrench management.

This suggests a reform path less vulnerable to First Amendment objection than expenditure bans: corporate governance rules. If a corporation wishes to spend treasury funds in candidate elections, it should disclose the proposed policy to shareholders, obtain board approval, report expenditures promptly, and perhaps secure periodic shareholder authorization. Such rules would not silence the corporation; they would determine who within the corporation has authority to speak with corporate assets. States routinely regulate corporate decision-making. Securities law already requires disclosure of material information. Political spending can create reputational, regulatory, and financial risks. Investors have a legitimate interest in knowing about it.

Shareholder approval is not a complete solution. Many shareholders are institutions—mutual funds, pension funds, index funds—whose own governance is imperfect. Many will be rationally apathetic because any one vote is small. Some corporations have controlling shareholders. But governance mechanisms would at least expose political spending to internal accountability. They would also complicate the assumption that corporate speech reflects a unified association. If management must defend political spending to investors, the spending is more likely to reflect genuine corporate interests rather than executive ideology.

Employees deserve attention too. Unions are often criticized for using dues in politics, but labor law has developed mechanisms, however contested, for objecting workers. Corporations face no equivalent obligation to consider employees whose labor helps generate the treasury used for political spending. I do not suggest that employees should have a veto over corporate expenditures; that would radically alter corporate governance. But disclosure to employees and protections against political coercion in the workplace are important. A corporation should not be able to pressure workers to support its preferred candidates or create the impression that employment depends on political alignment.

Lessig’s dependency-corruption theory remains relevant. Even if corporate spending is internally legitimate, it can still distort political dependence. A corporation authorized by shareholders to spend $10 million against a candidate may create the kind of anticipatory obedience Lessig describes. Corporate governance reform does not cure Congress’s dependency on funders. But it addresses a separate democratic deficit: the possibility that “corporate speech” is not meaningfully authorized by the human beings whose assets and labor make it possible.

It also helps distinguish among corporations. A nonprofit advocacy group whose members join because they support its mission presents a much stronger speech claim than a publicly traded corporation with millions of dispersed shareholders. A closely held family business differs from both. A media corporation raises still other concerns. The law should be careful before flattening these differences. Citizens United’s broad rule prevents many external expenditure limits, but it does not require us to pretend that all corporate speakers are normatively identical.

Disclosure should therefore occur at two levels. External disclosure tells voters who is trying to influence them. Internal disclosure tells shareholders and stakeholders what management is doing with corporate resources. Both should be timely, searchable, and specific. A vague annual report that the corporation spent money on “public policy” is not enough. If funds are routed through trade associations or nonprofit intermediaries, the corporation should disclose that too, at least to shareholders. Otherwise governance accountability can be evaded by laundering money through collective entities.

Trade associations present a special problem. Many corporations may prefer to avoid the reputational cost of direct political spending while supporting industry campaigns through associations. This allows them to benefit from political influence without public responsibility. After Citizens United, such intermediaries may become more important. Reform should require associations engaged in substantial election-related spending to disclose major funders, and corporations should disclose payments earmarked or reasonably expected to support political activity. The First Amendment protects association, but it should not create a right to deceive investors or voters about who is financing electoral advocacy.

Lessig is skeptical that disclosure can solve dependency corruption. He is right if disclosure is offered as the only remedy. But disclosure can change behavior, particularly for consumer-facing corporations. Managers may hesitate to spend in divisive races if they know customers, employees, and shareholders will react. Some corporations will decide that overt political spending is not worth the risk. Others will spend and defend their choices. That is healthier than hidden influence.

Public financing remains essential. Even perfectly disclosed and shareholder-approved corporate spending can leave candidates dependent on concentrated economic power. A small-donor matching system or voucher system would give candidates a democratic funding base and reduce the need to court corporate PACs, executives, unions, and wealthy individuals. Governance reform and public financing are complements: one legitimizes or restrains corporate political action from within; the other changes candidates’ incentives from without.

The courts should permit these reforms. Citizens United upheld disclosure, and nothing in its reasoning should forbid corporate-law rules about authorization. Requiring a board vote or shareholder disclosure is not the same as banning speech. To be sure, some will argue that such requirements burden speech and chill participation. But corporations already comply with complex rules governing mergers, executive compensation, securities filings, and related-party transactions. If political spending is important enough to protect constitutionally, it is important enough to authorize carefully.

Finally, reformers should abandon the slogan that corporations are not people as a complete argument. Corporations are not people, but people use corporations. The hard question is which people, with what authority, and under what conditions. A ban on corporate spending answers by saying the entity may not speak at all. Citizens United answers by saying management may speak with treasury funds subject mainly to disclosure. Neither answer is satisfactory. A democratic corporate-law approach would ask whether political spending has been authorized by those whose resources are being used and whether voters can identify the speaker.

Lessig has named the danger that candidates will become dependent on funders rather than voters. We should add another danger: corporate political spending may be dependent on managers rather than on the people invested in or affected by the corporation. Democracy after Citizens United requires attention to both sides of the transaction. Congress must be made less dependent on concentrated wealth, and corporations must be made more accountable when they deploy that wealth in politics.

  2. 02Corporate governance rules, such as requiring shareholder authorization and board approval for political expenditures, provide a constitutional path to regulate corporate speech without resorting to outright bans.
  3. 03Internal disclosure is necessary to prevent managers from using trade associations or intermediaries to hide political spending from the investors and employees whose labor and capital make that spending possible.
  4. 04Reforming the internal legitimacy of corporate spending complements public financing efforts by restraining corporate political action from within while reducing candidate dependency from without.
  5. 05The legal debate should move beyond the 'corporations are not people' slogan to address the specific conditions and authority under which individuals are permitted to use corporate resources for political ends.

Diversified Gemini

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies the problem of institutional corruption, he underestimates the power of radical, real-time transparency and corporate governance reform to neutralize the corrupting influence of corporate political spending."

Lawrence Lessig’s essay provides a brilliant and necessary framework for understanding the insidious nature of money in modern politics. His conceptualization of “institutional corruption” captures precisely what is wrong with Congress: it is not a den of thieves taking bribes, but a system fundamentally compromised by conflicting dependencies. However, in his despair over the *Citizens United* decision and his critique of the Supreme Court's "Lochnerism," Lessig underestimates a tool that the Court explicitly left intact, a tool that, if wielded aggressively, could neutralize the very "iceberg theory" he fears. That tool is radical, unsparing transparency.

Lessig correctly points out that in *Citizens United*, the Supreme Court, while striking down limits on corporate independent expenditures, upheld disclosure requirements by an 8-1 margin. The Court reasoned that transparency provides the electorate with the information necessary to evaluate the speaker and the message. Lessig dismisses this as a “simple solution” that overlooks the hidden threats of unspent money. He argues that if Exxon or Google threatens to spend a million dollars, the threat itself alters legislative behavior without ever triggering a disclosure form. But this objection assumes a static, outdated model of disclosure. It assumes we must accept the weak, loophole-ridden reporting system currently overseen by the feckless Federal Election Commission. We do not.

If Congress is currently paralyzed by the fear of hidden corporate threats, the solution is not to mourn the loss of speech restrictions we cannot currently enact, but to force the threats into the sunlight. We must modernize our concept of disclosure to match the speed and scale of the post-*Citizens United* world.

First, we must demand real-time transparency for all corporate political activity. Currently, campaign finance reporting operates on a delayed cycle, often allowing critical information about who funded an attack ad to remain hidden until weeks after an election. This delay allows the "coordination without coordination" Lessig mentions to thrive in the shadows. A modernized disclosure regime should mandate that any political expenditure, or any transfer of funds to a third-party organization (such as a 501(c)(4) or a trade association like the Chamber of Commerce) intended for political use, be disclosed on a public, searchable database within twenty-four hours.

But we must go further than just tracking the money after it has been allocated. To combat the "iceberg theory"—the credible threat of future spending—we must utilize the mechanisms of corporate governance. A corporation is not a monolithic entity; it is a collection of shareholders, many of whom are pension funds, retirement accounts, and everyday citizens who may fundamentally disagree with the political spending done in their name. Congress and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) have the unquestioned authority to regulate corporate governance.

We should mandate that any publicly traded corporation intending to spend treasury funds on political advocacy must first obtain the explicit, prior authorization of its shareholders. Furthermore, corporations should be required to publicly disclose their political budgets and the specific issues or candidates they intend to target, long before any money is actually spent. If a corporation's CEO intends to use shareholder money to threaten a politician who supports climate change legislation, that intent must be debated and voted upon in the daylight of a shareholder meeting.

This kind of radical transparency fundamentally alters the "economy of influence." The power of the unspoken threat, as Lessig notes, lies in its secrecy. If the threat is made public, it generates a counter-reaction. If it becomes known that Exxon is reserving a million dollars to destroy a candidate over a specific vote, that information becomes a potent political weapon for the candidate. They can use the threat to rally their own base, to raise counter-funds through small-dollar donations, and to publicly shame the corporation for attempting to bully a democratically elected official. The threat becomes the campaign issue.

Furthermore, forced transparency acts as a powerful deterrent to the corporations themselves. Most consumer-facing brands are deeply averse to alienating large segments of their customer base. If a corporation knows that every political threat it makes, and every dark-money group it funds, will be instantly traced back to its brand and flashed across the internet, the perceived return on investment of that political spending plummets. The risk of consumer boycotts and reputational damage becomes a powerful counterweight to the desire to buy political influence.

Lessig is right that the current system of privately funded campaigns breeds institutional corruption, and his support for voluntary public financing systems like the Fair Elections Now Act is commendable. But public financing alone cannot protect candidates from the barrage of outside spending unleashed by *Citizens United*. Until the ideological balance of the Supreme Court shifts, we cannot ban independent corporate expenditures. But we can make them incredibly painful, politically toxic, and administratively difficult to execute.

Justice Kennedy’s assertion that "the appearance of influence or access... will not cause the electorate to lose faith in our democracy" is, as Lessig points out, factually laughable. But Kennedy's corollary—that transparency allows voters to judge the influence for themselves—is an opening we must exploit. We must strip away the anonymity that protects corporate political bullies. By mandating absolute, real-time disclosure and empowering shareholders, we can melt the hidden bulk of the iceberg and force the economy of influence to operate entirely in the open, where the electorate can finally hold it accountable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Supreme Court’s 8-1 support for disclosure requirements in Citizens United provides a constitutional opening to enact much more aggressive transparency laws.
  2. 02Modernizing disclosure to require real-time, searchable reporting of all political expenditures within twenty-four hours would eliminate the 'shadows' where tacit coordination thrives.
  3. 03To counter the 'iceberg theory' of hidden threats, Congress and the SEC should mandate that corporations obtain prior shareholder authorization and publicly disclose political budgets before funds are spent.
  4. 04Radical transparency transforms corporate threats into political liabilities for the company, allowing candidates to rally public support against perceived bullying.
  5. 05Forcing political activity into the sunlight creates reputational risks and consumer backlash that deter corporations from attempting to buy influence in the first place.
