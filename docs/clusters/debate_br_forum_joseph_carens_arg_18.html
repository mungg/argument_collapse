"While amnesty for long-settled migrants is a necessary moral step, it is an insufficient remedy that fails to address the fundamental injustice of a state's presumed right to exclude peaceful people based on the arbitrary fact of their birthplace."

Joseph Carens’s case for amnesty is morally serious, but it remains too cautious. He begins by accepting, for the sake of argument, the conventional view that states have a broad right to exclude migrants. He then argues that this right weakens over time as unauthorized residents become members. That is a useful argument in the current political climate, but it leaves unchallenged the deeper injustice: the assumption that birth on one side of a border entitles some people to life chances that others are forcibly denied.

The moral scandal of immigration is not only that settled migrants are deportable. It is that peaceful people are prevented from moving to improve their lives. A worker born in Mexico, Haiti, or the Philippines may multiply his income by moving to the United States. His children may gain education, security, and opportunity. Yet because of birthplace—a morally arbitrary fact—he is told that he may not cross a line drawn by history, conquest, and state power. We would never accept such immobility within a country. Imagine saying that people born in Mississippi could not move to Massachusetts, or that residents of rural poverty counties needed visas to seek work in Chicago. We would see immediately that such restrictions preserve inequality.

Carens knows this; his broader philosophical work has often pressed the open-borders challenge. In this essay, however, he brackets it. Perhaps that is tactically wise. But the bracket matters. If we assume too quickly that the state has a right to exclude, amnesty becomes an exception to a fundamentally legitimate system. If we question that assumption, amnesty becomes a partial remedy for a system that is already morally suspect.

The usual defense of exclusion is democratic self-determination. Citizens, we are told, have the right to decide who joins their political community. But self-determination cannot mean absolute power to exclude outsiders from access to basic opportunities. Liberal democracies limit majority power when fundamental interests are at stake. Citizens may not vote to suppress a religion, censor dissenters, or deny education to a racial minority. Why should they have unlimited authority to deny outsiders the chance to work and live peacefully among them?

Another defense is that states owe special duties to their own citizens. True. But special duties do not erase duties to others. Parents owe special duties to their children, but they may not assault strangers to benefit them. Citizens may favor one another in some ways, but they need justification when they use coercion to keep poor foreigners out. The harms of exclusion are immense. They are not mere disappointments. They are lost lives, stunted possibilities, family separation, and dangerous journeys through deserts and seas.

From this perspective, Carens’s social membership argument is both compelling and limited. Of course people become members by living in a society. But why must they first live under fear for five or seven years before their humanity becomes politically legible? Why should Miguel Sanchez have had to hire a smuggler, cross on foot, work in vulnerability, and wait nearly a decade before the receiving society acknowledges his claim? The injustice began when no lawful path was available for a person whose labor and presence would later be accepted.

This does not mean borders can disappear overnight. Institutions require planning. Welfare states, labor markets, housing, schools, and political systems need rules. But the long-term direction should be toward much freer migration, not merely better management of illegality. Wealthy democracies should greatly expand legal admissions, especially for low-wage workers and family members. They should make temporary movement easier and circular migration safer. They should reduce the artificial scarcity that empowers smugglers and turns ordinary work into a deportable offense.

Amnesty is necessary now because millions are already here and because deportation would be cruel. But amnesty should not be sold as a one-time act after which the state returns to morally comfortable exclusion. The pattern is predictable: rich countries restrict entry, labor demand persists, migrants come unlawfully, enforcement becomes harsher, deaths increase, and then some are legalized after years of suffering. This is not a tolerable equilibrium. It is a system that manufactures vulnerability.

Critics worry that amnesty encourages more migration. I hope it does, in one sense: I hope it signals that migrants are not enemies. But if the concern is unauthorized migration, the answer is obvious: create lawful migration. People do not pay smugglers and risk death in the desert because they enjoy illegality. They do so because legal routes are closed. The more humane and rational policy is to open channels rather than punish movement.

The labor-market objection also fails to justify severe exclusion. Immigration can create distributional pressures, particularly for low-wage workers. But the proper response is not to trap foreign workers in poverty abroad or deport those already here. It is to strengthen labor protections, raise minimum wages, enforce workplace standards, support unions, and redistribute gains. Border restrictions are a blunt and morally costly substitute for social policy.

Carens’s examples—Grimmond, Ng, Miguel—work because they show that legal status can diverge from moral belonging. I would push further: legal exclusion often diverges from moral right even before belonging develops. A person does not need to spend seven years in the United States to have a strong interest in freedom of movement. Residence deepens claims, but the initial claim to move is already weighty.

In 2009, this may seem politically remote. Congress struggles even to pass modest legalization. But moral argument should not be confined to what Congress can enact this session. The abolition of slavery, women’s suffrage, and civil rights all required challenges to assumptions once considered practical necessities. The near-absolute right of states to exclude deserves similar scrutiny.

So yes, adopt Carens’s amnesty. Legalize long-settled unauthorized migrants. Protect children and families. End the terror of deportation for those who are already part of our communities. But do not stop there. The deeper goal should be a world in which people like Miguel Sanchez do not need amnesty because they did not need to become “illegal” in the first place.
