"The federal government should remove all sectarian religious holidays from its official calendar and replace them with purely secular observances to uphold the constitutional principle of religious neutrality."

Any candid look at the federal holiday calendar reveals a constitutionally uncomfortable truth: the government of a secular republic formally endorses a Christian holy day as one of its ten official holidays. Christmas stands alongside Independence Day and Veterans Day as if it were a civic occasion rather than the commemoration of a specific religious miracle. This arrangement is an artifact of a more culturally homogenous past, not a defensible principle for a religiously diverse present. The solution is not to multiply religious holidays in a crude bid for parity, but to purge the federal calendar of sectarian content entirely and build a set of holidays that truly belong to all citizens.

The Establishment Clause requires the government to avoid not only explicit religious establishments but also endorsements that send a message of inclusion or exclusion to religious minorities and nonbelievers. When the federal government shuts down to honor the birth of Christ, it unavoidably signals that Christianity enjoys a privileged public status that Islam, Judaism, Hinduism and secular philosophies do not. The Supreme Court has upheld the secular trappings of Christmas—the tree, the lights—by arguing that they have become culturally generic. Yet the same Court, in cases like *County of Allegheny v. ACLU*, has acknowledged that a government’s display of a crèche standing alone is an impermissible endorsement. A holiday whose very name is “Christ’s Mass” is, at its root, a crèche writ large on the calendar.

The most coherent remedy is to remove Christmas as a federal holiday and replace it with a purely secular winter recess, perhaps designated simply as “Winter Break” or “Year‑End Holiday.” Thanksgiving, too, should be carefully reframed. Though it has evolved as a family feast, its origins are entwined with Puritan prayers of gratitude to a Christian God. Renaming it as a “Harvest Festival” or moving it toward a more generic “Day of Thanks” would strip away the residual sectarianism without losing the communal spirit. Simultaneously, the government should add holidays that reinforce democratic citizenship. Making Election Day a federal holiday would do far more for the body politic than recognizing any saint’s day: it would expand ballot access and declare that the act of self‑governance is worthy of its own protected moment.

Such a secularized calendar need not be hostile to faith. Religious communities would remain entirely free to observe their holy days, and employers could accommodate them as they do now through flexible leave. What would change is that the government would stop lending its authority to one particular set of theological claims. A holiday calendar centered on the founding of the nation, the sacrifices of its soldiers, the dignity of labor, and the exercise of the franchise would constitute a genuinely common heritage. It would honor the First Amendment not in theory but in practice, and it would finally make the American calendar as neutral as the Constitution demands.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The official recognition of Christmas violates the Establishment Clause by signaling that Christianity holds a privileged status over other faiths and secular philosophies.
  2. 02The Supreme Court's defense of Christmas as a culturally generic holiday ignores the inherently religious nature of a day dedicated to 'Christ’s Mass.'
  3. 03Existing religious holidays should be replaced with secular alternatives, such as a 'Winter Break' or a 'Harvest Festival,' to maintain communal traditions without sectarian endorsement.
  4. 04The government should prioritize holidays that reinforce democratic citizenship, such as making Election Day a federal holiday to facilitate the act of self-governance.
  5. 05A secularized calendar does not infringe upon religious freedom, as individuals would remain free to observe their faith through flexible leave and private worship.
