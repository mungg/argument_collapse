"While the lead essay correctly identifies the systemic roots of Islamophobia in the post-9/11 security state, it unfairly dismisses liberal constitutional rhetoric and patriotism, which are essential tools for building a broad political coalition to reconstruct American civil liberties."

Akbar and Theoharis offer a necessary warning: Donald Trump’s anti-Muslim politics did not emerge from a vacuum. That point should be absorbed by everyone now celebrating judicial resistance to the travel ban. The national security state that grew after September 11 was bipartisan, legally sophisticated, and often insulated from democratic scrutiny. Watch lists, aggressive material support prosecutions, border searches, informant practices, and drone warfare all deserve renewed examination. Yet I want to defend something their essay treats with more suspicion than it deserves: the liberal constitutional language that appeared in the airport protests and in Khizr Khan’s Democratic convention speech.

The pocket Constitution was not merely sentimental theater. It was a claim of ownership. Khan’s gesture mattered precisely because Trump’s politics depended on casting Muslims as outsiders to the American constitutional community. By holding up the Constitution and asking whether Trump had read it, Khan did not simply flatter America’s self-image. He asserted that the document belonged to him too. In the face of a demagogue who proposed religious exclusion, that assertion was powerful and legitimate.

Of course constitutional rhetoric can be inadequate. The Constitution has coexisted with slavery, Indian removal, Jim Crow, Chinese exclusion, Japanese American incarceration, and many other injustices. Courts have often failed when fear and racism were strongest. But the conclusion cannot be that constitutional appeals are empty. American movements for justice have always had to live in the tension between condemnation and reclamation. Frederick Douglass could denounce the nation’s hypocrisy while eventually arguing that the Constitution could be read as an anti-slavery document. Civil rights activists exposed the hollowness of equal protection in practice while demanding its enforcement. Japanese American redress advocates appealed both to moral memory and constitutional failure. To invoke the Constitution is not necessarily to romanticize America; it can be to indict America by its own stated commitments.

This distinction matters for political strategy in February 2017. The travel ban is being fought in courts, airports, city halls, campuses, and churches. The coalition against it includes left critics of empire, immigrant-rights organizers, civil libertarians, veterans, business leaders, university presidents, religious groups, and many ordinary citizens who may not share a structural critique of American militarism. If the public defense of Muslims begins by telling these people that their constitutional outrage is merely another form of national mythology, we risk narrowing the coalition at the moment when breadth is essential.

The lead essay is strongest when it warns against defending Muslims only through innocence. I agree that rights cannot depend on sentimental examples: the grandmother, the child, the doctor, the translator, the soldier’s family. But politics often begins with concrete cases. A public that has been taught to fear Muslims may first recognize injustice in the face of a detained child before it can understand the injustice of watch-list secrecy or material support overbreadth. The task is not to reject innocence narratives altogether but to refuse to stop there. Sympathy can be a doorway to principle.

The same is true of patriotism. It is troubling if Muslims are admitted to public belonging only by proving military sacrifice. But we should be careful not to disparage those who choose to make patriotic claims. American Muslims have every right to criticize U.S. foreign policy, to reject militarism, and to refuse loyalty tests. They also have every right to say: this is my country, my son served it, my family has sacrificed for it, and you will not expel us from its story. The problem is not Khizr Khan’s argument. The problem is if the rest of us make his argument the only acceptable one.

On policy, Akbar and Theoharis are right to demand scrutiny of the post-9/11 security apparatus. The No Fly List is especially indefensible in its current form. A secret government list that burdens travel, stains reputation, and can be used to pressure people into becoming informants cannot be squared with due process unless there are meaningful notice and challenge procedures. Many liberals who praised proposals to bar gun purchases by people on the No Fly List should reconsider. It is tempting to use a tool one dislikes for an end one supports, but constitutional shortcuts rarely remain confined to friendly purposes.

Material support law also needs revision. The government must be able to punish real aid to violent organizations, but the current framework can blur dangerous distinctions. Humanitarian engagement, conflict resolution advice, legal advocacy, religious charity, and political speech should not be chilled by fear that they will be characterized as support for terrorism. Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project gave the government too much room to treat coordinated speech as criminal even when the speech sought peaceful ends. Congress should narrow the statute and require clearer proof of intent to further violence.

Where I part company somewhat with the lead essay is in its tendency to treat counterterrorism primarily as a system of racial and religious domination. It can function that way. It often has. But it is also a response, however flawed, to real violence. Al Qaeda did murder nearly three thousand people on September 11. ISIS has carried out and inspired atrocities from the Middle East to Europe to the United States. Plots against civilians are not fantasies. Muslim Americans themselves have been targets of jihadist recruitment and of anti-Muslim backlash. A serious democratic politics must protect Muslim rights and protect the public from violence. Those goals are not opposites.

The question is how to pursue security without collective suspicion. Here, the lead essay’s critique of Countering Violent Extremism is important but incomplete. CVE programs that single out Muslim communities, ask teachers to monitor religiosity, or treat political anger as a diagnostic sign are deeply flawed. But community-based prevention need not be inherently abusive. If designed transparently, voluntarily, and broadly—addressing white supremacist violence, anti-government militias, and other forms of political violence as well as jihadist violence—local partnerships might help identify genuine risks without criminalizing belief. The current danger under Trump is that CVE will be stripped of even its nominal breadth and made explicitly anti-Muslim. That should be resisted. But the alternative should be better prevention, not denial that prevention has any legitimate place.

The foreign-policy dimension is also complicated. Akbar and Theoharis are correct that U.S. wars and bombings shape the world in which anti-American militancy grows. The Iraq War in particular was a catastrophe whose consequences continue to radiate. Drone strikes raise grave legal and moral questions, especially when civilian casualties are minimized or hidden. But we should avoid sliding into a framework in which American action explains too much. Jihadist movements have their own ideologies, agency, local roots, sectarian politics, and authoritarian practices. Syrians fleeing Assad and ISIS are not simply victims of American policy. Iraqis faced both the disastrous U.S. invasion and brutal insurgent movements. Yemen’s catastrophe involves American support for Saudi action, but also local conflict. A mature critique should hold the United States accountable without making it the sole author of Muslim suffering.

The courts’ recent intervention against the travel ban shows both the promise and limits of legalism. The judiciary is more willing to check Trump’s crudely discriminatory order than it has been to examine ordinary national security practices. That inconsistency is real. But it is also an opportunity. Legal arguments against the ban have opened public space to discuss religious animus, due process, statutory limits, and the meaning of equal citizenship. Civil liberties lawyers should use that space to challenge watch lists, border abuses, and surveillance. The solution to judicial failure is not to abandon courts but to press them harder, while organizing outside them.

The lead essay asks Americans not to be satisfied with opposing Trump’s most visible excesses. That is the right demand. But I would frame the next step as constitutional reconstruction rather than constitutional disillusionment. We need a renewed commitment to principles that have been selectively honored: no religious tests, no guilt by association, no secret punishment without meaningful review, no criminalization of belief, no exile by executive fiat. These are not naïve ideals. They are tools, imperfect but real, that movements can sharpen.

The airport crowds mattered because they said that Muslims are part of “we the people.” That claim must grow broader and braver. It must defend not only the sympathetic traveler but the controversial speaker; not only the patriotic soldier’s father but the antiwar imam; not only the assimilated professional but the conservative believer. Still, the language of liberty and equal protection can help us get there. The Constitution is not self-enforcing, and it has often been betrayed. But in the hands of those whom power would exclude, it can become less a romantic filter than a fighting instrument.
