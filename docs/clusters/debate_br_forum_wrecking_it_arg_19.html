"While Zittrain correctly identifies the security threats facing the generative internet, his proposal to allow ISPs to screen for malware at the network level is a dangerous violation of the end-to-end neutrality principle that would inevitably lead to corporate and governmental censorship."

Jonathan Zittrain’s essay is a masterwork of even-handed analysis—right up to the moment he loosens his grip on the single most important architectural principle the Internet has ever known: end-to-end neutrality. He correctly identifies the threat that botnets and malware pose to the open, generative Net, and he thoughtfully proposes a suite of tools to empower users to reclaim their own security. But when he suggests that “strict loyalty to end-to-end neutrality should give way” and that ISPs might screen out viruses at the network level, he opens a gate that no amount of good intentions can later close. The cure he offers for the Internet’s ills is, in this one crucial respect, more dangerous than the disease.

The power and the beauty of the end-to-end principle are not merely technical. They are political. By pushing all the intelligence and decision-making to the endpoints—the PCs and servers operated by users and content creators—the Internet’s design inherently limits the capacity of network operators to discriminate among the bits they carry. It is this very dumbness that has prevented Comcast, AT&T, or Verizon from becoming gatekeepers who decide which applications prosper, which political voices are heard, and which innovations are allowed to compete. The moment we accept that ISPs should actively inspect traffic in order to protect security, we hand them a mandate to build the surveillance infrastructure that makes all other forms of discrimination possible. A deep-packet inspection engine tuned to spot botnet command-and-control can, with a flick of a software switch, be tuned to throttle BitTorrent, degrade Skype, or log every website a user visits. Zittrain acknowledges the “speculative risk” of undesirable filtering, but it is not speculative at all: by 2008, Comcast had already been caught forging reset packets to disrupt peer-to-peer traffic, using exactly the kind of network management tools that anti-malware screening would require.

Zittrain’s gesture toward ISP-level screening is an understandable response to a genuine crisis. Botnets are ravaging unsophisticated endpoints; the user cannot be trusted to maintain her machine. But the solution is not to shift the intelligence into the network’s core, where it will be controlled by a handful of corporate actors with incentives very different from the public good. The solution, which Zittrain himself brilliantly outlines in the first half of his argument, is to give the end-user better tools without compromising the end-to-end architecture. Virtual machines, easy reversion, community-based reputation systems—these all enhance the generative power of endpoints without creating a centralized chokepoint.

Consider an alternative path that honors the endpoint principle entirely. Rather than screening viruses at the ISP gateway, we can require ISPs to provide a simple, opt-in firewall service at the customer’s premises—running on the homeowner’s router or cable modem—that the user can configure and that is under her ultimate control. Better yet, we can mandate that consumer routers ship with robust, user-friendly security features that alert the owner when an unusual pattern of outbound traffic is detected, turning each household into a miniature, autonomous security operations center. Zittrain’s own Herdict project could feed data to these home routers, helping them recognize botnet signatures without any centralized inspection in the ISP’s cloud. The network remains dumb; the endpoints become smarter.

What about the argument that users are too lazy or clueless to manage such features? This is the purist’s dilemma, and Zittrain wrestles with it honestly. He fears that if we do not act at the network level, desperate consumers will abandon the endpoint entirely, fleeing to locked-down appliances. That is a real risk, but it is one that can be met by regulatory creativity rather than by abandoning the architectural principle that makes the generative internet worth saving. For instance, the Federal Trade Commission could require that any broadband connection sold to a residential consumer include, as a condition of service, a secure home gateway device maintained by the ISP but whose filtering policies are set by the user and informed by a neutral, nonprofit clearinghouse like StopBadware. The ISP remains a dumb pipe; it merely provides the equipment, while the intelligence and policy reside at the user’s premises and under a transparent governance structure. This divides the control between the user and a trusted community entity, not between the user and a profit-seeking network operator.

Zittrain worries about the “glacial death of a thousand cuts” if we fail to act. I worry more about the sudden death of a thousand filters. Once the principle that ISPs should screen out malicious code is enshrined, the political pressure to expand that mandate will be unstoppable. Hollywood will demand that ISPs screen out copyrighted files. Repressive governments will demand that ISPs screen out dissident content, and they will point to the West’s own network-screening practices as justification. The end-to-end Internet, which has been the greatest engine of free expression and economic innovation in human history, will be Balkanized into a series of gated communities where the gatekeepers owe allegiance to shareholders and politicians, not to the users.

Generativity, as Zittrain beautifully demonstrates, is the product of a network that treats all code bits as equal, that does not prejudge which experiments are worthy and which are not. ISP-level screening, however narrowly justified today, is a crack in that edifice. Water finds the crack, and so does power. I do not doubt Zittrain’s good faith; indeed, his essay shows a rare and nuanced appreciation for the stakes. But I would urge him and his readers to treat end-to-end neutrality not as a fetish to be relaxed, but as a load-bearing wall. The house of the generative internet can be renovated, modernized, and secured from the inside, with better locks on the doors and smarter occupants. It cannot survive the removal of a wall that keeps out the very forces—corporate and governmental—that long to own the network and everything on it.
