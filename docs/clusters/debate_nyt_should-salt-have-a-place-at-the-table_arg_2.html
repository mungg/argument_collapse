"Salt shakers should not be mandatory on every table, but they must be readily available to diners upon request without judgment."

The argument over salt shakers is really an argument over trust. Do we trust chefs to season food properly? Do chefs trust customers not to vandalize dinner? Do public health advocates trust individuals to make reasonable choices? The answer in each case should be: somewhat, but not absolutely.

The best compromise is simple: salt should not have to be on every table, but it should always be available quickly and without judgment.

There is no universal rule that fits a diner, a steakhouse, a neighborhood café, a hospital cafeteria, a sushi bar and a three-star tasting-menu restaurant. At a casual breakfast spot, removing the salt shaker feels silly. Eggs, potatoes and tomatoes are exactly the sorts of foods people season to taste, and the table is part of the cooking process. At a chef-driven restaurant where each course is composed, it is reasonable not to clutter the table with condiments. The dining experience there is partly an agreement to try the kitchen’s judgment.

But restaurants should be careful not to confuse artistry with domination. Customers are not children. A person with an unusually low-sodium diet may find ordinary restaurant food too salty; another person may need more seasoning to perceive the dish as flavorful. A chef cannot know every palate in the room. And there is something ungracious about making a guest feel unsophisticated for asking for salt.

The public health case, meanwhile, is important but limited. America’s sodium problem will not be solved by hiding shakers. Sodium is embedded in breads, cheeses, cured meats, canned goods, commercial sauces and restaurant stocks. A table without salt may look virtuous while the entrée still contains a day’s worth of sodium. If restaurants want credit for health-conscious cooking, they should be transparent about ingredients and moderate sodium in the kitchen.

So the rule should be neither “salt everywhere” nor “salt nowhere.” Taste first. Ask if needed. Provide it politely. That preserves the chef’s intention, respects the diner’s autonomy and avoids pretending that a missing shaker is a public health revolution.

I would be happy never to see a salt shaker automatically placed on a restaurant table again.

Part of the pleasure of restaurant dining is being cooked for. That means allowing the kitchen to make decisions: how long to cook the fish, how acidic the vinaigrette should be, whether the soup needs more reduction, how much salt belongs in the beans. We do not demand a sauté pan so we can adjust the browning ourselves. Salt is different only because habit has made it seem like a right.

The old table shaker also encourages a bad ritual. Many people salt food before tasting it. They do the same with pepper, shaking it over everything as though the kitchen forgot to finish the job. In some restaurants, the absence of the shaker is a useful pause. It says: try the food first. If it still needs something, ask.

This is not snobbery. It is attentiveness. Salt is powerful. A little can make tomatoes bloom; a little too much can flatten a sauce and make every subsequent bite taste the same. Chefs and line cooks are not infallible, but they are at least seasoning with the whole dish in mind. The diner with a shaker is usually seasoning only the surface.

There is a health reason, too. The medical debate over exactly how low sodium targets should go is complicated, and not everyone responds to sodium in the same way. Still, it is hard to deny that Americans’ sodium intake is generally high. A restaurant need not become a clinic to stop nudging customers toward more.

The key word is “automatically.” If a guest asks for salt, bring it. No raised eyebrow, no lecture, no ten-minute wait. But the table setting should not presume that the chef’s food requires correction. Removing the shaker is a modest, reasonable update to dining etiquette: taste first, trust the kitchen initially, and adjust only when necessary.

"Whether a salt shaker belongs on a restaurant table depends on the establishment's culinary skill and the social contract of the dining experience, rather than a universal rule."

The salt shaker debate is a proxy for a larger argument about who is in charge at the restaurant table: the person cooking the food or the person paying for it. Framed that way, the answer seems obvious—the customer. But the framing itself is wrong. A good meal is a collaboration, and collaboration requires that each party respect what the other brings.

I've eaten in restaurants where the chef removed the shaker and the food was indeed flawlessly seasoned. I've also eaten in restaurants where the chef removed the shaker and the food was bland, and I had to perform the small humiliation of asking a server for salt while the chef silently judged me from the open kitchen. The practice only works if the kitchen earns it. Most kitchens haven't.

There's a public health dimension that deserves a moment, too. Americans consume roughly 3,400 milligrams of sodium a day, well above recommended levels, and the overwhelming majority of that comes from processed and restaurant food—not from the shaker. If a restaurant is genuinely concerned about its customers' sodium intake, the meaningful intervention happens in the kitchen, not on the tabletop. Removing the shaker while serving a dish containing 2,000 milligrams of sodium is health theater.

So my answer is pragmatic. In a casual restaurant—a diner, a bistro, a neighborhood place—the shaker belongs on the table, because the food is meant to be customized and the social contract assumes it. In a restaurant where the chef has earned the trust through demonstrable skill, removing the shaker is a defensible artistic choice, provided salt is cheerfully available on request. What is not defensible is hiding the shaker as a gesture of self-importance, while doing nothing to ensure the food actually doesn't need it.

Let the chef cook.

You wouldn't walk into a concert hall and ask the conductor to play it again with more cello. You wouldn't visit a painter's studio and offer to brighten the reds. Yet diners routinely sit down at restaurants—including ones where the chef has spent decades perfecting a craft—and reach for the shaker before the first bite has even hit their tongue. This is not a defense of customer preference. It's a habit, often an unconscious one, and habits are worth interrupting.

Removing the salt shaker is one of the cheapest, most effective ways a restaurant can encourage diners to actually taste the food in front of them. It is not a ban; it is a pause. Anyone who wants salt can ask, and any decent restaurant will provide it without an eyeroll. What is gained is a moment of attention, which is the most valuable currency in eating well.

There's also a public health case, modest but real. Sodium consumption in this country is a measurable problem, linked to hypertension and cardiovascular disease, and while the shaker isn't the principal villain, defaults shape behavior. Cafeterias that move the salt from each table to a single station see consumption drop. Small frictions matter.

The objection that this is paternalistic confuses removing a default with removing a choice. The shaker isn't outlawed; it's relocated. The diner remains sovereign. What changes is that seasoning becomes a deliberate act rather than a reflex. That seems like a small price for better-tasting food and slightly healthier hearts.

The Salt Shaker Shouldn’t Vanish — It Should Wait in the Wings The absolutism on both sides of the salt debate misses the point: the shaker’s place is not on the table by default, but it should never be exiled. The ideal restaurant strikes a balance between trusting the chef and honoring the diner, and that balance can be achieved with a simple, elegant compromise: salt by request.

The kitchen’s argument holds real weight. A carefully composed dish is a fragile ecosystem. When a server brings a plate of hand-cut tagliatelle with a ragu simmered for hours, the salinity has been dialed in with intention. Pre-emptively dumping table salt over it is a bit like someone taking a Sharpie to a painting because they prefer bolder lines. Removing the shaker from automatic deployment forces a moment of mindfulness: taste first, season second. That’s a habit we’d all benefit from.

Yet the absolutist chef’s position can curdle into arrogance. There are legitimate reasons a diner might want more salt that have nothing to do with the kitchen’s skill. A head cold mutes flavors. A medication causes salt wasting. A preference simply runs toward the briny, and for the price of the entree, that preference shouldn’t be pathologized. Moreover, the performative withholding of salt often feels like a scolding, especially when the restaurant cuts corners elsewhere — serving a wine by-the-glass that’s been open too long or bread that’s barely a step above supermarket white.

The solution is the quiet offer. A server who sees an untouched plate might ask, “Would you like some flaky salt?” A small dish of Maldon or fleur de sel brought to the table elevates the experience far more than a grimy shaker of free-flowing iodized crystals ever could. The question itself is a gesture of hospitality, affirming that the diner’s satisfaction, not the chef’s ego, is the ultimate goal. Let the shaker disappear, but keep the salt within arm’s reach, ready to be invited to the party.

It is time to retire the salt shaker from the default restaurant table.

That does not mean salt is evil. Salt is one of cooking’s essential tools. When a diner dumps salt on top of a finished plate, the effect is not the same. It is surface salt: sharp, crude and usually uneven. The chef’s objection is not merely vanity. Removing the shaker is a small way of asking diners to taste first.

Health considerations strengthen the case. Sodium reduction is not a fad; it is a legitimate public health goal in a country with high rates of hypertension and heart disease. True, most sodium comes from processed and prepared foods, not from table salt. But restaurants are part of that prepared-food environment. If the salt shaker is treated as an automatic table ornament, it reinforces the idea that more salt is the normal finish to a meal.

None of this requires cruelty or arrogance. If a diner asks for salt, the restaurant should provide it graciously. But “available on request” is different from “always at the table.” We do not place bowls of sugar on every dinner plate or bottles of soy sauce next to every soup. The default matters. Let the kitchen do its work, let the diner taste the food, and let salt be a request rather than a reflex.

The reasonable middle path, which many good restaurants have already found, is this: don't put a shaker on the table by default, but have one available, and bring a small dish of good finishing salt if the kitchen anticipates a guest might want it. If asked, provide it without fuss or condescension.

This isn't about depriving customers. It's about a small shift in defaults that nudges people to taste before they season—a habit that almost always improves the meal. The shaker doesn't need to be banished. It just doesn't need to sit there, like a smoke detector, signaling that something might go wrong.

That's not gastronomy; it's hierarchy. And what's also not legitimate, on the other side, is salting food before tasting it, then complaining the dish was over- or under-seasoned. Both diner and chef have responsibilities here.

So: sometimes yes, sometimes no, and in all cases, please—taste first.
