"While offshore balancing is a superior alternative to global hegemony, it cannot succeed unless the United States achieves energy independence to break its strategic and economic dependency on Middle Eastern oil."

Stephen M. Walt’s essay, “In the National Interest,” provides a rigorous critique of the Bush administration’s foreign policy and a compelling argument for “offshore balancing.” He accurately diagnoses the exhaustion of the American military and the disastrous consequences of the Iraq war. Yet, Walt’s prescription suffers from a fundamental blind spot. He treats the Persian Gulf as one of the three “vital areas” of the globe, alongside Europe and Asia, requiring American engagement to prevent a hostile hegemon. But Walt never interrogates *why* the Persian Gulf is vital. He accepts it as a geopolitical given. In doing so, he ignores the actual root cause of our strategic overextension: America’s crippling addiction to petroleum.

Writing in 2005, as oil prices push past $50 a barrel and American soldiers die securing supply lines in the Middle East, it is astonishing that a sweeping treatise on grand strategy barely mentions energy. Walt advocates for a return to a "balance-of-power policy" in the Middle East to "ensure that no one state is able to dominate the others." But we must ask: why does it matter if one state dominates the Middle East? It matters only because that state would control a massive share of the world’s oil reserves, granting it the power to blackmail the global economy.

Offshore balancing, in this context, is just a cheaper way to manage our fossil fuel dependency. It means we will still arm unsavory regimes like Saudi Arabia to balance against Iran. It means our naval fleets will still patrol the Strait of Hormuz. It means our foreign policy will remain perpetually hostage to the internal stability of despotic petro-states. Walt wants to reduce our "footprint," but as long as our economy requires 20 million barrels of oil a day, our footprint in the sands of the Middle East will remain dangerously heavy.

A truly visionary grand strategy would recognize that energy independence—or at least a massive diversification away from petroleum—is the ultimate prerequisite for offshore balancing. If we want to genuinely play "hard-to-get" in the Middle East, as Walt suggests, we have to actually not need what they are selling.

Furthermore, Walt treats global warming as a secondary issue, useful mainly for public diplomacy. He suggests the U.S. should have offered a "fair-minded proposal" instead of abruptly abandoning the Kyoto Protocol, largely to avoid looking like a "rogue superpower." This fundamentally misunderstands the strategic landscape of the 21st century. Climate change is not a PR problem; it is a supreme national security threat.

The Pentagon itself has warned that climate change could bring severe resource shortages, massive migrations, and increased conflict over arable land and fresh water. If sea levels rise and weather patterns shift drastically, the resulting geopolitical chaos will render Walt’s neat "balance of power" models entirely obsolete. Nations will not be fighting for regional hegemony; they will be fighting for survival.

If American primacy is the goal, then the taproot of that primacy over the next century will be the transition to a post-carbon economy. The nation that pioneers the technologies for clean, renewable energy will dominate the global market, free itself from the extortion of petro-dictators, and wield immense soft power.

Instead of investing hundreds of billions of dollars in maintaining carrier strike groups to protect Middle Eastern shipping lanes, we should be launching a massive, Apollo-style national project for energy innovation. We should be investing heavily in nuclear, solar, and wind technologies, and aggressively raising fuel efficiency standards for our domestic auto industry.

Walt briefly acknowledges that offshore balancing doesn't preclude using power for humanitarian ends, but he misses how energy policy shapes those very crises. We fund the terrorism we fight every time we fill up our gas tanks. The wealth of the Saudi royal family, which funds the extremist Wahhabi madrasas that ideologically arm al Qaeda, comes directly from American consumers. By ignoring the energy equation, Walt’s strategy attempts to mop up the floor while ignoring the overflowing sink.

Walt is entirely correct that the neoconservative project of armed democratization is a disaster, and that preventive war sets a terrifying global precedent. His critique of our unconditional support for Israel and its impact on Arab public opinion is also brave and necessary. But his overall strategic framework is anchored in the 20th century.

We cannot simply balance our way out of the Middle East. As long as our economic lifeblood flows through the Persian Gulf, we will be forced to intervene, manipulate, and occupy, regardless of whether we call it "hegemony" or "offshore balancing." The only true way to husband American power, reduce anti-American resentment, and secure our long-term future is to break the link between our national security and fossil fuels. Until we change what powers our nation, we will never truly be able to change our foreign policy.
