"The proposed strategy of offshore balancing is a dangerous abdication of leadership that would leave the United States vulnerable and surrender global influence to hostile powers."

I must respectfully dissent from Stephen Walt's core argument. While his critique of the Bush administration's Iraq policy has merit, his proposed alternative—offshore balancing—would surrender American influence precisely when it matters most and leave the United States vulnerable to a world hewing to rules we did not make.

Walt's case against global hegemony rests heavily on the Iraq experience, but Iraq is not the norm; it is an exception. The Bush administration's execution was flawed, but the underlying strategic logic—removing a regime that had twice attacked its neighbors, violated UN resolutions for over a decade, and sought weapons of mass destruction—remained sound. Saddam Hussein was not a paper tiger; he had demonstrated genuine aggression and had every incentive to acquire nuclear weapons once his nuclear program was destroyed in 1991. Preventing the rehabilitation of Iraqi WMD capacity was a legitimate American interest.

Walt's alternative—continuing UN inspections indefinitely—was never realistic. Weapons inspectors cannot verify the absence of something that might be hidden; they can only confirm what they find. Saddam's regime had a demonstrated capacity for deception and a long history of evading international monitoring. The inspections regime was a palliative, not a solution. Walt's suggestion that the United States should have waited for international support before acting ignores the reality that international support often never comes—or comes too late.

The broader critique of preventive war conflates two distinct concepts. Preemption—the use of force to prevent an imminent attack—is legitimate under international law. Preventive war—acting against a non-imminent threat—may be unwise in most circumstances, but it is not categorically impermissible. Walt seems to argue that the United States should never use force unless it is directly threatened, but this rule would have forbidden the 1991 Gulf War (where Iraq had not directly threatened the United States) and might have forbidden action against Nazi Germany before 1939. Strategic necessity sometimes requires action before threats fully materialize.

On nuclear policy, Walt's grand bargain is dangerously naïve. Iran and North Korea are not moderate states that will respond to American conciliation; they are regimes with strong incentives to acquire nuclear weapons regardless of American behavior. Iran's theocratic leadership views nuclear weapons as both a status symbol and a deterrent against American pressure. North Korea's hereditary dictatorship sees nuclear weapons as its only guarantee of survival. Offering concessions in exchange for nuclear abandonment invites blackmail, not denuclearization.

The critique of American support for Israel is similarly mistaken. Yes, unconditional support has costs in Arab public opinion, but Walt underestimates the alternative. A hostile Israel allied with American enemies would be far worse for American interests than current anti-Americanism. Moreover, Walt's implicit suggestion that America should pressure Israel ignores the deep domestic political commitment to Israeli security and the legitimate strategic reasons for that commitment. Israel is America's most reliable ally in a critical region; abandoning or pressuring Israel would be a historic strategic blunder.

Offshore balancing is not a strategy; it is an abdication. It would cede the field to others—perhaps China in Asia, perhaps Russia in Europe—who do not share American values or interests. Walt correctly notes that American power is most effective when seen as legitimate, but legitimacy comes not only from restraint but from success. The United States must be willing to act, even unilaterally if necessary, to protect its vital interests. Walt's essay is elegantly argued but dangerously wrong.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Iraq War was a justified attempt to address a legitimate threat rather than a failure of global hegemony, as inspections were an unrealistic long-term solution for a deceptive regime.
  2. 02The categorical rejection of preventive war is strategically flawed because national security sometimes requires acting against threats before they become imminent.
  3. 03Offering concessions to regimes like Iran and North Korea in a 'grand bargain' is naive, as these states are driven by internal survival logic rather than reactions to American policy.
  4. 04Pressuring or distancing the United States from Israel would be a historic blunder that ignores both domestic political realities and the strategic value of America's most reliable regional ally.
  5. 05Withdrawing to an offshore position would create power vacuums that would be filled by rivals like China and Russia who do not share American values or interests.
