Diversified Gemini

"A truly revitalized left populism in the United States must abandon national chauvinism and center radical internationalism and anti-imperialism as inseparable from domestic economic justice."

Adom Getachew’s “Reclaiming Populism” arrives at a precipice. Writing in the chaotic early weeks of the COVID-19 pandemic, she correctly diagnoses that the left’s domestic ambitions have crashed against the rocks of the Democratic establishment, yet she holds out hope for a revitalized, multiracial populism. However, Getachew’s most crucial intervention—and the one that the American left must internalize if it is to survive the coming decades—is her insistence that the failure of left populism in the United States "must be reconsidered in a global frame." For too long, the American progressive movement has attempted to build a social democracy at home while turning a blind eye to, or even actively participating in, the mechanics of US imperialism abroad. A true left populism cannot be built on the spoils of empire; it must be inherently and fiercely internationalist.

The historical legacy of populism in the United States is deeply intertwined with nationalism and imperial expansion. From the agrarian populists of the late 19th century who supported the Spanish-American War to secure foreign markets for their crops, to the New Deal coalition that established domestic prosperity on a foundation of post-WWII global hegemony, American populism has often functioned as a pact: the working class receives a larger share of the pie at home, provided they do not challenge the exploitation of the Global South abroad.

Even the recent iteration of left populism under Bernie Sanders struggled to fully break from this paradigm. While Sanders offered a refreshing critique of "endless wars," his foreign policy vision often defaulted to a kind of progressive isolationism rather than genuine anti-imperial solidarity. The campaign’s primary focus was always domestic redistribution—Medicare for All, free college, a Green New Deal. But in a deeply interconnected global economy, where the wealth of the Global North is fundamentally dependent on the extraction of labor and resources from the Global South, attempting to implement a Nordic-style social democracy in the heart of the empire without dismantling that empire is an exercise in chauvinism.

Getachew’s mention of the November 2019 coup against Evo Morales in Bolivia is the perfect illustration of this blind spot. Morales’s Movimiento Al Socialismo (MAS) represented one of the most successful left-populist, Indigenous-led movements in modern history, significantly reducing poverty and asserting sovereign control over Bolivia’s natural resources, including its massive lithium reserves. When Morales was ousted in a violent, right-wing military coup, the reaction from the American political establishment was predictably supportive. Shockingly, however, large segments of the American liberal and progressive media either equivocated or tacitly endorsed the coup, parroting discredited Organization of American States (OAS) claims of electoral fraud. The inability of the US left to mount a massive, unified resistance to a US-backed coup against a socialist leader in the Americas revealed the shallowness of its internationalism.

The COVID-19 pandemic has brutally exposed the lethal consequences of this global inequality and US hegemony. As Getachew points out, the Trump administration’s refusal to lift crushing economic sanctions on countries like Iran and Venezuela during a global health crisis is an act of economic warfare that is costing thousands of lives. Furthermore, the global scramble for medical supplies resembles a grim imperial hunger games, where wealthy nations pirate shipments of masks and ventilators bound for poorer countries.

If the American left is to reclaim a populism worth fighting for, it must center the abolition of US imperialism as a core demand, inseparable from domestic economic justice. What does this mean in practice? It means that a Green New Deal cannot rely on the neo-colonial extraction of lithium and cobalt from South America and Africa to build electric vehicles for American commuters. It means that the demand to defund the police domestically must be coupled with the demand to defund the Pentagon and close the 800+ US military bases circling the globe. It means actively fighting for debt cancellation for the Global South, allowing developing nations to invest in public health rather than servicing predatory loans to Western financial institutions.

Unfortunately, the presumptive Democratic nominee, Joe Biden, represents a complete restoration of the bipartisan imperial consensus. Biden’s foreign policy record is a litany of catastrophic interventions, and his campaign is currently attempting to out-flank Trump from the right on China, producing jingoistic attack ads that fuel a terrifying rise in anti-Asian racism and set the stage for a new Cold War. A left populism that merely seeks to pressure Biden on domestic tax rates while acquiescing to his hawkish foreign policy is a moral failure.

The COVID-19 crisis has demonstrated that we live in an indivisible world. A virus that emerged in Wuhan brought the global economy to a halt in a matter of months. Climate change, the next great crisis, will respect no borders. The American left must realize that the "people" in a 21st-century left populism cannot be constrained by national boundaries. The working class of Chicago has more in common with the striking garment workers in Bangladesh or the lithium miners in Bolivia than they do with the billionaire class of their own country. Reclaiming populism requires abandoning the dream of social democracy in one country and embracing a radical, unapologetic internationalism. We must build a global popular front, because in the face of globalized capital and global crises, our solidarity is our only defense.

  2. 02Recent progressive movements, including the Bernie Sanders campaign, have failed to move beyond a 'progressive isolationism' that prioritizes domestic redistribution while ignoring the imperialist extraction required to fund it.
  3. 03The American left's weak response to the US-backed coup against Evo Morales in Bolivia demonstrates a shallow commitment to international solidarity.
  4. 04The COVID-19 pandemic and the climate crisis illustrate that global inequality and US hegemony are lethal, necessitating a globalized view of 'the people' that transcends national borders.
  5. 05Domestic policy goals like the Green New Deal or defunding the police are morally and practically incomplete unless they are coupled with demands to end neo-colonial resource extraction and dismantle the US military apparatus.
  6. 06The current Democratic establishment represents a restoration of the bipartisan imperial consensus that the left must actively oppose rather than merely pressure on domestic issues.
