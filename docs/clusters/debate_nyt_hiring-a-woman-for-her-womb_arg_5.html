"To ensure surrogacy is ethical and safe, commercial surrogacy should be prohibited in favor of tightly regulated altruistic arrangements that prioritize the surrogate's bodily autonomy and prevent economic exploitation."

We should be more willing to say that some things should not be for sale. Commercial surrogacy asks us to accept that a woman’s reproductive capacity can be rented by people with more money than she has. That bargain may be described in the language of choice, but in many cases it is choice under economic pressure.

The analogy to ordinary work is weak. Pregnancy is not like house painting or accounting. It involves major bodily change, pain, medical risk, emotional attachment, social stigma in some communities and possible long-term consequences. A contract signed before pregnancy cannot anticipate what it will feel like to carry a fetus for nine months, to undergo a complicated birth, or to be asked to terminate or reduce a pregnancy because the commissioning parents want a different outcome. The Baby M case remains a warning: when law tries to force human reproduction into a commercial contract, it often discovers that the people involved are not widgets.

This does not mean infertile people, gay couples or others who seek surrogacy are selfish. The desire for a child is powerful and often heartbreaking. But a compassionate desire does not automatically justify a market. Markets tend to follow inequality. If the typical pattern is that affluent parents hire poorer women to bear children, especially across borders, then the practice magnifies class and global disparities. The fact that a surrogate may prefer this option to worse-paid work does not settle the moral question. Poverty can make exploitation look like opportunity.

The safest and most ethical policy would prohibit commercial surrogacy while allowing tightly regulated altruistic surrogacy among adults who already have a relationship, with reimbursement for actual expenses. Such arrangements should include counseling, independent legal advice, medical screening and a waiting period. The surrogate should retain control over all medical decisions during pregnancy and should have access to care after birth. Courts should determine parentage in a way that protects the child and respects informed consent.

Critics will say prohibition drives the practice underground. That is a real concern, but it is not an argument for surrendering every contested human relationship to the market. We prohibit organ sales despite long waiting lists because we recognize that bodily need and poverty are a dangerous combination. Surrogacy deserves a similar caution.

A society can support family creation without authorizing womb rental. Adoption reform, fertility coverage, support for childless adults and noncommercial arrangements are better paths than building an industry on women’s reproductive labor.

"Commercial surrogacy should be prohibited because it commodifies the female body and exploits economic inequality, and it should be replaced by a strictly regulated altruistic model."

The phrase “hiring a woman for her womb” is harsh, but it captures something that polite language often hides. Commercial surrogacy asks one woman, usually less wealthy than the intended parents, to assume the medical risks and emotional burdens of pregnancy so that others may obtain a child. Calling the arrangement “choice” does not settle the question. Many choices are made under economic pressure, and pregnancy is not like renting a car or contracting for home repairs.

The risks are real: preeclampsia, hemorrhage, surgery, infertility, even death. There are also less measurable losses — months of restricted life, possible pressure over diet and sex, intrusive monitoring, and the wrenching experience of surrendering a baby one has carried. The fact that some surrogates report satisfaction should not blind us to the structure of the transaction. In a marketplace, the customer’s desire tends to dominate.

Cross-border surrogacy makes the problem sharper. Wealthy foreigners travel to poorer countries because prices are lower and regulation weaker. The recent Baby Gammy controversy in Thailand, whatever its disputed facts, exposed the ugliness of a system in which children, disability and parental obligation can become bargaining issues. India’s booming surrogacy trade has raised similar concerns about clinics, hostels and middlemen profiting from poor women’s reproductive labor.

The ethical answer is not better advertising copy or thicker contracts. Commercial surrogacy should be prohibited, including paid brokerage. No contract should be enforceable if it requires a woman to surrender a child or limits her medical choices during pregnancy. If a woman wishes to carry a child altruistically for a sister, friend or close relative, the law can allow it under strict safeguards: counseling, independent counsel, payment of expenses only, medical insurance and judicial review. Final consent to transfer legal parenthood should occur after birth, when the woman is no longer pregnant and under the unique pressures of the arrangement.

This approach will disappoint some intended parents. But no one has a right to a child at the cost of making another person’s pregnancy a commodity. Society should instead improve adoption, expand infertility treatment coverage where appropriate, and support women economically so that renting one’s reproductive capacity is not presented as opportunity. Some markets are too intimate and too unequal to be made ethical.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The physical and emotional risks of pregnancy, such as preeclampsia and the trauma of surrendering a child, are too significant to be treated as a commercial transaction.
  2. 02Economic pressure often compromises the true autonomy of women in the surrogacy market, particularly in developing countries where regulation is weak.
  3. 03The commercialization of surrogacy turns children and parental obligations into bargaining chips, leading to ethical crises like the abandonment of disabled infants.
  4. 04Surrogacy contracts should never be legally enforceable if they strip a woman of her medical autonomy or her right to keep the child after birth.
  5. 05Society should prioritize improving adoption and infertility treatments rather than validating the market-based 'right' to a child at the expense of another person's dignity.
