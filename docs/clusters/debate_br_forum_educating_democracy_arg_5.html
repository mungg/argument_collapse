"The solution to the failures of bureaucratic school systems is not local democratic control within the public system, but rather a system of pluralism and parental choice where public funding follows the student."

Deborah Meier’s critique of state-imposed standardization is persuasive in one respect: the large bureaucratic school system is incapable of producing the kind of education Americans want. It is too remote, too rule-bound, too indifferent to families, and too inclined to treat children as units. Her small schools show what can happen when adults have authority and parents choose a coherent educational community.

But Meier draws the wrong institutional lesson. She wants more local democratic control inside the public system. I would put greater weight on pluralism, competition, and parental choice. The problem with the standards movement is not only that it centralizes authority in state agencies. The problem is that it is an attempt to repair a monopoly without giving families meaningful exit.

A school that must attract families has a different relationship to the public than a school that receives children by assignment. It must explain itself. It must develop a character. It must persuade. Meier’s own examples—Central Park East, Mission Hill, Boston’s pilot schools—depend heavily on choice. Families and teachers join because they want that kind of school. The shared culture she praises is not produced by a district attendance zone; it is produced by voluntary association.

This is why charter schools have attracted such broad interest in the 1990s. At their best, they break the deadening bargain of conventional public education: centralized rules in exchange for guaranteed enrollment. A charter school receives public funds, agrees to basic public obligations, and gains freedom over staffing, schedule, curriculum, and mission. If it fails, it can be closed. If families dislike it, they can leave. This is a more promising form of accountability than making every school march through the same state framework.

Meier worries, reasonably, that privatization and for-profit “safe schools” may use standards as cover for pushing out difficult children. But the same danger exists in district systems. The question is not public versus private in the abstract; it is whether rules are clear, information is public, and families have real options. A district school can exclude by neglect as surely as a private contractor can exclude by design. A child trapped in a failing assigned school has not been protected by public ownership.

Choice alone is not enough. Markets can be cruel, and education is not like buying shoes. Children need continuity; parents need reliable information; schools may try to avoid costly students. Public funding must therefore come with civil-rights protections, admissions fairness, financial transparency, and consequences for fraud or abuse. But these safeguards should not become an excuse to preserve a system in which nobody at the school level can make important decisions.

Where do standards and tests fit? They are useful as information, dangerous as command. Parents choosing among schools need some comparable evidence. Graduation rates, attendance, safety, college-going, course offerings, parent satisfaction, and test results all matter. Standardized tests are imperfect, but they can reveal whether a school that advertises warmth and creativity is also teaching reading and mathematics. The mistake is to make test scores the sole definition of quality or to prescribe identical methods for raising them.

A genuine choice system would allow different schools to pursue different missions. Some would look like Meier’s democratic communities. Others might emphasize classical learning, direct instruction, Afrocentric curriculum, bilingual education, science and technology, Montessori methods, religious education if vouchers are included, or strict discipline. Families disagree about what children need. A free society should not force all of them into one pedagogical settlement.

Meier invokes democracy, but democracy is not only deliberation in local meetings. It is also the freedom of citizens to form institutions reflecting their convictions. The United States has always contained educational pluralism: common schools, Catholic schools, independent schools, home schooling, magnet programs, and now charters. Public policy should harness that pluralism rather than suppress it under statewide uniformity.

The hardest issue is equity. Critics of choice argue that advantaged parents will navigate options better, leaving the poor behind. That is a real risk. But the current system already gives affluent families choice through real estate and tuition. They buy into good districts or pay private school fees. Poor families are the ones most likely to be stuck. A well-designed choice system—with transportation, outreach, weighted funding for disadvantaged students, and lotteries when schools are oversubscribed—could extend to them some of the freedom the middle class already exercises.

Meier is right that small schools with strong adult relationships can transform children’s lives. But we should not depend on school boards or state departments to create them from above. Let educators found schools. Let parents choose them. Let money follow students, with extra money for those who are harder to educate. Let the state ensure safety, fairness, financial honesty, and basic public reporting. Then let schools differ.

The standards movement promises accountability by central command. The old district system promises accountability by local politics, though many parents experience that politics as closed and unresponsive. Choice promises accountability by attachment: families choose, schools commit, and both sides can judge whether the relationship is working. It is not perfect. No system is. But it is more consistent with the diversity and energy of American life than a bureaucratic effort to define one best education for all.

Meier has shown what freedom inside public education can produce. The next step is to make that freedom ordinary, not exceptional. Standards should inform parents, not govern classrooms. Public money should support children, not systems. And democracy should mean that families, especially poor families, are no longer told to wait patiently while distant authorities decide what is good for them.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Charter schools offer a superior form of accountability by breaking the monopoly of centralized rules in exchange for the freedom to innovate and the risk of closure if they fail to attract families.
  3. 03Standardized tests should be used as a tool for public information and transparency to help parents make informed choices, rather than as a mechanism for state command and control.
  4. 04A genuine choice system respects democratic pluralism by allowing families to select from diverse pedagogical approaches that reflect their specific convictions and needs.
  5. 05Educational equity is better served by providing poor families with the same 'exit' options and school choices that affluent families already exercise through real estate and private tuition.
  6. 06Public policy should focus on ensuring safety, fairness, and financial transparency while allowing educators the freedom to found schools and parents the freedom to choose them.
