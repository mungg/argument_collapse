Diversified DeepSeek

"The only way to achieve the diverse, human-scale, and accountable education Deborah Meier envisions is to dismantle the government's educational monopoly and implement a universal system of school choice where funding follows the child."

Deborah Meier is right to fear the emerging regime of state-mandated tests and centrally imposed curricula. She is right that the locus of authority has shifted shamefully away from parents and teachers toward distant officials who confuse standardization with quality. And she is certainly right that the factory-model school, with its anonymous corridors and interchangeable periods, is a disaster for human beings young and old. Yet for all her radical insight, Meier makes a fundamental error: she thinks the solution is to give the public system more internal flexibility while leaving its basic monopoly intact. That is like trying to turn a supertanker by rearranging the deck chairs. The only way to achieve the vibrant, diverse, accountable, and human-scale education she envisions is to break the link between government provision and education funding altogether—to let the money follow the child and trust families to choose their schools.

Local control, as Meier conceives it, remains public local control—control by a government entity, even if it is a small school council rather than a sprawling district bureaucracy. But a government school, no matter how small and autonomous, is ultimately subject to the same democratic processes that produced the Massachusetts frameworks and Chicago’s zero-tolerance policies. Give it a decade, and the state legislature, under pressure from the same business coalitions and academic experts Meier decries, will find a way to impose new mandates, new tests, and new standardization on those mini-schools. The logic is inescapable: when the state pays, the state calls the shots. If Mission Hill exists only because the Boston Public Schools and the teachers union have graciously allowed it to depart from some regulations, that permission is a political gift that can be withdrawn the moment the test-score winds shift.

Meier’s own essay unwittingly demonstrates why the public system cannot be trusted to sustain her vision. She notes that in the 1960s and ’70s there were over 200,000 school boards; today there are fewer than 20,000, and Miami and Los Angeles are erecting the largest high schools ever built. This consolidation of power was not a conspiracy of evil men; it was the natural outcome of a system in which decisions about education are made by majority vote in state capitols and big-city city halls. The very people who are least intimate with individual children—governors, state school chiefs, legislative staffers—end up making the rules because the funding pipeline runs through them. To expect that those same political bodies will now suddenly reverse course and empower thousands of autonomous public schools is to ignore the incentives that have driven this centralization for a century.

The genuine alternative to both mass-production schooling and top-down standards is not a patchwork of locally managed public schools but a robust system of parental choice among independent schools. The fledgling voucher experiments in Milwaukee and Cleveland, and the rapid spread of charter schools—which, though still public, enjoy substantial freedom from district rules—point in the right direction. A universal voucher or tax-credit program would allow any group of parents and educators to create the kind of school Meier runs at Mission Hill without having to beg permission from the central office. It would allow religious schools, Montessori programs, Core Knowledge academies, and progressive democratic schools to flourish side by side, each appealing to a different vision of the good life, each held accountable not by a state test but by the families who choose to stay or leave.

Meier herself acknowledges that the families at Mission Hill are there “by choice” and that this choice is central to the school’s culture. Yet she stops short of advocating a system in which all families enjoy the same power of exit. Why should the ability to escape a failing or culturally mismatched school be a privilege reserved for the lucky few who win a lottery or navigate a pilot-school bureaucracy? The same democracy she champions requires that poor parents have the same effective sovereignty over their children’s education as the affluent enjoy when they move to a suburb or write a private-school tuition check.

The objection, of course, is that a market in education would stratify society further. But Meier’s essay, for all its passion for equity, offers no concrete strategy for narrowing the resource gap she admits is the primary national responsibility. State-level funding formulas can address inequities regardless of whether the schools are public or private, as long as the money is attached to the child and weighted for special needs. Under a properly designed choice system, low-income families would have far more leverage than they do now: they could walk away from a school that fails to teach their children, and the school’s funding would walk with them. That is accountability far more immediate and powerful than a once-a-year standardized test whose results produce a newspaper article and a bureaucratic sanction months later.

I part company with Meier also in her romanticism about democratic deliberation as the core educational experience. Setting local standards through endless community dialogue is noble work for those who have the time and cultural capital to engage in it; for a single mother working two jobs in Dorchester, that process is a luxury. What she needs is a school that offers a clear, coherent, and demonstrably effective curriculum, and the freedom to choose it quickly. Meier’s school thrives because she and her colleagues are exceptional, but the history of utopian community governance suggests that, scaled up, such models often become dominated by the most vocal and connected, not the most disadvantaged. A market, for all its imperfections, at least gives the individual family a private, low-cost vote: re-enroll or leave.

Meier pleads for more “patience,” “confidence in the good sense of the people,” and “commitment to democracy.” I share that faith. But real democracy is not primarily exercised through school board meetings and staff-family suppers; it is exercised through the freedom to make one’s own most intimate decisions without having to persuade a majority. The path to the diverse, human-scaled, intellectually vital education Meier desires does not lie through pleading with the same state apparatus that gave us the MCAS. It lies in dismantling that apparatus’s financial monopoly and letting a thousand Mission Hills bloom not as permitted exceptions within the public system, but as autonomous schools fully accountable to the families they serve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Publicly funded schools, even if locally managed, will always remain vulnerable to the inevitable political pressure for centralized state mandates and standardization.
  2. 02The historical trend toward school consolidation and centralization is a natural outcome of government funding incentives that cannot be reversed through internal public-sector reforms.
  3. 03A market-based system of independent schools allows for a pluralism of educational philosophies to flourish simultaneously without requiring families to win a lottery or navigate a bureaucracy.
  4. 04True equity and accountability are best achieved by giving low-income families the power of exit, allowing them to withdraw funding from failing schools immediately.
  5. 05Relying on democratic deliberation to set local standards is a luxury of the elite that often excludes disadvantaged families, whereas a choice-based system provides a more accessible form of parental sovereignty.
