"While the redirection of AI is necessary to prevent inequality and surveillance, this effort must be globalized to ensure that AI development does not entrench a new form of dependency or close off development paths for the global South."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay speaks primarily to the United States and other rich democracies. Its core warning—that AI may intensify inequality and surveillance unless redirected—is powerful. But from the perspective of developing countries, the question looks somewhat different. The danger is not only that AI will destroy good jobs in wealthy societies. It is also that the rules created by wealthy societies may entrench a global division between countries that own AI, countries that supply data and raw materials, and countries whose workers are rendered less valuable before they have had a chance to industrialize.

For much of the twentieth century, development was built on labor absorption. Countries became richer by moving workers from low-productivity agriculture into manufacturing and services. Export-oriented manufacturing, in particular, allowed countries in East Asia to create jobs, acquire capabilities, and climb the technological ladder. This path was not easy or automatic, but it offered a way for poorer countries to participate in global growth.

AI and robotics may narrow that path. If apparel, electronics assembly, logistics, call centers, business processing, and even some coding or accounting work become more automated, the comparative advantage of low-wage labor weakens. Rich-country firms may reshore production or keep more value in highly automated facilities. Multinational companies may need fewer workers in supplier countries. The result could be premature deindustrialization: countries losing manufacturing opportunities before they reach middle-income prosperity.

Acemoglu is right to worry about automation’s impact on workers in the United States. But for a worker in Bangladesh, Kenya, Vietnam, Mexico, or the Philippines, the relevant question is not simply whether AI replaces existing employment. It is whether AI prevents future employment from emerging. A technology that allows a U.S. company to produce cheaply at home may look like productivity growth in America while closing off a ladder abroad. The social cost will not be visible if we measure only domestic labor shares.

This complicates the call to redirect AI toward “human-complementary” uses. Complementing which humans? A medical AI system that empowers nurses in Boston is desirable. But if the global AI agenda is shaped entirely by the needs of rich-country professionals, it may neglect the needs of community health workers, small farmers, informal traders, and local manufacturers in poorer countries. AI for education should not only personalize lessons in well-equipped classrooms; it should help under-resourced schools with multilingual materials, teacher training, and low-connectivity environments. AI for agriculture should not only optimize industrial farms; it should support smallholders facing pests, climate variability, and limited credit. AI for finance should not only improve fraud detection for major banks; it should expand fair access to credit while avoiding predatory scoring.

The current structure of AI development is badly suited to these goals. The largest firms are headquartered in the United States and China. They control cloud infrastructure, data pipelines, computing resources, platforms, and talent. Their business models depend on advertising, e-commerce, enterprise software, surveillance, and consumer services for large markets. Poorer countries often become users rather than producers. Their languages are underrepresented in datasets. Their institutions have limited capacity to regulate. Their citizens’ data may be extracted without building local capability. Their startups may be bought or crushed by platform giants.

This is a new form of dependency. It is not colonialism in the old sense, but it can reproduce unequal power. If African, Latin American, and South Asian governments rely on foreign vendors for digital identity, policing, welfare targeting, education platforms, and health systems, they may surrender public capacity to companies that are not accountable to their citizens. If those vendors provide opaque algorithmic systems, errors and biases become difficult to challenge. If data are stored abroad or controlled by foreign cloud providers, sovereignty is weakened.

The surveillance concern is especially acute outside established democracies. Many governments want tools to monitor opponents, journalists, minorities, and social movements. AI-powered surveillance is attractive because it promises control without the visible brutality of older repression. Facial recognition, phone hacking, social media monitoring, and predictive analytics can help regimes identify dissent early. Even governments that hold elections may use these tools to tilt the playing field. The export of surveillance technology from China, Israel, Europe, and the United States to fragile democracies should be treated as a human rights issue.

But developing countries also have legitimate security and administrative needs. They face crime, tax evasion, corruption, disaster risk, and limited state capacity. Digital systems can help deliver benefits, track disease, manage infrastructure, and reduce leakage. A blanket suspicion of state technology would be unrealistic. The challenge is to build accountable capacity rather than import digital authoritarianism. That requires local democratic institutions, independent courts, civil society, data protection laws, and technical expertise inside the public sector.

Acemoglu’s analogy to climate policy is useful but incomplete. In climate negotiations, developing countries have long argued that rich countries used fossil fuels to become wealthy and now ask poorer countries to constrain emissions. Something similar could happen with AI. Rich countries benefited from earlier waves of automation and industrialization. If they now set AI standards that protect their own workers while limiting technology transfer or imposing costly compliance burdens, poorer countries may see AI governance as protectionism disguised as ethics.

This does not mean that labor and privacy standards should be abandoned. On the contrary, workers everywhere need protection. But global AI governance must include development concerns. It should support open research, affordable computing access, multilingual datasets governed by communities, public-interest AI tools, and technology transfer. International institutions should help poorer countries build regulatory capacity rather than merely pressure them to adopt standards written in Brussels, Washington, or Beijing.

There is also a need to rethink intellectual property. If AI becomes a general-purpose technology essential to health, education, agriculture, and climate adaptation, excessive concentration of patents, models, and infrastructure will harm global welfare. Publicly funded AI research should be open where possible. Models developed for public health, disaster response, and education should be treated as global public goods. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the world has seen how unequal access to technology—vaccines most dramatically—can deepen global injustice. We should not reproduce that pattern in AI.

Labor policy in developing countries must also adapt. Many workers are informal, outside standard employment relationships. Calls for collective bargaining and wage floors remain important, but they must be designed for realities of informality, migration, and small enterprises. AI could help extend social protection by improving identification and payment systems, but it could also exclude people through rigid databases and automated eligibility decisions. Welfare algorithms in countries with weak appeal systems can become instruments of bureaucratic cruelty. Human discretion, local knowledge, and grievance mechanisms are essential.

A more hopeful path is possible. AI could help community health workers diagnose conditions, assist teachers in local languages, provide farmers with weather and market information, improve disaster warning systems, map pollution, support small manufacturers with design tools, and make government services more accessible. These applications would not simply copy Silicon Valley models. They would be rooted in local problems and local institutions. They would require public investment, universities, regional cooperation, and participation from the communities affected.

This is why the question “who controls AI?” must be globalized. It is not enough to reduce the influence of American tech giants if control merely shifts to Chinese giants or to a small circle of rich-country regulators. Countries in the global South need seats at the table. Their researchers need funding. Their languages and knowledge systems need representation. Their citizens need rights over data. Their workers need a development strategy that does not assume the old manufacturing ladder will remain intact.

Acemoglu’s insistence that AI’s future is not inevitable is the right starting point. But the “we” who must choose that future cannot be limited to affluent democracies worried about their postwar compact. There was no postwar compact for much of the world; there was colonial aftermath, Cold War intervention, debt crisis, structural adjustment, and uneven development. A just AI future must not only restore shared prosperity where it has eroded. It must expand the possibility of prosperity where it has long been denied.

  2. 02The definition of 'human-complementary' AI must be expanded beyond the needs of rich-country professionals to address the specific challenges of smallholders, community health workers, and under-resourced schools in poorer regions.

  4. 04The export of AI surveillance tools to fragile democracies and authoritarian regimes in the global South poses a distinct and acute threat to international human rights.
  5. 05Global AI governance must move beyond rich-country standards to include technology transfer, open-source public goods, and the protection of informal workers.
  6. 06A just AI future requires that developing nations have the funding, representation, and regulatory capacity to shape technology according to their own local institutions and needs.
