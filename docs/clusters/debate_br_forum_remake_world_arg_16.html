"The framework of racial capitalism provides a necessary lens for contemporary social movements to move beyond liberal appeals for recognition and instead pursue the material redistribution of power through abolition and reparations."

Walter Johnson’s essay arrives at a moment when the question of justice is no longer academic, if it ever was. The Movement for Black Lives has forced the country to confront police killing, prisons, surveillance, economic abandonment, gendered violence, queer and trans vulnerability, schools, housing, health, and reparations as parts of one structure. The election of Donald Trump has revealed how fiercely that structure will defend itself. Johnson gives us a name for it: racial capitalism. But the name matters only if it helps us fight.

His first point, about “dehumanization,” speaks directly to contemporary politics. We are often told that racism begins when people fail to see others as human. There is truth in that, but it is not enough. Police officers, jailers, landlords, employers, judges, teachers, and politicians often know quite well that the people they harm are human. The officer knows the person he stops can feel fear. The prison administrator knows solitary confinement breaks minds. The landlord knows eviction can destroy a family. The employer knows a worker needs wages to live. The state knows deportation separates parents from children. The problem is not always failure of recognition. It is organized power over people whose humanity is fully available to be used against them.

Slavery makes this clear. The master did not need to imagine the enslaved as literally nonhuman. He needed to know they loved their children so he could threaten sale. He needed to know they feared pain so he could whip. He needed to know they could learn so he could train and prohibit literacy. He needed to know women could bear children so he could count future property. Slavery was not a machine that accidentally included people. It was a system designed to extract value from human capacities while denying human claims.

That insight helps explain why body cameras, diversity trainings, and calls for empathy, while sometimes useful, cannot by themselves stop police violence. The issue is not merely that officers need to see black people as human. It is that the state has built institutions that treat black life as governable through suspicion, containment, extraction, and force. A camera may record a killing without preventing it. A training may teach respectful language while leaving intact the power to stop, frisk, arrest, jail, and shoot. Recognition without redistribution of power is not justice.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism also clarifies why the prison is central to any discussion of slavery’s afterlife. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude “except as a punishment for crime.” That exception did not by itself create mass incarceration, but it marked a path. After emancipation, black codes, convict leasing, chain gangs, vagrancy laws, debt peonage, and prison labor helped reimpose coercive labor and racial control. In our own time, prisons and jails warehouse people made surplus by deindustrialization, segregation, school failure, addiction, mental illness, and poverty. They also generate jobs, contracts, phone fees, commissary profits, construction bonds, political power, and disciplined labor. The carceral state is not outside the economy. It is one of its institutions.

Abolition, then, cannot mean only closing prisons tomorrow without building anything else. It means shrinking the power of police, courts, jails, prisons, probation, and surveillance while expanding the resources that actually make communities safe: housing, health care, mental health support, living wages, schools, youth programs, conflict mediation, restorative practices, and freedom from environmental harm. It means asking why we rely on cages to manage problems produced by inequality. It means refusing the idea that some people are disposable.

Johnson’s critique of human rights is useful here. Liberal rights can tell us that a person deserves due process, counsel, equal protection, and freedom from cruel punishment. These rights are vital. But they do not ask why entire neighborhoods are exposed to premature death, why some schools have police instead of counselors, why cash bail turns poverty into incarceration, why fines and fees fund local government by extracting from the poor, why black women are criminalized for defending themselves, why immigrants are detained for seeking survival, or why addiction is treated as a crime in one community and a public health crisis in another. Rights can restrain the cage. Abolition asks why the cage is there.

The Movement for Black Lives platform’s call for reparations is therefore not a symbolic add-on. It is the material counterpart to abolition. If slavery, segregation, redlining, exclusion from federal benefits, employment discrimination, predatory lending, mass incarceration, and environmental racism have extracted wealth and life from black communities, then justice requires return, repair, and transformation. Reparations should include direct compensation, but also community control of resources, debt cancellation, guaranteed income, free education, housing, health care, land, and investment in institutions accountable to black communities. Reparations are not charity. They are payment on a debt.

Critics ask how far back we must go. The answer is: as far back as the harm reaches into the present. Slavery is not past if its wealth built institutions that still stand, if its racial categories still organize suspicion, if its unpaid labor helped create fortunes, if its defeat was followed by new regimes of terror and extraction, if its descendants inherit disadvantage while others inherit accumulated advantage. The point is not inherited guilt. It is inherited structure.

Johnson’s essay also forces movements to think globally. Racial capitalism does not stop at the U.S. border. The same order that cages black people in American cities exploits migrants, disciplines the Global South through debt and trade, extracts minerals and labor, militarizes borders, and treats refugees as threats. The new administration’s ban targeting several Muslim-majority countries, its promised wall, and its contempt for refugees are not departures from racial capitalism. They are its nationalist expression. A politics for black lives must therefore be internationalist, linking anti-blackness, Islamophobia, settler colonialism, anti-immigrant violence, and imperial war without pretending they are all identical.

There is a strategic lesson here. Movements cannot win by appealing only to the conscience of elites. Conscience is unstable. Some people saw the videos of police killings and still blamed the dead. Some saw water protectors attacked and still defended the pipeline. Some heard Trump mock, threaten, and exclude and voted for him because the exclusions were part of the appeal. If domination does not depend on ignorance, liberation cannot depend on awareness alone. We need power: organized people, disruptive capacity, legal defense, electoral strategy where useful, labor action, mutual aid, independent media, sanctuary networks, and institutions that can survive repression.

Johnson’s warning about historians writing slavery without enslaved people applies to movements too. It is possible to build a policy agenda about black suffering that leaves ordinary black people as objects of expertise. Foundations, politicians, academics, and nonprofits can translate rebellion into grant language, metrics, and task forces. The people most affected become testimony providers rather than decision-makers. A black radical tradition worthy of the name must be democratic. It must center those most harmed: incarcerated people, formerly incarcerated people, black women, queer and trans people, disabled people, poor people, migrants, youth, elders, and families of those killed by police.

This is also why gender and sexuality cannot be secondary. Slavery’s political economy depended on reproductive control and sexual violence. Today, black women and girls face police violence, domestic violence, wage theft, maternal health disparities, criminalization, and the burden of care work. Black trans women face extraordinary danger. If a movement says “black lives” but imagines the subject as male, it repeats a narrowing that the history of slavery should make impossible. Reparations and abolition must be feminist and queer or they will not be liberatory.

Johnson’s discussion of Native land should also reshape abolitionist politics. The United States is a settler state. Police and prisons are not only responses to crime; they are institutions for managing populations on stolen land. Border Patrol, sheriffs, military bases, and local police all belong to histories of conquest, removal, slave patrols, strikebreaking, and colonial control. Solidarity with Native sovereignty is not optional. Standing Rock has shown the power of water protection as a struggle against extraction, policing, and settler law. Black liberation and Indigenous decolonization are not the same struggle, but they must be allied.

Some will say this agenda is too large. They will ask for a single reform: better training, sentencing reform, jobs programs, civilian review boards. We need immediate reforms that save lives. End cash bail. Stop solitary confinement. Demilitarize police. Decriminalize poverty. Free people serving extreme sentences. Restore voting rights. Invest in schools. Protect immigrants. These are urgent. But if reforms are not tied to a larger abolitionist horizon, they can strengthen the system they aim to soften. A “better” jail is still a jail. A “community” police force can still police poverty. A risk assessment algorithm can automate racism. The question is whether each reform reduces the reach of racial capitalism or modernizes it.

Johnson ends with Du Bois’s sense that history offers moments when the world can be remade. We are in such a moment, though it is terrifying. White nationalism has entered the White House with unusual openness, but it did not come from nowhere. It grew from prisons, wars, borders, foreclosures, austerity, and the refusal to repair. The danger is real. So is the possibility. Millions have marched in recent days. Airports have filled with protesters against the travel ban. Local organizers have been building for years. The question is whether outrage can become organization.

To remake the world, we must refuse the small dreams offered to us. Representation without redistribution is not enough. Diversity in police departments is not enough. Body cameras are not enough. Equal opportunity to enter an unjust economy is not enough. A kinder empire is not enough. We need a world where no one’s body is collateral, no community is a sacrifice zone, no child is born into a debt created by stolen labor and stolen land, and no state claims the right to cage away the problems it refuses to solve.

Johnson’s essay gives us history as accusation and resource. Slavery was not outside modernity; it helped make the modern world. Therefore the modern world can be unmade and remade. The enslaved did not need to prove their humanity, and neither do those struggling now. The task is not to ask power to recognize us. The task is to build enough power that recognition is no longer the condition of survival.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Because state violence and slavery rely on the exploitation of human capacities rather than a failure to recognize humanity, reforms focused on empathy or visibility are insufficient to dismantle oppressive structures.
  2. 02The carceral state functions as a central institution of the modern economy, necessitating an abolitionist strategy that replaces policing and prisons with community-led resources and safety nets.
  3. 03Reparations must be understood as a material requirement for justice that addresses the historical and ongoing extraction of wealth from Black communities through systems like redlining and mass incarceration.
  4. 04A politics for Black lives must be internationalist and intersectional, linking domestic struggles against anti-Blackness with global resistance to imperialism, settler colonialism, and gendered violence.
  5. 05To avoid replicating the hierarchies of racial capitalism, social movements must be democratic and led by those most marginalized rather than by elite experts or non-profit institutions.
  6. 06Effective political strategy requires building organized power and disruptive capacity rather than relying on the unstable conscience of elites or the mere awareness of injustice.
