Settler Colonialism and the Limits of Racial Capitalism

I want to thank Walter Johnson for an essay that, more than any recent intervention in the slavery-capitalism debate, takes seriously the entanglement of African slavery with Indigenous dispossession. Toward the end of his essay he turns to *Johnson v. McIntosh* and writes that "the foundation of the law of property in the United States combines, at once, the imperial assertion of U.S. sovereignty and the identification of that project with continental racial governance." That sentence is doing a great deal of work, and I want to spend this response unpacking it — both to credit what is right about it and to push on what I take to be the limits of the racial capitalism framework when it comes to Indigenous experience and the politics of land.

The basic claim that the cotton economy was built on stolen Indigenous land is not new to specialists, but its integration into the mainstream history of American capitalism remains uneven. The standard narrative of "King Cotton" tends to elide the Removal Era — the brutal expulsion of the Creek, Cherokee, Choctaw, Chickasaw, and Seminole nations that opened the lands of what we now call the Deep South to the cotton-and-slave complex. Without Removal, there is no cotton frontier; without the cotton frontier, there is no antebellum boom; without the antebellum boom, the entire economic and political history Johnson describes does not occur. To say that the political economy of nineteenth-century American slavery rested on the dispossession of Indigenous peoples is to state a basic and underappreciated fact.

What I want to press, though, is whether the conceptual apparatus of racial capitalism — even in the expanded form Johnson proposes — can adequately hold the analysis of Indigenous dispossession alongside the analysis of African enslavement. There is a temptation, when these histories are integrated, to treat them as two species of a single genus: two forms of "racial" subordination that capitalism produced and required. The result is a tidy taxonomy in which African Americans are exploited for their labor, Indigenous people are dispossessed of their land, and both are "racialized" in service of accumulation. This taxonomy has the virtue of acknowledging the centrality of both groups. It has the vice of obscuring the structural differences between the two systems of domination and, more importantly, of subsuming Indigenous politics into a framework that may not be Indigenous politics' framework of choice.

Patrick Wolfe argued, in his influential work on settler colonialism, that the "logic of elimination" structuring the relationship between settlers and Indigenous peoples is distinct from the "logic of exploitation" structuring the relationship between capital and enslaved or wage labor. Settler colonialism, on Wolfe's account, does not primarily want Indigenous bodies — it wants Indigenous land, and it wants that land cleared of its prior inhabitants. The settler relationship is therefore eliminationist in a way that the slave relationship is not. The slaveholder requires the continuous reproduction of enslaved bodies; the settler requires their disappearance. This is a difference in the basic structural orientation of the two systems, not a variant within a single system.

If Wolfe is right, then "racial capitalism" risks framing Indigenous dispossession as a kind of subspecies of racial capitalist exploitation, when in fact it is something different: an ongoing project of replacement that operates by a distinct logic and toward distinct ends. The risk is not merely taxonomic. It is political. Indigenous political demands have centered on the recovery of land, the recognition of sovereignty, and the restoration of treaty rights. These demands do not map neatly onto the framework of reparations Johnson invokes. They are not primarily demands for redistribution within an existing polity; they are demands that put the existing polity itself in question. Land Back, as the contemporary slogan goes, is not a redistributive demand; it is a sovereignty demand.

This matters for Johnson's argument because the six principal virtues he articulates at the end of his essay, though framed as virtues of an account rooted in racial capitalism, do not obviously generate a politics adequate to Indigenous claims. Consider his second virtue: "focuses on the extraction and distribution of resources between classes and areas of the world." This is a redistributive framework. It treats land and labor as resources to be more fairly distributed. But for many Indigenous peoples, the issue is not the more equitable distribution of resources within the U.S. polity; the issue is the legitimacy of U.S. sovereignty over their lands at all. A framework that begins from the question "how should resources be distributed?" has already conceded the prior question — "by what right does this polity claim the authority to distribute resources on these lands?" — that Indigenous politics has insisted on raising.

A related point concerns Johnson's treatment of *Johnson v. McIntosh*. He reads the decision correctly as establishing that Native American lands had to pass through the public domain of the United States before becoming private property — and thus as embedding U.S. sovereignty into the foundation of the property regime. But the decision also did something deeper. It invoked the so-called Doctrine of Discovery — the medieval-canonical principle that Christian European nations could claim sovereignty over lands inhabited by non-Christians — as the source of U.S. claims to Indigenous lands. The Marshall Court did not justify U.S. claims primarily in terms of racial capitalism; it justified them in terms of a longer European tradition of Christian-imperial sovereignty. Racial categories certainly did work in the decision and its aftermath. But the deeper structure is one of imperial sovereignty operating through religious-civilizational categories that predate the racial capitalism of the nineteenth century. To collapse this into "racial governance" is to miss part of what is going on.

Let me put the broader point this way. The history of the Americas involves at least three distinct, interlocking, but irreducible structures of domination: the enslavement of Africans, the dispossession of Indigenous peoples, and the exploitation of (initially European, later globally diverse) wage labor. These structures share certain features and frequently reinforce one another, but they have distinct logics and generate distinct political demands. Racial capitalism, as a framework, can hold all three only if it is sufficiently capacious — and the more capacious it becomes, the more it risks losing analytical specificity. The advantage of the framework is that it forces us to see the connections; the disadvantage is that it can homogenize the differences.

What I would urge, then, is that Johnson's framework be supplemented rather than treated as sufficient. The history of capitalism is also the history of settler colonialism, and settler colonialism is not just a variant of racial capitalism. Glen Coulthard's work on the Dene experience in northern Canada, Audra Simpson's work on Mohawk political theory, and the broader emergence of Indigenous studies as a field with its own analytical commitments — these traditions need to sit alongside the Robinson-Du Bois tradition Johnson invokes, not be folded into it.

The implications for politics are significant. If Indigenous dispossession is not just another instance of racial capitalism but a distinct structure with its own logic, then the politics of decolonization is not just another instance of racial capitalist redress but a distinct political project that may have different demands, different timeframes, and different relations to the existing state. A reparations program rooted in the history of African enslavement is not, by itself, a decolonial program. The two may be compatible — and at their best they should be — but they are not interchangeable. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson cites is, importantly, in conversation with Indigenous movements (the platform was developed with input from Indigenous organizers and includes recognition of Indigenous sovereignty), but the conversation is precisely between distinct movements with distinct demands. The framework Johnson articulates needs to register this distinctness.

I want to close with a more constructive observation. The strongest version of Johnson's argument — the one in which the history of slavery becomes the "source rather than the subject of knowledge" — should, by parity of reasoning, extend to the history of Indigenous dispossession. What if we used the history of dispossession as a source of knowledge about modern political economy? It would generate a different set of insights than the ones Johnson develops. It would foreground the relationship between sovereignty and property, the violence of the state in producing the conditions for private accumulation, the role of treaty and its breaking in the constitution of the modern legal order, and the question of what is owed when what was taken is land and life rather than labor. These insights are not incompatible with Johnson's; they are complementary to them. But they require their own intellectual genealogy, and that genealogy includes Vine Deloria Jr., Robert Williams, Taiaiake Alfred, and a growing body of Indigenous scholarship that has its own answers to the questions Johnson is raising.

The propitious moment Du Bois invoked, the moment for remaking the world, is also a moment when the question of Indigenous sovereignty is being raised in new and powerful ways — Standing Rock has, in the months leading up to this writing, made this unmistakable. An adequate account of justice will not arrive at the right answers if it begins from a framework that has not internalized this question. Johnson's essay opens the door. The next step is to walk through it with Indigenous interlocutors and let the framework be transformed by what they have to say.
