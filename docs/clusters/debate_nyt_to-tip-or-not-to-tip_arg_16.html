"The legal subminimum wage for tipped workers should be abolished so that tips serve as a bonus for good service rather than a substitute for a guaranteed base salary."

The debate should not be framed as tipping versus no tipping. The real question is whether tips should be allowed to substitute for wages. They should not.

There is nothing inherently wrong with a customer leaving extra money for especially good service. Voluntary gratitude can be pleasant, and in some settings it is deeply embedded in American habits. The injustice begins when that voluntary gesture becomes the foundation of a worker’s paycheck. A server should earn at least the ordinary minimum wage from the employer before a single tip is counted. Tips, if they exist, should be additional compensation.

That reform would preserve what many diners and workers like about tipping while eliminating its worst feature: insecurity. Under the current tipped-wage system, workers depend on customer behavior to reach a basic wage floor. Slow nights, bad sections, bad weather and biased customers all become labor-market events. The law says employers must make up the difference if tips fall short, but workers in low-wage industries are not always in a position to demand perfect compliance.

A service-charge model like Sushi Yasuda’s may solve this in one elegant way, but it raises its own questions. Who gets the money? Is it shared with bussers, runners and kitchen staff? Are former tipped workers actually earning as much as before? If a restaurant says “no gratuities,” customers may feel relieved, while workers may quietly lose bargaining power.

So yes, restaurants should be free to adopt service-included pricing if they commit to fair, transparent wages. But the broader reform should be legal: end the subminimum tipped wage. Once all servers and bartenders receive a real base wage, tipping can become what many people mistakenly think it already is — a bonus for service, not a lifeline.

That compromise is also more realistic. Americans are unlikely to abandon tipping overnight, especially in bars, taxis and casual restaurants. But we can stop using custom as an excuse for a two-tier wage system. Keep the tip jar if you must. Just do not build the payroll around it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current tipped-wage system creates financial insecurity for workers by making their basic livelihood dependent on unpredictable factors like weather, customer bias, and slow shifts.
  2. 02Relying on employers to make up the difference when tips fall short is ineffective because low-wage workers often lack the bargaining power to ensure legal compliance.
  3. 03While service-included models are a valid option, they can lack transparency regarding how funds are distributed among staff and may reduce worker bargaining power.
  4. 04Ending the subminimum wage is a more realistic reform than banning tipping entirely because it respects American cultural habits while ensuring fair compensation.
  5. 05A guaranteed minimum wage from the employer protects workers from the inherent volatility of voluntary customer gratuities.
