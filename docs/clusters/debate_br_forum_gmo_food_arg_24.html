"While genetic engineering has potential benefits, framing it as the primary solution to global hunger ignores the structural, political, and economic causes of food insecurity that technology alone cannot solve."

In her passionate defense of genetic engineering, Pamela Ronald repeatedly invokes the needs of the world’s poorest farmers, and she is right to place them at the center of the debate. Mama Moses in Uganda, the vitamin A–deficient children who could benefit from golden rice—these are not abstractions. But the very framing of GMOs as a solution to global hunger obscures more than it reveals about the structural causes of food insecurity and the political economy of seed technology. What we need is not a defense of GE crops per se, but a deeper interrogation of who controls the food system and for what purposes.

Let’s start with the facts on the ground. Ronald touts yield increases for Bt cotton farmers in India and China, yet the story in much of Africa remains one of technological dependence rather than empowerment. Cotton farmers in Burkina Faso, for instance, adopted Bt cotton with initial enthusiasm, but yields later declined due to fiber quality problems partly linked to the transgene, and the cost of seed and associated inputs squeezed margins. The gains Ronald describes often assume a package of high-input farming—hybrid seed, synthetic fertilizers, reliable water—that many smallholders simply cannot access. In those conditions, the promised yield advantage can evaporate.

Ronald argues that genetic engineering is not inherently tied to corporate control, and she calls for public-sector investment. I agree. But we must be honest: the current intellectual property regime makes it extremely difficult for public breeders to stack traits or develop new varieties from proprietary germplasm. Golden rice, often cited as a humanitarian triumph, involved navigating over 70 patents and negotiating licenses; it is the exception, not the rule. For most crops and most traits, the technology remains locked behind corporate walls. Until we fundamentally reform patent law as it applies to living organisms, the promise of decentralized, pro-poor GE will remain largely aspirational.

The bigger question, however, is whether genetic engineering is what we should be investing in to address the root causes of hunger. The global food system already produces more than enough calories per person; chronic malnutrition results from poverty, unequal land distribution, conflict, and trade policies that dump cheap grain on developing-country markets, destroying local agricultural livelihoods. In many regions, what farmers need most is not novel DNA but secure land tenure, access to local markets, affordable credit, and extension services that support diversified, resilient farming systems. Agroecological methods—such as push-pull systems for pest control, farmer-managed seed systems, and soil conservation techniques—have shown remarkable results in Africa without the need for transgenic traits. Yet these approaches receive a tiny fraction of the research dollars that flow to biotech.

Ronald’s essay dismisses concerns about corporate power as a “tribal” reflex of the left, but the anti-GMO movement in much of the Global South is not led by affluent Westerners. It is led by peasant organizations like La Via Campesina, which reject GMOs not because they are anti-science, but because they see them as the thin end of a wedge that deepens corporate control over the means of life. Their demand is not for more testing; it is for food sovereignty—the right of peoples to define their own food and agricultural systems. That is a political, not a scientific, claim, and it cannot be adjudicated by the FDA or the National Academy.

I do not believe we should abandon genetic engineering. There are traits—like resistance to BXW in bananas—that truly have no conventional counterpart, and public breeding programs should be pursuing them vigorously. But such technologies must be embedded in a broader democratic governance of food. That means reversing the concentration in the seed industry, mandating open-source genetics and non-exclusive licensing for traits of broad public benefit, and ensuring that farmers have the legal right to save, exchange, and sell seeds, regardless of the method used to breed them. If we want to talk about feeding the world sustainably, we need to talk about power as much as about genes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of GE crops often depends on high-input farming conditions that many smallholders cannot access or afford, leading to technological dependence rather than empowerment.
  2. 02Current intellectual property regimes and patent laws concentrate control of seed technology within corporations, hindering public-sector efforts to develop pro-poor crop varieties.
  3. 03Chronic malnutrition is primarily a result of poverty, land inequality, and trade policies rather than a lack of agricultural technology or calories.
  4. 04Agroecological methods and improved infrastructure are often more effective and accessible solutions for small farmers than transgenic traits, yet they receive significantly less research funding.
  5. 05Opposition to GMOs in the Global South is frequently a political demand for food sovereignty and resistance to corporate control over the food system rather than a rejection of science.
  6. 06Sustainable food security requires democratic governance of the food system, including the reform of seed industry concentration and the protection of farmers' rights to save and exchange seeds.
