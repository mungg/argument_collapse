"The failure of top-down defense on September 11 proves that the United States must dismantle its centralized, authoritarian military structures—specifically its nuclear arsenal—in favor of an egalitarian model based on democratic consent and shared civilian responsibility."

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” is a necessary intervention in a political climate that has grown suffocatingly militaristic since the attacks of September 11. While much of the public discourse over the past year has focused on expanding the powers of the state—through the USA PATRIOT Act, the invasion of Afghanistan, and the impending creation of a monolithic Department of Homeland Security—Scarry bravely points in the exact opposite direction. Her most crucial contribution is drawing a direct line between the failure of our top-down defense structures on 9/11 and the ultimate expression of authoritarian state violence: the American nuclear arsenal.

To read Scarry in October 2002 is to realize how completely the threat of terrorism has been hijacked to serve the pre-existing agendas of the military-industrial complex. Scarry is entirely correct to label nuclear weapons as “monarchic weapons.” They are the absolute antithesis of democracy. A system in which a single human being, the President of the United States, holds the unchecked power to incinerate millions of people and initiate the end of human civilization is a system that has already abandoned any pretense of egalitarian self-governance.

The irony, as Scarry so brilliantly dissects, is that this massive, centralized architecture of death—justified for half a century by the necessity of "speed" and the threat of Soviet missiles—proved entirely useless against a handful of men armed with box cutters. The state spent trillions of dollars building an apparatus designed to respond in minutes, bypassing constitutional checks and balances along the way, yet it could not defend its own military headquarters after more than an hour of clear warning.

Yet, rather than recognizing this failure as proof that our reliance on centralized, weapons-heavy deterrence is obsolete, the Bush administration is doubling down. The leaked Nuclear Posture Review from earlier this year revealed terrifying plans to expand the role of nuclear weapons, blurring the line between conventional and nuclear warfare, and openly contemplating their use against non-nuclear states like Iraq, Iran, and Syria. The administration is using the trauma of an asymmetric terrorist attack to justify an aggressively imperialistic, preemptive military doctrine.

Scarry’s argument that we must dismantle this nuclear autocracy is not merely idealistic; it is the only pragmatic path to long-term security. Nuclear weapons are, as she states, “an extreme form of aerial terrorism.” We cannot credibly lead a global non-proliferation effort while threatening "nuclear wipe-out" against states that defy us. If we insist that our security requires a genocidal arsenal on hair-trigger alert, we guarantee a world where other nations will pursue the exact same capabilities in self-defense. The current crisis with Iraq is a perfect example: we are threatening to go to war to prevent Saddam Hussein from acquiring the very weapons of mass destruction that we stockpile by the thousands and reserve the right to use preemptively.

The egalitarian model of defense that Scarry observed on Flight 93—where citizens gathered information, deliberated, and acted at the risk of their own lives—stands as a stark moral contrast to the cowardice of push-button warfare. When a nation relies on a professional military and remote-controlled weapons (or nuclear missiles) to project power, the citizens are shielded from the moral and physical costs of their government's actions. This decoupling makes war far too easy. It allows the American public to passively watch the bombing of Kabul or Baghdad on television without ever having to take a vote, bear arms, or risk their own communities.

Scarry notes that returning to a conventional, distributed military might require the reinstatement of the draft. This is a controversial proposition, but a deeply moral one. A draft forces the entire society—including the sons and daughters of politicians, CEOs, and the wealthy elite—to share the burden of defense. If the American public knew that their own children would be sent to die in the deserts of Iraq, the threshold for approving such a war would be exponentially higher. The debates would be rigorous, the evidence scrutinized, and the civil frame restored.

We are currently sleepwalking into an era of endless war and unchecked executive supremacy. By insisting that national defense is the responsibility of the people, not just the Pentagon, Scarry offers a radical but deeply traditional way out of this nightmare. True security cannot be bought with bigger bombs or more invasive surveillance. It can only be built on a foundation of democratic consent, shared sacrifice, and the moral courage to dismantle the monarchic weapons that threaten not only our enemies, but our own souls.
