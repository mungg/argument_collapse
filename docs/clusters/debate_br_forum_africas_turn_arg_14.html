"Western critics should move beyond resentment of China’s presence in Africa and instead focus on improving their own trade policies and supporting African institutional capacity to manage all foreign investment."

Edward Miguel’s treatment of China is balanced, but the larger Western conversation about China in Africa is not. Too often it carries an undertone of resentment: after decades in which Europe and the United States dominated African trade, aid, and diplomacy, a new actor has arrived and is playing by different rules. Suddenly Western governments have discovered labor standards, corruption, and human rights in resource extraction. Africans may be forgiven for noticing the selectivity.

Chinese engagement in Africa is neither charity nor conspiracy. It is commerce and strategy. China needs oil, copper, timber, and markets. African governments need roads, railways, power plants, telecommunications, and buyers for their exports. There are obvious risks in this bargain, but also obvious benefits. If the question is whether Africa’s recent recovery can continue, Chinese demand and Chinese construction capacity may be among the most important positive forces now available.

For many African countries, infrastructure is the binding constraint. Farmers cannot profit from higher prices if roads are impassable. Manufacturers cannot compete if electricity fails. Landlocked countries cannot export efficiently if corridors to ports are slow and corrupt. Western donors have talked about infrastructure for years, but much aid has drifted toward social sectors, consultants, and governance programs. Social spending is important, but no country becomes prosperous on workshops. China builds things.

This practical fact explains much of China’s appeal. African leaders can point to a road, a stadium, a government building, a mine, a dam, or a telephone network. Critics may complain that Chinese firms import their own workers or cut corners, and sometimes they do. But the alternative has often been no road at all. A dangerous mine should be made safer; an unbuilt mine employs no one. A poorly negotiated loan should be renegotiated; the absence of finance is not automatically virtuous.

The Chambishi mine in Zambia is a serious warning. Workers should not have to accept Chinese labor conditions as the price of employment. The deaths in the 2005 accident demand accountability. But the right comparison is not between Chinese exploitation and Western benevolence. Western mining companies have also polluted, underpaid, bribed, and manipulated weak states. The difference is that Western civil societies sometimes impose reputational costs. That suggests the remedy: strengthen African labor unions, inspection agencies, courts, newspapers, and parliamentary oversight so that all investors face scrutiny.

The Sudan case is harder. Chinese support for Khartoum, including diplomatic protection, has had ugly consequences. Darfur is not a minor blemish on China’s African policy. Yet Western policy is hardly a triumph. Sanctions that drive Western firms out may leave the field to actors less sensitive to public pressure, as Miguel notes. Moral withdrawal can become moral irrelevance. If the goal is to protect civilians, engagement must be judged by consequences, not by the purity of the engager’s hands.

There is also a broader irony. Western countries demand that African governments improve governance while maintaining agricultural subsidies that damage African farmers. Miguel’s cotton example is devastating. American cotton subsidies transfer wealth to a small number of U.S. farmers while depressing prices for millions of poorer African farmers. Europe’s agricultural protections are no better in principle. Before lecturing Africa about corruption, rich countries might stop corrupting world markets.

China’s rise has at least changed the bargaining environment. African governments now have options. Donor conditionality was often presented as partnership, but it frequently meant “take our advice or lose financing.” Some advice was good; much was ideological, inconsistent, or indifferent to local politics. Chinese finance weakens the monopoly of Western donors and international financial institutions. That may reduce leverage for governance reforms, but it also reduces paternalism. Competition among outsiders can benefit African states if they bargain intelligently.

The worry, of course, is that some rulers will use Chinese money to avoid accountability. Oil-backed loans and resource deals can fund patronage, arms, and prestige projects. But this is not a China problem alone; it is a resource-governance problem. Angola’s oil wealth, Nigeria’s oil politics, Chad’s pipeline controversy, and Congo’s mineral predation all show that natural resources can strengthen unaccountable states. The answer is transparency in contracts, public revenue reporting, legislative approval of major deals, and citizen monitoring. Western governments should support such transparency rather than merely complain that China does not impose it.

Nor should we exaggerate China’s power over African outcomes. Africa’s future will not be made in Beijing. Chinese demand may raise commodity prices, but African governments decide how revenues are spent. Chinese firms may build roads, but African institutions decide whether roads are maintained and whether local businesses can use them productively. Chinese goods may compete with African manufacturers, but African policymakers must address power, credit, skills, customs, and transport costs. Blaming China for deindustrialization can become an excuse for avoiding domestic reform.

Indeed, the competition from Chinese manufactured goods is a severe test. African textile producers benefited from AGOA, then suffered after the end of the Multi-Fiber Agreement allowed China to compete more freely. This is painful, but protection from Chinese competition cannot be the foundation of long-term industrialization. African firms need niches, regional markets, better logistics, and perhaps temporary, disciplined industrial policies. They cannot rely indefinitely on quota preferences.

The most promising possibility is that Chinese infrastructure, African entrepreneurship, and improved governance could reinforce one another. A road lowers transport costs. Mobile phones improve price information. A port upgrade expands export possibilities. A reliable power plant allows factories to operate. If democratic pressures force governments to spread benefits and regulate investors, Chinese engagement could become part of a broader development story.

Miguel is right that Chinese investment is “not wholly positive.” But no major force in development is wholly positive. Aid can save lives or prop up tyrants. Democracy can discipline rulers or inflame ethnic competition. Commodity booms can finance schools or palaces. The issue is not whether China is good or bad for Africa in the abstract. The issue is whether African societies can capture the benefits and control the costs.

Western policymakers should respond with humility and seriousness. End farm subsidies that hurt African producers. Invest in infrastructure without endless delays. Support transparency rules that apply to Western companies as well as Chinese ones. Fund African universities, engineers, auditors, and journalists. Offer partnership rather than sermons.

China’s arrival does not guarantee that Africa’s turn has come. But it has broken an old pattern in which Africa’s external choices were too narrow. That opening is valuable. Whether it becomes exploitation or opportunity depends less on Beijing’s intentions than on African bargaining power, public accountability, and the willingness of the West to compete by improving its own behavior.
