Diversified DeepSeek

"Robert Chapman’s proposal for 'Neurodivergent Power' fails because it incorrectly treats the vastly heterogeneous and unstable category of neurodivergence as a unified political identity capable of forming a revolutionary class."

Robert Chapman’s forum essay arrives at a moment when “neurodiversity” has become a buzzword so elastic that it seems to encompass everything from a TikTok self-diagnosis trend to corporate diversity reports. Chapman is right to take stock, and their critique of what they call Liberal Neurodiversity—an approach that prioritizes rights, representation, and a strengths-based reframing—is sharp. But I want to suggest that both the liberal paradigm and Chapman’s proposed antidote of Neurodivergent Power suffer from a common, debilitating flaw: they treat neurodivergence as a relatively stable, unified identity that can form the basis of a political program, whether reformist or revolutionary. This assumption is not just empirically shaky; it risks generating new forms of exclusion and erasure that the next generation of neurodiversity thinkers must confront.

Let me start with what Chapman gets right. The co-optation of neurodiversity by neoliberal capitalism is real and galling. When Ernst & Young boasts about its “neurodiversity centers of excellence” that seek out autistic workers for repetitive data-analysis tasks, we are witnessing what the late David Graeber might call a bullshit job engineered to extract cognitive niche skills while leaving the rest of the person behind. The point that rights-based frameworks often fail the multiply marginalized is well taken: an unhoused Black person with schizophrenia will find little solace in the ADA’s promise of reasonable accommodations when they are being swept from an encampment by police. Chapman’s internationalist scope, connecting the suffering in Gaza to the exploitation of autistic teens in Israeli military intelligence, is a moral wake-up call that the neurodiversity movement has largely ignored.

Where I part company is in the way Chapman constructs neurodivergence as an axis of oppression that can be productively compared to, and allied with, Black liberation struggles. The analogy risks a category error. The Black Power movement was grounded in a shared history of enslavement, colonialism, and a visible, racially marked identity that, however diverse internally, was forcibly homogenized by a white supremacist world. Neurodivergence, by contrast, is an umbrella term covering an astonishingly heterogeneous set of conditions: autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, Tourette’s, intellectual disabilities, bipolar, schizophrenia, and more. These diagnoses were invented at different historical moments, for different reasons, and confer vastly different lived experiences. What does a non-speaking autistic person with high support needs and a self-diagnosed ADHD tech worker who finds the 9-to-5 stifling actually share in terms of political interest? The former may need round-the-clock care, freedom from restraint, and protection from medical abuse; the latter may yearn for flexible hours and an end to open-plan offices. Both deserve justice, but the form of that justice is incommensurate. Lumping them into a single “neurominority” under capitalism obscures more than it illuminates.

The problem intensifies when Chapman proposes a revolutionary party as the vehicle for Neurodivergent Power. A party requires a program, a set of demands that unify its base. What could those demands possibly be that would not flatten the immense diversity within the neurodivergent community? Free housing for all, yes—but housing that supports independence is fundamentally different from housing that provides 24-hour skilled nursing. The end of psychiatric incarceration sounds radical, but what do we say to the person with severe psychosis who, during an acute episode, poses a genuine danger to themselves or their loved ones and whose family is desperate for a safe, humane place for stabilization? Slogans like “abolish the prison and psychiatric systems” sound bracing in a lecture hall but can become cruel when they deny the reality that some forms of suffering require intensive, even restrictive, interventions—interventions that we should make as humane and consensual as possible, not simply abolish. The call for a party, echoing Leninist structures, also threatens to recreate the very hierarchies of cognitive ability that disabled people have long criticized on the left. Who gets to be a cadre? Who writes the manifestos? Who is considered politically “reliable” when psychosis, mania, or intellectual disability can scramble one’s relationship to political consistency?

Chapman might reply that I am defending a liberal status quo, but I am not. I am arguing for a more difficult and uncomfortable politics that refuses the temptation to treat neurodivergence as a stable identity category. Instead, what if we oriented our activism around the specific needs and situated knowledges of people with specific impairments, understood within a universalist framework of human dependence? This is the kind of politics advanced by feminist disability scholars like Eva Feder Kittay and Rosemarie Garland-Thomson. It insists that we are all, at various points, deeply dependent on care, and that social justice requires building infrastructures that acknowledge this universal vulnerability rather than celebrating difference as a marketable asset or a revolutionary identity. Such a politics would fight for universal basic income, truly universal health care including mental health, public housing with embedded support services, and a dramatic reduction of the workweek—not because they liberate “neurodivergent people” specifically, but because they create the conditions in which all of us can live lives of dignity, whatever our cognitive or emotional configuration.

This does not mean giving up on neurodivergent pride or community. Autistic Pride Day and Weird Pride Day have been deeply meaningful for my own dyspraxic and ADHD self; they provide a counter-narrative to decades of shame. But those celebrations should remain cultural and communal, not become the basis for a revolutionary party line. The danger of Chapman’s vision is that it takes a collection of loosely related diagnostic categories, casts them as a revolutionary class, and then demands a discipline that many of us can’t or shouldn’t provide. I want a politics that holds complexity, that refuses the binary of medical tragedy and radical superpower, that can say: I need medication to manage my depression, and I deserve a world where that medication is free and developed in consultation with patients, and I also want a world where my melancholy and slow processing speed are not seen as deficits to be optimized away. That messy, non-identitarian, profoundly human vision is the future of neurodiversity I want to build.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The diverse conditions under the neurodiversity umbrella—ranging from high-support autism to ADHD—have incommensurate political interests and needs that cannot be reconciled into a single movement.
  2. 02The analogy between neurodivergent struggle and Black Power is a category error because neurodivergence lacks the shared history and forced homogenization of a racially marked identity.
  3. 03A revolutionary party structure risks flattening the specific needs of the most vulnerable and recreating hierarchies that exclude those with intellectual disabilities or fluctuating mental states.
  4. 04Radical slogans like the total abolition of psychiatric systems ignore the material reality that some forms of acute suffering require intensive, humane interventions.
  5. 05Instead of identity-based revolution, activism should focus on a universalist framework of human dependence and vulnerability to secure material needs like universal healthcare and housing for everyone.
