"While institutional structures are important for domestic intelligence, they must be supported by a robust legal framework of statutes and judicial oversight to ensure constitutional legitimacy and protect civil liberties."

Daniel Richman’s essay is, among other things, an elegantly framed argument about the limits of legal constraint. He writes that “the primary sources of accountability and due moderation will have to come from institutional structure,” because judicial and legislative controls are too distant or too blunt to shape the daily discretionary acts that constitute domestic intelligence. This is a powerful insight, but it risks underestimating the constitutive role that law plays—and must play—in any legitimate intelligence system. I want to pull the argument back toward the legal framework, not to claim that statutes and court rulings can do everything, but to insist that the institutional structure Richman favors cannot succeed without a sound legal foundation that clarifies authority, protects rights, and channels oversight.

Consider first the constitutional problems. The federal government’s authority to create a national domestic intelligence network is not unbounded; it derives largely from its powers over national defense and foreign affairs, and to some extent from the Necessary and Proper Clause. But when that network employs local police as its primary collectors, it collides with the anti-commandeering principle articulated in Printz v. United States (1997), which held that Congress cannot compel state executive officers to administer a federal regulatory program. While federal grants can induce cooperation, the proposed CLEAR Act and similar legislation that would require local enforcement of federal immigration law on pain of losing funds raise serious Tenth Amendment questions. The lower courts are currently divided on whether state and local officers have “inherent authority” to arrest for civil immigration violations. The Supreme Court has not squarely ruled, but the logic of Printz suggests that forcing them to do so is constitutionally problematic. Richman’s model is predicated on voluntary partnership, but the political reality, as he himself notes, is one of coercive funding cuts; a system built on dubious constitutional ground is not a stable one.

The Fourth Amendment presents a more profound hurdle. The Supreme Court has long distinguished between traditional law enforcement and “special needs” searches (such as airport screenings or drug tests for certain public employees) that serve purposes beyond ordinary crime control. In City of Indianapolis v. Edmond (2000), the Court struck down a program of vehicle checkpoints aimed at drug interdiction, holding that they could not be justified under the special needs doctrine because their primary purpose was to uncover evidence of ordinary criminal wrongdoing. Domestic intelligence-gathering, as Richman describes it, is not ordinary criminal investigation; its proximate aim is to prevent terrorist attacks. Does that take it outside the Edmond prohibition? Perhaps, but the Court has also been wary of allowing the government to evade the warrant requirement simply by invoking a need to “prevent” rather than “detect.” The Patriot Act’s expansion of administrative subpoenas and National Security Letters has already provoked successful constitutional challenges in several district courts, with judges finding that the associated gag orders violate the First Amendment and that the lack of meaningful judicial review offends the Fourth. Richman’s institutional structure, if it were to formalize a system of routine, suspicionless intelligence collection from local police, would face similar challenges. A federal court might well conclude that when a beat cop questions a community member at the behest of a JTTF, the state is conducting a “search” or “seizure” that must satisfy the reasonableness requirement.

Legislative attention is needed to address these ambiguities. Congress has never enacted a comprehensive domestic intelligence statute; instead, we have a patchwork of executive orders, National Security Act provisions, and piecemeal Patriot Act amendments. The Church Committee in its 1976 final report recommended a legislative charter for the FBI and other intelligence agencies that would define the scope of domestic investigations, set duration limits, require periodic reporting, and create independent civil liberties monitors. That recommendation was never fully implemented, and its absence is now felt. A modernized version of such a charter could establish that local officers acting in a counterterrorism capacity are subject to the same constitutional and statutory constraints as federal agents, thereby eliminating the “silver platter” loophole. It could require judicial warrants for certain categories of local-federal information sharing, and it could extend the Privacy Act’s protections to cover data originally collected by state and local entities.

Richman is correct that no legal regime can fully specify ex ante the myriad decisions that must be made in the field. But law can create a structure of precommitment, transparency, and review that shapes the institutional incentives he relies on. For instance, if the federal government were required by statute to report annually to Congress and to a public advisory body on the number and nature of local intelligence requests, and if local governments were required to keep public logs of the general categories of information shared, the political accountability Richman values would have an evidentiary basis. Without such legal scaffolding, the negotiations he champions will remain invisible, unauditable, and susceptible to decay.

In short, legal frameworks and institutional structures are not alternatives; they are complements. The “right fight” must be waged on both fronts: to design the intergovernmental partnerships wisely, and to enact the statutory and judicial safeguards that make democratic supervision possible. To let either languish is to invite the gradual erosion of liberties that no amount of local goodwill can repair.
