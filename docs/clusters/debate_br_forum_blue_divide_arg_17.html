Diversified DeepSeek

"While the Democratic Party has successfully moved left on economic policy, its refusal to address the downward pressure that low-skill immigration exerts on working-class wages creates a fatal economic and political contradiction within its coalition."

The essay by Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson is an important and insightful corrective, pushing back forcefully against the conventional wisdom that the Democratic Party has been seduced away from economic justice by the siren song of cultural elitism. They are right to point to the Biden agenda as proof of life for a vigorous, left-leaning economic program. Yet in their determination to recast the narrative around a unified pocketbook politics, they inadvertently perform the very act of strategic avoidance they attribute to party elites. They organize the most difficult and politically inconvenient truth “out” of their own analysis. The issue they systematically sidestep is immigration. To write nearly 6,000 words on the dynamics of the Democratic coalition without seriously engaging the one issue that most powerfully scrambles its class and racial alignments, and that fuels the authoritarian right they rightly fear, is to analyze the house while ignoring the fire on the lawn.

Consider the composition of the “U-shaped coalition.” On one side are affluent, college-educated professionals in high-productivity metro areas. On the other are less affluent workers, disproportionately people of color. Hacker and Pierson note the shared interests and values that bind them, and the elite strategy to manage internal divisions. What they fail to acknowledge is the immense, sustained downward pressure that high levels of low-skill immigration place on the wages and working conditions of the poorer side of that very coalition. A vast economic literature, including work by scholars like George Borjas at Harvard, has demonstrated that the post-1965 wave of immigration, while a boon for employers and consumers of services, has had significant negative wage effects on the most vulnerable native-born workers, particularly prior immigrants and Black men without a high school diploma. This is not a right-wing talking point; it is a material reality that the party’s economic agenda, for all its novelty, completely ignores.

The Biden-Sanders unity task forces, which Hacker and Pierson credit with shaping the 2021 agenda, notably did place immigration reform as a priority, and the authors reveal that party elites strategically put it on the “legislative backburner.” They present this as a savvy move to avoid activating racial schisms. But this framing is revealing. The issue is cast as one of “racial justice,” an agenda item that might upset moderate white voters. This completely misses the class dimension. The progressive case for restrictive immigration policy is not rooted in racial animus but in labor market analysis. A party that is serious about predistribution, about raising wages and empowering workers, cannot do so while simultaneously maintaining a policy of near-open borders for low-wage labor. It is an economic contradiction. You cannot dramatically expand the supply of labor in the low-wage care economy—an economy the Democrats explicitly want to restructure for better pay—and expect the price of that labor to rise organically. The market will not permit it.

The political ramifications of this contradiction are devastating. By refusing to even acknowledge the wage-and-immigration nexus as a legitimate economic concern, Democrats cede the entire terrain of border security to the ethno-nationalist right. They allow the GOP to define the issue of migration entirely on its terms—chaos, invasion, crime—foreclosing any possibility of a progressive economic argument for managed borders. The white working-class voters whom Hacker and Pierson hope to woo back with manufacturing jobs and child tax credits are also voters who observe a massive, decades-long experiment in labor market liberalization that progressive elites, with a mix of market fundamentalism and moral universalism, have actively cheered on. The message they hear is clear: your economic concerns about wage competition are morally illegitimate. The party’s pocketbook agenda is for everyone, except when it comes to the pocketbook issue of how labor supply affects your paycheck. That, the party has decided, is a conversation we don’t have.

This strategic silence is a central, not peripheral, cause of the authoritarian threat the authors decry. The political violence and anti-democratic mobilization on the right are fueled, in part, by the perception among a large segment of the electorate that the established political class—of both parties—has colluded in an economic and demographic transformation that harms them, while branding any objection as bigotry. By placing immigration in a sealed-off box labeled “racial justice,” Democrats confirm this narrative. They appear to be more concerned with the rights and inclusion of newcomers than with the economic security and social stability of the native-born working class, a group that includes many of the very Black and brown voters already in their coalition.

The new Blue Divide Hacker and Pierson chart is real, but it cannot be healed by a policy regime that willfully blinds itself to the distributive consequences of one of the most powerful economic forces shaping the nation. A truly coherent economic populism would confront the immigration question not through the lens of cruelty and nativism, but through the lens of labor power and wage solidarity. It would argue that control over the labor market is not a xenophobic project, but a prerequisite for a functional social democracy. Until that argument is made, the economic agenda they celebrate will remain built on a political fault line, and the right’s authoritarian exploitation of that fault line will continue to grow.
