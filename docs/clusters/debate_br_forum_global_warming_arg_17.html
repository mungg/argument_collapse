"Because international efforts to mitigate carbon emissions have failed and cannot act quickly enough to prevent catastrophe, humanity must pivot to geoengineering as the primary strategy for managing global temperatures."

In "Can We Stop Global Warming?", Kerry Emanuel lays out the sobering physical realities of anthropogenic climate change. He rightly warns of non-linear climate jumps, accelerating sea-level rise, and supercharged hydrological cycles. Yet, when he pivots to solutions, he falls back on the tired, orthodox framing of global climate policy: we must mitigate carbon emissions as an "insurance policy" against disaster. Writing at the dawn of 2007, it is time for us to look unflinchingly at the geopolitical reality and admit a difficult truth: the mitigation strategy has failed. If the consequences of a warming planet are as dire as Emanuel and the IPCC suggest, then relying on international carbon treaties is not an insurance policy; it is a suicide pact. We must urgently shift our focus toward deliberate climate intervention—geoengineering.

To understand why mitigation is a failed paradigm, one only needs to look at the scoreboard. In 1997, the world gathered to sign the Kyoto Protocol, a landmark treaty designed to bind nations to emissions reductions. A decade later, the treaty is in tatters. The United States, the world's largest historical emitter, refused to ratify it. China and India, the engines of 21st-century economic growth, were exempt from binding targets. Today, global greenhouse gas emissions are not just rising; their rate of acceleration is increasing. China is currently constructing a new coal-fired power plant nearly every week. To expect developing nations to forego the cheap, reliable fossil-fuel energy that powered the industrial rise of the West is politically naïve and morally fraught.

Emanuel asks, "Can we stop global warming?" If the "we" refers to the United Nations framework attempting to voluntarily restrict fossil fuel use, the answer is a resounding no. The tragedy of the commons dictates that no individual nation has an incentive to severely damage its own economic competitiveness for a fractional, imperceptible reduction in global temperatures decades hence.

Therefore, we must take Emanuel's warnings about "climate surprises" and sudden jumps incredibly seriously, but apply them to a different set of solutions. If we cannot stop the accumulation of greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, we must find a way to cool the Earth despite them. We must engineer the planet.

The concept of geoengineering—specifically, albedo modification via stratospheric aerosol injection—is not science fiction. We know with absolute certainty that it works because nature has demonstrated the mechanism repeatedly. In 1991, the eruption of Mount Pinatubo in the Philippines injected roughly 20 million tons of sulfur dioxide into the stratosphere. This aerosol layer reflected a small percentage of incoming sunlight back into space, cooling the entire planet by approximately 0.5°C for nearly two years. The Earth’s climate system is undeniably sensitive to solar radiation management.

Just last year, Nobel laureate Paul Crutzen published a controversial and groundbreaking essay in the journal *Climatic Change*, arguing that because the world is failing so spectacularly to rein in emissions, scientists must break the taboo and begin actively researching stratospheric aerosol injection. Crutzen’s intervention was a watershed moment because it acknowledged the desperate reality that traditional mitigation advocates ignore. If Emanuel is correct that the Greenland ice sheet might destabilize, or if the Siberian permafrost begins to vent massive quantities of methane, a carbon tax will not save us. The latency in the carbon cycle means that even if we stopped all emissions tomorrow, the planet would continue to warm for decades. Geoengineering is the only mechanism available to humanity that can arrest global temperature rise on a timescale of months.

Critics of geoengineering invariably raise the issue of moral hazard—the fear that if we develop a "technofix" for climate change, society will lose the political will to reduce carbon emissions. They also point to the potential side effects, such as unintended changes to global precipitation patterns or the continued acidification of the oceans (which solar radiation management does not address).

These are valid concerns, but they ring hollow when weighed against the catastrophic risks Emanuel outlines. If the choice is between a pristine atmospheric composition and the inundation of lower Manhattan and southern Florida, the choice is obvious. If the choice is between unpredictable changes in precipitation caused by geoengineering, and the completely unpredictable "sudden climate jumps" caused by unrestrained greenhouse warming, we are better off taking the path where our hand is on the thermostat. The moral hazard argument is a luxury for the comfortable; it assumes we have the time and political capacity to smoothly transition to a carbon-free economy. We do not.

Emanuel writes, "In pushing the climate so hard and so fast, we are also conscious of our own collective ignorance of how the climate system works." He uses this to argue for emissions reductions. But this same logic applies to geoengineering. Because we are ignorant of the precise tipping points, we cannot afford to lack an emergency brake.

We must immediately launch a massive, internationally coordinated research program into climate engineering. We need to design the delivery mechanisms for stratospheric aerosols, run extensive modeling to predict regional side effects, and develop international governance frameworks to manage the deployment of these technologies. We must also aggressively fund research into direct air capture and ocean iron fertilization to address the root concentration of atmospheric carbon.

We have already inadvertently geoengineered the planet through the reckless combustion of fossil fuels. Pretending we can simply wind back the clock through voluntary energy rationing is an illusion. We have firmly entered the Anthropocene, an era where human activity is the dominant driver of the Earth system. It is time we stop acting like passive victims of our own exhaust fumes and start taking active, deliberate responsibility for the management of the global climate. If we want to survive the climate surprises of the 21st century, we must become the architects of our own atmosphere.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The geopolitical reality of the tragedy of the commons and the energy needs of developing nations make voluntary global emissions reductions politically and economically impossible.
  2. 02The latency of the carbon cycle means that even a total cessation of emissions would not stop warming in the short term, whereas geoengineering can arrest temperature rise on a timescale of months.
  3. 03Natural events like the eruption of Mount Pinatubo provide scientific proof that stratospheric aerosol injection is an effective mechanism for cooling the planet.
  4. 04The catastrophic risks of climate tipping points, such as ice sheet collapse, outweigh the potential side effects or moral hazards associated with geoengineering technologies.
  5. 05As humanity has already inadvertently altered the climate through fossil fuel use, it must now take deliberate, active responsibility for atmospheric management through a coordinated research and deployment program.
