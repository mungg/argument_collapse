Diversified Gemini

"True full employment and ecological sustainability cannot be achieved through green-energy capitalism and increased consumption, but rather require a radical reduction in the workweek to shrink the economy's material footprint."

In "Back to Full Employment," Robert Pollin argues that environmental sustainability and full employment are natural allies. By transferring $165 billion from the fossil fuel sector to clean energy, he argues, we can simultaneously combat global warming and create millions of jobs. While Pollin’s intention to link ecological sanity with economic security is commendable, his underlying framework remains trapped in the very paradigm that caused the climate crisis: the relentless pursuit of aggregate economic growth and increased consumption.

Pollin explicitly states that a key benefit of full employment is that "total purchasing power in the economy" rises, meaning "people have more money in their pockets to spend," leading to "more buoyant markets" and business expansion. Herein lies the fatal contradiction of the mainstream green-Keynesian approach. You cannot "spend" your way out of an ecological crisis.

Even if the energy powering our factories and homes is shifted to wind and solar, a massive increase in total economic throughput—more consumer goods produced, shipped, and discarded—will continue to devastate the biosphere through resource extraction, habitat destruction, and non-carbon pollution. Clean energy requires rare earth metals, massive land use, and vast amounts of raw materials to construct.

If we take the climate crisis as seriously as Pollin claims we must (aiming for an 80 percent reduction in emissions by 2050), we cannot simply replace fossil-fuel capitalism with green-energy capitalism and expect to maintain the same trajectory of endless consumer growth. The goal should not be to maximize total purchasing power and market buoyancy. The goal should be to provide human well-being while dramatically shrinking the material footprint of the Global North.

How do we achieve full employment without supercharging ecological destruction? The answer is not just changing *what* we produce, but changing *how much* we work. We should be advocating for a drastic reduction in the working week.

If we reduced the standard workweek from 40 hours to 30 hours, or instituted a mandatory four-day workweek without a reduction in hourly pay for the working class, we could instantly distribute the available necessary work among the millions currently unemployed. This achieves Pollin’s goal of zero involuntary unemployment, but it does so by giving people back their time, rather than forcing them to produce more commodities to keep the GDP growing.

Shorter working hours have been proven to lower carbon footprints. People with more free time commute less, cook more of their own food rather than buying energy-intensive fast food, and engage in lower-impact leisure activities. Pollin’s vision of full employment relies on the mid-20th century industrial model of endless work and endless consumption. For a truly sustainable 21st century, we must redefine "a decent life" to mean not just a steady paycheck, but the time and freedom to enjoy it on a living planet.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The green-Keynesian approach of increasing purchasing power and market buoyancy is ecologically fatal because it ignores the environmental destruction caused by resource extraction and non-carbon pollution.
  2. 02Simply replacing fossil fuels with clean energy is insufficient because the production of green technology still requires vast amounts of raw materials and land use that devastate the biosphere.
  3. 03Reducing the standard workweek to 30 hours or four days would achieve zero involuntary unemployment by redistributing existing work rather than relying on unsustainable GDP growth.
  4. 04Shorter working hours naturally lower carbon footprints by reducing commuting and encouraging lower-impact, less energy-intensive lifestyles.
  5. 05A decent society should be defined by the time and freedom to enjoy life rather than the mid-20th-century industrial model of endless work and commodity production.
