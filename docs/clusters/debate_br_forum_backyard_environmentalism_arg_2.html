"While the authors' 'rolling-rule' model offers valuable insights into institutional learning and local participation, it unfairly dismisses market-based mechanisms that are often more efficient at decentralized information discovery and cost-effective environmental protection."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen present rolling-rule environmental regulation as an alternative to both command-and-control and market mechanisms. Their critique of traditional regulation is often convincing, but their dismissal of markets is too quick. The examples they admire—TRI, toxics-use planning, watershed management, Habitat Conservation Plans—do demonstrate that information, local knowledge, and institutional learning matter. They do not demonstrate that market-based approaches are inherently less capable of learning or less suited to complex environmental problems. On the contrary, many of the successes the authors attribute to deliberative democracy are better understood as examples of decentralized information and incentives—the very strengths of markets.

The essay’s treatment of tradable permits is oddly severe. It is true that permit markets require initial decisions about caps, allocations, monitoring, and enforcement. But every regulatory system requires such decisions. A command system must define pollutants, sources, technologies, compliance schedules, and penalties. A rolling-rule system must define participants, metrics, reporting obligations, benchmarks, and minimum standards. The need for information is not a distinctive flaw of markets; it is a condition of environmental governance. The relevant question is comparative: which institutional arrangement uses information most effectively, at least cost, and with the least susceptibility to political manipulation?

The sulfur dioxide trading program created under the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments is, as of 1999, an important test. It is not perfect, and the environmental problem it addresses is simpler than ecosystem management. But it has shown that a clear cap, reliable monitoring, and tradable allowances can reduce pollution at lower cost than technology mandates. The program did not require regulators to know in advance which plants could reduce cheaply. It allowed firms to reveal that knowledge through compliance choices. That is precisely the sort of decentralized discovery the lead essay celebrates. The difference is that markets coordinate discovery through prices rather than meetings.

Prices are not morally sufficient, but they are informationally powerful. If a firm can reduce a ton of pollution for less than the allowance price, it reduces and sells. If not, it buys. The result is not merely efficiency in the accountant’s sense. Lower compliance costs make stricter environmental goals politically and economically feasible. A regulatory method that wastes resources protecting the environment buys less environmental protection than one that economizes. Environmentalists should care about cost-effectiveness because every unnecessary dollar spent on one abatement method is a dollar unavailable for habitat, wastewater treatment, land acquisition, or public health.

The authors say artificial markets depend on secure ownership rights and therefore are hard to correct when initial allocations prove mistaken. But this is overstated. Allowances can be time-limited. Caps can decline. Banking rules can be adjusted prospectively. New information can inform future allocations. None of this is easy, but neither is revision of rolling standards. Indeed, deliberative systems may be more vulnerable to inertia because every change reopens bargains among stakeholders. Market systems at least separate the collective decision about the environmental goal—the cap—from the decentralized decision about means.

This distinction is a virtue. In the rolling-rule model, local participants set targets and devise means within broad limits. That sounds flexible, but it risks confusing public ends with private negotiations. A watershed committee dominated by agricultural interests may define “feasible” nutrient reductions differently from downstream fishers. An HCP negotiated by developers and a field office may trade away habitat under the banner of adaptive management. A toxics-planning process may identify reductions that save money but avoid those that impose costs. Markets cannot answer every environmental question, but when they work they create a transparent structure: government sets the aggregate limit; regulated actors decide how to meet it.

Of course, many environmental problems are not ready for full-blown permit markets. Nonpoint runoff is hard to monitor. Biodiversity cannot easily be reduced to fungible units. Toxics differ in toxicity, exposure pathway, persistence, and local impact. But these difficulties argue for careful design, not for elevating deliberation into a general theory. Water-quality trading within watersheds, wetland mitigation banking, transferable development rights, and conservation easements all attempt—imperfectly—to harness market incentives for land and ecosystem protection. Some will fail; some will improve. The same experimental humility the authors recommend for rolling rules should apply to markets.

TRI itself illustrates the point. The lead essay treats TRI as a precursor to democratic benchmarking. It is also a market-information instrument. Disclosure affects share prices, consumer behavior, employee morale, insurance costs, and community relations. Firms reduce emissions partly because the market penalizes bad environmental performance. Public information changes private incentives. That is not command, and it is not deliberation in any robust sense. It is reputational capitalism disciplined by transparency.

Massachusetts TURA likewise works partly because it reveals inefficiency. Many toxics are purchased inputs that later become wastes requiring handling, storage, reporting, and disposal. When firms examine processes carefully, they often discover savings. This is admirable, but it suggests that information failures within firms are important. Regulation can correct those failures by requiring audits or disclosure. But when reduction saves money, we should not exaggerate the democratic content of the result. Managers adopt cleaner processes because they are cheaper and less risky. When they are not cheaper, stronger incentives—taxes, fees, liability, permit limits—may be necessary.

The authors’ democratic claims also require scrutiny. Participation is valuable, but it is costly. Meetings consume time. Technical disputes can drag on. Consensus processes may empower those with the lowest opportunity cost of participation or the greatest ability to delay. The costs are not merely administrative. If a habitat plan takes years to negotiate, landowners face uncertainty and may preemptively develop land before restrictions tighten. If watershed committees avoid hard choices to maintain cooperation, pollution persists. If firms devote resources to planning documents rather than measurable reductions, public confidence erodes.

There is also a danger of local protectionism. National environmental law emerged partly because states and localities had incentives to attract investment by tolerating pollution. The lead essay says central authorities in rolling-rule regimes will monitor, benchmark, and enforce feasible standards. That is essential, but it brings back the very central functions the model is supposed to supersede. The center must still set floors, police data, sanction laggards, and correct local capture. If it does not, rolling regulation becomes devolution. If it does, the difference from well-designed performance standards plus market incentives is less dramatic than the authors suggest.

A more balanced architecture would combine the authors’ institutional learning with economic instruments. Use deliberative watershed bodies to identify ecological goals, monitoring points, and fairness constraints; then use nutrient trading or fees where sources can be measured. Use public HCP processes to define conservation priorities; then use conservation banking and transferable development rights to reduce the cost of protecting habitat. Use TRI-style disclosure to identify toxic releases; then consider chemical fees, liability rules, or insurance requirements that internalize risk. Use national benchmarking not only to shame laggards but to adjust caps, prices, and incentives.

The lead essay’s most persuasive sentence is its claim that expertise without local participation remains ignorant of crucial detail, while localism unprovoked by expertise remains parochial. I would add a third clause: deliberation without prices remains careless about costs. Environmental policy must learn, but it must also economize. Markets are not a substitute for public judgment about health, species, or the value of landscapes. They are tools for achieving public judgments more effectively. The task is not to move beyond markets, but to embed them in democratic institutions that set real environmental limits and revise them as knowledge improves.

Rolling rules deserve a place in the regulatory toolbox. They are especially useful where problems are complex, sources are diverse, and knowledge is widely dispersed. But the authors turn a useful tool into a governing ideal. The next generation of environmental reform should be experimental in a broader sense: willing to compare command standards, disclosure, liability, taxes, tradable permits, collaborative planning, and direct public investment. The measure should be environmental improvement, cost, fairness, and accountability—not whether an instrument fits a particular democratic theory.

"While performance-based, collaborative regulation offers a superior alternative to centralized command-and-control, it must be grounded in the protection of private property rights, clear legal limits on agency discretion, and the use of market-based incentives to ensure fairness and efficiency."

The lead essay identifies a real failure of environmental policy: centralized regulation too often issues commands without understanding local conditions. Farmers, landowners, small businesses, and local governments have lived with that failure for years. The Endangered Species Act can turn the discovery of a listed species into a land-use crisis. Wetlands rules can make ordinary property decisions legally perilous. Water-quality mandates can impose costs on communities that had little role in designing them. If “rolling-rule” regulation means that federal agencies will finally listen to local knowledge and allow practical problem solving, it deserves support.

But the essay’s democratic language should not obscure a hard question: will collaboration replace coercion, or merely make coercion more polite? Many Habitat Conservation Plans exist because landowners face the ESA’s severe take prohibition. When a landowner “voluntarily” dedicates large tracts as habitat in exchange for permission to use the rest, one may call it negotiation, but the background threat is unmistakable. Some environmentalists describe HCPs as compromise; some landowners see legalized extortion. Both perceptions contain truth.

The authors admire HCPs because they can produce ecosystem-scale planning superior to rigid species-by-species enforcement. That may be so. But a fair system must respect property rights. Landowners are not merely stakeholders in a collective ecological project. They are citizens with legal and moral claims to the use of their land. If the public wants habitat preserved, the public should often pay for it—through purchase, conservation easements, tax incentives, or compensation—not simply condition permits until private owners surrender value.

Rolling-rule regimes may actually provide a path toward fairness if designed correctly. Local collaboration can identify conservation measures that impose lower costs and produce better ecological outcomes than blanket prohibitions. It can distinguish land crucial to species survival from land of marginal value. It can encourage voluntary easements, habitat banking, and transferable development rights. It can align public conservation goals with private stewardship. Many landowners care deeply about their land and wildlife; they resent being treated as enemies.

The Chesapeake Bay Program also shows the potential of cooperation. Farmers are often blamed for nonpoint pollution, but they operate under tight margins and variable conditions. A uniform command from Washington cannot know the slope, soil, drainage, crop rotation, and weather patterns of each farm. Tributary teams and locally tailored best management practices are more sensible. They can achieve nutrient reductions while respecting the practical knowledge of those who work the land. Technical assistance and cost-sharing will usually accomplish more than threats.

Yet the lead essay remains too confident in central oversight. The authors insist that rolling-rule regulation is not devolution because central authorities will monitor, benchmark, and enforce standards. That reassurance may calm environmentalists, but it worries those who have seen federal agencies expand authority through vague standards. If local plans are constantly reviewed by Washington, if benchmarks become de facto mandates, if “continuous improvement” means requirements can always be ratcheted upward, local autonomy will be more appearance than reality.

A legitimate rolling-rule system must define limits on agency discretion. Landowners and local governments need predictability. If they enter an HCP or watershed agreement, comply with its terms, and invest in required measures, they should not face endless reopening of the bargain. Adaptive management is reasonable for unforeseen ecological developments, but it should not become a blank check. The Clinton administration’s “No Surprises” policy in HCPs reflects a real need: without some assurance, few landowners will participate. If every agreement can be revised whenever an agency or interest group demands more, cooperation will collapse.

This does not mean plans should ignore biological reality. If a species is declining because promised measures are not being implemented, enforcement is appropriate. If monitoring reveals a problem anticipated in the plan, agreed contingency measures should apply. But if the government wants additional conservation because science has changed or public preferences have shifted, the burden should not automatically fall on the permit holder. Public goals require public responsibility.

The essay’s skepticism toward market mechanisms is also misplaced. Markets and property rights are not obstacles to environmental protection; they are often the best way to secure it. Conservation easements have protected millions of acres because they respect ownership while purchasing public benefits. Tradable permits can reduce pollution efficiently. Water markets, if carefully governed, can move resources toward higher-valued uses while maintaining instream flows. Habitat banking can allow development in less sensitive areas while funding restoration or preservation where it matters more. These tools are not perfect, but neither are stakeholder committees.

Indeed, markets can solve some problems that deliberation cannot. Local committees are prone to conflict, delay, and capture by activists or officials. Prices reveal information about costs and preferences that meetings may conceal. If society wants more habitat, paying for habitat reveals the real cost of that choice. If polluters must pay for emissions, they have reason to reduce without regulators specifying methods. A practical environmentalism should combine local knowledge with market incentives, not oppose them.

The authors are right, however, that pure voluntarism is insufficient for some problems. Responsible Care shows that industry self-regulation can become public relations. The question is not whether public authority has a role; it does. The question is whether public authority is disciplined by law, federalism, and respect for ownership. National environmental goals should be clear. Agencies should provide science, information, and enforcement against genuine harm. But local communities and states should have wide latitude in choosing means, and private property should not be treated as a free reservoir of public amenities.

The democratic rhetoric of “deep use” also deserves scrutiny. Many citizens do not want to become quasi-regulators. They want to run farms, build homes, operate businesses, raise families, and enjoy parks and streams. Participation is valuable, but it can become burdensome. A system that requires endless meetings may empower professional activists, consultants, and agency staff rather than ordinary citizens. The truly ordinary citizen may prefer clear rules, prompt permits, compensation when public burdens are imposed, and the freedom to make responsible decisions.

Still, the essay points toward a constructive compromise. Environmental law should move away from one-size-fits-all commands and toward negotiated, performance-based approaches. But those approaches must be voluntary in a meaningful sense, bounded by property rights, supported by incentives, and constrained by clear statutes. The federal government should set broad goals and supply information; states and localities should adapt them; landowners should be partners, not targets; and when the public asks private citizens to provide public goods, it should be prepared to share the cost.

Backyard environmentalism began as a defense of home against distant powers. For many rural landowners and small communities, distant power now includes national environmental regulation itself. A new environmentalism will succeed only if it recognizes that stewardship and liberty must be reconciled, not traded off in closed-door bargaining.
