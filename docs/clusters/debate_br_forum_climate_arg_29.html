"The climate movement must prioritize comprehensive democratic reform as a "step zero" because current counter-majoritarian institutions are structurally designed to protect fossil capital from the popular will."

In “Climate, State, and Utopia,” Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò offers a compelling argument for a "two-step" political strategy, arguing that the left must prioritize seizing state power to dismantle fossil capital before it can hope to realize its broader utopian horizons. His critique of anti-statist tendencies within liberation movements is astute, and his insistence on confronting the material realities of climate change is deeply necessary. However, Táíwò’s strategic blueprint suffers from a profound institutional blind spot. He treats "the state" as a functional apparatus that can be wielded by whichever political coalition manages to win the battle of ideas. In reality, the state—particularly the United States government, which remains central to global climate trajectories—is not a neutral tool; it is constitutionally and institutionally gerrymandered to prevent exactly the kind of majority-backed, anti-capitalist climate action Táíwò envisions.

Before we can even attempt Táíwò’s "step one" (dethroning fossil capital), we face an unavoidable "step zero": comprehensive democratic reform to break the minoritarian stranglehold on state institutions.

Táíwò notes that states have institutional interests in maintaining fossil fuel capacity and that fossil fuel interests have amassed immense political power. But he underestimates the extent to which this power is hardwired into the architectural structure of the democratic system itself. In the United States, the Senate inherently overrepresents sparsely populated, rural, and resource-extractive states. A state like Wyoming, a massive coal and gas producer, holds the same structural power in the upper chamber as California, a massive pioneer in renewables. This geographical malapportionment essentially grants fossil capital a permanent, structural veto over federal climate legislation.

This minoritarian bias is compounded by the Electoral College, heavily gerrymandered House districts, and a campaign finance system flooded with dark money following *Citizens United*. Furthermore, even if a progressive coalition were to miraculously navigate this rigged electoral map and pass something akin to the Build Public Renewables Act at the federal level, they would immediately face a reactionary judiciary. The current Supreme Court, heavily influenced by the conservative legal movement (which itself is funded by fossil fuel billionaires like Charles Koch), has systematically dismantled the administrative state’s capacity to regulate greenhouse gases. Rulings like *West Virginia v. EPA* severely limit the EPA's authority to mandate systemic energy transitions, signaling that the Court will ruthlessly strike down any attempt to use executive power to dethrone fossil capital.

Táíwò suggests we can "simply" win elections to initiate his two-step process, or if that fails, we must find a way to align state interests against capital. But how does a movement do that when the institutional rules of the game are designed specifically to insulate capital from popular majorities?

Poll after poll shows that the American public supports aggressive action on climate change, desires a transition to renewable energy, and opposes the corrupting influence of fossil fuel companies. The barrier to action is not a lack of public will or a failure of the left to articulate a vision; the barrier is a set of counter-majoritarian institutions that allow a fossil-fueled minority to rule.

Therefore, any serious climate strategy cannot treat democratic reform as a secondary issue or a distraction. The fight to abolish the filibuster, expand and reform the Supreme Court, secure voting rights, and publicly finance elections is not a separate, liberal pet project distinct from the radical climate agenda. It is the absolute precondition for passing the radical climate agenda. If we ignore the mechanics of how state power is actually constructed and distributed, all calls to "seize the state" amount to little more than rhetorical posturing.

Táíwò is right to warn against the creeping authoritarianism represented by figures like Trump, Orbán, and Modi. But we must understand that this authoritarianism is fueled by fossil capital precisely to lock in its power as demographics and popular opinion turn against it. Fossil capital knows it cannot win a fair democratic fight in the twenty-first century, so it is aggressively dismantling democracy.

To achieve the energy transition, the climate movement must become a democracy movement. We cannot adopt a "two-step" strategy where we defeat ExxonMobil first and fix our broken political institutions later. We must fix the institutions in order to defeat ExxonMobil. The state is a weapon, yes, but it is currently locked in a glass case, and fossil capital holds the only key. We must smash the case—through sweeping, structural democratic reform—before we can take aim at the true enemy.
