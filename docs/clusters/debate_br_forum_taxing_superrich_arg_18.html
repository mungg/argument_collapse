Diversified Gemini

"While the goal of reducing wealth inequality is noble, Saez and Zucman’s proposed wealth tax—specifically the 'pay-in-kind' mechanism for private assets—is an administratively impossible scheme that would collapse due to the complexities of corporate law and valuation."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman’s proposal for a radical wealth tax on the superrich is driven by a noble pursuit of democratic equality. Their analysis of the regressive nature of the current tax system is spot-on, and their historical context regarding top marginal rates is illuminating. However, as any tax practitioner or legal scholar will attest, the devil of tax policy is entirely in the details of administration and enforcement. When we examine the specific mechanisms the authors propose for valuing and taxing illiquid assets—specifically their "pay-in-kind" scheme for private businesses—their policy transitions from an ambitious economic theory to an administrative catastrophe.

The central hurdle of any wealth tax is valuation. While valuing publicly traded stocks like Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway is trivial, a massive portion of the wealth held by the top 0.1 percent consists of closely held private businesses, complex partnerships, real estate portfolios, and intellectual property. Saez and Zucman attempt to wave away this profound difficulty by citing the example of Cargill, a massive private agribusiness. They claim that the IRS can simply update Cargill’s 1992 valuation based on profit growth and peer comparisons, and if the owners disagree, they can hand over 2 percent of their shares to the IRS to be auctioned off.

This proposal demonstrates a shocking naiveté regarding corporate law, valuation discounts, and market mechanics. Let us play out the authors' "pay-in-kind" scenario in reality.

Suppose the MacMillan family, owners of Cargill, dispute the IRS valuation. To pay their wealth tax, they transfer 2 percent of Cargill’s stock to the federal government. The IRS is now tasked with converting this illiquid asset into cash to fund government operations. The authors state the IRS will "sell the shares to the highest bidders on a market open to any and all bidders."

But what exactly is the IRS selling? They are selling a minority, non-controlling interest in a closely held family corporation. In the world of finance and tax law, minority interests in private companies are subject to massive "lack of marketability" and "lack of control" discounts. A venture capital firm or private equity fund will not pay anywhere near the intrinsic per-share value of Cargill for a 2 percent stake, because a 2 percent stake grants no power to force dividends, direct corporate strategy, or compel a sale of the company. The outside investor is entirely at the mercy of the MacMillan family.

Consequently, the only rational buyer for these shares at anywhere near their actual value is the MacMillan family themselves. The auction would be a farce. Outside investors would bid pennies on the dollar, recognizing the trapped nature of the minority stake. The MacMillan family could then swoop in, buy back their own shares from the IRS at a 50 or 60 percent discount from the IRS's original estimated value, and essentially slash their wealth tax bill in half. The government would collect a fraction of the expected revenue, and the family would retain full ownership.

To prevent this, the government would have to radically rewrite corporate law, perhaps requiring private companies to grant draconian shareholder rights to whoever buys the government-auctioned shares. This would violently disrupt the governance of every private company in America.

Furthermore, consider the scale of what the authors are proposing. There are tens of thousands of private businesses, family limited partnerships (FLPs), and complex offshore trusts subject to this tax. Is the IRS—an agency whose primary function is processing tax returns and auditing income—supposed to become the largest private equity broker in the world? The IRS would have to establish an entire new bureaucracy dedicated to holding, managing, and auctioning off minority stakes in thousands of varied enterprises, from mid-sized manufacturing firms in Ohio to complex real estate syndicates in Manhattan.

The potential for corruption, political favoritism, and market manipulation in these government-run auctions would be immense. Lobbyists would descend on the IRS auction house. Well-connected private equity firms would make a killing buying up distressed minority stakes at fire-sale prices from a government desperate to liquidate assets to meet revenue targets.

Moreover, the "pay-in-kind" solution does nothing to address the valuation of non-corporate assets. How does one pay in kind for an art collection? Does the billionaire hand over the Mona Lisa's frame? Do they saw off a room of their Aspen mansion and deed it to the Treasury? The authors ignore the vast ecosystem of bespoke financial products and luxury assets that cannot be subdivided and auctioned.

The administrative burden of a wealth tax will inevitably lead to a massive boom in the tax avoidance industry. The brightest legal and financial minds will be paid millions to construct asset structures specifically designed to exploit the valuation rules. We will see an explosion of opaque trusts, artificial debt structures to lower net worth, and the transfer of value into hard-to-value intellectual property. The IRS, chronically underfunded and outgunned, will spend decades mired in tax court, fighting over the discount rate applied to a specialized manufacturing patent or the marketability of a family limited partnership.

We do not need to invent an administratively impossible wealth tax to force the rich to pay their share. We have an income tax. If the problem is that billionaires borrow against their unrealized gains, we can change the law to treat loans secured by highly appreciated assets as realization events for tax purposes. We can institute mark-to-market accounting for tradable securities. We can close the step-up in basis loophole at death. These are complex tasks, but they operate within the established framework of the income tax and the existing competencies of the IRS.

Saez and Zucman's diagnosis is vital, but their "pay-in-kind" wealth tax is an academic thought experiment that would collapse upon contact with the real world of corporate finance and legal tax avoidance. We must reform the system we have, not create a new one doomed to fail.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'pay-in-kind' auction system would fail because minority stakes in private companies are subject to massive marketability discounts, allowing the wealthy to buy back their own shares at a fraction of their value.
  2. 02Implementing the proposal would require the IRS to transform into a massive, impractical private equity brokerage capable of managing and selling stakes in thousands of diverse private enterprises.
  3. 03The government-run auction process for private shares would create significant opportunities for political corruption, favoritism, and market manipulation by well-connected lobbyists and firms.
  4. 04The authors' proposal fails to address how non-corporate, indivisible assets like art or real estate could be practically taxed 'in kind.'
  5. 05A wealth tax would trigger a massive surge in the tax avoidance industry, leading to decades of litigation over complex asset structures that the IRS is ill-equipped to handle.
  6. 06Rather than creating an administratively disastrous new tax, the government should reform the existing income tax system by closing loopholes and treating loans against appreciated assets as realization events.
