"A radical wealth tax should be resisted because it undermines property rights, creates dangerous government power, and punishes productive success rather than addressing the root causes of inequality."

The case Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman make for a wealth tax is powerful because it appeals to a genuine anxiety: Americans worry that the game is rigged. They see billionaires funding campaigns, companies lobbying for special treatment, and families struggling despite working hard. It is tempting to conclude that the solution is to tax great fortunes until they shrink. But that temptation should be resisted. A radical wealth tax would damage property rights, politicize investment, punish productive success, and give government officials a dangerous new power over private life.

The premise of the argument is that “wealth is power.” This is partly true, but incomplete. Wealth can buy influence, yet government is power too. Indeed, government is the institution that can compel, prohibit, imprison, tax, subsidize, license, and redistribute. When wealthy people seek political favors, the problem is not only that they have money; it is that government has favors to sell. A tax system designed to reduce private fortunes may make the state more powerful without making ordinary citizens freer.

Saez and Zucman invoke Madison, but Madison’s constitutional project was not simply to equalize property. It was to control factions through representation, divided powers, federalism, and the rule of law. The American answer to concentrated power has traditionally been institutional restraint: prevent any one faction, class, or branch from dominating. A wealth tax that deliberately aims to reduce the number of multibillionaires may satisfy egalitarian impulses, but it risks creating a new kind of arbitrary power—the power of government to decide each year how much private property is too much.

There is a moral difference between taxing income and taxing wealth. Income is a flow. Wealth is accumulated property, often built from income that has already been taxed at the corporate, individual, property, sales, or estate level. To impose an annual tax on the stock is to say that ownership itself requires annual rent paid to the state. At low rates on real estate, Americans have long accepted this. But extending that principle to all assets, especially at rates of 6, 8, or 10 percent, is a radical transformation of property rights. At a 10 percent annual rate, a fortune can be cut dramatically even if the owner earns no current income. Saez and Zucman are candid that this is the point. That candor should alarm us.

The authors compare extreme wealth to carbon emissions, a negative externality. But wealth is not pollution. A fortune invested in a company is not smoke in the air. It may represent factories, software, logistics networks, research laboratories, consumer products, and jobs. To be sure, some fortunes are created through monopoly, cronyism, or rent-seeking. Those abuses should be addressed directly through antitrust law, deregulation where regulation protects incumbents, patent reform where patents are abused, and the elimination of subsidies and bailouts. But to treat the mere existence of a large fortune as a social harm is to confuse envy with analysis.

Consider Amazon. Many people dislike Jeff Bezos’s wealth, and there are legitimate questions about Amazon’s market power and labor practices. But Amazon also transformed retail logistics, cloud computing, and consumer convenience. Its market value reflects the expectations of millions of investors and customers. A wealth tax that forces founders to sell shares annually may not destroy Amazon, but it changes incentives for entrepreneurs and investors. If the reward for building a transformative company is capped by an annual levy designed to prevent “remaining” too wealthy, some future founders may build elsewhere, sell earlier, or avoid scaling ambitious companies.

Saez and Zucman say a radical tax would not make it harder to become a billionaire, only harder to remain a multibillionaire. That distinction is less reassuring than it sounds. Entrepreneurship is motivated not only by the first billion dollars. Founders often retain control because they believe they can continue building over decades. Long-term ownership can be valuable. A tax that gradually forces dilution may shift control from founders to financial buyers, public funds, or the government’s auction process. Is that obviously better? The authors assume that deconcentration is inherently democratic. But dispersed ownership through institutional investors can also create unaccountable power, and government-mediated sales of private shares could invite favoritism and political manipulation.

The practical problems are serious. Saez and Zucman minimize valuation difficulties by noting that financial analysts value private firms all the time. But private valuations are estimates, not facts. Analysts disagree. Values depend on assumptions about growth, risk, control, liquidity, and market conditions. In ordinary markets, errors are disciplined by voluntary transactions. A tax assessment is different: the taxpayer must pay based on the government’s number. The authors’ proposed solution—allowing payment in kind and government auction of shares—could create pressure to sell pieces of private businesses into thin markets. Minority stakes in family firms may fetch discounts. Buyers may demand confidential information. Competitors may seek access. The process could become a bonanza for lawyers, accountants, and politically connected funds.

The claim that liquidity concerns are mostly bad faith is too dismissive. Many valuable companies do not produce cash flows proportionate to their valuations, especially in technology and biotechnology. A founder whose company is valued highly but not profitable may be forced to sell shares simply to pay tax. Perhaps Saez and Zucman welcome that result. But it means the tax falls not on consumed luxury or realized gain, but on uncertain expectations about the future. If those expectations later prove wrong, the tax will have accelerated loss of control for no good reason.

There is also the problem of avoidance. The richer the taxpayer and the higher the rate, the stronger the incentive to restructure assets, relocate, renounce citizenship, litigate valuations, use trusts, shift ownership to foundations, or move capital abroad. The authors reply that political will can solve these problems. Political will is not a magic wand. European countries experimented with wealth taxes; many abandoned them because they raised less revenue than hoped and created administrative and avoidance problems. The United States is larger and has more sophisticated capital markets, but it also has exceptionally sophisticated tax planning and constitutional constraints.

Those constitutional constraints matter. A federal wealth tax may be an unapportioned direct tax. Supporters argue otherwise, but no one can honestly deny the risk. To enact a major tax that may be struck down is not bold; it may be irresponsible. If the aim is to raise revenue for public goods, lawmakers should use clearly constitutional instruments: income taxes, consumption taxes, estate taxes, and user fees.

What should be done instead? First, remove special privileges. Tax carried interest as ordinary income. Reduce crony subsidies. End regulations that protect incumbents from competition. Simplify the tax code so that wealth cannot buy loopholes unavailable to everyone else. Second, enforce antitrust law against genuine monopolistic conduct, not bigness as such. Third, improve education, housing supply, occupational licensing, and labor mobility so that more Americans can build wealth of their own. Fourth, if more revenue is needed, broaden the tax base rather than pretending that a tiny class of billionaires can fund every ambition of the modern state.

A society committed to opportunity should care less about pulling down the rich and more about lifting the poor and middle class. That means wage growth, affordable housing, good schools, safe neighborhoods, entrepreneurship, and family stability. A wealth tax does little to address these foundations. It may finance programs, but only if it works as advertised; and if its deeper purpose is to reduce fortunes rather than raise revenue, its success will eventually shrink the base it taxes.

The political danger is perhaps greatest of all. Once government claims the authority to impose annual taxes on net worth above a high threshold, the threshold may not stay high. Income tax history itself warns us: taxes introduced for the very rich often expand. Supporters will say that Warren’s and Sanders’s proposals affect fewer than 0.1 percent of households. But future fiscal pressures could lead politicians to lower exemptions. The middle class already pays property taxes, Saez and Zucman note. That is true—and many homeowners find those taxes burdensome. Extending annual wealth taxation to financial assets may begin with billionaires, but the principle has no natural stopping point.

The United States should not be indifferent to corruption or unfairness. If billionaires use government to enrich themselves, stop them. If tax rules allow indefinite avoidance, reform those rules. If campaign finance law permits undue influence, debate constitutional ways to reduce it. But do not confuse all large fortunes with theft, or imagine that democracy is strengthened whenever the state cuts private citizens down to size.

A free society will always have unequal outcomes because people differ in talent, luck, ambition, thrift, risk tolerance, and timing. The task of public policy is to preserve equal rights and broad opportunity, not equalize fortunes by force. Saez and Zucman have identified real frustrations, but their remedy would move America toward a politics of confiscation. That is not tax justice. It is a dangerous bargain: surrender more power to government in the hope that it will use that power only against people we resent.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Wealth is not a social harm like pollution, and taxing it as such ignores the productive value of invested capital in creating jobs and innovation.
  2. 02A wealth tax transforms the nature of property rights by requiring citizens to pay annual rent to the state for the mere privilege of ownership.
  3. 03The proposed solution for valuing private assets—government-mediated auctions and in-kind payments—would politicize investment and create opportunities for favoritism and corruption.
  4. 04High tax rates on wealth would disincentivize long-term entrepreneurship and force founders to cede control of their companies to unaccountable financial buyers.
  5. 05The tax would likely face significant constitutional challenges and practical avoidance issues that have led many European nations to abandon similar policies.
  6. 06True reform should focus on eliminating cronyism, enforcing antitrust laws, and expanding opportunity for the middle class rather than confiscating private fortunes.
  7. 07History suggests that wealth taxes initially aimed at the ultra-rich would eventually be expanded to target a broader segment of the population to meet fiscal demands.
