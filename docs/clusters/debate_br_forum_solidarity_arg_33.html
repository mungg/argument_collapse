"Deference politics should be defended and supplemented rather than replaced by epistemic humility, as explicit deference structures are necessary to prevent the reproduction of dominant power dynamics and to ensure organizational functionality."

Mie Inouye's essay is a significant intervention in debates about solidarity, and I want to engage with its central argument about deference politics. Inouye, drawing on Olúfémi Táíwò's critique, argues that deference politics treats people as "fully determined" by their social location and assumes full transparency about motivations and interests. This leads to fragile coalitions and evades necessary conflict. I want to defend deference politics against this critique while also arguing that it needs supplementation rather than replacement.

My defense of deference politics begins with an observation about power. In organizing contexts, the relative privilege of some participants is not merely a matter of their social location; it is an active dynamic that shapes what happens in the room. When white participants or middle-class participants speak first, they set the terms of discussion. When they dominate discussion, they marginalize others. The traditional response to this dynamic has been to explicitly create space for less privileged voices—through formal structures that prioritize certain perspectives, through norms of listening before speaking, through practices of checking privilege.

Inouye's concern is that these practices assume "full transparency" about social location and "full determination" by structural position. But this concern, while intellectually legitimate, underestimates the practical value of deference norms. In my experience, the alternative to explicit deference structures is not more honest engagement but the reproduction of dominant patterns. When organizations abandon norms that create space for marginalized voices, those voices tend to disappear. Not because their holders are being "determined" by theory, but because they are being dominated by real people in real meetings.

There is also a pragmatic argument for deference politics. Organizations need to function, which means they need to make decisions and move to action. Decision-making processes that fully open every question to contestation—about who has standing to speak, whose analysis is correct, whose interests are really at stake—can paralyze organizations. Explicit norms about who gets to define the terms of discussion, even when those norms are imperfect and even when they involve a certain fiction about transparency and determination, can enable organizations to act.

This pragmatic argument does not resolve the deeper epistemological question about whether Táíwò's critique of deference politics is correct. But it does suggest that the alternative—radical openness to contestation—has its own costs and risks. Organizations that embrace constant conflict over fundamental questions may fail to develop the internal solidarity necessary for sustained action. The choice is not between perfect theory and imperfect practice; it is between imperfect practices that enable action and perfect theories that produce paralysis.

Inouye's alternative of "epistemic humility"—holding beliefs open to challenge while retaining ultimate responsibility for judgment—sounds reasonable in the abstract. But I worry about how it translates into organizational practice. When a white organizer disagrees with a Black organizer about how to respond to police violence, what does "epistemic humility" require? Does it mean deferring to the Black organizer's analysis? Does it mean maintaining open disagreement indefinitely? Does it mean seeking some third position that transcends both perspectives? Inouye does not fully specify.

I want to suggest that deference politics and epistemic humility are not opposites but complements. Deference structures create conditions in which less privileged voices can be heard. Epistemic humility, practiced by all participants, creates conditions in which those voices can be engaged without being tokenized. The combination is not perfect, but it is more practical than either approach alone.

Inouye's essay contributes to an ongoing conversation about how to organize across difference. I hope that conversation continues, and that it includes more attention to the organizational practices that enable rather than disable collective action. Theory is important; so is pragmatism. Inouye's essay models the former. We also need models of the latter.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Explicit deference norms are essential practical tools to counteract the tendency of privileged participants to dominate discussions and marginalize less privileged voices in real-world meetings.
  2. 02Deference politics provides a pragmatic framework for decision-making that prevents organizations from becoming paralyzed by constant contestation over standing and interests.
  3. 03The concept of epistemic humility lacks clear organizational application and fails to provide a concrete mechanism for resolving disagreements between differently situated organizers.
  4. 04Deference politics and epistemic humility function best as complementary practices where formal structures ensure marginalized voices are heard while humility ensures those voices are engaged authentically.
