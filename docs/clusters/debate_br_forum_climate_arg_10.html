"While defeating fossil capital is necessary, relying on state-centered politics as the primary path to climate justice risks reproducing the same extractive and oppressive systems that created the crisis."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò’s essay is a bracing call to seriousness. Yet I worry that its seriousness is too quickly identified with the state. The argument is not crude statism; Táíwò knows the state’s colonial origins, its carceral present, its capture by capital, and its dependence on coercion. But the conclusion remains that the “likeliest path” to defeating fossil capital runs through state politics. For those of us shaped by abolitionist, anarchist, Indigenous, and autonomist traditions, this is precisely where the warning bells ring. The state is not merely an imperfect instrument that can be turned toward emancipation if the right coalition gains leverage. It is one of the principal forms through which extraction is organized.

The lead essay asks state skeptics to explain how withdrawal from state politics could plausibly end in the planetary defeat of fossil capital. That is a fair challenge. But the reverse challenge is equally necessary: advocates of a state-centered two-step politics must explain why the attempt to capture, discipline, or realign the state will not reproduce the very forms of domination that made fossil capitalism possible.

The modern state did not stumble into fossil fuels. Coal, oil, and gas were bound up with state formation, imperial war, border-making, infrastructure, and development. States built railways, navies, highways, electrical grids, pipelines, and military supply chains. They subsidized extraction, secured concessions, policed workers, displaced Indigenous peoples, and treated land as a resource base. The fossil economy is not just a private capitalist imposition on an otherwise neutral public authority. It is a joint project of capital and state power.

This is why public ownership, by itself, is an ambiguous slogan. Táíwò acknowledges Norway and Saudi Arabia, but the problem goes deeper. Nationalization has often meant the nationalization of extractive purpose. State-owned oil companies are among the largest fossil fuel producers in the world. Many states defend them not because a few corrupt officials have been bought, but because state managers understand national power through revenue, growth, export earnings, military capacity, and energy-intensive development. A public company can poison a river. A public grid can be built on stolen land. A public development bank can finance sacrifice zones. A public police force can repress those who object.

The danger of “first dethrone fossil capital; then transform the world” is that the second step can be indefinitely deferred. Liberation movements know this pattern intimately. First win independence, then women’s freedom. First defend the revolution, then democracy. First grow the economy, then redistribute. First defeat the external enemy, then address internal hierarchy. The “then” is where emancipatory promises go to die. Táíwò tries to avoid this by insisting that struggles retain equal moral worth even when they differ in temporal priority. But temporal priority has political effects. Movements told to wait often lose organizational power. Communities asked to sacrifice for a larger transition often discover that the benefits are captured elsewhere.

Climate politics already contains examples of this danger. Large renewable projects can reproduce extractive relations if they are imposed on Indigenous territories without consent. Lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earth supply chains can become new frontiers of dispossession. Transmission lines can be routed through rural and Native lands for the benefit of distant cities. Hydropower can flood communities. Conservation policy can become green colonialism. If the first step is defined too narrowly as rapid decarbonization through state capacity, then those harmed by the transition will be told they are obstructing history. That is not climate justice. It is fossil modernity with different inputs.

A more convincing climate politics would begin not with the state but with relations of care, land, subsistence, and democratic control. This does not mean small-is-beautiful nostalgia or atmospheric irrelevance. It means recognizing that the legitimacy of any transition depends on the social forms through which it is built. Energy democracy cannot be an administrative program handed down by benevolent planners. It must be rooted in communities that can say no, workers who can govern production, tenants who can control housing retrofits, and Indigenous nations whose sovereignty is not overridden by emergency rhetoric.

Táíwò cites Chiapas and Rojava as admirable but limited examples of autonomy. The implication is that they carve out niches but cannot solve a planetary problem. Perhaps. But they also reveal something state-centered politics often forgets: durable transformation requires institutions of everyday self-rule. The Zapatistas have survived not because they captured the Mexican state, but because they built systems of education, health, land use, and collective decision-making that made autonomy material. Rojava, under conditions of war and geopolitical abandonment, has tried to institutionalize gender liberation and plural governance in ways that many recognized states have never attempted. These projects are imperfect and vulnerable, but their vulnerability is not proof of irrelevance. It is evidence of the hostile world any emancipatory politics must face.

The state-centered strategist may respond: fine, build autonomy, but ExxonMobil and the Pentagon will still emit. True. But the same can be said of state reforms. Pass a public renewables bill in New York, and Saudi Aramco still drills. Elect a climate government in one country, and capital flight begins. Create a national green bank, and courts, trade agreements, bond markets, or right-wing backlash may constrain it. No strategy escapes scale. The question is not whether local autonomy alone can solve the climate crisis. It cannot. The question is whether autonomous institutions are secondary ornaments to a state project or the indispensable base from which any legitimate planetary coordination must grow.

Movements should contest the state, but contestation is not the same as faith in state power. We can make demands on the state without making the state the horizon of politics. Abolitionists have long understood this distinction. Demanding that a city close a jail, reduce a police budget, or fund non-police crisis response does not require believing that the city government is the agent of liberation. It means forcing concessions while building capacities outside the carceral state. Climate movements can do likewise: block pipelines, stop export terminals, force disclosure, win moratoria, redirect subsidies, defend land defenders, create community energy systems, build mutual aid for climate disasters, and establish worker-community assemblies. The point is not purity; it is orientation.

Táíwò’s formulation risks treating “fossil capital” as the loadbearing enemy while treating the state as a contested terrain. But for many communities, the state is not terrain; it is the immediate adversary. It is the police at the pipeline blockade, the court enforcing injunctions, the agency approving permits, the border guard stopping climate migrants, the military consuming vast quantities of fuel, the debt negotiator demanding austerity, the development authority seizing land. When people say they are wary of demands that give the state power over their realization, they are not indulging in lifestyle radicalism. They are describing lived political knowledge.

A better two-step might be this: first build the organized social power to make extraction ungovernable; then use every available institution, including but not limited to states, to wind it down and repair its harms. That reverses the emphasis. The decisive question becomes not how to align state interests against capital, but how to create popular power that can discipline both state and capital. Sometimes that will involve elections. Sometimes litigation. Sometimes municipalization. Sometimes sabotage of machinery, if one accepts Andreas Malm’s argument. Sometimes strikes. Sometimes Indigenous jurisdiction. Sometimes refusal.

The climate movement should be wary of any strategy that measures success primarily in gigawatts built or emissions reduced while leaving domination intact. Of course emissions matter. Physics is real; carbon budgets are unforgiving. But politics that treats justice as something to be completed after decarbonization may produce a low-carbon world of mines, borders, surveillance, militarized adaptation, and sacrifice zones. That world is not a stepping-stone to liberation. It is a new regime to be fought.

The most powerful sentence in Táíwò’s essay may be: “It’s the getting more, not the wanting, that is the problem.” I agree. But getting more requires refusing the blackmail of scale, the idea that because the crisis is planetary, salvation must come from the largest coercive institutions now available. The state can be forced to retreat, to pay, to prohibit, to build, to recognize, and to repair. But it should not be trusted to lead.

Climate justice must dethrone fossil capital. On that point, Táíwò is right. But if it enthrones the state in the process, even temporarily, it may discover that the throne itself is part of the machinery we needed to dismantle.
