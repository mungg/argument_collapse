"The global open-source AI ecosystem provides the best existing practical framework for achieving the 'eolithic' and democratic technological future that Morozov envisions."

Evgeny Morozov’s essay is at its best when it recalls that technological paths are contingent. Artificial intelligence did not have to grow from the priorities of Cold War funders, bureaucratic rationalizers, and corporate platforms. It could have been otherwise. But his account underplays one of the most important ways it is already becoming otherwise: the unruly, imperfect, global open-source AI ecosystem.

To be sure, open source is no magic solvent for power. Open models can be captured by corporations, used for surveillance, exploited by malicious actors, or maintained by underpaid volunteers. They can reproduce the same data injustices and cultural biases as closed systems. Yet if Morozov is searching for eolithic possibility—playful repurposing, vernacular adaptation, unexpected recombination—then open-source AI is not a side issue. It is one of the few existing spaces where that possibility is visible at scale.

The past two years have shown that generative AI is not confined to the largest proprietary labs. Meta’s Llama releases, Mistral’s models, Stability AI’s earlier image tools, EleutherAI’s research culture, Hugging Face’s model repositories, countless fine-tunes, quantizations, adapters, local inference projects, and domain-specific experiments have created a lively ecology of tinkering. People run smaller models on laptops. Communities fine-tune systems for minority languages, role-playing games, legal research, coding help, accessibility tools, and local archives. Researchers inspect weights, compare methods, and build alternatives. Hobbyists stitch together speech recognition, text generation, image models, retrieval systems, and home automation in ways no product manager would have approved.

This looks very much like Morozov’s stonefield. A model released for one purpose becomes a translation aid, a writing partner, a game master, a tutor, a dataset cleaner, a search interface, a tool for blind users, a companion for language preservation, or a component in an art installation. The users are not merely consumers. They are modifiers. They do not just prompt; they fork, fine-tune, compress, evaluate, and share. The results are uneven, often fragile, sometimes irresponsible. But eolithic craft was never tidy.

Closed AI systems, by contrast, are anti-eolithic even when they feel flexible. They invite experimentation at the surface while forbidding deeper alteration. You may ask the model anything allowed by policy, but you cannot examine its training mix, change its weights, run it without permission, adapt it fully to your community, or preserve it if the company changes terms. The interface simulates openness; the infrastructure remains closed. In that sense Morozov’s language-learning app is a perfect example: creative at the user level, dependent at the system level.

Open-source AI changes the dependency relation. It allows schools, cities, libraries, small firms, cooperatives, researchers, and communities to build systems without sending every query to a corporate API. It makes possible local deployment where privacy matters. It permits adaptation to low-resource languages and specialized domains that are unprofitable for major firms. It enables public audits and red-team evaluations beyond what companies choose to disclose. It gives students and citizens a chance to understand AI as a material practice, not a magical service.

This is not an argument for abolishing all restrictions. Some model capabilities raise real safety concerns. Open image and voice tools can facilitate fraud, nonconsensual sexual imagery, and political manipulation. Open language models can assist spam, cyber abuse, or biological misinformation. The open-source community sometimes treats these worries too lightly, as if any limit were corporate propaganda. But the answer cannot be to centralize all advanced AI behind a few company gates. That path creates its own dangers: monopoly, state capture, opaque censorship, dependency, and the permanent infantilization of users.

A democratic AI politics should distinguish between openness as ideology and openness as institutional design. We need layered openness. For many models, open weights, open training recipes, open evaluations, and permissive local use should be encouraged. For higher-risk systems, access may need staged release, researcher review, licensing conditions, or compute thresholds. Public-interest auditing should be mandatory for both closed and open systems. But the default democratic instinct should be that people deserve the ability to inspect and modify general-purpose infrastructure, not merely rent it.

Morozov is right that the market ethos can colonize open source. Major firms release open models for strategic reasons: to commoditize complements, recruit developers, shape standards, or undercut rivals. Open repositories often run on corporate cloud infrastructure. Volunteer labor can become free research and development for capital. Still, this ambivalence is not unique to AI. The internet itself, Linux, Python, Wikipedia, and many scientific software communities have long existed in contested territory between commons and commerce. Their answer has not been purity. It has been governance: licenses, foundations, norms, public funding, and institutional alliances.

That is where Morozov’s “Humanity Lobby” can meet the open-source world. Instead of treating open AI as a libertarian hobby, public institutions should help turn it into a commons. Governments and universities could fund open models for public-interest domains: health information, legal access, education, climate adaptation, local government services, and language preservation. Libraries could host community model labs. Public broadcasters and museums could contribute curated datasets under democratic terms. Labor unions and professional associations could develop worker-governed tools. Cities could share open AI components rather than each purchasing proprietary systems from vendors. International consortia could support models for languages and regions ignored by Silicon Valley.

The key is not only open code or open weights, but open capacity. Compute is the bottleneck that can turn formal openness into practical dependency. If only large corporations can train or serve useful models, then “open” becomes ornamental. Public compute infrastructure—a research cloud, nonprofit inference services, shared university clusters, municipal or regional AI facilities—should be part of any democratic AI agenda. So should technical education. A community cannot govern tools it cannot understand.

Open source also disciplines the philosophical debate. Morozov contrasts instrumental and ecological reason, but in actual hacker practice the two constantly intermingle. Someone begins with a goal—make a model run on a cheap laptop—then discovers new uses. Someone fine-tunes for a narrow task, then the community repurposes the result. Someone builds a tool for efficiency, then artists misuse it beautifully. This is not the absence of goals; it is goal plasticity. The telos changes through contact with materials and other people. Open systems are valuable because they preserve that plasticity.

Closed platforms reduce plasticity. They decide which forms of misuse are permitted and which are forbidden; which integrations are supported; which prices make experimentation possible; which communities are worth serving. They also erase history. A model may be updated overnight, breaking workflows or removing behaviors users valued. Open models can be archived, compared, studied, and maintained. That continuity matters for democratic culture.

The strongest objection is that open-source AI empowers bad actors. It does. But so does literacy, encryption, chemistry, drones, and the internet. Democratic societies do not respond by confining all powerful knowledge to licensed monopolies. They build norms, laws, enforcement mechanisms, and counter-capacities. Moreover, closed systems are hardly safe by default. They can be abused through APIs, integrated into surveillance products, or quietly aligned with state and corporate interests. Concentration is not safety; it is merely a different risk profile.

Morozov asks for technologies that enhance ecological reason rather than replacing human intelligence. Open-source AI is not sufficient for that task, but it is necessary. Ecological reason requires environments rich in affordances. A locked subscription chatbot offers affordances chosen by a company. An open ecosystem offers materials people can reshape. It lets the junkman, the teacher, the teenager, the municipal technologist, the disability activist, and the small-language community pick up the stone and ask: what else could this become?

The AI we deserve will need public institutions, democratic governance, and political struggle. But it will also need the freedom to tinker. If we want an alternative to the Efficiency Lobby, we should not overlook the messy workshops already forming outside its walls.
