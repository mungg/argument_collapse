"While Martha Nussbaum’s defense of cosmopolitan education is essential, it must be radicalized by incorporating a discipline of listening and an awareness of global power dynamics to ensure that universalism does not become a form of imperial arrogance."

Martha Nussbaum’s defense of cosmopolitan education is both morally attractive and politically urgent. Yet if cosmopolitanism is to avoid reproducing the very arrogance it opposes, it must be joined to a discipline of listening. The danger in patriotic education is that it mistakes the local for the universal. The danger in cosmopolitan education is that it may mistake the universalist’s own voice for the voice of humanity.

This is not a reason to reject Nussbaum’s argument. It is a reason to radicalize it. A genuine cosmopolitanism cannot consist merely in teaching American students more facts about India, Bolivia, Nigeria, and Norway, though that would already be a great improvement. It must teach them to ask how knowledge about other people is produced, who speaks for whom, and how global inequalities shape the conversation.

Nussbaum’s own work on development and quality of life points in this direction. The language of “development” has often been used by wealthy nations to instruct poorer nations in what they lack. Sometimes the instruction is necessary: preventable disease, illiteracy, hunger, and political repression are not charming local customs. But sometimes the language of development carries hidden assumptions about family, labor, religion, gender, consumption, and progress. A cosmopolitan education must therefore combine universal moral concern with humility about cultural interpretation.

This is especially important for women. Appeals to “culture” have often been used to justify women’s subordination: child marriage, denial of education, domestic confinement, unequal property rights, sexual violence, and exclusion from public life. For that reason, feminists cannot be simple relativists. We need universal standards by which to say that a woman’s bodily integrity, literacy, political participation, and economic opportunity matter whether she is born in Chicago, Calcutta, Cairo, or La Paz.

But feminist universalism also has to guard against imperial condescension. Westerners are too ready to imagine women elsewhere only as victims waiting to be rescued by modernity. They forget women’s movements in the global South, women’s labor in households and markets, women’s religious agency, women’s forms of collective power, women’s criticisms of both local patriarchy and international capitalism. A cosmopolitan curriculum should not present “other cultures” as static backgrounds against which American students display sympathy. It should present other people as arguers, organizers, theorists, and critics of us.

Nussbaum’s example of the family is well chosen. Americans often speak as though the isolated nuclear family were natural, traditional, and morally self-evident. A comparative study reveals otherwise. Child-rearing has been shared by extended kin, villages, older siblings, women’s associations, servants, neighbors, and state institutions. Such knowledge can free us from false necessities. It can also help us see the costs of our own arrangements: the loneliness of mothers, the invisibility of unpaid labor, the vulnerability of children in privatized households, and the economic penalties borne by caregivers.

Yet comparison must not become tourism. It is not enough to say, “Look, other people arrange families differently.” We must ask how power operates within each arrangement. Extended families may support women, but they may also subject daughters-in-law to surveillance. Wage labor may liberate women from domestic dependence, but it may also exploit them in factories. State child care may enable equality, but it may also become bureaucratic and cold. The point of cosmopolitan education is not to romanticize difference. It is to enlarge practical judgment.

Nussbaum’s critique of patriotism is most persuasive when she exposes its moral arbitrariness. Why should a child on one side of a border command our political imagination, while a child on the other side becomes an object of occasional benevolence? Why should the suffering of Americans be narrated as tragedy and the suffering of foreigners as “instability”? The answer cannot be that we lack capacity to care, for our indifference is often carefully taught. News, textbooks, maps, and political speeches arrange the world into zones of relevance and irrelevance.

Still, I would put less emphasis than Nussbaum does on loneliness and exile. For many people, cosmopolitanism does not begin by leaving home; it begins because home has already been crossed by the world. Migrants send money across borders. Refugees carry memories of one country into another. Children grow up translating for parents. Workers in one nation depend on consumers in another. Religious communities stretch across continents. Music, food, labor, debt, and war make cosmopolitans of people who have never read the Stoics.

The task of education is to make these actual interdependencies visible and morally intelligible. Consider a simple object in an American classroom: a shirt, a banana, a radio, a pair of sneakers. Where did it come from? Who made it? Under what conditions? What trade rules, debts, land arrangements, and political histories brought it here? What pollution was produced? Who profited? Who was made vulnerable? Such questions would teach children that global justice is not an after-school charity project. It is woven into everyday life.

This kind of education would also change how we discuss American policy. The debate over trade, for example, cannot be conducted only in terms of American jobs or American prices. It must include workers abroad, environmental standards, migration pressures, and the political power of corporations. Immigration cannot be discussed only as a matter of border control; it must be connected to labor demand, foreign policy, family separation, and histories of intervention. Military action cannot be discussed only as national interest; it must be judged by the lives of civilians who will bear its consequences.

Nussbaum’s Stoic language of reason and moral capacity is valuable, but I would supplement it with the language of vulnerability. Human beings are not only reasoners; they are embodied creatures who can be hungry, raped, displaced, silenced, poisoned, and bereaved. A curriculum centered on world citizenship should teach not merely that all persons are rational agents but that all persons are dependent at some moments, and that societies are judged by how they respond to dependency. This is where women’s experience, long relegated to the private sphere, becomes central to political thought.

The cosmopolitan school I imagine would teach languages seriously. It would teach world religions without reducing them to exotic festivals. It would teach colonialism and anti-colonialism, slavery and abolition, the world economy, ecology, and comparative constitutionalism. It would put American history in conversation with other histories of revolution, empire, race, and democracy. It would invite students to see themselves not as benevolent spectators but as implicated participants.

But it would also teach the ethics of speaking. Before students announce what “we” should do for “them,” they should ask what people are already doing for themselves. Before condemning a practice, they should understand who defends it, who contests it, and what alternatives exist. Before celebrating a culture, they should ask who pays the price for its preservation. Before invoking universal rights, they should be prepared to apply the same standards to their own country.

Nussbaum is right: patriotism is morally dangerous when it asks for worship. But cosmopolitanism is morally dangerous when it becomes pride in one’s own broad-mindedness. The cure in both cases is the same: a relentless subordination of identity to justice, and a willingness to be changed by what one learns.

World citizenship should not mean that we have no home. It should mean that our homes have windows, doors, and obligations.

"While cosmopolitanism provides essential moral scrutiny, a chastened form of patriotism is necessary to provide the practical solidarity and political accountability required to achieve justice."

Martha Nussbaum is right to warn against the worship of country. The century now drawing to a close has provided too many examples of nations made into gods: Europe’s fascisms, ethnic cleansing in the former Yugoslavia, the poisonous politics of religious nationalism in South Asia, and many lesser forms of swaggering indifference. She is also right that American education remains scandalously provincial. A student can graduate from a good school knowing little of Africa beyond famine, of Islam beyond oil politics, of India beyond mysticism and poverty, or of Latin America beyond immigration and debt. Such ignorance is not merely discourteous; it is politically dangerous in a world of nuclear weapons, global markets, epidemics, ecological interdependence, and vast inequalities.

But I do not think the remedy is to make world citizenship the “primary” allegiance and national citizenship secondary. The better answer is a patriotism chastened by cosmopolitan morality: a love of one’s country that is neither ultimate nor innocent, but still indispensable. Nussbaum’s target is patriotic idolatry, and against that she is eloquent. Yet she too quickly treats patriotism itself as though it naturally slides into jingoism, exclusion, and faction. It can do so. But it can also be one of the chief ways universal principles acquire practical force.

Consider the American examples Nussbaum herself has in view. The most powerful critics of American injustice have often spoken not as exiles from American identity but as claimants to its betrayed promises. Frederick Douglass’s Fourth of July oration, Lincoln’s appeal to the Declaration, the abolitionists’ invocation of natural rights, and Martin Luther King Jr.’s insistence that America honor its “promissory note” all drew energy from a patriotic moral vocabulary. King’s language was not merely tribal; it was universalist Christianity and natural-rights liberalism. But it mattered that he addressed Americans as Americans, recalling them to a shared story they could not easily disown. A purely cosmopolitan rebuke might have been morally correct; it would not necessarily have moved the conscience of Birmingham, Washington, or Selma.

This suggests that patriotism need not compete with universal justice. At its best, it is a historically situated commitment to make one political community more just, more decent, and more answerable to the claims of humanity. We do not love our country because it is morally supreme. We love it because it is ours to repair. The possessive pronoun here is not metaphysical; it is practical. These are the institutions through which we vote, tax, litigate, educate, punish, defend, and redistribute. These are the fellow citizens with whom we must make binding decisions. If we abandon the language of common membership, we may weaken the very solidarities that make democratic justice possible.

Nussbaum rightly invokes concentric circles, but her argument gives insufficient weight to the inner political circle of citizenship. Families are not morally ultimate; nevertheless, children are ordinarily best cared for when particular adults bear special responsibility for them. So too with political communities. A citizen of the United States has obligations to hungry children in Bangladesh and Sudan, but she also has distinctive obligations to the public schools of Chicago, the prisons of Texas, the wages of farmworkers in California, and the constitutional rights of unpopular minorities in her own polity. These obligations are not grounded in the superior worth of Americans. They arise from participation in a coercive scheme of law and collective power. I help authorize, benefit from, and am represented by American institutions. That gives me special duties to reform them.

Nor is cosmopolitanism free from dangers of its own. It can become an elegant abstraction, more comfortable in seminars than in neighborhoods. To say “I am a citizen of the world” may enlarge sympathy; it may also release one from the more tedious burdens of actual citizenship: attending meetings, compromising with disagreeable compatriots, paying taxes, serving on juries, learning local history, accepting responsibility for national crimes. The world citizen has no world legislature to vote in, no world welfare state to sustain, no world public school board to confront. We should build stronger international institutions, and we should teach students to care about them. But in 1994 the effective instruments of democratic accountability remain overwhelmingly national and local. Moral imagination may be global; political responsibility is still mediated.

Nussbaum asks why the values that bind citizens together should “lose steam” at the border. They should not. But it does not follow that borders are morally meaningless. They are not magical lines transforming strangers into persons; they are jurisdictional lines marking shared institutions. A Chinese immigrant in New York and a Chinese citizen in Shanghai are both entitled to human respect. But the former is also a participant, actual or prospective, in our constitutional order. We owe both justice; we owe the former the additional duties generated by common citizenship. The distinction is not between full humanity and lesser humanity. It is between universal moral regard and special political relationship.

The educational implication is not patriotic indoctrination. Schools should not teach children to “rejoice” uncritically in America, still less to rank American lives above others. They should teach the crimes as well as the achievements: slavery, conquest of native peoples, exclusion of immigrants, Hiroshima, support for dictatorships, as well as constitutionalism, emancipation, labor struggles, civil liberties, and democratic reform. They should also teach world history, comparative religion, foreign languages, global ecology, colonialism, development, and the unequal distribution of resources. On this central curricular point Nussbaum’s case is compelling.

But the student should learn to say not simply, “I am above all a citizen of the world,” nor simply, “I am above all an American.” She should learn a harder sentence: “I am an American citizen whose country is answerable to the world.” That formula preserves what Nussbaum most wants—universal moral scrutiny—while retaining what democratic politics most needs: a sense of shared fate and mutual obligation among citizens.

Tagore’s Nikhil is right that Right is greater than country. But it does not follow that country is negligible. The task is to love without worshiping, to criticize without disowning, to belong without excluding, and to remember that the nation is not a god but a responsibility.

Patriotism Is Not the Enemy

Professor Nussbaum has written a beautiful essay about an ideal she calls cosmopolitanism, but she has not, I think, written a fair essay about the thing she calls patriotism. The patriotism she attacks is a straw figure: a worship of country as god, a Sandip-like intoxication with symbols, a refusal to look beyond one's borders. No one I know defends that. Certainly Richard Rorty, the proximate target of her essay, does not. What Rorty defended in his Times piece was something much more modest and much more defensible: the proposition that a left which cannot bring itself to feel any pride in the country it seeks to reform will not be able to reform it.

This is not a metaphysical claim about the priority of the nation over humanity. It is a sociological observation about how political movements actually mobilize. The civil rights movement appealed not to abstract humanity but to the promises of the Declaration of Independence. The labor movement appealed to American traditions of dignity and republican self-rule. The suffragists invoked the Constitution. Each of these movements expanded the circle of moral concern, often dramatically, and each did so by speaking in the dialect of national membership rather than against it. To call this jingoism is to mistake an instrument for an idolatry.

Nussbaum's argument has, at its heart, what philosophers sometimes call a category error. She treats patriotism and cosmopolitanism as rival answers to the same question—"To which community do I owe my first allegiance?"—and concludes that the cosmopolitan answer is more defensible. But patriotism, as practiced by sane people, is not a candidate answer to that question at all. It is not a claim about ultimate moral priority. It is an affective relation to particular institutions, places, and histories, accompanied by a sense of responsibility for their improvement. One can have such a relation while also believing, with full Kantian conviction, that every human being is of equal worth. One can love one's country without thinking it the best, just as one can love one's spouse without thinking her the most beautiful woman in the world.

Indeed, the comparison is illuminating. Suppose someone told us that marital love was morally dangerous, because it elevates a "morally arbitrary" person—the one you happen to have married—above the rest of humanity, who are equally deserving of love. Suppose she added that we should rather love human beings as such, and that the special affection given to spouses is at best a concession to practical necessity. We would, I hope, regard this as a misunderstanding of what love is. Marital love is not a ranking; it is a relation. Patriotism, in its decent form, is similar. It is the relation a citizen has to the polity she helps to constitute and is constituted by, and the demand that she repudiate it in favor of a relation to humanity at large is a demand that she become, morally speaking, no one in particular.

Nussbaum will reply that humanity is not "no one in particular" but the most morally significant community of all. Yet humanity is not a community. It does not deliberate. It does not have institutions through which obligations can be discharged and disputes adjudicated. It has no laws, no taxes, no schools, no army, and no court whose writ runs unimpeded across borders. The "community of human argument and aspiration" of which Seneca spoke is a metaphor, and a noble one, but it cannot do the work that actual political communities do. To owe one's first allegiance to it is to owe one's first allegiance to an abstraction—and abstractions, as the bloody history of this century has shown, can become idols as easily as flags can.

There is a further point. Nussbaum worries that defending the nation's moral weight undermines arguments for crossing internal barriers of race and gender. I would say the opposite. The moral logic that integrated the American workplace and the American school was the logic of equal citizenship: these are our fellow Americans, and they must be treated as such. That argument has bite precisely because "American" names a real community with real obligations of mutual treatment. Replace it with "fellow human being" and the argument loses traction—not because human beings do not deserve respect, but because the institutional vehicle for delivering that respect is unclear. Civil rights are claims against a state. They have to be made by people who consider themselves entitled to make them, against a state they consider their own.

I share Nussbaum's hope that American students learn more geography, more history, more languages, more about the texture of lives lived elsewhere. I share her alarm at the smug provincialism that has too often passed for civic education here. But these are arguments for cosmopolitan content within a patriotic frame, not for the dissolution of the frame. The child who learns about India in an American school is being prepared to be a better American citizen of a world her country shares with others. That is not a betrayal of the cosmopolitan ideal. It is, I would argue, the only way the ideal has ever made any practical headway at all.

"While Martha Nussbaum’s critique of national self-worship is necessary, a viable cosmopolitanism must not replace local identities with an abstract 'view from nowhere' but should instead demand that our particular affiliations be held accountable to universal justice."

I read Martha Nussbaum’s essay with admiration and unease. The admiration comes easily. Her attack on national self-worship is eloquent and necessary. The unease comes from the phrase “citizen of the world,” which can sound, in American ears, like an escape from the unfinished business of citizenship here.

For many Americans, patriotism has never been simple. Black Americans, Native Americans, Mexican Americans in the Southwest, Asian immigrants excluded by law, women denied the vote until this century—all have had reason to hear patriotic rhetoric as both promise and threat. “America” has been a flag carried by abolitionists and by slaveholders, by civil rights marchers and by sheriffs blocking schoolhouse doors. To ask whether we should be patriots or cosmopolitans may therefore begin in the wrong place. Some of us have never been permitted the innocence of either.

Nussbaum is right that the accident of birth is morally arbitrary. But the consequences of that accident are not arbitrary in lived experience. To be born black in Mississippi, Jewish in Europe, Hindu or Muslim in India, Palestinian in Gaza, or Tutsi in Rwanda is to inherit a location in histories of power. Moral universalism must not float above those histories. It must enter them. Otherwise cosmopolitanism risks becoming another language spoken by those who can afford to imagine themselves unmarked.

The great American dissenters have often practiced something like what might be called wounded patriotism. Frederick Douglass’s “What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?” did not reject the Declaration of Independence; it hurled the Declaration back at a nation that celebrated liberty while holding human beings in bondage. Martin Luther King Jr. appealed both to natural law and to the American creed. He spoke as a Christian, a democrat, a black Southerner, and a human being. His moral force came not from choosing one circle of identity and discarding the others, but from arranging them under the judgment of justice.

That, I think, is the most promising correction to Nussbaum’s argument. The problem is not that people love particular communities. The problem is that they refuse to let justice interrogate those loves. The answer is not necessarily to teach children that they are “above all” citizens of the world. It is to teach them that every identity they bear—American, Christian, Muslim, Jewish, black, white, Navajo, woman, worker, immigrant—is morally answerable to the humanity of others.

The difference matters. If cosmopolitanism is taught as a view from nowhere, it will fail. Worse, it will deserve to fail. Children do not first encounter humanity in the abstract. They encounter it through mothers, grandfathers, neighborhoods, churches, languages, foods, songs, insults, fears, holidays, and stories. A child learns dignity when someone’s particular dignity is honored. A curriculum that leaps too quickly to “the world” may miss the local wounds through which universal questions become real.

Take multicultural education. Nussbaum worries that national identity and the “politics of difference” are closer than Richard Rorty admits. She is surely right that national majorities can use patriotic language to suppress internal minorities. But many forms of ethnic and racial assertion are not miniature nationalisms. They are responses to erasure. When black students demand African and African-American history, when Native students demand truthful teaching about conquest and treaty violation, when Asian-American students ask to be seen as more than foreigners, they are not necessarily retreating from humanity into tribe. They may be demanding entry into a common world on terms that do not require self-forgetting.

A cosmopolitan education worthy of the name must therefore be both global and historical. It should teach Rwanda, Bosnia, India, China, South Africa, and Latin America. But it should also teach Tulsa, Wounded Knee, Manzanar, Stonewall, Montgomery, Delano, and Seneca Falls. It should show how the global and the domestic illuminate one another: how colonialism shaped race; how migration links economies; how civil rights movements borrow from and inspire one another; how women organizing in one country learn from women in another; how religious nationalism in India and racial nationalism in America share certain temptations.

Nussbaum’s strongest point is that human rights cannot be a decorative add-on to patriotic education. If “all persons have dignity” is true, then it is true before and after one crosses a border. American children should know that the shirt on their back, the fruit on their table, the oil in their car, and the weapons bought with their taxes connect them to workers and civilians far away. They should understand that poverty abroad is not merely unfortunate but often bound up with decisions made in wealthy countries. They should be morally troubled by the fact that American comfort may depend on global arrangements we would not accept if we were born elsewhere.

Still, I would resist making exile the model of moral maturity. Nussbaum’s Diogenes stands outside the city and says he is a citizen of the world. But some people have been exiled too often already. The descendants of enslaved people, the displaced indigenous, refugees, and immigrants may need not another lesson in detachment but a just home. The moral task is to build forms of belonging that do not require domination.

Perhaps the phrase we need is not anti-patriotism but accountable belonging. I may belong to a nation, a people, a faith, a family, a movement. But I must be prepared to answer for what is done in their names. I must be able to hear the stranger’s claim. I must be able to say, with Nikhil, that Right is greater than country. Yet I may also say that the struggle to make my country less false is one of the ways I serve Right.

Nussbaum’s essay is a needed rebuke to smug Americanness. But cosmopolitanism will persuade more deeply if it does not ask people to thin out their identities before entering the moral community. Let them enter carrying their histories. Then let justice do its work.

"Martha Nussbaum’s proposal for cosmopolitan education is flawed because it ignores the necessary role of national identity in providing the motivational force and institutional framework required to achieve justice."

There is much to admire in Nussbaum's essay—the seriousness with which she takes our obligations to distant strangers, her insistence that the boundaries of moral concern not coincide with the boundaries of the nation-state, her recovery of the Stoic tradition for contemporary debate. Yet I find myself unpersuaded that the contrast she draws between "cosmopolitan" and "patriotic" education is as clean as she suggests, and worried that her proposal misdescribes both the moral psychology of attachment and the political conditions under which her own ideals might be realized.

Begin with the moral psychology. Nussbaum concedes, following Hierocles, that we may keep our concentric circles of attachment, but she asks us to draw them "toward the center" so that humanity as such commands our first allegiance. The trouble is that "first allegiance" is doing two different jobs in her argument. As a claim about the ultimate source of moral justification—that reasons must in principle be defensible to all rational beings—it is a familiar Kantian thesis and seems right. This brings me to the institutional question Nussbaum largely sidesteps. Rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness are not floating Kantian abstractions; they are protected, when they are protected at all, by particular states with taxing powers, courts, and armies. The civil rights revolution, the labor movement, the construction of whatever welfare state we possess—each required citizens to recognize obligations to fellow citizens they had never met, and each was carried forward by an argument that ran, in part, "we Americans must live up to what we say we are." That is patriotism doing moral work. To call the nation a "morally arbitrary" boundary is true in the metaphysical sense in which any institution is arbitrary, but false in the sense that matters: the nation is the actually existing site where redistribution, deliberation, and accountability presently occur. Until something replaces it, telling students that their primary allegiance lies elsewhere risks dissolving the only solidarity strong enough to fund the schools in which cosmopolitanism would be taught.

Nussbaum's fourth argument—that we cannot consistently demand mutual respect across ethnic lines inside the nation while withholding it across the national border—has rhetorical force but proves too much. The same logic would dissolve the special obligations she explicitly preserves toward our own children. If giving one's children extra care is justified because, as she puts it, "it is good for children, on the whole, that things should work out this way," then perhaps giving one's fellow citizens extra care is justified on parallel grounds: democratic self-government, mutual accountability, and the cultivation of public virtues all seem to require bounded communities of fate. Rorty's position, as I read it, is not the jingoism Nussbaum fears but precisely this insight—that shame and pride in a shared "we" are the affective preconditions of reform.

What I would urge instead is not a choice between Rorty and Nussbaum but a layered civic education: one that teaches American history with neither piety nor contempt, that situates that history within a genuinely global frame (the slave trade, the world wars, the current ecological predicament Nussbaum rightly emphasizes), and that builds outward from particular loyalties rather than asking students to begin from the species. The Stoics were right that reason recognizes no borders. But Aristotle was also right that we become just by performing just acts in the company of those with whom we share a common life. Cosmopolitanism without a polis to stand on is not a destination; it is a homelessness.

The lesson of Tagore’s novel is not that patriotism is a curse, but that a patriotism unmoored from justice becomes idolatry, while a cosmopolitanism unmoored from concrete political community becomes ineffectual. Martha Nussbaum has done something genuinely important: she has reopened, in a public forum, a question that has lain quiet in American political philosophy for too long. The question is whether the moral cosmopolitanism that almost all of us profess—the conviction that every human being is of equal worth—can be reconciled with the political nationalism that almost all of us practice. Her answer, broadly, is that it cannot, and that the cosmopolitan side should win. I want to give two cheers for her position and explain why I withhold the third.

The first cheer is for her diagnosis. The recent vogue, on both right and left, for what is sometimes called "communitarianism" has produced some impressive scholarship and a great deal of mush. Among its mushier products has been the suggestion that liberal political philosophy has erred by treating individuals as abstracted from their thick particular attachments, and that the remedy lies in restoring the moral weight of nation, locality, tradition. Rorty's piece is a relatively sophisticated specimen of the type, but the genre as a whole has tended to forget that one of liberalism's central achievements was precisely to limit the moral claims of inherited collective identity. Nussbaum is right to insist that the cosmopolitan tradition—Stoic, Kantian, Enlightenment—is not some bloodless rationalism but a continuous moral protest against the idolatry of the local. We forget this at our peril.

The second cheer is for her educational proposals. The provincialism of American schooling is a national embarrassment. Our students learn next to nothing about the histories, languages, religions, and political experiences of the four-fifths of humanity that does not live in Western Europe or North America. The result is not only ignorance but a kind of moral disability: a citizenry incapable of imagining itself into the situations of those whose lives are affected by its government's choices. When the United States debates trade policy with Mexico, or aid to sub-Saharan Africa, or intervention in the Balkans, it does so in a fog of unfamiliarity that no democracy worthy of the name should tolerate. Nussbaum's call for a curriculum that treats the world as the proper object of civic study is exactly right, and I would go further than she does in pressing it.

The withheld third cheer concerns the philosophical frame. Nussbaum wants to say not merely that we have substantial obligations to non-citizens, and not merely that national borders are morally arbitrary in some fundamental sense, but that our "first allegiance" should be to humanity as such rather than to the nation. This formulation strikes me as both unnecessary for her practical conclusions and damaging to their political prospects.

It is unnecessary because everything Nussbaum wants to say about pollution, hunger, child-rearing, and the universal scope of Kantian respect can be said without the ranking. One can hold that obligations to non-citizens are morally weighty, often weightier than ordinary patriotic discourse acknowledges, without claiming that they always trump obligations to one's compatriots. The image she borrows from Hierocles—of concentric circles to be drawn "somehow toward the center"—is in fact a non-ranking image. It does not say that the outer circle is most important; it says that all circles deserve moral attention and that the outer ones are systematically neglected. That, I take it, is the operative point, and it does not require the further claim that humanity comes "first."

It is damaging because the further claim invites a response Nussbaum cannot afford: that her position is philosophically tone-deaf to the actual phenomenology of moral life. People do not, in fact, encounter humanity as such. They encounter their children, their neighbors, their coreligionists, their countrymen, and—at increasing remove—the rest. A moral theory that tells them that their first allegiance is to an abstraction will not be believed, and what is not believed cannot guide. The genius of the Stoic tradition, when it was at its best, was to recognize that universal reason had to be lived out through particular attachments, not against them. Marcus Aurelius did not stop being a Roman emperor when he meditated on the unity of the human limb-and-part. He governed Rome better, perhaps, but he governed Rome.

Nussbaum knows all this; her concessions in section three acknowledge much of it. But the rhetorical thrust of her essay—and especially its framing against Rorty—obscures the concessions. Readers will come away thinking she has argued for the demotion of patriotism, when what she has actually argued for is its chastening. The chastening I support without reservation. The demotion I think a philosophical error and a tactical mistake.

So: two cheers. The third is owed not to Nussbaum but to whoever can reconcile her substantive program—a humane, knowledgeable, world-aware citizenship—with the patriotic emotional grammar through which Americans actually conduct their political lives. That synthesis is the real work, and the lead essay, for all its eloquence, points toward it more than it accomplishes it.

An education that skips over national allegiance in favor of global citizenship risks cultivating a generation of detached observers, equipped with universal ideals but lacking the grounded political machinery to implement them.

We should indeed educate students about the diverse cultures, families, and crises of the wider world, as Nussbaum suggests. However, a healthy patriotism is not the enemy of global responsibility; it is its prerequisite. We learn to love the world by first learning to take responsibility for our own particular corner of it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human affection and moral motivation naturally diminish as they expand outward, making abstract love for humanity less practically effective than localized loyalties.
  2. 02Democratic achievements like the welfare state and civil rights depend on a sense of special, bounded obligation among citizens of a nation.
  3. 03Solving global crises requires the concerted action of strong nation-states rather than a generation of detached global observers without national political machinery.
  4. 04A healthy patriotism serves as a prerequisite for global responsibility because individuals learn to care for the world by first taking responsibility for their own community.
