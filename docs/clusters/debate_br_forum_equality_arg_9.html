"The primary goal of egalitarianism should be 'relational equality'—a society of equals free from domination and hierarchy—rather than the lead essay's proposal of 'equal flourishing.'"

Christine Sypnowich asks whether equal opportunity is enough and answers no: egalitarians should care about equal flourishing. I agree with the no but resist the proposed yes. The deepest egalitarian ideal is neither equal opportunity nor equal flourishing. It is a society of equals: a political community in which people stand to one another without domination, caste, servility, or social humiliation. Opportunities and outcomes matter, but they matter because of the relations they create and express.

The trouble with opportunity is familiar. It can coexist with hierarchy. Indeed, opportunity talk often presupposes hierarchy: there will be winners and losers, but the contest will be fair. The ideal image is the open competition, the transparent application, the scholarship for the talented poor child, the promotion based on merit rather than prejudice. These are not trivial achievements. A society that excludes people by race, sex, disability, religion, caste, or class violates equality in an especially direct way. But an open contest for unequal standing remains a contest for unequal standing. It does little for those who lose, and it leaves unexamined why so many goods are attached to winning.

Sypnowich’s critique of responsibility is also persuasive. In actual societies, “choice” is soaked through with circumstance. The child of secure, educated parents is not simply making better choices when she studies in a quiet room, receives tutoring, applies to university with confidence, and enters adulthood debt-free. The child navigating eviction, family stress, underfunded schools, and neighborhood violence is not simply making worse ones. Nor is adulthood a clean realm of responsibility. Low wages, illness, addiction, caring duties, discrimination, and mental distress all shape the field in which choices are made. A just society should not operate as a tribunal sorting the deserving from the undeserving.

Yet equal flourishing is not the right replacement. It shifts our attention from fair contests to quality of life, and that is an improvement. But it may still frame equality as a distributive pattern among individuals: this person has more flourishing, that person less; justice calls for equalization. Something is missing in that picture. Inequality is not only bad because some individuals have less welfare, fewer capabilities, or poorer lives. It is bad because it organizes social relations. It lets some command and others obey; some speak and others plead; some move through public space with ease and others with shame; some make decisions and others endure them.

Consider poverty. Its wrongness is not exhausted by the fact that poor people have fewer resources or less flourishing. Poverty also places people under the power of landlords, bosses, creditors, welfare officials, police, and charity providers. It forces supplication. It exposes private life to inspection. It marks people as failures in a culture that moralizes market success. Even if a poor person finds meaning, friendship, and resilience, the relational injustice remains. Conversely, if an affluent person is miserable, lonely, and spiritually empty, that is a tragedy, but not necessarily an egalitarian injustice. Equality is centrally about standing, power, and membership.

This relational perspective clarifies the importance of outcomes without making equal flourishing the aim. Large inequalities of wealth are objectionable not simply because the rich flourish more. Sometimes they may not. They are objectionable because wealth buys political influence, legal advantage, residential segregation, educational privilege, immunity from risk, and the power to shape the lives of others. A billionaire need not be happier than a nurse for billionaire wealth to be democratically toxic. It creates private government. It makes a mockery of equal citizenship.

Similarly, educational inequality is not wrong only because some children develop more talents or enjoy richer lives. It is wrong because it assigns children to social positions before they can participate in shaping them. It produces classes of people expected to lead and classes expected to comply. Schools do not merely distribute skills; they confer recognition and confidence. An egalitarian society must therefore ask what kinds of educational institutions produce citizens capable of meeting one another as equals, not merely what package of instruction maximizes or equalizes flourishing.

Sypnowich’s emphasis on community is therefore welcome, but I would put solidarity before flourishing. Solidarity is not simply caring that others live well. It is sharing a world on terms that do not degrade anyone. It involves institutions that prevent dependency from becoming subordination. We all depend on others: children on adults, patients on caregivers, workers on consumers and colleagues, the elderly on younger generations, citizens on public servants. The egalitarian question is whether these dependencies are organized reciprocally and respectfully or hierarchically and humiliatingly.

This distinction has practical implications. A relational egalitarian politics would begin with anti-domination. It would strengthen unions and labor law because workers should not be at the mercy of employers. It would guarantee health care because illness should not make people dependent on bosses, spouses, or charity. It would provide housing security because eviction is not merely a loss of shelter but a form of social power. It would fund public schools equally and integrate them where possible because caste-like separation corrodes democratic relations. It would reduce the role of private money in politics because political voice should not be purchased. It would reform policing and prisons because equal citizenship is incompatible with arbitrary state violence and mass civic exclusion. It would support disability rights and care institutions because bodily dependence must not mean inferior status.

These policies would certainly improve flourishing, and likely make it more equal. But their public justification would not require agreement on the full content of a flourishing life. That is an advantage. Modern democratic societies contain reasonable disagreement about values. A relational ideal can say: live religiously or secularly, ambitiously or quietly, communally or independently, artistically or practically, so long as the institutions we share do not rank people as superiors and inferiors. It does not pretend the state is neutral in every respect; it insists the state’s central value judgment should be equal standing.

Sypnowich might reply that standing itself is part of flourishing. True. But if everything valuable is folded into flourishing, the concept becomes too capacious to guide political judgment. Friendship, beauty, autonomy, health, love, achievement, play, and respect may all contribute to flourishing. They do not all raise the same claims of justice. Loneliness is a social problem and often an egalitarian one when produced by poverty, disability exclusion, racism, age segregation, or market institutions that destroy public life. But loneliness as such cannot be abolished by justice, and not every unequal distribution of friendship is an inequality of social standing. We need distinctions.

The same is true of the arts. I share Sypnowich’s support for music and arts education. The arts open worlds, build capacities, and should not be the property of the affluent. But the relational argument for public arts is not that everyone must be brought to equal aesthetic flourishing. It is that citizens should not be divided between those who inherit cultural confidence and those taught that beauty, performance, and interpretation belong elsewhere. Public culture is part of democratic membership. It gives people shared references and means of expression. A child should encounter music not because the state has ranked music as necessary to her flourishing, but because exclusion from cultural inheritance is a form of civic diminishment.

Relational equality also handles responsibility better than both luck egalitarianism and equal flourishing. It does not deny agency. People make choices, sometimes foolish or harmful ones. A society of equals can hold people accountable for violence, fraud, neglect, and exploitation. But accountability should be compatible with continued membership. The person who loses a job, becomes addicted, leaves school, or makes bad financial decisions should not thereby fall into a lower caste. Social provision should not depend on moral innocence. Nor should punishment become civic death. The point is not that choices are unreal; it is that equal status is not forfeited by ordinary human fallibility.

This approach also avoids the specter of leveling down. Equality does not require making everyone equally happy, talented, cultured, or successful. It requires preventing advantages from becoming domination and disadvantages from becoming subordination. If some people earn more because they work unpleasant hours or take on demanding responsibilities, that may be acceptable within limits. But if their greater income buys political control, better justice, superior schooling, or deference, it violates equality. The focus is not a flat pattern but the social meaning and power of inequalities.

Sypnowich is right that the language of equal opportunity is too easily captured by meritocracy. Meritocracy promises mobility while preserving contempt for those who remain below. It says, in effect, that hierarchy is legitimate once the talented poor are permitted to rise. Egalitarians should reject that bargain. But the alternative should be democratic equality, not equal flourishing. A good society is one in which no one has to escape their class to be respected, no one has to win a competition to be secure, and no one’s dependence becomes an occasion for domination.

Equal opportunity is not enough because it leaves hierarchy standing. Equal flourishing is too much, or at least too indeterminate, because it invites politics to take responsibility for the whole shape of human fulfillment. Equal standing is the better ideal. It is radical where it must be—against class power, racial caste, patriarchy, ableism, plutocracy, and workplace domination. It is restrained where it should be—leaving citizens room to pursue different, even conflicting, visions of the good. Equality is not a race with fair rules, and it is not a ledger of flourishing. It is a way of living together without masters and inferiors.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Equal opportunity is insufficient because it merely creates a fair contest for unequal standing, leaving social hierarchies and the plight of the 'losers' unaddressed.
  2. 02Equal flourishing is an inadequate metric because it treats equality as a distributive pattern of individual welfare rather than a structural organization of social relations.
  3. 03Inequality is primarily objectionable when it translates into social power, such as when wealth buys political influence or creates private government.
  4. 04A relational approach avoids the need for state-mandated agreement on the content of a 'good life,' allowing for diverse personal values while insisting on equal civic standing.
  5. 05Solidarity should be prioritized over flourishing, focusing on institutions that ensure human dependencies do not devolve into subordination or humiliation.
  6. 06Justice should not function as a tribunal of responsibility; instead, social provision and equal status should be guaranteed regardless of an individual's choices or fallibility.
