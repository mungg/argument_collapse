"The proposed integration of local police into a national counterterrorism intelligence network should be rejected because it erodes the constitutional separation of powers and undermines the structural protections of federalism."

As someone who came of age revering the constitutional architecture of the Founders, I read Daniel Richman’s essay with a mixture of admiration and deep unease. The admiration is for his thorough and unsentimental mapping of the incentives that drive federal-state-local relations in law enforcement. The unease stems from the direction in which he wants to take that analysis: toward a vision of “counterterrorism-based federalism” that, however well-meaning, would further erode the structural protections of limited government and the separation of powers. Richman’s proposal is a clear-eyed path to a destination we should not wish to reach.

The Constitution assigns to the federal government the responsibility for “the common defense” and for protecting the United States from foreign threats. The states, in turn, retain the general police power to regulate health, safety, and morals—including the apprehension of ordinary criminals. This division is not a historical accident; it is one of the principal safeguards against the concentration of power. When the federal government acts in its sphere, it is limited by enumerated powers, by the Bill of Rights, and by the political constraints of being a distant sovereign. When state and local governments act, they are tempered by their proximity to the people and by the fact that citizens can vote with their feet. Blurring the line between the two, as the post-9/11 counterterrorism imperative has done, confuses accountability and invites the growth of an unaccountable national police force—not in name, but in effect.

Richman is candid about the history that led to this confusion. From the Johnson administration through the Clinton years, Washington poured billions into local law enforcement to fight the “War on Drugs” and later violent street crime. Many conservatives, myself among them, have long argued that this was a mistake, both constitutionally and practically. It created a dependency on federal funds, distorted local policing priorities, and led to the federalization of crimes that properly belong in state courts. The COPS program and the Byrne grants may have been politically popular, but they were a departure from the first principles of federalism. That departure has now made it harder for local governments to resist the next federal demand—this time in the area of counterterrorism and immigration enforcement—because they have become accustomed to treating Washington as a patron.

Richman’s solution to the resulting tensions is not to restore a proper division, but to double down on entanglement. He wants local police to “exact a toll” and shape federal intelligence policy from within. But why should we expect that to safeguard liberty? Once local departments are integrated into a national intelligence network, they will inevitably be subject to federal directives over which they have little real control. The “toll” they can exact is whatever Washington finds convenient to grant—and can later revoke. The Portland police may have declined to ask certain questions in 2001, but the Clear Law Enforcement for Criminal Alien Removal Act now pending in Congress would compel them to enforce federal immigration law under penalty of losing all federal law-enforcement grants. So much for local leverage.

There is a deeper, more principled reason to resist the fusion Richman advocates. The Fourth Amendment and other procedural protections apply most strongly when the federal government acts directly. Through the use of Joint Terrorism Task Forces and information-sharing agreements, the federal government can achieve through local proxies what it might not be able to do with its own agents. An FBI agent might need a court order to conduct certain surveillance; but if a local officer, acting under color of state law and perhaps not constrained by the same warrant requirements, collects the information and shares it voluntarily, the constitutional protections are bypassed. This “silver platter” doctrine, once repudiated by the Supreme Court in Elkins v. United States (1960), threatens to return with a vengeance in the intelligence context, where the lines between criminal and national-security investigations are deliberately muddled.

Some will object that the threat of terrorism is too urgent for such constitutional fastidiousness. But our constitutional structure was not designed only for calm times. It was meant to be a bulwark precisely when fear pushes government to overreach. The proper response to the post-9/11 security challenge is not to enlist every local beat cop as an ad hoc federal agent, but to ensure that the federal agencies with statutory responsibility for counterterrorism—the FBI, the CIA, and the new Department of Homeland Security—are adequately funded, properly supervised, and bound by clear statutory limits. If they need more manpower, Congress can expand their ranks. If they need better coordination, improved information-sharing protocols can be devised without subsuming local governments into the national security state.

Let the federal government do its job; let local police do theirs. Cooperation in specific, case-by-case investigations is inevitable and can be useful, but institutionalizing a permanent intelligence-gathering role for local police is a category error. It will not make us safer, and it will certainly weaken the constitutional barriers that protect our freedoms. The right fight, from a federalist perspective, is to rebuild the wall between federal and state functions, not to tear it down in the name of a false efficiency.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The division between federal responsibility for national defense and state responsibility for local policing is a vital safeguard against the concentration of unaccountable power.
  2. 02Historical federal funding of local law enforcement has already created a dangerous dependency that distorts local priorities and makes municipalities vulnerable to federal mandates.
  3. 03Integrating local police into federal networks will not grant them leverage but will instead subject them to federal directives and the threat of withheld grants.
  4. 04The fusion of federal and local law enforcement allows the government to bypass Fourth Amendment protections by using local proxies to collect information that federal agents could not legally obtain without warrants.
  5. 05Counterterrorism needs should be met by properly supervising and funding federal agencies rather than institutionalizing a permanent intelligence-gathering role for local police.
