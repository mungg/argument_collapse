"The Deep South remains more conservative than the rest of the country because it is the primary region where racial resentment and evangelical religious identity have fused into a singular, powerful political culture."

The Deep South remains more conservative because it is the part of the country where America’s two great political divides — race and religion — overlap most powerfully. Neither factor alone explains the pattern. Plenty of white Northerners are racially resentful, and plenty of non-Southerners are religious conservatives. But in much of the Deep South, white evangelical Protestantism, Republican identity, opposition to federal power and a long memory of racial conflict have fused into one political culture.

The “post-racial New South” was always more aspiration than description. It is true that Atlanta, Charlotte, Nashville and other metropolitan areas have become more diverse, educated and globally connected. It is true that Southern states now compete for auto plants, research universities and technology firms. But social change has not erased the political meaning of race. The civil rights revolution was experienced by many white Southerners not only as justice for black citizens, but as an assertion of federal authority over local society. That history still shapes attitudes toward Washington, welfare, voting rights, schools and criminal justice.

The white working class is especially susceptible to this politics because it has suffered real economic insecurity while being offered a cultural explanation for its loss of status. Deindustrialization, declining unions, low wages and weak public services have hit the South hard. Yet Southern conservatism often teaches voters to see government as the enemy, taxes as theft, and social programs as benefits for undeserving others. Race need not be mentioned explicitly for the message to be understood.

Religion reinforces the pattern. In much of the South, churches are not just houses of worship but civic networks, information channels and identity markers. Abortion, same-sex marriage, school prayer and gun rights have become symbols of whether candidates respect “people like us.” A Massachusetts Republican like Mitt Romney may not be culturally Southern, but he is still preferable to Barack Obama for many white working-class Southerners because party identity has become tribal.

The South has changed enormously, but not evenly. Its cities are becoming less conservative; its black voters remain overwhelmingly Democratic; its growing Latino population may alter politics over time. But among white working-class voters in the Deep South, the old alignments have not disappeared. They have been modernized, nationalized and absorbed into Republican conservatism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical civil rights revolution is still viewed by many white Southerners as an overreach of federal authority, shaping a lasting skepticism toward Washington and social programs.
  2. 02Economic insecurity among the white working class is channeled into a conservative ideology that frames government intervention and taxes as threats to their status.
  3. 03Southern evangelical churches function as civic and tribal networks that reinforce Republican identity through shared cultural symbols and social issues.
  4. 04While Southern metropolitan areas are diversifying, the traditional political alignments among white working-class voters have been modernized and absorbed into the national Republican platform rather than erased.
