Diversified Gemini

"While the Port Huron Statement’s ideal of participatory democracy is inspiring, it fails to address how informal, consensus-based organizing often replicates the structural inequalities and hierarchies of the broader society."

Tom Hayden’s reflection on the Port Huron Statement reads like an inspiring victory lap for the New Left, finding the "spirits of the ’60s" alive and well in today’s activist movements. It is an appealing narrative, especially for those of us who have spent the last year marching, organizing, and occupying. Hayden’s assertion that participatory democracy allows us to "steer our boats in a wider sea with many currents" is poetic and validating. However, as an active participant in today’s movements, I must point out a glaring blind spot in Hayden’s retrospective: he completely ignores the persistent, structural inequalities that exist *within* the very spaces of participatory democracy he so fervently champions.

The harsh reality, which feminist and civil rights organizers have pointed out for decades, is that "participatory democracy" and "consensus decision-making" frequently replicate the hierarchies of the society they are protesting against. Without rigorous, formal structures to protect marginalized voices, participatory spaces inevitably default to rule by the loudest, the most privileged, and the most unencumbered.

When the Port Huron Statement was written fifty years ago, the Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) was overwhelmingly white and male. The women of the early New Left were notoriously sidelined—expected to type up manifestos, make coffee, and serve as sexual partners to the male leaders, rather than shape ideological strategy. Hayden touches on this briefly, noting that the Statement was written before the "modern women’s movement" and before the "great coming out" of LGBTQ individuals. He frames this as an innocent historical anachronism, suggesting that participatory democracy naturally evolved to include everyone.

But this inclusion did not happen naturally or easily. It happened because Black feminists, radical lesbians, and women of color fought bitterly against the white male left establishment, demanding spaces where their specific material realities were not submerged under universalizing rhetoric about "joint-creation of our community."

Fast forward to the Occupy Movement of 2011 and 2012, which Hayden upholds as the prime modern example of the Port Huron ideal. I spent weeks in Occupy encampments, and while the spirit was undoubtedly powerful, the mechanics of our participatory democracy were deeply flawed. The General Assemblies, with their complex hand signals and demand for absolute consensus, were brutal marathons of endurance. Who has the capacity to sit in a park for six hours on a Tuesday night debating the wording of a manifesto? Not the single mother working two jobs. Not the disabled activist who cannot navigate the physical space. Not the undocumented worker who cannot risk arrest.

The people who thrived in the "structurelessness" of Occupy were disproportionately young, white, able-bodied men with a safety net to fall back on. As Jo Freeman warned in 1970, structurelessness is a myth; informal cliques always form, and because they are informal, they are unaccountable. In our quest for radical, leaderless consensus, we often ended up silencing the very people we claimed to be fighting for. When women in Occupy camps raised issues of sexual assault and safety, they were frequently told that addressing these "divisive" issues would disrupt the movement's unity against the banks.

Hayden’s essay fails to grapple with the fact that true participatory democracy requires incredibly hard, intentional work to dismantle internalized racism, sexism, and classism. It cannot simply be a big tent where "Marxists, feminists, anarchists... and even conservatives" happily coexist. The interests of a feminist fighting for reproductive justice do not "easily fit in" with a conservative who fundamentally denies her bodily autonomy. To suggest they can unite around a vague framework of "participatory democracy" is to reduce politics to a purely procedural exercise, devoid of moral stakes.

Furthermore, Hayden’s "inside-outside" strategy feels hopelessly naive from an intersectional perspective. He praises politicians who open doors to the "winds of change," yet these institutions remain deeply entrenched in white supremacy and patriarchy. The system does not just need a few pragmatic insiders to push through reforms like the Americans with Disabilities Act—though such victories are crucial, as Hayden notes. The system itself must be fundamentally transformed, because it was built from the ground up to exclude.

If the Port Huron Statement is to have any relevance today beyond nostalgia, it cannot be treated as a holy text whose core message is simply applied to new movements. We must learn from the failures of the 1960s, not just repeat their slogans. We need a model of participatory democracy that does not shy away from structure, but embraces transparent, accountable leadership. We need processes that explicitly center the most marginalized voices, rather than hoping they are heard in the chaotic din of a general assembly.

Participatory democracy is a beautiful ideal, but without a rigorous commitment to intersectional equity, it is just another mechanism for the privileged to hear themselves speak. We owe it to the future to build a movement that is not only democratic in its protests, but deeply and structurally just in its internal operations.

  3. 03The logistical demands of modern participatory movements, such as Occupy Wall Street’s marathons of consensus, effectively exclude those with limited time, physical mobility, or legal security.
  4. 04The concept of 'structurelessness' is a myth that allows unaccountable informal cliques to form and silences internal critiques regarding issues like sexual assault and safety.
  5. 05A truly just movement requires more than a procedural framework; it necessitates intentional, structural efforts to dismantle internalized racism, sexism, and classism within its own operations.
