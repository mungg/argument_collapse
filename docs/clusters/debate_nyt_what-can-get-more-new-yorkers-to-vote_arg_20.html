"To increase voter turnout in New York, the state must end partisan gerrymandering by transferring redistricting power from the legislature to an independent, non-partisan citizens' commission."

The hand-wringing over low voter turnout in New York City's Democratic primaries misses the fundamental truth of political behavior: voters are rational actors. When they believe their vote can change an outcome, they show up. When they believe the outcome is preordained by party bosses and backroom deals, they stay home. The crisis of turnout in New York is directly tied to the crisis of uncompetitive elections, which is driven entirely by partisan gerrymandering.

Earlier this year, we witnessed yet another shameful redistricting cycle in Albany. The Legislative Task Force on Demographic Research and Reapportionment (LATFOR)—a thinly veiled incumbent-protection racket controlled by the legislature itself—drew the state’s new political maps. While a federal court eventually had to intervene to finalize the congressional lines, the overall political culture of New York remains one where districts are meticulously engineered to protect the status quo.

In New York City, lines are drawn to concentrate partisan advantages, ensuring that incumbents face virtually zero threat in the November general election. More insidiously, these lines are often drawn to splinter natural communities of interest to protect favored incumbents from serious primary challenges. When a district is engineered to be a safe fortress for a sitting politician, the primary ceases to be a dynamic contest of ideas and becomes a mere formality.

Why should a voter in Queens or Brooklyn take an hour out of their Tuesday to cast a ballot in a race where the incumbent is functionally unopposed, or where the challenger has been structurally boxed out of viability? They shouldn't, and they don't.

If we want robust participation, we need robust competition. The only way to achieve this is to strip the power of redistricting from self-interested politicians and hand it to a truly independent, non-partisan citizens' commission. We need districts drawn based on logical geographic boundaries and community cohesion, not on the addresses of incumbents and their potential rivals.

Until New York enacts binding, independent redistricting reform, the vast majority of our elections will remain uncompetitive. As long as the politicians are allowed to choose their voters, the voters will rightly conclude there is little point in choosing their politicians.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Low voter turnout is a rational response to uncompetitive elections where outcomes are perceived as preordained by political incumbents.
  2. 02The current redistricting process functions as an incumbent-protection racket that meticulously engineers districts to eliminate political threats.
  3. 03Gerrymandering splinters natural communities of interest to ensure that primary challenges are structurally non-viable.
  4. 04Voters will continue to abstain from participating as long as districts are drawn to favor politicians' interests over geographic and community cohesion.
