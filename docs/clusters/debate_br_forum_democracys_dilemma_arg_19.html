"The proposed institutional and technical solutions to 'Democracy’s Dilemma' are dangerous because they treat democratic discourse as a security threat to be managed, potentially leading to censorship and the suppression of dissent."

Farrell and Schneier have performed a valuable service by puncturing the technological determinism of both the cyber‑utopians and the new Cassandras. Their call for a dynamical model of democracy that can accommodate deep disagreement is exactly right. But the institutional solutions they propose, drawn from information security, point in a worrying direction. By treating democratic discourse as an “attack surface” and ordinary citizens as potential vectors for “information attacks,” they inadvertently embrace a mindset that has historically been used to justify censorship and the suppression of dissident voices. Instead of retreating from the Millian conviction that more speech is the answer to bad speech, we should double down on it—recognizing that democracy’s strength lies precisely in its capacity to absorb, filter, and argue over a chaotic torrent of claims without resorting to top‑down gatekeeping.

The essay’s central metaphor is revealing. An attack surface is something to be minimized, hardened, and monitored. In computing, that makes sense. In democracy, it reclassifies political speech as a threat to be managed. The authors are careful to distinguish their approach from censorship: they want to channel dissent, not suppress it. But the instruments they propose—authentication of commenters, enforcement against “actively deceitful political communications strategies,” and institutional redesign to maintain “common political knowledge”—would inevitably require authorities to distinguish legitimate from illegitimate speech. That is a power no government or platform should be entrusted with.

Consider the FCC commenting process. It is true that the flood of fake comments supporting the repeal of net neutrality undermined the process’s integrity. But the solution offered—some combination of authentication and auditing—would fundamentally alter the nature of public commenting. Currently, anyone can submit a comment without providing a verified identity, enabling not only astroturfing but also genuine grassroots participation by people who fear retaliation. Requiring identification would chill the speech of undocumented immigrants, whistleblowers, and those who hold unpopular views. The New York attorney general’s investigation may eventually punish wrongdoers, but a blanket authentication system would be a permanent infrastructure of surveillance. Moreover, the real problem was not that the FCC could not tell real from fake, but that it chose to ignore the overwhelming substantive opposition. Agency capture, not comment fraud, was the democratic failure. Technical fixes would not have changed Ajit Pai’s vote.

The Doug Jones case illustrates a similar logic. Democratic operatives created a deceptive Facebook page. That is sleazy, but political campaigns have always used dirty tricks. The appropriate response is exposure and public shaming, not a new regulatory regime for “truthful” political speech. The First Amendment rightly protects a broad space for political lying, because the power to decide what is a lie is too easily abused by incumbents to silence critics. The fear that such tactics will spiral into mutual distrust is real, but the more dangerous spiral is one where each side seeks to use the state to police the other’s communications. That is the path to illiberalism.

As for the census, the debate over the citizenship question is a profound democratic disagreement, not an “information attack.” Immigrant rights activists who tell noncitizens to boycott the census are engaging in a time‑honored form of protest; they are contesting the legitimacy of a process they view as coercive. The authors seem to want a census that produces an accurate count all sides accept as “common political knowledge.” But what if one side believes that the census itself is a tool of oppression? In the 1980s, many African American leaders urged boycotts to protest undercounting. That agonistic contestation is part of the democratic dynamic, not a bug. We can fund outreach and education, but we should not pathologize those who refuse to participate.

The underlying assumption of the essay is that there exists a set of shared facts democracy requires, and that these facts are under assault from information attacks. That is true to a point—elections need agreed outcomes—but it underestimates the extent to which factual claims themselves are always contested in politics. The climate change example is instructive: Republicans reject the scientific consensus because it threatens their economic interests. That is a political problem, not an informational one. No amount of fact‑checking will resolve it; only political organizing and power shifts will. The goal should be to win the argument, not to design a system that forces acceptance.

Democracies have survived far worse information environments. The nineteenth‑century American press was a cesspool of partisan fabrication. The early days of radio and television saw panics about propaganda. In each case, the system adapted without centralized control: norms of professional journalism emerged, media literacy grew, and audiences learned to be skeptical. Today, we are in another such transition. The path forward is not to build a secure, managed public sphere, but to encourage a multiplicity of voices, strengthen independent media and nonprofit newsrooms, teach critical thinking, and foster a culture that celebrates argument rather than fearing it.

Farrell and Schneier claim that democracy’s vulnerabilities are “internal” and require institutional redesign. But the internal strength of democracy is its tolerance for noise, dissent, and even deception. When we try to filter the noise from the signal, we often end up filtering out the very voices that need to be heard. Democracy is not a system to be optimized; it is a perpetual argument. Let us keep that argument as open and unmanaged as possible, for the alternative—a curated debate—is the true twilight of democracy.
