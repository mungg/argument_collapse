Diversified DeepSeek

"The American system of checks and balances is not the cause of political paralysis but a necessary safeguard against majoritarian tyranny and authoritarianism that must be preserved and restored."

Lisa L. Miller is right about one thing: the American system of government is in deep trouble. But her diagnosis is exactly backward. The checks and balances she sees as a dead end are not the cause of our paralysis; they are the last, fraying guardrails protecting the republic from the majoritarian impulsiveness and concentrated power that have doomed democracies throughout history. The real crisis is not that these constraints exist, but that they have been progressively weakened by decades of executive overreach, judicial activism, and congressional abdication, leaving them unable to perform their essential function—tempering popular passions and forcing the kind of extended deliberation that produces durable law. Miller’s call to dismantle these safeguards in favor of more efficient majoritarianism is a prescription for authoritarianism, not a cure for it.

Her central error is to conflate responsiveness with democratic legitimacy. She argues that because the system blocks popular policies like universal health care or a higher minimum wage, it is anti-democratic. But democracy is not simply the mechanical translation of majority preferences into law, especially in a continent-sized, heterogeneous nation. The Framers understood that majority rule without robust minority protections can rapidly become tyranny. They designed a system that would slow the pace of change, require broad coalition-building across geographic and ideological lines, and protect fundamental liberties from transient electoral winds. The fact that this system has frustrated some progressive ambitions is a feature, not a bug. It has also, over time, prevented radical rollbacks of social insurance, protected civil liberties during periods of hysteria, and ensured that major changes enjoy deep, sustained public support before becoming entrenched.

Consider Miller’s own examples. The New Deal and Great Society programs she lionizes did not come about because a slim majority muscled them through; they were the product of overwhelming electoral mandates and durable governing coalitions that crossed party lines. Social Security passed with strong Republican support in both chambers. The Civil Rights Act was enacted with a bipartisan supermajority that broke a filibuster. These were not majoritarian steamrolls; they were consensus achievements. The Affordable Care Act, by contrast, was rammed through on a party-line vote using procedural tricks, and it remains politically contested more than a decade later precisely because it lacked broad legitimacy. If anything, the ACA saga illustrates the dangers of attempting to bypass the system’s demand for consensus: a law that never gained majority public approval and was immediately a target for repeal.

Miller’s account of health care history is also tendentious. She attributes the repeated failure of universal coverage to veto points exploited by elites, but this severely understates the genuine ideological and prudential disagreements among the public. Americans are not clamoring for a British-style NHS; they consistently tell pollsters they want choice, quality, and innovation, even if it comes with inequality. The system’s complexity reflects that divided public mind. The fact that other democracies achieved universal coverage earlier says more about their smaller, more homogeneous populations and different cultural attitudes toward the state than about any inherent flaw in our constitution. France, for instance, built its system through a centralized administrative state that predated mass democracy, not through majoritarian legislation. The comparison is misleading.

More troubling is Miller’s dismissal of federalism. She views it as a vestige of racial oppression, but this history, while real, does not discredit the principle of dispersed power. Federalism is why blue states today can protect reproductive rights, expand health coverage, and set their own environmental standards in defiance of a hostile federal government. It is precisely this vertical division of authority that prevents any single national administration from monopolizing all levers of coercion. Miller calls for states to resist Trump, yet simultaneously denigrates the constitutional authority that makes that resistance possible. Should a future left-wing president attempt to impose a national takeover of industry or speech restrictions, federalism would be an essential safeguard. Tearing it down in the name of majoritarian efficiency would be shortsighted in the extreme.

The gravest danger in Miller’s argument, however, is her hostility to judicial review. She paints the courts as simply another veto point for elites, but the Supreme Court has also been the principal defender of rights against majorities—from *Brown v. Board of Education* to *Obergefell v. Hodges*. True, the Court has also issued reactionary rulings, but that is the nature of a counter-majoritarian institution: it can err in either direction. The solution is not to eliminate its power, but to ensure a less politicized appointment process. Abolishing the filibuster for judicial nominations—a partisan move by both parties—has already accelerated the politicization Miller laments. Further weakening the courts in the name of checking “elites” would leave unpopular minorities at the mercy of whichever party controls the legislature and executive. In a country where political coalitions can shift dramatically every two years, that is a recipe for chaos and oppression.

At bottom, Miller’s vision is a democratic purism that mistakes majority rule for justice. But majorities are often illiberal, vengeful, and misinformed. The genius of the American system has always been its ability to temper those passions while still allowing for change. The current dysfunction is not due to too many checks, but to a political culture that has abandoned the art of compromise and a citizenry that increasingly huddles in tribal echo chambers. The remedy is not to remove the brake lines from the vehicle just because the driver is erratic; it is to restore the norms of negotiation, rebuild civic education, and recommit to the painstaking work of coalition that the system demands. Let us not dismantle the architecture that has historically preserved freedom in the name of a majoritarian efficiency that, in the wrong hands, could just as easily destroy it.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While the American system of checks and balances has historically empowered elites, it remains an essential tool for democratic self-defense against current authoritarian threats and should be democratized rather than discarded."

Lisa L. Miller’s bracing indictment of American checks and balances arrives with the force of a gale-force wind. She is right to expose how the system’s multiple veto points have routinely empowered the few to block the many, turning the Framers’ vision of restrained government into a machine for minority rule. Her historical narrative—from the AMA’s sabotage of health care reform to the filibuster’s role in preserving racial hierarchy—is a necessary corrective to the sanitized civics textbook story. And her call for a revitalized mass politics that forces institutional reform is urgent and welcome. Yet, at this perilous moment in May 2025, the case for bypassing checks and balances risks discarding the very tools that, however imperfect, are currently holding back an administration bent on dismantling democratic norms.

The flaw is not in Miller’s diagnosis of elite capture, but in a prescription that too hastily assigns responsibility to the architecture of government itself, as if the structure were inherently oligarchic rather than one that can be made more or less democratic depending on who wields it. The fact is, checks and balances are not a fixed monolith. They are a set of levers that, depending on the balance of organized power, can either entrench the few or protect the many. During the current administration, those levers are being pulled by state attorneys general, federal judges, and civil society organizations to block the dismantling of public services, the gutting of regulatory protections, and the lawless actions of DOGE. When a district judge halts an executive order that would strip health coverage from millions, or when California and other states refuse to enforce unconstitutional immigration directives, those are not nostalgic appeals to a golden age—they are contemporary acts of democratic self-defense. Calling them part of a “dead end” narrative risks demobilizing the very coalitions that need to hold the line right now.

Miller’s own reform agenda implicitly acknowledges that the solution lies not in abolishing checks and balances but in recalibrating them. Ending the filibuster, expanding the House, eliminating the Electoral College, and amending judicial tenure all aim to make the system more responsive to majorities without removing the institutional friction that can sometimes guard against sudden authoritarian capture. Those reforms would still leave the United States with an unusually fragmented government by comparative standards, but they would tilt the playing field away from narrow interests. In other words, the problem isn’t that checks exist; it’s that they’ve been rigged to favor those who can afford the most influence. The answer is to democratize those checkpoints, not to treat them as permanently anti-democratic.

The historical evidence that Miller marshals is powerful, but it cuts both ways. The same Jim Crow filibusters that she cites were eventually defeated by the Civil Rights Act of 1964—a legislative achievement that required overcoming multiple veto points, yes, but that also relied on the Supreme Court’s check on state-sponsored segregation in *Brown v. Board of Education* and on the executive branch’s enforcement powers. These were not victories against the system; they were victories *through* a system that, under sustained pressure from mass movements, bent toward justice. Similarly, the New Deal and Great Society programs she rightly celebrates were enacted during periods of unified government, but they also survived constitutional challenges precisely because the Supreme Court eventually validated the national government’s authority to address economic crises. That Court was itself a check that had to be won over, not abolished.

The danger of framing checks and balances primarily as a narrative trap is that it underestimates how much the current authoritarian threat depends on discrediting any institution that can say “no” to the executive. Trump’s open flouting of court orders, his denigration of Congress as a rubber stamp, and his allies’ attacks on “deep state” bureaucrats all aim to remove friction from the system. In this context, defending the principle that power must be accountable to law—even when the law is imperfect and the courts are inconsistent—is not a sentimental attachment to a failed order. It is a direct challenge to the logic of executive supremacy. Majorities will only be able to reclaim democratic control if they first preserve the institutional infrastructure that makes counter-mobilization possible: an independent judiciary, subnational governments that retain some autonomy, and a legislature that, however malapportioned, still has the constitutional tools to investigate and push back.

Miller’s vision of a mass democratic politics is inspiring and necessary. But that politics cannot be conjured from thin air; it needs breathing room. The urgent task is to use the checks we have—the courts, federalism, the First Amendment protections that allow protest and press to function—to slow the assault on the rule of law long enough for deeper organizing to take root. That means, yes, making the case for structural reform while simultaneously blocking the worst excesses of the current administration through the very veto points that have often served elite ends. The story of American democracy is not a straight line from oligarchy to popular rule; it is a zigzag in which the same institutional features that once protected planters or industrialists were later turned, by movements and strategic litigation and electoral victories, into shields for workers, minorities, and dissidents. The system’s engineering is not destiny; its operation depends on the political forces animating it.

In 2025, it would be a tragic mistake to abandon the defensive power of institutional checks just when they are most needed. The better path is to democratize them relentlessly while recognizing that, for all their flaws, they remain the bridges that can carry us from a moment of grave peril to the more open, responsive politics Miller rightly envisions. The goal isn’t to resurrect a mythical constitutional order but to keep the rudder in democratic hands long enough to steer toward a new one.

Diversified MiniMax

"While the lead essay correctly identifies political dysfunction, its solution of dismantling checks and balances in favor of mass majoritarianism risks enabling the tyranny of transient majorities and undermining essential protections for fundamental rights."

Miller's diagnosis of American political dysfunction is largely correct, but her proposed cure—expanded mass mobilization—risks substituting one form of majoritarian excess for another. The real lesson of checks and balances is not that they paralyze government, but that they protect fundamental rights against the tyranny of transient majorities. Her essay conflates two distinct problems: the legitimate frustration that popular policies cannot pass through our institutional maze, and the dangerous premise that making policy passage easier would produce better outcomes. The history she invokes to challenge checks and balances— segregationist Southerners blocking civil rights legislation, for instance—actually demonstrates their protective function for minority rights, even if those minorities were themselves majorities in their regions. The filibuster that blocked housing discrimination in 1966 was the same procedural mechanism that once enabled Southern opponents of desegregation to be overruled by Northern majorities in 1964. Checks and balances do not inherently favor elites; they create friction that can cut either way, depending on political alignments. The more serious concern Miller raises about elite capture is valid, but her solution of mass mobilization would not resolve it. Majorities can be just as captured by organized interests as minorities can be. The real problem is the quality of our political discourse and the corruption of our campaign finance system, not the constitutional structure itself. Reforming money in politics, strengthening transparency, and revitalizing civic education would address the genuine pathologies Miller identifies without abandoning the essential protections that prevent any single faction from implementing its program without regard for rivals' fundamental interests.

Vanilla Claude

"While the lead essay correctly critiques the American system's legislative veto points for obstructing popular reform, it dangerously conflates those obstructive mechanisms with essential institutional restraints on executive power that are currently necessary to prevent authoritarian consolidation."

Lisa Miller's essay performs a useful service by puncturing the reflexive piety with which Democratic politicians have invoked "checks and balances" since January. She is right that the phrase often functions as incantation rather than analysis, and right that America's profusion of veto points has long protected entrenched wealth against popular majorities. The story she tells about health care—a century of broadly supported reform strangled in committee, killed by filibuster, hollowed out by federalism—is largely correct, and it should embarrass anyone who treats our constitutional architecture as self-evidently virtuous.

But her essay collapses two very different things under one heading, and in doing so reaches conclusions that, in the spring of 2025, are dangerous.

The two things are these. First, there is the veto-point-heavy structure of American lawmaking: bicameralism, the filibuster, committee chairs, federalism, judicial review of statutes. This is the system Miller diagnoses, and her critique is on solid ground. Comparative political science—Lijphart, Tsebelis, Hacker and Pierson—has documented for decades that the United States is an outlier in how many points of obstruction it offers organized minorities, and that this generally favors capital over labor and incumbents over reformers.

Second, there is the system of restraints on executive power: an independent judiciary, an independent civil service, congressional oversight, inspectors general, state attorneys general, a free press, the constitutional separation between the president and the agencies that prosecute crimes. These are also commonly called "checks and balances," but they do something quite different. They do not block popular legislation. They constrain the unilateral exercise of arbitrary power by a single official.

Miller's essay slides between these two senses as if they were the same problem. They are not. The Trump administration is not primarily a story about veto points frustrating a popular agenda; it is a story about executive aggrandizement against duly enacted statutes and constitutional limits. When the administration fires inspectors general en masse, impounds appropriated funds, deports people in defiance of court orders, and lets a private billionaire roam through Treasury payment systems, the "checks" that matter are not committee chairs sitting on bills. They are judges, state AGs, career lawyers willing to resign, and the residual capacity of federalism to keep blue-state institutions out of federal capture. To dismiss all of this as "doubling down on dysfunction" is to give up the firewall while the building burns.

The comparative record should give Miller more pause than it seems to. The democracies that have suffered the sharpest authoritarian erosions in the past fifteen years—Hungary, Turkey, India, arguably Israel—are mostly more majoritarian than the United States, with fewer veto points and weaker federalism. Orbán did not need to defeat a Senate filibuster to capture the Hungarian judiciary; the absence of one made his task easier. The United Kingdom, the paradigm Westminster system Miller's framework would seem to prefer, executed Brexit on a thin parliamentary majority over deep public ambivalence, with consequences few defend. The lesson is not that veto points are good. It is that the relationship between institutional thickness and democratic resilience is contingent and direction-specific: veto points that block redistribution can be the same veto points that block autocratic consolidation.

This matters for Miller's reform agenda, much of which I support. Abolishing the filibuster, ending the Electoral College, expanding the House, curbing gerrymandering, regulating money in elections, allowing term limits on judges—these are good ideas, and they target the obstructive function of our system rather than the restraining function. But notice that her own list quietly concedes the distinction: she wants to "clarify the scope of executive power," which is to say, strengthen a check. A genuinely majoritarian system in which the president, having won 49.8 percent of the vote, faced no judicial review and no federalist friction would not be a more democratic system. It would be the system Viktor Orbán built.

Miller's deepest move is to identify mass mobilization itself as a check on power, and here she is genuinely illuminating. The New Deal, civil rights, the labor movement—these did more to discipline American elites than any constitutional provision. A democratic politics that mobilizes broad majorities around tangible material interests is, she rightly insists, what "good, broad-based politics" looks like, and it is the only thing that has ever durably overcome elite obstruction.

But mass movements take years to build, and they require the institutional space in which to operate: courts that will enjoin retaliatory prosecutions, states that will protect organizers, universities and unions and newspapers that are not yet captured. The civil rights movement she invokes did not flourish in spite of federalism; it exploited federalism, finding refuge in northern states and federal courts when local majorities were hostile. Mass politics and institutional checks are complements, not substitutes.

The honest synthesis is this: reform the veto points that strangle legislation, defend the restraints that constrain executives, and build the movements that can do both. Miller has written half of that essay brilliantly. The other half—why, in May 2025, the courts and the states and the inspectors general still matter—needs writing too.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A critical distinction exists between veto points that block democratic lawmaking (like the filibuster) and checks that constrain arbitrary executive power (like an independent judiciary and civil service).
  2. 02The lead essay's dismissal of institutional checks ignores the comparative historical evidence that majoritarian systems with fewer veto points are often more vulnerable to rapid authoritarian erosion.
  3. 03The lead essay's own reform agenda implicitly acknowledges the need for restraints by calling for a clarification of executive power, suggesting that total majoritarianism is not the goal.
  4. 04Mass political movements, while essential for long-term change, require the protection of existing institutional checks and federalist structures to survive and operate during periods of autocratic pressure.
  5. 05A viable democratic strategy must simultaneously reform the veto points that paralyze social progress while defending the institutional guardrails that prevent the unilateral exercise of power.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Weakening federalism and centralizing power in national majorities would destroy essential safeguards for local self-determination and create a more dangerous, unrestrained government."

Lisa L. Miller’s essay is a tour de force of progressive frustration, and it contains a kernel of truth that few Americans would dispute: our government often feels paralyzed, distant, and captured by special interests. But her proposed cure—weakening federalism, diluting the Senate, and empowering direct national majorities—is far more dangerous than the disease. The nightmare scenario for American liberty is not a government that sometimes fails to act; it is a centralized, unrestrained government that acts all too easily, sweeping away regional differences, local self-determination, and the ability of communities to shield themselves from an overreaching center. Federalism, the object of Miller’s particular scorn, is not a relic of racial oppression but the most resilient check of all against the concentration of power in a single, distant capital.

Miller’s indictment of federalism rests heavily on its association with slavery and segregation. No one can deny that states’ rights rhetoric has been deployed to defend abhorrent systems. But the same federal structure that allowed Southern states to maintain Jim Crow was also what allowed Northern states to abolish slavery, enact progressive labor laws, establish public universities, and, more recently, expand health coverage, protect LGBTQ+ rights, and resist federal immigration enforcement. The problem is not federalism; it is what states choose to do with their powers. The existence of Wyoming or Mississippi does not cause racial injustice; people and their elected officials do. Abolishing federalism would not make those people disappear; it would simply remove the ability of other states to pursue different, more just paths and would concentrate all decisions in a single national government that might one day fall under the control of a Trump or a successor even more authoritarian.

Consider the present moment. Trump’s administration is openly hostile to many core constitutional values. In a unitary, centralized system, that hostility would have no counterweight. But because education, elections, policing, and public health are largely administered at the state and local levels, the administration’s capacity to impose a comprehensive vision is limited. California can defend environmental standards; Illinois can protect reproductive rights; Massachusetts can maintain near-universal health coverage. This is not a flaw; it is a feature. Federalism, as Gavin Newsom’s statement inadvertently acknowledges, is the reason resistance is possible. Miller wants to have it both ways—to applaud state defiance while disparaging the constitutional authority that makes it possible.

Her historical recounting of the civil rights struggle misses a crucial point: the ultimate triumph of racial justice came not by abolishing federalism but by using national power to set a floor beneath which no state could sink. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments, the Civil Rights Acts, and the Voting Rights Act did not eliminate states; they constrained them. That model—national minimum standards with state experimentation—is far superior to the complete nationalization of policy. It allows states to act as laboratories of democracy, testing ideas that can later be adopted nationally if successful. The Affordable Care Act’s reliance on state Medicaid expansion, which Miller criticizes, actually allowed some states to move forward immediately and created political pressure on others to follow. The result, imperfect as it is, has still reduced the uninsured rate dramatically.

Moreover, Miller’s faith in majoritarian national solutions overlooks the extent to which local and state governments are often more responsive to ordinary citizens than the federal behemoth. It is easier for local activists to influence a city council than the U.S. Congress. It is easier to hold a state legislator accountable than a Senator from a state of 40 million. Subsidiarity—the principle that decisions should be made at the most local level practicable—is not an elite plot; it is a democratic principle in its own right. The left’s growing disdain for localism is a political blunder: it cedes the language of community empowerment to the right and fuels the perception that liberalism is a project of coastal elites imposing their preferences on the heartland.

Miller’s alternative vision of constitutional reform—eliminating the Electoral College, reducing the Senate’s power, allowing citizen assemblies—would centralize decision-making in a way that would disproportionately empower metropolitan majorities and marginalize rural communities, small states, and cultural minorities. That is a recipe for national division, not unity. It is worth remembering that the United States is not a single polity like Denmark or New Zealand; it is a sprawling, deeply varied continental nation. The systems that work for a homogeneous parliamentary democracy simply do not map onto our geography and demography. A purely plebiscitary national democracy would not only fail to produce just outcomes; it would likely generate intense backlash and regional alienation, potentially even secessionist sentiment.

The real pathology in our system is not federalism but the capture of the federal government by concentrated corporate power. That is the issue Miller and other reformists should train their fire on. Reversing *Citizens United*, closing the revolving door between Wall Street and the Treasury, breaking up monopolies—these are battles that can be won without dismantling the constitutional architecture. Federalism can actually be an ally in this fight, as states like California have shown by enacting net neutrality rules and stronger data privacy protections in the absence of federal action.

Checks and balances, including federalism, are not a dead end. They are the institutional expression of a deep American insight: that power, wherever it resides, must be divided, constrained, and brought as close to the people as possible. The answer to elite capture is not to centralize power in a national majority that elites will find just as easy to manipulate; it is to disperse power even further, deepen local democracy, and ensure that no single set of institutions can ever monopolize the levers of control. That vision is more faithful to the best traditions of American democracy than the majoritarian bulldozer Miller proposes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Federalism provides a vital defensive mechanism for states to protect civil liberties and pursue progressive policies when the national government is hostile to those values.
  2. 02The historical failures of states' rights do not justify abolishing federalism, as the system allows for national floors of justice while maintaining state-level experimentation.
  3. 03Local and state governments are inherently more responsive and accountable to ordinary citizens than a centralized national bureaucracy.
  4. 04Dismantling the Senate and the Electoral College would marginalize rural communities and small states, leading to intense regional alienation and national instability.
  5. 05The primary cause of political dysfunction is corporate capture of the federal government, which can be addressed through targeted reforms without dismantling the constitutional architecture.
  6. 06Dispersing power through federalism is a more effective way to prevent elite monopolization than centralizing power in a national majority that is equally susceptible to manipulation.
Diversified Gemini

"While the American system of checks and balances has significant flaws that empower elites, abandoning it in favor of unfiltered majoritarianism would destroy the essential deliberative friction that prevents civil fracture and protects pluralism in a diverse nation."

In her provocative essay, Lisa L. Miller takes aim at one of the foundational myths of the American republic: the inherent virtue of checks and balances. By detailing how the proliferation of veto points has historically thwarted the public will and enriched entrenched elites, she forces a necessary confrontation with the failures of our institutional design. However, in diagnosing the disease of modern American politics, Miller prescribes a cure—unfiltered majoritarianism—that fundamentally misapprehends the philosophical purpose of democratic friction.

Miller evaluates the success of a political system primarily by its throughput: how quickly and efficiently it translates public preference (as captured by polls) into national policy. From this aggregative view of democracy, our system is an abysmal failure. She notes that less than 5 percent of bills become law, and popular initiatives like universal healthcare languish for decades. To Miller, a veto point is simply an undemocratic obstacle, a weapon wielded by the American Medical Association or Southern segregationists to defy the majority.

But this critique reduces politics to a simple equation of numerical superiority. It ignores the deliberative tradition of democracy, which argues that the legitimacy of a law derives not just from how many people support it, but from the process by which it is enacted. The Madisonian architecture of the United States—for all its undeniable flaws and historical complicities with gross injustice—was designed to slow down the translation of public passion into coercive state power. It is an architecture of friction.

Why value friction? Because speed in politics is frequently the enemy of stability and pluralism. Miller looks at the current Trump administration and concludes that our checks and balances have failed to stop his authoritarianism, and therefore we must abandon them to enact a progressive counter-agenda. But imagine a system entirely stripped of veto points—a unicameral legislature, elected by simple majority, with no filibuster, a weak presidency, and no judicial review. In such a system, whoever captures 51% of the vote has absolute, unmitigated power to rewrite the social contract.

In a diverse, continent-spanning nation of 330 million people divided by region, religion, and deep ideological rifts, a system that allows a bare majority to continuously bulldoze the minority without compromise is a recipe for civil fracture. When the stakes of every election are absolute—when losing means your values and interests are entirely subordinated to the victor—politics ceases to be a negotiation and becomes a cold civil war.

The necessity of passing a bill through the House, the Senate, and the President's desk is not just about giving lobbyists multiple chances to kill it, though that is a tragic side effect. It is about forcing a narrow, partisan majority to broaden its coalition. It demands that a policy be modified, tempered, and adjusted until it achieves a broader consensus that crosses regional and ideological lines. A policy that survives the gauntlet of checks and balances is far more likely to be durable and accepted as legitimate by those who initially opposed it.

Miller points to the New Deal and the Great Society as the models of proper governance, when "one party decisively controlled both the legislative and executive branches." But those eras of unified dominance were historic anomalies, born of the Great Depression and the aftermath of a presidential assassination. We cannot design our everyday constitutional expectations around the hope of achieving crisis-level supermajorities. In the deeply polarized reality of 2025, demanding absolute majoritarian dominance ensures only permanent gridlock or rapid, destabilizing pendulum swings of policy every election cycle.

Furthermore, Miller's confidence in the inherent wisdom of "mass majorities" betrays a remarkably selective reading of human psychology and political history. She writes that Americans are broadly supportive of fair economic opportunity and equal treatment. While this may be true in abstract polling, mass publics are also highly susceptible to demagoguery, moral panics, and the scapegoating of out-groups. The populist right, led by figures like Trump, has been incredibly successful at mobilizing "mass majorities" at the local and state levels to ban books, restrict reproductive rights, and target marginalized communities.

The checks and balances that Miller wishes to dismantle—such as an independent judiciary—are precisely the mechanisms that require a society to pause and evaluate its majoritarian impulses against enduring constitutional principles. When the passions of the moment cry out for the deportation of millions or the suppression of unpopular speech, the slow, cumbersome machinery of the courts and the separation of powers forces a vital, sobering delay.

Miller is entirely correct that our current system has become too tilted toward the status quo, and that extreme minority gerrymandering and the anti-democratic nature of the Electoral College demand urgent reform. We must recalibrate the balance. We can reform the filibuster to require talking rather than silent holds, or implement ranked-choice voting to moderate extremism, without abandoning the fundamental principle of separated powers.

But to declare checks and balances a "dead end" is to abandon the profound insight that a healthy democracy requires more than just the raw aggregation of votes. It requires institutions that force us to listen to one another, to compromise, and to recognize that the other half of the country, even when we fiercely disagree with them, cannot simply be legislated out of existence by a temporary majority. In an era of terrifying political acceleration, the friction of our constitutional design may be the only thing preventing the machine from spinning entirely out of control.
