"The implementation of a financial transaction tax is the most effective way to curb destructive high-frequency trading strategies while funding necessary market surveillance."

A specter is haunting Wall Street — the specter of a financial transaction tax. And after the Knight Capital meltdown, it’s an idea whose time has come. A tiny levy on every stock, bond, and derivative trade would not only prevent the most destructive high-frequency strategies but would also generate revenue to pay for the market surveillance we desperately lack.

The core problem is that HFT firms have hijacked the price discovery process. They profit not by evaluating a company’s long-term prospects but by front-running institutional orders, exploiting sub-penny price discrepancies, and flooding exchanges with quotes they cancel within microseconds. This activity has no social value; it is a private tax on the rest of the market. A well-designed transaction tax — say, 0.01% on equity trades and a basis point or two on derivatives — would make these scalping strategies instantly unprofitable. Trades that facilitate genuine investment over a time horizon of minutes or days would be virtually unaffected, because the tax would be negligible compared to the expected return.

Skeptics warn that a tax would drive trading offshore or into dark pools. But the European Union is already moving toward a financial transaction tax, and France enacted its own version this month. With coordinated action, leakage can be minimized. More importantly, the tax can be structured to apply to trades by any counterparty with a U.S. nexus, regardless of venue, exactly as we already do for withholding taxes. The claim that markets would evaporate is self-serving fantasy.

The Knight disaster also shows why we need better regulatory technology. The SEC’s budget has been starved for years, and its antiquated systems cannot monitor a market that generates millions of quotes per second. A transaction tax would provide a dedicated funding stream to build a 21st-century surveillance system — real-time pattern recognition that could have spotted Knight’s aberrant behavior and halted it before the losses mounted. For an industry that demands nanosecond precision from its own systems, there is no excuse for forcing the public watchdog to operate on dial-up.

Regulation does not have to be a choice between laissez-faire chaos and Soviet-style planning. A financial transaction tax is a market-based tool that would re-align private incentives with public stability. The Knight fiasco is only the latest alarm. Let’s not hit snooze again.

  1. 01A small levy would make predatory high-frequency scalping strategies unprofitable without harming genuine, long-term investment.

  3. 03Concerns about trading moving offshore can be mitigated through international coordination and by applying the tax to any trade with a domestic nexus.
  4. 04High-frequency trading currently functions as a private tax on the market that provides no social value or genuine price discovery.
