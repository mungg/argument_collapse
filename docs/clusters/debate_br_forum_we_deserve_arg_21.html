Diversified DeepSeek

"While Evgeny Morozov’s proposal for 'ecological reason' seeks to challenge AI's efficiency-driven origins, it remains a Western-centric framework that fails to incorporate a truly decolonial perspective or recognize the non-Western epistemologies necessary for a radical technological transformation."

Evgeny Morozov’s essay offers a sweeping critique of AI’s Cold War origins and proposes a recovery of “ecological reason” through a creative rereading of intellectual history. The Latin American experiments he invokes—Cybersyn, the Bariloche Foundation, Oscar Varsavsky’s normative planning—are meant to show that a different technological path is possible, one oriented toward collective deliberation and open-ended simulation rather than efficiency and control. But the essay reproduces a familiar pattern: it treats the Global South as a reservoir of alternative experiments for the North’s theoretical enrichment, without truly grappling with the epistemic and political demands of decolonization.

Morozov admires Varsavsky’s willingness to chart “hippie,” “authoritarian,” “creative,” and “people-centric” styles of development, and he praises the Bariloche Foundation for contesting the geopolitical assumptions of The Limits to Growth. Yet the tools these projects employed—systems dynamics modeling, statistical forecasting, large-scale computation—were themselves products of the same Western scientific tradition that produced the Efficiency Lobby. Varsavsky’s numerical experimentation may have been politically radical, but it remained epistemologically Northern. It presumed that the future could be modeled, that societal choices could be represented as alternative parameters in a simulation, and that experts had a privileged role in constructing and interpreting those simulations. These are not universal assumptions; they are the legacy of a particular strand of Western rationalism.

A genuinely decolonial approach to AI would begin from the epistemologies of the colonized, not from a redirection of cybernetic tools. Indigenous data sovereignty movements, for example, reject the idea that all information is a neutral resource to be collected and processed. They insist that data about communities belong to those communities, that the categories used to organize data are cultural artifacts, and that knowledge production must be governed by protocols rooted in Indigenous law and custom. When Maori scholars in Aotearoa develop frameworks for Maori data governance, they are not asking for a seat at the table of ecological reason; they are asserting a different table altogether, one that Morozov’s European intellectual lineage—Storm, Veblen, Dewey—does not encompass.

The same is true of other non-Western traditions. The African concept of Ubuntu, often translated as “I am because we are,” posits a relational ontology that resonates with some aspects of ecological reason but goes far deeper in its insistence that personhood is constituted through community. A computational system built on Ubuntu would not merely permit open-ended tinkering; it would be oriented toward collective flourishing, mutual recognition, and the restoration of broken relationships. That is a far more radical vision than anything implied by eolithism. And it cannot be reduced to a footnote in a narrative that centers a San Francisco bohemian’s posthumous essay and the musings of a Heidegger-influenced German philosopher.

Morozov’s framing also underplays the extent to which the Cold War AI project was a colonial enterprise in its own right. The data that powered early computing research was often extracted from colonized territories—census data collected under imperial administrations, satellite imagery used to manage populations, linguistic data gathered by missionaries and anthropologists. The military applications that absorbed DARPA funding were deployed in proxy wars across Asia, Africa, and Latin America. When Morozov laments that the AI pioneers were insulated from democratic oversight, he might also note that the populations most affected by their creations had no voice in the matter at all.

The Latin American experiments themselves are more ambiguous than his account suggests. Cybersyn, for all its innovations, was a project of the Chilean state, designed to strengthen central economic coordination. It relied on the expertise of British cybernetician Stafford Beer and was embedded in a political conjuncture that, as Morozov acknowledges, ended in a military coup. Its legacy is contested: for some, it represents a technocratic fantasy of real-time socialist management; for others, an authentic experiment in workers’ information democracy. But it is too thin a reed on which to hang a global alternative.

What would it mean to take decolonization seriously in AI? It would mean ceasing to treat technologies as culturally neutral. It would mean supporting Indigenous and community-led data initiatives, such as the First Nations Information Governance Centre in Canada. It would mean directing public research funds toward epistemologically diverse AI projects, including those based on oral traditions, relational databases, and protocols that do not conform to Western notions of intellectual property. It would mean, above all, a redistribution of power from the centers of AI production—overwhelmingly in North America, Europe, and China—to the peripheries that have long been their experimental subjects.

Morozov is right about one thing: the AI we deserve will require deep political transformation. But that transformation must be anti-colonial at its core. Otherwise, ecological reason will just be another Northern export, dressed up this time in the language of flânerie and stones.
