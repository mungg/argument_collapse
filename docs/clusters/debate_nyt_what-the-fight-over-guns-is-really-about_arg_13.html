"The gun debate is intensely emotional because gun owners perceive gun control efforts as a moral condemnation of their lifestyle and a threat to their fundamental right to self-defense."

The fight over guns is emotional because gun owners increasingly believe they are not being argued with, but marked. The suburban New York newspaper’s publication of handgun permit holders’ names and addresses was a perfect example. These were people who had followed the law. Yet they were exposed to neighbors, employers, burglars and cranks as if the mere possession of a permit were a public shame. When the same newspaper hired armed guards, many gun owners heard the familiar message: protection for me, suspicion for thee.

To millions of Americans, a firearm is not a fetish or a political prop. It is a practical tool and a symbol of adult responsibility. Police cannot be everywhere, and in rural counties they may be many minutes away. Even in cities, a 911 call is not a shield. A woman threatened by an ex-husband, a shopkeeper closing late, an elderly couple in an isolated house: these people do not regard self-defense as an abstraction.

The Second Amendment matters here not because every gun owner imagines himself a revolutionary, but because constitutional rights set limits on what majorities may do in moments of panic. We would not repeal free speech because some speech is vile, nor abolish criminal procedure because some guilty people escape punishment. Rights are most necessary when fear tempts us to treat innocent people as hazards to be managed.

Gun-control advocates often say no one wants to take away ordinary people’s guns. But many proposals after each horrific shooting point in that direction: bans on broad categories of firearms, restrictions based on cosmetic features, registration systems that owners fear could become confiscation lists. One can call that fear exaggerated, but it is not irrational. History teaches that governments usually expand power by promising safety first.

None of this means the status quo is perfect. The background-check system should contain accurate records. Violent felons and adjudicated dangerous individuals should not have guns. Straw purchasers and gun traffickers should be prosecuted seriously. Schools should think soberly about security rather than pretending that signs and good intentions stop killers. Families, gun clubs and communities should teach safe storage and responsible handling.

But punishing the law-abiding because a murderer committed an atrocity is not moral seriousness. It is symbolism aimed at the easiest target. The criminal, the gang member and the deranged killer are not deterred by the fact that a suburban homeowner must give up a magazine or navigate another form.

What makes the debate burn so hot is that gun owners sense a deeper accusation: that they are dangerous, backward or morally defective for wanting the means to defend themselves. Until gun-control advocates stop treating ownership itself as a social problem, they should not be surprised by the fervor of the response.
