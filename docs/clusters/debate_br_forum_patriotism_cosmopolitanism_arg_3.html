"While cosmopolitan education is necessary for moral enlargement, it must be integrated with a sense of national responsibility and statecraft because political action and the enforcement of human rights still depend on the power of individual nation-states."

Martha Nussbaum’s essay is a morally bracing document, but it underestimates the stubborn fact that politics remains organized by states. We may be citizens of the world in conscience; we are not citizens of the world in law. That distinction matters.

The world of 1994 offers grim evidence. In Rwanda, hundreds of thousands were slaughtered while the international community hesitated, debated terminology, and avoided responsibility. In Bosnia, Europe and America have struggled to respond to ethnic cleansing without either committing fully or withdrawing honestly. In Somalia, humanitarian intervention became entangled with armed conflict and American casualties, producing a backlash that now shadows every proposal to act elsewhere. Haiti has just reminded us that even in our own hemisphere, the defense of democracy depends on power, diplomacy, and the willingness of particular governments to bear costs.

These cases do not refute Nussbaum’s cosmopolitanism. They show why it is necessary. If Americans thought of Rwandan lives as morally equal to American lives, our public debate might have been less complacent. If Europeans had internalized a deeper lesson about the dangers of ethnic nationalism, Bosnia might not have been allowed to fester so long. A world in which citizens learn only national pride will not respond adequately to famine, genocide, refugees, or ecological crisis.

But moral enlargement is not the same as political capacity. Nussbaum’s “citizen of the world” has no ballot to cast in a world government, no army accountable to a world parliament, no tax system for global redistribution, no police force reliably able to stop massacre. The United Nations can help, but it depends on member states. International law matters, but it is unevenly enforced. Human rights language is indispensable, but without institutions and power it can become a vocabulary of lament.

For this reason, the education we need is neither simple patriotism nor pure cosmopolitanism. It is education in responsible statecraft under universal moral constraint. Students should learn that foreigners have equal moral worth. They should also learn how treaties are made, why sovereignty arose, how alliances work, why intervention is difficult, how humanitarian aims can conflict with prudence, and why good intentions do not guarantee good results.

Nussbaum is strongest when discussing problems that obviously cross borders: pollution, population, hunger, and development. These require international cooperation, and Americans are poorly prepared to think about them. Our public debate still treats many global issues as optional acts of generosity rather than as matters of shared fate. Cosmopolitan education would correct that error.

Yet her suspicion of patriotism may deprive us of one of the motives needed to act. It is not humanity in general that sends peacekeepers, appropriates aid, admits refugees, or negotiates environmental agreements. Particular publics do so through particular governments. If citizens feel little attachment to their own political community, they may be even less willing to sacrifice for strangers. The challenge is to persuade Americans that their country should act decently in the world because justice requires it and because national honor is bound up with such decency.

National honor is a dangerous phrase, but not always a bad one. It can mean swagger, but it can also mean shame at betrayal. Americans should be ashamed if their government supports tyrants, ignores genocide, or consumes resources with no regard for the poor. That shame is patriotic as well as cosmopolitan: it says that this country, with its professed ideals, must not behave so.

The curriculum should therefore teach three concentric responsibilities. First, loyalty to constitutional democracy at home: without that, citizens cannot control the power exercised in their name. Second, serious knowledge of other societies: without that, foreign policy becomes ignorance armed. Third, commitment to universal human rights: without that, national interest becomes an excuse for brutality.

Nussbaum fears that patriotism leads to idolatry. Sometimes it does. But cosmopolitan sentiment without institutional discipline can lead to posturing, overreach, or despair. The task is not to choose between country and world. It is to build citizens who understand that their country is one actor in a morally common world—and who are prepared to make that actor behave responsibly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lack of global governing institutions means that 'world citizenship' remains a moral concept rather than a legal or political reality.
  2. 02Pure cosmopolitanism may lack the motivational force required to convince specific publics to sacrifice resources or take risks for international humanitarian causes.
  3. 03A healthy form of patriotism, rooted in national shame or honor, can serve as a powerful tool to hold one's own government accountable to universal moral standards.
  4. 04Effective education should focus on 'responsible statecraft,' teaching students how to navigate the practical complexities of sovereignty, treaties, and alliances to achieve moral ends.
  5. 05The curriculum should balance loyalty to domestic constitutional democracy with a serious knowledge of other societies and a commitment to universal human rights.
