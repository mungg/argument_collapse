"While experimentalist governance is a necessary corrective to failed global climate diplomacy, it will only succeed if it is backed by muscular public authority and a commitment to social and economic justice."

David Victor and Charles Sabel are right to start from a hard truth: the climate regime we have built is not adequate to the climate problem we face. The mismatch is not subtle. For thirty years diplomats have convened ever more elaborate meetings, communiqués, frameworks, protocols, pledges, and reviews, while atmospheric concentrations of greenhouse gases have continued to rise. If judged by emissions rather than by institutional activity, the record is close to failure.

Their proposed remedy—experimentalist governance—is therefore a welcome corrective to a stale debate. Climate politics has too often been framed as a choice between one grand global bargain and atomized national voluntarism. The former has proved impossible; the latter is too weak. Victor and Sabel ask us to look instead at how decarbonization actually happens: sector by sector, technology by technology, locality by locality, through iterative problem solving. This is a powerful insight. But it needs one addition. Experimentalism will not succeed unless it is joined to a much more muscular account of public power.

The strongest part of their essay is its insistence that uncertainty is not an excuse for inaction. In conventional climate diplomacy, uncertainty often functions as a brake. If governments do not know the cost of decarbonizing steel, cement, aviation, or electricity, they hesitate to commit. If they cannot predict how much voters will tolerate, they hedge. If they cannot know whether others will act, they wait. Victor and Sabel turn this logic around. Precisely because we do not know enough, we must act in ways that generate knowledge. Regulation, investment, and industrial collaboration should be designed as learning systems.

The Montreal Protocol is a useful model in this respect. It did not solve the ozone problem by discovering, in advance, the perfect substitute for every ozone-depleting chemical and then imposing a global mandate. It created a process in which firms, regulators, scientists, and users discovered which substitutes worked, adjusted schedules where needed, and then tightened rules as alternatives became feasible. The key was not simply agreement. It was organized learning under pressure.

That lesson applies directly to climate policy. No legislature can specify in 2021 the precise technical pathway for zero-carbon cement in 2040, the optimal configuration of a renewables-heavy grid in every region, or the best fuel for transoceanic shipping. But public institutions can create deadlines, standards, procurement markets, demonstration projects, and review mechanisms that force the search. They can identify successful experiments and turn them into broader requirements. They can distinguish genuine difficulty from bad-faith delay.

This is an especially important point for progressives. A Green New Deal, if it is to be more than a slogan, needs governance. It is not enough to call for trillions of dollars in investment, millions of jobs, and rapid decarbonization. Those goals are necessary, but they do not answer the questions that implementation will raise. Which technologies should be subsidized? Which communities should receive investment first? How should standards tighten over time? How should local opposition to infrastructure be handled? How should failing projects be corrected without becoming scandals that discredit the whole enterprise? Victor and Sabel are right that mandates and vision are not the same as institutions capable of learning.

Yet the essay risks understating the coercive and distributive dimensions of the climate transition. Experimentalist governance sounds consensual, pragmatic, and inclusive. In its best form, it is all three. But decarbonization is also a struggle over property, profits, employment, and power. The fossil fuel industry is not merely a laggard awaiting instruction. It is a political actor with enormous resources, deep ties to states, and a demonstrated willingness to obstruct, confuse, and delay. Utilities, automakers, airlines, cement producers, petrochemical companies, and agribusiness firms will not all become cooperative participants simply because sectoral forums invite them to innovate.

Montreal worked in part because the economic structure of the ozone problem was unusually favorable. The number of key chemicals and firms was relatively limited. Substitutes were plausible. Major producers could profit from transition. The public health symbolism of a damaged ozone layer was powerful. The climate problem is wider, deeper, and more embedded in everyday life. Fossil fuels are not a discrete chemical input in a few products; they are the energetic foundation of modern industrial society. Even a beautifully designed experimentalist regime will encounter actors whose rational strategy is to slow the process, capture the rules, or shift costs onto workers and consumers.

For that reason, experimentalism needs strong public authority behind it. The “penalty default” cannot remain vague. It must be made concrete through law, procurement, regulation, finance, and trade. Governments should set performance standards that become stricter over time: clean electricity standards, zero-emission vehicle requirements, methane rules, building codes, appliance standards, low-carbon fuel standards, and embodied-carbon requirements for public construction. They should use public purchasing to create early markets for green steel, low-carbon cement, electric buses, heat pumps, and grid technologies. They should finance demonstration projects but withdraw support from firms that fail to meet milestones. They should condition bailouts, permits, leases, and tax benefits on credible transition plans.

This does not contradict experimentalism; it makes it real. Learning without pressure becomes consultation. Participation without enforcement becomes delay. Public-private collaboration without anti-capture safeguards becomes industrial policy for incumbents. The genius of the California Air Resources Board, at its best, is not that it merely talks to automakers and technology suppliers. It regulates them. It creates a direction of travel and then adjusts in light of evidence. That combination—flexibility inside a hardening framework—is the heart of effective climate governance.

The same point applies internationally. Victor and Sabel are persuasive that Paris cannot itself run the sectoral experiments needed for deep decarbonization. But Paris remains more than a symbolic umbrella. Its temperature goals, reporting processes, and five-year cycles can help legitimate national and regional policies that would otherwise be attacked as protectionist or arbitrary. A country that adopts border adjustments on carbon-intensive goods, for example, will need to show that such measures are part of a good-faith climate strategy rather than disguised mercantilism. Sectoral clubs for steel, cement, shipping, aviation, and methane reduction will need some connection to a broader global framework if they are to avoid becoming rich-country cartels.

This is where public power must also be redistributive. The Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund is not a side detail; it is central. It recognized that technological feasibility does not automatically create administrative capacity or political consent in poorer countries. Climate policy will require that insight on a vastly larger scale. If rich countries want developing countries to build clean grids, industrialize without coal, protect forests, and adopt low-carbon infrastructure, finance cannot be an afterthought. It must be part of the institutional design. So must technology access, debt relief, and support for state capacity.

The domestic version of this problem is just as important. Coal miners, oil workers, refinery communities, rural households, renters in inefficient buildings, and low-income drivers cannot be told simply that experimentation will eventually produce cheaper technologies. Climate policy will be durable only if it visibly improves lives. That means wage standards, union jobs, transition assistance, public ownership where appropriate, affordable electricity, transit investment, and targeted support for communities dependent on fossil fuel revenues. A governance system that learns from firms but fails to learn from workers will not deserve the name democratic experimentalism.

Victor and Sabel invoke Dewey’s line that the person wearing the shoe knows where it pinches. That line should be taken seriously. Citizens are not merely sources of local information; they are holders of legitimate claims. If a transmission line crosses Indigenous land, if a carbon pipeline threatens a rural community, if a building retrofit raises rents, if a solar project disrupts local ecosystems, those affected are not obstacles to be managed. They are participants whose knowledge and consent shape whether climate action is just. Experimentalist institutions must therefore include not only firms, regulators, and technical experts, but labor unions, environmental justice organizations, Indigenous governments, consumer advocates, local officials, and public health representatives.

The real promise of experimentalist climate governance is that it can move us beyond the paralyzing search for a single master instrument. Carbon pricing has a role, but it is not sufficient. Global treaties have a role, but they are not sufficient. Industrial policy has a role, but it can fail. Protest has a role, but it must be connected to implementation. The transition requires a dense ecology of institutions that can set goals, fund innovation, discipline incumbents, share learning, and correct mistakes.

In that sense, Victor and Sabel offer not a technocratic alternative to climate politics but a better map of where climate politics will occur. It will occur in utility commissions, port authorities, procurement offices, building departments, financial regulators, trade negotiations, city councils, standards bodies, and sectoral alliances. These are not glamorous arenas, but they are where the carbon-intensive economy will either be dismantled and rebuilt or quietly reproduced.

The task now is to democratize and empower them. The Biden administration’s return to Paris will matter, but it will not be decisive. What matters more is whether the United States and other major economies can build institutions that combine ambition with correction, pressure with learning, and expertise with public accountability. Experimentalism is indispensable for that task. But it must be backed by law, money, and movements strong enough to defeat obstruction. Otherwise it will become one more elegant theory of climate action that underestimated the power of those profiting from the climate status quo.
