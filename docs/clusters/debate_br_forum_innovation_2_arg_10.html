Innovation in the Age of Climate Crisis

Reading Dan Breznitz's essay, I kept thinking about what is conspicuously almost absent: the climate crisis. He mentions decarbonization once, in passing, as an example of "fundamental system innovation." The rest of the essay treats the question of innovation strategy as if the relevant problem were inequality and regional decline, full stop. These are real problems and I do not minimize them. But any serious account of innovation policy in 2021 has to grapple with the fact that we have roughly a decade to reorient the global economy off fossil fuels, and that reorientation will swamp every other consideration about which "stage" of innovation a given community ought to specialize in.

Once you put climate at the center of the analysis, several of Breznitz's framings start to look incomplete. Consider his celebration of Taiwanese semiconductor fabrication and Pearl River Delta assembly as models of inclusive prosperity. Both of these clusters are extraordinarily energy- and water-intensive. TSMC's fabs consume a meaningful fraction of Taiwan's electricity and have been straining the island's water supply during recent droughts. Shenzhen's assembly economy depends on China's still-coal-heavy grid and on container shipping that is itself a major emitter. The "good jobs at multiple skill levels" Breznitz celebrates are embedded in production systems whose carbon footprint is currently incompatible with a 1.5- or even 2-degree pathway. A model of inclusive local prosperity that ignores its planetary footprint is not really a sustainable model.

This is not just a quibble about externalities. It is a claim that climate fundamentally reorders the strategic landscape Breznitz describes. The next two decades will see the largest reallocation of capital in human history as energy systems, transportation, buildings, agriculture, and industrial processes are rebuilt around low-carbon alternatives. Whichever localities and firms occupy strong positions in that reallocation will define the global economy of mid-century. Conversely, localities that double down on incumbent fossil-dependent specializations—however well-paid the jobs may be today—are setting themselves up to become the Cobalts of the 2040s.

So the most important question for innovation policy in 2021 is not stage one versus stage three. It is whether a community's chosen specialization is positioned for the decarbonization transition. A stage three component innovation hub for internal combustion engines is a dead end. A stage three component innovation hub for battery management systems, heat pumps, or electrolyzers is a once-in-a-century opportunity. A stage four assembly cluster for fast fashion is, climate-wise, a liability; a stage four assembly cluster for solar panels or grid-scale storage is a strategic asset.

Breznitz's framework can accommodate this point, but it does not lead with it, and I think that is a real weakness. The questions he asks—how to embed firms locally, how to build collaborative ecosystems, how to choose stage specializations—are downstream questions. The upstream question is: what should the next economy look like, and how do we navigate the transition from here to there? Innovation strategy without a substantive vision of what we are innovating toward becomes a kind of formalism.

There is also an opportunity here that the essay underplays. Breznitz mentions, almost in passing, that fundamental system innovation requires "the government stepping in as the innovative agent forcing significant changes in multiple sectors." Yes. Decarbonization is exactly such a project, and it is the rare innovation project that has both the moral urgency and the potential coalition support to motivate the kinds of state-led investment Taiwan and Korea made during their developmental periods. Climate policy is potentially the vehicle through which a serious American industrial policy could be built. The Biden administration's Build Back Better proposals, the infrastructure bill, and the climate provisions of various pending legislation gesture in this direction. If they pass in robust form, they could lay the foundation for exactly the kind of public-private R&D and procurement ecosystem Breznitz wants. If they don't pass, or pass in attenuated form, we will have missed a generational opportunity.

Climate also reframes the inequality question that animates Breznitz's essay. The clean energy transition is potentially one of the most powerful tools we have for building broad-based prosperity in regions left behind by globalization. Wind manufacturing, transmission construction, building retrofits, EV component production, hydrogen infrastructure, and so on are all labor-intensive activities that can be sited in places like the Midwestern manufacturing belt or the Appalachian coalfields. They produce union-wage jobs of exactly the kind that disappeared with deindustrialization. The "inclusive prosperity" Breznitz wants is most plausibly built around the climate transition, not in parallel to it.

There are real risks in this framing that I should acknowledge. Industrial policy that picks technology winners can fail expensively; Solyndra is the obligatory cautionary tale. Procurement-based industrial policy can be captured by incumbent suppliers. State-led decarbonization in democracies has to navigate political backlash from communities tied to fossil employment. These are not trivial obstacles, but they are obstacles to be managed, not reasons to default to the laissez-faire stance that has dominated American innovation policy for forty years.

If I were rewriting Breznitz's conclusion, I would say: communities should embrace a wider range of innovation strategies, yes, but they should select among them with an eye to the decarbonization transition. The taxonomy of stages is useful, but it needs to be paired with a substantive view of which technological systems are growing and which are dying. The Brenta of 2040 will not be the Brenta of 2020. The Pearl River Delta of 2040 will be a green-electronics hub or it will be in serious trouble. The Bay Area of 2040 will be more or less consequential depending on whether it pivots toward climate technology or remains dominated by ad-tech and consumer software.

Breznitz's essay is a useful corrective to Silicon Valley triumphalism. I would push it further: the question is not just which stage of innovation to specialize in, but which technological future to bet on. We have run out of time to treat that question as secondary.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A model of inclusive prosperity is unsustainable if it ignores the massive planetary footprint of energy- and water-intensive production hubs like those in Taiwan and the Pearl River Delta.
  2. 02The impending massive reallocation of capital toward green energy means that communities specializing in fossil-dependent industries risk becoming the next 'bust' towns.
  3. 03The choice of which innovation stage to pursue is secondary to whether that specialization aligns with the technological needs of decarbonization.
  4. 04The climate crisis provides the necessary moral and political urgency to justify the robust state-led industrial policy and public-private R&D ecosystems required for regional growth.
  5. 05The clean energy transition offers a unique opportunity to build broad-based prosperity and create labor-intensive, high-wage jobs in regions previously left behind by globalization.
  6. 06Innovation strategy must move beyond formalism to include a substantive vision of which technological systems are growing and which are dying in a warming world.
