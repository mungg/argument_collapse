Vanilla GPT

"Atheism is not a religion because it is a negative position on a single question rather than a comprehensive system of faith, though it can adopt religious social forms to satisfy human needs for community and ritual."

Atheism is not a religion in the ordinary sense, but it can become religious in some of its social forms. The distinction matters. To say there is no God, or that one lacks belief in God, is not the same kind of thing as saying the Nicene Creed, keeping kosher, praying five times a day or venerating ancestors. Atheism has no necessary scripture, clergy, ritual calendar, doctrine of salvation or commandment to gather weekly. It is a negative position on one question: whether gods exist.

But that does not mean atheists have no worldview, no moral commitments and no need to explain themselves. Susan Jacoby is right that in moments of tragedy atheism may offer something: not providence, perhaps, but honesty about randomness, solidarity without metaphysical consolation, and a refusal to say that suffering is part of a divine plan. Gary Gutting is also right that atheists should articulate their reasons. If a believer may be asked why faith is rational, an atheist may be asked why disbelief is rational — or, more precisely, why the available arguments and evidence do not justify belief.

The “atheist church” phenomenon in Britain shows both the attraction and the limit of the comparison. People do not go to church only because they believe propositions about God. They go for music, moral exhortation, child-rearing, mutual aid, rites of passage, memory, belonging and a sense that life should be lifted above shopping and television. A secular gathering with songs, reflection and community service can provide some of that. In a country where Anglicanism often functions more as heritage than conviction, it is not surprising that people would want the architecture of church without the theology.

Still, atheism alone cannot replace religion, because religion has always been more than an answer to the God question. Atheism can clear ground; it cannot by itself build a full moral or communal life. Humanism, liberalism, socialism, scientific naturalism, existentialism or civic republicanism may furnish richer accounts of meaning and obligation. An atheist may draw on any of these, just as a believer may draw on traditions outside formal doctrine.

Nor should critics label atheism a religion merely to score a legal or rhetorical point. If every comprehensive conviction is a religion, then democracy, capitalism and environmentalism are religions too, and the word loses usefulness. Better to say this: atheism is not a religion, but atheists are human beings, and human beings reliably make communities, rituals and moral languages. The interesting question is not whether atheism secretly is church, but whether secular people can create institutions that satisfy human needs without pretending to possess sacred certainty.

Vanilla Claude

"While atheism is fundamentally a philosophical position rather than a religion, organized atheist movements are increasingly adopting religious structures and rituals to fulfill essential social and emotional needs."

Atheism, strictly speaking, is no more a religion than abstaining from chess is a hobby. It is a position on a single question — whether any gods exist — and that's all the word, taken at its etymological root, can honestly be made to carry. A person who answers "no" shares that answer with Stalinists, Ayn Randians, Theravada Buddhists (on some readings), Richard Dawkins, and a great many people who simply never gave the matter much thought. No creed, no liturgy, no community follows from the bare denial.

But the question gains traction because atheism in public life is rarely just the bare denial. The "new atheism" of Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris and Dennett comes with characteristic texts, in-group vocabulary, conferences, moral commitments (often a robust liberal humanism), and a recognizable enemies list. Alain de Botton's "Religion for Atheists" and now Sanderson Jones's Sunday Assembly in London push the analogy further: songs, sermons, fellowship, a weekly gathering to feel uplifted among like-minded strangers. If you grew up Anglican and walked into one of these meetings, the family resemblance would be hard to miss.

That resemblance, though, is to the social scaffolding of religion, not to religion itself. Durkheim taught us long ago that churches do many things besides making metaphysical claims — they bind communities, mark time, console the grieving, school the young. Susan Jacoby's point about tragedy is precisely that secular people need those functions too, and have been ill-served by a movement that often confined itself to debunking. Building atheist congregations is an admission that the New Atheist polemic, by itself, leaves people cold in a hospital waiting room.

So the honest answer is twofold. As a proposition, atheism is not a religion; calling it one is usually a rhetorical move to neutralize it ("you have faith too!") and it should be resisted. As a movement and a way of life, organized atheism is increasingly borrowing religion's forms, and there is nothing embarrassing about that — Gutting is right that unbelievers owe themselves reasons, and Jacoby is right that they owe themselves rituals. Whether the result deserves the name "church" matters less than whether it actually consoles, educates, and binds.

  3. 03The emergence of atheist congregations demonstrates that secular individuals still require the social scaffolding of religion, such as community binding and consolation during tragedy.
  4. 04Labeling atheism as a religion is often a rhetorical tactic used by critics to falsely equate lack of belief with blind faith.
Vanilla DeepSeek

"Atheism is not a religion because it lacks belief in a supernatural reality or divine order, even if it occasionally adopts the social structures and community functions typically associated with religious life."

To ask whether atheism is a religion is to stumble over a definitional threshold that is more than semantic. Atheism, in its simplest and most widely accepted sense, is the absence of belief in a god or gods. Religion, by contrast, almost universally involves some form of belief in supernatural reality, a transcendent object of worship, and a framework of sacred narrative and ritual. By that standard, atheism fails the first and most essential test: it posits no deity, no divine order, no revealed truth. It is not a religion in any substantive sense. But the question persists because the visible culture of atheism in 2013 — from the rise of “atheist churches” in Britain to the arguments of public intellectuals like Susan Jacoby and Gary Gutting — increasingly borrows the outward forms and community functions of religious life, blurring the line for casual observers.

The Sunday Assembly, a London gathering launched this very month, illustrates the confusion. Its founders describe it as part atheist church, part secular celebration, with songs, inspirational talks, and a collective focus on living better. Here are people assembling for purpose and fellowship, rituals that mimic a worship service. Yet the content is entirely secular: no prayer, no supernatural claims, no sacred text. It is a social technology borrowed from religion, not a new faith. One might as well call a book club a religion because it meets regularly and discusses existential themes. The packaging does not determine the substance.

Jacoby, in her recent writings, argues that moments of tragedy remind us “of what atheism has to offer” — presumably, a stoic humanism that finds solace in shared fragility rather than divine plan. Gutting adds that atheists, like religious believers, ought to articulate reasons for their worldview, acknowledging that unbelief is a positive epistemological stance, not a mere void. Both are right to stress that atheism can be morally and intellectually robust. But neither claim transforms a philosophical position into a religion. A worldview that provides meaning and ethical guidance does not automatically become a religion; otherwise, Stoicism, Marxism, and even devoted fandom would qualify.

The deeper question — can atheism replace religion? — probes sociology, not theology. Religion historically supplies community, moral formation, rites of passage, and existential consolation. Atheism can replicate some of these by creating voluntary associations and humanist ceremonies, but it cannot replicate the numinous experience or the binding authority of a divine command. Whether it can satisfy the underlying human needs that religion addresses is an empirical question still unfolding. The rapid growth of nonbelief in Britain suggests that for many, the answer may be yes; for others, the hunger for transcendence endures. Atheism, then, is not a religion but a potential competitor in the marketplace of meaning — a distinction that keeps the debate both live and necessary.

  2. 02Providing a robust moral framework or intellectual worldview does not qualify a philosophy as a religion, as many secular ideologies offer similar ethical guidance.
  3. 03Atheism lacks the essential religious elements of numinous experience and the binding authority of divine command.
  4. 04While atheism may compete with religion to satisfy human needs for community and meaning, it remains a philosophical position based on the absence of belief in a deity.
Vanilla MiniMax

"Atheism is not inherently a religion because it lacks supernatural belief and formal structure, though it may adopt quasi-religious social functions to meet human needs for community and meaning."

The question of whether atheism constitutes a religion hinges on how we define the latter term. By traditional definitions, religion typically encompasses belief in supernatural forces, organized rituals, and moral frameworks grounded in transcendent authority. Atheism, understood simply as disbelief in gods, meets none of these criteria. It is, at its core, a negative answer to a single metaphysical question rather than a comprehensive system of belief and practice.

However, this answer requires nuance. When Susan Jacoby speaks of what atheism "has to offer," she gestures toward something beyond mere disbelief. New atheist movements have indeed exhibited quasi-religious tendencies—their charismatic leaders, their polemical tracts serving as scripture, and their calls for a rationalist moral framework. The emerging "atheist churches," including movements like those emerging in Britain, explicitly seek to provide the communal and ethical dimensions that secular life often lacks.

Gary Gutting is right that atheists bear an intellectual burden: articulating reasons for their positions. But this obligation differs fundamentally from religious apologetics. Atheism lacks scripture, clergy, sacraments, or ultimate commitments that characterize functional religions.

Can atheism replace religion? Perhaps in its propositional content—answering the god-question—but not as a total substitute for what religion historically provides: meaning-making narratives, communal solidarity, and rituals marking life's transitions. A thoughtful atheism, if it hopes to address human needs that religion has served, must develop these dimensions itself. Whether that makes it a religion depends less on theism than on what secular communities choose to build.

"Atheism is not a religion because it is defined by an absence of belief rather than a set of dogmas, though secular humanism can effectively replace the social and psychological functions religion once provided."

To call atheism a religion is to commit a profound category error. At its core, atheism is simply the absence of belief in deities; it dictates no dogma, requires no rituals, ordains no clerics, and reveres no holy texts. As the old adage goes, if atheism is a religion, then not collecting stamps is a hobby. However, the recent emergence of "atheist churches," such as the Sunday Assembly in London, highlights a crucial distinction: while atheism is not a religion, secularism can certainly replace the communal and psychological functions that religion has historically monopolized.

When Susan Jacoby points out what atheism has to offer in times of tragedy, or when contemporary thinkers argue for a secular reclamation of community, they are acknowledging basic human nature. We are social creatures who crave meaning, ethical frameworks, and solidarity. For centuries, traditional faith has bundled these deeply human needs with supernatural claims. As the "nones"—those unaffiliated with organized religion—rapidly become one of the fastest-growing demographics in both the United States and Britain, they are proving that this bundle can be unspooled. People can gather, reflect, support one another, and mourn without appealing to divine intervention.

Gary Gutting is right that atheists ought to articulate the reasons for their worldview. But that affirmative worldview is typically secular humanism, not merely "atheism." Atheism only tells you what a person does not believe; it leaves a blank slate. It is humanism, science, and secular philosophy that provide the positive values to live by. Atheism itself will never be a religion, but the vibrant, community-oriented secularism emerging today proves it is more than capable of replacing one.

"Atheism can be considered a religion because it often functions as a communal, moral, and identity-forming system that mirrors the practices and structures of traditional faiths."

Of course atheism can be a religion. The only way to deny it is to define religion so narrowly that large parts of actual religious life disappear from view. Many religious people do not spend most of their time reciting metaphysical propositions. They gather with others. They honor heroes. They tell stories about liberation from error. They teach children what is admirable and what is shameful. They divide the world into the enlightened and the benighted. They have rituals, festivals, slogans, saints and heretics.

By that functional standard, modern organized atheism often looks quite religious. The “New Atheist” movement has its canon: Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris and Dennett. It has conversion narratives, from childhood superstition to adult clarity. It has revival meetings in the form of conferences. It has missionary zeal, especially online. It has a moral drama in which religion is not merely mistaken but dangerous, childish or corrupt. And now, in Britain, it has something close to church: a gathering of unbelievers who want the communal benefits of religion without God.

This is not necessarily a criticism. Religion is not a dirty word. Human beings are ritual-making animals. We seek belonging and shared symbols. We want to know how to live and how to die. If atheists are discovering that a purely private skepticism is too thin to sustain a life, they are not being hypocritical. They are being human.

The real problem is that many atheists want the social power of religion while retaining the prestige of being “just rational.” They often present themselves as people who simply follow evidence, while believers are said to rely on faith. But atheism, especially when expanded into a confident worldview about meaning, morality and human destiny, depends on philosophical commitments that cannot be settled by laboratory experiment. The claim that there is no God is not the same as the claim that this or that miracle report is false. It is a sweeping judgment about reality.

So yes, atheism can replace religion for some people because it can become religious in practice: communal, moral, identity-forming and comprehensive. Whether that is good depends on whether atheist communities cultivate humility. A church without God can still have dogmatism, self-righteousness and excommunication. It can also have fellowship, service and courage. The absence of theology does not guarantee wisdom.

"While modest agnosticism is not a religion, comprehensive or 'thick' atheism functions as a religion because it provides a rival faith-based framework for explaining human existence, morality, and purpose."

Atheism is not usually called a religion by atheists, but the stronger forms of contemporary atheism deserve the label more than their adherents admit. A modest agnosticism—“I do not know whether God exists”—is not a religion. A private absence of belief is not a religion. But a comprehensive atheism that claims to explain what human beings are, what reason can know, what moral progress requires and why religion must be overcome is functioning as a rival faith.

The point is not that atheists are stupid or immoral. Many are neither. The point is that every serious view of life rests on assumptions that cannot themselves be proved by the methods of natural science. If one says that only material reality exists, that human dignity can be secured without reference to a creator, that moral obligation survives the death of God, and that death is simply extinction, one has said a great deal. These are not small conclusions. They shape conduct, education, politics and hope.

The atheist church in Britain is therefore revealing. It suggests that unbelief does not abolish the religious impulse; it redirects it. People still want sermons, even if they are called talks. They still want hymns, even if the lyrics are secular. They still want fellowship, moral aspiration and comfort in grief. The question is whether these goods can be sustained once detached from the truths that gave rise to them.

Christianity, Judaism, Islam and other traditions do not merely provide community. They make claims about reality: that the universe is not an accident, that persons have a worth deeper than social usefulness, that suffering is not the final word, that repentance and forgiveness answer something real in the human condition. Atheism can imitate some of the outward forms, but can it give an equally deep account of why human beings matter?

Susan Jacoby is right that atheists may offer courage after tragedy. There can be nobility in facing loss without consoling illusions. But religious consolation is not merely a narcotic. At its best it is a conviction that love and justice are grounded in something more enduring than our preferences.

So is atheism a religion? Thin atheism is not. Thick atheism often is. And if it hopes to replace religion, it should be judged not only by its criticisms of belief, but by the adequacy of the world it asks us to inhabit.

"Atheism is not a religion because it is simply the absence of belief in a deity rather than a comprehensive system of faith or metaphysical commitment."

Atheism is not a religion, and the question itself reveals more about the anxieties of believers than about atheism. To say someone "believes in no gods" is no more a religious position than to say someone "collects no stamps" is a hobby. Atheism is a single answer to a single question: Do you believe a deity exists? The answer is no. That is the entire content of the term.

The confusion arises because some atheists go further and adopt humanism, secularism, naturalism, or a particular ethical philosophy. These are belief systems, and one can argue about whether they function religiously. But they are not synonymous with atheism. There are libertarian atheists and Marxist atheists, Buddhist atheists and Ayn Rand atheists. The only thing they share is what they reject.

The recent emergence of an "atheist church" in London — Sanderson Jones and Pippa Evans's Sunday Assembly — is being treated as proof that atheism is becoming a religion. It proves nothing of the sort. It proves that human beings like singing together, hearing inspiring talks, and gathering on weekends. Churches did not invent community; they monopolized it for a long stretch of Western history. Reclaiming the form without the supernatural content is not a concession that atheism is a religion. It is the opposite: a demonstration that the social goods religion provides can be detached from the metaphysical claims people no longer find credible.

Gary Gutting is right that atheists should articulate reasons for their position, and most prominent atheists — Russell, Mackie, Sobel, more recently Philipse — have done so at considerable length. But articulating reasons does not make a worldview a religion. Scientists articulate reasons for accepting evolution; that does not make biology a faith.

The "atheism is just another religion" move is rhetorically convenient because it lets believers say, in effect, "we're all in the same boat." We are not. One boat is loaded with specific metaphysical commitments, sacred texts, rituals, and supernatural claims. The other simply declines to board.

The question “Is atheism a religion?” often functions less as an inquiry than as a trap. Some believers ask it in order to say, “You atheists have faith too.” Some atheists reject it because they hear an accusation of irrationality. Both reactions obscure the more useful issue: what parts of religion are worth preserving when belief in God fades?

In Britain, where established religion has lost much of its authority, the rise of an atheist church should not surprise anyone. A state church can decline faster as an object of belief than as a model of social architecture. The parish, the hymn, the sermon, the holiday calendar and the funeral are powerful technologies of belonging. They answer needs that do not depend on the truth of the Nicene Creed.

Alain de Botton has recently argued that atheists should be less embarrassed about borrowing from religion. He is right, up to a point. There is no reason a nonbeliever cannot value repetition, moral exhortation, beauty, silence, pilgrimage, confession or communal meals. The danger is not borrowing but shallowness: turning religion into a lifestyle boutique while ignoring the discipline and obligations that made it durable.

Atheism itself is only a negative proposition. It says no to God or gods. But most people cannot live by a “no.” They need practices and institutions that help them become patient, generous, brave and truthful. Religious traditions have often failed at this, sometimes catastrophically. They have also often succeeded. Secular people should be honest enough to admit both facts.

Can atheism replace religion? Not alone. But a humane secular culture can replace many of religion’s functions if it does not confuse liberation from dogma with liberation from dependence on others. In moments of tragedy, atheism offers no providential plan and no reunion in heaven. What it can offer is a severe kind of comfort: this life is not a rehearsal, the beloved are not instruments in a divine lesson, and our duty is to one another here and now.

That is not a religion. It is a starting point. What comes next—community, ethics, ritual, art, service—may look religious because religion has long been the name for our deepest attempts to make mortal life bearable.

"While atheism is not inherently a religion as a philosophical proposition, it increasingly functions as one in its organized forms through the adoption of communal rituals, sacred texts, and social structures."

The question "Is atheism a religion?" is usually posed as a gotcha, but it deserves a more honest answer than the standard atheist dismissal allows. The honest answer is: it depends on what we mean by religion, and increasingly, in practice, yes — at least in some of its organized forms.

Consider what we are watching. In London, atheists are now gathering on Sunday mornings to sing, listen to inspiring readings, hear a lecture meant to edify, and form community. Alain de Botton has proposed "temples for atheists." Sam Harris has written about contemplative practice and the value of meditation. American "freethought" groups hold conferences that look unmistakably like revivals, complete with charismatic speakers, sacred texts (Dawkins, Hitchens, Harris, Dennett), and a strong sense of in-group identity. Susan Jacoby explicitly speaks of what atheism "has to offer" in moments of grief — that is, she is positioning atheism as a source of consolation, which is one of religion's classic functions.

Scholars of religion have long noted that "religion" need not involve gods. Theravada Buddhism in many of its forms is functionally non-theistic. Confucianism is debatably a religion. Durkheim defined religion sociologically, by its function of binding communities through shared rituals and sacred symbols. By that definition, organized atheism in the New Atheist mode increasingly qualifies.

The more interesting question is whether this is a problem for atheists. I think it should be. Many atheists came to their position precisely because they were skeptical of tribalism, dogma, sacred texts, and charismatic authority. To recreate those structures around the negation of God is to reproduce the disease while changing the label. If atheism becomes a religion, it loses the very critical posture that justified it in the first place.

So my answer is: atheism as a bare proposition is not a religion, but atheism as a movement — with its assemblies, its scriptures, its evangelists, and its felt need to console the bereaved and instruct the young — is rapidly becoming one. Whether atheists embrace or resist that drift will determine what kind of cultural force atheism becomes in the coming decades.

By itself it has no scriptures, no sacraments, no clergy, no calendar of holy days, no account of salvation, no shared moral code and no settled view of politics, economics or even the meaning of life. An atheist may be a utilitarian, a conservative, a Marxist, a libertarian, a lover of ritual or a hater of institutions. The word tells us almost none of that.

This does not mean atheism is empty. It can be a profound liberation from fear, superstition and inherited authority. Susan Jacoby is right that moments of tragedy can reveal what unbelief offers: not a promise that everything happens for a reason, but the courage to say that some suffering is senseless and that our duty is therefore to one another, not to an invisible plan. That is not “nothing.” It is moral seriousness without cosmic consolation.

But the rise of an “atheist church” in Britain shows the limits of mere disbelief. People miss singing together, marking births and deaths, hearing moral reflection, eating in a hall with neighbors, and belonging to something larger than the private self. Those needs are not arguments for God, but neither are they satisfied by saying, “I don’t believe.” They require institutions.

So the better distinction is this: atheism is not a religion, but atheists may build religious-looking communities. Secular humanism, Ethical Culture, Unitarian fellowships and new atheist congregations can borrow forms that churches have refined over centuries. There is no shame in that. Religion has been a carrier of music, architecture, charity and public ritual; unbelievers need not pretend these goods are contaminated.

Gary Gutting is right that atheists should give reasons. But the reasons will not constitute a religion unless they harden into a comprehensive creed. Atheism can replace certain functions of religion; it cannot itself be religion’s replacement. It is the clearing of ground. What gets built there is a different question.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Atheism lacks the defining characteristics of religion, such as scriptures, clergy, shared moral codes, or settled views on the meaning of life.
  2. 02While atheism offers moral seriousness and liberation from superstition, it does not provide the communal and institutional benefits that religions traditionally offer.
  3. 03Atheists may adopt religious-looking forms and communities to satisfy human needs for ritual and belonging without those forms constituting a religion.
  4. 04Atheism serves as a rejection of belief rather than a comprehensive replacement for the multifaceted functions of religious institutions.

"Atheism is not a religion because it lacks the essential element of worship and response to a transcendent God that defines religious life."

The question “Can atheism replace religion?” assumes that religion is mainly a social service with metaphysics attached. For many believers, that is already a misunderstanding.

A church is not only a place to find community, music, consolation and moral exhortation. It is, at its heart, a response to God. If God is real, then religion cannot be replaced by a more cheerful Sunday meeting any more than a marriage can be replaced by a club that performs similar household functions. Worship is not reducible to fellowship.

This does not mean atheists are immoral or empty. They can face tragedy with dignity, and sometimes with a bracing honesty believers should respect. Nor should Christians, in Britain or America, take comfort in institutional decline by sneering at unbelief. The churches have often earned distrust through complacency, cruelty or irrelevance.

But an “atheist church” seems to me less like atheism becoming a religion than like a compliment paid to religion. It concedes that human beings need ritual, common memory, ethical teaching and communal obligations. The question is whether those practices can endure when severed from transcendence. Perhaps they can for a time; perhaps inherited moral capital will last longer than believers expect.

Still, atheism is not a religion. It is a denial of the premise on which theistic religion rests. It may borrow religion’s architecture, but it cannot replace its altar.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Religion is fundamentally a response to the reality of God rather than a mere social service or community gathering.
  2. 02Worship is a unique spiritual practice that cannot be reduced to secular fellowship or moral exhortation.
  3. 03The adoption of rituals and communal structures by atheists is a tribute to religious utility rather than a transformation of atheism into a faith.
  4. 04Atheism functions as a denial of theistic premises and cannot sustain the same sense of transcendence that an altar provides.

"Atheism can be considered a religion because it often functions sociologically and psychologically as a community of moral formation with its own sacred values and rituals."

Yes, atheism can be a religion — not always, but often enough that atheists should stop treating the comparison as an insult.

If religion is defined narrowly as belief in a supernatural being, atheism obviously fails the test. But that definition is too Christian and too thin. Religions are also communities of moral formation. They provide stories about the human condition, ceremonies for life’s transitions, authorities to quote, heretics to denounce, and a sense of what must be defended against profanation. Durkheim’s old insight still matters: religion is about the sacred, not merely about gods.

Many contemporary atheists have sacred things. They may not worship them, but they treat them as inviolable: reason, science, intellectual honesty, human autonomy, the natural world, freedom from priestly authority. There are atheist saints and anti-saints, even if the labels are denied: Darwin, Hume, Russell, Ingersoll, Dawkins, Hitchens; on the other side, creationists, inquisitors and clerical hypocrites. There are conversion narratives — “I was raised Catholic, then I read science and escaped” — and there are evangelists. There are conferences, books, badges, slogans and communities. The new “atheist church” only makes explicit what already existed in looser form.

This is not necessarily a criticism. Religious forms persist because they answer human needs. The desire to gather on a Sunday morning, sing, hear an exhortation and be reminded to live better is not irrational. It is human. If atheists can do that without teaching children to fear hell or accept miracles on authority, so much the better.

The danger is that atheists who imagine themselves immune to religion may become blind to their own dogmas. Any movement can reward conformity, caricature outsiders and mistake cleverness for wisdom. New Atheism at its worst has sometimes sounded less like free inquiry than like an anti-clerical revival meeting.

Calling atheism a religion should not be a legal trick to smuggle prayer into schools or deny unbelievers equal standing. But sociologically and psychologically, the boundary is porous. Some atheism is simply private disbelief. Organized, missionary, meaning-making atheism is much closer to religion than its adherents like to admit.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Religion should be defined by its focus on the sacred and communal moral formation rather than just belief in a supernatural being.
  2. 02Contemporary atheism possesses the hallmarks of religion, including conversion narratives, evangelism, authoritative figures, and inviolable values like reason and science.
  3. 03The emergence of atheist churches demonstrates that atheism can fulfill the same human needs for community and ritual that traditional religions address.
  4. 04Atheists who deny their religious structure risk becoming blind to their own dogmas and the tendency to reward conformity.
  5. 05While private disbelief is not religious, organized and missionary atheism operates as a meaning-making system that mirrors religious structures.
