Diversified Gemini

"The Occupy movement should be understood and developed as a global uprising against transnational financial capitalism rather than a purely American reformist project."

The opening statement from the Boston Review offers a stirring articulation of American ideals, but it suffers from a fatal limitation: an ingrained national myopia. By framing the Occupy movement exclusively within the context of "The United States," "our country's history," and American democratic principles, the editors fundamentally misapprehend the nature of both the crisis and the movement responding to it. Occupy Wall Street is not merely the latest chapter in American reformism; it is a localized node of a synchronized, global uprising against transnational financial capitalism.

To ask whether "our country" can build a movement to realize "our ideals" is to use a 20th-century map to navigate a 21st-century landscape. The economic forces that have crushed the American middle class, immiserated the poor, and concentrated wealth into the hands of the top 1% are not bounded by the Atlantic and Pacific oceans. The deregulation of finance, the race to the bottom in labor standards, the privatization of public goods, and the imposition of austerity are global phenomena orchestrated by international institutions like the IMF, the World Bank, and multinational banking conglomerates.

Consequently, the resistance is global. It is impossible to understand Occupy Wall Street without understanding that its DNA was forged in the Arab Spring. The occupation of Tahrir Square in Cairo demonstrated the power of reclaiming public space to challenge entrenched, seemingly invincible regimes. This tactic immediately jumped borders. Months before anyone pitched a tent in Zuccotti Park, the *Indignados* in Spain took over the Puerta del Sol in Madrid to protest austerity, youth unemployment, and the collusion between politicians and banks. In Greece, citizens violently clashed with police over crushing debt burdens imposed by the European Union.

Occupy Wall Street is the American translation of this global language. The young people in the United States holding signs about student debt and foreclosures have more in common materially and politically with the youth of Madrid, Athens, and Santiago than they do with the historical American figures of the abolitionist or suffrage movements. They are the first genuinely globalized generation, confronting a globalized oligarchy.

If we limit our vision to bringing American institutions into better alignment with American principles, we will fail. We cannot regulate a transnational financial network solely through the US Congress, nor can we address the looming, existential threat of global climate change—driven by the same imperative of endless corporate growth—on a purely national basis.

The future of the Occupy movement depends on its ability to strengthen its international solidarity. The "99%" is a global demographic. The movement must foster cross-border alliances among labor unions, environmental groups, and indigenous rights organizations. It must target the institutions of global governance, demanding transparency and democratic accountability not just in Washington, but in Davos, Brussels, and at the World Trade Organization. We must occupy the future, yes, but we must recognize that the future has no borders.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic forces causing inequality, such as financial deregulation and austerity, are global phenomena that transcend national borders.
  2. 02The tactics and political identity of Occupy Wall Street were directly inspired by and share more in common with international protests like the Arab Spring and the Spanish Indignados than with American historical movements.
  3. 03National policy solutions are insufficient to regulate a transnational financial network or address global crises like climate change.
  4. 04The movement's success depends on building international solidarity and targeting global institutions of governance rather than just domestic ones.
