"The proposed 'rolling-rule regime' of collaborative local experimentation is a dangerous detour that should be rejected in favor of a more robust, market-based system of tradable environmental rights and clear price signals."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are right about one thing: traditional command-and-control regulation is a cumbersome, information-starved dinosaur. They are also right that the bright hope of the 1990s—tradable emissions permits—has proven harder to implement than its proponents claimed. But their proposed alternative, a “rolling-rule regime” of collaborative local experimentation and centralized information pooling, is a dangerous detour. It replaces the overambitious state with an overambitious process, substituting endless meetings and negotiation for the clear price signals and secure property rights that we know from two centuries of economic history can actually coordinate decentralized action efficiently. The true path forward is not to navigate between command and markets with a messy hybrid, but to push more boldly toward a genuine market-based system: a comprehensive regime of tradable environmental rights backed by rigorous, independent enforcement.

The authors’ critique of tradable permits is instructive but ultimately superficial. They note that markets in pollution permits require regulators to define the commodity, allocate initial rights, and set trading rules—a daunting informational task. That is true, but it is equally true of any regulatory system. The rolling-rule regime also requires a center that sets minimum performance standards, defines acceptable metrics, and polices local reporting. The informational burden is simply shifted from the front end (defining the tradable commodity) to an ongoing process of synthesizing local experimentation. There is no escape from the need for central judgment. The difference is that a well-designed market harnesses the price mechanism to do the heavy lifting of coordination once the initial rules are set, while the rolling-rule model relies on continuous bureaucratic oversight and committee deliberation. History suggests that bureaucracies are far more susceptible to capture and inertia than markets. A market for sulfur dioxide emissions, once established, uses prices to allocate abatement effort to where it is cheapest, without the need for the EPA to pore over every utility’s internal processes or to decide which “best practice” should be replicated. That is precisely why the SO2 trading program under the 1990 Clean Air Act amendments has been so dramatically successful, achieving sharp reductions at a fraction of the predicted cost. It required an initial cap and allocation, yes, but once set, it worked.

The rolling-rule model, by contrast, is essentially a planned economy in environmental quality. It substitutes benchmarking and public comparison for the price system. The Toxics Release Inventory works by public shaming, a crude form of reputation signal. But reputation is a poor substitute for a price. It produces erratic, non-comparable incentives; a firm in a consumer-facing industry may feel intense pressure to reduce, while a chemical processor selling to other industrial firms may not. The result is an inefficient allocation of abatement effort. A properly designed toxics tax or a tradable toxics release permit would give every firm a uniform marginal incentive to reduce, driving reductions where they are cheapest. TURA’s planning requirement forces firms to engage in internal deliberation, which the authors celebrate. But that deliberation is costly, and its outputs—plans—are not transparent to the wider economy in the way a price is. A firm that innovates a new way to eliminate a toxic substance can sell its permits and profit from the innovation directly, without having to wait for a state institute to study it and promulgate it as a new best practice. The market diffuses innovation far more rapidly than any central pooling mechanism.

The authors’ hidden anti-market assumption is that the “information necessary to create artificial markets” is somehow uniquely difficult to amass. But they simultaneously argue that in the rolling-rule regime, the center can somehow amass enough information to periodically reformulate “minimum performance standards, desirable targets, and paths for moving from the former to the latter.” This is a heroic assumption. If regulators cannot determine the correct initial allocation of tradable water pollution rights because they lack knowledge of local circumstances and abatement costs, why would they be better able to determine, a few years later, what the new benchmarks should be after reviewing a grab-bag of local plans? The informational problem is identical. A market sidesteps this by letting the price emerge from decentralized trades, revealing the marginal cost of abatement in real time without any central authority ever needing to know it. The regulator need only adjust the overall cap over time—a task that requires scientific knowledge of environmental harm, not detailed knowledge of production technologies—and the market reallocates effort automatically. The rolling-rule regime, by contrast, requires the center to interpret and synthesize a vast, messy stream of self-reported local data, an task that invites political manipulation and is as vulnerable to the “immobilism” of central planning as any old Soviet industrial ministry.

The case of Habitat Conservation Plans is particularly ripe for market-based reform. Instead of forcing developers, scientists, and officials into a grueling, case-by-case negotiation over how many acres to dedicate and where, why not institute a system of tradable habitat conservation credits? A regional authority could establish a cap on total permissible habitat loss, then distribute credits to landowners, perhaps in proportion to current development plans. A developer who wanted to build on habitat-rich land would have to purchase credits from a landowner who restored or preserved equivalent habitat elsewhere. This would create a market that, over time, would direct conservation to the most ecologically valuable lands and development to the least sensitive areas, all through the price signal, without the endless, contentious stakeholder process the authors describe as a virtue. The current HCP process is, as the essay admits, plagued by secretive bilateral deals, uneven monitoring, and the absence of a system-wide learning mechanism. That is the hallmark of a regulatory vacuum, not a promising new architecture.

None of this is to deny that information disclosure and local participation have value. TRI is a useful supplement to a regulatory system, but it is not a regulatory system itself. The right to know is a necessary condition for both market and democratic accountability, but it cannot replace the clear, enforceable, and transferable property rights that make markets work, nor the hard caps on total pollution that give those markets environmental meaning. The tragedy of the current debate is that the perceived failures of early market-based proposals have led too many environmental thinkers to retreat into a woolly communitarianism of collaborative governance. The authors call for a synthesis of Jacksonian transparency and Progressive expertise. But the true synthesis lies in a market framework, where the Jacksonian ideal of open, transparent, impersonal exchange marries the Progressive commitment to science-based, enforceable goals. A graduated excise tax on toxics, a cap-and-trade system for nitrogen and phosphorus in the Chesapeake Bay watershed, a system of tradable development rights and habitat credits under the ESA—these are the bold next steps. They require us to face the information problem head-on, not to bury it under an avalanche of participatory process.

  2. 02Bureaucratic benchmarking and public shaming are inefficient substitutes for the uniform marginal incentives provided by taxes or tradable permits.
  3. 03The market diffuses innovation more rapidly and effectively than a central authority attempting to synthesize and promulgate 'best practices' from local data.
