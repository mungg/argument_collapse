"Lawrence Lessig’s proposal to remedy institutional corruption through increased speech regulation and public financing will exacerbate the problem of incumbent entrenchment and government capture rather than solving it."

**Lessig Diagnoses the Symptom and Prescribes More of the Disease**

There is a great deal in Lawrence Lessig's essay that ordinary Americans, including those of us who do not call ourselves liberals, would recognize. Yes, Congress is broken. Yes, lobbyists own too much of Washington. Yes, the institution is captured by interests that the people did not vote for and cannot easily dislodge. The Tea Party movement that has erupted across the country over the last eighteen months is in significant part a reaction to exactly the institutional decay Lessig describes. But the prescription that follows from his diagnosis—a vast expansion of federal regulatory power over political speech, coupled with new public-financing schemes that route billions of taxpayer dollars to political campaigns—will make the disease worse, not better.

Begin with a point Lessig glances over. He treats *Citizens United* as a catastrophic worsening of an already-bad system. But the system was bad in significant part because of the regulatory regime Lessig wants to extend. The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act did not clean up American politics. It pushed political activity into ever more obscure channels—527 organizations, issue-advocacy nonprofits, sham PACs, and the various creative workarounds that wealthy actors have always found when faced with restrictions on their political activity. The bipartisan name on the law was misleading; BCRA was a triumph of incumbent self-protection. It made it harder for outsiders to challenge sitting members of Congress in the weeks before elections, when challenges most matter. The Tea Party candidates who have won primaries against established Republicans in 2010 owe their opportunity, in part, to *Citizens United*'s loosening of the restrictions that previously protected incumbents.

This is not an academic point. McCain-Feingold was a law passed by incumbents, signed by an incumbent, and designed to entrench incumbents. The 60-day blackout period before elections was, transparently, a period during which incumbents wished to be insulated from independent criticism. The case that became *Citizens United* arose because a small nonprofit wanted to release a film critical of a presidential candidate. Lessig presents this as the entering wedge of corporate dominance. It is actually the entering wedge of citizen ability to organize against the political establishment. The same legal infrastructure that allows the Chamber of Commerce to spend on conservative candidates allows the SEIU, MoveOn, and labor unions to spend on liberal candidates. The neutrality is real, even if the symmetry of actual spending is not.

The deeper problem with Lessig's framework is its assumption that Congress, properly insulated from "dependencies," would do better. Better by what standard? Lessig drops hints throughout. He laments that Congress has not acted on global warming. He treats the Great Recession as evidence of corruption-driven policy failure. He sees the milk subsidies and sugar tariffs as outrageous (here he is correct). The implicit standard is that a Congress freed from improper dependencies would pass climate legislation, more financial regulation, and fewer producer-favoring subsidies. Maybe so. But maybe not. There are millions of Americans who oppose cap-and-trade legislation not because Exxon paid them to but because they accurately perceive that such legislation would raise their energy costs in a difficult economy. There are millions of Americans who are skeptical of further financial regulation, having watched the existing financial regulatory apparatus utterly fail to prevent the crisis and then bail out the very institutions that caused it. Lessig's framework cannot accommodate the possibility that the policies he favors are unpopular on the merits. It must reach for corruption as an explanation.

This pattern—treating policy disagreement as evidence of corruption—is one of the unhealthier tendencies in contemporary American politics, and Lessig's essay, for all its sophistication, exemplifies it. When you cannot persuade your fellow citizens that climate legislation or financial regulation is in their interest, the temptation is to explain their resistance as the product of manipulation by malign interests. The corrupted-democracy frame thus becomes a way of avoiding the harder work of building genuine majoritarian support. Worse, it becomes a justification for regulatory regimes that suppress the political speech of the actors—corporations, advocacy groups, citizens-united-against-the-political-class—who are inconveniently effective at giving voice to the dissenting majority.

The voucher system Lessig endorses is a particularly clear case. Ackerman-Ayres democracy vouchers would, by his own estimate, pump roughly $7 billion of taxpayer money into political campaigns per cycle. Set aside the question of whether voters want their tax dollars spent funding politicians at all (polls suggest they emphatically do not). Set aside the question of how vouchers would interact with the inevitable workarounds. Consider the simpler point: the proposal asks Americans to fund the campaigns of candidates they disagree with as a remedy for a corruption problem that the proposal does not actually solve. Independent expenditures would continue. Wealthy donors would continue to organize. The donor class would adapt, as it always has. The only certain effect would be the transfer of billions in taxpayer money to political consultants, ad buyers, and incumbents.

A genuinely populist response to institutional corruption would look very different from Lessig's. It would focus on shrinking the targets that money can buy: a Congress that does less, regulates less, taxes less, and exempts less has fewer rent-seeking opportunities for lobbyists. It would push for radical transparency without expenditure caps: let every dollar be tracked and disclosed, and let voters make their own judgments. It would address the revolving door through tough cooling-off periods. It would shrink the administrative state's discretionary authority, which is the real prize that lobbyists pursue. And it would resist the temptation to ban speech in the name of cleansing democracy, because the cure of speech bans has, throughout American history, been wielded most effectively by the very establishments that need challenging.

Lessig is right about the disease. The diagnosis of dependency corruption captures something real. But the cure he prescribes—more regulation of speech, more public funding of politicians, more federal control over the political conversation—will worsen the very capture it claims to remedy. The remedy for a captured Congress is not to give Congress more power to regulate its critics. It is to reduce the stakes of capture by limiting what Congress can do. That is a lesson the framers understood, and one we would do well to remember.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Campaign finance regulations like the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act often serve as tools for incumbent self-protection by insulating sitting politicians from independent criticism.
  2. 02The 'dependency corruption' framework incorrectly assumes that policy disagreements on issues like climate change or financial regulation are the result of corruption rather than legitimate public opposition.
  3. 03Restricting corporate or group expenditures suppresses the ability of citizens to organize effectively against the political establishment.
  4. 04Public financing schemes, such as democracy vouchers, unconstitutionally force taxpayers to fund candidates they oppose while failing to stop wealthy donors from finding new workarounds.
  5. 05The most effective way to reduce corruption is to shrink the size and discretionary power of the federal government, thereby reducing the 'rent-seeking' opportunities that lobbyists and special interests can buy.
  6. 06True reform should prioritize radical transparency and the reduction of the administrative state's authority over the suppression of political speech.
