"While the lead essay correctly identifies the limitations of moderation as a political strategy, its empirical claims against moderation and its proposed alternatives lack the necessary caution and humility required for such a complex, high-noise political environment."

Bonica and Grumbach offer a welcome antidote to overconfident punditry, but their empirical claims should be read with more caution than their rhetoric allows. The case for moderation is often sloppy; the case against moderation can become sloppy in the opposite direction. Candidate ideology may have smaller effects than it once did, and nationalization certainly constrains local differentiation. But “no consistent, measurable effect” is not the same as “no meaningful effect,” and the leap from congressional research designs to presidential strategy is perilous.

Start with close-primary designs. Comparing general-election outcomes after narrowly decided primaries is one of the better tools political scientists have for estimating causal effects. If a moderate barely beats a progressive, and a progressive barely beats a moderate elsewhere, the winners may be treated as almost randomly assigned. That is elegant. But the design estimates a local effect: the effect of nominating the kind of moderate or progressive who appears in a close primary, in the kinds of districts where such primaries occur, during the periods studied. It does not necessarily tell us what would happen if a national party became strongly associated with positions voters dislike, or if presidential candidates adopted highly salient stances on immigration, crime, or cultural issues.

Measurement is also difficult. Ideology is not one thing. Roll-call votes, donor networks, campaign websites, interest-group endorsements, expert ratings, and voter perceptions each capture different dimensions. A candidate may be economically left and culturally moderate, or vice versa. A candidate may hold progressive positions but communicate them in nonthreatening language. Another may be moderate on paper but symbolically tied to unpopular national groups. If ideology is measured imprecisely, estimated effects will shrink. The absence of large measurable effects may reflect both a real decline and the limits of our instruments.

The authors criticize Split Ticket’s WAR model for opacity and excessive certainty. Fair enough. Proprietary political metrics should not be treated as scientific authority. But the same humility should apply to academic models. Transparent uncertainty is better than hidden assumptions, but it remains uncertainty. Elections are low-N, high-noise events influenced by incumbency, fundraising, district partisanship, national mood, candidate quality, scandals, turnout, advertising, local media, and random shocks. The effect of moderation may be small relative to these factors and still decisive in close races.

Consider the statement that of twenty-two Democratic incumbents who lost competitive seats between 2016 and 2024, twenty-one were moderates. This sounds damning, but it may mostly reflect selection. Democrats tend to nominate moderates in the most competitive or Republican-leaning districts. Progressives more often represent safer blue seats. If the battlefield is populated by moderates, most casualties will be moderates. The authors know this and offer more sophisticated evidence elsewhere, but rhetorically the statistic invites a misleading inference.

Likewise, the claim that moderation “hit its ceiling” in 2024 may be true in House races where Democrats already nominated moderates. But presidential campaigns are different. A national nominee is not merely one candidate among many; she defines the party brand. Harris’s 2024 campaign did attempt moderation, but voters evaluated her through years of prior party positioning, the Biden administration’s record, inflation, immigration pressures, foreign crises, and her own 2019 primary statements. A late pivot to kitchen-table rhetoric does not constitute a clean test of popularism. If voters had already concluded that Democrats were too far left or too culturally alien, last-minute reassurance may have lacked credibility.

Bonica and Grumbach’s strongest theoretical point is that voters are not fixed preference bundles. Leaders shape opinion. But this cuts both ways. If politicians influence voters, then unpopular positions taken by prominent Democrats can also shape the party brand negatively. Activist language can be amplified by opponents and become a heuristic for what the party represents. Popularists are not wrong to worry about this. They may overestimate the ease with which Democrats can avoid such associations, but they are identifying a real mechanism in a nationalized media environment.

The authors are right that authenticity matters. Strategic repositioning can look fake. Yet authenticity and moderation are not opposites. Some politicians are authentically moderate. Many voters are not asking for ideological daring; they are asking for competence, stability, and respect for their values. In a polarized era, a candidate who sincerely breaks with her party on a salient issue may signal independence in a way voters understand. The fact that many such efforts fail does not mean none can work.

Where the essay is most persuasive is in attacking the universal claim “Moderation = Democratic votes.” There is no reason to believe every Democrat everywhere would benefit from moving rightward. There is even less reason to believe that sacrificing base enthusiasm for marginal persuasion is always wise. Turnout effects matter, and progressive candidates may mobilize some Democratic voters more effectively. But a serious strategy should ask when mobilization gains exceed persuasion losses, and among whom. The answer likely varies by race, office, issue, and year.

The anti-corruption proposal illustrates the complexity. Anti-corruption can be left, right, or neither. A Democrat can use it to attack billionaires, corporate PACs, Supreme Court gifts, and congressional stock trading. A demagogue can use it to attack civil servants, journalists, universities, and “globalists.” Its electoral power depends on credibility, specificity, and coalition management. Polls showing high concern about corruption are useful, but broad agreement at the level of abstraction does not settle the politics of remedies. Voters may support banning stock trades but divide over campaign finance, court reform, antitrust, or public financing.

Similarly, turnout is vital but not straightforward. The essay notes that nonvoters in 2024 likely leaned slightly toward Trump. That complicates the idea that mobilization automatically helps Democrats. The composition of nonvoters changes. Low-propensity young people may be progressive on some issues and alienated on others. Low-propensity working-class men may be attracted to authoritarian populism. Mobilizing “the disengaged” without persuading them could produce unpleasant surprises. Democrats need turnout operations linked to political education and community organizing, not simply higher participation as an abstract good.

A more modest and stronger version of the lead essay would say: moderation is not a reliable master strategy; its effects are smaller and more context-dependent than pundits claim; Democrats should experiment with bolder anti-corruption and mobilization strategies; and analysts should stop treating opaque models or simplistic comparisons as decisive. That argument is hard to dispute. The essay sometimes goes further, implying that the old rules are dead and a new mobilizational politics can replace them. I would be careful. In politics, old rules rarely die completely. They decay, mutate, and reappear under new conditions.

The median voter theorem in its textbook form is too simple. But proximity to voter concerns still matters. Candidate quality still matters. Issue salience still matters. Party reputation still matters. A position that is perceived as extreme on a highly salient issue can hurt, even if congressional ideology scores show weak average effects. Conversely, a bold position on a low-salience or cross-cutting issue may help by signaling conviction. The challenge is not to abandon moderation or embrace progressivism; it is to understand multidimensional voter perception in a polarized, low-trust environment.

Bonica and Grumbach have done a service by demanding better evidence. Their critics should answer with transparent models, not slogans. But evidence should make everyone humbler, including those of us skeptical of moderation. The Democratic Party faces a strategic environment so noisy that sweeping claims are suspect. It should run experiments, support varied candidates, measure turnout and persuasion carefully, and learn without turning every result into factional ammunition.

Authoritarianism raises the stakes, but high stakes do not eliminate uncertainty. Democrats need moral clarity about democracy and corruption. They also need empirical modesty. The worst strategic error would be to replace one dogma—move to the center—with another dogma that treats mobilization as a cure-all. The data do not justify either certainty. They justify disciplined experimentation under conditions of danger.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The research designs used to dismiss the effects of moderation, such as close-primary studies, estimate local effects that may not apply to national presidential strategies or highly salient cultural issues.
  2. 02Ideology is a multidimensional and difficult-to-measure construct, meaning that the absence of large measurable effects may reflect the limits of analytical instruments rather than the total disappearance of a moderation advantage.
  3. 03The statistic that most losing Democratic incumbents were moderates is likely a result of selection bias, as moderates are more frequently nominated in the most competitive and difficult-to-win districts.
  4. 04The authors' focus on mobilization as a solution ignores the risk that nonvoters and disengaged citizens may not lean progressive and could potentially favor authoritarian populism if mobilized.
  5. 05The proposal to use anti-corruption as a unifying frame overlooks the fact that voters are deeply divided on specific remedies, and such rhetoric can be co-opted by demagogues for anti-democratic ends.
  6. 06Rather than replacing the dogma of moderation with a new dogma of mobilization, the Democratic party should adopt a strategy of disciplined experimentation and empirical modesty.
