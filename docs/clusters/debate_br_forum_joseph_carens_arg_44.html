"While the passage of time creates a moral claim to stay, a successful amnesty policy must move beyond abstract time-based membership to include specific institutional designs for labor market integration and civic requirements."

Joseph Carens grounds his argument for amnesty in a universal liberal democratic morality, but he also wisely draws on European experience to show that alternatives to perpetual deportation are possible. Yet he pays too little attention to the specific design of European regularization programs and the lessons they offer for making amnesty work. A close examination of countries like Spain, Italy, and France reveals that successful legalizations have been those that combine a path to residence with robust integration requirements and a commitment to labor market regulation. If the United States is to adopt Carens’s recommendation, we would do well to learn from these examples and not simply assume that the passage of time alone can confer full membership.

Europe has a long history of what are sometimes called “regularization processes,” ranging from large-scale amnesties to ongoing individual procedures. Spain, for instance, conducted several major regularizations in the 1990s and 2000s, most recently in 2005, which legalized over 700,000 people. A key feature of the Spanish model was its linkage to employment: applicants had to prove that they had a work contract or an offer of employment. This emphasized that regularization was not just about forgiving a past violation but about integrating the migrant into the formal labor market, with all the rights and responsibilities that entails. The Spanish approach also required registration with the municipal authorities, which gave local governments a role in attesting to the migrant’s social roots.

Italy, too, has used regularizations tied to domestic work and caregiving, recognizing that many irregular migrants are employed in households. These sector-specific amnesties have acknowledged the reality that Italian families depend on undocumented workers for child and elder care. By making regularization conditional on documented employment, Italy sought to bring these relationships out of the shadows and ensure basic labor protections. The results have been mixed, but the principle is valuable: amnesty should be part of a broader strategy to regulate the labor market, not an isolated act of forgiveness.

France’s approach, which Carens mentions, long included a ten-year residence card that created an entitlement to stay, but even that was not purely time-based. Applicants had to demonstrate continuous residence, but they also had to show integration into French culture and society—for instance, through language proficiency and absence of a serious criminal record. The 2003 reform that made the process more discretionary was indeed a retreat, but it highlights a political reality: blanket, unconditional amnesties are difficult to sustain in democracies because they are perceived as rewarding illegal behavior without demanding anything in return. The move toward “integration contracts” in several European countries reflects a public insistence that legal status should come with reciprocal obligations.

Carens is suspicious of administrative discretion and favors automatic rules. His concern about discrimination is legitimate, but Europe’s experience shows that well-designed criteria need not be arbitrary. A system that considers employment, language ability, community ties, and clean criminal record can be structured with clear, transparent standards and an appeals process. This is not the hopelessly subjective inquiry Carens fears; it is the kind of assessment that immigration authorities already make for naturalization applicants. Why should the threshold for permanent residency be lower than for citizenship? A reasonable path would be to grant a probationary legal status after a certain period of residence, convertible to permanent status upon fulfillment of integration conditions.

The United States can also learn from Europe’s use of collective bargaining and social partnership. In Spain, regularization rounds were often negotiated with unions and employer associations, ensuring that the newly legalized workers were brought into the formal social security system and that employers faced sanctions for hiring undocumented workers going forward. This approach built a stakeholder coalition in favor of enforcement, rather than pitting business against labor. The U.S. Congress could similarly involve the AFL-CIO, the Chamber of Commerce, and immigrant advocacy groups in designing both the amnesty criteria and the accompanying workplace enforcement regime. Such a deal would be harder to undo than a top-down executive or judicial mandate.

Carens’s essay is an essential moral starting point. But to translate his moral insight into durable law, we must move beyond the abstract idea of time-as-membership and engage with the institutional design of integration. Europe’s mixed record shows that amnesty can work, but only if it is embedded in a broader framework of labor regulation, civic integration, and political consensus. Let us not just grant amnesty; let us build the institutions that make amnesty the beginning of full and equal membership, not the end.
