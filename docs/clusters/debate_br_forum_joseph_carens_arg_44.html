Diversified Gemini

"Joseph Carens’s proposal for amnesty is a morally provincial approach that ignores the devastating impact of human capital extraction on the Global South and allows wealthy nations to evade their responsibilities for global economic justice."

Joseph Carens’s argument for amnesty is a well-intentioned exercise in Northern moral provincialism. By focusing exclusively on the dynamics between irregular migrants and the liberal democratic states of the Global North where they settle, Carens completely obscures the broader global inequalities that drive migration and the devastating impact of this migration on the countries of the Global South.

Carens treats the migrant as an isolated moral agent whose story begins the moment they cross the border into the United States or Europe. But Miguel Sanchez did not materialize out of thin air; he left a community, a local economy, and a developing nation. When liberal democracies in the North implement policies that absorb the most ambitious, able-bodied, and resourceful individuals from the developing world, they are participating in a massive extraction of human capital.

While much is made of the remittances sent back by migrants—and indeed, Carens mentions Miguel sending money to his father—this financial flow cannot compensate for the structural "brain drain" and "brawn drain" that cripples developing economies. When a country loses its young men and women to the construction sites and restaurants of Chicago and London, it loses the very workforce needed to build local infrastructure, demand better governance, and develop a sustainable domestic economy.

Carens’s proposal for a guaranteed amnesty after five to seven years would institutionalize and accelerate this extraction. If it becomes a known, codified fact that making it to the US or EU guarantees permanent residency after a few years, the incentive for the brightest and most capable to leave their home countries will become irresistible. We are essentially proposing to use the Global South as a nursery, extracting workers only once they reach their productive prime, while leaving the sending countries to bear the costs of their upbringing and the burden of the elderly left behind.

Furthermore, Carens’s focus on integrating irregular migrants into the wealthy societies of the North allows those societies to evade their responsibilities for global justice. The Northern demand for cheap labor, coupled with structural adjustment programs and unfair trade policies that destroy livelihoods in the South, creates the conditions for irregular migration. Amnesty serves as a convenient pressure valve. It allows the North to secure its cheap labor force, soothe its liberal conscience by eventually granting them rights, and entirely ignore the imperative to reform the global economic order so that people do not have to leave their homes in the first place.

Instead of focusing on how to integrate the few who manage to navigate the smuggler routes and survive the journey, we should be asking how to make migration a choice rather than a necessity. True moral policy would focus on massive wealth transfers, fair trade, and debt relief for the Global South, aiming to build prosperity there. Carens’s amnesty is merely a localized band-aid that inadvertently solidifies a parasitic relationship between the wealthy North and the impoverished South.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The migration of ambitious and resourceful individuals constitutes a 'brain drain' and 'brawn drain' that cripples the development of local economies and infrastructure in the Global South.
  2. 02Institutionalizing amnesty after a fixed period would create an irresistible incentive for the most capable citizens of developing nations to leave, effectively treating the Global South as a nursery for Northern labor.
  3. 03Focusing on the integration of migrants into Northern societies serves as a convenient pressure valve that allows liberal democracies to ignore the structural inequalities and unfair trade policies they impose on the South.
  4. 04Amnesty acts as a localized band-aid that fails to address the moral imperative of making migration a choice rather than a necessity through wealth transfers and debt relief.
