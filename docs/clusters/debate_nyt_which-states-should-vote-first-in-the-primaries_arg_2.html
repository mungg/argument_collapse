"Iowa and New Hampshire should remain the first states to vote because their small scale forces candidates to engage in face-to-face deliberation rather than relying solely on media spending and consultants."

Iowa and New Hampshire are easy targets, but they perform an important democratic function that more “representative” states may not. They slow politics down. In an era when campaigns are dominated by money, consultants, cable television and super PACs, the first contests still require candidates to meet ordinary voters face to face. That is not nostalgia; it is a real institutional check on packaged politics.

The critique that these states are too white is fair. But it is not obvious that the remedy is to replace them with large diverse states. Put California or Florida first and presidential nominations become even more dependent on fundraising and media buys. Lesser-known candidates would have little chance to break through. Voters would not get more democracy; they would get more advertising.

Iowa’s caucuses are admittedly demanding and imperfect. They privilege activists who can attend at a specific time. New Hampshire’s primary is more accessible, and its voters have a reputation for rewarding candidates who answer questions rather than recite talking points. Both states also take their responsibilities seriously. Turnout, press scrutiny and local knowledge matter. There is something to be said for electorates that understand they are not merely casting ballots but interviewing presidents.

The solution should be modest. Iowa and New Hampshire can remain first, but they should be immediately followed by states that broaden the test. South Carolina and Nevada already help do that. The parties should strengthen that four-state sequence and prevent any candidate from being treated as inevitable until all four have voted. Media organizations and donors bear responsibility here: they should stop writing obituaries after one caucus or one primary.

Reform should also focus on participation. Iowa should consider moving from a caucus to a primary, or at least making absentee participation easier. New Hampshire should remain open to independents. Nevada should improve caucus administration. The calendar can become fairer without destroying the virtues that small early states provide.

Representation matters, but so does deliberation. The presidential selection process needs both. Iowa and New Hampshire should not be the whole gate, but they remain useful hinges on which a more balanced system can turn.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small early states act as an institutional check on 'packaged politics' by allowing lesser-known candidates to compete without massive fundraising or advertising budgets.
  2. 02Replacing small states with large, diverse states like California or Florida would make the nomination process even more dependent on super PACs and television ads.
  3. 03The voters in Iowa and New Hampshire possess a unique culture of civic responsibility and local knowledge that treats the primary process as a rigorous interview of the candidates.
  4. 04The lack of diversity in the first two states can be mitigated by immediately following them with a diverse sequence of states like South Carolina and Nevada to broaden the electoral test.
  5. 05The primary system can be improved by reforming participation rules, such as moving from caucuses to primaries, without abandoning the benefits of small-state retail politics.
Diversified Claude

"The current primary system starting with Iowa and New Hampshire should be maintained because it functions better than the likely realistic alternatives."

A modest defense of the indefensible: the current system works better than its replacements would.

I say this as someone who finds the demographic critique persuasive on its face. Iowa is whiter than the country. New Hampshire is whiter still. Both are rural in ways that distort the policy conversation. If you were designing a presidential nominating system from scratch, you would not put them first. Probably you would not put them in the early window at all.

But political institutions are rarely redesigned from scratch, and the relevant comparison is not Iowa-and-New-Hampshire versus an ideal alternative but Iowa-and-New-Hampshire versus what we would actually get if we changed the rules. What we would actually get, I suspect, is one of three things: a national primary dominated by money and name recognition; a series of regional primaries that empower whichever region goes first and turn that region's parochial interests into the new ethanol subsidy; or a perpetual calendar war as states leapfrog one another into December and November.

Iowa's and New Hampshire's outsized influence is real but bounded. Iowa Republicans have picked Huckabee, Santorum, and Cruz in the last three contests, none of whom won the nomination. New Hampshire picked McCain in 2000 (he lost) and Buchanan in 1996 (he lost). The early states amplify and winnow; they do not anoint. The party as a whole, voting over months, still chooses.

Meanwhile, the demographic critique cuts both ways. Yes, the early states underrepresent nonwhite voters. But they also overrepresent the kind of working-class, less-college-educated white voters who have been drifting away from the Democratic Party and who Republicans need to hold. There is a case—not a compelling one, but a case—that this corrective is useful.

If the parties can negotiate a sensible rotation that preserves the small-state, retail-politics character of the early window while sharing the privilege more broadly, I would support it. But the burden of proof is on the reformers to explain what they would build, not just what they would tear down.

  3. 03The demographic makeup of Iowa and New Hampshire provides a useful focus on working-class white voters who are politically pivotal for both parties.
  4. 04The current system preserves a small-state, retail-politics character that would be lost in a more centralized or large-scale primary format.

"Small states should continue to vote first in the primary process to ensure that retail politics and face-to-face campaigning remain more influential than money and media saturation."

I grew up in New Hampshire, and I will defend its primary, though not on the usual grounds.

The argument I hear from defenders—that we are uniquely civic-minded, that we take the responsibility seriously, that candidates get a fair hearing—is partly self-flattery. New Hampshire voters are not wiser than Ohioans or Georgians. We are just more practiced. Anyone who has been asked their views on Medicare by three presidential candidates in a single February becomes a more informed voter than someone whose primary is decided before their state even votes. That is an artifact of the calendar, not a New Hampshire virtue.

But here is the honest case for going first: the alternative is worse. A national primary, or even a series of large-state primaries up front, would mean campaigns waged almost exclusively through television, social media, and direct mail. There would be no place where a long-shot candidate—a Jimmy Carter in 1976, a Gary Hart in 1984, a Barack Obama in 2008—could outwork the front-runner by knocking on doors and showing up at house parties. Money would matter more, not less. Celebrity would matter more. Substance, where it still exists, would matter less.

Iowa and New Hampshire are demographically unrepresentative. So is every individual state. The relevant question is whether the early window as a whole—Iowa, New Hampshire, Nevada, South Carolina—exposes candidates to a meaningful slice of the party coalition before the nomination is effectively decided. The answer is yes, imperfectly.

If reformers want to rotate the small states going first, I am open to it. Maine could do what New Hampshire does. So could Vermont, Delaware, or West Virginia. The principle worth defending is not New Hampshire's special status but the principle that small states should kick off the calendar, so that face-to-face campaigning still matters. Replace us if you must. Just don't replace the model.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Small-state primaries provide a necessary platform for long-shot candidates to gain momentum through grassroots organizing and personal contact with voters.
  3. 03While individual states like New Hampshire are demographically unrepresentative, the collective early window of small states provides a meaningful cross-section of the party coalition.
  4. 04The specific identity of the first-voting state is less important than maintaining a model where small states allow for substance-based, retail campaigning.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Iowa and New Hampshire should retain their first-in-the-nation status because their small-scale, retail-style politics provide a unique and necessary test of a candidate's character and viability."

Iowa and New Hampshire are often derided as pale, rural anachronisms that distort our presidential primaries. Yet this criticism misses the profound democratic value embedded in their first-in-the-nation status. These states do not serve as a demographic mirror of America; they serve as a crucible. The intimacy of their contests forces candidates to submit to relentless, face-to-face scrutiny in living rooms, diners, and town halls. There is no place to hide from an undecided voter who has shaken your hand three times and still has questions. This process tests not only a candidate’s message but also their character, stamina, and ability to build grassroots organization—skills that translate directly to a general election.

History is replete with underdogs who used this terrain to prove their mettle. Jimmy Carter, a one-term Georgia governor, camped out in Iowa and turned relentless retail politicking into a nomination. Barack Obama’s 2008 Iowa win signaled that a black candidate with a message of hope could build a multiracial coalition even in a predominantly white state. These were not flukes; they were stress tests that national polls could never replicate. The early contests reward authenticity and punish phoniness in ways that media-driven campaigns in larger states cannot.

Critics point to the lack of racial diversity, but representation is not the sole purpose of a primary calendar. The early states should not be a miniaturized electorate; they should be a starting line that tests viability. Moreover, the intense national media focus on these two states means that issues of concern to communities of color are not ignored—they become part of the national conversation precisely because candidates are pressed on them in town halls seen by millions. The solution is not to jettison a system that works but to ensure that the early windows of the calendar also include diverse states soon after, as Nevada and South Carolina now do. Iowa and New Hampshire remain the best filters for a grueling process.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The intimate nature of these contests forces candidates to undergo rigorous face-to-face scrutiny that cannot be replicated in larger, media-driven states.
  2. 02The early primary format allows underdog candidates to prove their mettle and build momentum through grassroots organizing rather than relying solely on national polling or name recognition.
  3. 03The retail politicking required in these states effectively filters for authenticity and punishes candidates who lack genuine connection with voters.
  4. 04The intense national media attention focused on these early states ensures that diverse issues are elevated to the national conversation even if the states themselves are not demographically representative.
  5. 05The current system successfully functions as a stress test for viability, provided that more diverse states like Nevada and South Carolina follow closely in the calendar.
Diversified MiniMax

"Iowa and New Hampshire should remain the first primary states because their small scale and retail-politics culture provide a necessary proving ground that prioritizes candidate engagement over financial power."

The hand-wringing over Iowa and New Hampshire's "disproportionate" influence misunderstands what these states actually provide. It's not about demographic representation—it's about electoral laboratory conditions. Both states' formats force candidates to engage directly with voters, build organizations from scratch, and compete on retail politics rather than raw spending. A caucus in a living room teaches candidates things no amount of polling or advertising can.

The diversity argument cuts both ways. Yes, Iowa is 92% white—but it's also economically diverse, spanning struggling rural communities and growing suburban Des Moines. New Hampshire combines Yankee independence with genuine socioeconomic range. Both states have produced primary winners who lost the general election and dark horses who surged. That's not a flaw; that's the system working as intended.

What's the alternative? A mega-state like California or Texas voting first would simply amplify money's importance. Candidates with early name recognition andfat fundraising bases would dominate, while insurgent candidates with novel ideas would never get their footing. Small states moderate this dynamic by lowering the cost of retail campaigning.

The real issue isn't which states vote first—it's the frontloaded calendar that compresses everything into a few chaotic weeks. Imagine if primaries stretched across four months with meaningful breaks. Regional primaries could work, rotating every cycle. But dismantling the small-state threshold entirely would transform primaries into something closer to general elections, where name ID and cash dominate.

Iowa and New Hampshire aren't perfect. No system would be. But they serve a genuine function as proving grounds where organizing matters and retail politics rewards authenticity. That's worth preserving.

  4. 04The perceived flaws in the primary system are more a result of a compressed, frontloaded calendar than the specific demographics of the early-voting states.

Diversified Claude

"The current system of starting the presidential primary process in small states like Iowa and New Hampshire should be preserved with only modest reforms because it effectively vets candidates in a way that large-scale or national primaries cannot."

Before we tear down Iowa and New Hampshire, it is worth asking what they actually do for the nominating process. The answer, I think, is more than critics admit.

Presidential primaries serve two functions. They aggregate voter preferences, and they test candidates. The first function is best served by a large, diverse electorate. The second is best served by a small, attentive one. You cannot vet twenty candidates in a national primary; voters in California or Texas would never meet most of them, and the contest would collapse into a name-recognition and money race. Iowa and New Hampshire, whatever their demographic deficiencies, force candidates to answer unscripted questions from voters who have heard the stump speech four times and are not impressed. Senators and governors who cannot do this get weeded out. That is a public service.

The demographic critique is real but overstated. Yes, both states are overwhelmingly white. But the early window now includes Nevada and South Carolina, which together include large Latino, African American, and union constituencies. By the time a candidate has run that gauntlet, they have been tested in front of a reasonably broad cross-section of the party coalition. Hillary Clinton's struggles with Black voters in 2008, and Bernie Sanders's current effort to broaden beyond a white liberal base, have been exposed precisely because South Carolina exists.

What about the claim that Iowa picks the nominee? It doesn't. Recent Iowa Republican winners include Mike Huckabee, Rick Santorum, and Ted Cruz—none of whom became the nominee. Iowa winnows; it does not anoint. New Hampshire has a better track record but has also blessed insurgents (Buchanan, McCain twice) who flamed out.

I would accept modest reform: rotating Iowa and New Hampshire's positions with two other small states from different regions every cycle, so the privilege is shared. But replacing them with a national primary or with Florida and California first would simply hand the nomination to whoever raises the most money the earliest. The current system, for all its flaws, allows underfunded candidates a fighting chance. Throw that away carelessly and we'll regret it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small, attentive electorates force candidates to engage in retail politics and answer unscripted questions, weeding out those who cannot handle direct voter scrutiny.
  2. 02The inclusion of Nevada and South Carolina in the early primary window sufficiently addresses demographic concerns by testing candidates before a diverse cross-section of the party coalition.
  3. 03Early states serve to winnow the field of candidates rather than definitively anoint the final nominee.
  4. 04Starting with small states prevents the nominating process from becoming a contest decided solely by early fundraising and name recognition.
  5. 05Rotating the first positions among various small states would be a better reform than moving to a national primary or starting with large, expensive states.
Diversified Gemini

"Iowa and New Hampshire should retain their first-in-the-nation status because their small size and inexpensive media markets protect the democratic necessity of retail campaigning."

The quadrennial outrage over Iowa and New Hampshire’s privileged status in the presidential nominating process is as much a political tradition as the primaries themselves. Critics correctly point out that these two states are older, whiter, and more rural than the rest of the country. But replacing them at the front of the calendar with mega-states like California, New York, or Texas would destroy the one remaining democratic equalizer in American presidential politics: retail campaigning.

Iowa and New Hampshire serve a vital, irreplaceable function. Because they are geographically small and feature relatively inexpensive media markets, they force candidates to campaign on a human scale. A candidate cannot simply buy a victory in Iowa or New Hampshire with a barrage of television advertisements and a massive super PAC. They must hold town halls, answer unscripted questions in diners, and look voters in the eye.

If we move the starting line to states with massive, diverse populations, the barrier to entry for presidential candidates will skyrocket. Only the most famous, establishment-backed, or independently wealthy candidates will be able to compete. The underdog campaigns that have historically energized our democracy—from Jimmy Carter in 1976 to Barack Obama in 2008—would be suffocated in their cribs, unable to afford the tens of millions of dollars required to broadcast their message across multiple major media markets.

Yes, the demographics of Iowa and New Hampshire are not perfectly representative of the United States. But demographic representation is not the only metric of a healthy primary system. The current setup tests a candidate's stamina, their ability to organize at the grassroots level, and their capacity to connect with everyday people. We already have a system where money dictates too much of our politics. Stripping the first-in-the-nation status from small states would only hand more power to billionaires and party elites, effectively ending the era of the grassroots underdog.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small states force candidates to engage in human-scale campaigning, such as town halls and direct voter interaction, rather than relying solely on television advertisements.
  2. 02Moving the initial primaries to large, diverse states would create an insurmountable financial barrier to entry that favors only wealthy or establishment-backed candidates.
  3. 03The current system allows grassroots underdogs and lesser-known candidates to build momentum and compete on a national stage.
  4. 04The early primary process serves as a vital test of a candidate's organizational skills and ability to connect with everyday people beyond just their demographic appeal.
