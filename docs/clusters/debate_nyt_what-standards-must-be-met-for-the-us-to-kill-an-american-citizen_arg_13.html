"The U.S. government should only kill American citizens under strict, transparent standards of necessity, unavailability of capture, genuine imminence, and post-action accountability."

The question asks what standards must be met to kill an American citizen. The uncomfortable answer is that the standards should be nearly the same for killing anyone. Citizenship triggers the Fifth Amendment in an especially visible way, but noncitizens are not simply targets in a video game. A sound rule must be one the United States would be willing to see used by other governments.

That rule begins with necessity. Lethal force should be used only against a person who is actually part of an enemy armed force or is directly involved in a concrete plot to kill. Association, rhetoric, ideological sympathy and past wrongdoing are not enough. If a citizen is a propagandist, indict him for material support or treason if the evidence allows. If he is an operational commander, the government should say so and be prepared to defend that conclusion to someone outside the chain of command.

Second, capture must be genuinely unavailable. This cannot mean “risky” or “diplomatically awkward.” Capture is often risky; that is not a reason to bypass the Constitution. But if the target is in a remote area controlled by hostile forces and the local government cannot act, the analysis changes.

Third, imminence must retain ordinary meaning. The government need not wait until a bomber is walking toward an airport, but it should have evidence of an ongoing operational threat, not merely a belief that the person will remain dangerous indefinitely. A standard that treats every senior terrorist as perpetually “imminent” is not a standard.

Fourth, there must be accountability: written findings, presidential responsibility, notification to Congress, review by an independent court or inspector after the fact, and disclosure of the legal reasoning. Families of mistakenly killed citizens should have a path to challenge the government’s account.

Does targeted killing deprive citizens of due process? It can, but it need not always. A citizen shooting at American forces in Afghanistan has received all the process the battlefield permits. A citizen secretly placed on a kill list far from any battlefield has not. The administration’s white paper gestures toward important limits, but its secrecy and elastic language make those limits unreliable. The United States needs rules strict enough to protect life, public enough to preserve legitimacy, and practical enough to address real threats.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lethal force must be restricted to individuals who are active members of an enemy force or directly involved in a concrete plot, rather than those who merely provide propaganda or ideological sympathy.
  2. 02The government must demonstrate that capturing the individual is genuinely unavailable rather than simply risky or diplomatically inconvenient.
  3. 03The definition of an 'imminent threat' must be based on evidence of an ongoing operational threat rather than a vague assumption of indefinite danger.
  4. 04There must be a robust system of accountability, including written findings, congressional notification, and independent judicial or administrative review after the fact.
  5. 05Targeted killing only satisfies due process if it is limited to active battlefield scenarios where traditional legal proceedings are impossible.
