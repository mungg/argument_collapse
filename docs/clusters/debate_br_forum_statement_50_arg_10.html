"For participatory democracy to be truly meaningful and effective, it must extend beyond the political sphere and be central to the workplace through a revitalized and inclusive labor movement."

The Port Huron Statement’s anniversary arrives at a moment when the American labor movement is fighting for its life. That fact should shape how we read Tom Hayden’s essay. Participatory democracy is an inspiring phrase, but for millions of people the most immediate experience of powerlessness is not the university, the campaign, or the public square. It is the workplace.

Most adults spend much of their waking life under authority they do not elect and can rarely challenge. They are told when to arrive, when to leave, what to wear, what to say, how fast to work, and sometimes when they may use the bathroom. Their wages, schedules, benefits, and job security are set by managers whose decisions can transform a family’s life overnight. In theory, a worker may quit. In practice, rent, health insurance, children, debt, and a weak labor market make that freedom thin. If participatory democracy does not reach the workplace, it leaves democracy at the factory gate, the office door, the warehouse entrance, and the service counter.

Hayden mentions workplace decision-making, unions, and Wisconsin, but they deserve to be central. The struggle in Wisconsin is not only a budget fight. It is a democratic fight over whether public workers have a collective voice. Governor Scott Walker and his allies present collective bargaining as a special privilege for government employees. But collective bargaining is one of the few institutions through which ordinary workers can negotiate with power on something like equal terms. To strip it away while claiming democratic legitimacy is to reduce democracy to elections held every few years, with silence in between.

The attack on public-sector unions has succeeded in part because private-sector unionism has been so weakened. Many workers without pensions or decent health benefits are encouraged to resent those who still have them rather than ask why standards have fallen for everyone else. This is a politics of downward leveling. It converts insecurity into envy. A democratic labor politics would move in the opposite direction: defend public workers while organizing private workers, raise the floor, and make dignity at work a common expectation rather than an exception.

The New Left often had an uneasy relationship with labor. Students saw unions as bureaucratic, conservative, compromised by Cold War politics, and insufficiently attentive to race, gender, war, and democracy. Some of those criticisms were justified. Many unions were hierarchical. Some excluded black workers or women. Some supported the Vietnam War. Some leaders were too close to the Democratic establishment. But the student left also underestimated what unions had built: durable organizations with dues, elected leadership, steward systems, bargaining power, political education, and a culture of solidarity rooted in daily life. Movements need moral energy; they also need institutions that can last.

Today, Occupy has done something important by naming the 99 percent and challenging the legitimacy of an economy organized for the benefit of the wealthy. But the next step must involve the workplace. Marches against banks are necessary but not sufficient. The power of capital is not located only in Wall Street speculation; it is reproduced in wage-setting, scheduling, subcontracting, union-busting, benefit cutting, and the classification of workers as temporary, part-time, independent, disposable. A democracy worthy of the name must give workers more power over these decisions.

That will require labor law reform, but not only labor law reform. The current system of union elections is badly broken. Employers can intimidate workers, delay votes, fire organizers, and treat penalties as a cost of doing business. The failure of the Employee Free Choice Act after Obama’s election was a devastating reminder that labor cannot rely on friendly rhetoric from Democrats. Without stronger rights to organize, collective bargaining will continue to shrink. But unions must also experiment: worker centers, community-labor alliances, minority unionism, sectoral campaigns, living-wage ordinances, domestic worker bills of rights, and organizing among immigrants and low-wage service workers.

Immigrant workers are crucial to this future. The undocumented undergraduates Hayden praises are part of a broader struggle against an economy that depends on labor it keeps frightened. Deportability is a tool of workplace discipline. Employers benefit when workers are afraid to report wage theft, unsafe conditions, or abuse. A serious labor movement must therefore be an immigrant-rights movement. The demand for legalization is not charity; it is a demand to remove a weapon used to divide and exploit the working class.

The same is true of gender. Care work, domestic labor, retail, health care, education, and food service are central to the modern economy, and they are disproportionately performed by women, including women of color and immigrants. A labor politics that imagines the worker as a male industrial breadwinner will fail. Participatory democracy at work must include scheduling rights, paid sick leave, parental leave, protection against harassment, recognition of domestic workers, and respect for the labor of care.

Hayden’s phrase “inside-outside strategy” is exactly right for labor. Unions need street heat and legislative work. They need strikes and contracts, recall campaigns and shop-floor committees, political alliances and rank-and-file democracy. The civil rights movement’s success depended in part on its ability to disrupt business as usual while negotiating enforceable gains. Labor must do the same. The recall effort in Wisconsin shows both the promise and peril of electoral strategy: elections can channel anger, but they can also narrow a movement’s imagination to one candidate or one office. Win or lose, the deeper work is rebuilding worker power.

Participatory democracy also has to apply inside unions themselves. Members will not fight for organizations they experience as insurance agencies or political ATMs. They need to debate, elect, organize, and lead. A union meeting at its best is a school of democracy. A steward who helps co-workers confront a supervisor is teaching citizenship as surely as any civics class. A strike vote, a contract campaign, a picket line, a bargaining committee—these are democratic practices that shape people’s sense of their own power.

The Port Huron Statement spoke of alienation, and that word remains useful. But alienation is not only cultural or psychological. It is economic. People are alienated when they produce wealth they do not control, when productivity rises but wages stagnate, when corporations close profitable plants in search of cheaper labor, when a worker’s knowledge of how to do the job is ignored by managers chasing quarterly numbers. Workplace democracy is not a sentimental add-on to political democracy. It is a response to the structure of power in capitalism.

At fifty, Port Huron should be reread from the shop floor, the classroom, the hospital ward, the call center, the warehouse, the restaurant kitchen, and the home where a care worker tends an elderly person for low wages and no benefits. The universal human need to participate in creating our community includes the need to participate in the decisions that govern our labor. A democracy that stops at the voting booth is too thin. A democracy that enters the workplace would be worthy of the name.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The workplace is the primary site of powerlessness for most adults, making democratic participation there essential to overcoming economic alienation.
  2. 02Collective bargaining is a vital democratic institution that allows ordinary workers to negotiate with power on equal terms.
  3. 03A modern labor movement must move beyond its historical biases to embrace and defend the rights of immigrant, female, and low-wage service workers.
  4. 04Labor organizations must practice internal participatory democracy to ensure members are active leaders rather than passive consumers of services.
  5. 05While electoral strategies and legislative reforms are necessary, the movement's priority must be rebuilding durable worker power and institutions that can disrupt business as usual.
  6. 06The struggle for labor rights should be viewed as a response to the structural power of capitalism rather than just a series of policy or budget disputes.
