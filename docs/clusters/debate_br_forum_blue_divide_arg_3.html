"The Democratic Party's shift toward a bold redistributive agenda, despite its increasingly affluent and educated voter base, reflects a fundamental change in class politics where metropolitan professionals now view public investment and social stability as essential to their own material security."

Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson are right about the central surprise: the Democratic Party has moved left on economics at the same time that its electoral coalition has moved up the income and education ladder. That is not what many theories of party competition would predict. Nor is it what the party’s own recent history seemed to foretell. A party increasingly dependent on professionals in Northern Virginia, suburban Atlanta, Orange County, the Main Line, and the Bay Area might have been expected to become more socially liberal and economically cautious. Instead, in 2021, Democrats put forward a program of child allowances, paid leave, public care investments, green industrial policy, antitrust enforcement, expanded health subsidies, union-friendly procurement, and higher taxes on corporations and the very rich.

That shift deserves to be taken seriously. It also deserves to be understood not as a paradox but as a sign that the politics of class in the United States have changed.

The old picture of economic politics imagined class interests in relatively simple terms. Lower-income voters wanted more government spending and redistribution; affluent voters wanted lower taxes and market freedom. That picture was always too simple, but it has become especially inadequate in the economy Hacker and Pierson describe. The most affluent Democratic voters are not simply “capital” in the old sense. They are often salaried professionals, public-sector workers, nonprofit employees, academics, health care workers, engineers, lawyers, and managers whose material security depends less on owning firms than on living in high-functioning metropolitan regions. They want good schools, transit, broadband, scientific investment, climate mitigation, stable health systems, social peace, and a federal government that can actually govern.

This does not make them radicals. Many of them are homeowners with retirement accounts and a keen interest in preserving local privilege. But it does mean that the assumed hard ceiling on redistribution is higher than many critics suppose. A household earning $180,000 in a high-cost metro area may resist broad-based tax increases, but it may also see public child care, student debt relief, climate investment, and health insurance subsidies as material benefits rather than as charity for someone else. A household earning $300,000 may not want its property taxes raised, but it may understand that wildfires, pandemics, decaying infrastructure, underpaid care workers, and collapsing democratic institutions are not problems that private consumption can solve.

This is one reason Biden’s 2021 agenda was politically plausible. It did not ask affluent Democratic voters to become class traitors in any heroic sense. It offered them a version of social democracy adapted to an unequal, high-cost, metropolitan country: broad benefits, visible public investment, tax increases concentrated above the upper-middle class, and a promise that the state could make life less precarious without demanding that most Democratic voters give up much immediately. Critics can call that evasive. In some ways it is. But it is also how majoritarian reform often works in the United States. Social Security was not built by asking people to embrace redistribution in the abstract. Medicare was not built by leading with a lecture on inequality. Durable programs are built when broad constituencies see themselves in them.

The child tax credit is a useful example. Its temporary expansion in 2021 was one of the most consequential anti-poverty policies enacted in decades. It reduced child poverty sharply, especially among Black and Latino children, but it did so through a broad family benefit rather than a narrowly racialized or stigmatized program. That design was not a retreat from justice. It was a recognition of American political reality: policies that are visibly universal or near-universal are harder to isolate, demonize, and dismantle. In a country where racial resentment has repeatedly been used to attack social provision, universalism can be a vehicle for racial equality.

The same is true of the care economy. Paid leave, child care subsidies, elder care, and pre-K are often described as “family policy,” but they are also labor policy, gender policy, race policy, and productivity policy. They recognize that the market has long depended on underpaid or unpaid care work, disproportionately done by women and women of color. A serious public care agenda would reduce poverty, raise labor force participation, improve child development, stabilize families, and create better jobs in a sector that cannot simply be outsourced. If that is not economic populism, the term has become too narrow to be useful.

Still, Hacker and Pierson’s argument is strongest when they emphasize the careful curation of the Democratic economic turn. The party has moved left, but not everywhere. It has embraced climate investment more readily than it has embraced fossil fuel phaseout. It has embraced housing subsidies more readily than it has challenged exclusionary zoning. It has embraced taxing billionaires and corporations more readily than it has asked upper-middle-class professionals to pay European-style taxes for European-style benefits. It has embraced racial equity inside broad spending programs more readily than it has foregrounded reparations, immigration legalization, or deep police reform. These choices are not accidental. They are the operating manual of a U-shaped coalition.

The question is whether such a coalition can do enough. The answer, I think, is yes—but only if Democrats do not mistake coalition management for coalition building. Avoiding divisive issues may be necessary in a polarized two-party system, but it cannot substitute for creating constituencies that will fight for the programs once enacted. The Affordable Care Act became more popular over time partly because millions came to rely on it. Social Security and Medicare endure because beneficiaries understand them as earned, concrete, and theirs. By contrast, the expanded child tax credit expired before it could become an institution with its own political army. A benefit that arrives for six months and disappears in a fog of congressional procedure cannot be expected to transform partisan identity.

This is the deepest lesson of the “near miss” of 2021. Policy can build power, but only if it survives long enough to become part of ordinary life. Democrats’ agenda was bold in conception but institutionally fragile. It depended on a 50–50 Senate, reconciliation rules, a tiny House majority, and the assent of senators representing very different political worlds. That is not a recipe for reconstructing American political economy. It is a recipe for almost doing so.

Hacker and Pierson are also right that the constitutional structure compounds the difficulty. The Senate, Electoral College, gerrymandering, and geographic concentration of Democratic voters create a system in which a party can represent most voters and still lack governing power. This matters not merely because it frustrates Democrats, but because it distorts policy. A party trying to hold together multiracial metro America must also win enough power in a system biased toward low-density, disproportionately white states. That forces caution even when public opinion might support action.

But the density tax should not become an alibi. Democrats still have choices. They can invest more seriously in state parties outside the largest metros. They can rebuild labor capacity in care, logistics, education, manufacturing, and public services. They can make industrial policy visible in places that do not currently trust them. They can speak about economic power in a language less filtered through credentialed liberalism. They can confront housing scarcity in Democratic-governed regions, where their own voters and donors often defend the structures that make metropolitan opportunity so unequal.

The new blue divide is real, but it is not fatal. In fact, it may contain the basis for a governing majority: a coalition of working-class voters who need security, young voters priced out of stability, people of color denied equal opportunity, and educated professionals who understand that private affluence cannot protect them from public breakdown. That coalition will not agree on everything. No large democratic coalition ever does. The task is not to erase its contradictions but to build institutions and policies strong enough to make solidarity practical.

The Democratic Party’s recent economic turn is therefore neither a mirage nor a revolution. It is an opening. It shows that the party of affluent suburbs can still pursue redistribution; that racial justice can be advanced through broad economic programs; that industrial policy can be joined to climate policy; and that the exhausted assumptions of the Clinton era no longer command the party. But an opening is not a settlement. Unless Democrats turn temporary ambition into durable institutions, the new blue divide will remain a clever diagnosis of a coalition that almost governed.
