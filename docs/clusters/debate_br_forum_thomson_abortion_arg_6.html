"While judicial victories like Roe v. Wade are important, the pro-choice movement must secure the abortion right through moral and democratic persuasion to overcome the political instability and violence caused by judicial fiat."

**On the Politics, If Not the Philosophy**

Set aside, for a moment, the philosophical question of whether fertilized eggs have rights. Thomson's essay is most powerful, I think, not in its careful treatment of Dworkin or its analysis of "interests" and "rights," but in its description of what is actually happening on the ground in the United States in 1995.

A man walks into two Brookline clinics and opens fire, killing two and wounding five. He then drives to Norfolk and shoots up another clinic. He is the latest in a string of assassins. Doctors who provide abortions are being murdered. Their colleagues are receiving "Wanted" posters with their faces on them. Insurance premiums for clinics are soaring. Medical schools, under pressure, have stopped teaching abortion procedures. In vast stretches of rural America, the constitutional right declared in *Roe* and reaffirmed in *Casey* has become a paper right—legally available, practically unobtainable.

This is the situation, and Thomson is right to insist that it cannot be permitted to wash over us as if "all that matters is that the legal right was won." A right that no one can exercise is not a right.

What I want to add to Thomson's essay is a thought about the political dynamics that produced this situation, and what they imply about the path forward.

The dominant narrative on the legal left has long been that *Roe* settled the matter and that ongoing opposition is mere reactionary refusal to accept settled law. Thomson herself flirts with a version of this when she defends the propriety of judicial settlement. She cites the recent empirical work suggesting that access would have been worse had the issue been left to legislatures, and she concludes that there is no principled objection to judicial resolution of the question.

I am not so sure. The empirical claim may well be correct as to access, narrowly defined. But the political claim—that constitutionalizing the question has produced a stable settlement—seems harder to sustain in light of the very phenomena Thomson describes. Twenty-two years after *Roe*, the issue is more divisive, not less. Opponents have not reconciled themselves to the constitutional status quo; they have organized, mobilized, and in extreme cases, taken up arms. The Christian Coalition's political ascendancy is in significant part a story about abortion.

Compare the trajectory of abortion politics in Europe and Canada. In most European democracies, abortion was liberalized through legislation, often with significant compromises—gestational limits, counseling requirements, public funding tied to indication. The results have been politically stable settlements that command broad public acceptance, and access in practice that is in most cases superior to American access. The American exceptionalism on this question—the violence, the rage, the political polarization—is at least partly attributable, I suspect, to the particular path by which the right was established here.

This is not to say that *Roe* was wrongly decided. It is to say that the question of how a contested moral issue is resolved matters, politically, in ways that abstract jurisprudence sometimes obscures. A right won by judicial fiat does not, in general, achieve the kind of legitimacy that a right won through democratic deliberation does. This is especially true when the right concerns a question on which (as Thomson herself acknowledges) reasonable people deeply disagree.

Where does this leave the pro-choice movement in 1995? I would suggest two things.

First, defend the right vigorously, including against extralegal violence. The murders in Brookline and elsewhere are acts of terrorism, and they should be prosecuted as such. The federal government should use the full weight of its authority—including the FACE Act and federal civil rights law—to protect clinic workers and patients. Thomson's call to take the threat seriously is exactly right.

Second, and harder: do not be satisfied with constitutional victories. The pro-choice movement needs to win the moral and political argument as well, in legislatures, in churches, in op-ed pages, on talk shows, in clinics. It needs to articulate why women's autonomy matters, why early abortion is morally different from late abortion, why a society that denies women reproductive control denies them full citizenship. The constitutional right will only be secure when it is supported by a durable popular majority that understands and accepts the reasons for it.

Thomson's essay does some of this work, and does it well. But her argument is pitched at a level of abstraction that is unlikely to move anyone who is not already convinced. To the abortion opponent who genuinely believes a baby is being killed, the response "the constrained must be unreasonable in rejecting the constraint" will sound like sophistry. Something more is needed. The pro-choice movement has, in figures like Cuomo, articulate spokespeople who can make the case in moral terms that reach across the divide. We need more of that, not less.

We also need, I think, to be honest about the moral complexity of what is at stake. A fertilized egg is not a baby, but it is also not nothing. An eight-week fetus is not a person, but its destruction is not morally trivial. The pretense that abortion is morally uncomplicated has done the pro-choice cause real damage. It is not necessary to embrace the encyclical's view that abortion is murder in order to acknowledge that something morally weighty is involved in the destruction of a developing human organism. The honest acknowledgment of that weight, coupled with a defense of women's authority to make this decision themselves, is a more sustainable politics than the rhetoric of unproblematic choice.

The Brookline murders should remind us that this is not an academic dispute. Real lives—of women, of doctors, of clinic workers, and yes, in the view of opponents, of unborn children—hang in the balance. Thomson is right to insist on engagement. The question is what kind of engagement we offer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current climate of violence and restricted access in the U.S. proves that a constitutional right is insufficient if it lacks practical protection and broad public legitimacy.
  2. 02The American reliance on judicial resolution, rather than legislative compromise as seen in Europe, has fueled extreme political polarization and reactionary violence.
  3. 03A right won through judicial action fails to achieve the same durable legitimacy as one won through democratic deliberation, especially on deeply contested moral issues.
  4. 04The federal government must treat anti-abortion violence as terrorism and use the full weight of the law to protect the physical exercise of the right.
  5. 05The pro-choice movement must move beyond abstract legalism and engage in moral arguments that acknowledge the complexity of fetal life while prioritizing women's autonomy.
  6. 06Honest acknowledgment of the moral weight of developing human life is a more sustainable political strategy than treating abortion as a morally uncomplicated choice.
