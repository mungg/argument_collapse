"While anger is a messy and potentially corrupting emotion, its ultimate moral purpose should be directed toward restorative justice and public repair rather than the cycle of personal revenge and permanent grudges."

Agnes Callard’s essay is bracing because it refuses a consolation that many of us want: that righteous anger can be kept morally clean if only we give it the right name. “Indignation,” “resentment,” “transition anger”—these terms often function as laundering devices. They let us enjoy the posture of rage while denying its appetite for harm. Callard insists that the appetite is not accidental. Anger, she argues, is bound to grudges and revenge because wrongdoing gives us permanent reason to be angry, and because revenge is the way anger holds the wrongdoer responsible.

I think she is half right in a way that should unsettle both sides of the debate. She is right that anger is not a neat moral instrument. She is wrong that revenge is the deep form of accountability. The missing term in her analysis is restoration. Not sentimental reconciliation, not compulsory forgiveness, not cheap therapeutic closure, but the hard social work of building a world in which the truth of the wrong is publicly acknowledged and the relations damaged by it are reconstituted as far as possible.

The difference matters because Callard’s examples are mostly dyadic: I steal from you; you are angry at me; I apologize; you decide whether to remain angry or retaliate. But many of the injustices she invokes near the end—racism, anti-Semitism, sexism, classism, ableism—are not dyadic in that way. They are institutional and historical. They are carried not only by individual malice but by law, habit, memory, spatial arrangement, economic distribution, educational sorting, policing, inheritance, and silence. In such cases revenge is not only morally dangerous; it is conceptually inadequate. Whom exactly does one revenge oneself upon? The particular bigot? The bureaucrat? The ancestor? The neighbor who benefited? The child born into the benefiting class? The dead?

Restorative justice begins from a different premise: wrongdoing creates obligations. It creates obligations in the wrongdoer, but also in bystanders, institutions, and communities that allowed the wrong to happen or profited from it. Anger can be one way of detecting those obligations. It says, “This is not normal; this must not be smoothed over.” But the task is not to turn the offender’s bad into the victim’s good. It is to insist that the wrong be named and that repair become real.

Callard says that apology and restitution do not change the fact that the theft occurred. True. But they can change whether the wrong is still being socially enacted. A theft that is denied, minimized, or excused continues in one form; a theft confessed, repaid, and transformed into new conduct continues in another. The past does not change, but the social meaning of the past can. That is why truth commissions matter, why memorials matter, why reparations matter, why apologies matter when they are backed by material change. They do not erase. They refuse erasure.

This point is especially important in cases of collective wrong. The descendants of slaves, the survivors of genocide, the targets of segregation, the women excluded from institutions, the workers poisoned by unsafe conditions: none of these groups needs to be told that the past cannot be undone. The moral question is whether society will organize itself around denial or around repair. Anger directed toward repair is not pure, perhaps, but it is not merely a moderated form of revenge. It has a different object.

Callard’s account of revenge treats wrongdoing as the creation of a principle between two people: you made my bad your good, so I now make your bad my good. But oppression often works by denying that the victim’s bad counts at all. The oppressor does not always experience himself as locked in a dramatic moral opposition. He may experience nothing. He may think the suffering of others is the background condition of ordinary life. In that situation, anger’s first demand is not “I will harm you as you harmed me,” but “You will see me.” The aim is recognition.

Of course recognition can be violent. Callard’s warning is serious. The demand to be seen can become the demand that the other be made to suffer until he sees. But restorative politics tries to interrupt that movement by shifting from pain to obligation. The wrongdoer must not merely feel bad; he must do something. He must tell the truth, relinquish benefit, accept constraint, compensate loss, submit to changed rules, and rebuild trust over time. These are burdens, and they may feel like punishment. But they are not revenge because their point is not the wrongdoer’s suffering. Their point is the repair of the moral world.

It might be said that this is just another philosopher’s fiction, a sanitized substitute for anger’s bloody heart. Yet restorative practices are not imaginary. Families, schools, indigenous legal traditions, religious communities, and some criminal justice experiments have long used forms of accountability that center acknowledgment, restitution, and reintegration. These practices are imperfect, sometimes coercive, sometimes naive about power. They can be misused to pressure victims into premature forgiveness. But their existence shows that human beings can hold one another accountable without making the offender’s suffering the governing good.

Callard’s language of “moral corruption” also needs challenge. She writes that long-term oppression makes the oppressed morally worse people. I understand the point: oppression can produce hatred, suspicion, cruelty, and a taste for revenge. But the phrase risks doing what oppressive societies have often done—placing the moral condition of the oppressed under inspection while the structures that injured them remain oddly abstract. It is one thing to say that injustice wounds moral agency. It is another to say that victims are morally worse.

The distinction between wound and corruption is not cosmetic. If a person raised under constant threat becomes hypervigilant, the hypervigilance may lead to misjudgments. But it is also an intelligent adaptation to danger. If a persecuted community transmits suspicion of outsiders, that suspicion may become prejudice. But it may also preserve life. If survivors cannot forgive, their refusal may burden future relations. But it may also bear witness to the enormity of what was done. We need moral vocabulary subtle enough to name deformation without converting injury into blame.

Callard’s story of her grandmother is powerful because it resists easy judgment. A Holocaust survivor sees her granddaughter marching with a German child and experiences horror. The child experiences the grandmother’s response as prejudice. Both perceptions contain truth. But I would not say that the grandmother’s anger simply made her morally corrupt. Her anger was a mode of memory. It carried knowledge the child did not have. It may also have misrecognized the German girl. The tragedy lies in the collision between memory’s truth and childhood’s possibility.

Here restoration would not require the grandmother to “let go” in the therapeutic sense. It would require a world in which her memory was held by more than her own anger. One reason victims cling to rage is that the surrounding society refuses to carry the truth. When courts, schools, neighbors, churches, governments, and families deny or minimize wrong, anger becomes an archive. The grudge says: if I release this, it will disappear. That is not irrational. It is often accurate.

So the proper response to destructive anger is not to scold victims for failing to transcend it. It is to build public forms of remembrance and repair strong enough that individuals are not forced to preserve justice by keeping wounds open. Museums, trials, reparations, changed institutions, historical education, and truthful rituals are not luxuries. They are ways of relocating moral memory from the solitary nervous system into the common world.

Callard is right that we cannot be good in a bad world if “good” means untouched by badness. But that was never the right standard. The question is whether we can be responsible in a damaged world. Responsibility is not innocence. It is the willingness to answer damage with truth and repair rather than denial or imitation. Anger may initiate that answer. It may guard the truth when everyone else wants peace without justice. But if anger is to serve the world rather than devour it, it must eventually hand its evidence to institutions, practices, and relationships capable of doing more than striking back.

We should not purify anger by renaming it. But neither should we enthrone revenge as accountability’s secret essence. The deepest answer to wrongdoing is not that your bad becomes my good. It is that the wrong becomes an obligation—yours, mine, and ours—to make a world in which the truth can be remembered without being endlessly reenacted.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Revenge is a conceptually inadequate form of accountability for systemic and historical injustices like racism or anti-Semitism because it cannot identify a single target for retaliation.
  2. 02Restorative justice shifts the focus of anger from the desire to inflict suffering on the wrongdoer to the enforcement of obligations to repair the moral world.
  3. 03The social meaning of a past wrong can be transformed through public acknowledgment, truth-telling, and material reparations, even if the historical fact of the wrong remains unchanged.
  4. 04Victims often cling to anger and grudges as a rational form of memory when the surrounding society refuses to publicly archive or acknowledge the truth of their suffering.
  5. 05The moral deformation caused by oppression should be viewed as a wound or an adaptation to danger rather than a personal moral corruption of the victim.
  6. 06The goal of moral responsibility in a damaged world is to move beyond the 'imitation' of harm toward building institutions capable of holding moral memory without reenacting violence.
