"Society must reject the normalization of terrorism and instead wage a decisive campaign to defeat it, as accepting it as a permanent condition inevitably leads to the erosion of civil liberties through the expansion of a surveillance state."

The idea that we can calmly “live with” terrorism while preserving our freedoms is a dangerous delusion. If large-scale attacks become a recurring feature of European life — a truck here, a bombing there — the public will not respond with philosophical resilience. They will demand, with ever greater fury, that the state protect them at any cost. Politicians will compete to promise ever harsher measures. The end result will not be a stoic, liberty-loving society; it will be the steady construction of a surveillance state, built on fear, justified by the very attacks we were told to accept.

We have already seen this pattern. After the November 2015 attacks, France declared a state of emergency. Emergency powers that were supposed to be temporary have been renewed again and again, with talk of enshrining some measures permanently in the constitution. Police raids without warrants, house arrests without trial, expanded digital surveillance — these are becoming routine. If we follow Valls’s logic and normalize terrorism, we normalize its antidotes as well. The security regime will grow layer by layer, crisis by crisis, until we wake up in a country where the liberty to protest, to keep a secret, or to move freely is a memory.

The only way to defend freedom, paradoxically, is to refuse to accept terrorism as a permanent condition. We must treat it as an enemy to be defeated, not a chronic pain to be managed. That means a far more aggressive military and intelligence campaign against ISIS strongholds in Syria and Libya. It means dismantling the funding networks, the safe houses, the online echo chambers. It means, when necessary, deploying troops to protect critical infrastructure so citizens can gather without feeling like targets. These are serious measures, but they can be temporary and targeted. They do not require indefinite suspension of civil law or the mass internment of communities.

A clear-eyed look at recent history shows that half-measures and talk of “living with it” only prolong the agony. After Madrid, London, Paris, Brussels, and now Nice, we have neither crushed the jihadist networks nor maintained full civil liberties. We are stuck in a gray zone of fear, where each new attack is met with a fresh round of security theater and political recriminations. This is the worst of both worlds. The humane answer is not to learn to live with terrorism, but to wage a decisive, finite campaign to render it incapable of mass operations, and then — and only then — to reclaim the full spectrum of our freedoms. Accepting a body count as the price of an open society is not wisdom; it is a political failure that will eventually cost us the very openness we cherish.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Normalizing terrorism triggers a cycle of public fear that pressures politicians to implement increasingly harsh and permanent security measures.
  2. 02The repeated extension of emergency powers in France demonstrates that treating terrorism as a routine threat leads to the normalization of warrantless raids and mass surveillance.
  3. 03Treating terrorism as an enemy to be defeated through targeted military and intelligence actions is the only way to avoid the indefinite suspension of civil law.
  4. 04The current approach of 'living with' terrorism has resulted in a failure to both eliminate jihadist networks and preserve full civil liberties, leaving society in a perpetual state of fear.
  5. 05Accepting a recurring body count as a price for an open society is a political failure that will ultimately destroy the very freedoms it claims to protect.
