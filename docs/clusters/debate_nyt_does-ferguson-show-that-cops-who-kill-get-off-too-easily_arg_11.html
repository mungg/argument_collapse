"The Ferguson case and the low rate of police convictions do not prove that officers get off too easily, but rather that the public is incorrectly looking to criminal law to resolve complex social and systemic issues."

The Ferguson case shows something important, but not what many protesters claim. It does not show that police officers “get off” too easily. It shows that the public increasingly expects the criminal law to resolve every tragic and ambiguous encounter, even when the evidence may not support a charge.

Police officers are authorized to use force because society asks them to do things most citizens avoid: confront violent people, respond to robberies, enter chaotic domestic disputes, and make instant decisions under threat. The law cannot require them to wait until they are shot or overpowered before defending themselves. If an officer reasonably believes he faces serious danger, deadly force may be lawful even if, afterward, the suspect turns out to be unarmed. That is not a loophole; it is recognition of reality.

The anger in Ferguson is understandable. A young man is dead. The history of police treatment of black communities is real. But criminal prosecution is not supposed to be a referendum on history. It is supposed to be based on evidence against a particular defendant. If witnesses contradict one another, physical evidence supports some parts of the officer’s account, and the state cannot prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, then an indictment may create only the illusion of justice. Charging an officer to satisfy public outrage would be as wrong as refusing to charge one to protect the police.

The fact that police killings rarely lead to criminal convictions does not by itself prove the standard is too low. Many police shootings occur in circumstances where officers are confronting armed suspects. The FBI number includes a wide range of incidents, many of them justified. A comparison to civilian homicide rates can be misleading because civilians are not routinely sent into dangerous confrontations by the state.

That said, police departments should not hide behind legality. A shooting can be legally justified and still reveal bad tactics, poor training, racial suspicion, or reckless escalation. The answer is not to make cops criminally liable whenever an encounter ends badly. It is to improve hiring, training, supervision, early-warning systems, and review boards; to use cameras when practical; and to release as much information as possible after a shooting.

If we want better policing, we should demand it directly. Turning every controversial shooting into a criminal trial will not repair trust. It may instead make police more defensive and the public more cynical when acquittals follow.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Police officers must be legally permitted to use force based on a reasonable perception of danger because their role requires them to confront violent and chaotic situations.
  2. 02Criminal prosecution must be based on specific evidence and the standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt rather than serving as a referendum on historical racial grievances or public outrage.
  3. 03The low frequency of criminal convictions for police is not inherently proof of a low legal standard, as officers are uniquely tasked by the state to enter dangerous confrontations.
  4. 04A shooting can be legally justified while still being the result of poor tactics or training, suggesting that administrative and systemic reforms are more appropriate than criminal charges.
  5. 05Attempting to resolve every controversial shooting through the criminal justice system risks damaging public trust and making police more defensive without actually improving policing outcomes.
