"Muslims do not need to do more to defend their faith against extremists because they are already actively condemning terrorism, but their efforts are being ignored by a biased media."

The question "Do Muslims need to defend their faith?" is incredibly frustrating because it implies that we have been silent. We are constantly defending our faith. The problem is not Muslim silence; the problem is systemic deafness.

Since the rise of the Islamic State, there has been an unprecedented avalanche of condemnations from the Muslim world. The Organization of Islamic Cooperation, representing 57 countries, has vehemently denounced ISIS. The highest religious authorities in Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and Turkey have declared their actions entirely un-Islamic. The Council on American-Islamic Relations (CAIR) and the Muslim Council of Britain issue press releases condemning every single act of terror. Ordinary Muslims have launched viral campaigns against extremism.

Yet, public commentators and television pundits continue to ask, "Where are the moderate Muslims?"

The media ecosystem is fundamentally skewed. A single masked militant with a knife and a video camera guarantees weeks of wall-to-wall coverage, endless panel discussions, and front-page headlines. Conversely, a peace rally organized by thousands of Muslims, or a dense theological rebuttal written by leading clerics, might garner a brief mention on page twelve, if it is covered at all.

Peace, normality, and integration are not profitable news stories. Conflict, fear, and the exoticism of the "barbaric East" drive ratings.

Muslims do not need to do more to defend their faith against extremists. They are already doing everything they can. What needs to change is the broader society's willingness to listen. We must demand better from our media institutions, forcing them to amplify the voices of the peaceful majority rather than acting as a free public relations wing for terrorists.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01There has been an unprecedented and widespread volume of condemnations against extremism from global Islamic organizations, religious authorities, and ordinary citizens.
  2. 02The media ecosystem prioritizes sensationalist violence over peaceful Muslim counter-narratives because conflict and fear drive higher ratings.
  3. 03The persistent public questioning of where 'moderate Muslims' are is a result of systemic deafness and selective reporting rather than Muslim silence.
  4. 04Society should focus on demanding that media institutions amplify the voices of the peaceful majority instead of placing more burden on Muslims to speak out.
