Realism in the Age of Austerity: A Pragmatic Path to More Jobs

Robert Pollin’s impassioned call for a return to full employment is a bracing reminder of how far our economic thinking has drifted from the goals that defined the postwar golden age. The scale of the current jobs emergency—with one in six workers effectively unemployed or underemployed—demands a serious response. Yet Pollin's proposed solution, a $330 billion annual reallocation from military and fossil fuels to education and clean energy, illustrates the gap between the economics of hope and the politics of the possible. In the current political climate, with the Tea Party ascendant, a Republican House committed to slashing spending, and the White House on the defensive over deficits, a blueprint that calls for large-scale spending shifts and a frontal assault on powerful industries is a non-starter. The hard truth is that we must find a more realistic, incremental path toward lowering unemployment, one that can actually be enacted and sustained.

The first obstacle is the budget deficit. Pollin acknowledges the $1.4 trillion deficits but waves them away by saying that full employment would be worth it. That may be true in the abstract, but it ignores the palpable anxiety that the deficit has created among voters and financial markets. Rightly or wrongly, the American public has been convinced that the country is on an unsustainable fiscal path, and the midterm elections of 2010 were a clear repudiation of big government. A proposal to redirect $330 billion—even if it were technically a reallocation rather than new spending—would be framed as a massive new entitlement by opponents, and the complexity of cutting the military and fossil-fuel subsidies while simultaneously scaling up green energy and education would provide endless opportunities for obstruction. The idea that such a plan could survive the legislative meat grinder is a fantasy.

A more productive approach would focus on policies that can attract bipartisan support or at least divide the opposition. One such idea is a substantial, temporary payroll tax cut, which puts money directly into workers’ pockets and reduces the cost of hiring for employers. The Obama administration secured a 2-percentage-point reduction in the employee-side payroll tax for 2011, but it should be expanded to cover employers as well, and extended for several years. This is deficit-financed stimulus, to be sure, but it is distributed broadly, avoids “picking winners,” and can be sold as a pro-growth tax cut. Similarly, an infrastructure bank that leverages private capital with a modest federal seed investment could accelerate repair of roads, bridges, and the electric grid—creating construction jobs without a huge upfront appropriation.

Pollin’s job-creation multipliers are impressive, but they assume that the reallocated dollars are actually spent on domestic labor-intensive activities. The reality of government procurement is that much of the money leaks into inflated contracting costs, legal fees, and imports. Instead of channeling funds through Washington’s broken budget process, we should reform regulations to lower the cost of private-sector job creation. Streamlining the permitting process for renewable-energy projects, for example, would do more to expand wind and solar installation than billions in subsidies that take years to flow. The fossil-fuel industries Pollin wants to shrink happen to be major employers and sources of tax revenue; a more politically viable path is to gradually price carbon and recycle the revenue into lower taxes on work and investment, a strategy that could shift consumption and investment without picking a direct fight with the oil lobby.

Finally, Pollin underestimates the extent to which the unemployment problem is not simply a shortage of jobs but a mismatch of skills and geography. Construction workers who lost their jobs in Las Vegas cannot easily relocate to North Dakota, where the oil boom has created a labor shortage. Retraining programs have a mixed record, but they are far more effective when they are tightly linked to actual employer needs. A practical full-employment agenda would massively expand on-the-job training, apprenticeship programs, and relocation assistance, turning the existing unemployment insurance system into a reemployment system. These steps are incremental, but they address the real barriers that keep millions of people out of work even when aggregate demand returns.

None of this is as stirring as Pollin’s vision of a green, high-wage, full-employment America. But politics is the art of the possible, and in 2011 the possible is to prevent backsliding into a double-dip recession, to stabilize the labor market, and to lay the groundwork for a more robust recovery. The grand ideological struggle against neoliberalism will take a generation, not a single legislative session. For now, we must rescue what we can and build the political alliances that might one day make full employment thinkable again.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The massive budget deficit and public anxiety over government spending make Pollin’s plan to redirect $330 billion a political non-starter.
  2. 02Bipartisan, market-oriented policies like payroll tax cuts and infrastructure banks are more effective and enactable tools for stimulus than centralized spending shifts.
  3. 03Regulatory reform, such as streamlining permits for renewable energy, can stimulate private-sector job growth more efficiently than government subsidies.
  4. 04Addressing the structural mismatch between worker skills and geographic job availability is more critical than simply increasing aggregate demand through spending.
  5. 05A gradual approach that builds political alliances and prevents immediate economic backsliding is more realistic than an immediate ideological assault on neoliberalism.
