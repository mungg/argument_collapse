"The Affordable Care Act is not sustainable in its current form because it relies on for-profit insurers whose fiduciary duties to shareholders conflict with the goals of universal healthcare."

What Aetna's withdrawal reveals is the fundamental tension between private insurance company incentives and universal healthcare goals. These companies have fiduciary obligations to shareholders, not to the public health. When exchanges become unprofitable, they leave—even if that means abandoning customers who have no other options.

This is not a bug in the ACA but a feature of relying on for-profit intermediaries for social welfare functions. The exchanges work best when insurers can cross-subsidize across lines of business, using profitable employer plans to offset exchange losses. But when the political environment makes that look risky, or when shareholder pressure mounts, companies bolt.

The sustainable solution is to reduce our dependence on private insurer goodwill. This means either moving toward a public option that competes directly with private plans and can absorb losses during market transitions, or embracing Medicare buy-in for all adults, which would spread risk across the entire population rather than a self-selected exchange pool.

Market-based healthcare reform has consistently underestimated the difficulty of managing actuarial risk in a small, sicker-than-average pool. The ACA exchanges demonstrate that we cannot engineer affordable universal coverage through regulated competition alone. We need systemic risk pooling at a national scale that no single insurer can walk away from.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Private insurance companies will inevitably abandon marketplace exchanges when they become unprofitable, leaving consumers without options.
  2. 02The current exchange model fails because it attempts to manage actuarial risk within small, sicker-than-average pools rather than across the entire population.
  3. 03Sustainability requires reducing dependence on private insurers by introducing a public option or a Medicare buy-in to provide a stable alternative to the private market.
  4. 04Regulated competition alone is insufficient to engineer affordable coverage without systemic, national-scale risk pooling.
