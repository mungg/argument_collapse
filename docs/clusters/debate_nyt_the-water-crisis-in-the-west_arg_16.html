**Restore the Rivers, or There Is No "Sharing" to Discuss**

Most of the debate over Western water assumes a fixed pie to be divided among farms, cities, and industry. The environment, when mentioned, is treated as a competing "user" — one more interest at the table, often the weakest. This framing is exactly wrong, and it is the deepest source of our crisis.

Rivers, wetlands, and aquifers are not water users. They are the infrastructure that delivers water in the first place. Healthy watersheds store snowpack, filter runoff, recharge groundwater, moderate floods, and sustain the fisheries and ecosystems that make the West more than a sunbaked grid of subdivisions. When we drain the Colorado before it reaches the Gulf of California, or pump the Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta until salmon runs collapse, we are not making a tradeoff between nature and people. We are dismantling the system that makes water reliably available to people.

The evidence is everywhere. Subsidence in the San Joaquin Valley is destroying the canals that deliver irrigation water. The decline of Sierra snowpack — which has historically functioned as the West's largest reservoir — cannot be replaced by any feasible amount of dam construction. The collapse of Delta smelt populations is the canary signaling a freshwater ecosystem under terminal stress, and that ecosystem is precisely the throat through which water from northern California passes to the south.

Any serious plan to "share the water" must therefore start by giving the environment a senior, non-negotiable claim. That means minimum in-stream flows that have legal standing equal to or above agricultural and municipal rights. It means dam removal where dams no longer pencil out — the Elwha River restoration in Washington shows what's possible. It means reconnecting floodplains, restoring riparian forests, and managing forests for fire and watershed health rather than timber yield. The 2013 Rim Fire near Yosemite reminded us that a century of fire suppression has loaded our watersheds with fuel that, when ignited, sterilizes soils and chokes reservoirs with sediment.

This is not anti-agriculture or anti-urban. Sustainable farming and thriving cities both require functioning ecosystems. The Murray-Darling reforms in Australia included explicit environmental water allocations precisely because the country learned, painfully, that without them no other use would be reliable either.

It will require renegotiating the Colorado River Compact, which was written in a wet year and accounts for the environment hardly at all. It will require California to enforce public-trust doctrine more aggressively. It will require painful adjustments by senior rights holders who have long treated rivers as plumbing.

But the alternative is the slow conversion of the West into a landscape of dust, salt, and litigation. You cannot share what you have destroyed. Restoration is not a luxury to be funded after the human users have been satisfied. It is the precondition for everything else.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Healthy watersheds, rivers, and aquifers are the essential infrastructure that stores, filters, and delivers water, rather than being mere competitors for its use.
  2. 02The degradation of natural systems, such as land subsidence and the loss of snowpack, directly undermines the physical capacity to deliver water to farms and cities.
  3. 03Environmental claims to water must be granted senior legal standing through minimum in-stream flows to prevent the total collapse of the water delivery system.
  4. 04Restoration efforts, including dam removal and floodplain reconnection, are necessary to repair the damage caused by treating rivers as simple plumbing.
  5. 05International examples, such as Australia's Murray-Darling reforms, demonstrate that explicit environmental water allocations are required to make agricultural and urban water use reliable.
  6. 06Securing the West's water future requires renegotiating historical agreements like the Colorado River Compact to account for environmental health and the reality of a changing climate.
