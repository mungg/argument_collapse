Diversified Gemini

"While Robert Chapman correctly identifies the failures of 'Liberal Neurodiversity,' the proposed solution of a vanguardist 'Neurodivergent Power' movement is practically unworkable; instead, activists should focus on collective labor organizing to dismantle the specific technological and algorithmic mechanisms of modern cognitive exploitation."

Robert Chapman’s compelling essay, "The Future of Neurodiversity," diagnoses a real and pervasive problem: the assimilation of the neurodiversity movement into the very capitalist structures it once sought to challenge. Chapman’s critique of "Liberal Neurodiversity"—with its emphasis on corporate "superpowers," HR-driven inclusion initiatives, and the shallow representation of quirky-but-brilliant detectives on television—strikes at the heart of how late capitalism defangs radical critique. However, while Chapman correctly identifies the disease, their proposed cure—a vanguardist "Neurodivergent Power" movement centered on mass anti-capitalist organization and the creation of a "revolutionary party"—is both theoretically flawed and practically unworkable. In attempting to merge neurodivergent liberation with orthodox Marxist and anti-imperialist struggle, Chapman ignores the unique, specific mechanisms of modern labor exploitation that must be the actual focus of our resistance.

To understand why the "revolutionary party" model is the wrong tool for this fight, we must look at how capitalism is currently restructuring the concept of work and cognitive capability. We are no longer in the era of the industrial factory floor, nor are we in the 1960s political landscape that birthed the Black Panther Party. We are in the era of algorithmic management, surveillance capitalism, and the gig economy.

Capitalism today does not merely exclude neurodivergent people; it actively *disables* them through specific technological and organizational mechanisms. The modern workplace is characterized by open-plan offices, constant digital connectivity, the expectation of immediate emotional regulation in service jobs (affective labor), and algorithmic tracking that punishes anyone who deviates from a rigidly defined pace. Consider the Amazon warehouse worker whose bathroom breaks and picking speeds are tracked to the second. This environment is inherently hostile to anyone with executive functioning differences, ADHD, or physical/sensory processing issues.

Chapman argues that "Liberal Neurodiversity" only values individuals from whom profit can be extracted. This is true. But the corollary is that modern capitalism has created a vast, disposable underclass of the "cognitively non-compliant." When algorithms dictate hiring (via AI resume screeners or video-interview micro-expression analysis), anyone who doesn't maintain "neurotypical" eye contact or conventional speech patterns is automatically filtered out.

Given this reality, how do we fight back? Chapman’s call for a grand, overarching alliance against global imperialism and capitalism, while ideologically coherent from a socialist perspective, dilutes the specific, targeted action required to dismantle these systems of cognitive oppression. By demanding that neurodivergent activists also take on the burdens of abolishing the prison-industrial complex, ending global imperialism, and solving the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, Chapman demands an ideological pureness that fractures mass movements rather than builds them.

Instead of looking to the Black Panthers for a model of "Neurodivergent Power," we should look to the labor movement—specifically, a modernized labor movement that integrates cognitive and neurological demands into the core of its collective bargaining. The failure of "Liberal Neurodiversity" is that it relies on individual HR accommodations: one worker asking for a sensory room, another asking for noise-canceling headphones. These are individualized, fragile concessions granted by management.

Real power is collective bargaining over the fundamental conditions of work. We need unions to demand an end to algorithmic surveillance. We need contracts that limit open-plan offices and enforce the right to disconnect. We need collective agreements that protect workers from being penalized for variations in affective display—meaning a retail worker shouldn't be fired because their autistic flat affect is scored poorly by a customer satisfaction algorithm.

Furthermore, we must address the gig economy, which functions as a massive repository for neurodivergent individuals who cannot survive the traditional W-2 workplace. Many people with ADHD or autism turn to Uber, DoorDash, or freelance platform work because they need flexible hours and cannot navigate corporate social politics. Yet, this work is precarious, lacks healthcare benefits (crucial for those needing psychiatric medication or therapy), and shifts all the risk onto the worker. Organizing gig workers, classifying them as employees, and securing universal healthcare independent of employment are far more pressing, material goals for the neurodivergent working class than establishing "neurodiversity studies departments in universities"—a goal Chapman champions but which primarily benefits the academic elite.

Chapman’s critique of the ADA's "reasonable adjustments" is spot on; "reasonable" in a capitalist court means "cheap." But the answer is not to abandon the legal framework or wait for a revolution. The answer is to redefine "reasonable" through aggressive labor organizing and legal challenges funded by collective power.

There is also a profound risk in Chapman’s desire to politicize neurodivergent identity to this extreme. As we have seen in recent years, when a specific demographic’s civil rights are explicitly tied to a maximalist left-wing agenda, it invites vicious right-wing backlash, framing basic accommodations as "woke" overreach. While we should not police our demands to appease conservatives, we must be strategic. The demand for a workplace that does not induce autistic burnout, or a school system that does not criminalize ADHD, are broad, majoritarian demands. Many neurotypical people also hate open-plan offices, algorithmic surveillance, and the relentless pacing of modern work. By framing the fight against these conditions as a universal labor struggle led by neurodivergent insights—rather than a vanguardist "Neurodivergent Power" party—we can build actual working-class solidarity.

Chapman is right that liberal reformism is defenseless against elite capture. But ideological maximalism is defenseless against irrelevance and fracture. To build a future where neurodivergent people can thrive, we must focus ruthlessly on the material conditions of labor, technology, and care. We must build unions, not just revolutionary parties, and we must dismantle the specific architectures of modern cognitive exploitation, one algorithmic tracking system at a time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern capitalism disables neurodivergent people through specific architectures like algorithmic management, surveillance, and affective labor requirements that punish cognitive non-compliance.
  2. 02Chapman’s demand for a maximalist, anti-imperialist ideological agenda risks fracturing the movement and inviting political backlash rather than achieving material gains.
  3. 03The movement should shift from seeking individualized HR accommodations to collective bargaining for structural workplace changes, such as ending open-plan offices and algorithmic tracking.
  4. 04Securing material protections for neurodivergent workers in the precarious gig economy and decoupling healthcare from employment are more urgent priorities than establishing academic neurodiversity studies departments.
  5. 05By framing the fight against oppressive modern work conditions as a universal labor struggle, neurodivergent activists can build broader working-class solidarity with neurotypical people who suffer under the same systems.
