"Lowering the voting age to under 18 would likely result in minimal changes to the political landscape because teenagers' voting patterns and campaign interactions would mirror those of the existing electorate."

I doubt that letting teenagers vote would transform presidential politics as much as people imagine. Campaigns are not moral philosophers; they are vote-maximizing machines. If under-18 voters turned out at low rates, candidates would mostly ignore them, just as they often ignore young adults now. If they did vote, campaigns would microtarget them with the same mixture of slogans, fear and flattery used on everyone else.

There is also a tendency to romanticize “the youth vote” as if teenagers all share one ideology. They do not. Some would favor President Obama because of student loans, immigration or gay rights. Others would favor Governor Romney because of religion, taxes, abortion or their parents’ small businesses. Many would simply reflect the political culture of their families and communities. That is not a reason to deny them rights, but it is a reason to be realistic about what would change.

The central issue of 2012 is still the economy. Teenagers care about jobs because they want summer work, future careers and families who are not under constant financial stress. But so does everyone else. Candidates might add more lines about college affordability, school technology and social media freedom, yet the basic campaign would remain about unemployment, health care, taxes and the deficit.

A better reform would be to make the first vote at 18 easier and more meaningful: automatic or same-day registration, stronger civics classes, nonpartisan mock elections and preregistration in high school. The habit of voting matters, but the age line is not the only barrier. If politics seems broken to young people, handing them a ballot earlier will not by itself make them believe the system is worth their trust.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Political campaigns would likely ignore or manipulate teenage voters using the same cynical tactics they apply to current low-turnout demographics.
  2. 02Teenagers do not possess a monolithic ideology and would likely reflect the diverse political divisions already present in their families and communities.
  3. 03The core issues driving teenage voters, such as the economy and jobs, are largely identical to the concerns of the general adult population.
  4. 04Lowering the voting age does not address the underlying cynicism and systemic barriers that discourage young people from trusting the political process.
