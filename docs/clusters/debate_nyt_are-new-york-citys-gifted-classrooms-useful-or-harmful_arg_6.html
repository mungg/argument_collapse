"New York City should preserve its gifted and talented programs to meet the needs of advanced learners, but it must overhaul the admissions process to ensure it is equitable and based on more than a single test for preschoolers."

New York should keep gifted classrooms, but stop treating a single test taken by 4-year-olds as a fair way to decide who deserves them. The problem is not that some children need more advanced work. They do. The problem is that the city’s current system too often confuses early advantage with unusual academic readiness.

In a class of 28 children, the spread of skills can be enormous. Some kindergarteners are sounding out letters; others are already reading chapter books. Some third graders are still mastering multiplication; others are ready for algebraic reasoning. Telling teachers simply to “differentiate” for everyone sounds humane, but it can become a slogan that leaves the fastest learners waiting. Advanced children, including low-income children, need instruction that is paced and pitched for them. Decades of research on acceleration and ability grouping suggest that when advanced students receive appropriately challenging work, they learn more and remain more engaged.

There is also a civic argument for offering these programs in public schools. If the system provides no serious academic options for advanced learners, families with money will buy them privately or leave for selective private schools and suburbs. A strong public system should be able to serve children at both ends of the learning spectrum.

But defending gifted education does not mean defending New York’s admissions machinery. Testing preschoolers rewards families who know the process, can pay for test preparation, and live near programs with available seats. It also produces classrooms whose demographics look very different from the city’s school population. That is not proof that some children are not advanced; it is proof that the city is bad at finding them.

A better system would screen all children during the school day, not only those whose parents sign up. It would use multiple measures: teacher observations, student work, nonverbal assessments, language background, and evidence of rapid learning. It would retest children later, because talent develops and early test scores are unstable. It would use local norms so that high-potential children in every neighborhood can be identified. And it would expand enrichment in ordinary classrooms so that “gifted” does not mean “the only room with exciting work.”

Chancellor Fariña is right that gifted practices should spread: inquiry, rich literature, problem solving, the arts and projects should not be reserved for a few. But “for all” cannot mean “the same for all.” Equity requires both broad enrichment and targeted acceleration. New York’s task is not to abolish gifted classrooms, but to make them less exclusive, more flexible and more educationally honest.
