Diversified Gemini

"Tom Hayden’s celebration of 'participatory democracy' misinterprets modern youth activism, which is driven by dire material and economic desperation rather than the existential self-actualization of the 1960s New Left."

Tom Hayden looks out at the landscape of American political activism in 2012 and sees the vindication of a fifty-year-old ideal. From the DREAMers to the Occupy Wall Street protestors, he perceives the "spirits of the ’60s" and the manifestation of the Port Huron Statement’s call for participatory democracy. While Hayden’s essay is a moving personal reflection from a veteran of the civil rights and anti-war struggles, it ultimately functions as a soothing lullaby for a generation that desperately needs a wake-up call. By casting modern youth movements as the spiritual heirs to the 1960s, Hayden completely misapprehends the terrifyingly different economic reality facing millennials today. We are not the New Left. We are the Lost Generation, and our protests are born not of a desire for self-actualization, but of sheer material desperation.

The Port Huron Statement was a document born of profound privilege. As Hayden and his SDS colleagues gathered in Michigan in 1962, they were standing at the zenith of the post-war American economic boom. They were surrounded by unprecedented middle-class prosperity, secure industrial jobs, a robust social safety net, and a higher education system that was largely affordable or entirely free. Their critique, as articulated in the Statement, was existential. They looked at the comfortable, bureaucratic, suburban life laid out before them and asked: "Is this all there is?" They demanded a society where individuals could be involved in the "creation of ourselves."

For the young people taking to the streets today, that existential angst sounds like a luxury we could never afford. The economic foundation that allowed the boomers to engage in their grand democratic experiment has been systematically dismantled over the last forty years—often under the watch of the very politicians Hayden praises as "pragmatic representatives."

When college students protest escalating tuitions or when undocumented youth fight for the Dream Act, we are not simply exercising a "universal human need" for community. We are fighting for our economic survival. Student loan debt in the United States has just surpassed one trillion dollars, an unfathomable burden chaining an entire generation to perpetual financial anxiety. Youth unemployment is in the double digits. The social contract that promised a better life to those who worked hard and played by the rules has been shredded by Wall Street malfeasance, globalization, and austerity politics.

Hayden looks at Occupy and sees a demand for "direct and transparent participatory democracy." But what drove thousands of people to sleep in freezing parks wasn't just a desire for consensus-based hand signals; it was the fury of seeing the architects of the 2008 financial crisis bailed out with our tax dollars while our friends and families lost their homes and jobs. The core issue isn't procedural; it is material. It is about a savage late-stage capitalism that the Port Huron Statement, for all its radical posturing, never successfully combated.

Hayden’s "inside-outside" strategy—the idea that mass movements push the political establishment to make pragmatic concessions—feels utterly detached from the reality of 2012. We helped elect Barack Obama in 2008 in what Hayden calls a "moving upswell of commitment." We were the outside energy that pushed him inside. And what did we get? No prosecutions of Wall Street bankers, a compromised healthcare bill that entrenched the private insurance industry, the continuation of the Bush tax cuts for the wealthy, and a crushing lack of action on the student debt crisis.

The establishment has not embraced participatory democracy's reforms; it has insulated itself from them entirely through Citizens United and the flood of dark money into our electoral system. The system does not "work" when we win, as Hayden claims. The system absorbs our energy, commodifies our slogans, and leaves the underlying distribution of wealth completely undisturbed.

We respect the elders of the 1960s for their courage in dismantling Jim Crow and ending the draft. But we cannot afford to adopt their theoretical frameworks. The Port Huron model of middle-class moral awakening is insufficient for an era defined by extreme oligarchy and the impending, irreversible catastrophe of global climate change. When Hayden notes that we seek the unattainable to avoid the unimaginable in the fight against global warming, he misses the point that the unimaginable is already happening, and our political institutions are wholly incapable of stopping it.

Participatory democracy is a means, not an end. It is not enough to simply be "involved in the joint-creation of our community" if that community is sinking into poverty and ecological ruin. Millennials do not want merely a seat at the table to participate in a broken system; we want to flip the table entirely. Hayden’s vision of a wide sea with many currents is a beautiful sentiment, but our boat is taking on water fast, and we need a fundamental restructuring of the global economy to survive. That is the manifesto my generation must write.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Port Huron Statement was a product of unprecedented post-war middle-class privilege that allowed for existential critiques, whereas today’s youth face a dismantled social safety net and crushing debt.
  2. 02Modern protests like Occupy Wall Street and tuition strikes are fights for basic economic survival against late-stage capitalism rather than mere demands for procedural democratic participation.
  3. 03The 'inside-outside' strategy of pushing the political establishment has failed millennials, as the system now absorbs activist energy while leaving the underlying distribution of wealth and power undisturbed.
  4. 04The establishment has insulated itself from democratic reform through the influence of dark money and corporate interests, rendering the 1960s model of pragmatic concessions obsolete.
  5. 05Participatory democracy is an insufficient goal for a generation facing extreme oligarchy and ecological collapse, necessitating a fundamental restructuring of the global economy rather than just a seat at the table.
