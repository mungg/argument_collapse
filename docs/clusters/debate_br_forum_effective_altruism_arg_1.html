"While effective altruism’s focus on saving lives is admirable, its reliance on individual philanthropy risks ignoring and even legitimizing the systemic injustices and economic structures that create global poverty and inequality."

Peter Singer’s effective altruism begins with a noble impulse: the conviction that a child’s chance to live should not depend on whether she was born in Princeton, San Francisco, or a malaria zone. That moral universalism is admirable. But Singer’s argument also reveals the danger of a politics reduced to donor choice. If the affluent are invited to “do the most good” mainly by optimizing their charitable spending, the structures that produce both affluence and deprivation may be left intact, and even morally laundered.

The story of Matt Wage is meant to be inspiring. A gifted philosophy student turns away from Oxford and goes to Wall Street so that he can donate large sums to lifesaving charities. Singer presents this as the modern equivalent of rushing into a burning building to save one hundred people. But Wall Street is not an ethically neutral location from which money simply emerges. The financial sector helped produce the crisis of 2008, which cost millions of people their homes and jobs. More broadly, it has become a powerful engine of inequality and political influence. Some firms profit from arbitrage strategies that may be legal and clever but contribute little to human flourishing. Others lobby against regulation, exploit informational advantages, or intensify the short-termism that damages workers and communities.

Perhaps Wage’s particular firm does no such harm. The point is not to condemn one young man. The point is that effective altruism often evaluates the charitable dollar more carefully than the dollar’s origin. It asks, with impressive seriousness, how many bed nets $100,000 can buy. It asks with less seriousness whether the economic arrangements that allowed one young American to accumulate that $100,000 are just, sustainable, or democratically accountable. A morality that counts the lives saved by donations but discounts the social damage created in earning them risks becoming a chaplaincy for inequality.

Singer would reply that effective altruists do care about consequences, including systemic ones. In principle, yes. In practice, the movement’s favored examples—bed nets, deworming, cash transfers—are attractive partly because they are measurable, individualizable, and politically modest. They do not require confronting pharmaceutical monopolies, tax avoidance, trade rules, debt burdens, climate injustice, land dispossession, corrupt elites, or the legacy of colonialism. They let rich-world donors retain their role as choosers while poor people remain recipients. That may produce real benefits, but it is a narrow image of global justice.

The contrast between Make-A-Wish and malaria prevention illustrates both the strength and weakness of Singer’s framework. He is right that a dollar spent saving lives is generally more urgent than a dollar spent producing a beautiful experience for one already-treated child. But why is the choice posed as one donor deciding between two charities? Why are preventable childhood deaths left to philanthropy at all? Malaria control, sanitation, vaccination, and basic health systems are public responsibilities. They require states, taxes, infrastructure, trained workers, and accountable institutions. Charity can fill gaps, but gaps of this magnitude are political failures.

Effective altruism’s impatience with “warm glow giving” is understandable, yet it can slip into disdain for ordinary moral attachments. People give locally not only because they are irrationally biased toward a nearby face, but because they inhabit communities. A union member contributing to a strike fund, a parishioner helping a refugee family, a neighborhood group supporting a clinic, or a citizen donating to a civil rights organization may not have a randomized controlled trial proving the highest global cost-effectiveness. But such acts can build solidarity, power, and democratic capacity. Those goods are harder to quantify than mosquito nets, but they are not therefore less real.

Singer’s treatment of justice, freedom, and equality is also too instrumental. He suggests many effective altruists value them because they help people live better lives. That is partly true. But justice is not simply a technology for producing welfare. It concerns who has power over whom, who must ask for help and who gets to bestow it, who makes the rules, who bears risks, and who captures gains. A society in which billionaires give effectively while workers lack bargaining power and governments lack revenue may contain much generosity and little justice.

None of this means affluent people should stop donating to proven global health interventions. On the contrary, they should give more. If a person can prevent a child’s death by foregoing luxuries, she has a serious moral reason to do so. Singer’s famous pond argument remains compelling. The existence of structural injustice does not excuse indifference to immediate suffering. Children should not die while we wait for a better world order.

But the reverse is also true: the urgency of immediate suffering should not excuse indifference to structure. Effective altruists should expand their moral accounting to include political work: tax justice, climate policy, labor rights, campaign finance reform, access to medicines, and support for public health systems. These efforts are uncertain and difficult to measure, but uncertainty is not the same as ineffectiveness. Many of the greatest moral advances—abolition, women’s suffrage, civil rights, decolonization—would have looked speculative to a donor demanding near-term measurable outcomes.

Singer has given affluent people a bracing question: how can I use my resources to do the most good? The answer cannot be only, or even mainly, “become a better philanthropist.” It must also be: become a better citizen, a better worker, a better participant in movements that change the rules by which resources are produced and distributed. Charity can save lives. Justice asks why those lives were left to charity in the first place.

"While Peter Singer’s effective altruism provides a necessary critique of philanthropic complacency, its narrow focus on measurable 'emergency moral arithmetic' risks devaluing the pluralistic cultural and civic institutions essential for long-term human flourishing."

Peter Singer’s essay is a bracing attack on philanthropic complacency, but it also expresses a characteristically modern temptation: the wish to reduce the plurality of human goods to a single scale. “Doing the most good” sounds unassailable until one asks what kind of good, for whom, over what time horizon, and within what form of life. Singer’s examples are carefully chosen, and in many of them his conclusion is plainly right. But a society guided too narrowly by his reasoning would risk starving institutions and practices whose value is real precisely because it cannot be captured by emergency moral arithmetic.

Begin where Singer is strongest. Gifts to wealthy universities and elite cultural institutions deserve scrutiny. When an alumnus gives millions to a university with a multibillion-dollar endowment, one may reasonably suspect that gratitude, status, nostalgia, or naming rights are doing more work than justice. Likewise, a $100 million gift to renovate a concert hall in New York is hard to defend as the best use of money in a world of preventable disease. Singer’s question—what else could this money have done?—should haunt every donor wall.

But the conclusion should not be that art, universities, libraries, parks, local theaters, and museums are luxuries to be considered only after extreme poverty has vanished. Human beings do not live by survival alone. We live in languages, histories, disciplines, neighborhoods, rituals, and shared spaces. The arts enlarge imagination; universities preserve and generate knowledge; local institutions knit citizens together; monuments and museums help societies remember and argue about their past. These goods can be abused by elites, but they are not mere ornaments.

Singer says that in a world that had overcome major problems, promoting the arts would be worthy. This sets the bar too high. The world will always contain urgent suffering. If every cultural expenditure must wait until no child is dying from preventable disease, culture will always lose. The same logic could apply to almost anything not directly lifesaving: basic research in mathematics, preservation of endangered languages, public broadcasting, historical archives, botanical gardens, even some forms of education. Yet many such activities contribute to human flourishing in ways that are diffuse and intergenerational.

The issue is not whether saving a life is more important than improving an opera hall. In a direct choice, it usually is. The issue is whether all moral decisions should be modeled as direct choices between marginal dollars. Societies require portfolios of value. We need emergency relief and public health, but also courts, schools, art, science, journalism, democratic forums, and spaces of beauty. Some of these will never rank well in a cost-per-life-saved comparison, because that is not what they are for.

Effective altruism may respond that individual donors in affluent societies should prioritize the most urgent needs precisely because other institutions already fund culture and education. Sometimes this is true. But donor behavior also shapes public culture. If the language of “ineffectiveness” spreads too broadly, fragile local institutions may suffer while only the wealthiest museums and universities survive through endowments and elite patronage. A community arts center serving immigrants, a historically black college, a rural library, or a prison education program is not equivalent to a vanity gift to an Ivy League university. Singer’s categories need finer distinctions.

There is also a democratic concern. Effective altruism often places moral authority in the hands of those who can compare outcomes across the world: evaluators, philosophers, economists, and unusually conscientious donors. Their work is valuable. But democracy depends on citizens deliberating together about common goods, not only on donors optimizing private transfers. When public goods are funded by philanthropy, even effective philanthropy, priorities may reflect the judgments of the wealthy rather than the community. A billionaire who funds malaria prevention may do great good. A billionaire who decides the future of education reform, urban policy, or scientific research may exercise power that ought to be publicly accountable.

Singer’s framework also underplays uncertainty about long-run cultural effects. The university he criticizes may produce the scientist who develops a vaccine, the judge who defends constitutional rights, or the writer who changes public imagination. Of course, Princeton and Yale do not need marginal gifts as urgently as poor children need health interventions. But the general point matters: institutions that cultivate knowledge and creativity may have vast, unpredictable benefits. Their value cannot be dismissed because it resists measurement.

A better approach would distinguish between moral emergencies and sustaining goods. Extreme poverty, preventable disease, and mass suffering create urgent claims on affluent people. These claims should command a significant share of our resources, far more than they currently do. Singer is right that giving almost nothing to the world’s poorest while funding luxuries is indefensible. But once we recognize those urgent claims, we should not conclude that every other good is morally suspect. We should ask how to sustain a rich ecology of institutions while correcting grotesque imbalances.

For an individual donor, this may mean giving a large portion—perhaps the largest portion—to highly effective poverty or health interventions, while also supporting local and cultural institutions that serve genuine public purposes. It may mean refusing prestige philanthropy while defending public funding for the arts and universities. It may mean evaluating cultural gifts not by lives saved, but by access, inclusion, civic value, and intellectual contribution.

Singer’s essay is a needed rebuke to self-flattering generosity. But moral seriousness should not become moral monism. The good is plural. Lives must be saved; suffering must be reduced; distant strangers must count. Yet we must also build and preserve the forms of common life that make a world worth living in. Effective altruism can help us see urgent need. It should not be allowed to define the whole horizon of value.

**The Bed Net Is Not Enough**

Peter Singer's case for effective altruism rests on a vivid moral image: a young man on Wall Street who, by donating half his salary to the Against Malaria Foundation, will save more lives than a firefighter could pull from a burning building. It's a powerful picture, and like all powerful pictures it deserves scrutiny. As someone who has spent years in development work, my worry about effective altruism is not its altruism but its theory of what makes interventions effective.

The bed net is the movement's totem because GiveWell can put a price on a life saved with reasonable confidence. But the very features that make bed nets evaluable — discreteness, short causal chains, easily counted outputs — also mark out a narrow slice of what actually drives human development. The dramatic reductions in child mortality over the past forty years owe more to broad processes: rising incomes, female education, urbanization, vaccine programs run by national health ministries, and the slow grind of state capacity building. None of these would have scored well on a 2007 GiveWell scorecard. Indeed, programs like oral rehydration therapy, which has saved tens of millions, looked unimpressive in pilot RCTs before national systems took them up.

Singer concedes that effective altruists debate cause prioritization, and he gestures toward "systemic change" being part of the conversation. But the movement's epistemology has a built-in tilt. When the test for a good intervention is "evidence and reason" operationalized as randomized trials and cost-per-DALY calculations, you end up favoring whatever can be measured cleanly over what cannot. Funding a malaria net is measurable. Funding a campaign to reform a country's tax-collection capacity, or to support a journalists' union, or to push rich-country governments to change agricultural subsidies that depress African farm incomes, is not. The latter may matter more. We don't know — and that is precisely the problem. "Don't know" gets coded as "don't fund."

There is a deeper issue. Singer presents the choice as Make-A-Wish versus the Against Malaria Foundation, framed so that any reasonable person must pick AMF. But the truly contested comparison in global poverty is not Batkid versus bed nets; it is bed nets versus, say, GiveDirectly's cash transfers, or versus advocacy for migration liberalization (which Michael Clemens has estimated could generate trillions in welfare gains), or versus investment in long-run scientific research. On these comparisons, effective altruism's confident hierarchies fray fast. A movement that markets itself on the certainty of "doing the most good" is forced, in practice, to admit it is making bets under deep uncertainty — bets shaped as much by what is legible to spreadsheets as by what is actually consequential.

I also want to register a worry about the "earning to give" pathway that Matt Wage embodies. Singer treats it as a near-miraculous arbitrage: take a high-paying job, donate the surplus, multiply your impact. The argument assumes the financial-sector job is morally neutral — that Wage's arbitrage trading itself does no harm and displaces no good. But finance is not a neutral conduit. Some activity in those firms is socially valuable; some is rent extraction; some, as we saw in 2008, can metastasize into systemic harm. If we are being rigorous about consequences, we have to count the expected impact of the work itself, not just the donations it funds. The fact that effective altruists rarely run those numbers suggests the framework's quantification has limits it doesn't acknowledge.

None of this is a reason to dismiss effective altruism. Singer is right that the average American giver donates emotionally, that charity is under-evaluated, that GiveWell has done real work in pulling tens of millions of dollars toward demonstrably effective programs. A movement of young people willing to give 10 or 50 percent of their income is a genuinely heartening development in a culture that more often celebrates conspicuous consumption.

But the movement would be stronger if it were humbler. The right slogan is not "do the most good you can" — a phrase that promises an optimization most of us cannot perform — but something closer to "give more, give thoughtfully, and stay open to evidence that the leaderboard is wrong." The danger of Singer's framing is that it converts ethics into a problem of execution rather than of judgment. Real moral life, including real philanthropy, requires judgment in the face of irreducible uncertainty about what helps and what hurts. Bed nets are good. They are not the whole of doing good.

"While effective altruism provides a vital, evidence-based corrective to sentimental and ineffective philanthropy, it must evolve to account for the systemic harms of certain careers and incorporate a stronger respect for individual rights and human dignity."

Peter Singer’s essay has the rare merit of making a moral claim that is both radical and immediately actionable. One need not wait for Congress, a revolution, or the purification of one’s soul. One can, this afternoon, move money from a low-impact use to a high-impact use. In a world where children still die of malaria, diarrhea, measles, and other preventable causes, that matters. Singer’s central point—that affluent people ought to ask not merely whether their giving expresses concern, but whether it actually helps—is difficult to reject without retreating into sentimentality.

The most powerful part of the effective altruist argument is not that it has solved every problem in moral philosophy. It has not. The most powerful part is its comparative discipline. If $7,500 can either provide a magical day for one sick child in San Francisco or prevent several children from dying of malaria, the burden of justification falls on those who choose the wish. It is not enough to say that Batkid moved us, or that the child was visible, nearby, and charming. Those facts explain the donation; they do not justify it.

This distinction between explanation and justification is where effective altruism performs a public service. Most charitable giving in affluent societies follows psychological grooves laid down by proximity, identity, storytelling, and institutional habit. Alumni give to already wealthy universities. Museum patrons give to museums they enjoy attending. Disaster relief surges after televised catastrophes and collapses when the cameras leave. None of this proves that these gifts are worthless. But it does show that our giving often resembles consumption: we buy the moral experience we want. Effective altruism asks us to treat beneficiaries, not donors, as the center of the transaction.

Singer is also right to resist the charge that effective altruism must be joyless. One of the oddest objections to utilitarian giving is that it drains life of meaning. In fact, many people find modern affluence draining precisely because it gives them so few purposes beyond acquisition, display, and private comfort. The decision to give substantially and intelligently can be liberating. It converts a salary into a tool. It makes ordinary professional life part of a larger project. The young graduate who donates half his Wall Street income may be less trapped by money than the lawyer or professor who earns less but spends every marginal dollar preserving a status position.

Still, the example of Matt Wage also points to the most serious internal challenge for effective altruism: the difference between doing good with money and doing good through institutions. Earning to give is morally attractive when the work itself is neutral or only mildly regrettable. But not all high-paying work is neutral. Finance can allocate capital productively; it can also extract rents, increase systemic risk, and encourage talented people to design socially useless instruments. A person who earns a fortune by producing harm and donates a fraction to repair the damage cannot claim victory simply because the donated dollars are measurable. Effective altruism needs a rigorous account of the negative externalities of careers, not only the positive externalities of donations.

That is not a refutation. It is a demand that effective altruism apply its own standards consistently. If a hedge fund job indirectly worsens market instability or political inequality, those effects should count. If a pharmaceutical career helps bring useful drugs to market, that should count too. If a civil servant improves vaccine delivery for millions, the value of that career may exceed anything she could donate from a higher salary elsewhere. “Earning to give” should be one option in a broader menu of high-impact lives, not the movement’s signature myth.

Singer’s discussion of rights and rules is reassuring but too brief. He says effective altruists know that violating basic moral constraints will “almost always” have worse consequences. That is true, but it understates the reason many of us prize rights. Rights are not merely useful rules of thumb that happen to maximize welfare most of the time. They also protect persons from being treated as raw material for other people’s projects. Effective altruism will be stronger if it can speak fluently in the language of dignity, consent, and democratic equality, not only in the language of aggregate benefit.

Yet this criticism should not obscure the achievement. Effective altruism has already changed the moral conversation. It has made it harder for donors, foundations, and universities to speak as though every cause were equally urgent. It has exposed the evasiveness of “follow your passion” philanthropy. Passion is not a moral compass; it is, at best, fuel. The compass must be evidence, impartial concern, and a willingness to change one’s mind.

Singer’s essay should be read less as a complete ethical system than as a corrective to complacency. It reminds affluent people that our ordinary decency is often compatible with preventable death. We can pay our taxes, love our children, refrain from cruelty, and still ignore opportunities to save lives at little cost to ourselves. Effective altruism gives that uncomfortable fact a practical form. We do not have to become saints. But we do have to stop pretending that all generosity is morally equal. Some gifts save lives. Some mostly flatter the giver. The difference is real, and it is our responsibility to know it.

"While effective altruism provides a necessary, evidence-based framework for philanthropy, it must be expanded to value systemic, long-term, and complex interventions that are difficult to quantify but essential for human flourishing."

As someone sympathetic to the demand for evidence in philanthropy, I welcome Peter Singer’s essay. For too long, charitable giving has been insulated from the standards we apply elsewhere. We would not buy medicine merely because an advertisement moved us. We would not invest retirement savings in a company that refused to disclose its performance. Yet donors routinely give to organizations with little idea whether their programs work. Singer and the effective altruists are right to insist that good intentions are not enough.

Still, the phrase “doing the most good” can create a misleading impression of precision. In some domains we have relatively strong evidence. Insecticide-treated bed nets reduce malaria transmission. Vaccination saves lives. Some deworming programs appear to improve health and school attendance, though the broader long-term effects remain debated. Cash transfers can increase consumption and give poor households flexibility. Randomized controlled trials and careful cost-effectiveness analysis have improved our understanding of such interventions.

But philanthropy is not medicine alone. Much of what people reasonably value—legal empowerment, women’s rights, investigative journalism, government accountability, institution building, peacebuilding, climate mitigation—is difficult to evaluate in the same way. A campaign to change a law may fail for ten years and then suddenly transform millions of lives. A watchdog organization may prevent abuses that never appear in statistics because they did not happen. A community organizer may build trust that later becomes political capacity. If our evidentiary standards privilege interventions with easily counted outputs, we may systematically underfund work whose impact is indirect, delayed, or collective.

This is not an argument for abandoning measurement. It is an argument for matching methods to questions. The effective altruist movement has sometimes been caricatured as believing only randomized trials count. That is unfair; many effective altruists are more sophisticated than their critics. But the public examples—bed nets, cost per life saved, dollars per disability-adjusted life year—encourage a narrow view. Donors attracted by Singer’s essay may conclude that the only responsible gifts are those blessed by a small number of evaluators. That would be unfortunate.

Even in global health, cost-effectiveness estimates are not as simple as they look. The cost “to save a life” depends on assumptions about disease prevalence, proper use of bed nets, local health systems, distribution costs, insecticide resistance, and what would have happened without the donor’s gift. Charities can absorb only so much money at a given level of effectiveness. A program that looks excellent at $5 million may look weaker at $50 million. Conversely, an organization that appears less efficient may be working in harder places or serving populations others neglect.

There is also the problem of comparability. Singer asks us to recognize that saving a life is better than granting a wish. In that case, comparison is easy. But many real choices are not. How should we compare preventing a child’s death with reducing severe depression, preserving a forest, protecting a dissident newspaper, preventing animal suffering, or reducing the risk of catastrophic climate change? Effective altruism does not eliminate moral judgment; it relocates it. The weights assigned to different outcomes are not delivered by data. They come from ethical commitments.

The best version of effective altruism is therefore not a calculator that issues final answers. It is a disciplined practice of inquiry. It asks donors to be explicit about goals, to seek the best available evidence, to consider opportunity costs, to update beliefs, and to take seriously the equal worth of distant people. This is a profound improvement over “follow your passion.” Passion can help choose a field of concern, but it cannot be the final test of whether one is helping.

Singer’s criticism of large gifts to wealthy universities and cultural institutions is especially apt. When Princeton and Yale have endowments measured in tens of billions, alumni should ask what their next dollar actually accomplishes. The same question applies to hospitals that put donors’ names on buildings while basic public health remains underfunded. The problem is not that education or art lacks value. It is that marginal donations to elite institutions often buy prestige more than impact.

Where Singer is less satisfying is in his confidence that effective altruism can tell us what a “minimally acceptable ethical life” requires. I share his view that affluent people should give substantially more than many now do. But the ethical life includes more than donation. It includes how we vote, work, consume, parent, invest, and participate in institutions. A person might give 20 percent of income to excellent charities while working for a company that pollutes, exploits, or corrupts. Another might give little money but spend her life reforming a public agency that serves millions. Measurement must not tempt us to count only what comes with receipts.

What should donors do now? First, give more than they are comfortable giving, especially if they are affluent. Second, move a significant portion of their giving toward high-confidence, high-impact interventions benefiting the very poor. Third, reserve some giving for riskier systemic work, while demanding plausible theories of change rather than sentimental appeals. Fourth, support better evaluation itself, including evaluation of advocacy and institutional reform. Finally, remain humble. Evidence does not free us from uncertainty; it teaches us to navigate uncertainty responsibly.

Singer has done philanthropy a service by challenging donors to ask whether their generosity works. The next step is to ensure that “works” is understood broadly enough to include not only what is easily measured, but also what is essential to human flourishing and justice.

Effective altruism is undoubtedly a step forward from giving based entirely on emotional resonance. Buying bed nets is a good and necessary thing to do. They must also engage in the far more difficult, unquantifiable work of reforming the economic and political structures that make such charity necessary in the first place.
