"While a wealth tax is a useful starting point, it is an insufficient technocratic fix that fails to dismantle the underlying power structures of the superrich and the private ownership of essential social assets."

Saez and Zucman’s essay arrives at a peculiar moment: a public-health emergency is exposing the rottenness of a state that has been starved for decades, while the stock market, in its last days of irrational buoyancy before the pandemic fully hits Main Street, still bestows hundreds of billions on the same class that looted the treasury in the first place. Their wealth tax proposal is elegant, well-calibrated, and politically timely. And yet, if we are serious about dismantling the power of the superrich, a tax—even a radical one—will not be enough. It is a liberal bandage on a gangrenous limb, a reform that leaves the underlying machine of wealth extraction intact.

The authors themselves acknowledge the heart of the problem: “Wealth is power.” In a capitalist economy, owning a great deal of property confers command over investment, employment, and the direction of innovation. It gives a tiny minority the ability to decide what gets produced, where, and for whom. A wealth tax would certainly reduce the yachts and private jets, and over time it would shrink the largest fortunes, but it would not alter the basic fact that a handful of asset managers and inheritors control the productive apparatus of society. Those who still hold a billion dollars after the tax—and even under the radical proposal, someone worth ten billion would keep nine—would continue to exercise disproportionate sway over the economy and the state. The tax nibbles at the edges of plutocracy; it does not abolish it.

The deeper question is not how to tax wealth but why we allow such wealth to be privately held in the first place. The fortunes of a Bezos or a Zuckerberg are not the result of isolated genius. They are built on public investment: Internet protocols developed with government grants, logistical infrastructure using publicly maintained roads and ports, a workforce educated in public schools, and a legal system that enforces intellectual property and limited liability. The returns on that collective investment have been captured almost entirely by private shareholders. A just society would not merely tax a fraction of those returns; it would socialize the ownership of the underlying assets.

Consider what a genuinely radical program would entail. Instead of asking the IRS to value Cargill and take two percent in shares, the government could take a controlling equity stake in every firm above a certain size, placing voting shares in a social wealth fund whose dividends would be distributed equally to all citizens. That would convert the passive wealth of the rich into collective property without the elaborate apparatus of appraisal, liquidity, and in-kind payment that the authors propose. Worker cooperatives, not billionaire owners, could govern the firms they work for, breaking the link between capital ownership and managerial authority. Antitrust enforcement could be used not to promote “competition” within the same corporate structure but to break up firms into federations of smaller, more accountable economic units.

The wealth tax, for all its merits, is a technocratic fix that accepts the existing distribution of assets as a given. It says: fine, the billionaires can keep their empires, as long as they pay a toll. But history shows that tolls can be negotiated down, loopholes can be carved out, and the political influence that survives the tax will be deployed to hollow it out over time. Already, the presidential candidates who back a wealth tax have exempted vast swaths of wealth—pension holdings, middle-class housing—and have built in administrative flexibility that future Congresses could exploit to grant exemptions for “job-creating” investments or “vital” industries. A decade after its enactment, a wealth tax could easily come to resemble the current estate tax: full of holes, resented but ineffective, a symbol of progressive intent more than a tool of redistribution.

The authors are honest that a radical wealth tax would eventually reduce its own revenue base by shrinking the taxable fortunes. Yet they treat this as a feature, not a bug. I agree, but I would go further. If the goal is to deconcentrate wealth, why stop at a tax? Why not set a maximum limit on the total wealth any individual can own, as some ancient democracies and pre-modern republics did? A simple law that no person may possess assets of more than, say, fifty million dollars—with the excess escheated to a public trust—would be simpler to enforce, harder to evade, and would eliminate the billionaire class altogether. In one stroke it would restore the “state of mediocrity” that Madison praised. This is not a fantasy; it is a logical extension of the principle that extreme wealth is incompatible with self-government.

Critics will scream that such a limit would destroy incentives and tank investment. But the experience of the mid-twentieth century, which the authors rightly invoke, suggests otherwise. It was not the ninety-percent marginal tax rate alone that tamed the plutocrats; it was a combination of taxation, strong unions, direct public investment in housing and education, and a political culture that was deeply suspicious of great fortunes. The wealthy still existed, but they were forced to operate within social constraints that have since been dismantled. Restoring those constraints requires more than a new line on the Form 1040. It requires reviving the labor movement, ending shareholder primacy in corporate law, and democratizing the Federal Reserve’s lending facilities. In the current crisis, as trillions are being created to bail out financial institutions, the real question is not whether the rich should pay more tax next year. It is who will own the economy after the dust settles. If we accept the framework of Saez and Zucman, we will haggle over rates and thresholds. But if we want to break the link between wealth and power, we must be willing to think beyond the tax code entirely.

The authors have given us a lever; I am glad to have it. But a lever by itself cannot move the world. We need a fulcrum, and that fulcrum must be a mass movement that demands not only that the rich pay more, but that they cease to exist as a class with dominion over our common life. The wealth tax is a worthy opening bid. It should not be the final demand.
