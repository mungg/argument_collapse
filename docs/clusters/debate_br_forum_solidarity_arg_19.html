"To effectively organize the professional-managerial class (PMC) for solidarity, we must move beyond using the term as a catchall insult and instead employ a precise class analysis that distinguishes between those with institutional power and those undergoing proletarianization."

Mie Inouye’s essay turns on a dilemma posed by the professional-managerial class: if white PMC liberals are among the constituencies most available to movements for racial justice, how should organizers work with their contradictions? This is the right question, but the category “PMC” may obscure as much as it reveals. If solidarity requires people to know what they want and why they are in the room, then we need a more precise account of class than the current shorthand often provides.

The term PMC, popularized by Barbara and John Ehrenreich in the late 1970s, was meant to identify salaried professionals whose role in capitalist society involves managing, administering, designing, teaching, supervising, and reproducing labor power. It captured a real class location: neither capitalist owners nor traditional workers, often possessing credentials and autonomy, often committed to meritocratic liberalism, often positioned to discipline other workers. In recent debates, however, “PMC” has become a catchall insult for educated liberals, nonprofit staff, academics, journalists, tech workers, teachers, nurses, graduate students, social workers, and anyone who uses the wrong jargon. As a result, it can flatten major differences in power.

A tenured professor, an adjunct teaching five courses, a public school teacher, a hospital nurse, a DEI consultant, a software engineer, a nonprofit caseworker, a medical resident, a middle manager at Amazon, and a graduate worker may all be called PMC. But their relationship to capital, authority, job security, and exploitation differs enormously. Some supervise; some are supervised relentlessly. Some own homes and retirement accounts; others carry debt and pay half their income in rent. Some enforce institutional policies; others are crushed by them. Some have cultural capital but little economic security. If we treat them all as one bloc, we will misorganize them.

Inouye is interested in the relatively privileged white PMC whose antiracist commitments may remain symbolic because material interests diverge from transformative redistribution. That figure exists. Homeowning professionals may support racial justice signs while opposing affordable housing. University administrators may speak the language of inclusion while union-busting campus workers. Lawyers and consultants may turn diversity into a career. Parents with graduate degrees may defend school systems that hoard opportunity. Any serious left politics must confront this.

But another story is unfolding at the same time: the proletarianization of many professional and semi-professional workers. Teachers strike because they are underpaid and disrespected. Nurses organize against unsafe staffing and hospital corporatization. Graduate students unionize because stipends do not cover rent and universities depend on their labor. Journalists, museum workers, nonprofit employees, public defenders, and digital media workers unionize against precarity. Tech workers confront layoffs, surveillance, and ethical conflicts over the uses of their labor. These workers may possess degrees, but credentials no longer guarantee autonomy or security.

This matters for solidarity because people’s stakes are not only ideological. A nurse’s interest in Medicare for All may involve patient care, staffing, wages, and moral distress. A teacher’s interest in racial justice may involve school funding, segregation, curriculum, and working conditions. A graduate worker’s interest in unionization may involve rent, health care, harassment protections, and visa precarity. These are not merely privileged people seeking absolution through politics. They are workers whose contradictory class location can become a site of organizing.

The labor upsurge in higher education is especially instructive. Universities are often caricatured as factories of deference politics, and not without reason. Academic spaces can turn identity into currency and conflict into performance. Yet campuses are also workplaces where custodians, dining workers, clerical staff, adjuncts, graduate workers, researchers, nurses, and faculty are linked by institutional budgets and hierarchies. A campus labor coalition can force professional workers to confront their relationship to lower-paid staff, and it can force staff unions to engage questions of race, gender, immigration, and academic labor. The shared employer provides an antagonist that pure discourse lacks.

Inouye’s own position as a professor is relevant. She worries that she organizes because she needs meetings, a confession that Henry’s material answer chastens. But professors also have workplaces, bosses, students, budgets, and institutional power. The question is not whether professors’ motives are pure. It is whether they can organize where they stand: support staff unions, fight adjunctification, protect students from debt and policing, resist donor control, democratize governance, and use research in service of movements. The PMC problem is not solved by self-suspicion; it is addressed by workplace politics.

The critique of deference politics may look different in such settings. In a university, “centering marginalized voices” can indeed become a substitute for redistributing resources. Administrators are adept at hosting listening sessions while raising tuition and suppressing unions. But the answer is not to abandon standpoint insights. It is to connect them to material demands. If first-generation students describe food insecurity, the demand is not merely to honor their narratives; it is to fund dining access, housing, emergency grants, and debt relief. If Black staff describe discrimination, the demand is not another climate survey alone; it is promotion pathways, pay equity, grievance procedures, and changes to supervisory power. If disabled students describe exclusion, the demand is staffing and enforcement for accommodations, accessible buildings, remote options, and curricular flexibility.

This is the bridge between epistemic humility and material politics. Lived experience identifies problems that institutional metrics hide. Organization turns those problems into demands. Power determines whether demands are met.

The PMC also raises questions about leadership. Professional-class activists often have skills movements need: writing, research, law, data analysis, media, teaching, grant writing, technical knowledge. They also often have habits that movements do not need: domination through language, impatience with those who lack credentials, conflict avoidance masked as process, and a tendency to convert politics into personal morality. Solidarity does not require such people to pretend they have no skills. It requires them to put those skills under democratic discipline.

This is different from both deference and self-assertion. A lawyer in a tenant campaign should not decide strategy simply because she understands housing court. Nor should she withhold her legal knowledge in fear of being elitist. She should explain options clearly, answer questions, and accept that tenants may choose a path involving risks she would not choose. A data analyst in a union campaign should not reduce workers’ experience to spreadsheets; nor should the campaign ignore useful data. A professor should not turn every meeting into a seminar; nor should movements disdain study. The issue is not whether expertise is allowed, but who governs it.

Inouye’s “social endurance” is important because cross-class organizing among professionals and nonprofessionals is full of irritation. Professionals may be irritated by what they perceive as inefficiency or imprecision. Nonprofessionals may be irritated by jargon, condescension, and endless process. Professionals may want policies; others may want immediate relief. Professionals may fear reputational risk; others may face bodily or economic risk. These tensions cannot be wished away. They can be worked through only in organizations with clear purposes and accountability.

But endurance alone will not resolve the structural incentives that pull professionals away from solidarity. Universities reward individual publication, not collective action. Nonprofits reward grant deliverables, not base-building. Media rewards hot takes, not loyalty. Professions reward credentialed authority, not democratic humility. To organize the PMC, movements must create counter-incentives: unions, caucuses, professional associations with teeth, ethical pledges tied to collective enforcement, strike funds, and career protections for dissenters. People are braver when they are not alone.

We should also be careful about opposing the PMC to the “traditional working class” too starkly. The contemporary working class includes care workers, teachers, nurses, warehouse workers, call center workers, delivery drivers, coders, adjuncts, retail workers, home health aides, and public employees. It is multiracial, feminized in many sectors, immigrant, indebted, and often credentialed. Nostalgia for a male industrial worker as the authentic subject of class politics can obscure where exploitation now occurs. Conversely, celebrating professional precarity without acknowledging relative privilege can obscure the harsher conditions faced by those without credentials or citizenship.

A renewed labor politics can cut through these confusions. When workers organize around a common employer or industry, class becomes concrete. The question is not “Are you PMC?” in the abstract. It is: do you have power over other workers? Who controls your labor? What risks can you take? What skills can you contribute? Whose work makes your work possible? What would it mean to refuse management’s attempt to divide you?

Inouye’s essay asks us to build organizations where people can uncover needs and forge demands across difference. To do that, we need class analysis that is sharp enough to identify contradictions but flexible enough to organize through them. The PMC is not simply the obstacle to solidarity, nor is it the vanguard. It is a terrain of struggle. Some of its members will defend hierarchy. Some will drift with institutional incentives. Some can be organized as workers and as comrades.

The point is not to absolve professionals. It is to give them something more useful to do than confess.
