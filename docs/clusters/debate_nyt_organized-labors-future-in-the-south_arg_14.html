"To successfully organize Southern factories, labor unions must overcome deep-seated racial polarization by building genuine interracial solidarity and explicitly addressing how race is used to divide workers."

In the South, race remains an undercurrent in every union election, and Chattanooga was no exception. The UAW has long positioned itself as a champion of civil rights, and many black workers in the plant were receptive to the union’s message. But for many white workers, especially those from rural backgrounds, the union evoked fears of racial integration and a loss of workplace status. The anti-union campaign played on these fears subtly: warning that the UAW would bring “Chicago-style politics” or that union dues would fund liberal causes. Such dog whistles are potent in a region where racial polarization still runs deep.

Historically, Southern unions have struggled to build interracial solidarity. The textile mills of the Piedmont were organized only after decades of effort, and often segregated. In the auto industry, the UAW made progress in the North, but its Southern organizing attempts in the 1940s and 1950s failed in part because of white workers’ resistance to joining a union that would bargain for black workers equally.

Today, the workforce at Southern auto plants is racially diverse. The UAW must explicitly address racial divisions and build a coalition that bridges them. That requires more than colorblind economic appeals; it means confronting the ways that employers use race to divide workers and listening to black workers’ specific concerns without taking their support for granted. The good news is that demographics are slowly shifting: a younger, more diverse workforce may be less susceptible to old racial appeals. But overcoming the racial barrier requires a sustained commitment to organizing communities with a clear anti-racist stance that is credible to all workers.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Anti-union campaigns in the South successfully exploit racial fears and status anxieties among white workers through coded political language.
  2. 02The historical failure of Southern labor movements is rooted in the resistance of white workers to join unions that advocate for racial equality.
  3. 03Economic appeals alone are insufficient to bridge racial divides, requiring unions to adopt a clear anti-racist stance that addresses the specific concerns of black workers.
  4. 04Shifting demographics and a younger, more diverse workforce provide a potential opportunity for unions to move past traditional racial appeals.
