"The focus of feminist critique and policy should shift from blaming mothers for 'over-caring' to addressing the systemic failure of fathers to perform their fair share of domestic and caregiving labor."

The title “Mothers Who Care Too Much” captures attention, but the more accurate title might be “Fathers Who Care Too Little.” Nancy Hirschmann’s frustration is aimed at women who embrace stay-at-home motherhood, at care feminists who have overpraised maternal labor, and at mothers who use care as an excuse for entitlement. Yet in nearly every problem she describes, men are the missing beneficiaries. Men keep careers, names, authority, leisure, and the presumption that their time is valuable. Women are then urged to solve the resulting inequality by caring less, working more, bargaining harder, and refusing the feminine role.

That advice is not wrong. But it risks repeating the pattern it criticizes: women are again made responsible for fixing gender. If men are not doing their share, then the political and cultural spotlight should fall on men’s failure, not primarily on women’s overinvestment.

The evidence Hirschmann cites is familiar and stubborn. Married women do far more housework and childcare than married men. Mothers are penalized at work; fathers often receive a wage and status premium. Men who take caregiving seriously risk stigma. Family-friendly policies go unused by men. Academic fathers may benefit from parental leave while academic mothers use it for actual care. All of this shows that the central obstacle is not an excess of maternal feeling but the continuing organization of masculinity around exemption from dependency work.

We need a feminism that treats fatherhood as a site of political struggle. For too long, public policy has imagined fathers mainly as breadwinners or child-support payers. Conservative family rhetoric praises “responsible fathers,” but responsibility usually means marriage, wages, and discipline, not cleaning vomit at 2 a.m., arranging childcare, remembering shoe sizes, or taking a career hit for a child. Liberal workplace policy often uses gender-neutral language, but in practice “parental” accommodations become maternal accommodations because workplaces still presume that serious men have wives or substitutes handling home.

Changing this requires more than exhortation. Hirschmann is skeptical of state and employer policy because such policies can leave men unchanged. But the right policies can target men directly. The most promising examples are nontransferable paid leave reserved for fathers—what some European countries call “daddy quotas.” If a family loses the leave unless the father takes it, the policy changes the default. It signals that infant care is not merely a mother’s entitlement but a father’s obligation and right. It also gives individual men cover against workplace stigma: they are not making an eccentric personal request; they are using a standard social provision.

The United States is far from such a system. The Family and Medical Leave Act provides only unpaid leave and covers many but not all workers. Unpaid leave is least useful to those who most need support. Employers may offer paid maternity leave or flexible arrangements to professionals, but these benefits often reinforce class privilege and maternal specialization. A serious fatherhood policy would be universal, paid, and structured to prevent transfer to mothers. It would include job protection, wage replacement, and cultural campaigns presenting fathers as competent caregivers rather than assistants.

Of course policy cannot by itself produce equality. Cuba’s Family Code, as Hirschmann notes, did not magically equalize housework. But the failure of command does not prove the futility of structure. Laws against sex discrimination did not eliminate sexism; they still changed expectations, provided tools, and shifted legitimacy. Paid father leave would not end patriarchy, but it would alter the bargaining terrain inside households and the assumptions of employers. If every male employee of childbearing age is a potential leave-taker, employers have less reason to treat women as uniquely risky hires.

Work hours matter too. American professional culture often treats long hours as proof of commitment, even when productivity does not require them. Working-class jobs may impose unpredictability rather than overwork: rotating shifts, mandatory overtime, last-minute scheduling. Both patterns undermine caregiving. If fathers are to care, they need time. That means shorter standard hours, predictable schedules, limits on mandatory overtime, and benefits that attach to part-time work. Otherwise “shared parenting” will remain a slogan for families with unusual resources or unusually egalitarian men.

Culture is equally important. Boys should grow up expecting to care for younger siblings, cook, clean, and comfort. Schools can teach domestic competence to all children. Pediatricians, teachers, and coaches can call fathers as readily as mothers. Religious communities and civic organizations can honor caregiving men without treating them as heroes for ordinary work. Media images matter: the bumbling dad is not harmless comedy when it reinforces the idea that fathers are lovable incompetents.

But women’s choices also matter, and here Hirschmann’s challenge is useful. Many heterosexual couples slide into unequal arrangements early, sometimes before children. The man’s job is treated as more serious; the woman manages social life; her flexibility becomes the family’s buffer. After childbirth, biology gives this pattern a push—pregnancy, birth, breastfeeding—but biology does not require years of female domestic management. Women who want equality should negotiate before crisis: whose job moves for whom, who takes leave, who handles nights, whose career gets protected, how money is controlled, what standards of cleanliness and involvement are nonnegotiable. A woman who assumes she can later persuade a husband to become an equal caregiver may find that the habits of inequality have hardened.

Still, we should avoid a tone that blames women for men’s convenience. Some women become stay-at-home mothers because their husbands will not do half the work and their workplaces will not accommodate care. In such cases, “choice” is often surrender to a rigged situation. The moral pressure should not fall only on the person who sacrifices income to meet children’s needs. It should fall on the spouse who allowed the sacrifice to become necessary.

The entitled mothers in Hirschmann’s opening stories are not simply too caring; they are doing what patriarchal family ideology asks mothers to do: merge their identities with their children’s success. If a child’s achievement is the mother’s achievement, then the child’s failure is the mother’s shame. No wonder some mothers lie, excuse, and intervene. They are defending not only the child but the maternal self the culture has demanded of them. A more equal fatherhood would help here too. If both parents are equally responsible for raising moral agents, and if children are understood as members of a community rather than private trophies, maternal overidentification may weaken.

Care feminism should therefore become fatherhood feminism. It should insist that dependency work is not women’s moral specialty. It should measure progress not by whether mothers receive more praise but by whether men lose the option of irresponsibility. A society in which men do half the care will look different at work, in marriage, in politics, and in children’s imaginations. Boys will see masculinity joined to nurture. Girls will see adulthood as something other than choosing between selfhood and service. Employers will see workers as caregivers of both sexes. Public policy will stop using mothers as the shock absorbers of social neglect.

Hirschmann ends by saying women’s power, not care itself, will enable equality. I would revise that: women’s power and men’s care must rise together. If women gain power while men remain care-free, the result may be outsourcing, exhaustion, or a small professional elite freed by the labor of poorer women. If men care without women gaining power, the old hierarchy may soften but not disappear. The feminist task is to make care powerful and power caring. That begins by asking not why mothers care too much, but why fathers are still allowed to care so little.
