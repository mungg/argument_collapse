"While acknowledging the limitations of liberal human rights and the validity of the 'racial capitalism' framework, we should retain and radicalize the language of dehumanization and the rights tradition as essential tools for justice."

Walter Johnson’s essay begins with an arresting admonition: stop saying that slavery “dehumanized” enslaved people. I understand the force of the claim. Johnson wants to prevent an unintended insult, the suggestion that the victim’s humanity is a status that domination can remove. He also wants to challenge the comforting thought that cruelty requires a prior failure of recognition. Slaveholders did not need to imagine enslaved people as nonhuman in order to torture, rape, sell, or exploit them. They often understood quite well that the people they owned could think, suffer, love, remember, plan, and mourn. Indeed, the system depended on those capacities.

Yet I want to defend, with qualifications, the language of dehumanization and the broader liberal human-rights tradition Johnson criticizes. The defense is not a denial of racial capitalism, nor a rejection of reparative justice. It is an argument that we should be careful before discarding moral and legal tools that have been central to struggles against slavery, colonialism, fascism, apartheid, and segregation.

When people say that slavery dehumanized enslaved people, they usually do not mean that the enslaved literally became less human. They mean that slavery was an institutionalized practice of treating human beings as if they lacked the moral status that human beings possess. Dehumanization, in this sense, describes an act by the oppressor and a structure of social relations, not an ontological change in the oppressed. It names the conversion of persons into property in law and practice. It names the refusal to accord equal moral standing. It names the ideological labor by which domination is made to appear natural.

Johnson anticipates this reply and worries that the word still does damage. He is right that language can carry residues its users do not intend. Historians should not write as if black humanity is a hypothesis awaiting confirmation. But the best use of “dehumanization” does not ask whether enslaved people preserved their humanity. It begins from the fact of their humanity and condemns the system that denied it. If I say that a prisoner was degraded, I am not saying the prisoner became degraded in essence. I am saying someone degraded him. If I say a woman was objectified, I do not mean she became an object. I mean she was treated as one. Likewise, to say slavery dehumanized is to accuse the enslavers, not to diminish the enslaved.

This distinction matters because the rhetoric of dehumanization has analytic value. Enslavers did not simply exploit labor; they built and defended a worldview in which black people were said to be naturally servile, childlike, animalistic, hypersexual, incapable of citizenship, or destined for subordination. Johnson is correct that slaveholders recognized enslaved people’s human capacities when it suited them. But this recognition coexisted with systematic ideological denial. Human beings are often capable of holding contradictory beliefs when those contradictions serve power. A master could punish a slave for intentional disobedience, thereby acknowledging will, and still insist that Africans were incapable of self-government. A society can know and not know at the same time.

The language of dehumanization helps us describe this moral schizophrenia. It also connects slavery to other atrocities without collapsing their differences. The twentieth century gave us horrifying examples of propaganda and bureaucracy that represented target populations as vermin, disease, contagion, or waste. The point is not that every such case is identical to slavery. It is that domination frequently involves a degradation of moral perception, a training of people to see other people as killable, rapeable, ownable, deportable, or disposable. “Dehumanization” remains a useful word for that training.

Johnson’s critique of human rights raises a parallel concern. He is right that human rights, especially in their postwar institutional form, are inadequate to the full demands of justice after slavery. Rights can be too abstract, too individualistic, too inattentive to political economy, too easily appropriated by powerful states. The language of rights has sometimes traveled with empire. Humanitarian intervention can become a velvet glove for domination. International law can condemn spectacular violence while leaving ordinary exploitation untouched. Liberalism can congratulate itself on formal equality while tolerating vast inherited disparities.

But inadequacy is not uselessness. Rights are not the whole of justice, but they are one of its necessary languages. Enslaved people and abolitionists claimed rights long before modern international human-rights institutions existed. Black Americans demanded the rights of citizens, the rights of workers, the rights of parents, the rights of worshippers, the rights of soldiers, and the rights of human beings. Frederick Douglass’s constitutional arguments, the antebellum petitions of free black communities, the demands of freedpeople during Reconstruction, the anti-lynching campaigns, the labor struggles of black workers, the legal assault on segregation, and the civil rights movement all drew upon rights language. They did so not because they were dupes of bourgeois abstraction, but because rights can be weapons.

The right not to be enslaved is not trivial. The right to bodily security is not merely formal. The right to vote, to testify, to serve on juries, to own property, to marry, to keep one’s children, to attend school, to move freely, to speak, to organize, and to worship are not shallow ornaments of liberal modernity. They are conditions without which deeper forms of emancipation become difficult to imagine, much less secure. Johnson quotes Marx’s distinction between political emancipation and human emancipation. Marx was right that political emancipation is not enough. But he also called it a “big step forward.” Those four words should not be rushed past.

One lesson of Reconstruction is that rights require institutions, power, and material support. The freedpeople wanted land as well as suffrage, schools as well as formal liberty, protection from violence as well as equal citizenship. Their tragedy was not that they demanded rights, but that the nation refused to enforce and enlarge those rights into a durable social order. A right to vote without protection from paramilitary terror is fragile. A right to contract without land or capital may reproduce dependency. A right to equal treatment in theory may coexist with exclusion in housing, credit, and education. But the answer is not to turn away from rights; it is to thicken them.

A human-rights tradition informed by slavery would have to include social and economic rights, collective rights, indigenous rights, gender justice, reproductive autonomy, and reparations. It would have to recognize that the harms of slavery were not exhausted by chains and whips, but included wealth extraction, family separation, sexual violence, legal dishonor, educational deprivation, ecological transformation, and political exclusion. It would need remedies adequate to historical harm. But this is an argument for expanding human rights, not abandoning them.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism is indispensable here. The history of slavery demonstrates that markets are never merely markets. They are constituted by law, violence, culture, and state power. Enslaved people were collateral, laborers, commodities, producers, reproducers, and kin. Native land was converted into property through imperial law and military force. Cotton linked the plantation South to Northern finance and British industry. These facts should permanently unsettle any clean story in which capitalism is born in free labor and then unfortunately contaminated by slavery. The modern economy was built through coercion as well as contract.

Still, one should be cautious about the claim that “there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery.” As a historical claim about the world that actually developed, it has force: Atlantic slavery was central to the rise of modern capitalism. As a conceptual claim that capitalism cannot exist without slavery, it is too strong. Capitalism has taken many forms, and free wage labor is not a fiction simply because it has always coexisted with unfree labor. The distinction between slavery and wage labor mattered enormously to enslaved people, abolitionists, workers, and political economists. To insist on connection should not erase difference.

Similarly, Johnson is right to criticize histories of slavery that become histories of slaveholders’ ledgers, banks, and productivity while losing sight of enslaved people’s lives. But the solution is integration, not inversion. We need histories of markets and histories of memory, histories of credit and histories of kinship, histories of imperial law and histories of prayer, music, sabotage, care, and rebellion. The economic system mattered because it structured the daily lives of people who were never reducible to economic functions.

The deepest issue between Johnson’s essay and a liberal human-rights defense may be the meaning of universalism. Johnson worries that universal humanity often smuggles in a bourgeois European subject. That worry is justified. Too often “the human” has meant the propertied man, the citizen, the rational contractor, the rights-bearing individual abstracted from dependency, reproduction, land, and history. But the answer to false universalism is not particularism alone. It is a better universalism, one built from the experiences of those whom previous universals excluded.

A universalism that begins with enslaved women would look different from one that begins with the property-owning citizen. It would understand bodily autonomy as inseparable from family integrity and freedom from sexual domination. A universalism that begins with indigenous dispossession would treat land and sovereignty as central, not peripheral. A universalism that begins with plantation labor would see economic rights as basic. A universalism that begins with black resistance would understand freedom as collective as well as individual. Such a universalism would not require proving anyone’s humanity. It would ask what institutions are necessary because everyone’s humanity is already given.

In this sense, Johnson’s essay and the human-rights tradition need not be enemies. His critique can rescue rights from complacency. Rights without history become thin. Rights without redistribution become hollow. Rights without power become promises. Rights without attention to race, class, gender, empire, and ecology become alibis. But history without rights can also become politically indeterminate. It can reveal domination without specifying enforceable claims. Reparations themselves require a language of obligation, entitlement, standing, injury, and remedy. Those are rights-bearing concepts, even when they exceed liberal individualism.

We should therefore retain the word “dehumanization,” but use it carefully. We should retain human rights, but radicalize them. We should affirm that slavery was not an aberration outside modernity, but one of modernity’s engines. And we should insist that the humanity of the enslaved was never in question. What was in question, and remains in question, is whether our institutions can be made to honor what has always been true.

"While Walter Johnson’s critique of the Eurocentric and liberal limitations of contemporary human rights is valid, the framework should be radicalized and reconstructed through the lens of slavery and racial capitalism rather than abandoned."

Walter Johnson’s essay offers a powerful critique of contemporary human rights from the standpoint of slavery and racial capitalism. As someone sympathetic to the human-rights tradition, I think the critique should be taken seriously—but not as a reason to abandon human rights. Rather, it should be read as a demand to reconstruct them.

Johnson argues that the dominant human-rights framework emerged from twentieth-century Europe and therefore emphasizes genocide, conscience, political violence, and democratic self-determination more than slavery, colonial extraction, racial capitalism, gendered reproduction, and reparations. There is truth in this. The post-1945 human-rights order was shaped profoundly by the Holocaust and world war. Its institutions often focused on state violence, imprisonment, torture, censorship, and mass killing. Economic exploitation, racial hierarchy, colonial plunder, and historical repair were more difficult to address, especially when powerful states had little interest in scrutiny of their own empires, markets, or racial orders.

The human-rights movement has also sometimes been enlisted in imperial projects. Powerful nations have invoked rights selectively, condemning adversaries while excusing allies. Humanitarian language can legitimate military intervention, sanctions that harm civilians, or tutelage over former colonies. Non-governmental organizations may speak for communities rather than with them. Legalism can narrow political imagination. A rights report can document abuses while leaving untouched the global economic arrangements that make vulnerability predictable.

Yet the history of human rights is broader and more contested than Johnson’s account allows. Anti-slavery, anti-colonial, labor, feminist, and civil rights movements all contributed to the moral vocabulary of universal dignity. The Haitian Revolution, black abolitionism, slave petitions, indigenous diplomacy, labor internationalism, Pan-Africanism, anti-apartheid activism, and women’s movements helped shape claims that later entered human-rights law. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights itself includes social and economic rights as well as civil and political ones. The problem has not been simply that human rights are European; it is that the most powerful institutions have often privileged the rights least threatening to property and empire.

Johnson’s essay should therefore push us toward a more radical human-rights practice. What would human rights look like if slavery were central rather than peripheral?

First, they would take economic and social rights seriously. The right to work under just conditions, the right to education, the right to health, the right to housing, the right to social security, and the right to an adequate standard of living are not optional add-ons. They address precisely the gap between formal freedom and material domination. Emancipation without land, schooling, safety, or wages left freedpeople vulnerable to new forms of coercion. A human-rights framework shaped by slavery would understand poverty not merely as misfortune but as a political outcome often tied to historical theft.

Second, human rights would include robust anti-discrimination principles linked to structural remedies. It is not enough to prohibit intentional discrimination by identifiable bad actors. Slavery and segregation created durable institutions: wealth gaps, residential patterns, school inequalities, health disparities, carceral exposure, and political exclusion. Human-rights law and advocacy must become more capable of addressing disparate impact, institutionalized disadvantage, and cumulative harm.

Third, reparations must be recognized as a human-rights issue. This is already implicit in principles of remedy for gross injustice: victims of wrongful harm are entitled to restitution, compensation, rehabilitation, satisfaction, and guarantees of non-repetition. If these principles apply to torture, unlawful imprisonment, and ethnic cleansing, why should they not apply to slavery, colonial dispossession, and racial terror? The difficulty is scale and time, not moral logic. Johnson is right that a synchronic focus on present rights cannot answer historical injury. But human rights need not be synchronic. They can include duties to repair.

Fourth, human rights must attend to gender, sexuality, and reproduction. Slavery involved rape, forced reproduction, family separation, and denial of parental rights. A slavery-centered human-rights framework would connect sexual violence, reproductive autonomy, maternal health, child welfare, and economic justice. It would reject both the patriarchal household and the market as natural domains beyond rights scrutiny. It would ask how law and policy shape the ability to form and sustain families.

Fifth, human rights must take collective rights seriously, including indigenous sovereignty and land rights. Liberal rights often imagine isolated individuals. But many harms are inflicted on peoples: removal, cultural destruction, language suppression, land theft, and ecological devastation. The right of a people to self-determination is not reducible to the rights of its individual members. A human-rights practice adequate to settler colonialism must honor treaties, land claims, jurisdiction, and consent.

Sixth, human rights must confront the global economy. Supply chains, debt regimes, trade rules, resource extraction, migrant labor systems, and austerity policies can produce rights violations without a single dictator ordering repression. If a woman works in dangerous conditions for starvation wages producing goods for distant consumers, if a community loses water to mining, if debt policy forces cuts to health systems, human-rights analysis should not stop at the nearest local official. It must follow power across borders.

In this sense, Johnson’s “racial capitalism” can enrich human rights by supplying a theory of causation. Human-rights advocacy often excels at documenting violations but struggles to explain why they recur. Racial capitalism directs attention to profit, property, labor hierarchy, and historical accumulation. It asks who benefits from vulnerability. It reveals that rights abuses are not merely failures of rule of law but sometimes features of economic order.

Still, I would resist Johnson’s tendency to contrast rights and justice too sharply. Rights can be thin or thick, conservative or radical, individualistic or collective. The right not to be enslaved was once a revolutionary claim. The right of colonized peoples to self-determination challenged empires. The right to organize threatened employers. The right to vote threatened racial oligarchy. The right to education threatened caste. The meaning of a right depends partly on the movement that wields it.

Consider the civil rights movement in the United States. It used constitutional and human-rights language while also engaging in direct action, economic boycotts, labor organizing, and moral critique. Some activists appealed to the United Nations to expose American racism before the world. Others emphasized domestic constitutional promises. The movement’s limitations did not arise from rights language alone, but from political compromise, white backlash, Cold War constraints, economic structures, and the assassination or repression of radical leaders. Rights opened doors; power determined how far they would stay open.

Johnson is correct that human-rights rhetoric can become self-congratulatory. A nation may celebrate its commitment to freedom while maintaining prisons, poverty, militarized borders, and racialized policing. International Human Rights Day ceremonies can coexist with arms sales and exploitation. But hypocrisy is not a refutation of principle. It is an indictment of practice. The answer is to make human-rights claims more dangerous to the powerful.

The current political moment underscores the need for both rights defense and structural critique. In the first days of the Trump administration, immigrants and refugees have already become targets of executive power. Muslims face suspicion encoded in policy. Black protest is vilified. Voting rights remain contested. The press is attacked. In such a context, civil liberties are not bourgeois luxuries. They are shields. People need lawyers at airports, courts willing to review executive orders, sanctuary institutions, due process, equal protection, and freedom of assembly. A politics that dismisses rights as inadequate may be correct philosophically and disastrous practically.

At the same time, rights defense alone will not meet the crisis. If communities hollowed out by deindustrialization are offered only anti-discrimination norms, demagogues will continue to exploit resentment. If black communities receive formal legal equality while facing police violence, unemployment, school segregation, and environmental harm, rights will sound hollow. If refugees are admitted into societies that then exploit their labor and stigmatize their religion, protection remains partial. Johnson’s insistence on political economy is indispensable.

The best path is a “both/and” approach: defend rights fiercely while expanding the horizon of justice beyond them. Human-rights institutions should partner with movements such as the Movement for Black Lives, immigrant worker centers, indigenous water protectors, prison abolitionists, labor unions, and feminist organizations. They should learn to treat community platforms, not only legal conventions, as sources of normative insight. They should measure success not only by court victories or treaty ratifications but by changes in material conditions.

A reconstructed human-rights practice would also be humbler. It would not imagine Geneva, New York, Washington, or The Hague as the natural centers of moral authority. It would begin from Ferguson, Flint, Standing Rock, Port-au-Prince, Lagos, Chiapas, Johannesburg, and the many places where people analyze power from below. It would not arrive to bestow humanity. It would recognize political thought already present in struggle.

Johnson asks us to use the history of slavery as a source rather than merely a subject of knowledge. Human-rights advocates should accept that challenge. Slavery teaches that rights without remedies can be mockery, that personhood without power can be fragile, that labor exploitation can hide behind legal forms, that sexual domination can be economic, that land theft can become property law, and that racial hierarchy can outlive its formal abolition.

But slavery also teaches why universal claims matter. Enslaved people used every available language—biblical, natural rights, republican, familial, military, labor, and human—to assert that no one could justly own them. Their descendants used rights language to dismantle segregation and claim citizenship. The task now is not to choose between human rights and racial justice. It is to make human rights answer to the history that Johnson recounts, until they become less a language of liberal innocence and more a weapon of repair.

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies slavery as a foundational component of racial capitalism, we should refine rather than abandon the concepts of dehumanization, universal rights, and humanity to better serve the pursuit of reparative justice."

Walter Johnson is right to insist that slavery was not a premodern residue, a moral aberration standing outside the history of capitalism, or a theater in which enslaved people’s humanity was somehow absent until recovered by historians. His essay is most powerful when it refuses the comforting distance created by words such as “inhuman.” The auction block, the ledger, the mortgage, the rape, the whipping post, the cotton bale, the insurance contract, and the bill of exchange all belonged to the same world. They were not capitalism’s opposite; they were among its conditions of possibility. To say this is not to flatten every distinction between a plantation and a factory, or between enslaved and wage labor. It is to ask why those distinctions were produced, who profited from them, and how they became racial common sense.

Still, I would hesitate before giving up entirely on the language of dehumanization, humanity, and rights. Johnson’s warning is indispensable: if “dehumanization” means that enslaved people lost their humanity, then the word repeats the injury it means to condemn. Enslaved people did not become less human because slaveholders treated them as property. Indeed, as Johnson argues, slavery depended precisely on enslaved people’s capacities as human beings: their knowledge, skill, memory, kinship, fear, hope, sexuality, fertility, and imagination. A mule could be worked; a person could be coerced, humiliated, threatened through loved ones, converted into collateral, and forced to reproduce wealth across generations. The violence of slavery lay not in a failure to perceive humanity but in the decision to exploit it.

But “dehumanization” can also name something else: not the condition of the oppressed, but the practice of the oppressor. Frederick Douglass did not need to prove his humanity to us, but he did spend his life exposing a society organized to deny it in law, custom, and political economy. Harriet Jacobs’s account of sexual coercion under slavery is not a plea to be admitted into humanity; it is an indictment of a regime that made black women’s bodies available to white power while wrapping white households in the language of virtue. Sojourner Truth’s “Ain’t I a Woman?”—whatever the complications of its transmission—has endured because it captured the cruelty of a world in which supposedly universal categories such as “woman,” “man,” “citizen,” and “human” were distributed through race. In this sense, dehumanization does not describe what happened inside the souls of the enslaved. It describes a social order that converted persons into things while never ceasing to draw upon their personhood.

The same caution applies to Johnson’s critique of rights. He is persuasive that a thin liberal account of rights is inadequate to slavery and its afterlives. The right to contract means little when one party inherits land, credit, police protection, and whiteness, while another inherits dispossession, debt, surveillance, and vulnerability. Formal equality can coexist with substantive domination; Reconstruction proved that with terrible clarity. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments were magnificent achievements, yet they did not prevent the overthrow of biracial democracy, the rise of convict leasing, debt peonage, disfranchisement, lynching, and Jim Crow. Political emancipation was real, and it was insufficient.

Yet the insufficiency of rights is not the same as their uselessness. Enslaved and formerly enslaved people did not generally treat rights as bourgeois distractions. They demanded the right to marry, to hold their children, to learn to read, to testify, to travel, to own land, to worship, to bear arms, to vote, to sit on juries, to be paid for labor, and to be protected from white violence. These demands were not reducible to possessive individualism. They were often collective, familial, religious, and material. They were claims about what kind of world freedom required. When freedpeople asked for land, schools, and political power, they were not merely asking to be recognized as abstract citizens. They were asking for the conditions under which citizenship could mean something.

For that reason, I would frame the matter less as rights versus reparative justice than as rights made honest by reparative justice. Human rights discourse, especially in its postwar institutional form, has often been selective and imperial. It has denounced atrocities while leaving untouched the economic arrangements that make whole populations disposable. It has been more comfortable with the language of conscience than with redistribution. But the answer cannot be to abandon universal claims altogether. A universalism reconstructed from Haiti, Reconstruction, anticolonial struggle, black feminism, indigenous sovereignty, and the Movement for Black Lives would look very different from one inherited from European liberalism alone. It would begin not with the unencumbered individual but with histories of theft, captivity, labor, reproduction, land, and state violence.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism is especially useful here because it prevents us from separating race from economy as if one were merely cultural and the other material. The value of enslaved people as collateral, the expropriation of Native land, the dependence of British mills on Southern cotton, and the racial privileges extended to white workers were not separate stories. They were mutually reinforcing parts of a global system. Du Bois’s “wages of whiteness” remains one of the sharpest tools we have for understanding why people injured by capital may nevertheless cling to racial hierarchy. In the winter of 2017, when open white nationalism has become newly emboldened and economic grievance is so often narrated through fantasies of racial restoration, this insight is not antiquarian. It is urgent.

At the same time, racial capitalism should be used as an analytic, not a solvent. If everything becomes racial capitalism in the same way, the concept loses its edge. The plantation, the prison, the border, the mortgage market, the low-wage service economy, the school district, the reservation, and the polluted neighborhood are connected, but not identical. Each has its own mechanisms, legal forms, and openings for struggle. The power of Johnson’s essay lies in showing connection without denying differentiation. That discipline should be preserved. Otherwise we risk replacing the old abstraction—capitalism without slavery—with a new abstraction that gestures toward totality while failing to guide action.

The question of action is where Johnson’s conclusion matters most. Reparations are not a symbolic addendum to justice; they are a test of whether we believe history has material consequences. If slavery, dispossession, segregation, exclusion from New Deal protections, redlining, discriminatory policing, and unequal schooling helped produce present distributions of wealth and insecurity, then repair must also be material. It must include money, land, housing, education, health, environmental protection, and democratic power. It must also attend, as Johnson insists, to gender and reproduction: to the long history by which black women’s labor, sexuality, and motherhood have been coerced, stigmatized, and exploited.

But reparations should not be imagined only as settlement, as if a nation could write a check and close the books. They should be understood as reconstruction: a remaking of institutions that continue to allocate life chances along lines laid down by slavery and empire. That means confronting the carceral state, predatory credit, segregated schooling, poisoned water, precarious labor, and the inherited sanctity of property claims rooted in conquest. It also means building solidarities that do not ask the descendants of the enslaved, indigenous peoples, immigrants, and poor whites to pretend their histories are the same. Solidarity worth having begins from the truth of unequal injury.

Johnson asks us to let the history of slavery become a source of knowledge rather than merely an object of study. That is the essay’s deepest challenge. Begin with Johnson's specific complaint about the historiography. He argues that historians who frame enslaved people's actions in terms of "agency," "independent will," and the like are anachronistically projecting the bourgeois liberal subject onto people whose actions were structured by quite different idioms. He cites Philip Morgan's *Slave Counterpoint* as an example. The criticism is not without bite. There is a tendency in some scholarship to treat any act of enslaved self-assertion as a vindication of universal liberal selfhood, as though the only meaningful question about the enslaved is whether they pass the test of resembling us.

It is that the historical archive they work with — produced overwhelmingly by people who insisted, both legally and ideologically, on the non-personhood of the enslaved — generates an interpretive obligation. If your sources call a human being "wool" or "hand," and your discipline once took those sources at their face, then the work of demonstrating personhood is not paternalist; it is corrective. Johnson treats this work as if it placed black humanity "on probation." I think it is better described as releasing black humanity from the probation that the archive imposed upon it. The frame Johnson criticizes is the frame from which historians are escaping, not the frame in which they are stuck.

Marx, whom Johnson quotes, did not argue that political emancipation should be discarded; he called it "a big step forward" and described it as the most complete form of emancipation available within "the hitherto existing world order." They claim it, often more rigorously than the governments that nominally champion it. The vocabulary of rights is, at this moment in history, one of the few cross-cultural moral idioms with real political traction. To abandon it because it has been put to bad uses is to surrender one of the few weapons that consistently embarrasses the powerful.

I do not want to be misread as defending the neoliberal status quo, which has weaponized rights talk against substantive equality in any number of ways. Johnson is right that property rights, freedom of contract, and the like have done immense work in entrenching the inequalities he describes. The libertarian appropriation of rights discourse is a genuine problem. The history of racial capitalism is real, and any adequate response to it must reckon with the global patterns of extraction Johnson describes. The choice is not between rights and reparations. It is between a rights tradition that includes reparative obligations and one that does not. Johnson's history should be marshaled in service of the former, not against the rights tradition as such.

Walter Johnson's essay accomplishes something important and, for that reason, deserves the kind of friction that comes from taking it seriously rather than nodding along. His indictment of the "dehumanization" trope is largely persuasive, his insistence on the entanglement of slavery and capitalism is by now (and not coincidentally) the most generative line of inquiry in the field, and his summons to ground a theory of justice in the history of slavery and racial capitalism rather than in twentieth-century European catastrophe is exactly the kind of provocation American intellectual life needs. Let me press on three points.

First, on dehumanization. Johnson is right that the word, taken literally, performs a kind of accidental violence: it suggests that enslavers managed (somehow) to extract or suspend the humanity of their captives, and that historians' principal task is to demonstrate that this humanity persisted, was restored, or was reclaimed. That framing turns black humanity into a perpetually probationary fact and turns slaveholding into an inhuman aberration rather than a recognizable human practice. Johnson's reminder that slavery depended on the human capacities of the enslaved—their intelligence, their fertility, their sentience, their capacity to suffer in legible ways—is essential, and he is right to point out that violence presupposed rather than negated the humanity of its objects. The torturer needs a witness.

Still, I would resist the conclusion that "dehumanization" is simply a category error to be discarded. The term names something real about the social and legal status that slaveholders sought to fix on the enslaved: a condition in which one's status as a moral or jural person was systematically denied even as one's biological and cognitive capacities were exploited. Orlando Patterson's "social death," for all its limits, gestures at this gap between what was extracted from enslaved people and what was conceded to them. Saidiya Hartman, whom Johnson surely has in mind, has shown how the law's selective recognition of slave humanity—its acknowledgment of criminal agency, for instance, alongside the denial of contract or kinship—was itself a technology of subjection. "Dehumanization" need not denote the metaphysical erasure of personhood; it can name the political project of producing a being whose humanity is exploited without being recognized. Johnson's critique is best read as a warning about lazy usage, not as a verdict that the concept has no purchase.

Second, on racial capitalism. Johnson, following Cedric Robinson and Du Bois, argues that there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery: Manchester did not happen without Mississippi. As an empirical claim about the cotton economy of the long nineteenth century, this is now hard to dispute; the work of Sven Beckert, Walter Johnson himself in *River of Dark Dreams*, Edward Baptist, and the contributors to *Slavery's Capitalism* has made James Oakes's counterfactual ("would Britain have industrialized without slavery?") look exactly as ahistorical as Johnson says it is. And Du Bois's collapsing of the orthodox firewall between slavery and capitalism in *Black Reconstruction* deserves its belated canonization.

But it is one thing to insist that the historical capitalism we actually had was constituted through racial slavery, settler expropriation, and colonial extraction; it is another to argue, as Robinson sometimes does and as Johnson seems to here, that capitalism is *constitutively* racial—that race and capital are inseparable in principle, not just in fact. The first claim is a historical thesis. The second is closer to a philosophical one, and it carries a heavy burden. If racial differentiation is intrinsic to capital accumulation, what do we make of capitalist economies whose hierarchies of expendability run along non-racial lines (caste, sect, region, citizenship)? Are these to be reabsorbed into "race" by definitional fiat, or do we need a more capacious account of how capital opportunistically seizes upon whatever lines of human difference are at hand? I suspect the answer is the latter, and I suspect Johnson would agree, but the rhetoric of his essay tends to elide the distinction. The danger is that "racial capitalism" becomes a name for everything bad about modernity, at which point it loses its analytical edge precisely because it has become a synonym for the world.

Third, on rights. Here I think Johnson moves too quickly. He follows Samuel Moyn in dating the contemporary regime of human rights to the late twentieth century and in seeing it as the ideological scaffolding of liberal neo-imperialism. He then proposes that justice be reconceived not through rights but through reparations, restorative justice, and a critique of racial capitalism. The six "virtues" he enumerates at the end of the essay are genuinely attractive: a Global South standpoint, attention to extraction and distribution, an emphasis on the historical sedimentation of present privilege, sensitivity to gender and social reproduction, a unified analysis of race and class, and a link between the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature. These are virtues worth wanting.

But what is the relationship between this program and the rights tradition? Johnson sometimes writes as if rights talk is to be jettisoned in favor of something deeper; at other times as if it is to be supplemented or radicalized. The Movement for Black Lives platform he cites, after all, is not anti-rights; it makes rights claims (to reparations, to investment, to economic justice) that rest on universalist premises even as they are anchored in the particular history of slavery and Jim Crow. The history of abolition itself—from David Walker to Frederick Douglass to Anna Julia Cooper—was not a history outside the rights tradition; it was a history of forcing that tradition to live up to commitments it had betrayed. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction*, which Johnson rightly celebrates, treats the postwar amendments as both an unfinished revolution and a usable inheritance. The question is whether we treat the language of rights as a thin veneer over racial capitalism (to be peeled away) or as a contested terrain on which redistributive and reparative claims can be lodged. The latter strikes me as both more historically faithful and more politically generative.

A final note. Johnson ends by warning, with Du Bois, that there are moments propitious for remaking the world, and that the cost of missing them is paid in the blood of subsequent generations. Writing in February 2017, with a new administration committed to nativism, deregulation, and the open celebration of white grievance, the warning lands. If the "new history of capitalism" is to do more than enrich our footnotes, it must connect its account of how the world was made to an argument about how it can be unmade. Johnson's essay is one of the more serious efforts to draw that connection. Its arguments deserve to be tested, not embraced—which is, I take it, what he would want.

"The liberal tradition of justice is not bankrupt but should be deepened and reconstructed from within to address the historical wrongs of slavery and racial capitalism."

There is an old saying among lawyers: hard cases make bad law. The idea is that a particularly extreme or emotionally freighted set of facts can lead judges to craft rules that work poorly when applied to more ordinary circumstances. The case of a child burned by a defective product, for instance, might lead to a rule of strict liability that makes sense for that case but creates perverse incentives in other areas of the law. The solution is not to ignore the hard case, but to think carefully about how the principles it demands can be generalized without doing violence to other important values.

I thought of this maxim while reading Walter Johnson’s essay. Johnson is, in effect, arguing that the history of slavery is a hard case for liberal theories of justice—so hard that it cannot be accommodated within the existing framework of rights, liberties, and distributive fairness. The wrongs of slavery, he argues, are not just extreme instances of injustice that can be remedied by extending existing rights to the enslaved and their descendants. They are qualitatively different: a form of “racial capitalism” that turned human beings into capital, that rested on the reproductive exploitation of women, that was inseparable from Native dispossession and global imperialism, and that continues to structure the distribution of wealth and power in the present. A justice adequate to this history cannot be a justice of individual rights and formal equality; it must be a justice of reparations, redistribution, and fundamental transformation.

I am not unsympathetic to this argument. Johnson is right that the liberal tradition has struggled to account for the legacy of slavery, and right that the remedies it has offered—the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments, the Civil Rights Acts, affirmative action—have been partial and inadequate. But I want to push back against the implication that the liberal tradition is therefore bankrupt. Hard cases, I want to suggest, do not always make bad law. Sometimes they reveal the true meaning of the principles we already hold.

Take the principle of equal citizenship. The liberal tradition, from Locke to Rawls, has insisted that all persons have a fundamental moral claim to be treated as free and equal members of the political community. Johnson sees this principle as inherently limited, because it abstracts away from the material inequalities that make formal equality a mockery for the descendants of enslaved people. But the principle of equal citizenship, properly understood, does not require abstraction from material conditions. On the contrary, it requires that the state take affirmative steps to ensure that all citizens have the means to participate effectively in political and social life. This is the insight behind Rawls’s difference principle, which holds that inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least advantaged. It is the insight behind the idea of “substantive” as opposed to “formal” equality of opportunity—the idea that it is not enough to remove legal barriers to advancement; one must also provide the resources, the education, the health care, and the social supports that make advancement genuinely possible. And it is the insight behind the growing body of scholarship on “structural” or “systemic” discrimination, which recognizes that racial inequality is not simply the product of individual prejudice but of institutional arrangements that reproduce disadvantage across generations.

If we take these insights seriously—and I believe they are firmly within the liberal tradition, even if they have not always been honored in practice—then the case of slavery does not require us to abandon the framework of equal citizenship. It requires us to deepen it. The descendants of enslaved people are not just citizens who happen to be poor; they are citizens whose poverty is the direct result of a centuries-long program of state-sponsored expropriation, exploitation, and exclusion. Equal citizenship for them requires not just formal equality but remedial action: action that acknowledges the specific history of their disadvantage and aims to eliminate its ongoing effects. This is, in its essentials, the argument for reparations, and it is an argument that can be made entirely within the language of liberal justice—indeed, some of its most eloquent exponents, from Thurgood Marshall to Ta-Nehisi Coates, have done exactly that.

Johnson might reply that this is precisely the problem: that the liberal tradition can be stretched to accommodate reparations, but only by losing its distinctiveness. If liberal justice requires the wholesale redistribution of wealth and power, then what distinguishes it from the radical tradition Johnson favors? But this reply misses something important. The radical tradition, as Johnson presents it, is grounded in the particular experience of one group—the enslaved and their descendants—and it calls for remedies that are tailored to that experience. The liberal tradition, by contrast, is grounded in general principles that apply to everyone. The advantage of the liberal approach is precisely that it is general: it provides a framework within which the claims of different groups can be weighed against one another, and it provides a language in which those claims can be justified to people who do not share the same history.

Let me give an example. Johnson’s six virtues include the claim that a justice rooted in the history of slavery should focus on “the extraction and distribution of resources between classes and areas of the world: on the relationship of African American history to Native American history, for example, or on the relationship of either or both of those to the history of the white workers (and merchants and bankers) in the financial and manufacturing centers of the United States and Europe.” This is a commendably expansive vision. But it raises a host of practical questions. How do we balance the claims of African Americans, whose ancestors were enslaved, against the claims of Native Americans, whose ancestors were dispossessed and genocidally slaughtered? Are these claims in competition with one another, or can they be harmonized? What about the claims of more recent immigrants, whose ancestors may have been victimized by other forms of colonialism or oppression but who arrived in the United States after the end of slavery? What about the claims of the white working class, which Johnson acknowledges is both a beneficiary of racial privilege and a victim of capitalist exploitation?

These questions do not answer themselves. They require a framework for adjudication—a set of principles that can tell us, however imperfectly, how to weigh competing claims. The liberal tradition, for all its flaws, has at least attempted to provide such a framework. It has proposed principles of desert, of need, of equality, of reciprocity. These principles are far from settled, and their application to the specific case of slavery and its legacies is a matter of ongoing dispute. But the dispute is productive. It forces us to be precise about what we think justice requires, and to justify our conclusions to others in terms they can, at least in principle, accept.

Johnson’s alternative, by contrast, is to derive justice from a particular historical experience. But this raises the question of authority: why should someone who does not share that experience accept the conclusions that follow from it? If the answer is simply “because the experience reveals truths that you cannot otherwise see,” then we are in the territory of an epistemic privilege that is not easily squared with democratic deliberation. If the answer is “because you are complicit in the structures that produced that experience, whether you know it or not,” then we need an argument about complicity that Johnson does not provide.

None of this is to deny the power of Johnson’s critique. The history of slavery is indeed a hard case for liberal justice, and coming to terms with it may require transforming liberal justice in ways that are hard to predict. But the solution, I want to suggest, is not to abandon the liberal tradition for a particularist account of justice grounded in the history of one group. It is to do the hard work of reconstructing the liberal tradition from within—to use the history of slavery as a test case for our principles, and to revise those principles until they can meet the test. This is a slower, messier process than the one Johnson envisions. But it is, I believe, more likely to produce a justice that can be shared.

Moyn, Antislavery, and the Vexed History of Human Rights

Walter Johnson's essay treats the history of human rights with a glancing touch. He cites Samuel Moyn's *The Last Utopia* approvingly for its argument that the dominant contemporary regime of human rights is a creature of the mid-twentieth century rather than a continuation of the longer antislavery tradition. He then uses this argument to license a broader claim: that the human-rights framework is inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, and that we should turn instead to a justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism. I want to spend my response unpacking the history of human rights more carefully, because I think Johnson's quick adoption of Moyn's periodization obscures both what Moyn gets right and what Johnson's argument actually requires.

Moyn's argument in *The Last Utopia* is, in brief, that "human rights" as a globally circulating moral and political vocabulary emerges late — in the 1970s rather than in 1948 (the Universal Declaration), 1789 (the French Declaration), or 1776 (the American Declaration). The earlier moments Moyn treats as essentially nationalist or revolutionary projects that used the language of rights instrumentally; what is distinctive about human rights properly understood, on Moyn's account, is the appeal to a transnational moral authority that sits outside and above the nation-state. That appeal, Moyn argues, becomes politically operative only with the collapse of competing utopias (Marxist, anticolonial, nationalist) in the 1970s, when human rights becomes the "last utopia" for a generation disillusioned with state-based projects.

This is a controversial argument, and Moyn's critics — Lynn Hunt, Micheline Ishay, others — have argued that the longer history of human rights, including antislavery, has more continuity with the contemporary regime than Moyn allows. The debate is unresolved. But for our purposes, what matters is what Johnson does with Moyn's argument and whether the argument can bear the weight Johnson places on it.

Even if we grant Moyn that human rights as a transnational vocabulary emerges in the 1970s, it does not follow that the substantive content of that vocabulary is uninflected by antislavery. The 1948 Universal Declaration, which Moyn de-emphasizes but does not dismiss, contains provisions (the prohibition of slavery, the prohibition of torture, the protection against arbitrary detention) that have clear genealogical relations to abolitionist arguments. The framing of human rights as "inherent" in the human person draws on a long tradition that the antislavery movement helped to develop. The institutional architecture of contemporary human rights (treaty bodies, special rapporteurs, the U.N. system) was built by people who took the abolitionist precedent seriously even when they did not invoke it explicitly.

What is true, and what Moyn is right about, is that the contemporary human-rights regime does not foreground the history of slavery in the way it foregrounds, say, the Holocaust or twentieth-century totalitarianism. The genealogy of the Universal Declaration runs more directly through the experience of Nazi atrocity than through the experience of Atlantic slavery. The institutional development of human rights in the postwar period was shaped by Cold War politics in ways that often suppressed engagement with the colonial and racial dimensions of the order it claimed to regulate. These are real points, and they constrain what human rights as a vocabulary can do.

But they do not establish what Johnson needs them to establish — namely, that the human-rights framework is fundamentally inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, such that an alternative framework rooted in racial capitalism is required. The relationship between the human-rights vocabulary and the project of racial justice is more dialectical than Johnson's brisk treatment allows.

They were not entirely successful — Cold War politics suppressed serious engagement with the petitions — but they established a precedent that has been picked up repeatedly since. The U.S. CERD reports, the periodic engagement of U.S. civil-rights groups with U.N. human-rights bodies, the recent reparations claims being developed by CARICOM nations under international law — all of these use the human-rights framework as a vehicle for racial-justice claims that domestic frameworks have been inadequate to advance.

It draws on human-rights language in places (the language of dignity, of self-determination) while also developing claims that exceed the human-rights frame (the reparations demand in its specificity, the abolitionist demand). The platform is, in this sense, neither a vindication of the human-rights framework nor a rejection of it; it is a strategic and substantive elaboration of how a movement rooted in the history of racial capitalism can use human-rights tools without being captured by them.

This is, I think, the more productive way to think about the relationship Johnson is gesturing at. The human-rights framework is not adequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, but it is not inadequate in the simple sense Johnson's brisk dismissal suggests. It is partially adequate. It captures some dimensions of the injustice (the violation of bodily integrity, the denial of political participation, the imposition of arbitrary state violence) while occluding others (the structural extraction of resources across generations, the racialization of life chances, the entanglement of race and class). The political task for movements working in the inheritance of slavery is to use the framework where it serves them, to push it where it can be pushed, and to develop additional frameworks where it cannot.

The development of additional frameworks is where Johnson's contribution lies, and I want to credit it. The history of racial capitalism does generate analytical and moral resources that the human-rights framework does not. The standpoint of the Global South, the focus on extraction and distribution, the emphasis on reparations rather than synchronic rights — these are real additions, and they enrich the moral vocabulary available to contemporary movements. What I am resisting is the framing that treats the new framework as a replacement for, rather than a supplement to, the human-rights tradition.

There is also a strategic point worth making. The human-rights framework, for all its limitations, has institutional purchase that newer frameworks do not. Treaty bodies, U.N. mechanisms, regional courts, NGO networks — these exist, they have track records, and they can be deployed in service of racial-justice claims even when their original architects did not anticipate that deployment. Movements that operate purely within new vocabularies face the additional burden of building institutional infrastructure from scratch. Movements that operate within the human-rights framework can leverage existing infrastructure even as they critique and transform it.

This is not an argument against developing the framework Johnson proposes. It is an argument for developing it in addition to, and in dialogue with, the existing framework. The history of social movements suggests that the most effective approaches typically operate on multiple vocabularies at once — using each where it serves, holding them in productive tension rather than picking one and discarding the others.

There is a deeper point lurking here about the relationship between historical genealogy and political utility. Moyn's argument is in significant part a historical one: human rights as we know them are a recent invention. Even if this is correct as history, it does not settle the question of political utility. A vocabulary can be useful for purposes its inventors did not envision, and the abolitionist tradition that Moyn de-emphasizes can be retroactively reclaimed as an ancestor of contemporary human rights even if the actual historical lineage is more discontinuous than such retrieval suggests. The question for contemporary movements is not "what did the framers of human rights actually think they were doing?" but "what can the human-rights vocabulary do for us now?" The answer to that question is not "nothing."

The new administration in Washington is, by all appearances, hostile to the human-rights framework — both in its rhetoric and in its early policy moves. The international institutions that have housed the human-rights regime are under pressure from multiple directions. The framework Johnson critiques is, in a sense, on the defensive. Defending it imperfectly may be more useful, at this moment, than developing alternatives that will not have political traction for years to come. The framework Johnson proposes is important for the long term. The framework Moyn analyzes is what we have to work with in the short term. The relationship between the two is a strategic question, and I do not think Johnson's essay quite reckons with that question's urgency.

By demanding that justice focus on the history of reproductive invigilation and natal alienation, Johnson aligns the historical critique of slavery with modern intersectional struggles for reproductive justice.

Similarly, his sixth point—linking the instrumentalization of human beings to the instrumentalization of nature—offers a profound historical lens for the contemporary climate crisis. The logic of racial capitalism is fundamentally extractive. The same worldview that looked at a human being and saw only a unit of calculable labor and collateral looked at the ancient forests and rich soils of the Mississippi Delta and saw only real estate and raw material. The exhaustion of the soil and the breaking of bodies were twin engines of capitalist accumulation. As we grapple with the escalating realities of environmental degradation today, Johnson reminds us that the fight for ecological survival cannot be divorced from the fight for racial and economic justice. Domination and dominion are, indeed, intimately dialectical.

In his conclusion, Johnson offers a six-point blueprint for an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism. It is a breathtakingly ambitious and morally urgent framework. It demands that we look southward, that we focus on extraction and distribution, that we pursue restorative justice and reparations, that we center gender and sexuality, that we collapse the false dichotomy between race and class, and that we recognize the linked exploitation of humans and the earth.

This vision provides a rigorous and necessary roadmap for anyone committed to social justice. It correctly diagnosis the limitations of a purely synchronic, liberal rights-based approach that ignores the deep, bleeding wounds of historical accumulation. We cannot achieve a just world simply by declaring abstract equality while leaving the inherited architectures of racial capitalism intact.

Yet, as we move forward to heed Du Bois’s warning and seize the propitious moments to remake the world, we need not discard the language of our humanity to do so. We can agree with Johnson that the history of capitalism is a history of violence, extraction, and racialized accumulation, and that the enslaved were vital, human actors within this nightmare. But we can also assert that the system’s primary offense was its relentless attempt to deny that humanity legal and social breathing room—an attempt we rightly call dehumanizing.

The struggle to remake the world requires a ruthless, clear-eyed analysis of the material conditions of racial capitalism. It also requires an unyielding, aspirational assertion of human dignity. By fusing Johnson’s material history with a renewed, expansive demand for human rights—one rooted not in the imaginary universality of the bourgeoisie, but in the specific, historical resistance of the dark and dispossessed—we can perhaps finally begin to dismantle the world racial capitalism built.
