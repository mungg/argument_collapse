"While acknowledging the limitations of liberal human rights and the validity of the 'racial capitalism' framework, we should retain and radicalize the language of dehumanization and the rights tradition as essential tools for justice."

Walter Johnson’s essay begins with an arresting admonition: stop saying that slavery “dehumanized” enslaved people. I understand the force of the claim. Johnson wants to prevent an unintended insult, the suggestion that the victim’s humanity is a status that domination can remove. He also wants to challenge the comforting thought that cruelty requires a prior failure of recognition. Slaveholders did not need to imagine enslaved people as nonhuman in order to torture, rape, sell, or exploit them. They often understood quite well that the people they owned could think, suffer, love, remember, plan, and mourn. Indeed, the system depended on those capacities.

Yet I want to defend, with qualifications, the language of dehumanization and the broader liberal human-rights tradition Johnson criticizes. The defense is not a denial of racial capitalism, nor a rejection of reparative justice. It is an argument that we should be careful before discarding moral and legal tools that have been central to struggles against slavery, colonialism, fascism, apartheid, and segregation.

When people say that slavery dehumanized enslaved people, they usually do not mean that the enslaved literally became less human. They mean that slavery was an institutionalized practice of treating human beings as if they lacked the moral status that human beings possess. Dehumanization, in this sense, describes an act by the oppressor and a structure of social relations, not an ontological change in the oppressed. It names the conversion of persons into property in law and practice. It names the refusal to accord equal moral standing. It names the ideological labor by which domination is made to appear natural.

Johnson anticipates this reply and worries that the word still does damage. He is right that language can carry residues its users do not intend. Historians should not write as if black humanity is a hypothesis awaiting confirmation. But the best use of “dehumanization” does not ask whether enslaved people preserved their humanity. It begins from the fact of their humanity and condemns the system that denied it. If I say that a prisoner was degraded, I am not saying the prisoner became degraded in essence. I am saying someone degraded him. If I say a woman was objectified, I do not mean she became an object. I mean she was treated as one. Likewise, to say slavery dehumanized is to accuse the enslavers, not to diminish the enslaved.

This distinction matters because the rhetoric of dehumanization has analytic value. Enslavers did not simply exploit labor; they built and defended a worldview in which black people were said to be naturally servile, childlike, animalistic, hypersexual, incapable of citizenship, or destined for subordination. Johnson is correct that slaveholders recognized enslaved people’s human capacities when it suited them. But this recognition coexisted with systematic ideological denial. Human beings are often capable of holding contradictory beliefs when those contradictions serve power. A master could punish a slave for intentional disobedience, thereby acknowledging will, and still insist that Africans were incapable of self-government. A society can know and not know at the same time.

The language of dehumanization helps us describe this moral schizophrenia. It also connects slavery to other atrocities without collapsing their differences. The twentieth century gave us horrifying examples of propaganda and bureaucracy that represented target populations as vermin, disease, contagion, or waste. The point is not that every such case is identical to slavery. It is that domination frequently involves a degradation of moral perception, a training of people to see other people as killable, rapeable, ownable, deportable, or disposable. “Dehumanization” remains a useful word for that training.

Johnson’s critique of human rights raises a parallel concern. He is right that human rights, especially in their postwar institutional form, are inadequate to the full demands of justice after slavery. Rights can be too abstract, too individualistic, too inattentive to political economy, too easily appropriated by powerful states. The language of rights has sometimes traveled with empire. Humanitarian intervention can become a velvet glove for domination. International law can condemn spectacular violence while leaving ordinary exploitation untouched. Liberalism can congratulate itself on formal equality while tolerating vast inherited disparities.

But inadequacy is not uselessness. Rights are not the whole of justice, but they are one of its necessary languages. Enslaved people and abolitionists claimed rights long before modern international human-rights institutions existed. Black Americans demanded the rights of citizens, the rights of workers, the rights of parents, the rights of worshippers, the rights of soldiers, and the rights of human beings. Frederick Douglass’s constitutional arguments, the antebellum petitions of free black communities, the demands of freedpeople during Reconstruction, the anti-lynching campaigns, the labor struggles of black workers, the legal assault on segregation, and the civil rights movement all drew upon rights language. They did so not because they were dupes of bourgeois abstraction, but because rights can be weapons.

The right not to be enslaved is not trivial. The right to bodily security is not merely formal. The right to vote, to testify, to serve on juries, to own property, to marry, to keep one’s children, to attend school, to move freely, to speak, to organize, and to worship are not shallow ornaments of liberal modernity. They are conditions without which deeper forms of emancipation become difficult to imagine, much less secure. Johnson quotes Marx’s distinction between political emancipation and human emancipation. Marx was right that political emancipation is not enough. But he also called it a “big step forward.” Those four words should not be rushed past.

One lesson of Reconstruction is that rights require institutions, power, and material support. The freedpeople wanted land as well as suffrage, schools as well as formal liberty, protection from violence as well as equal citizenship. Their tragedy was not that they demanded rights, but that the nation refused to enforce and enlarge those rights into a durable social order. A right to vote without protection from paramilitary terror is fragile. A right to contract without land or capital may reproduce dependency. A right to equal treatment in theory may coexist with exclusion in housing, credit, and education. But the answer is not to turn away from rights; it is to thicken them.

A human-rights tradition informed by slavery would have to include social and economic rights, collective rights, indigenous rights, gender justice, reproductive autonomy, and reparations. It would have to recognize that the harms of slavery were not exhausted by chains and whips, but included wealth extraction, family separation, sexual violence, legal dishonor, educational deprivation, ecological transformation, and political exclusion. It would need remedies adequate to historical harm. But this is an argument for expanding human rights, not abandoning them.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism is indispensable here. The history of slavery demonstrates that markets are never merely markets. They are constituted by law, violence, culture, and state power. Enslaved people were collateral, laborers, commodities, producers, reproducers, and kin. Native land was converted into property through imperial law and military force. Cotton linked the plantation South to Northern finance and British industry. These facts should permanently unsettle any clean story in which capitalism is born in free labor and then unfortunately contaminated by slavery. The modern economy was built through coercion as well as contract.

Still, one should be cautious about the claim that “there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery.” As a historical claim about the world that actually developed, it has force: Atlantic slavery was central to the rise of modern capitalism. As a conceptual claim that capitalism cannot exist without slavery, it is too strong. Capitalism has taken many forms, and free wage labor is not a fiction simply because it has always coexisted with unfree labor. The distinction between slavery and wage labor mattered enormously to enslaved people, abolitionists, workers, and political economists. To insist on connection should not erase difference.

Similarly, Johnson is right to criticize histories of slavery that become histories of slaveholders’ ledgers, banks, and productivity while losing sight of enslaved people’s lives. But the solution is integration, not inversion. We need histories of markets and histories of memory, histories of credit and histories of kinship, histories of imperial law and histories of prayer, music, sabotage, care, and rebellion. The economic system mattered because it structured the daily lives of people who were never reducible to economic functions.

The deepest issue between Johnson’s essay and a liberal human-rights defense may be the meaning of universalism. Johnson worries that universal humanity often smuggles in a bourgeois European subject. That worry is justified. Too often “the human” has meant the propertied man, the citizen, the rational contractor, the rights-bearing individual abstracted from dependency, reproduction, land, and history. But the answer to false universalism is not particularism alone. It is a better universalism, one built from the experiences of those whom previous universals excluded.

A universalism that begins with enslaved women would look different from one that begins with the property-owning citizen. It would understand bodily autonomy as inseparable from family integrity and freedom from sexual domination. A universalism that begins with indigenous dispossession would treat land and sovereignty as central, not peripheral. A universalism that begins with plantation labor would see economic rights as basic. A universalism that begins with black resistance would understand freedom as collective as well as individual. Such a universalism would not require proving anyone’s humanity. It would ask what institutions are necessary because everyone’s humanity is already given.

In this sense, Johnson’s essay and the human-rights tradition need not be enemies. His critique can rescue rights from complacency. Rights without history become thin. Rights without redistribution become hollow. Rights without power become promises. Rights without attention to race, class, gender, empire, and ecology become alibis. But history without rights can also become politically indeterminate. It can reveal domination without specifying enforceable claims. Reparations themselves require a language of obligation, entitlement, standing, injury, and remedy. Those are rights-bearing concepts, even when they exceed liberal individualism.

We should therefore retain the word “dehumanization,” but use it carefully. We should retain human rights, but radicalize them. We should affirm that slavery was not an aberration outside modernity, but one of modernity’s engines. And we should insist that the humanity of the enslaved was never in question. What was in question, and remains in question, is whether our institutions can be made to honor what has always been true.

"While Walter Johnson’s critique of the Eurocentric and liberal limitations of contemporary human rights is valid, the framework should be radicalized and reconstructed through the lens of slavery and racial capitalism rather than abandoned."

Walter Johnson’s essay offers a powerful critique of contemporary human rights from the standpoint of slavery and racial capitalism. As someone sympathetic to the human-rights tradition, I think the critique should be taken seriously—but not as a reason to abandon human rights. Rather, it should be read as a demand to reconstruct them.

Johnson argues that the dominant human-rights framework emerged from twentieth-century Europe and therefore emphasizes genocide, conscience, political violence, and democratic self-determination more than slavery, colonial extraction, racial capitalism, gendered reproduction, and reparations. There is truth in this. The post-1945 human-rights order was shaped profoundly by the Holocaust and world war. Its institutions often focused on state violence, imprisonment, torture, censorship, and mass killing. Economic exploitation, racial hierarchy, colonial plunder, and historical repair were more difficult to address, especially when powerful states had little interest in scrutiny of their own empires, markets, or racial orders.

The human-rights movement has also sometimes been enlisted in imperial projects. Powerful nations have invoked rights selectively, condemning adversaries while excusing allies. Humanitarian language can legitimate military intervention, sanctions that harm civilians, or tutelage over former colonies. Non-governmental organizations may speak for communities rather than with them. Legalism can narrow political imagination. A rights report can document abuses while leaving untouched the global economic arrangements that make vulnerability predictable.

Yet the history of human rights is broader and more contested than Johnson’s account allows. Anti-slavery, anti-colonial, labor, feminist, and civil rights movements all contributed to the moral vocabulary of universal dignity. The Haitian Revolution, black abolitionism, slave petitions, indigenous diplomacy, labor internationalism, Pan-Africanism, anti-apartheid activism, and women’s movements helped shape claims that later entered human-rights law. The Universal Declaration of Human Rights itself includes social and economic rights as well as civil and political ones. The problem has not been simply that human rights are European; it is that the most powerful institutions have often privileged the rights least threatening to property and empire.

Johnson’s essay should therefore push us toward a more radical human-rights practice. What would human rights look like if slavery were central rather than peripheral?

First, they would take economic and social rights seriously. The right to work under just conditions, the right to education, the right to health, the right to housing, the right to social security, and the right to an adequate standard of living are not optional add-ons. They address precisely the gap between formal freedom and material domination. Emancipation without land, schooling, safety, or wages left freedpeople vulnerable to new forms of coercion. A human-rights framework shaped by slavery would understand poverty not merely as misfortune but as a political outcome often tied to historical theft.

Second, human rights would include robust anti-discrimination principles linked to structural remedies. It is not enough to prohibit intentional discrimination by identifiable bad actors. Slavery and segregation created durable institutions: wealth gaps, residential patterns, school inequalities, health disparities, carceral exposure, and political exclusion. Human-rights law and advocacy must become more capable of addressing disparate impact, institutionalized disadvantage, and cumulative harm.

Third, reparations must be recognized as a human-rights issue. This is already implicit in principles of remedy for gross injustice: victims of wrongful harm are entitled to restitution, compensation, rehabilitation, satisfaction, and guarantees of non-repetition. If these principles apply to torture, unlawful imprisonment, and ethnic cleansing, why should they not apply to slavery, colonial dispossession, and racial terror? The difficulty is scale and time, not moral logic. Johnson is right that a synchronic focus on present rights cannot answer historical injury. But human rights need not be synchronic. They can include duties to repair.

Fourth, human rights must attend to gender, sexuality, and reproduction. Slavery involved rape, forced reproduction, family separation, and denial of parental rights. A slavery-centered human-rights framework would connect sexual violence, reproductive autonomy, maternal health, child welfare, and economic justice. It would reject both the patriarchal household and the market as natural domains beyond rights scrutiny. It would ask how law and policy shape the ability to form and sustain families.

Fifth, human rights must take collective rights seriously, including indigenous sovereignty and land rights. Liberal rights often imagine isolated individuals. But many harms are inflicted on peoples: removal, cultural destruction, language suppression, land theft, and ecological devastation. The right of a people to self-determination is not reducible to the rights of its individual members. A human-rights practice adequate to settler colonialism must honor treaties, land claims, jurisdiction, and consent.

Sixth, human rights must confront the global economy. Supply chains, debt regimes, trade rules, resource extraction, migrant labor systems, and austerity policies can produce rights violations without a single dictator ordering repression. If a woman works in dangerous conditions for starvation wages producing goods for distant consumers, if a community loses water to mining, if debt policy forces cuts to health systems, human-rights analysis should not stop at the nearest local official. It must follow power across borders.

In this sense, Johnson’s “racial capitalism” can enrich human rights by supplying a theory of causation. Human-rights advocacy often excels at documenting violations but struggles to explain why they recur. Racial capitalism directs attention to profit, property, labor hierarchy, and historical accumulation. It asks who benefits from vulnerability. It reveals that rights abuses are not merely failures of rule of law but sometimes features of economic order.

Still, I would resist Johnson’s tendency to contrast rights and justice too sharply. Rights can be thin or thick, conservative or radical, individualistic or collective. The right not to be enslaved was once a revolutionary claim. The right of colonized peoples to self-determination challenged empires. The right to organize threatened employers. The right to vote threatened racial oligarchy. The right to education threatened caste. The meaning of a right depends partly on the movement that wields it.

Consider the civil rights movement in the United States. It used constitutional and human-rights language while also engaging in direct action, economic boycotts, labor organizing, and moral critique. Some activists appealed to the United Nations to expose American racism before the world. Others emphasized domestic constitutional promises. The movement’s limitations did not arise from rights language alone, but from political compromise, white backlash, Cold War constraints, economic structures, and the assassination or repression of radical leaders. Rights opened doors; power determined how far they would stay open.

Johnson is correct that human-rights rhetoric can become self-congratulatory. A nation may celebrate its commitment to freedom while maintaining prisons, poverty, militarized borders, and racialized policing. International Human Rights Day ceremonies can coexist with arms sales and exploitation. But hypocrisy is not a refutation of principle. It is an indictment of practice. The answer is to make human-rights claims more dangerous to the powerful.

The current political moment underscores the need for both rights defense and structural critique. In the first days of the Trump administration, immigrants and refugees have already become targets of executive power. Muslims face suspicion encoded in policy. Black protest is vilified. Voting rights remain contested. The press is attacked. In such a context, civil liberties are not bourgeois luxuries. They are shields. People need lawyers at airports, courts willing to review executive orders, sanctuary institutions, due process, equal protection, and freedom of assembly. A politics that dismisses rights as inadequate may be correct philosophically and disastrous practically.

At the same time, rights defense alone will not meet the crisis. If communities hollowed out by deindustrialization are offered only anti-discrimination norms, demagogues will continue to exploit resentment. If black communities receive formal legal equality while facing police violence, unemployment, school segregation, and environmental harm, rights will sound hollow. If refugees are admitted into societies that then exploit their labor and stigmatize their religion, protection remains partial. Johnson’s insistence on political economy is indispensable.

The best path is a “both/and” approach: defend rights fiercely while expanding the horizon of justice beyond them. Human-rights institutions should partner with movements such as the Movement for Black Lives, immigrant worker centers, indigenous water protectors, prison abolitionists, labor unions, and feminist organizations. They should learn to treat community platforms, not only legal conventions, as sources of normative insight. They should measure success not only by court victories or treaty ratifications but by changes in material conditions.

A reconstructed human-rights practice would also be humbler. It would not imagine Geneva, New York, Washington, or The Hague as the natural centers of moral authority. It would begin from Ferguson, Flint, Standing Rock, Port-au-Prince, Lagos, Chiapas, Johannesburg, and the many places where people analyze power from below. It would not arrive to bestow humanity. It would recognize political thought already present in struggle.

Johnson asks us to use the history of slavery as a source rather than merely a subject of knowledge. Human-rights advocates should accept that challenge. Slavery teaches that rights without remedies can be mockery, that personhood without power can be fragile, that labor exploitation can hide behind legal forms, that sexual domination can be economic, that land theft can become property law, and that racial hierarchy can outlive its formal abolition.

But slavery also teaches why universal claims matter. Enslaved people used every available language—biblical, natural rights, republican, familial, military, labor, and human—to assert that no one could justly own them. Their descendants used rights language to dismantle segregation and claim citizenship. The task now is not to choose between human rights and racial justice. It is to make human rights answer to the history that Johnson recounts, until they become less a language of liberal innocence and more a weapon of repair.

"To be adequate to the present, a justice framework rooted in the history of slavery must be expanded into a project of environmental justice that addresses the racialized instrumentalization of nature."

Walter Johnson’s essay ends by linking slavery, racial capitalism, and the instrumentalization of nature. That final point deserves to be moved closer to the center. The plantation was not only a labor regime and racial order; it was an ecological project. It transformed land, water, plants, animals, bodies, and climates of risk. If justice rooted in the history of slavery is to be adequate to the present, it must become environmental justice.

Johnson is right to resist versions of the “Anthropocene” that treat humanity as a single geological actor. The human species did not collectively decide to clear Indigenous lands, build plantations, transport Africans, exhaust soils, burn coal, extract oil, and distribute toxins along racial lines. Some humans commanded; others were coerced. Some accumulated wealth; others accumulated disease, displacement, and death. To speak of “humanity” as the agent of planetary crisis can obscure the histories of empire, slavery, capitalism, and race that produced unequal responsibility and unequal vulnerability.

The plantation is a key site for understanding this. Cotton cultivation in the American South depended on Native dispossession, enslaved labor, global finance, and ecological transformation. Forests were cleared. Soils were depleted. Rivers became commercial arteries. Monoculture spread. The demand for new land intensified expansion westward, which meant further Indigenous removal and further extension of slavery. The plantation did not merely occupy nature; it reorganized nature for commodity production. It treated land as an input, enslaved people as capital, and ecological exhaustion as a reason to move on.

This pattern was not limited to cotton. Sugar plantations in the Caribbean remade islands through deforestation, soil exhaustion, brutal labor discipline, and deadly disease environments. Colonial extraction of rubber, ivory, timber, minerals, and later oil similarly joined racial domination to ecological violence. Du Bois’s image of piano keys and billiard balls carrying the hidden blood of elephants and African labor is an environmental history in miniature. Metropolitan pleasure was assembled from distant suffering, animal death, and colonial terror. Consumers encountered polished surfaces; the violence remained elsewhere.

That “elsewhere” is one of racial capitalism’s central technologies. Distance makes extraction morally manageable. The investor does not see the mine. The consumer does not see the plantation. The city does not see the poisoned watershed. The suburban household does not see the refinery community. The wealthy nation does not see the island losing shoreline or the drought-stricken farmer. Racial categories help organize this distance by marking some communities as naturally suited for danger, heat, waste, and sacrifice.

Environmental racism in the United States is therefore not a recent aberration. It is part of a long history in which black, Indigenous, Latino, Asian, and poor white communities have been differentially exposed to harm. Slavery itself was an environmental health regime: heat, malnutrition, disease, overwork, polluted water, inadequate shelter, and violence structured bodily life. After emancipation, sharecropping, convict leasing, industrial labor, segregated housing, and urban pollution continued to distribute environmental risk. Today toxic dumps, highways, petrochemical corridors, lead exposure, inadequate infrastructure, and climate vulnerability are mapped through histories of race and class.

The lead crisis in Flint, Michigan, still unfolding as Johnson writes, is a stark example. A majority-black, economically battered city was exposed to poisoned water through decisions made under austerity governance. Residents’ complaints were dismissed. Children’s bodies became evidence only after official denial. Flint is not slavery, but it belongs to the afterlife of a political order in which some communities are treated as expendable. The question “who must drink the poison?” is inseparable from “whose lives are discounted?” and “who controls public resources?”

Climate change intensifies these questions. Rising seas, storms, heat waves, droughts, fires, and food instability will not strike evenly. Poor communities and formerly colonized regions face disproportionate danger despite contributing least to the atmospheric accumulation produced by industrial capitalism. Within the United States, communities already burdened by segregation, inadequate infrastructure, and health disparities will be less able to adapt. A human-rights framework can say that all persons have a right to life, health, and security. That is important. But Johnson’s framework asks the deeper historical question: how did the wealth that produced the emissions accumulate, and why are the costs distributed as they are?

The term “racial capitalism” is useful because it prevents environmentalism from becoming a politics of generic stewardship. Calls to “save the planet” can sound noble while leaving intact the social relations that made the planet profitable to destroy. If the problem is humanity as such, then the solution may be technocratic management: carbon markets, geoengineering, efficiency, conservation, population control. Some of these tools may have roles, but none addresses the racialized history of extraction. Worse, population control has often targeted the reproduction of poor and racialized women rather than the consumption of wealthy societies.

A justice rooted in slavery would ask different environmental questions. Who owns the land? Who decides what is grown, mined, burned, transported, dumped, and protected? Who labors in dangerous conditions? Whose neighborhoods lack trees, grocery stores, clinics, and clean water? Whose sacred sites are destroyed? Whose children breathe asthma-inducing air? Who profits from fossil fuels? Who pays for transition? Who has the power to say no?

Johnson’s emphasis on reproduction is relevant here. Environmental harm is reproductive harm. Lead damages children’s development. Air pollution affects pregnancy and asthma. Toxic chemicals alter endocrine systems. Climate disasters separate families and disrupt care. Food insecurity shapes bodies across generations. If slavery subordinated black reproduction to property, environmental racism continues to undermine the capacity of communities to sustain life. Reparations must therefore include ecological repair: clean water, soil remediation, safe housing, health care, renewable infrastructure, and community control.

This also means that labor and environmental politics must be joined rather than opposed. Workers in fossil fuel industries, industrial agriculture, logistics, and manufacturing often depend on jobs that expose them and others to harm. A just transition cannot simply shut down industries and abandon workers. Nor can labor justice be used as an excuse to poison communities or accelerate climate crisis. The history of racial capitalism teaches that capital thrives by dividing workers from communities, jobs from health, and present wages from future survival. A reparative environmental politics would guarantee employment, retraining, income, and democratic planning while rapidly reducing ecological harm.

Indigenous sovereignty is central to such a politics. Many of the most important environmental struggles are also struggles over treaty rights, sacred land, water protection, and consent. Standing Rock has reminded the country that pipelines are not merely infrastructure; they are assertions about whose land and water can be risked. Indigenous movements have long articulated a relation to land that challenges the property-centered worldview of racial capitalism. This does not mean romanticizing Native peoples as ecological symbols. It means respecting Native nations as political actors whose sovereignty is indispensable to environmental justice.

Johnson’s critique of “dehumanization” can also be extended to nature. Just as slavery did not simply fail to recognize enslaved people’s humanity but exploited their human capacities, capitalism does not simply “de-naturalize” nature. It recognizes the capacities of soil, rivers, forests, animals, and fossilized life in order to instrumentalize them. Cotton grows in particular climates. Rivers carry goods. Coal burns. Oil stores ancient sunlight. The system knows these capacities intimately, scientifically, financially. The problem is not ignorance of nature but domination of it under the sign of profit.

Yet we should be careful. Human beings are not the same as rivers, and the language of slavery should not be casually applied to nonhuman nature in ways that blur moral distinctions. Enslaved people were persons with claims, voices, and histories. Still, the histories are linked because the same imperial and capitalist projects treated both racialized people and land as sources of value to be extracted without reciprocal obligation. “Dominion” over nature and domination over people grew together.

What would environmental reparations look like? They would begin with historical accounting: mapping the relationship between slavery, segregation, industrial siting, land theft, and environmental exposure. They would require massive investment in communities harmed by pollution and disinvestment. They would include replacing lead pipes, cleaning contaminated land, funding public health, relocating communities when necessary and desired, supporting black farmers and Indigenous land recovery, protecting workers, and democratizing energy. They would require climate policy that recognizes historical responsibility, both within the United States and globally. They would treat clean water and air as owed, not gifted.

They would also require changing consumption in affluent communities. Racial capitalism has allowed some people to experience the world as convenience: cheap food, cheap clothing, cheap energy, cheap delivery, cheap waste disposal. The cheapness is an illusion produced by unpaid costs borne elsewhere. Slavery was one form of this illusion: cotton appeared as a commodity, not as stolen life. Today a plastic package, a smartphone, a gallon of gasoline, or a winter strawberry may likewise hide labor and ecological violence. Justice requires making those hidden relations politically visible.

The present moment is not encouraging. The new administration seems committed to fossil fuel expansion, deregulation, and hostility toward climate science. Environmental protections are likely to be portrayed as elitist obstacles to jobs, while vulnerable communities are asked to bear more risk. This makes Johnson’s framework all the more important. Environmentalism cannot succeed if it speaks only in the language of polar bears, atmospheric parts per million, or wilderness. It must speak in the language of race, labor, health, land, and repair.

To remake the world is to remake our relation to the earth and to one another. Slavery teaches that prosperity built on extraction is not innocent prosperity. It teaches that distance is a moral technology. It teaches that bodies remember political economy. It teaches that land, labor, race, and reproduction cannot be separated. An environmental justice movement adequate to the age of climate crisis must begin there: not with “humanity” as an undifferentiated species, but with the histories that made some humans masters of extraction and others its raw material.

Walter Johnson’s essay arrives at a moment when the question of justice is no longer academic, if it ever was. The Movement for Black Lives has forced the country to confront police killing, prisons, surveillance, economic abandonment, gendered violence, queer and trans vulnerability, schools, housing, health, and reparations as parts of one structure. The election of Donald Trump has revealed how fiercely that structure will defend itself. Johnson gives us a name for it: racial capitalism. But the name matters only if it helps us fight.

His first point, about “dehumanization,” speaks directly to contemporary politics. We are often told that racism begins when people fail to see others as human. There is truth in that, but it is not enough. Police officers, jailers, landlords, employers, judges, teachers, and politicians often know quite well that the people they harm are human. The officer knows the person he stops can feel fear. The prison administrator knows solitary confinement breaks minds. The landlord knows eviction can destroy a family. The employer knows a worker needs wages to live. The state knows deportation separates parents from children. The problem is not always failure of recognition. It is organized power over people whose humanity is fully available to be used against them.

Slavery makes this clear. The master did not need to imagine the enslaved as literally nonhuman. He needed to know they loved their children so he could threaten sale. He needed to know they feared pain so he could whip. He needed to know they could learn so he could train and prohibit literacy. It was a system designed to extract value from human capacities while denying human claims.

The issue is not merely that officers need to see black people as human. It is that the state has built institutions that treat black life as governable through suspicion, containment, extraction, and force. A camera may record a killing without preventing it. A training may teach respectful language while leaving intact the power to stop, frisk, arrest, jail, and shoot. Recognition without redistribution of power is not justice.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism also clarifies why the prison is central to any discussion of slavery’s afterlife. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery and involuntary servitude “except as a punishment for crime.” That exception did not by itself create mass incarceration, but it marked a path. After emancipation, black codes, convict leasing, chain gangs, vagrancy laws, debt peonage, and prison labor helped reimpose coercive labor and racial control. Rights can restrain the cage. Abolition asks why the cage is there.

Reparations should include direct compensation, but also community control of resources, debt cancellation, guaranteed income, free education, housing, health care, land, and investment in institutions accountable to black communities. Reparations are not charity. They are payment on a debt.

Critics ask how far back we must go. The answer is: as far back as the harm reaches into the present. The point is not inherited guilt. It is inherited structure.

The danger is real. So is the possibility. Millions have marched in recent days. Airports have filled with protesters against the travel ban. Local organizers have been building for years. The question is whether outrage can become organization.

To remake the world, we must refuse the small dreams offered to us. Representation without redistribution is not enough. Diversity in police departments is not enough. Body cameras are not enough. Equal opportunity to enter an unjust economy is not enough. A kinder empire is not enough. We need a world where no one’s body is collateral, no community is a sacrifice zone, no child is born into a debt created by stolen labor and stolen land, and no state claims the right to cage away the problems it refuses to solve.

Johnson’s essay gives us history as accusation and resource. Slavery was not outside modernity; it helped make the modern world. Therefore the modern world can be unmade and remade. The enslaved did not need to prove their humanity, and neither do those struggling now. The task is not to ask power to recognize us. The task is to build enough power that recognition is no longer the condition of survival.

"The essay argues that justice for the legacies of slavery requires moving beyond the limited framework of liberal human rights toward a politics of 'racial capitalism' and structural reparations."

Walter Johnson’s essay is at its strongest when it asks us to stop treating slavery as a moral exception to modern history. The phrase “dehumanization” often functions as a ritual of absolution: it allows us to say, with appropriate horror, that slavery was so terrible that it must have depended on some departure from humanity. Johnson’s counterclaim is more disturbing and more useful. Slavery did not require slaveholders to cease recognizing the humanity of enslaved people. It required them to exploit that humanity with precision.

This is not a small semantic point. The plantation depended on human intelligence, human memory, human fear, human kinship, human grief, human endurance, human sexuality, and human creativity. The enslaved were not treated as rocks or machines, though they were often compared to both. They were treated as people whose capacities could be disciplined, priced, mortgaged, bred, coerced, punished, and made productive. The whip was not applied to an object that could not feel. It was applied to a person because he or she could feel. The auction block did not merely misrecognize kinship; it monetized it by threatening to destroy it. Rape under slavery was not an incidental cruelty appended to labor exploitation; it was a form of domination made possible by law and by the political economy of human property.

Johnson is right, then, to worry that “dehumanization” can smuggle in a dubious premise: that humanity is something that can be lost by the victim rather than violated by the perpetrator. We can say, of course, that slaveholders denied the humanity of enslaved people. But even this needs qualification. They denied equality, dignity, autonomy, rights, and moral claims; they did not deny, in any stable or comprehensive way, the human capacities on which slavery rested. Indeed, the slave codes, patrols, passes, literacy prohibitions, marriage restrictions, and punishments for rebellion all testify that slaveholders knew perfectly well they were ruling human beings. No one writes a law against a plow learning to read.

The more provocative part of Johnson’s essay is his move from this critique of language to a critique of liberal human rights. Here, too, his argument deserves serious attention. The conventional moral architecture of rights has often been too thin to reckon with slavery’s afterlives. Rights can prohibit formal enslavement, formal disfranchisement, and formal segregation. They can recognize persons before the law. These are monumental achievements, and no one who studies slavery should sneer at them. But rights alone do not restore stolen land, return stolen wages, repair broken lineages, or dismantle inherited structures of wealth and exposure. A freed person with a right to contract but no land, no protection from terror, no access to credit, and no claim on the wealth created by generations of forced labor is politically emancipated but materially cornered.

Johnson’s invocation of Marx on political emancipation is therefore apt, even if one need not be a Marxist to see the problem. Legal equality can coexist with economic dependency. The formal subject of rights can be hungry, indebted, evicted, incarcerated, undereducated, overpoliced, and still told that justice has already been done because the law no longer names him property. The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments remade the constitutional order, but Reconstruction’s overthrow demonstrated that rights without power can be strangled by organized counterrevolution. The later victories of the civil rights movement were likewise indispensable, but the persistence of racialized poverty, residential segregation, policing disparities, and wealth inequality suggests that juridical inclusion is not the same as repair.

Johnson’s term “racial capitalism” helps describe this deeper continuity. The point is not that every institution in a capitalist society is identical to a plantation. Nor is it that class disappears into race, or race into class. It is that modern capitalism developed through racial differentiation: through slavery, conquest, colonial extraction, immigration exclusion, segregated labor markets, and uneven distributions of vulnerability and entitlement. The plantation and the factory were not the same workplace, but neither were they separate worlds. Cotton moved from enslaved hands in the Mississippi Valley to merchants, insurers, shippers, bankers, manufacturers, and consumers across the Atlantic. The abstraction of “the market” hides a series of concrete relationships: credit secured by enslaved bodies; land made available by Native dispossession; metropolitan industry fed by colonial and plantation raw materials; white labor politics shaped by proximity to and distance from racialized unfreedom.

One of the virtues of Johnson’s essay is that it refuses the comforting division between “economic” and “racial” history. Too often public debate proceeds as if class injustice is real and material, while racial injustice is symbolic and attitudinal. Or, conversely, as if racism is merely a prejudice floating above a neutral economy. Slavery exposes the absurdity of both views. Race was made in law, labor, property, sexuality, policing, accounting, and empire. It was not just an idea in someone’s head. It organized who could be owned, who could own, who could inherit, who could testify, who could move, who could be raped with impunity, who could claim land, who could borrow, and who could be believed.

That insight matters urgently in 2017. We are living through a political moment in which some commentators insist that economic anxiety among white voters must be understood apart from race, while others reduce white grievance to irrational bigotry. Johnson’s essay helps explain why the separation is false. White working-class politics in the United States has long been shaped by both vulnerability to capital and claims on racial entitlement. The white worker could be exploited by employers and still receive public recognition, legal protection, or psychological wages denied to black workers. Sometimes those wages were meager; sometimes they were substantial. But they mattered politically. They helped make cross-racial labor solidarity difficult, and they helped translate economic fear into racial backlash.

Still, Johnson’s argument should not be read as a counsel of despair. If racial capitalism was made, it can be unmade. But unmaking it requires a more ambitious politics than nondiscrimination. Reparations are central, not peripheral, to such a politics. The word is often caricatured as a demand for individual checks, as if the only question were whether a living white person owes money to a living black person for something neither personally did. That framing is designed to trivialize the issue. Reparations should begin from institutions and structures: the federal government that protected slavery and segregation; states and municipalities that enforced exclusion; universities, banks, insurers, churches, and corporations that profited from slavery or Jim Crow; property regimes built on indigenous dispossession; public policies that subsidized white wealth accumulation while denying the same to black citizens.

A reparative politics could include direct payments, but it should not end there. It should include land claims and tribal sovereignty; massive investment in black communities; debt relief; baby bonds or wealth-building accounts; housing restitution; school funding equalization; criminal justice transformation; health care access; environmental remediation; and democratic control over institutions that have extracted from the poor and racialized. The test is not whether a policy makes liberal observers feel innocent. The test is whether it changes the distribution of life chances produced by centuries of expropriation.

Johnson’s critique of “humanity” also pushes us to think differently about dignity. The task is not to prove that enslaved people were human by finding moments when they acted like liberal individuals choosing among options. The task is to understand the worlds they made under conditions they did not choose: families formed under threat of sale, worship under surveillance, knowledge transmitted despite bans, work slowed or redirected, rebellions planned, escapes attempted, children loved, dead mourned, futures imagined. These histories do not demonstrate humanity; they begin from it.

There is a danger, of course, that “racial capitalism” becomes a master phrase used to explain everything and therefore nothing. Johnson’s essay sometimes risks that. The concept must be disciplined by historical specificity. The cotton plantation, the Caribbean sugar estate, convict leasing, colonial rubber extraction, domestic service, migrant farm labor, prison labor, and global sweatshops are related but not identical. To call them all racial capitalism should sharpen our questions, not replace investigation. How exactly did law, credit, violence, kinship, gender, ecology, and labor combine in each case? Who profited? Who resisted? What changed? What persisted?

But this is a caution about use, not a rejection of the framework. Johnson’s central demand is that we change the standpoint from which we think about modern justice. If we begin from Europe after genocide, we may imagine human rights primarily as protections against state atrocity and violations of conscience. If we begin from slavery, colonialism, and indigenous dispossession, we will ask different questions: Who owns the land? Who accumulated wealth from forced labor? Whose reproduction was controlled? Whose communities were made disposable? Whose exposure to premature death was treated as the cost of prosperity? These are not questions that the language of universal rights can easily answer by itself.

The essay ends with Du Bois’s warning that there are moments when the world can be remade and that failure leaves a blood debt to future generations. That warning feels acute today. The United States has just inaugurated a president who rose in politics through birtherism, campaigned on walls and bans, and entered office amid open appeals to white grievance. But the deeper crisis did not begin with him. It lies in the long refusal to confront how our wealth, institutions, borders, police powers, and common sense were made.

To remake the world, we must first name it accurately. Johnson’s essay does not ask us to abandon the moral language of humanity because the enslaved were less than human. It asks us to abandon it because they were always fully human, and because their humanity was the very substance slavery exploited. That recognition should not make us more comfortable. It should make us less able to live with the arrangements that have descended from it.

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies slavery as a foundational component of racial capitalism, we should refine rather than abandon the concepts of dehumanization, universal rights, and humanity to better serve the pursuit of reparative justice."

Walter Johnson is right to insist that slavery was not a premodern residue, a moral aberration standing outside the history of capitalism, or a theater in which enslaved people’s humanity was somehow absent until recovered by historians. His essay is most powerful when it refuses the comforting distance created by words such as “inhuman.” The auction block, the ledger, the mortgage, the rape, the whipping post, the cotton bale, the insurance contract, and the bill of exchange all belonged to the same world. They were not capitalism’s opposite; they were among its conditions of possibility. To say this is not to flatten every distinction between a plantation and a factory, or between enslaved and wage labor. It is to ask why those distinctions were produced, who profited from them, and how they became racial common sense.

Still, I would hesitate before giving up entirely on the language of dehumanization, humanity, and rights. Johnson’s warning is indispensable: if “dehumanization” means that enslaved people lost their humanity, then the word repeats the injury it means to condemn. Enslaved people did not become less human because slaveholders treated them as property. Indeed, as Johnson argues, slavery depended precisely on enslaved people’s capacities as human beings: their knowledge, skill, memory, kinship, fear, hope, sexuality, fertility, and imagination. A mule could be worked; a person could be coerced, humiliated, threatened through loved ones, converted into collateral, and forced to reproduce wealth across generations. The violence of slavery lay not in a failure to perceive humanity but in the decision to exploit it.

But “dehumanization” can also name something else: not the condition of the oppressed, but the practice of the oppressor. Frederick Douglass did not need to prove his humanity to us, but he did spend his life exposing a society organized to deny it in law, custom, and political economy. Harriet Jacobs’s account of sexual coercion under slavery is not a plea to be admitted into humanity; it is an indictment of a regime that made black women’s bodies available to white power while wrapping white households in the language of virtue. Sojourner Truth’s “Ain’t I a Woman?”—whatever the complications of its transmission—has endured because it captured the cruelty of a world in which supposedly universal categories such as “woman,” “man,” “citizen,” and “human” were distributed through race. In this sense, dehumanization does not describe what happened inside the souls of the enslaved. It describes a social order that converted persons into things while never ceasing to draw upon their personhood.

The same caution applies to Johnson’s critique of rights. He is persuasive that a thin liberal account of rights is inadequate to slavery and its afterlives. The right to contract means little when one party inherits land, credit, police protection, and whiteness, while another inherits dispossession, debt, surveillance, and vulnerability. Formal equality can coexist with substantive domination; Reconstruction proved that with terrible clarity. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments were magnificent achievements, yet they did not prevent the overthrow of biracial democracy, the rise of convict leasing, debt peonage, disfranchisement, lynching, and Jim Crow. Political emancipation was real, and it was insufficient.

Yet the insufficiency of rights is not the same as their uselessness. Enslaved and formerly enslaved people did not generally treat rights as bourgeois distractions. They demanded the right to marry, to hold their children, to learn to read, to testify, to travel, to own land, to worship, to bear arms, to vote, to sit on juries, to be paid for labor, and to be protected from white violence. These demands were not reducible to possessive individualism. They were often collective, familial, religious, and material. They were claims about what kind of world freedom required. When freedpeople asked for land, schools, and political power, they were not merely asking to be recognized as abstract citizens. They were asking for the conditions under which citizenship could mean something.

For that reason, I would frame the matter less as rights versus reparative justice than as rights made honest by reparative justice. Human rights discourse, especially in its postwar institutional form, has often been selective and imperial. It has denounced atrocities while leaving untouched the economic arrangements that make whole populations disposable. It has been more comfortable with the language of conscience than with redistribution. But the answer cannot be to abandon universal claims altogether. A universalism reconstructed from Haiti, Reconstruction, anticolonial struggle, black feminism, indigenous sovereignty, and the Movement for Black Lives would look very different from one inherited from European liberalism alone. It would begin not with the unencumbered individual but with histories of theft, captivity, labor, reproduction, land, and state violence.

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism is especially useful here because it prevents us from separating race from economy as if one were merely cultural and the other material. The value of enslaved people as collateral, the expropriation of Native land, the dependence of British mills on Southern cotton, and the racial privileges extended to white workers were not separate stories. They were mutually reinforcing parts of a global system. Du Bois’s “wages of whiteness” remains one of the sharpest tools we have for understanding why people injured by capital may nevertheless cling to racial hierarchy. In the winter of 2017, when open white nationalism has become newly emboldened and economic grievance is so often narrated through fantasies of racial restoration, this insight is not antiquarian. It is urgent.

It must include money, land, housing, education, health, environmental protection, and democratic power. It must also attend, as Johnson insists, to gender and reproduction: to the long history by which black women’s labor, sexuality, and motherhood have been coerced, stigmatized, and exploited.

To heed those lessons is not to despair of humanity. It is to stop using “humanity” as an alibi and begin treating justice as a historical obligation.

A Defense of Rights, Reluctantly Offered

Walter Johnson's essay is so wide-ranging and so eloquent that any response risks pedantry. Let me concede at the outset what I take to be its central historical contribution: the integration of slavery into the history of capitalism is overdue, and the older view that consigned the plantation to a residual category outside the dynamics of modern accumulation looks more untenable with each passing year of new scholarship. I agree with most of Johnson's six "principal virtues." Where I want to push back is on his treatment of human rights — both as an analytical category in the historiography of slavery and as a political horizon for present-day struggles for justice. Johnson treats the liberal vocabulary of rights as a kind of trap. I think it remains one of the more powerful instruments we have, and that abandoning it is a mistake the left repeatedly makes to its cost.

Begin with Johnson's specific complaint about the historiography. He argues that historians who frame enslaved people's actions in terms of "agency," "independent will," and the like are anachronistically projecting the bourgeois liberal subject onto people whose actions were structured by quite different idioms. He cites Philip Morgan's *Slave Counterpoint* as an example. The criticism is not without bite. There is a tendency in some scholarship to treat any act of enslaved self-assertion as a vindication of universal liberal selfhood, as though the only meaningful question about the enslaved is whether they pass the test of resembling us.

But I think Johnson overstates the trap. The reason historians keep returning to questions of agency and humanity is not that they have been seduced by liberal anachronism. It is that the historical archive they work with — produced overwhelmingly by people who insisted, both legally and ideologically, on the non-personhood of the enslaved — generates an interpretive obligation. If your sources call a human being "wool" or "hand," and your discipline once took those sources at their face, then the work of demonstrating personhood is not paternalist; it is corrective. Johnson treats this work as if it placed black humanity "on probation." I think it is better described as releasing black humanity from the probation that the archive imposed upon it. The frame Johnson criticizes is the frame from which historians are escaping, not the frame in which they are stuck.

A similar point applies to the broader category of human rights. Johnson, following Moyn, dates the dominant contemporary regime of human rights to the mid-twentieth century, severs it from the longer history of antislavery, and treats it as the intellectual architecture of liberal neo-imperialism. There is something to this. The selective invocation of human rights as a pretext for military intervention is a real and ugly feature of the post-Cold War order. But to move from this critique to the suggestion that we should look elsewhere for the conceptual basis of justice — that the language of universal rights is fundamentally unresponsive to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery — is to give up too much.

Consider what rights actually do, at their best. They name a floor below which no human being is to be pushed, and they make that floor enforceable by institutions external to the immediate parties of any dispute. The reason the language of rights has been useful to enslaved and formerly enslaved people, again and again across the centuries, is not that it perfectly captures their experience or worldview. It is that it provides a vocabulary that can be addressed to those who hold power, in terms those powerholders are committed (however hypocritically) to recognizing. Frederick Douglass did not need to be reminded that the framers' invocation of universal rights was bourgeois, partial, and stained by complicity with slavery. He nevertheless saw, more clearly than many of his successors, that holding the bourgeois at his own word was the most effective lever available. The Civil Rights Movement made similar use of the same lever a century later.

Johnson would respond, I think, that rights-based reform produces only partial, fragile, and easily reversible gains, and that the deeper structures of racial capitalism remain untouched. The historical record gives this argument some weight. The dismantling of legal segregation has not produced economic parity. The formal end of slavery did not end the global pattern of racial extraction. Fair enough. But the question is whether the inadequacy of rights-based emancipation is grounds for moving past it, or grounds for taking it more seriously and pushing it further. Marx, whom Johnson quotes, did not argue that political emancipation should be discarded; he called it "a big step forward" and described it as the most complete form of emancipation available within "the hitherto existing world order." The implication is that you do not skip the step. You build on it.

I worry that Johnson's program — restorative justice and reparations rooted in a standpoint of the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed" — risks becoming a substitute for, rather than a supplement to, the slower work of universal rights enforcement. Reparations, to be politically effective, will need a framework of universal rights to operate within. The argument for reparations is fundamentally an argument about historical violations of rights that human beings hold simply by being human. Without that universal premise, the reparations claim collapses into either a claim of group entitlement (which is politically weaker) or a claim of historical particularity (which is politically narrower). The strongest moral case for reparations runs through, not around, the universalist tradition Johnson treats as exhausted.

There is also an empirical case for the continuing utility of rights talk. Consider the geography of human-rights mobilization over the past two decades — Tahrir Square, Hong Kong, the indigenous rights movements of Latin America, the Movement for Black Lives itself. None of these are content with the neo-imperial deployment of human rights by Western governments, but neither do they reject the underlying universalist vocabulary. They claim it, often more rigorously than the governments that nominally champion it. The vocabulary of rights is, at this moment in history, one of the few cross-cultural moral idioms with real political traction. To abandon it because it has been put to bad uses is to surrender one of the few weapons that consistently embarrasses the powerful.

I do not want to be misread as defending the neoliberal status quo, which has weaponized rights talk against substantive equality in any number of ways. Johnson is right that property rights, freedom of contract, and the like have done immense work in entrenching the inequalities he describes. The libertarian appropriation of rights discourse is a genuine problem. But the response is to fight for a richer conception of rights — one that includes the social and economic rights long recognized in international declarations but persistently weakened in domestic implementation — not to abandon the discourse to its libertarian appropriators.

So I read Johnson's essay as offering a powerful historical reframing that does not, in the end, require the political conclusion he draws. The history of racial capitalism is real, and any adequate response to it must reckon with the global patterns of extraction Johnson describes. But the moral and political resources for responding to those patterns include, ineliminably, the universal language of rights — not as the final form of human emancipation, but as the most powerful tool available within the world order we actually inhabit. The choice is not between rights and reparations. It is between a rights tradition that includes reparative obligations and one that does not. Johnson's history should be marshaled in service of the former, not against the rights tradition as such.

The sixth of Walter Johnson's "principal virtues" is the one I want to take up: the suggestion that an account of justice rooted in racial capitalism can connect a critique of the instrumentalization of human beings to a critique of the instrumentalization of nature, "over and against many recent efforts which assert that a forthright treatment of global environmental history requires the elevation of the categories of the 'human' and the 'Anthropocene' over and against other historical categories." The debate Johnson is alluding to is the debate over the Anthropocene — the proposed geological epoch defined by human impact on the Earth system. Scholars working in environmental humanities have, over the past decade, raised the question of whether "the human" is the right unit of analysis for the planetary crisis we are confronting. The argument that has emerged from critical quarters — Donna Haraway, Jason Moore, Kathryn Yusoff, T. J. Demos, and others — is that the Anthropocene framing obscures more than it illuminates. The "anthropos" that has driven planetary transformation is not humanity in general but particular human populations, organized in particular economic systems, pursuing particular forms of accumulation. To say "humans" caused climate change is to occlude the historical fact that the carbon emissions in question were generated overwhelmingly by particular polities (the industrialized North Atlantic), particular classes (the owners of fossil capital), and particular projects (industrial capitalism, colonial extraction, the postwar consumer society).

Alternatives have been proposed. Moore has argued for "Capitalocene," locating the geological signature of human impact in the rise of capitalism specifically rather than in human activity generally. Haraway has proposed "Chthulucene" and, more pointedly for our purposes, "Plantationocene," arguing that the plantation — as a specific form of biological and labor organization — is the deeper origin of the planetary patterns we are now living with. Yusoff, in work that is just beginning to circulate, has argued that geology itself, as a science and as a mode of relating to the Earth, is implicated in the racial and colonial structures that the Anthropocene framing tends to obscure.

Johnson's essay sits comfortably alongside these alternatives without quite endorsing any of them by name. What he offers is a framework — racial capitalism — that can do similar work to "Capitalocene" or "Plantationocene" but with more historical specificity than either. The strength of Johnson's framework, for environmental purposes, is that it locates the origin of the planetary transformation we are now living through in a specific set of historical processes: the integration of African enslavement, Indigenous dispossession, and fossil-powered industrial production into a single global system of accumulation. The plantation, the mill, and the railroad were not three separate developments that happened to coincide; they were components of a single system that, taken together, set the planet on its current trajectory.

Let me develop this with some specifics. The cotton plantation of the antebellum South was, by the standards of its time, one of the most ecologically transformative institutions on Earth. The clearing of forest for cotton cultivation, the exhaustion of soil that drove the constant westward expansion of the plantation frontier, the displacement of native ecologies by monocultural production, the introduction of new species (cotton itself, but also the various plants and animals that traveled with the plantation system) — these were not side effects of plantation production but constitutive features of it. The plantation was, among other things, a machine for converting ecological diversity into commodity uniformity, with consequences for soil, water, biodiversity, and climate that we are still living with.

Crucially, the plantation also pioneered modes of biological and labor organization that have been generalized far beyond their original site. The disciplined, time-managed, output-optimized organization of labor that we associate with industrial capitalism was, as historians like Caitlin Rosenthal have argued, developed first on the plantation. The use of detailed accounting techniques to measure and increase labor output; the systematic breeding of plants and animals (and, monstrously, of human beings) for productive characteristics; the management of large populations in confined spaces — these technologies of biopower were refined on the plantation before they were generalized to the factory, the feedlot, and the modern industrial farm. Haraway's term "Plantationocene" tries to capture this lineage: the planetary patterns of biological management we now live within have a specific origin in the plantation form, and that form was racial from its inception.

If we follow this line of thought, the planetary environmental crisis is not detachable from the racial-capitalist history Johnson narrates. The carbon emissions that are now altering the Earth's climate were generated by an industrial economy whose original capitalization came from plantation slavery. The species extinctions we are now witnessing trace back, in significant part, to land-use patterns established by plantation agriculture and colonial extraction. The vulnerabilities to climate disruption are distributed along racial and colonial lines: the populations most exposed to rising seas, intensifying storms, agricultural disruption, and heat stress are predominantly the populations whose ancestors bore the brunt of the racial-capitalist accumulation that generated the crisis in the first place. This is not coincidence. It is the continuing operation of the system Johnson describes.

What follows for the politics of climate? Several things, I think.

First, climate justice cannot be reduced to a question of emissions reduction abstractly considered. The "we" of humanity-in-general that the Anthropocene framing posits is not the relevant political subject. The relevant subjects are differentiated: those most responsible for emissions, those most vulnerable to consequences, those whose forms of life have been most destroyed by the system that generated the crisis. A climate politics adequate to the moment must hold these differentiations at its center.

Second, climate redress has a reparative dimension that is continuous with the reparative dimension of the racial-capitalist accumulation that produced the crisis. The Global North owes the Global South not just emissions reductions but transfers — of resources, of technology, of accumulated knowledge — that respond to the historical fact of asymmetric extraction. The "loss and damage" framework that has been struggling for traction in international climate negotiations is one institutional expression of this. So is the broader argument that climate finance must be additional to, not substitutive for, development aid that was itself inadequate compensation for colonial extraction.

Third, the response to the climate crisis cannot be technocratic in a way that leaves the underlying system intact. Carbon capture, geoengineering, market-based emissions trading — these can be parts of a response, but they cannot be the whole response, because they leave intact the patterns of differential extraction and vulnerability that constitute the deeper problem. A genuinely transformative climate politics has to be a politics of system change, in something close to the sense the climate-justice movement has been arguing.

Fourth, the relationship between ecological domination and racial domination is not, as Johnson rightly insists, analogical. It is historical and structural. The same processes that produced the racialization of human populations also produced the instrumentalization of nature, and the categories that organized one organized the other. Sylvia Wynter has argued that the Western conception of "Man" — bourgeois, rational, propertied, white — was constructed in opposition not just to non-Western humans but to nature, and that the same conceptual operation that produced racial hierarchy produced the domination of the natural world. The undoing of one is bound up with the undoing of the other.

This is where I think Johnson's framework can do more than he claims. It can serve not just as a framework for understanding the history of racial capitalism but as a framework for understanding the planetary crisis we are now confronting. The "world-wasting race to the end of time" Johnson invokes is not a metaphor; it is a description of the trajectory the system is on. The plantation, the slave ship, the cotton mill, the coal pit, the oil well, the suburban subdivision, the data center — these are episodes in a single history, and that history is now bringing us up against planetary limits.

The political implication is not despair. It is that the work of dismantling racial capitalism and the work of addressing the planetary crisis are, properly understood, the same work. The frameworks that treat them as separate — environment over here, justice over there — are inadequate to both. The frameworks that treat them as continuous — racial capitalism, Plantationocene, climate justice — are doing the deeper diagnostic work the moment requires. Johnson's essay points in this direction. The next step is to make the connection explicit and to develop the politics that follows from it.

Walter Johnson's essay accomplishes something important and, for that reason, deserves the kind of friction that comes from taking it seriously rather than nodding along. His indictment of the "dehumanization" trope is largely persuasive, his insistence on the entanglement of slavery and capitalism is by now (and not coincidentally) the most generative line of inquiry in the field, and his summons to ground a theory of justice in the history of slavery and racial capitalism rather than in twentieth-century European catastrophe is exactly the kind of provocation American intellectual life needs. But the essay's strongest moves are sometimes also its most overdrawn, and the version of "racial capitalism" it leaves us with risks becoming a master concept that, like the liberal-humanist frame it displaces, does too much work too easily. Let me press on three points.

First, on dehumanization. Johnson is right that the word, taken literally, performs a kind of accidental violence: it suggests that enslavers managed (somehow) to extract or suspend the humanity of their captives, and that historians' principal task is to demonstrate that this humanity persisted, was restored, or was reclaimed. That framing turns black humanity into a perpetually probationary fact and turns slaveholding into an inhuman aberration rather than a recognizable human practice. Johnson's reminder that slavery depended on the human capacities of the enslaved—their intelligence, their fertility, their sentience, their capacity to suffer in legible ways—is essential, and he is right to point out that violence presupposed rather than negated the humanity of its objects. The torturer needs a witness.

Still, I would resist the conclusion that "dehumanization" is simply a category error to be discarded. The term names something real about the social and legal status that slaveholders sought to fix on the enslaved: a condition in which one's status as a moral or jural person was systematically denied even as one's biological and cognitive capacities were exploited. Orlando Patterson's "social death," for all its limits, gestures at this gap between what was extracted from enslaved people and what was conceded to them. Saidiya Hartman, whom Johnson surely has in mind, has shown how the law's selective recognition of slave humanity—its acknowledgment of criminal agency, for instance, alongside the denial of contract or kinship—was itself a technology of subjection. "Dehumanization" need not denote the metaphysical erasure of personhood; it can name the political project of producing a being whose humanity is exploited without being recognized. Johnson's critique is best read as a warning about lazy usage, not as a verdict that the concept has no purchase.

Second, on racial capitalism. Johnson, following Cedric Robinson and Du Bois, argues that there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery: Manchester did not happen without Mississippi. As an empirical claim about the cotton economy of the long nineteenth century, this is now hard to dispute; the work of Sven Beckert, Walter Johnson himself in *River of Dark Dreams*, Edward Baptist, and the contributors to *Slavery's Capitalism* has made James Oakes's counterfactual ("would Britain have industrialized without slavery?") look exactly as ahistorical as Johnson says it is. And Du Bois's collapsing of the orthodox firewall between slavery and capitalism in *Black Reconstruction* deserves its belated canonization.

But it is one thing to insist that the historical capitalism we actually had was constituted through racial slavery, settler expropriation, and colonial extraction; it is another to argue, as Robinson sometimes does and as Johnson seems to here, that capitalism is *constitutively* racial—that race and capital are inseparable in principle, not just in fact. The first claim is a historical thesis. The second is closer to a philosophical one, and it carries a heavy burden. If racial differentiation is intrinsic to capital accumulation, what do we make of capitalist economies whose hierarchies of expendability run along non-racial lines (caste, sect, region, citizenship)? Are these to be reabsorbed into "race" by definitional fiat, or do we need a more capacious account of how capital opportunistically seizes upon whatever lines of human difference are at hand? I suspect the answer is the latter, and I suspect Johnson would agree, but the rhetoric of his essay tends to elide the distinction. The danger is that "racial capitalism" becomes a name for everything bad about modernity, at which point it loses its analytical edge precisely because it has become a synonym for the world.

Third, on rights. Here I think Johnson moves too quickly. He follows Samuel Moyn in dating the contemporary regime of human rights to the late twentieth century and in seeing it as the ideological scaffolding of liberal neo-imperialism. He then proposes that justice be reconceived not through rights but through reparations, restorative justice, and a critique of racial capitalism. The six "virtues" he enumerates at the end of the essay are genuinely attractive: a Global South standpoint, attention to extraction and distribution, an emphasis on the historical sedimentation of present privilege, sensitivity to gender and social reproduction, a unified analysis of race and class, and a link between the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature. These are virtues worth wanting.

But what is the relationship between this program and the rights tradition? Johnson sometimes writes as if rights talk is to be jettisoned in favor of something deeper; at other times as if it is to be supplemented or radicalized. The Movement for Black Lives platform he cites, after all, is not anti-rights; it makes rights claims (to reparations, to investment, to economic justice) that rest on universalist premises even as they are anchored in the particular history of slavery and Jim Crow. The history of abolition itself—from David Walker to Frederick Douglass to Anna Julia Cooper—was not a history outside the rights tradition; it was a history of forcing that tradition to live up to commitments it had betrayed. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction*, which Johnson rightly celebrates, treats the postwar amendments as both an unfinished revolution and a usable inheritance. The question is whether we treat the language of rights as a thin veneer over racial capitalism (to be peeled away) or as a contested terrain on which redistributive and reparative claims can be lodged. The latter strikes me as both more historically faithful and more politically generative.

A final note. Johnson ends by warning, with Du Bois, that there are moments propitious for remaking the world, and that the cost of missing them is paid in the blood of subsequent generations. Writing in February 2017, with a new administration committed to nativism, deregulation, and the open celebration of white grievance, the warning lands. If the "new history of capitalism" is to do more than enrich our footnotes, it must connect its account of how the world was made to an argument about how it can be unmade. Johnson's essay is one of the more serious efforts to draw that connection. Its arguments deserve to be tested, not embraced—which is, I take it, what he would want.

Walter Johnson’s essay is bracing, brilliant, and, in crucial respects, dangerously wrong. Let me begin with a point of agreement. Johnson’s critique of “dehumanization” talk is a necessary corrective. The idea that slaveholders had to somehow trick themselves into believing their slaves were not human before they could exploit and abuse them is a comforting fiction for those of us who live after. It implies that atrocity requires a prior ideological erasure of the victim’s humanity—that normal, decent people could never do such things to fellow humans. But history teaches otherwise. Humans have proven themselves abundantly capable of inflicting extraordinary cruelty on people they know perfectly well are human: enemies in war, heretics in religious conflict, political rivals, and, yes, enslaved people whose labor, reproduction, and sentience were the very reasons for their enslavement. The slaveholder who beats a field hand does not need to believe that hand is an animal; he needs to believe he has the right to do it, and that the hand’s humanity—her capacity to feel pain, fear, and humiliation—is precisely what makes the exercise of power satisfying and effective. Johnson is right: the “dehumanization” thesis lets perpetrators off the hook by pathologizing their actions, and it patronizes the enslaved by implying that their humanity is something that can be taken away by an oppressor’s actions rather than an inalienable fact of their existence.

But here the trouble begins. Having diagnosed the problem with one form of universalism—the abstract, bourgeois liberalism that treats enslaved people as proto-citizens striving for rights they could not yet name—Johnson proceeds to indict the entire tradition of human rights as inadequate to the task of justice. He draws on Samuel Moyn’s argument that human-rights thinking is a creature of the late twentieth century, not the age of abolition, and that it has served as “the justifying terms of continuing European and American intervention in the affairs of former colonies.” Against this, he proposes an alternative genealogy of freedom, one rooted in the black radical tradition and culminating in calls for reparations and a thoroughgoing restructuring of the global political economy. He sketches six “virtues” of an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery, including its focus on resource extraction between Global North and South, its attention to past patterns of privilege and abjection, its insistence on restorative rather than merely rights-based remedies, and its linking of racial and environmental domination.

This is stirring stuff, but it raises a question Johnson never adequately answers: Who is going to implement this justice, and by what authority? The universalist language of human rights, for all its flaws and hypocrisies, provides at least a partial answer to that question. It says that every person, by virtue of being human, has certain claims that can be made against any power—against the state, against the corporation, against the international order. Those claims can be articulated in courts of law, in international forums, in the court of public opinion. The language of rights is a language of demand, and it has been taken up by the dispossessed again and again, not because they are dupes of liberal imperialism, but because it gives them a tool with which to fight back.

Consider the movement for reparations that Johnson himself celebrates. The case for reparations does not rest solely on the particular history of African American suffering, important as that history is. It also rests on a universal principle: that when a wrong has been done, the wrongdoer has an obligation to make it right. That principle is not the exclusive property of the black radical tradition; it is a fundamental tenet of justice across cultures and legal systems. When advocates for reparations argue that the United States government owes a debt to the descendants of enslaved people, they are appealing to norms of fairness and accountability that are, in their most general form, universal. They are saying: You did this; you benefited from this; you must repair this. Without that universalist backing, the demand for reparations becomes merely an assertion of group interest—no more binding on white Americans than their own assertion of group interest in keeping what they have.

Johnson might reply that the universalism he rejects is a specifically Western, liberal universalism, one that smuggles in bourgeois assumptions about property, individuality, and the state. What would such a justice look like in practice? What institutions would embody it? What principles would guide the distribution of resources, the adjudication of disputes, the restraint of power? The black radical tradition is rich in critique, but it has been less forthcoming with blueprints. Political emancipation may not be the “final form” of human freedom, but it is not nothing. The danger of Johnson’s argument is that, in its zeal to expose the inadequacies of liberal universalism, it risks ceding the moral high ground to those who would dismantle even the limited protections that exist. We live in a moment when the language of human rights is under sustained assault—from ethnonationalists who see it as a cover for globalism, from authoritarians who see it as a threat to sovereignty, from corporate interests that see it as an obstacle to profit. In such a moment, it is not enough to say that liberal rights are insufficient. We must also defend them, even as we push beyond them. The challenge is to hold two thoughts at once: that the rights-bearing individual of liberal theory is an abstraction that obscures the real structures of racial and economic domination, and that the idea of rights-bearing individuals has been a powerful weapon in the hands of the dominated. Frederick Douglass understood this. So did Martin Luther King Jr. So did the women who organized at Seneca Falls, the workers who struck at Flint, the activists who faced down tanks in Tiananmen Square. They did not imagine that winning their rights would bring about the final emancipation of humanity. But they knew that rights were worth fighting for.

Johnson concludes by invoking Du Bois’s warning about the cost of missed opportunities, the blood of subsequent generations who pay for the failures of their forebears. It is a powerful image, and it cuts both ways. In Defense of "Dehumanization," Cautiously

Walter Johnson opens his essay with a striking claim: that the language of "dehumanization," as applied to slavery, is misleading, harmful, and worth resisting. The argument is provocative, and Johnson develops it with the care his readers have come to expect. But I think he is wrong, or at least more wrong than he is right, about this particular term — and I want to spend my response defending a chastened version of "dehumanization" against his critique.

Johnson's argument against the term has two main parts. First, it is misleading because slavery in fact depended on the human capacities of the enslaved — their labor, their sentience, their fear, their pain. Second, it is harmful because the language imposes on the enslaved a kind of secondary injury: it suggests they were alienated from their humanity, and it places observers in a paternalist position of judging when and whether that humanity has been restored. Both criticisms have force. But I think the force can be acknowledged without abandoning the term, and there are good reasons not to abandon it.

Consider the first criticism. Johnson is right that slavery did not require the enslaved to actually become non-human in any descriptive sense. The slaveholder needed the slave to be human in order to exploit, fear, manipulate, and torture her. But this is not what the term "dehumanization" denotes when philosophers and historians use it carefully. Dehumanization, in the philosophical literature, is typically not a descriptive claim about the actual ontological status of the dehumanized; it is a claim about the perceptual and ideological structure of the dehumanizer. To dehumanize is to perceive, or to ideologically construct, the other as less than fully human — even while continuing to depend on capacities that one's perceptual frame officially denies. The contradiction is internal to the perpetrator, not to the victim.

This is, I think, the structure of most serious accounts of dehumanization in the literature on mass violence — David Livingstone Smith's work, for example, or the older sociological literature on dehumanization in modern warfare. The dehumanizer is engaged in a kind of perceptual self-deception, holding the other as both human (in capacities) and sub-human (in moral standing). The contradiction is not resolved; it is sustained, and it is what allows the violence to proceed. To say that slavery dehumanized the enslaved, on this understanding, is not to say that the enslaved actually became less than human. It is to say that the slaveholder operated within a perceptual and ideological frame that classified them as less than human, even as it depended on their human capacities. This is closer to what historians like Philip Morgan are typically trying to say, even when they put it less precisely.

Now Johnson might respond that even this more carefully formulated version of dehumanization smuggles in a problematic normative claim — namely, that there is a stable category of "the human" against which dehumanization can be measured, and that the project of restoring or affirming the humanity of the enslaved presupposes our ability to occupy that category in a way the enslaved supposedly could not. This is the deeper version of his second criticism, and it is the one I want to engage with most seriously.

There is something right in the worry. The category of "the human" has been a fraught one in Western thought, repeatedly defined in ways that excluded those who did not fit a particular cultural or biological template. The history of "humanism" is, as Sylvia Wynter and others have argued, a history of differentiated humanity, in which "Man" — typically European, male, propertied, rational — has been positioned as the model against which all other humans are measured. To invoke "the human" as the standard against which dehumanization is to be measured is, on this view, already to be operating within a problematic framework.

But notice what happens if we follow this critique all the way down. We end up with a position in which there is no stable notion of the human from which moral claims about slavery can be made. Slavery cannot be called "inhuman" because there is no category of "the human" to contrast it with. The violations of slavery cannot be called violations of human capacity because the category of human capacity is itself ideological. This is, I think, an untenable position. The reason "dehumanization" has persisted as a category in spite of its difficulties is that it names something real and important: the construction of moral hierarchies that classify some human beings as less deserving of moral consideration than others. To abandon the term is to lose a vocabulary we cannot yet do without.

The question, then, is whether we can hold on to the term while addressing Johnson's legitimate concerns. I think we can. The first move is to distinguish carefully between dehumanization-as-description and dehumanization-as-perception. The enslaved were not, descriptively, dehumanized; they remained fully human throughout, in all the capacities that constitute humanity. But they were subjected to a system that perceived and treated them as less than fully human. The dehumanization is in the system, not in the persons. This is a subtle distinction but a crucial one, and it preserves the moral force of the term without imposing on the enslaved the secondary injury Johnson worries about.

The second move is to be careful about the relationship between dehumanization talk and the project of historical recovery. Johnson is right that there is something unsatisfying about a historiography that frames its central task as "discovering" the humanity of the enslaved, as if that humanity were ever in genuine question. But I do not think most contemporary historians are doing exactly this. What they are doing — what Morgan is doing in the passages Johnson quotes — is reconstructing the lifeworlds, agency, creativity, and moral seriousness of the enslaved against an archive that systematically obscured these things. The point is not to prove that the enslaved were human (which no one with any sense doubts) but to give weight, texture, and specificity to lives that the archive was designed to flatten. Johnson conflates these two projects, and the conflation does damage to his argument.

There is also a generational point worth making. The historiographic moves Johnson criticizes — the insistence on the agency and humanity of the enslaved — were developed in the 1960s and 1970s against an older historiography (Phillips, Elkins) that had genuinely treated the enslaved as objects of history rather than its subjects. The work of John Blassingame, Eugene Genovese, Lawrence Levine, and others was a corrective to a tradition that did need correcting. Johnson is in a sense complaining about the success of that corrective: now that the agency of the enslaved is established, perhaps the framework that established it has become a confinement. There is something to this, but it is worth noting that the framework remains contested in some quarters, and that its dismantling in academic discourse may not be received in the same way in public discourse, where the older paternalist framings retain considerable life.

Where I think Johnson is most powerful is in his observation about the implicit liberalism of the "agency" framework — the way it tends to assimilate enslaved action to the model of the bourgeois liberal subject choosing among options. Singing a spiritual, breaking a tool, fomenting a revolution: these can be read as expressions of liberal selfhood, and there is something thin and anachronistic about that reading. The cultural and religious idioms within which enslaved action took place do not reduce to the framework of intentional choice. A richer historiography would attend to the specific moral and cosmological universes the enslaved inhabited — and there has been important work in this direction, by scholars like Stephanie Camp, Walter Rucker, and others.

So my position is this: Johnson is right that the language of agency, humanity, and dehumanization has been deployed in ways that can flatten the specificity of enslaved experience and impose anachronistic frameworks. He is wrong that this requires abandoning the language. A more careful use of these terms — one that distinguishes perceptual from descriptive dehumanization, that does not frame the historian's task as proving humanity, and that attends to the specific cultural idioms of enslaved life — preserves what is valuable in the inherited vocabulary while addressing what is problematic.

There is one final point I want to register, which concerns the politics of vocabulary. Johnson's critique of "dehumanization" is being mounted at a moment when the actual political defense of the humanity of black people remains, in the United States and elsewhere, an ongoing struggle. The Movement for Black Lives takes its very name from an insistence on the basic moral status of black life that significant portions of the polity continue to deny. In this context, the philosophical refinement of our vocabulary needs to be calibrated against the political work that vocabulary continues to do. "Dehumanization" remains a useful word in public discourse because it names, with rhetorical force, a moral failure that is still being committed. To strip it from the vocabulary on philosophical grounds, however well-reasoned, is to risk disarming a discourse that needs the weapons it has.

Let us begin with a concession: Walter Johnson is a historian of formidable talent and moral seriousness. His essay prosecutes a case against the concept of “dehumanization” with a prosecutor’s zeal and a philosopher’s precision. And he is right that the history of capitalism cannot be told without the history of slavery, and that the history of slavery cannot be told without the history of race. These are important arguments, and I am grateful to have them made so forcefully.

But I want to suggest that Johnson’s essay, for all its radical ambitions, is curiously conservative in its understanding of what justice requires. By “conservative,” I do not mean politically right-wing—Johnson’s politics are clearly left—but rather something like “rooted in the past.” Johnson’s account of justice is almost entirely backward-looking. It insists that present distributions of privilege and abjection are related to past patterns; that reparations are the only adequate form of redress; that the standpoint from which we should critique modern injustice is the standpoint of the enslaved and the dispossessed. What it does not do is offer a vision of the future. It does not describe, even in outline, what a just society would look like once the wrongs of the past had been acknowledged and repaired. It does not specify the principles that should govern our common life going forward. It leaves us, in the end, with a diagnosis but not a cure.

This backward-looking orientation is not unique to Johnson; it is characteristic of a certain strand of left thought that has gained prominence in recent years, one that is more comfortable with critique than with construction. The reasons for this are understandable. The left has been burned many times by utopian projects that turned into nightmares; by blueprints for a just society that, when implemented, produced new forms of injustice. Better, perhaps, to focus on exposing the injustices of the present than to risk repeating the mistakes of the past. But this posture of permanent critique has costs. It cedes the terrain of the future to those who are perfectly willing to offer their own visions: the libertarian who promises freedom through markets, the nationalist who promises security through walls, the techno-optimist who promises abundance through innovation. If the left cannot say what kind of world it wants, it should not be surprised when other people fill the silence.

He has cited a tradition of thinkers—Du Bois, Williams, Robinson, Davis—who, between them, have provided the intellectual resources for a radical critique of the existing order. It requires an account of what human beings need to flourish, and of what we owe each other as a matter of principle. The liberal rights tradition that Johnson dismisses has, for all its flaws, attempted to provide such an account. It has said, in various formulations, that every person has a claim to certain basic liberties, certain material minimums, and a certain degree of social recognition. This is not to say that a universalist account of justice is easy to construct, or that the liberal tradition has gotten it right. Far from it. The liberal tradition has been blind, again and again, to the ways that its universalism has been undercut by racism, sexism, and imperialism. That work has been done, brilliantly, by thinkers like Amartya Sen, Martha Nussbaum, and Charles Mills, who have taken the liberal tradition seriously while exposing its failures. Johnson, by contrast, seems to want to throw the whole tradition overboard and start fresh from the history of slavery. I am not convinced that this is possible, or desirable.

Let me conclude with a suggestion. Johnson’s six “virtues” of an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery are, in fact, excellent criteria for evaluating any theory of justice. Any adequate theory should be attentive to global inequalities, to historical legacies, to gender, to the entanglement of race and class, to the relationship between human and environmental exploitation. These are not alternatives to universalist principles; they are tests that any universalist principle must pass. The task before us is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, but to bring them into productive tension—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and hypocrisies in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to generalize the insights gained from the history of slavery. This is a dialectical project, not a dismissive one. It requires patience, nuance, and a willingness to live with contradiction. But it is, I believe, the only way to honor the legacy of those whom Johnson rightly remembers, while also building a future in which their suffering can finally be redeemed.

But this distinction is less sharp than he imagines. The experience of slavery is, among other things, an experience of radical rights-violation. Enslaved people were denied the right to control their own bodies, to keep their own children, to speak their own languages, to worship their own gods. To say that they were denied these rights is not to impose a liberal framework on their experience; it is to describe, in the plainest possible language, what slavery did. And when enslaved people resisted—when they ran away, when they broke tools, when they poisoned masters, when they sang spirituals that encoded dreams of freedom—they were, among other things, asserting rights. Not rights in the narrow legal sense, but rights in the deeper sense that my grandmother recognized: the right to be a person among persons, to have one’s dignity acknowledged, to not be used as a means to another’s end.

Johnson’s problem with rights language is not just that it is insufficient; it is that it is distorting. By framing enslaved people as rights-bearers manqués, he argues, historians project a liberal subjectivity onto people who lived in a world radically different from our own. This is a legitimate concern. We should not assume that enslaved people wanted the same things we want, or that they would have expressed their aspirations in the vocabulary of the U.S. Constitution. But Johnson overcorrects. The desire for freedom from domination, for bodily integrity, for family intactness, for meaningful control over one’s labor—these are not parochial products of the European Enlightenment. They are, as far as I can tell, human universals, or close to it. They show up in every culture, in every historical period, however differently they are inflected by local circumstances. To deny this, as Johnson comes close to doing, is to risk a different kind of distortion: a kind of radical particularism that leaves us without the moral resources to criticize practices we find abhorrent in cultures other than our own.

Let me put the point more sharply. If the rights tradition is as thoroughly compromised as Johnson suggests—if it is inextricably bound up with imperialism, with capitalism, with the “hitherto existing world order”—then what is the basis for his own critique? From what standpoint does he condemn the “resource-extracting, race-differentiating, world-wasting race to the end of time” that Du Bois described? If the answer is “the standpoint of the enslaved,” I am not sure that gets us very far. The standpoint of the enslaved is not a unified thing; enslaved people had different experiences, different strategies, different aspirations. Some of them collaborated with their masters; some of them became masters themselves when circumstances allowed. The “black radical tradition” is a tradition because later thinkers selected and synthesized elements of that experience, not because it speaks with one voice. And the selection and synthesis were themselves informed by values—equality, solidarity, liberation—that are not the exclusive property of any one tradition.

I do not want to be unfair. Johnson’s essay is a powerful reminder that the history of slavery has not been adequately integrated into our thinking about justice, and that the particular forms of wrong involved in slavery—the turning of human beings into capital, the reproductive exploitation of women, the destruction of family ties, the theft of land and labor—require particular forms of redress. The call for reparations that he endorses is not just a matter of cutting checks; it is a demand for a fundamental reckoning with the structure of the American political economy. On this, we agree completely.

But I worry that Johnson’s rejection of rights language will make that reckoning harder to achieve, not easier. The movement for reparations in the United States has gained traction in recent years not because activists abandoned the language of rights but because they deepened it. They argued that the harms of slavery and Jim Crow were not just private wrongs but violations of legally cognizable rights—rights to property, to equal protection, to due process—that the U.S. government had systematically denied. The groundbreaking work of scholars like Mary Frances Berry and Charles Ogletree has been to show that reparations claims can be framed in the language of American law, drawing on precedents from the restitution of Japanese American internees to the settlements with Native American tribes. This strategy may not satisfy the radical demand for a wholesale overthrow of racial capitalism, but it has the advantage of being actionable in courts and legislatures.

My grandmother died in 2011, before the Movement for Black Lives, before Ta-Nehisi Coates’s essay on reparations, before the current resurgence of white nationalism. I do not know what she would have made of these developments. But I know that she would have been pleased to see so many young people marching, organizing, demanding justice. She would have recognized in their demands something of her own: the simple, stubborn conviction that she was a human being, that her humanity was not up for debate, and that the world owed her more than it had given. Call that conviction what you will. To her, it was a right.

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies reproductive exploitation as the foundation of racial capitalism, a truly transformative politics of justice must bridge the gap between the specific history of enslaved women and the universal moral force of the human rights tradition."

Walter Johnson has done us a service by writing this essay, and the *Boston Review* has done us a service by publishing it. It is a rich, dense, ambitious piece of work that deserves to be read slowly and argued with vigorously. I have learned from it, even where I have disagreed.

I want to focus my response on a single thread that runs through the essay but is never fully pulled: the thread of reproduction. Johnson argues that the history of racial capitalism is, in a crucial sense, a history of reproduction. The enslaved person was both labor and capital, and the reproduction of that dual entity—through the slave trade on one hand, through natural increase on the other—was the hinge on which the entire system swung. “The entire ‘pyramid’ of the Atlantic economy,” he writes, “was founded upon the capacity of enslaved women’s bodies: upon their ability to reproduce capital.” This is a powerful formulation, and it draws on the work of scholars like Deborah Gray White and Angela Davis, who have long insisted that the experience of enslaved women must be at the center of any account of slavery. But Johnson’s treatment of reproduction raises as many questions as it answers, and I want to open some of them up here.

The first question is conceptual. What does Johnson mean by “reproduction”? The term is slippery, and it has a complex history in Marxist and feminist theory. In the Marxist tradition, “reproduction” refers to the processes by which a mode of production maintains itself over time: not just the production of commodities, but the reproduction of the labor force (through food, shelter, child-rearing) and the reproduction of the social relations that make production possible. In the feminist tradition, “reproduction” has been used to highlight the often-invisible work of bearing and raising children, caring for the sick and elderly, and maintaining households—work that is disproportionately done by women and that is systematically devalued under capitalism. These two uses of “reproduction” overlap but are not identical, and Johnson does not always make it clear which sense he has in mind.

When he says that enslaved women’s bodies were the foundation of racial capitalism because of “their ability to reproduce capital,” he seems to mean something like: enslaved women gave birth to children who were themselves enslaved, and those children became the labor force of the next generation of plantations. This is true, and important. But it is also too narrow. Enslaved women did more than give birth. They worked in the fields alongside men; they cooked, cleaned, sewed, and nursed; they raised children not only their own but those of the master’s family; they provided emotional and sexual labor to both white and black communities. All of this work was essential to the functioning of the plantation economy, and all of it is part of what “reproduction” should mean in an adequate account of racial capitalism.

Johnson nods toward this broader sense when he cites J. D. B. DeBow’s argument for reopening the Atlantic slave trade: either import more slaves, DeBow warned, or “await with folded arms the coming of population and of labor which will be the result of natural increase.” The alternative to the slave trade, in other words, was the reproductive labor of enslaved women. This alternative was cheaper than the slave trade—that was the point—but it was not costless. It required the systematic sexual violation of enslaved women, the breaking of family ties, the constant threat of sale that made every mother a potential childless mourner. The violence of slavery was not just about extracting labor from bodies; it was about controlling reproduction, and that control was both a means of economic accumulation and a site of particular, gendered forms of terror.

Johnson is right to insist that this matters for justice. His fourth virtue is that an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery should be “attentive to questions of gender and sexuality, on the ways that reproductive invigilation and natal alienation—the subordination of the social reproduction of one group of people to the purposes of another—were core features of the human wrongs of slavery.” I agree entirely. But I want to press Johnson on what this means in practice. When he calls for reparations, does he mean that reparations should be specifically gendered? Should the descendants of enslaved women receive a different form of compensation than the descendants of enslaved men, to reflect the different forms of violence they suffered? Should there be specific remedies for the ongoing effects of reproductive exploitation, such as the disproportionate rates of maternal mortality among Black women in the United States today? These are not rhetorical questions; they are questions that any serious reparations program will have to address, and they are far from settled within the Movement for Black Lives or the broader left.

There is a deeper issue here, one that connects the question of reproduction to the question of rights that Johnson raises at the beginning of the essay. Johnson is critical of the liberal rights tradition for its abstraction, its universalism, its failure to account for the specific wrongs of slavery. But when it comes to reproduction, the liberal rights tradition has been, historically, one of the most powerful resources for feminist struggle. The right to control one’s own body, to choose whether and when to bear children, to be free from sexual violence and coercion—these are rights claims that have been central to the movement for reproductive justice, a movement that was pioneered by women of color precisely because they understood that reproductive oppression was inseparable from racial and economic oppression. The name of that movement is important: reproductive *justice*, not just reproductive rights. The shift from “rights” to “justice” signals a recognition that formal legal equality is not enough, that the conditions under which women make reproductive decisions—the availability of health care, the absence of coercion, the presence of social support—are as important as the decisions themselves. This is, in many ways, exactly the kind of justice that Johnson is calling for: historically grounded, attentive to the intersections of race and class, focused on the material conditions of life rather than on abstract entitlements.

And yet Johnson seems to want to distance himself from the language of rights entirely, even as his own argument depends on a notion of bodily integrity and self-determination that is, at bottom, a rights claim. When he describes the violation of enslaved women’s bodies as a core wrong of slavery, he is appealing to a standard that goes beyond the particular experience of the enslaved and calls on a principle that is, I would argue, universal. The principle is this: to use a person’s body against their will for the purposes of another is a fundamental violation of that person’s dignity. This principle is not the exclusive property of the liberal tradition; it can be found in many cultures and many philosophies. But it has been articulated most clearly and defended most systematically within the rights tradition, and I do not think we should abandon that tradition just because it has been historically allied with imperialism and capitalism.

The challenge, then, is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, or between particularism and universalism. The challenge is to hold them together—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and silences in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to give moral force to the demands that emerge from the history of slavery. Reproductive justice is a model for how this can be done. It begins with the specific experiences of women of color—their histories of forced sterilization, of coerced contraception, of inadequate health care, of family separation—and it uses those experiences to articulate demands that are framed in the language of rights while also challenging the limits of that language. The result is a politics that is both radical and legible, both particular and universal, both historical and forward-looking.

Johnson’s essay is a call for such a politics, even if it does not fully achieve it. He is right that the history of slavery is a standpoint from which we can see the inadequacies of liberal justice more clearly. He is right that reproductive exploitation was a core feature of racial capitalism and must be a core concern of any movement for repair. What remains is to do the work of translation: to turn the history into a politics, the critique into a program, the memory of the dead into a promise to the living. That work is not yet done, and Johnson’s essay is an invitation to take it up. I am grateful for the invitation, even as I recognize how much remains to be done.

"The liberal tradition of justice is not bankrupt but should be deepened and reconstructed from within to address the historical wrongs of slavery and racial capitalism."

There is an old saying among lawyers: hard cases make bad law. The idea is that a particularly extreme or emotionally freighted set of facts can lead judges to craft rules that work poorly when applied to more ordinary circumstances. The case of a child burned by a defective product, for instance, might lead to a rule of strict liability that makes sense for that case but creates perverse incentives in other areas of the law. The solution is not to ignore the hard case, but to think carefully about how the principles it demands can be generalized without doing violence to other important values.

I thought of this maxim while reading Walter Johnson’s essay. Johnson is, in effect, arguing that the history of slavery is a hard case for liberal theories of justice—so hard that it cannot be accommodated within the existing framework of rights, liberties, and distributive fairness. The wrongs of slavery, he argues, are not just extreme instances of injustice that can be remedied by extending existing rights to the enslaved and their descendants. They are qualitatively different: a form of “racial capitalism” that turned human beings into capital, that rested on the reproductive exploitation of women, that was inseparable from Native dispossession and global imperialism, and that continues to structure the distribution of wealth and power in the present. A justice adequate to this history cannot be a justice of individual rights and formal equality; it must be a justice of reparations, redistribution, and fundamental transformation.

I am not unsympathetic to this argument. Johnson is right that the liberal tradition has struggled to account for the legacy of slavery, and right that the remedies it has offered—the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments, the Civil Rights Acts, affirmative action—have been partial and inadequate. But I want to push back against the implication that the liberal tradition is therefore bankrupt. Hard cases, I want to suggest, do not always make bad law. Sometimes they reveal the true meaning of the principles we already hold.

Take the principle of equal citizenship. The liberal tradition, from Locke to Rawls, has insisted that all persons have a fundamental moral claim to be treated as free and equal members of the political community. Johnson sees this principle as inherently limited, because it abstracts away from the material inequalities that make formal equality a mockery for the descendants of enslaved people. But the principle of equal citizenship, properly understood, does not require abstraction from material conditions. On the contrary, it requires that the state take affirmative steps to ensure that all citizens have the means to participate effectively in political and social life. This is the insight behind Rawls’s difference principle, which holds that inequalities are permissible only if they benefit the least advantaged. It is the insight behind the idea of “substantive” as opposed to “formal” equality of opportunity—the idea that it is not enough to remove legal barriers to advancement; one must also provide the resources, the education, the health care, and the social supports that make advancement genuinely possible. And it is the insight behind the growing body of scholarship on “structural” or “systemic” discrimination, which recognizes that racial inequality is not simply the product of individual prejudice but of institutional arrangements that reproduce disadvantage across generations.

If we take these insights seriously—and I believe they are firmly within the liberal tradition, even if they have not always been honored in practice—then the case of slavery does not require us to abandon the framework of equal citizenship. It requires us to deepen it. The descendants of enslaved people are not just citizens who happen to be poor; they are citizens whose poverty is the direct result of a centuries-long program of state-sponsored expropriation, exploitation, and exclusion. Equal citizenship for them requires not just formal equality but remedial action: action that acknowledges the specific history of their disadvantage and aims to eliminate its ongoing effects. This is, in its essentials, the argument for reparations, and it is an argument that can be made entirely within the language of liberal justice—indeed, some of its most eloquent exponents, from Thurgood Marshall to Ta-Nehisi Coates, have done exactly that.

Johnson might reply that this is precisely the problem: that the liberal tradition can be stretched to accommodate reparations, but only by losing its distinctiveness. If liberal justice requires the wholesale redistribution of wealth and power, then what distinguishes it from the radical tradition Johnson favors? But this reply misses something important. The radical tradition, as Johnson presents it, is grounded in the particular experience of one group—the enslaved and their descendants—and it calls for remedies that are tailored to that experience. The liberal tradition, by contrast, is grounded in general principles that apply to everyone. The advantage of the liberal approach is precisely that it is general: it provides a framework within which the claims of different groups can be weighed against one another, and it provides a language in which those claims can be justified to people who do not share the same history.

Let me give an example. Johnson’s six virtues include the claim that a justice rooted in the history of slavery should focus on “the extraction and distribution of resources between classes and areas of the world: on the relationship of African American history to Native American history, for example, or on the relationship of either or both of those to the history of the white workers (and merchants and bankers) in the financial and manufacturing centers of the United States and Europe.” This is a commendably expansive vision. But it raises a host of practical questions. How do we balance the claims of African Americans, whose ancestors were enslaved, against the claims of Native Americans, whose ancestors were dispossessed and genocidally slaughtered? Are these claims in competition with one another, or can they be harmonized? What about the claims of more recent immigrants, whose ancestors may have been victimized by other forms of colonialism or oppression but who arrived in the United States after the end of slavery? What about the claims of the white working class, which Johnson acknowledges is both a beneficiary of racial privilege and a victim of capitalist exploitation?

These questions do not answer themselves. They require a framework for adjudication—a set of principles that can tell us, however imperfectly, how to weigh competing claims. The liberal tradition, for all its flaws, has at least attempted to provide such a framework. It has proposed principles of desert, of need, of equality, of reciprocity. These principles are far from settled, and their application to the specific case of slavery and its legacies is a matter of ongoing dispute. But the dispute is productive. It forces us to be precise about what we think justice requires, and to justify our conclusions to others in terms they can, at least in principle, accept.

Johnson’s alternative, by contrast, is to derive justice from a particular historical experience. But this raises the question of authority: why should someone who does not share that experience accept the conclusions that follow from it? If the answer is simply “because the experience reveals truths that you cannot otherwise see,” then we are in the territory of an epistemic privilege that is not easily squared with democratic deliberation. If the answer is “because you are complicit in the structures that produced that experience, whether you know it or not,” then we need an argument about complicity that Johnson does not provide.

None of this is to deny the power of Johnson’s critique. The history of slavery is indeed a hard case for liberal justice, and coming to terms with it may require transforming liberal justice in ways that are hard to predict. But the solution, I want to suggest, is not to abandon the liberal tradition for a particularist account of justice grounded in the history of one group. It is to do the hard work of reconstructing the liberal tradition from within—to use the history of slavery as a test case for our principles, and to revise those principles until they can meet the test. This is a slower, messier process than the one Johnson envisions. But it is, I believe, more likely to produce a justice that can be shared.

Moyn, Antislavery, and the Vexed History of Human Rights

Walter Johnson's essay treats the history of human rights with a glancing touch. He cites Samuel Moyn's *The Last Utopia* approvingly for its argument that the dominant contemporary regime of human rights is a creature of the mid-twentieth century rather than a continuation of the longer antislavery tradition. He then uses this argument to license a broader claim: that the human-rights framework is inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, and that we should turn instead to a justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism. I want to spend my response unpacking the history of human rights more carefully, because I think Johnson's quick adoption of Moyn's periodization obscures both what Moyn gets right and what Johnson's argument actually requires.

Moyn's argument in *The Last Utopia* is, in brief, that "human rights" as a globally circulating moral and political vocabulary emerges late — in the 1970s rather than in 1948 (the Universal Declaration), 1789 (the French Declaration), or 1776 (the American Declaration). The earlier moments Moyn treats as essentially nationalist or revolutionary projects that used the language of rights instrumentally; what is distinctive about human rights properly understood, on Moyn's account, is the appeal to a transnational moral authority that sits outside and above the nation-state. That appeal, Moyn argues, becomes politically operative only with the collapse of competing utopias (Marxist, anticolonial, nationalist) in the 1970s, when human rights becomes the "last utopia" for a generation disillusioned with state-based projects.

This is a controversial argument, and Moyn's critics — Lynn Hunt, Micheline Ishay, others — have argued that the longer history of human rights, including antislavery, has more continuity with the contemporary regime than Moyn allows. The debate is unresolved. But for our purposes, what matters is what Johnson does with Moyn's argument and whether the argument can bear the weight Johnson places on it.

Johnson uses Moyn to license the claim that the contemporary human-rights regime is "not significantly inflected by the history of slavery, although it would be better if it were." This is a much stronger claim than Moyn himself makes, and it deserves scrutiny. Even if we grant Moyn that human rights as a transnational vocabulary emerges in the 1970s, it does not follow that the substantive content of that vocabulary is uninflected by antislavery. The 1948 Universal Declaration, which Moyn de-emphasizes but does not dismiss, contains provisions (the prohibition of slavery, the prohibition of torture, the protection against arbitrary detention) that have clear genealogical relations to abolitionist arguments. The framing of human rights as "inherent" in the human person draws on a long tradition that the antislavery movement helped to develop. The institutional architecture of contemporary human rights (treaty bodies, special rapporteurs, the U.N. system) was built by people who took the abolitionist precedent seriously even when they did not invoke it explicitly.

What is true, and what Moyn is right about, is that the contemporary human-rights regime does not foreground the history of slavery in the way it foregrounds, say, the Holocaust or twentieth-century totalitarianism. The genealogy of the Universal Declaration runs more directly through the experience of Nazi atrocity than through the experience of Atlantic slavery. The institutional development of human rights in the postwar period was shaped by Cold War politics in ways that often suppressed engagement with the colonial and racial dimensions of the order it claimed to regulate. These are real points, and they constrain what human rights as a vocabulary can do.

But they do not establish what Johnson needs them to establish — namely, that the human-rights framework is fundamentally inadequate to the patterns of injustice flowing from slavery, such that an alternative framework rooted in racial capitalism is required. The relationship between the human-rights vocabulary and the project of racial justice is more dialectical than Johnson's brisk treatment allows.

Consider the actual deployment of human-rights frameworks by movements working against the legacies of slavery. The NAACP brought a petition to the United Nations in 1947 (the "Appeal to the World," edited by Du Bois himself) and another in 1951 (the "We Charge Genocide" petition, brought by the Civil Rights Congress). These efforts used the emerging human-rights vocabulary to indict the United States for its treatment of African Americans. They were not entirely successful — Cold War politics suppressed serious engagement with the petitions — but they established a precedent that has been picked up repeatedly since. Where Johnson’s essay shines brightest is in its devastating dismantling of the traditional “capitalism versus slavery” binary. For generations, orthodox economic history—whether liberal or Marxist—has treated capitalism as a system defined by free wage labor, originating in the factories of Europe, while dismissing slavery as an archaic, residual mode of production. Johnson, standing on the shoulders of Du Bois, Eric Williams, and Robinson, demolishes this artificial wall.

By centering the concept of “racial capitalism,” Johnson forces us to recognize that the mills of Manchester and the plantations of Mississippi were functionally integrated components of a single global machine. The enslaved were not merely workers; they were capital. As Johnson points out, the bodies of enslaved people served as the primary collateral upon which the entire nineteenth-century credit system was built. They were the financial instruments that capitalized the expansion of global trade. Furthermore, this system was violently mapped onto the expropriation of Native American land, merging the mechanisms of continental imperialism with racialized chattel slavery.

This integrated approach solves one of the most persistent analytical problems in American history: how to reconcile the development of modern democratic institutions with the persistence of white supremacy. If capitalism and racism are not separate spheres but co-constitutive historical forces, then the periodic resurgence of white nationalism and the deep, persistent racial wealth gaps we see in 2017 are not aberrations of the capitalist system. They are its intended features.

Du Bois’s concept of the “wages of whiteness,” which Johnson invokes, remains one of the most potent tools for understanding the American political economy. By offering white workers a psychological and social premium—a share of the spoils of empire and racial hierarchy—the system effectively pacified class solidarity and entrenched a global division of labor. Any history of capitalism that attempts to analyze the white working class without confronting the material and psychological wages of whiteness is, as Johnson suggests, little more than historical science fiction.

Equally crucial is Johnson’s insistence on bringing gender and the environment into this framework of racial capitalism. By highlighting the reproductive hinge of the slave economy, Johnson challenges male-centric labor histories that focus primarily on the production of cash crops. The forced reproduction of enslaved women, driven by the ban on the Atlantic slave trade and the insatiable demand for labor in the expanding Cotton Kingdom, was a foundational pillar of American capitalism. The commodification of the womb was just as central to nineteenth-century economic growth as the steam engine. By demanding that justice focus on the history of reproductive invigilation and natal alienation, Johnson aligns the historical critique of slavery with modern intersectional struggles for reproductive justice.

Similarly, his sixth point—linking the instrumentalization of human beings to the instrumentalization of nature—offers a profound historical lens for the contemporary climate crisis. The logic of racial capitalism is fundamentally extractive. The same worldview that looked at a human being and saw only a unit of calculable labor and collateral looked at the ancient forests and rich soils of the Mississippi Delta and saw only real estate and raw material. The exhaustion of the soil and the breaking of bodies were twin engines of capitalist accumulation. As we grapple with the escalating realities of environmental degradation today, Johnson reminds us that the fight for ecological survival cannot be divorced from the fight for racial and economic justice. Domination and dominion are, indeed, intimately dialectical.

In his conclusion, Johnson offers a six-point blueprint for an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism. It is a breathtakingly ambitious and morally urgent framework. It demands that we look southward, that we focus on extraction and distribution, that we pursue restorative justice and reparations, that we center gender and sexuality, that we collapse the false dichotomy between race and class, and that we recognize the linked exploitation of humans and the earth.

This vision provides a rigorous and necessary roadmap for anyone committed to social justice. It correctly diagnosis the limitations of a purely synchronic, liberal rights-based approach that ignores the deep, bleeding wounds of historical accumulation. We cannot achieve a just world simply by declaring abstract equality while leaving the inherited architectures of racial capitalism intact.

Yet, as we move forward to heed Du Bois’s warning and seize the propitious moments to remake the world, we need not discard the language of our humanity to do so. We can agree with Johnson that the history of capitalism is a history of violence, extraction, and racialized accumulation, and that the enslaved were vital, human actors within this nightmare. But we can also assert that the system’s primary offense was its relentless attempt to deny that humanity legal and social breathing room—an attempt we rightly call dehumanizing.

The struggle to remake the world requires a ruthless, clear-eyed analysis of the material conditions of racial capitalism. It also requires an unyielding, aspirational assertion of human dignity. By fusing Johnson’s material history with a renewed, expansive demand for human rights—one rooted not in the imaginary universality of the bourgeoisie, but in the specific, historical resistance of the dark and dispossessed—we can perhaps finally begin to dismantle the world racial capitalism built.
