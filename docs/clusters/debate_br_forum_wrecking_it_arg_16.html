"The shift from generative PCs to tethered appliances like the iPhone represents a rational and positive maturation of technology that makes the digital world usable and secure for mainstream consumers."

Jonathan Zittrain’s elegy for the generative PC is exquisitely argued, but it rests on a value judgment that deserves to be challenged: that a world of tethered appliances is a loss, and that mainstream users are being forced, against their deeper interests, into prisons of convenience. I want to offer the view from the other side of the screen—the perspective of the ordinary, non-technical consumer who is not a digital tinkerer, does not aspire to write code, and who genuinely, rationally prefers the iPhone to the Apple II. What Zittrain sees as a dangerous slide toward sterility, I see as a maturation that makes the digital world usable by everyone, not just a self-selected priesthood of the curious.

The generative PC, for all its historical glory, was an accident of history that served a narrow slice of humanity very well and left the rest baffled and endangered. Zittrain’s own evidence makes this clear: 81 percent of home computers lack basic security protections. The botnet armies he laments are not composed of servers in data centers but of the very home PCs that Zittrain wants to preserve in their open, unfettered state. Those machines have become unwilling foot soldiers in a criminal economy precisely because their owners were never qualified to administer them. The average person does not know what a “port” is, does not want to decide whether an executable should be allowed to “listen for incoming connections,” and should not have to learn. To call the resulting lockdown of the iPhone a retreat from generativity is to forget that for the vast majority, the Apple II’s blinking cursor was never a liberation; it was a wall.

Zittrain’s genealogy of the iPhone as the antithesis of the Apple II is rhetorically powerful but analytically incomplete. The iPhone does not exist in a vacuum; it is a node in a much larger digital ecosystem that is itself wildly generative at the services layer. Yes, the phone’s onboard software is controlled by Apple, but the device ships with a fully capable Web browser. Through that browser, any user can access Google Docs, Facebook, Wikipedia, and a universe of Web-based applications that are being written and deployed by innovators without asking Apple’s permission. The generativity has not vanished; it has migrated one level up, from the operating system to the Web platform. The security problem Zittrain documents—botnets, drive-by downloads, keystroke loggers—is overwhelmingly an OS-level and application-level phenomenon. By sealing the OS, Apple has not killed the creativity of the Internet; it has merely created a safe vessel for accessing that creativity. The untold thousands of Web applications that will be built in the next decade will run just fine on iPhones, and their creators will not need to worry about being infected by a rootkit when they test their code.

Zittrain worries that if the PC ceases to be the center, we lose the “safety valve” that keeps appliances honest. But this argument imagines a monoculture of sealed devices where no competition exists. In reality, the market in 2008 is still teeming with alternatives. Linux PCs, open-source netbooks, and the entire server-side ecosystem remain generative. The existence of TiVo did not prevent the emergence of MythTV, and the existence of the iPhone will not prevent the emergence of open mobile platforms—Google’s Android, which Zittrain himself mentions approvingly, is already on the horizon. The safety valve is maintained not by forcing every consumer to be a potential tinkerer, but by ensuring that the tinkerer’s market remains accessible to those who want it. If Apple’s lockdown becomes too oppressive, an open competitor will exploit the opportunity. That is how markets work, and Zittrain’s call for legal mandates of “appliance neutrality” is a solution in search of a problem.

His data portability proposal, on the other hand, is a genuinely useful consumer protection that does not require us to denigrate the appliance model. Mandating that users can extract their photos, contacts, and documents from a service is common sense. But portability need not imply that the device must remain a blank slate for all future code. My car allows me to take my belongings out of the trunk without requiring me to be able to reprogram the engine control unit. Appliances can be sealed and still respect my ownership of my data.

Where Zittrain and I truly part ways is on the anthropology of the user. He looks at the millions who are fleeing to safe, simple devices and sees a crisis of trust that must be repaired so that they will come back to the PC. I look at those same millions and see people making a perfectly sound judgment about their own needs and limitations. The modern information economy does not require everyone to be a system administrator. It requires that everyone can read their email, share photos, listen to music, and write documents without fear. Tethered appliances deliver that. They are not a dystopia; they are the fulfillment of the original promise of computing for the rest of us, a promise that the generative, anarchic PC could never keep because the price of its freedom was eternal troubleshooting.

Zittrain’s vision is one that mourns a particular kind of digital citizenship—the hacker ethos, the hobbyist’s romance. That ethos produced wonderful things, and it should be preserved in its own enclaves. But we should not confuse our own nostalgia for a universal good. The Internet will continue to produce astonishing innovations, and many of them will be delivered to users through safe, sealed endpoints that just work. That is not the wrecking of the Internet; it is the building of something that finally, after two decades, includes everyone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The open nature of generative PCs has turned them into a security liability, as average users are unqualified to manage the complex administrative tasks required to prevent them from becoming part of criminal botnets.
  2. 02Generativity has not been destroyed but has simply migrated from the operating system level to the web services layer, where innovation continues to flourish through browsers on sealed devices.
  3. 03The market provides a natural safety valve through competition, ensuring that open platforms remain available for tinkerers without forcing every consumer to adopt a high-maintenance generative system.
  4. 04Data portability is a valid consumer protection concern, but it can be achieved through legal mandates without requiring devices to remain open to third-party code.
  5. 05The preference for tethered appliances is a sound judgment by users who prioritize reliability and ease of use over the 'hacker ethos' of eternal troubleshooting.
