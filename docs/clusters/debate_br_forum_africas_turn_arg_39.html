Diversified Gemini

"The Kenyan crisis demonstrates that macroeconomic growth in Africa is unsustainable and fragile without fundamental institutional reform to replace extractive, neo-patrimonial systems with inclusive governance."

Edward Miguel correctly identifies the tragic irony of Kenya in early 2008: a country that was supposed to be the poster child for Africa’s democratic and economic renaissance abruptly descended into ethnic violence and institutional failure following a rigged election. However, Miguel draws the wrong conclusion from this tragedy. He views it as a "heartbreaking disappointment" and a "fragile" setback on a long trajectory akin to 19th-century Latin America. In reality, the Kenyan crisis is a blaring siren warning us that macroeconomic growth without fundamental institutional reform is building a house on sand.

The academic consensus, led by economists like Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson, has increasingly demonstrated that "institutions rule." Sustainable development requires inclusive political and economic institutions—secure property rights, an independent judiciary, constraints on the executive, and the rule of law. What the 2000s boom in Africa has largely produced, instead, is growth under *extractive* institutions, fueled by high commodity prices.

Kenya’s economy grew impressively under Mwai Kibaki. But the institutional software of the country—the deeply entrenched neo-patrimonial system where state power is the ultimate prize for ethnic patronage—never changed. The violence in the Rift Valley was not a random outbreak of ancient hatreds; it was a predictable, politically orchestrated response to a winner-take-all political system where losing an election means losing access to state resources, land, and economic security.

This institutional deficit is exactly why Miguel’s proposal for Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS) is fundamentally flawed. Miguel points to Botswana’s Drought Relief Program as evidence that this can work. But Botswana is the exception that proves the rule. Botswana succeeds because it has the strongest, most inclusive institutions on the continent, dating back to its independence. Its government is accountable, its bureaucracy is relatively meritocratic, and property rights are respected.

If the international community were to create an RCPS fund and hand cash to the governments of Chad, Sudan, or even Kenya during an economic shock, what does Miguel honestly think would happen to that money? In countries lacking institutional checks and balances, sudden influxes of cash—whether from oil, Chinese loans, or Western "conflict insurance"—are inevitably captured by the ruling elites. They are used to buy arms, pay off cronies, or fund the very militias that perpetuate the conflict. You cannot use a technocratic financial instrument to solve a deeply political problem of governance.

The long view of history—comparing Africa to the 19th-century United States or Latin America—is historically accurate but offers cold comfort. State-building is indeed a bloody, centuries-long process. But if we want to support Africa today, the focus must ruthlessly prioritize institutional capacity. Celebrating 5% GDP growth in countries where the judiciary is corrupt, the electoral commission is compromised, and the presidency is treated as an ethnic chiefdom is dangerously naive. True development will only be Africa’s when its institutions, not just its export prices, finally mature.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Sustainable development requires inclusive institutions like an independent judiciary and secure property rights rather than growth fueled merely by high commodity prices.
  2. 02The violence in Kenya was a predictable consequence of a winner-take-all political system where state power is the primary mechanism for ethnic patronage and resource allocation.
  3. 03Technocratic solutions like Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS) are fundamentally flawed because, without institutional checks, such funds will be captured by corrupt elites to maintain power.
  4. 04Botswana’s success with social programs is an exception that proves the rule, as it stems from its pre-existing strong and accountable institutional framework.
  5. 05The international community must prioritize building institutional capacity over celebrating GDP growth in countries where governance remains corrupt and compromised.
