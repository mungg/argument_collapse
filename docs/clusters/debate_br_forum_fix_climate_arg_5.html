Vanilla MiniMax

"While Victor and Sabel’s experimentalist governance model offers valuable insights into iterative learning, it underestimates the immense political resistance and geopolitical complexities involved in scaling such a framework to address deep decarbonization."

The diagnosis that Victor and Sabel offer of international climate diplomacy is largely compelling. The gap between ambitious pledges and actual emissions reductions has grown so wide that it strains credulity to treat existing frameworks as anything other than elaborate exercises in managed disappointment. Their core insight—that the assumption that solutions must be global from the start has been counterproductive—deserves serious engagement. Yet their proposed remedy, experimentalist governance, raises as many questions as it answers, and the Montreal Protocol analogy they champion is less straightforward than they acknowledge.

Experimentalist governance captures something real about how effective regulation often works: iterative adjustment, feedback from practitioners, and graduated pressure rather than all-or-nothing commitments. The authors are right that this contrasts sharply with the top-down, bottom-up dichotomy that has constrained climate diplomacy. But translating these principles from successful domestic regulatory contexts like CARB or the EU's Water Framework Directive to global scale encounters fundamental obstacles they underestimate.

The Montreal Protocol succeeded in part because the problem it addressed was comparatively tractable. Ozone-depleting substances were produced by a concentrated set of firms, substitutes were chemically analogous to the originals, and the economic costs of transition, while significant, were manageable. Deep decarbonization involves transforming entire energy systems, transportation infrastructure, industrial processes, and consumption patterns—changes that dwarf anything involved in phasing out CFCs. The authors acknowledge this scale difference but perhaps not its implications for governance. When stakes are lower and solutions more technically straightforward, experimentalist learning can proceed relatively smoothly. When transformation requires dismantling fossil-fuel-dependent systems that constitute the backbone of modern economies, the political resistance is categorically different.

This points to a deeper problem: experimentalist governance as the authors describe it still requires someone to create and maintain the framework conditions that enable experimentation. The Montreal Protocol was sponsored by the United States and other wealthy nations that provided both the Multilateral Fund and the political cover for trade sanctions against laggards. The penalty defaults that made the regime work depended on asymmetries of power that no longer obtain in the same form. Today's global landscape features a fragmented hegemon, a rising China that is simultaneously essential to any climate solution and increasingly willing to contest Western-led governance, and a constellation of emerging economies with divergent interests. Designing penalty defaults that function across these power dynamics is a far more daunting task than the authors' confident prescription suggests.

Their sector-based approach is intuitively appealing but faces its own challenges. Industrial sectors like steel and cement are indeed more globally integrated than, say, building codes, and international coordination within these sectors may be more feasible than country-level agreements. Yet sectors are not apolitical abstractions. The steel industry has spent decades building institutional capacity to resist exactly the kind of intrusive regulation the authors envision. The transportation sector encompasses everything from zero-emission vehicle mandates to airline emissions—domains with very different political economies. Identifying which sectors are amenable to experimentalist governance and which will resist it requires attention to political geography that the authors' framework does not fully provide.

The authors' treatment of the Paris Agreement is perceptive but perhaps too dismissive of its continued relevance. They argue that Paris serves only to authorize protest and punish laggards—a limited but essential function. Yet this understates how much the agreement has already shifted the terrain of climate politics. The existence of NDCs, however inadequate, creates institutional hooks for domestic litigation, investor pressure, and subnational leadership. Several cities, states, and corporations have used Paris not just as a symbolic reference point but as a legal and practical framework for their own commitments. Dismissing this as insufficient misses the possibility that the agreement's value lies precisely in providing legitimacy to actors the authors would likely support: state-level regulators, firms experimenting with clean technology, and civil society groups demanding accountability.

What the authors call experimentalist governance has much to recommend it as a heuristic for thinking about institutional design. The emphasis on iterative learning, the rejection of premature closure on means and ends, and the insistence on connecting technical expertise to democratic accountability are all valuable. But implementing these principles at the scale climate change demands requires not just clever governance design but mobilization of political will that governance arrangements alone cannot generate. The Montreal Protocol worked because there was a moment—a hole in the sky, an engaged U.S. government, a corporate outlier willing to break ranks—when the politics aligned with the technical possibilities. Something similar will be needed for decarbonization: not a single dramatic breakthrough but a series of contingent convergences between technological readiness, political opportunity, and institutional innovation. The authors point in useful directions; they should be more candid about how much remains uncertain.

  1. 01The Montreal Protocol is an imperfect analogy because phasing out ozone-depleting substances was a far more tractable problem than the total transformation of global energy and industrial systems.
  2. 02The 'penalty defaults' required for experimentalist governance are difficult to implement in a modern, fragmented geopolitical landscape lacking a clear global hegemon to enforce standards.
  3. 03A sector-based approach faces significant hurdles because powerful industries have spent decades building the institutional capacity to resist intrusive regulation.
  4. 04The authors are too dismissive of the Paris Agreement, which provides essential legal and institutional legitimacy for domestic litigation, investor pressure, and subnational climate action.
  5. 05Effective climate action requires the mobilization of political will and contingent historical convergences that clever governance design alone cannot generate.
Vanilla Claude

"While experimentalist governance is a clarifying meta-theory for climate action, its practical application faces significant hurdles due to the unprecedented scale of decarbonization, the lack of global enforcers for penalties, and the urgent timeline required by the climate crisis."

Sabel and Victor have written one of the most clarifying diagnoses of the climate diplomacy stalemate in years. Their core insight—that the architects of the UNFCCC misread the Montreal Protocol, taking from it a global treaty form while leaving behind the sectoral problem-solving machinery that actually made it work—deserves to be central to any serious post-Paris reckoning. So does their argument that experimentalist governance, with its braided top-down framework goals and bottom-up technical learning, fits the structure of decarbonization better than either the Kyoto-style mandate or the Paris-style voluntarism. And yet several features of the climate problem make me less optimistic than they are that experimentalism, even applied with the discipline they describe, can carry the load they want it to carry.

Begin with the Montreal analogy itself. The chlorofluorocarbon problem was extraordinary in two respects that make it a poor template for greenhouse gases. First, ODS were produced by a remarkably concentrated industry—a handful of chemical companies in a handful of countries—and used in a finite set of applications. The "sectoral committees" Sabel and Victor celebrate could canvass essentially every relevant engineer on the planet. Carbon dioxide, by contrast, is the joint product of nearly every economic activity humans undertake, generated by hundreds of millions of point sources, and embedded in capital stocks—buildings, power plants, industrial boilers, vehicles, land-use patterns—with replacement cycles measured in decades. Second, and decisively, DuPont's defection from industry orthodoxy turned on the fact that fluorochemical alternatives to CFCs were patentable and potentially more profitable than the products being phased out. The penalty default worked because the technological substitution was, for the dominant incumbent, a business opportunity. ExxonMobil and Saudi Aramco face nothing remotely comparable. Their core asset—proven reserves—is what decarbonization renders worthless. There is no analogue to DuPont's "break ranks" moment available to a major oil company without dissolving the company. Sabel and Victor acknowledge that decarbonization is "much more modest" in scale than ODS phaseout—they have it backwards; it is vastly larger—and they do not adequately reckon with what this asymmetry means for the politics of penalty defaults.

This connects to a second worry: penalty defaults need an enforcer. In the Montreal case, the United States and the European Community could credibly threaten trade sanctions and exclusion from lucrative markets because they were the markets, and they were aligned. The world Sabel and Victor describe in their opening pages—of fraying global order, of a paralyzed WTO, of receding American hegemony, of mistrust between China and the West—is precisely a world in which credible cross-border penalty defaults are hard to assemble. A carbon border adjustment mechanism of the kind the EU is contemplating may eventually serve this function for some industries, but the political coalition needed to wield it consistently across steel, cement, aluminum, shipping, and aviation does not yet exist, and Sabel and Victor do not say much about how to build one. Their argument tends to treat the penalty default as a technology that can be installed once a sectoral institution is up and running. In practice it is the scarcest resource in the entire scheme.

A third point concerns time. Experimentalist governance is, by design, iterative and patient: thin consensus thickens through cycles of local trial, peer review, and adjustment. Montreal took roughly thirty years from the Vienna Convention to near-universal phaseout. We do not have thirty years for the steel sector. The IPCC's 1.5°C pathways require global emissions to be cut roughly in half by 2030—nine years from now—and to reach net zero around mid-century. Sabel and Victor sometimes write as if the alternative to experimentalism is the failed Kyoto template; but the actually pressing alternative, which they barely engage, is aggressive carbon pricing, sectoral mandates, and large-scale public investment in deployment. These tools are blunter than experimentalist learning, but they have the virtue of moving capital quickly. A more honest framing would be that experimentalism is essential for the hard-to-abate sectors where the technology is genuinely undetermined—green steel, sustainable aviation fuel, cement chemistry—and supplementary in the sectors where the technology is already known and the bottleneck is deployment finance and political will. Power generation in most large grids is now the latter case, not the former.

Fourth, I think the authors are too dismissive of what Paris can become. They concede Paris a single function—legitimating sanction by activists and governments against laggards—but the Agreement also institutionalized a five-year ratcheting cycle and a global stocktake whose first iteration begins in 2023. If the stocktake were paired with sectoral assessments analogous to Montreal's technical and economic panels—organized by industry and by region rather than by country—the existing Paris architecture could be made considerably more experimentalist than Sabel and Victor allow. The proposals are out there: the various initiatives under the Climate and Clean Air Coalition, the Mission Innovation framework launched in 2015, the IEA's sectoral roadmaps, the Nordhaus-style climate clubs that link trade access to participation. Sabel and Victor's framework is most useful, I think, when read as a guide for how to wire these existing efforts into a coherent feedback loop, rather than as a reason to consign Paris itself to symbolic status.

Finally, a Deweyan point Sabel and Victor invoke but do not fully cash out. They are right that decarbonization will reach into everyday life and that the legitimacy of expert-driven decisions depends on integrating where the shoe pinches. But the pinching here is profoundly unequal. Coal communities in West Virginia or Shanxi, smallholder farmers in palm oil regions, and informal-sector workers in fossil-dependent megacities will bear costs that knowledge workers in coastal cities will not. Experimentalist governance has historically been better at convening producers and regulators than at incorporating the people who lose their jobs and tax bases. The Green New Deal vision the authors gesture toward, for all its governance vagueness, is centrally a claim that distributional commitments must be hardwired into climate institutions, not negotiated downstream. An experimentalism that does not absorb that claim will reproduce the elite distrust the authors themselves identify as a structural obstacle.

None of this refutes Sabel and Victor; it sharpens what their proposal must do. Experimentalist governance is the right meta-theory. The harder question is whether we can install its missing parts—penalty defaults, sectoral institutions, distributive guarantees—on a calendar the atmosphere will accept.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Montreal Protocol is an imperfect template because greenhouse gas emissions are embedded in far more complex economic activities and capital stocks than ozone-depleting substances.
  2. 02Unlike the ozone crisis where industry leaders found profitable alternatives, decarbonization threatens the core existence of major fossil fuel interests, making 'penalty defaults' much harder to implement politically.
  3. 03The fraying global order and receding American hegemony make it difficult to establish the credible cross-border enforcers necessary to impose sanctions on laggard nations or industries.
  4. 04The iterative and patient nature of experimentalist governance may be too slow to meet the IPCC’s urgent 2030 emission reduction targets compared to blunter tools like carbon pricing and public investment.
  5. 05The Paris Agreement's existing architecture could be reformed to incorporate experimentalist sectoral assessments rather than being dismissed as merely symbolic.
  6. 06To be successful and legitimate, experimentalist governance must hardwire distributive justice and protections for vulnerable communities into its institutional design to address the unequal costs of decarbonization.
