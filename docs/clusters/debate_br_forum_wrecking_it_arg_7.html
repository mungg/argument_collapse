"The rigid dichotomy between 'generative' PCs and 'sterile' appliances is a false binary that fails to account for the market-driven spectrum of openness and the shift of innovation to higher layers of the digital ecosystem."

The False Binary of Sterile and Generative

Jonathan Zittrain has built his argument around a dichotomy: the generative PC, descendant of the Apple II, versus the sterile appliance, exemplified by the iPhone and Xbox. The PC, in his telling, accepts code from anywhere; the appliance accepts only what its manufacturer permits. The future hangs in the balance between these two paradigms. I want to suggest that the dichotomy, while rhetorically powerful, does not survive contact with the actual landscape of digital devices in early 2008, and that the prescriptions that follow from it are correspondingly off-target.

Begin with the iPhone, Zittrain's central exhibit. The iPhone shipped in mid-2007 as a closed device. But within months a community of jailbreakers had produced functional third-party software, and Apple's response was telling: not the legal warfare one might have expected from a determined gatekeeper, but a series of cat-and-mouse software updates, and then — last October — the announcement that an official software development kit would ship in February 2008. We are now living through that release. Whatever else one wants to say about Apple's terms, the iPhone is becoming a platform for third-party developers, on a faster timeline than the Macintosh did in the 1980s. Zittrain's essay acknowledges the SDK in a parenthetical and moves on, but this is the very evidence that complicates his thesis. The "sterile appliance" did not stay sterile. Market pressure, developer pressure, and consumer pressure converted it.

The same story repeats elsewhere. The Xbox 360 has been the target of homebrew communities since launch, and Microsoft announced XNA Game Studio Express in 2006 specifically to allow non-Microsoft developers to write games for it. The TiVo has spawned an entire ecosystem of hacks, mods, and at least one quasi-supported developer program. The Kindle, just released, accepts user-uploaded content via a documented (if clunky) path. Even my mobile phone runs Java applets I can install over the air. To describe these devices as belonging to a single category called "sterile" and another called "generative" is to flatten distinctions that matter.

A more accurate picture would describe a spectrum, with at least three meaningful axes: who can write software for the device, who decides what gets installed, and how reversible the lock is. The Apple II scores high on all three: anyone could write, the user decided, and there was no lock to reverse. A perfectly closed pacemaker would score low on all three. The interesting devices — iPhone, Xbox, Android phones being announced as we speak — sit at various intermediate points, and crucially, they move along these axes over time, often toward greater openness rather than away from it. The historical trajectory Zittrain warns against, in which "appliances tethered to a network of control" displace generative endpoints, is genuinely contested by these movements.

Why is the appliance opening up? Because the same generative pressures Zittrain celebrates in PCs also operate on appliance manufacturers, just with a delay. A closed device that resists third-party innovation forgoes the network effects that come from a developer ecosystem. The iPhone with an SDK is more valuable than the iPhone without one — to users, to Apple, and to AT&T — because the long tail of niche applications turns a phone into a personal computing platform people will pay more for and switch carriers for. Apple is not being forced into openness by regulators; it is being pulled there by the same logic that made the Apple II succeed where the Lisa did not.

This matters for policy because Zittrain's prescriptions assume the appliance threat is exogenous — an external force that must be checked by legal innovation like "appliance neutrality." But if the threat is partially self-correcting through ordinary market dynamics, then heavy interventions like obligating Microsoft to allow third-party code on the Xbox after some bait-and-switch tipping point are solutions to a problem that is partly solving itself. Worse, such interventions may discourage exactly the experimentation with intermediate models — moderated platforms, curated app stores, sandboxed runtimes — that we should want to see. The walled garden model, of all things, may turn out to be a generative compromise: it admits third-party code while screening out the worst of the malware that Zittrain rightly worries about. App store curation is, in a real sense, the StopBadware idea built into the platform itself.

Zittrain also underestimates how much generativity has moved up the stack. He nods to "Web 2.0" but treats it largely as a constraint — software-as-service whose generativity is contingent on platform vendors. This understates what has happened. Facebook's developer platform launched in May 2007 and within months had tens of thousands of applications. The mash-up culture around Google Maps, Flickr, and Twitter's nascent API has produced more end-user-facing innovation in two years than the Windows ecosystem did in some five-year stretches. Yes, these platforms can withdraw permission. So can Microsoft revoke a digital signature, and so could the long-dead BBSes of the 1980s shut down their phone lines. Conditional generativity is still generativity, and the relevant question is not whether the platform vendor could in principle become hostile, but whether the conditions under which it would do so are economically plausible.

The deeper point is that "generativity" is not a property of a single device, viewed in isolation. It is a property of an ecosystem, and ecosystems contain mixes of open and closed components. The Internet itself runs on closed router firmware. The Apple II ran on closed Microsoft BASIC. The Web runs on browsers some of which are open and some of which are not. The question is not whether any given component is generative, but whether the system as a whole leaves room for innovation by unprivileged participants. By that measure, 2008 is, despite the malware plague, a more generative moment than 1998.

None of this is to dismiss Zittrain's worry. The trends toward tethering are real, and the regulatory choices made now will shape how the next decade unfolds. But the framing of generative vs. sterile, hero PC vs. villain appliance, forecloses the more interesting and more accurate question: how do we cultivate the conditions under which platforms, of whatever architectural shape, continue to invite outside contribution? That question has many answers, and very few of them look like the legal mandates Zittrain proposes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Market and consumer pressures naturally push supposedly 'sterile' devices, like the iPhone and Xbox, to open up to third-party developers over time to increase their value.
  2. 02The 'walled garden' model of curated app stores represents a successful generative compromise that provides security against malware without requiring heavy-handed legal intervention.
  3. 03Generativity has successfully migrated 'up the stack' to Web 2.0 platforms and APIs, which foster massive amounts of innovation despite being technically contingent on platform vendors.
  4. 04Digital openness should be evaluated as a property of the entire ecosystem rather than the architecture of a single isolated device.
  5. 05Proposed legal mandates like 'appliance neutrality' are unnecessary and potentially harmful because they address a problem that is already being solved by ordinary market dynamics and developer pressure.
