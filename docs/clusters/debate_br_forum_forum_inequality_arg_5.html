"While institutional market reform is valuable, tax-based redistribution remains a necessary and powerful tool for reducing inequality that should be defended rather than minimized."

David Grusky offers a useful warning against treating taxation as the only tool for reducing inequality. But in his effort to rescue the institutional critique of Occupy Wall Street from a narrow “tax the rich” agenda, he understates the power, legitimacy, and necessity of redistribution. If anything, the lesson of the last thirty years is not that we have relied too much on taxes and transfers. It is that we have relied on them too little.

The fact that pre-tax inequality has risen dramatically does not prove that tax policy is secondary. It proves that market incomes have become more unequal. But tax policy can shape market behavior itself. High top marginal rates in the middle decades of the twentieth century did not merely redistribute income after the fact; they reduced the incentive for executives and financiers to fight for enormous pay packages in the first place. When the after-tax value of an additional dollar at the top is smaller, the appetite for rent extraction changes. Grusky calls taxation a blunt instrument, and of course it is. But blunt instruments can be effective when the problem is broad, systemic, and hard to isolate.

Consider executive pay. Grusky is persuasive that corporate boards are often poorly positioned to bargain aggressively with CEOs. But what is the institutional fix? More independent boards? Say-on-pay votes? Better disclosure? These reforms are worth trying, yet they may have limited bite. Shareholders are often institutional investors with their own conflicts. Directors move in the same social world as executives. Compensation consultants can always devise new rationales. The very opacity of modern corporations makes it hard to identify the precise boundary between reward for performance and rent extraction. Progressive taxation does not require us to solve that mystery firm by firm. It says: if the economy is producing huge gains at the top, a substantial share of those gains should return to the public.

The same applies to finance. In the wake of the 2008 crisis, it is difficult to believe that all pre-tax incomes in the financial sector reflected social contribution. Some reflected implicit government guarantees, excessive leverage, regulatory arbitrage, and the ability to privatize gains while socializing losses. Yes, we need stronger financial regulation. But we also need taxes that reclaim some of the windfalls produced by a system whose stability ultimately depends on public authority. The bank bailouts made plain that the market and the state are not separate spheres. When risks threaten the whole economy, taxpayers become the backstop. It is reasonable that taxpayers share in the upside.

Grusky’s education example also points back to redistribution more than he acknowledges. Equalizing educational opportunity is expensive. Poor children do not need only formal access to college slots. They need decent prenatal care, safe neighborhoods, stable housing, high-quality early childhood education, well-funded schools, experienced teachers, summer enrichment, affordable community colleges, and universities that do not require crushing debt. These things require public money. Public money requires taxes. It is no answer to say that education reform should be our civil rights struggle if we are unwilling to fund it on the scale equal opportunity demands.

Moreover, expanding college access is not the same as reducing inequality. It can reduce some wage gaps, but it cannot address the full distribution of income. The top 1 percent are not simply college graduates receiving a scarcity premium. They include executives, financiers, partners in law and consulting firms, entrepreneurs, and owners of capital. The bottom of the labor market, meanwhile, includes people doing indispensable work that will not become highly paid merely because more people get degrees. A society still has to decide whether low-wage workers should receive wage subsidies, health insurance, food assistance, unemployment insurance, and retirement security. Those are redistributive questions.

Grusky is right that Americans often speak the language of fair play rather than equality. But we should be careful not to let polling about “opportunity” become a veto on social democracy. Americans also like Social Security, Medicare, public schools, unemployment insurance, veterans’ benefits, and progressive taxation when framed concretely. They distrust “taxes” in the abstract, but they rely on and defend many programs financed by taxes. The political task is not to avoid redistribution because conservatives have stigmatized it. The task is to defend it as the price of a decent common life.

There is also a moral point. Even if all market incomes were generated under perfectly competitive conditions, we would still face human beings with unequal needs, unequal luck, and unequal starting points. Markets reward scarcity and bargaining position, not moral worth. A brilliant hedge fund manager may earn vastly more than a gifted teacher, a nurse, or a parent caring for a disabled child. That fact may or may not involve rent. But democratic citizens are entitled to ask whether the resulting distribution is compatible with equal citizenship.

The most successful egalitarian countries do not rely solely on predistribution. They combine relatively compressed wages, strong labor institutions, high-quality public services, and substantial taxes and transfers. There is no reason the United States must copy any country wholesale, but there is also no reason to pretend that tax-based redistribution is a mere band-aid. It is one of the central ways a democracy asserts that economic outcomes are subject to public judgment.

Occupy Wall Street’s slogan, “We are the 99 percent,” was powerful because it joined institutional critique to distributive demand. It objected to rigged markets, but it also objected to the concentration of income, wealth, and political voice. Grusky is right that reformers should attack rents at their source. But a politics that waits for perfectly repaired markets before redistributing income will leave too much suffering untouched and too much power intact. Tax the rich, regulate the powerful, democratize the workplace, and invest in opportunity: these are complements, not rivals.
