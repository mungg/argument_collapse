Diversified DeepSeek

"New York City should maintain and reform its gifted and talented programs rather than eliminating them, as they are essential for meeting the needs of high-ability students and preventing middle-class flight from the public school system."

Carmen Fariña’s skepticism toward New York City’s gifted and talented programs misunderstands both their purpose and their constituency. Setting aside the political appeal of the phrase “gifted practices for all,” the reality is that separate, accelerated classrooms are not a frill—they are a lifeline for families who would otherwise abandon a public school system that too often fails its highest-ability students. Eliminating these programs would not magically redistribute excellence; it would accelerate middle-class flight and deepen the very segregation Fariña claims to oppose.

New York City is not a small district where a few bright children per grade can be easily accommodated by a single teacher. In many neighborhood schools, a fourth-grade classroom includes students reading at a second-grade level alongside peers ready for middle-school texts. A generalist teacher, no matter how skilled, cannot consistently provide the depth, pacing, and intellectual peer interaction that an advanced learner needs. Gifted classrooms offer that environment—and they do so while keeping families committed to public education. When ambitious parents see that their children will be challenged, they stay. When they see a stubborn refusal to group students by readiness, they either pay for private schools or move to suburbs with magnet options, draining resources and energy from the system.

Critics point to the racial and economic disparities in gifted enrollment as evidence that the programs are inherently harmful. The test-preparation industry and unequal access to screening do create real inequities, and those should be addressed through universal screening, multiple entry points, and free Saturday enrichment academies, not by eliminating the classes themselves. The solution to underrepresentation is to cast a wider net, not to sink the boat. A properly reformed gifted program can identify overlooked talent in high-poverty communities—talent that would be left to stagnate in heterogeneous classrooms without sustained intellectual challenge.

Fariña’s own track record is instructive. As a principal, she disbanded her school’s gifted program, but we do not know what was lost—which students quietly disengaged, which families left the zone. High achievers do not simply thrive on their own; they need structured acceleration, complex texts, and the company of motivated peers. The chancellor’s call for “gifted practices” that serve all students sounds egalitarian, but it ignores the tension between enrichment that is genuinely advanced and enrichment that simply adds busywork. If every child is doing “passion projects,” the child ready for seventh-grade math is not being served. New York’s elected officials should resist the easy rhetoric of destruction and instead demand rigorous outreach, transparent admissions, and an unwavering commitment to meeting the needs of every student—including those at the top.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The presence of gifted programs encourages ambitious families to remain in the public school system rather than moving to private schools or the suburbs.
  3. 03The solution to racial and economic disparities in gifted enrollment is to expand access through universal screening and outreach rather than abolishing the programs entirely.
  4. 04The concept of 'gifted practices for all' fails to provide the genuine academic acceleration and complexity that high-achieving students require to stay engaged.
Diversified Claude

"New York City should reform and maintain its gifted and talented programs rather than eliminating them, as they are essential for retaining middle-class families and providing high-achieving students with an appropriate education."

Eliminating gifted and talented programs would be a serious mistake, and Chancellor Fariña's musings about "gifted practices for all" amount to wishful thinking dressed up as equity policy. The honest question is not whether NYC's G&T system is perfect — it isn't — but whether the city's public schools can hold onto academically ambitious families and serve high-achieving children of all backgrounds without it. The evidence suggests they cannot.

Start with the practical reality. New York is one of the few large American cities where middle-class and professional families still send their children to public elementary school in significant numbers. Gifted programs are a major reason. Take them away, and many of those families will decamp to private schools, to the suburbs, or to charters that offer rigorous tracking by another name. The children left behind in the "common" classroom will not benefit from this exodus; they will inherit a school system with fewer engaged parents, less political clout, and shrinking budgets tied to enrollment.

Second, the pedagogical claim that teachers can simply "differentiate" within a single classroom is more aspiration than reality. Asking one teacher to simultaneously serve a child reading three grade levels above and a child still learning letter sounds, in a class of 25 to 30, is asking the impossible. Something gives, and usually it is the advanced child, who is told to read quietly while the teacher addresses more urgent needs. Bright kids from poor families — precisely the children equity-minded reformers say they care about — are the ones most harmed, because their parents cannot supplement at home.

Third, the diversity problem in G&T is real but fixable. The city could use multiple measures rather than a single test; it could reserve seats for high scorers from every district; it could fund universal pre-K test preparation so the playing field is leveled before the gate. Several scholars, including those at the Fordham Institute, have laid out workable models. Eliminating the programs is a far cruder response than reforming admissions.

Finally, "gifted practices for all" is a slogan, not a plan. If it means richer curriculum everywhere, wonderful — but that is compatible with keeping accelerated classrooms for students who need them. If it means pretending that all children learn at the same pace, it will fail the same way every previous attempt to abolish ability grouping has failed. New York should reform G&T, not retreat from it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The removal of gifted programs would lead to an exodus of middle-class families to private or suburban schools, depriving the public system of vital resources and political support.
  2. 02Expecting teachers to effectively differentiate instruction for vastly different skill levels within a single classroom is unrealistic and often results in advanced students being neglected.
  3. 03Gifted programs are particularly vital for high-achieving children from low-income backgrounds who lack the resources for supplemental education outside of school.
  4. 04The diversity issues within gifted programs can be addressed through admissions reforms and better preparation rather than the total abolition of the system.
  5. 05The concept of 'gifted practices for all' is an impractical slogan that fails to account for the reality that children learn at different paces and require different levels of academic challenge.
Diversified Gemini

"New York City should maintain its Gifted and Talented programs because they are essential for meeting the specific academic needs of high-achieving students and for ensuring the long-term stability of the public school system."

Schools Chancellor Carmen Fariña’s suggestion that we can simply provide "gifted practices to all students" in neighborhood schools relies on a utopian vision of the modern classroom that simply does not exist. While her egalitarian impulses are understandable, systematically dismantling New York City’s Gifted and Talented programs would do a profound disservice to high-achieving students and ultimately damage the public school system as a whole.

The prevailing educational trend is to push for "differentiated instruction," the idea that a single teacher can simultaneously cater to students reading three years below grade level, average students, and students reading three years above grade level, all within a standard class of thirty children. Any experienced educator will tell you that this is nearly impossible to execute successfully every day. When classrooms have wide disparities in readiness, teachers are forced to teach to the middle or focus on remediating those who are falling behind. Consequently, the most advanced students are left to languish, given busywork, or asked to act as peer tutors—a dynamic that stunts their academic growth and breeds deep frustration.

Gifted programs exist because some children genuinely require a faster pace and a more complex curriculum to remain engaged. Grouping these students together is not an act of elitism; it is a pedagogical necessity. It allows teachers to deliver appropriately challenging material without leaving other students in the dust.

Furthermore, if the Department of Education eliminates specialized programs for accelerated learners, parents who have the means will simply look elsewhere. New York City’s public school system relies heavily on retaining middle-class families who might otherwise depart for the suburbs or private schools. Gifted and Talented programs serve as a crucial anchor keeping these families invested in the public system. Losing them would lead to a broader erosion of resources, advocacy, and diversity across the district.

We should absolutely ensure that our general education classrooms are as robust and engaging as possible. Every child deserves access to a rich curriculum, art, music, and project-based learning. But pretending that all children have the exact same academic needs at the exact same time is a willful denial of reality. Fariña should embrace gifted classrooms for what they are: a vital tool for serving students who desperately need to be challenged.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Differentiated instruction in a standard classroom is often impossible to execute, leading teachers to ignore the needs of advanced students in favor of the middle or struggling learners.
  2. 02Gifted programs provide a necessary pedagogical environment where high-achieving students can access a faster-paced and more complex curriculum alongside their intellectual peers.
  3. 03Eliminating specialized programs would drive middle-class families out of the public school system, resulting in a loss of resources, advocacy, and diversity.
  4. 04The idea that 'gifted practices' can be effectively applied to all students simultaneously is a denial of the reality that children have vastly different academic needs.
