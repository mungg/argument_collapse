"The climate crisis is fundamentally a matter of historical responsibility and global inequity rather than a mere technical problem, requiring a solution centered on justice for the global South."

When the editors of the *Boston Review* ask, “Can we stop global warming?” they are, perhaps without fully intending it, asking a question that is shot through with unexamined assumptions about who “we” are and what “stopping” entails. Kerry Emanuel’s lead essay elegantly distills the physics and the climatology, yet it, too, operates within a discursive frame that treats the planet as a single system to be managed by a unitary species. This frame is both scientifically tidy and politically anaesthetising. It obscures the fact that the climate crisis is not, at heart, a technical problem awaiting a clever enough engineering fix; it is a problem of justice, of historical responsibility, and of radically unequal distributions of power and vulnerability. Reframed in this way, the genuine question is not whether global warming can be stopped in some abstract sense, but whether the rich, high-emitting nations of the North can be compelled to stop causing it, while the poor, low-emitting nations of the South are given the space and resources to pursue a dignified development. The answer, on 1 January 2007, is that it remains profoundly uncertain—not because the physics is obscure, but because the politics of equity have been systematically marginalised from the centre of the climate debate.

Consider first the distribution of responsibility. According to data compiled by the World Resources Institute and other bodies, the United States alone, with less than 5 percent of the world’s population, has contributed roughly 30 percent of the cumulative carbon dioxide emissions from fossil-fuel burning since the industrial revolution. The European Union adds another quarter or so. In per-capita terms, the average American emits as much carbon dioxide in two and a half days as a typical sub-Saharan African farmer emits in an entire year. This staggering asymmetry means that the global climate problem is overwhelmingly the product of the industrialisation of a handful of nations. Yet the costs of climate change fall most heavily on those who have contributed least. Small island states in the Pacific and Caribbean face existential threats from sea-level rise; Bangladesh, a country with per-capita emissions a fraction of that in the United States, is home to tens of millions of people who live in the deltaic lowlands that increasingly intense cyclones will flood with ever greater frequency and depth. It is no accident that the single deadliest storm Emanuel mentions—the 1970 Bangladesh cyclone that killed some 300,000 people—struck a nation of the global periphery.

The lead essay’s treatment of this moral geography is symptomatically detached. Emanuel notes the human toll of tropical cyclones and laments the staggering loss of life, but he does not pause to ask whether the international community has any special obligation to bear the costs of protecting these vulnerable populations. Nor does he enquire into the political economy that allows the global North to emit without restraint while the global South is effectively told that its own development must now be constrained for the sake of an atmosphere it had no hand in filling. This silence is pervasive in the mainstream literature on climate change, and it reflects a structural failure of imagination. The IPCC, that “valuable communication channel,” has been remarkably resistant to integrating ethical analysis into its assessments. Its reports are thick with scenario-building and process modules, but thin on the distributional consequences of both the impacts and the proposed mitigation policies.

When one restores justice to the centre of the frame, the entire discussion of costs and benefits changes character. The classical cost-benefit analyses that have dominated the economic modelling of climate change—the discounted utilitarian frameworks of William Nordhaus and even the somewhat more aggressive Stern Review—treat the world as a single, spatially undifferentiated agent, and in doing so they routinely privilege the interests of the rich. A dollar of lost GDP in the United States counts equally with a dollar of lost livelihood in the Sahel, but because the former is far larger in absolute terms, the mathematics of aggregation tends to favour policies that protect wealthy economies against moderate damage even at the expense of allowing very poor people to suffer catastrophic loss. A truly equitable approach would start from a different premise: that every human being has an equal right to the waste-absorbing capacity of the atmosphere, and that those who have already exceeded their fair share owe a debt to those who have not. This is the core insight of the “contraction and convergence” framework that was put forward by the Global Commons Institute and has since been endorsed by a number of international NGOs and some northern governments. Under this approach, a global carbon budget is set that is consistent with climate stabilisation, and emission rights are allocated to nations on a per-capita basis that converges from today’s unbalanced distribution to an equal per-capita allocation by a target date. Rich nations that exceed their allocation would need to buy permits from poor nations that fall below theirs, creating a permanent flow of resources from the historical emitters to the historical victims.

Such a framework is not merely an ethical abstraction. It offers a practical path out of the diplomatic deadlock that has plagued climate negotiations since the 1992 Rio Earth Summit. The principal reason that the United States walked away from the Kyoto Protocol, and that major developing nations like China and India have refused to accept binding emission caps, is that the developed world has never been willing to confront the equity question squarely. The US Senate, in the 1997 Byrd-Hagel resolution, explicitly conditioned its acceptance of any climate treaty on meaningful participation by developing countries, a demand that developing countries interpreted as a demand that they accept the costs of a problem they did not create. The Kyoto Protocol’s “clean development mechanism,” which allows rich countries to earn carbon credits by financing emission-reducing projects in poor countries, was meant to be a bridge, but it has been chronically underfunded and has been plagued by concerns about additionality and local exploitation. The path forward cannot be an endless rearguard action by the North to avoid paying for the atmospheric damage it has already done.

Sceptics of this justice-oriented approach often argue that the sums involved would be impossibly large. But the sums are large only because the historical debt is large—and to argue that a debt is too big to repay is not a moral defence; it is a declaration of bankruptcy. Moreover, the actual resource transfers envisioned under a well-designed cap-and-trade system with per-capita allocation are not beyond the realm of political feasibility. Estimates vary, but a convergence target that aimed to stabilise atmospheric carbon dioxide at around 450 parts per million might involve annual flows on the order of $100 billion to $200 billion from industrialised to developing countries. This is a large number, but it is comparable to what the United States has been spending annually on the Iraq war, and it is far smaller than the subsidies that the world’s governments currently provide to fossil-fuel production and consumption. The obstacle is not economic capacity; it is a lack of political will rooted in a refusal to accept that the North’s historical emissions constitute a genuine liability.

There is, of course, no guarantee that even a perfectly just allocation of emission rights would “stop” global warming in the physical sense. The climate system has immense inertia, and we are already committed to a certain amount of additional warming and sea-level rise. But a just framework would fundamentally change the quality of the global response. It would liberate developing countries from the invidious choice between growth and climate responsibility, and it would create a self-reinforcing mechanism in which the rich have a direct financial incentive to decarbonise the fastest. It would also transform the ethical architecture of the international system. For too long, the climate debate has been conducted in the language of universal peril, a language that flattens difference and obscures accountability. The question “Can we stop global warming?” is, in this sense, a bad question, because it presumes a “we” that does not exist. There is no single “we” with a shared history, a shared capacity, or a shared exposure to risk. There is a fractured humanity in which a small minority has burned its way to prosperity and a large majority is being asked to bear the consequences.

The coming year, 2007, will be a pivotal one. The new IPCC report due in February will almost certainly sharpen the scientific consensus. The Democratic majority in the United States Congress will face pressure to pass meaningful climate legislation. The Kyoto Protocol’s first commitment period begins to expire in 2012, and a new round of negotiations is already underway. Whether these events culminate in a robust and equitable global accord will depend overwhelmingly on whether the question of justice is allowed to surface as more than an afterthought. If we cannot find the language to say that the polluter must pay, that the atmosphere belongs to all humanity in equal measure, and that the poor are owed a gift of clean development by the rich, then the technical debate about emission pathways and carbon cycles will remain an exercise in planetary management conducted for the benefit of those who already have the most. Global warming, on that trajectory, will be stopped only when it has already ruined the lives of the people who could least afford to stop it themselves.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The global North, particularly the United States and Europe, is historically responsible for the vast majority of cumulative carbon emissions while the global South bears the disproportionate burden of climate impacts.
  2. 02Mainstream climate discourse and institutions like the IPCC systematically marginalize ethical analysis and the distributional consequences of climate change.
  3. 03Standard cost-benefit economic models are biased toward protecting the wealth of rich nations at the expense of the livelihoods of the poor.
  4. 04A 'contraction and convergence' framework based on equal per-capita rights to the atmosphere is the only viable way to break the diplomatic deadlock between developed and developing nations.
  5. 05The financial resources required for global climate justice are economically feasible and comparable to existing military spending or fossil fuel subsidies.
  6. 06The concept of a unitary 'we' in climate discussions is a false premise that obscures the reality of fractured humanity with unequal histories and risks.
