Democracy, Common Knowledge, and the Trouble with Pluralism

Deborah Meier frames her argument as a defense of democracy. The phrase "educating a democracy" runs through her essay like a refrain, and her deepest claim is that standardization of curriculum and assessment is incompatible with the kind of citizenship a democratic society requires. I want to take this framing seriously and then dispute it on its own ground. I think Meier has the relationship between democracy and common knowledge almost exactly backwards.

Begin with what Meier says. A democracy, she argues, requires citizens who can think for themselves, who have learned to negotiate disagreement, who have seen adults exercise judgment in the face of uncertainty, and who feel themselves to be agents capable of influencing their world. From this she infers that schools must be plural in their definitions of "well-educated," that authority should be local, and that experts should be "subservient to citizens." A uniform national standard, on her view, undermines the democratic personality by training children in deference to distant authority.

There is something to this. A school that runs on test prep is unlikely to graduate independent thinkers. A teacher who has been reduced to a delivery mechanism for a state framework will not model intellectual seriousness. Meier is right that democratic dispositions cannot be taught by lecture; they have to be practiced, and they have to be witnessed in the practice of the adults around the child. So far, so good.

But Meier slides from these observations to a much stronger claim: that pluralism about what counts as a good education is itself democratic, and that any common standard is a threat to democratic life. This is where I get off the train. The democratic tradition has, in fact, always insisted on something like a common education — not a uniform education, but a shared one. Jefferson, whom Meier quotes selectively, was an advocate of common public schooling for exactly the reason that self-government requires a citizenry that can read the same texts, follow the same arguments, and participate in the same public conversation. Horace Mann's common school movement, which built the institutional foundation of American public education, was animated by the same idea. So was W. E. B. Du Bois's argument for academic, not merely vocational, education for black children. The tradition Meier invokes is in fact much more friendly to shared standards than her essay acknowledges.

Why? Because democracy is not just a matter of personal disposition. It is also a matter of shared cognitive resources. Citizens have to be able to talk to one another about public questions, which requires that they share enough knowledge — historical, scientific, mathematical, literary — to make the conversation possible. A democracy in which children in different schools learn different histories, encounter different canonical texts, develop different vocabularies for thinking about the natural world, is not more democratic than one with common content; it is more fragmented. E. D. Hirsch has been making this argument for over a decade, and while I think he overreaches in particulars, the central insight is sound. The democratic case for common content is at least as strong as Meier's case against it.

Meier's response would presumably be that common content can emerge from local dialogue, and that families and teachers, working together, can decide what their children should know. But this answer founders on scale. A democracy of 270 million people cannot operate on the cognitive resources of 90,000 separately negotiated curricula. At some point, we need to be able to assume that a high-school graduate has read the Declaration of Independence, knows the basic outline of the Civil War, can read a graph, can solve a linear equation, can identify the major branches of government. Without these common reference points, public deliberation becomes harder, not easier. The fragmentation of common knowledge is one of the things that is wrong with American democracy in 1999, not one of its hopeful features.

There is a second problem with Meier's democratic argument, which is her treatment of expertise. She writes that "experts should be subservient to citizens." This formulation will appeal to anyone who has been bullied by a credentialed bureaucrat. But it conflates two different things: the question of who sets the goals of public education (citizens, through democratic process — agreed) and the question of who decides whether those goals are being met (which often requires technical expertise that ordinary citizens, including ordinary parents and teachers, do not have). A democracy that wants effective public institutions cannot dispense with expertise; it can only insist that expertise be accountable to democratic purposes. Meier's framing — experts versus citizens — obscures this.

Her stronger formulation is that children need to see "the adults in their lives" exercising judgment. Yes. But the adults in our lives include not only family members and neighborhood teachers but also the wider community of fellow citizens, present and past, whose accumulated knowledge constitutes the curriculum. A child who has read Frederick Douglass has been mentored by Frederick Douglass; a child who has worked through a geometry proof has been mentored by Euclid. The "experts" Meier wants to demote include the dead, who have a vote in this democracy too. Curriculum is not a usurpation of local judgment by distant power; it is the way the past speaks to the present.

A third point. Meier's diagnosis of the real American crisis — the disconnection of young people from responsible adults — is partly persuasive. But I would add to it a diagnosis she resists: the disconnection of young Americans from a shared cultural inheritance. A child who graduates from high school knowing little of American history, less of world history, almost no literature outside her own contemporary moment, and unable to engage with serious public arguments is not a free citizen. She is a vulnerable consumer of whatever passes by on her screen. The standards movement, in its better forms, is partly an attempt to insist that public schools transmit the cultural and intellectual inheritance that democratic citizenship requires. To frame this as anti-democratic is to misunderstand what democracy needs.

None of this is a defense of the punitive, test-driven, narrow version of standards reform Meier rightly criticizes. The Chicago expulsion stories are appalling. The 80-percent fail-rate Massachusetts test is a scandal. The reduction of teachers to script-readers is a betrayal of the profession. On these points Meier and I are allies.

But the answer to bad standards is good standards, not pluralism for its own sake. A democratic curriculum is one that is rich, demanding, shared, and open to argument — not one that varies from school to school depending on local taste. The point of a democratic education is not to confirm children in the views of their parents and neighbors; it is to give them access to the wider human conversation so that they can ultimately judge for themselves. That access is hard to provide without some shared content, some common assessments, and some willingness on the part of adults to say: these are the things we believe an educated person should know.

Meier ends her essay with an appeal for "a little more commitment to democracy." I share the appeal. But the democracy I want my children to inherit is one in which they can argue with their fellow citizens because they have learned the same difficult things — not one in which every school is its own republic. Meier's vision is humane, and her schools are admirable. But her democratic theory is, I think, too thin to bear the weight she puts on it. A democracy needs both: local communities that know and love their children, and a wider commons of shared knowledge that makes citizenship possible. The standards debate, at its best, is about how to honor both. Meier honors only the first.

  1. 01Democracy is not merely a personal disposition but a collective practice that necessitates shared cognitive resources and common cultural reference points among citizens.
  2. 02Pluralism in educational definitions leads to social fragmentation rather than democratic empowerment, as it prevents citizens from participating in a unified public conversation.
  3. 03The democratic tradition, supported by figures like Jefferson and Du Bois, has historically advocated for common public schooling as a means of ensuring self-government.
  4. 04Expertise in education should not be viewed as an enemy of citizenship but as a necessary tool for ensuring that public institutions effectively meet democratically established goals.
  5. 05A shared curriculum provides students with an intellectual inheritance that protects them from being vulnerable consumers and grants them access to a wider human conversation beyond their local community.
  6. 06The solution to the flaws of current reform is to develop rich and demanding common standards rather than abandoning the concept of shared content in favor of local pluralism.
