"While Edward Miguel’s proposal for economic insurance is useful, a true African renaissance requires addressing the deeper structural and political drivers of violence, such as the global arms trade, resource looting, and political impunity."

Edward Miguel’s most original contribution in this essay is his proposal for rapid conflict prevention support, an insurance mechanism for states at risk of climate or commodity shocks. The idea is elegant, and I agree that conflict prevention must become a central priority. But I want to argue that Miguel’s lens on conflict, while insightful, remains too narrow. Africa’s wars are not simply triggered by income shocks; they are sustained by political economies of violence that span borders, by the global arms trade, and by a culture of impunity that allows warlords and state-backed militias to slaughter civilians without consequence. To talk about Africa’s turn without confronting these structural drivers is to miss the heart of the matter.

Miguel acknowledges that the proportion of African countries engaged in armed conflict has not declined since 1995, and he points to Congo and Sudan as the gravest threats. His analysis is correct but incomplete. The Democratic Republic of Congo’s war, which has killed millions since 1998, is not fundamentally about rainfall or copper prices; it is about the looting of mineral resources — coltan, cassiterite, gold — by a network of armed groups, neighboring states, and international corporations. The peace deal signed in 2002 has never been fully implemented because the belligerents profit from chaos. In Darfur, the janjaweed militias are armed, trained, and directed by the Sudanese state, which uses ethnic cleansing to clear land and consolidate power. Economic shocks may have contributed to the background conditions, but the proximate drivers are political strategies of survival by elites.

Addressing such conflicts requires more than weather-indexed aid transfers. It demands a sustained international effort to disrupt the war economies. The UN’s growing use of targeted sanctions — travel bans, asset freezes — on individuals who violate peace agreements is a start, but it has been unevenly applied and often undermined by the very Security Council powers that are supposed to enforce it. China’s protection of Khartoum, which Miguel notes, is the most egregious example. The International Criminal Court, operational since 2002, has issued arrest warrants for leaders of the Lord’s Resistance Army in Uganda and is investigating atrocities in Darfur and Congo. These efforts signal that impunity is no longer absolute, but they are politically contested and face open defiance from some African governments and the African Union, which argues that the court disproportionately targets Africans. This controversy must be engaged, not bypassed. Building a durable peace requires credible justice mechanisms that enjoy local legitimacy.

Migration also points to the regional nature of these conflicts, whose dynamics Miguel touches on but does not fully pursue. The fact that Kenya’s post-election violence disrupted fuel supplies to Uganda is a reminder that Africa’s economic and security futures are interlinked. Yet regional organizations like the African Union and the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) remain underfunded and overly dependent on external donors, limiting their capacity to mediate disputes and field peacekeepers. The African Standby Force, envisaged for years, remains largely aspirational. Strengthening African-led peace and security architecture is arguably more important than any amount of bilateral aid for development projects, because without stability, even the best projects will collapse.

Finally, the arms that fuel these wars do not originate in Africa. The massive flow of small arms and light weapons into the continent comes from factories in Eastern Europe, China, and the West, often through illicit brokers who exploit weak regulation. A serious commitment to ending Africa’s wars must include enforceable global arms controls and a crackdown on the illicit trade. So far, the international community has been far more willing to talk about aid and trade than about the weapons it supplies.

In sum, Miguel’s RCPS is a useful tool, but it sits within a much larger, messier challenge. If Africa is to avoid another generation of devastating war, the world must combine emergency insurance with a genuine assault on the political economy of violence: accountable peacekeeping, consistent pressure on predatory rulers, a fair and effective international justice system, and an end to the supply of guns. That is a tall order, but nothing less will secure the African renaissance Miguel hopes for.
