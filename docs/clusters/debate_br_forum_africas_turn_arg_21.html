"External actors should shift from trying to drive African development to providing supportive resources that respect and empower local agency and knowledge."

Edward Miguel's essay is strongest when it grounds abstract claims about African development in specific observations from his fieldwork in Kenya. The Busia of his narrative—a town with supermarkets, ATMs, and internet cafés where a decade earlier there was only desperation—is compelling precisely because it is observed rather than inferred. I want to offer a complementary perspective grounded in my own understanding of what rural development research reveals about the possibilities and limits of external intervention.

The central lesson of development economics over the past two decades is that contexts matter profoundly. Policies that succeed in one setting may fail in another. Interventions that work at small scale may not scale. What works when external funding is abundant may not be sustainable when it dries up. Miguel's own evidence supports this lesson. Foreign aid has flowed to Africa in enormous quantities since the 1960s, yet African per capita income declined for twenty-five years. Democratic transitions have occurred in dozens of countries, but many have been followed by violence and reversals. Commodity booms have created windfall revenues, but most African countries remain dependent on exporting raw materials rather than processing them domestically.

This evidence counsels humility about external actors' ability to transform African development. Foreign aid, however well-intentioned, cannot break countries out of poverty traps if governance is weak and institutions are extractive. Democracy cannot deliver development if it operates in societies where ethnic polarization trumps programmatic politics. Chinese investment cannot catalyze transformation if it reinforces primary commodity dependency rather than building local capacity. These are not failures of Africa; they are limitations of what external actors can accomplish in contexts they do not fully understand and where sustainability depends on local conditions.

The appropriate role for external actors is not to drive development but to support it. This means providing resources—aid, investment, market access—that African actors can use to build on their own strengths. It means sharing knowledge about what works in comparable contexts without presuming that lessons transfer automatically. It means respecting African agency and holding African leaders accountable for using resources responsibly. It means engaging with African civil society, not just governments, to ensure that development benefits reach ordinary citizens rather than flowing exclusively to elites.

Miguel's recommendations are broadly consistent with this approach. Reducing agricultural subsidies would open markets for African producers without dictating how they should respond. Using aid as conflict insurance would provide resources to African states and societies facing acute stress without imposing externally designed solutions. Supporting climate adaptation would help African farmers manage risks that affect them directly. These are supportive rather than transformative interventions—they accept that development must ultimately be African-led while recognizing that external actors can make African efforts more likely to succeed.

I would add one more recommendation: invest in African knowledge systems. Development ultimately depends on African understanding of African conditions. This means supporting African universities and research institutions, creating opportunities for African intellectuals to study and work on African problems, and building partnerships that bring local knowledge into dialogue with global expertise. It means recognizing that the best solutions to African development challenges will be generated by Africans who understand the contexts in which those solutions must operate.

The Busia story is ultimately a story about African agency. The entrepreneurs who opened supermarkets and internet cafés, the workers who took jobs in flower farms, the farmers who responded to price incentives by increasing production—these are Africans making decisions in response to changing conditions. External actors like Miguel and the development organizations he works with provided support, but the dynamism was African. This is where hope lies: not in commodity booms or Chinese investment or Western aid, but in the creative energy of African individuals and communities building lives and livelihoods in challenging circumstances. Supporting that energy while respecting its independence is the proper role for international engagement.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical failure of large-scale foreign aid and democratic transitions demonstrates that external interventions are limited by local contexts they do not fully understand.
  2. 02Development cannot be sustained by external actors if local governance is weak, institutions are extractive, or investment reinforces commodity dependency.
  3. 03International engagement should focus on supportive measures like reducing trade subsidies and providing conflict insurance rather than imposing transformative, externally designed solutions.
  4. 04Investing in African universities and research institutions is essential because the most effective development solutions must be generated by those who understand the local context.
  5. 05The economic progress seen in places like Busia is primarily the result of African entrepreneurs and workers exercising their own agency in response to changing conditions.
