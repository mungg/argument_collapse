"The United States should reject gender quotas and parity laws because democratic legitimacy should be based on equal opportunity for individuals rather than state-engineered proportional representation for demographic groups."

Jennifer Piscopo asks Americans to rethink women’s political equality by looking abroad to countries that have adopted gender quotas, parity laws, and other forms of positive action. Her essay is thoughtful, historically informed, and appropriately impatient with women’s underrepresentation in Congress. Yet I am not convinced that the solution to an imperfect democracy is to sort candidates by sex and engineer outcomes in the name of representation. The United States should work to remove unfair barriers to candidacy, but it should resist the idea that democratic legitimacy depends on proportional representation by group.

The first difficulty is conceptual. Piscopo relies heavily on the idea that women possess a collective “right to presence.” But elected office in a liberal democracy is not something any group owns. Citizens have the right to vote, speak, organize, donate, persuade, run for office, and hold office if elected. They do not have a right to see their demographic characteristics mirrored in a legislature. If they did, the principle would not stop at sex. We would need to ask whether Congress reflects the population by religion, age, income, profession, disability, marital status, parenthood, region, ideology, and countless other categories. Some of these categories matter deeply to people’s lives. But a legislature cannot be a scale model of society in every respect.

Piscopo anticipates this objection by distinguishing politically salient “social facts” from trivial traits such as hair color. Fair enough. Sex is obviously politically salient. So are race and class. But once the state begins deciding which identities require proportional political presence, it gains a power that should make democrats nervous. Representation becomes less about citizens choosing among candidates and more about institutions classifying citizens. That may be manageable in some political systems. It fits uneasily with an American constitutional tradition that treats citizens as individuals with equal rights, not as members of state-certified blocs.

The second difficulty is practical. The United States does not have the party-list systems that make quotas easier to administer. We have primaries, decentralized ballot access, single-member districts, and weak parties. Piscopo acknowledges this, and she suggests softer forms of positive action: lower filing fees for women, party funds for women candidates, gender-balanced shortlists, and party quotas for internal bodies. Some of these ideas are less troubling than others. Political parties are voluntary associations and may decide to recruit more women. Donors may support women candidates. Training programs may encourage women to run. But government-created advantages for candidates based on sex would be a mistake.

Imagine a state lowering filing fees for women but not men. Is a wealthy woman lawyer more in need of state assistance than a working-class man trying to challenge a machine-backed incumbent? Is the daughter of a political family structurally disadvantaged in the same way as a male immigrant small-business owner with no donor network? Sex matters, but it is a blunt instrument. Policies that target actual barriers—poverty, ballot access burdens, lack of childcare, harassment, and unequal media treatment—are more defensible than policies that assume women candidates as a class deserve preferential treatment over men candidates as a class.

The third difficulty is political. Quotas and parity rules risk deepening cynicism about women’s achievements. That does not mean women are less qualified; indeed, Piscopo cites evidence that women candidates often have to be more qualified than men. But in a polarized environment, any formal preference will be used to dismiss women as beneficiaries of special treatment. Supporters may respond that opponents already dismiss women anyway. True. Sexism does not need a reason. Still, reforms should seek to strengthen the perceived legitimacy of officeholders, not hand their enemies an easy talking point.

More fundamentally, parity arguments can undervalue persuasion. If women are underrepresented partly because party leaders, donors, journalists, and voters harbor biased assumptions, then those assumptions should be confronted directly. Recruit women. Fund women. Vote for women. Challenge sexist coverage. Condemn harassment. Build networks. Teach girls and boys that public leadership belongs to both. None of this is passive. It is the hard work of civil society. The success of women in 2018, while incomplete, suggests that mobilization can matter. The answer to slow progress should be more organizing, not a theory that government must guarantee group outcomes.

Piscopo criticizes the “women don’t run” narrative for putting the burden on women. She is right that the phrase can be glib. Women often do not run because politics is hostile, expensive, invasive, and incompatible with family responsibilities. But candidacy is always a choice. Many men also decline to run because the process is miserable. The goal should be to make political life more humane and accessible for everyone. Public financing would reduce dependence on wealthy donors. Reasonable ballot access rules would help challengers. Allowing campaign funds to cover childcare would help parents. Stronger enforcement against threats would protect candidates. More flexible legislative schedules would make officeholding easier for people with families. These reforms would likely help women substantially without turning sex into a formal qualification.

We should also be careful about the assumption that women’s presence necessarily advances women’s interests. Piscopo herself notes the problem with instrumental arguments. Women are not a party. They disagree about abortion, policing, taxes, war, schools, religion, and the proper size of government. Many women voters supported Donald Trump in 2016; many oppose him fiercely. Some women politicians are feminists; others reject feminism. If the justification for parity is that women will make policy better for women, then we must decide which women’s views count. If the justification is simply that women are half the population, then we are back to demographic mirroring as an end in itself.

There is, of course, a symbolic harm when a legislature is overwhelmingly male. Children notice who governs. Citizens notice who is treated as authoritative. A Congress with more women would almost certainly be healthier. I do not deny the importance of example, nor do I deny that informal discrimination has kept many women out. But symbolism must be balanced against the central democratic principle that voters choose representatives for reasons that cannot be reduced to identity. A woman voter may prefer a male candidate who shares her views on health care to a woman candidate who does not. A man may vote for a woman candidate because he thinks she is the best person for the job. This freedom is not a defect in democracy; it is democracy.

The comparison to voting rights also has limits. Denying women the vote was a direct legal exclusion. Underrepresentation in office is different. It may result from discrimination, but it also reflects choices by voters, parties, candidates, and communities. The law should punish discrimination where it can be identified. It should not presume that any disparity proves a rights violation requiring proportional correction. In many domains, disparities are warning signs that deserve investigation. They are not always proof of injustice in each individual case.

What, then, should be done? First, parties should recruit more women because it is politically smart and morally sound. A party that overlooks half the talent in the country is incompetent. Second, campaign finance reform should make it easier for candidates without wealthy networks to compete. Third, civic organizations should train candidates from underrepresented communities, including but not limited to women. Fourth, legislatures should modernize workplace rules, including parental leave and anti-harassment protections. Fifth, media organizations should examine sexist coverage and apply standards consistently. Sixth, voters should take responsibility for the patterns they produce.

These measures are not as dramatic as parity laws. They may not satisfy those who see women’s underrepresentation as a collective rights violation. But they preserve a crucial distinction: equal opportunity is a democratic obligation; equal outcomes by sex are not. The United States has often failed even at equal opportunity, and reformers should attack those failures vigorously. They should not redefine citizenship so that the state must distribute candidacies according to demographic categories.

The centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should remind us that women demanded entrance into the political community as equal citizens. The best tribute to that achievement is not to create a new architecture of group entitlement, but to make the old promise real: no legal barriers, no informal gatekeeping, no harassment, no double standards, and no presumption that politics is men’s work. Women do not need offices reserved for them. They need a fair fight—and a political culture wise enough to recognize their leadership when it appears.
