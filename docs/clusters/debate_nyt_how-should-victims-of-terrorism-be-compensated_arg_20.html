"The federal government should not create special compensation funds for specific high-profile tragedies, but should instead strengthen existing state-level victim compensation systems that serve all victims of violent crime."

The federal government should not create special compensation funds every time a horrific act of mass violence captures national attention. The impulse is generous, but the policy is arbitrary.

The Sept. 11 Victim Compensation Fund was born in extraordinary circumstances: an act of war-scale terrorism, thousands dead, and an urgent desire to stabilize the aviation system by preventing a wave of lawsuits. That does not make it a template for Boston, Newtown, Aurora, Virginia Tech or the next terrible event. If Congress writes checks after some tragedies and not others, compensation will depend less on justice than on television coverage, political pressure and whether the victims fit a national narrative.

The family of a child killed in a school shooting grieves no more deeply because the crime is famous. The survivor of a neighborhood shooting, a domestic violence attack or a workplace massacre may face the same funeral bills, medical debts and lost wages. A special federal fund for highly visible attacks tells those victims, unintentionally but unmistakably, that their suffering counts for less.

The right answer is to strengthen the systems that already exist for all victims of violent crime. State victim compensation programs, supported in part by federal crime-victim funds, should be better financed, faster and more generous. They should cover counseling, relocation, lost wages and long-term disability more consistently. Emergency rooms should treat first and bill later; prosecutors and victim advocates should help families navigate benefits; mental health services should not vanish after the memorial services end.

Private donations also have a legitimate place. They are imperfect, but they are expressions of civil society, not entitlements. Donors can choose to support a school, a hospital, a scholarship fund or a family. Those funds should be transparent, but they need not be federalized.

Lawsuits, meanwhile, should remain available when someone was negligent: a security company, a venue, a manufacturer, or another responsible party. Courts are slow and imperfect, but they are designed to assign responsibility, not simply distribute sympathy.

Compassion should be steady, not episodic. The federal government should help victims of violence because they are victims, not because their tragedy became a national symbol.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Creating special funds for only certain tragedies makes compensation dependent on media coverage and political pressure rather than justice.
  2. 02Providing federal funds only for highly visible attacks unfairly implies that the suffering of victims of less famous crimes is less significant.
  3. 03Existing state victim compensation programs should be better financed and expanded to provide consistent support for counseling, lost wages, and medical needs for all victims.
  4. 04Private donations should remain a voluntary expression of civil society rather than being federalized into government-run entitlements.
  5. 05The legal system should remain the primary mechanism for assigning responsibility and seeking damages in cases where negligence can be proven.
