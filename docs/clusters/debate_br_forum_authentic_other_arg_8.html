Diversified Gemini

"While Marjorie Perloff correctly identifies the Orientalism behind the Araki Yasusada hoax, her defense of the hoaxer as performing a 'service' is ethically flawed and relies on a narrow, Western-centric definition of Japanese authenticity."

Marjorie Perloff’s essay on the Araki Yasusada hoax offers a compelling taxonomy of the American poetry establishment's blind spots. She rightly names the phenomenon at work: Orientalism. The willingness of esteemed editors to swallow a fabricated, Zen-inflected, tragic Japanese victim speaks volumes about the racialized expectations that govern literary reception in the United States. However, Perloff’s analysis ultimately falters because she is too eager to celebrate the white hoaxer’s "brilliant job" while simultaneously replacing one limiting stereotype of Japanese identity with another.

Let us be clear about what Kent Johnson (or his circle) has done. He has engaged in a sophisticated form of literary blackface—or rather, yellowface. By adopting the persona of a Hiroshima survivor, the hoaxer usurped a space of profound historical trauma that belongs to marginalized others, repackaging it to suit the palate of white avant-garde editors. Perloff identifies this, noting how the poems cater to Western guilt and fantasies of "gentler" Eastern grace. Yet, rather than condemning this minstrelsy, Perloff applauds it as a necessary corrective, an "invaluable service" that exposes the foibles of multiculturalism. This is a staggering ethical leap. It suggests that the pain and history of Asians are merely raw materials for white Americans to use in their intramural theoretical debates. If the poetry market is indeed "glutted and cutthroat," as Perloff argues, how does that justify a white man advancing his career by counterfeiting the voice of a radioactive Japanese corpse?

Furthermore, Perloff’s attempt to contrast the fake Yasusada with "authentic" contemporary Japanese poetry reveals her own ideological biases. To prove that real Japanese poets don't write like Yasusada, she quotes Shiraishi Kazuko and Ito Hiromi—poets who write highly colloquial, sexually explicit, postmodern free verse. Perloff approvingly notes that these poets deal with "pretty much all the themes that would characterize our own poetry" and that they will not allow themselves to be "patronized" as victims.

But notice the underlying assumption here: for Japanese poetry to be "authentic" and worthy of respect, it must mirror the cosmopolitan, advanced-capitalist aesthetic of the West. Perloff suggests that modern Japan is "too close to our own advanced capitalist world" to be exotic. In her view, the true Japanese subject is the one who sounds like a New York Language poet or a San Francisco Beat. She replaces the Orientalist stereotype of the Zen ascetic with the globalist stereotype of the deracinated postmodern consumer. If a Japanese poet were to write with restraint, or focus on the atomic bomb, or employ traditional aesthetics, would Perloff dismiss them as catering to Western fantasies?

The Yasusada hoax is not an indictment of "The New Multiculturalism," as Perloff bizarrely claims. It is an indictment of a literary culture still entirely dominated by white editors, writers, and critics who believe they possess the right to define what is and isn't authentic for people of color. The editors at *APR* were fooled because they don't know any better and didn't care to ask actual Japanese scholars. Perloff sees through the hoax, but her ultimate takeaway is that we should applaud the white hoaxer for showing us how silly we are. Both positions center the white American ego while treating the actual history of Hiroshima as an expendable prop.
