"The editors who published the Yasusada poems should be defended because their errors were the result of standard literary practices and genuine aesthetic appreciation rather than intellectual complacency or a failure of character."

**A Modest Defense of the Editors**

Marjorie Perloff's essay reaches a verdict on the editors who published Yasusada—not quite a guilty verdict, but something close. They are described as having operated under "the Foucauldian notion of cultural construction"; as no longer trusting "the individual talent to rise above mass culture"; as practitioners of a "New Multiculturalism" that, despite itself, has become "Orientalism's inadvertent promoter." Their failure to consult "bona fide Japanese poets, scholars, or translators" is treated as evidence of a deeper intellectual complacency. I want to enter a modest defense.

The premise of Perloff's argument is that the Yasusada poems' anachronisms—scubadivers in a 1925 haiku, *Empire of Signs* in a 1967 letter, Paul Celan as an interwar Japanese influence—were obvious enough that careful readers should have caught them. She marshals these clues in numbered lists and treats them as glaring. But this is hindsight criticism. Once one knows a manuscript is a hoax, the clues organize themselves into a pattern. Before one knows, they are scattered oddities in a thicket of other oddities, no more salient than the many genuinely strange features of any unfamiliar foreign literature.

Consider: is "scubadiver" obviously anachronistic in a translated 1925 haiku? Only if one happens to know the history of scuba technology. The word in Japanese for an underwater diver predates scuba; one could imagine a translator using "scubadiver" loosely. Is the 1967 letter's reference to *Empire of Signs* obviously impossible? Only if one happens to know the publication history of Barthes in French, which most American poetry editors do not have at their fingertips. Is the appearance of Celan in a prewar Japanese avant-garde circle absurd? Yes, but the relevant sentence in the translator's introduction refers somewhat loosely to "undated haiku" that "bear the stamp" of Celan, and the *Soun* group's "discussion" of Celan is alleged for a postwar moment as well. Reading the translator's note quickly, one might absorb this as referring to later influence. Perloff is right that on careful inspection the chronology does not hold, but careful inspection is not the default mode of magazine editing.

Perloff also faults the editors for not consulting Japanese specialists. This sounds reasonable until one considers what would have to be involved. Every translation that arrives at an American poetry journal would need to be passed through a network of area-studies scholars before publication. The poetry world does not have such a network, has never had such a network, and could not afford one. The implicit demand is that small magazines maintain the equivalent of academic peer review. Were they to do so, they would publish less, more slowly, and the work they did publish would be filtered through scholarly rather than literary judgment—a different and not obviously better kind of magazine.

There is also the question of what the editors actually did once they saw the work. They read it; they found it moving; they decided it deserved an audience. These judgments were not foolish. The poems are good poems—Perloff herself calls Johnson's invention "brilliant," concedes that the Yasusada world is "at once ritualized and yet startlingly modern, timeless yet documentary, archaicized yet au courant," and acknowledges that the work has affected sophisticated readers like Ron Silliman in serious ways. If the poems were worthy of admiration as poems, then the editors were not wrong to admire them. The fact that they were also wrong about the authorship is a separate matter.

This brings me to a point I think Perloff understates. She frames the editors' embrace of Yasusada as if it required the Hiroshima biography to function—as if the work would have been ignored without the survivor's frame. I am not so sure. Some of the Yasusada poems—"Telescope with Urn," "Mad Daughter and Big-Bang"—are striking compositions by any measure, and at least some of the editors involved have made clear that the literary qualities, not the biographical frame, were what attracted them. Rod Smith, on Perloff's own report, suspected fraud all along and published anyway because he found the work "charming and apropos." That is not the response of an editor moved primarily by authenticity. That is the response of an editor moved by craft.

I want, finally, to push back on the broader cultural diagnosis. Perloff argues that the editors' susceptibility to Yasusada reflects a general decline in confidence about individual talent and a corresponding need to locate poetic interest in marginal or victimized positions. There is something to this, but I think it overstates the case. The editors who published Yasusada have also published, between them, an enormous range of work by uncontroversially "central" American poets—John Cage, Jackson Mac Low, the figures Perloff catalogues from the *Aerial* issue itself. They have not abandoned aesthetic judgment in favor of identity politics. They have, at most, broadened the range of positions they are interested in. Yasusada occupies one extreme of that range. He is not its center.

What the affair does show, I grant, is that the broadening has introduced new vulnerabilities. When a literary culture cultivates an appetite for previously unheard voices, it becomes susceptible to fabricators who manufacture such voices. This is a real problem and it deserves serious thought. But the appropriate response is not to retract the broadening. The appropriate response is to develop the critical habits that let us evaluate previously unheard voices with the same care we apply to familiar ones—including, when necessary, the modest verification that would have caught Yasusada.

In the meantime, I would ask Perloff and others not to be too hard on the editors. They did what editors do: they read, they responded, they published. When the fraud emerged, they corrected the record and adjusted their procedures. That is what one can ask of editorial culture. The deeper questions—about Orientalism, about the demand for authenticity, about the strange afterlife of Foucault's anti-authorship—are real and important, but they are not the editors' failings to bear alone. They are everyone's, and addressing them will take more than calling the *APR* office in search of an apology.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The anachronisms cited as obvious clues are only clear in hindsight and require specialized historical knowledge that most poetry editors do not have at their fingertips.
  2. 02Expecting small poetry journals to implement academic peer review for every translation is financially and logistically impossible and would prioritize scholarly judgment over literary merit.
  3. 03The editors' decision to publish was based on the high quality and brilliance of the poems themselves, a literary judgment that remains valid regardless of the author's true identity.
  4. 04The claim that editors were only interested in the poems due to the 'survivor' frame is undermined by the fact that some editors published the work despite suspecting it was a hoax.
  5. 05The susceptibility to the hoax does not indicate an abandonment of aesthetic standards for identity politics, but rather a vulnerability inherent in the positive effort to broaden the range of poetic voices.
  6. 06The responsibility for the broader cultural issues raised by the hoax, such as the demand for authenticity and Orientalism, belongs to the entire literary community rather than the editors alone.
