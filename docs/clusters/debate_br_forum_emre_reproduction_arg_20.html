Diversified MiniMax

"Merve Emre’s universalization of 'assisted reproduction' misdiagnoses the political stakes by obscuring how these technologies are often tools of patriarchal and capitalist interests rather than genuine liberation."

Merve Emre's essay is technically sophisticated but fundamentally misdiagnoses the political stakes of reproductive technology. By universalizing the category of "assisted reproduction," she obscures the specific ways that particular technologies serve particular interests—often patriarchal and capitalist ones.

The essay's case studies illustrate this problem. S's egg freezing at a Silicon Valley company is presented as a win for LGBT employees, but this framing ignores how corporate egg freezing actually serves employer interests. Tech companies offer egg freezing not because they care about women's reproductive autonomy but because it keeps workers productive and delay-focused during their peak childbearing years. The $20,000 lifetime cap doesn't cover IVF, adoption assistance, or on-site childcare—all of which might actually support workers who want families. Egg freezing is the perk that defers the reproductive question indefinitely while extracting maximum labor.

Similarly, B's IVF debt represents a failure of healthcare financing, not a testament to reproductive technology's liberatory potential. The fact that women must borrow money from friends and colleagues to access basic reproductive healthcare reveals a system that prioritizes profit over people. Emre treats these material constraints as obstacles to individual choice rather than structural features of reproductive capitalism.

The essay gestures toward Dorothy Roberts's reproductive justice framework but doesn't engage seriously with its implications. Roberts emphasizes that reproductive justice requires not just access to technology but conditions that make parenting viable: living wages, universal healthcare, safe housing, environmental protections. Emre's focus on the subjective experiences of women navigating assisted reproduction, while valuable, doesn't center these structural demands.

There's also a troubling ableism in the essay's assumptions about whose reproduction counts. The case studies focus exclusively on women with "good" fertility—women whose bodies respond well to hormones, who produce abundant eggs, who have viable embryos. What about women whose bodies don't respond to treatment? Women who can't carry pregnancies? Women with chronic illnesses that make pregnancy dangerous? The essay doesn't address how assisted reproduction intersects with disability, except briefly in the account of N and K's doctors advising against pregnancy.

Emre quotes cárdenas's poem about trans women and reproduction, but this feels like tokenism rather than genuine engagement with disability perspectives. cárdenas's observation that "the cost of IUI, ICSI and IVF are in the tens of thousands, oh the privilege of cis-hetero reproduction" points toward a critique that Emre's philosophical framing can't quite reach: reproductive technology is not liberatory when it's inaccessible.

What would a genuinely progressive reproductive politics look like? It would demand universal access to fertility treatment regardless of income, sexual orientation, gender identity, or disability status. It would oppose the corporate enclosure of reproductive labor. It would center the needs of women of color, disabled women, and poor women whose reproductive lives don't fit the paradigm of individual choice within markets.

Emre's essay raises important questions but doesn't provide adequate answers. The "all reproduction is assisted" framing, while philosophically interesting, can obscure more than it reveals about the political economy of reproductive technology.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Corporate egg-freezing policies serve employer interests by deferring motherhood to maximize worker productivity rather than supporting actual family-building needs like childcare.
  2. 02The financial burdens and debts incurred by individuals seeking IVF highlight a system of reproductive capitalism that prioritizes profit over universal healthcare access.
  3. 03The focus on subjective experiences of choice fails to center the structural demands of reproductive justice, such as living wages and safe housing, which make parenting viable.
  4. 04The essay exhibits ableism by centering case studies of 'successful' biological responses to treatment while failing to address those whose bodies cannot conform to these technological interventions.
  5. 05A truly progressive reproductive politics must move beyond philosophical framing to demand the dismantling of the corporate enclosure of reproductive labor and ensure universal access regardless of socioeconomic status.
