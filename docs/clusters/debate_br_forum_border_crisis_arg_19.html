"While Ypi correctly identifies migration as a symptom of structural class inequality, a successful progressive response requires acknowledging that modern democratic solidarity was historically built on exclusionary national boundaries and must now be rebuilt through concrete institutional reform rather than just discursive shifts."

Lea Ypi's essay offers a compelling diagnosis of how migration became framed as a crisis rather than understood as a symptom of deeper structural failures. Her argument that we must situate migration within the context of class inequality, neoliberal restructuring, and the decline of democratic representation resonates with my own understanding of global political economy. However, I want to push her analysis further by considering an element she only partially addresses: the temporal dynamics of political change and the role of institutional path dependence in perpetuating exclusionary borders.

Ypi correctly identifies the post-Cold War reversal in how Western states approached migration. What strikes me as crucial is understanding why this reversal happened when it did. The answer, I believe, lies not only in the strategic calculations of political elites but also in the institutional legacy of how borders were constructed during the welfare state era. When social democracy expanded citizenship rights in the mid-twentieth century, it did so within clearly bounded territorial communities. The solidarity Ypi celebrates was forged precisely through the distinction between insiders and outsiders—those within the redistributive apparatus and those outside it.

This creates a paradox for contemporary progressive politics. The workers' movements Ypi mentions built their power on the premise that solidarity would be organized nationally. Universal suffrage and welfare state expansion presupposed a bounded demos. When we now argue for expanding solidarity to include migrant workers, we are asking for something historically unprecedented: solidarity without the territorial and legal boundaries that previously defined it.

This does not mean we should abandon the project. Rather, it means we must be realistic about the institutional transformations required. Ypi's call for fighting for expansion of political representation beyond inherited citizenship is correct, but she underestimates how deeply embedded the link between citizenship and political participation has become in democratic systems. Electoral rules, party structures, trade union organization—all are built around the citizen/non-citizen distinction. Changing these requires not just discursive struggle but concrete institutional reform.

I would argue that the most promising entry point is the workplace. Unlike electoral politics, labor relations are less constrained by citizenship categories. Unions organizing across legal status distinctions, domestic and immigrant workers coordinating strike actions, and collective bargaining that ignores immigration status are all achievable goals that can build the kind of cross-border solidarity Ypi envisions. The Amazon workers' campaign she cites provides one model, though we should note how limited this remains in practice.

The harder question concerns what to do in the meantime. Ypi suggests we should refuse to play catch with exclusionary discourse even at the cost of electoral losses. This may be correct as a matter of principle, but it requires acknowledging that the political landscape will become considerably darker before it brightens. If social democratic parties abandon the immigration question to the right, the immediate consequences for migrants—including detention, deportation, and family separation—will be severe. The essays she cites from Labour representatives embracing anti-migration rhetoric represent not strategic wisdom but moral capitulation, yet the human cost of that capitulation will fall on the most vulnerable.

What is needed is a dual strategy: maintaining moral clarity about the structural causes of migration while building practical solidarity organizations that can make a difference in people's lives now. This means supporting sanctuary city movements, defending undocumented workers' rights, and building labor solidarity that transcends status categories. It also means being willing to take short-term political losses for long-term structural change, as Ypi suggests.

The Enlightenment values Ypi invokes were always more aspirational than actual. The liberal democratic state was never the haven of freedom and equality she describes, but neither was it simply a tool of class domination. Understanding these tensions, and working within them without succumbing to either naive optimism or cynical despair, may be the most honest position available to us.
