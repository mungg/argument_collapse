"Parents should support high school seniors by providing honest financial clarity and realistic expectations rather than pressuring them to navigate a modern economy that is far more volatile and expensive than the one their parents entered."

Parents owe seniors an apology. We often talk to them as if they are entering the same world we entered, only with better gadgets. They are not. Many of us went to college when tuition was lower, housing was cheaper and a summer job could make a noticeable dent in expenses. We found work in an economy that seemed to have more room for ordinary beginnings. Then we turn around and tell our children to “follow their dreams” while handing them loan forms.

No wonder they are anxious. They hear that they must be passionate, practical, well-rounded, specialized, debt-averse, prestigious, employable and happy—all before they are old enough to rent a car.

The best gift parents can give seniors is not panic disguised as advice. It is clarity. We should talk honestly about what we can afford. We should compare colleges by value, not bumper-sticker status. We should respect community college, public universities, apprenticeships and work. And we should remind our children that the first plan is rarely the final plan.

The future looks frightening partly because adults keep pretending certainty is possible. It is not. But steadiness is.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic landscape has shifted significantly, making college tuition and housing much less affordable than they were for previous generations.
  2. 02Students are overwhelmed by contradictory societal pressures to be simultaneously passionate, practical, and debt-free in a competitive environment.
  3. 03Parents should prioritize discussing college value and affordability over the prestige of specific institutions.
  4. 04Alternative paths such as community college, public universities, and apprenticeships should be treated with the same respect as traditional four-year degrees.
  5. 05Adults need to stop projecting a false sense of certainty and instead teach children that career plans are rarely final and require steadiness.
