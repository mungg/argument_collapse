Diversified Gemini

"Aziz Rana’s proposal to dismantle American global primacy in favor of an anti-imperialist domestic solidarity is a dangerous utopianism that would destabilize the international order and empower authoritarian regimes."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" offers a sweeping, historically grounded critique of how American conceptions of liberty have been intertwined with imperial expansion and racial exclusion. His argument that domestic freedom is inextricably linked to foreign policy is a crucial insight often ignored by both mainstream Democrats and Republicans. However, as a framework for understanding and guiding U.S. foreign policy in the 21st century, Rana’s analysis falls into the trap of a Utopian, anti-imperialist idealism that fundamentally misapprehends the nature of global geopolitics. By advocating for a radical dismantling of American military power and global primacy in the name of domestic "solidarity," Rana prescribes a course of action that would destabilize the international order, empower authoritarian adversaries, and ultimately endanger the very freedoms—both domestic and global—he seeks to protect.

Rana’s central foreign policy thesis is that the United States must abandon its quest for global hegemony and drastically reduce its defense spending (which he characterizes as "bankrolling corporate militarism") in order to foster an "inclusive and solidaristic version of freedom at home." He views the projection of American military authority not as a stabilizing force, but as an engine of exploitation and violence that violates the "meaningful self-rule" of postcolonial nations. To Rana, the solution is a retreat from primacy and an embrace of a multipolar, solidaristic global order.

This perspective is rooted in a fundamental misreading of the international system. The global arena is not a community of inherently peaceful, solidaristic nations yearning to cooperate if only the American hegemon would get out of the way. It is an anarchic environment defined by competition, where power vacuums are inevitably filled by the most capable actors. Rana’s vision of "worldmaking after empire" assumes that a U.S. retreat would usher in an era of equitable, multipolar cooperation akin to the unfulfilled dreams of the New International Economic Order.

The grim reality of 2023 tells a vastly different story. We do not have to guess what a multipolar world looks like; it is actively taking shape, and it is defined by the predatory aggression of revisionist autocracies. If the United States were to heed Rana’s advice and unilaterally dismantle its global military footprint, the immediate beneficiaries would not be the global poor or the working class. The beneficiaries would be Xi Jinping’s China and Vladimir Putin’s Russia.

China is actively seeking to rewrite the rules of the international system to accommodate its authoritarian model, utilizing economic coercion (such as the Belt and Road Initiative) and aggressive military expansion in the South China Sea to subjugate its neighbors. Russia has already demonstrated its vision for a post-American order with its brutal, unprovoked invasion of Ukraine—a blatant attempt at imperial conquest and the erasure of a sovereign democratic state. Iran continues to fund proxy militias that destabilize the Middle East.

In this context, Rana’s critique of U.S. military spending as a mere "misallocation of the public treasure" is dangerously naive. While there is undoubtedly waste and corporate profiteering within the military-industrial complex that requires rigorous oversight, the core function of the U.S. defense budget is deterrence. American military primacy underwrites the security architecture of Europe, the Indo-Pacific, and vital global trade routes. It is the hard power that guarantees the existence of the liberal international order—an order that, despite its many flaws and historical hypocrisies, has facilitated unprecedented global economic growth and prevented great power conflict for nearly 80 years.

Rana invokes the post-colonial struggles of the 20th century and contemporary tragedies, like the conflict in Gaza, to argue that U.S. power is inherently oppressive. There is no denying that American foreign policy has suffered from hubris, catastrophic miscalculations (such as the invasion of Iraq), and morally compromising alliances. But foreign policy requires making difficult choices in a world of imperfect options. The alternative to American primacy is not a global utopia of collective self-rule; it is a sphere-of-influence world where strong nations devour the weak, and where the democratic values Rana cherishes are systematically dismantled by regimes that view "freedom" not as a flawed concept to be reformed, but as an existential threat to be eradicated.

Furthermore, Rana’s argument that global U.S. power structurally prevents domestic solidarity is historically tenuous. He argues that imperial rents created a middle class focused on individualistic, market-driven prosperity rather than collective well-being. But the post-World War II era, which saw the apex of American global dominance, also coincided with the greatest expansion of the domestic middle class, the highest rates of unionization, and the massive social investments of the Great Society. Strong global standing and robust domestic social democracy are not mutually exclusive; indeed, the former often provides the economic security and material abundance necessary to sustain the latter.

Rana is entirely correct that foreign policy shapes domestic culture. The disastrous "Global War on Terror" undeniably militarized American policing and fueled a paranoid, xenophobic nativism at home. But the solution to the excesses of the post-9/11 era is a disciplined, grand strategy of realism and restraint, not a total abdication of global leadership. The United States must prioritize its strategic interests, pivot its focus to managing the long-term competition with China, and rely more heavily on capable allies.

A truly progressive foreign policy must balance the desire for global justice with the cold realities of power politics. It must recognize that while American power has sometimes been used poorly, its absence would be catastrophic. Retreating into a defensive crouch in the hopes of fostering "domestic solidarity" is a luxury the United States—and the free world—cannot afford. To secure a world where true freedom and self-determination are even possible, the United States must remain the indispensable anchor of global security, actively checking the authoritarian powers that seek to plunge the 21st century into darkness.
