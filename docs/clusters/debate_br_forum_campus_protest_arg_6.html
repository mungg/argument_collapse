"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly warns against the neoliberal domestication of trauma, his critique risks creating a false hierarchy that devalues the essential role of care, vulnerability, and mental wellness in radical political struggle."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s warning about the language of trauma is necessary, but it risks reproducing a hierarchy that black feminist, queer, and disability politics have long challenged: the hierarchy between “real” structural struggle and supposedly softer questions of feeling, care, vulnerability, and repair. The problem is not that students talk too much about trauma. The problem is that our institutions know how to individualize trauma better than our movements know how to politicize care.

Kelley is right that oppression cannot be reduced to interior pain. Racism is not a misunderstanding, a bad feeling, or a failure of cultural competence. It is organized through housing, policing, debt, labor markets, schools, prisons, borders, and war. Universities can eagerly absorb the language of harm while leaving those structures intact. They can provide mindfulness workshops to students whose tuition bills produce anxiety. They can tell black students to seek counseling while refusing to disarm campus police. They can offer trigger warnings while ignoring sexual assault. This is not liberation.

Yet trauma has been central to radical politics, not opposed to it. The feminist claim that “the personal is political” did not mean every feeling was automatically revolutionary. It meant that what appeared private—rape, domestic labor, reproductive control, shame, depression, fear—was structured by power. Black feminist thought has been especially clear about this. The Combahee River Collective connected intimate life to capitalism, racism, heterosexism, and imperialism. Audre Lorde wrote about the uses of anger, the erotic, illness, survival, and care without reducing politics to self-help. To speak of wounds can be a way of mapping power.

Disability justice also offers a corrective. Movements often imagine the ideal activist as tireless, fearless, physically able, emotionally invulnerable, and always available. That fantasy serves capitalism as much as revolution. Students dealing with depression, anxiety, chronic illness, racial stress, gender violence, or family trauma are sometimes told, implicitly or explicitly, that their needs are distractions from the “real” work. But a movement that burns through people in the name of liberation is practicing a politics of disposability. Care is not the opposite of militancy. It is what makes sustained militancy possible.

This matters on campus because universities are pressure machines. They produce competition, isolation, debt, impostor syndrome, professional fear, and constant evaluation. Black students and other marginalized students encounter these pressures alongside racism, sexism, homophobia, transphobia, ableism, and class contempt. To ask for mental health services is not necessarily to ask the university for love. It may be to demand resources already paid for by tuition and labor. It may be to insist that black life includes the right not only to resist death but to seek wellness.

Kelley worries that words such as trauma, PTSD, microaggression, and triggers have replaced oppression, repression, and subjugation. Sometimes they have. But the vocabulary of microaggression, for example, emerged to describe how domination is reproduced through repeated, often deniable encounters. A single slight may be small; the pattern is not. Likewise, a trigger warning can be misused as a shield against difficulty, but it can also be a simple access practice: a way to let students prepare themselves to encounter material about rape, lynching, war, or suicide. Preparation is not censorship. Access is not fragility.

The deeper issue is whether care remains individualized or becomes collective. A neoliberal university prefers individual accommodation: a counseling appointment, an extension, a private note from disability services, a bias report processed through official channels. Radical care asks different questions. Why are so many students in crisis? Why are black students isolated? Why are survivors forced to navigate hostile procedures? Why are trans students made administratively illegible? Why are disabled students treated as exceptions to a norm rather than participants in designing the institution? Why is productivity valued over life?

The answer cannot be only more services, though services matter. It must include changing workloads, policing, housing, financial aid, labor conditions, classroom practices, and governance. It must include peer networks that do not depend on administrative permission. It must include political education about the body—not the body as a passive object of violence, but the body as a site of knowledge, pleasure, exhaustion, resistance, and interdependence.

Kelley criticizes Ta-Nehisi Coates for emphasizing the vulnerability of the black body. I understand the criticism, but I also understand why Coates resonates. Many young black people are tired of being told that their terror must be quickly converted into uplift, faith, patriotism, or strategy. Coates gives language to fear without immediately redeeming it. That is not enough, but it is not nothing. The task is not to stop at the vulnerable body; it is to ask what forms of collective life protect, nourish, and free bodies.

“Resistance is our healing,” Kelley writes. Yes—but healing is also part of resistance. The enslaved did not only revolt; they also sang, worshiped, hid, healed, birthed, buried, taught, touched, and rested when they could. Freedom dreams require more than critique. They require practices of sustaining life before victory arrives.

Campus movements should therefore refuse the administrative domestication of trauma without abandoning the language of care. They should demand counseling and debt relief, safe spaces and worker justice, trigger warnings where useful and fearless engagement with difficult texts, survivor support and abolitionist critique. They should build study groups where people can bring their whole selves, not as confessional consumers but as comrades.

Love, study, struggle—and care. Without care, study becomes another performance of mastery. Without study, care can become therapy without politics. Without struggle, both are easily commodified. The challenge is to hold them together.

  4. 04Care and healing are not distractions from militancy but are the necessary practices that make sustained resistance and collective life possible.
