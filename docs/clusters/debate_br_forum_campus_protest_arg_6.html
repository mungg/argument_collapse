Diversified Claude

"While supporting the spirit of contemporary student activism, the essay argues that universities are inherently limited institutions that cannot serve as the primary site or engine for radical social transformation."

Kelley's essay is the kind of intervention that makes everyone uncomfortable, which is what political education is supposed to do. I want to focus on what I take to be its single most important argument: that the contemporary student movement risks confusing the university for the world.

This confusion takes several forms in Kelley's account. The reformists want the university to deliver on its self-advertised promise of post-racial inclusion. The radicals want the university to serve as the leading edge of socialist transformation. Both, Kelley argues, expect from the university what the university cannot give. Both treat the university as if it were a kind of microcosm of society in which struggles waged at the campus level could substitute for, or generate, social transformation more broadly.

I think Kelley is exactly right about this, and I think it explains a great deal about the strange affective tone of contemporary campus activism—the combination of utopian demand and bureaucratic specificity that produces, say, a manifesto calling simultaneously for the abolition of capitalism and for a particular dean's resignation. The genres do not quite fit together. The grandness of the analysis and the smallness of the addressee create a kind of category confusion. Why should the dean of student affairs be the entity tasked with answering for five centuries of racial capitalism?

Part of the answer is that the dean is who is available. The student is a captive of the institution in which she is enrolled; her political agency is in many ways most exercisable where she has structural leverage, which is to say on campus. This is understandable. But Kelley is right that it produces a strange and ultimately self-limiting politics. The university becomes the stage on which a much larger drama is enacted, and victories on that stage—the resignation of a chancellor, the renaming of a building—become metonyms for victories that have not actually occurred in the larger drama. The danger is not just that these victories are insufficient. The danger is that they create the illusion of progress that demobilizes the broader struggle.

This is where Kelley's invocation of the Mississippi Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon women's collective is so apt. These were political education projects that took place in communities, not in institutions. They were not asking the school district to deliver anti-racist curricula; they were creating the curricula themselves, in church basements and living rooms. The model presumes that the relevant institutions—schools, governments, corporations—are adversaries to be confronted rather than parents to be appealed to. The work of building political consciousness happens elsewhere.

A skeptical reader might object that Kelley is romanticizing. The Freedom Schools were sustained by a mass movement that took specific institutional aims—voting rights, desegregation—dead seriously. SNCC was not above making demands of the federal government. The opposition between fugitive study and institutional engagement is not as clean as Kelley sometimes suggests. He knows this, of course; his own career has been built within universities, and he speaks with affection of his graduate students. But the rhetorical structure of the essay can make it sound as though institutional engagement is always already corrupted, while autonomous study is always already pure. Neither is true.

What I take from Kelley, then, is not the strong claim that universities are useless to radical politics, but the more modest claim that they cannot be the primary site of radical politics. They can be sources of resources, networks, time, and—crucially—books. They can shelter the political study group, as the philosophy department at Atlanta University once sheltered the Institute of the Black World. They cannot, and never will, generate from within themselves the radical reconstruction of American society that the most ambitious student demands envision. To expect that of them is a kind of category mistake.

The corollary, which I wish Kelley had pressed harder, is that the work of off-campus political education is in some serious trouble. The black radical study groups he describes from his youth in the 1980s have largely thinned out. Where do today's students go to read Cabral and Luxemburg together? The answer is increasingly: nowhere, except online, where the affordances of the medium discourage the slow, communal reading practice that political study requires. Rebuilding that infrastructure—of study groups, freedom schools, community-based political education—seems to me the urgent task. The campus protests cannot do that work, but they might catalyze it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Student activists often commit a 'category mistake' by directing grand revolutionary demands toward low-level university bureaucrats who lack the power to address systemic racial capitalism.
  2. 02Campus victories, such as the renaming of buildings or the resignation of administrators, risk creating a false sense of progress that demobilizes broader political struggles.
  3. 03The university should be viewed as a source of tactical resources—such as books, networks, and space—rather than a 'parental' institution to be appealed to for moral or social validation.
  4. 04True political education must be rebuilt in community-based spaces outside of formal institutions, similar to the historical models of the Mississippi Freedom Schools.
  5. 05The current reliance on digital platforms for political engagement undermines the slow, communal reading and study practices necessary for deep political consciousness.
