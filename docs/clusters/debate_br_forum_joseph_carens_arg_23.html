"While compassion is necessary in immigration policy, granting automatic legal status based on the passage of time undermines the rule of law and the democratic right of a community to determine its own membership."

Joseph Carens offers a humane argument, but it is ultimately an argument for dissolving the practical meaning of immigration law. He says he accepts the state’s right to control borders, yet his proposal would make that right conditional on the state’s ability to find and remove unauthorized migrants within a relatively short period. If the state fails, the violation ripens into a claim. That may sound moderate, but it changes the incentives and the moral structure of immigration policy in ways he underestimates.

The central problem is not that Carens cares about families or children. Everyone should. Deportation is often painful. The problem is that he treats the pain of enforcement as if it were a reason to invalidate the rule being enforced. Many laws become harder to enforce with time. Tax evasion, zoning violations, unlawful construction, benefit fraud, and nonpayment of child support can all become entangled with ordinary life. Yet we do not conclude that time alone converts violations into entitlements. We ask what maintaining the rule requires.

Immigration law is not like a speed limit. Carens’s analogy minimizes the stakes. The right to decide who may become a member of the political community is one of the defining powers of democratic self-government. Immigration affects labor markets, schools, hospitals, public budgets, language, civic identity, and the future electorate. Reasonable people can favor generous immigration while still believing that admission should be lawful, public, and democratically authorized. A country that cannot enforce its membership rules is not merely tolerating “deviance”; it is losing control over a core political function.

Carens replies that many unauthorized migrants had no legal line available to them. That is often true. But it is not an argument for allowing self-selection to replace law. Most of the world’s poor have no realistic path to immigrate to the United States. The United States cannot admit everyone who would improve his or her life by moving here. Scarcity is exactly why rules are needed. Unauthorized migrants are not simply jumping ahead of people in a neat queue; they are claiming a benefit that many others, equally needy and equally deserving, never receive because they lack the resources, geography, family connections, or willingness to violate the law.

This unfairness is not abstract. A Mexican worker who crosses the border unlawfully may eventually receive legal status under Carens’s rule. A Filipino, Ghanaian, or Guatemalan who obeys the law and remains abroad receives nothing. A family that waits years for a visa sees others rewarded for evasion. A refugee stuck in a camp may be told that America has limited capacity, while someone who succeeded in entering without authorization gains membership through presence. Carens’s “social membership” principle privileges those who are able to get here.

Nor is deterrence a minor concern. Carens says migrants may not calculate the possibility of legalization five or seven years later. Perhaps not in a precise way. But migration networks transmit information. If the rule is known to be that remaining undetected for a fixed period leads to legal status, that fact will shape expectations. Smugglers will advertise it. Employers will rely on it. Families will make decisions around it. The 1986 amnesty was accompanied by promises of employer sanctions and future control. Yet the unauthorized population grew dramatically afterward. To say the lesson is simply that enforcement was insufficient is true but incomplete. Amnesty itself became part of the political memory of migration.

Carens’s proposal also risks weakening consent. He argues that people become members by living and working among us. But democratic membership is not only sociological; it is institutional. A person may be socially connected to a community without having a right to political membership in it. Foreign students, temporary workers, diplomats, tourists, and business visitors may form attachments. Some stay for years. Legal categories matter because they define the terms on which presence is permitted. If those terms can be rewritten unilaterally by the migrant’s decision to remain, the receiving society’s consent becomes secondary.

That does not mean the United States should deport all eleven million unauthorized migrants. Mass deportation would be impractical, costly, and cruel. But rejecting mass deportation does not require accepting Carens’s automatic amnesty. A better approach would combine stronger enforcement with narrower forms of relief. People brought as young children deserve special treatment because they did not choose the violation and have often known no other country. Spouses of citizens may deserve case-by-case waivers, especially where the citizen spouse and children would suffer extreme hardship. Longtime residents with clean records might receive renewable legal status short of citizenship. But automatic permanent residence after five years goes too far.

The distinction between legal residence and citizenship is important. Carens assumes that once social membership is recognized, eventual citizenship should follow. Yet citizenship includes the right to vote and shape the laws, including immigration laws. Granting citizenship to those who entered or remained unlawfully is not simply mercy; it changes the political community. A democracy may reasonably decide that humanitarian relief should not always entail full membership. Legalization without automatic citizenship would reduce fear and exploitation while preserving some distinction between lawful and unlawful paths.

There is also the issue of enforcement credibility. If Congress enacts another broad legalization without first restoring public confidence in the border and workplace rules, the political backlash will be severe. The failure of immigration reform in 2006 and 2007 showed that voters do not trust promises of “enforcement later.” Many Americans are not anti-immigrant; they are anti-chaos. They want assurance that the law means something. Carens’s essay does not take that concern seriously enough.

A defensible compromise would begin with enforcement that is lawful and humane: secure documents, serious penalties for employers who knowingly hire unauthorized workers, protection against racial profiling, and expedited removal for recent entrants. Then Congress could create relief for the most sympathetic groups: DREAM Act students, military service members, spouses and parents in mixed-status families, and long-term residents who can show extraordinary equities. Such relief should be earned, limited, and transparent.

Carens is right that time matters. It should matter in discretion, in prioritization, and in humanitarian waivers. But time should not automatically erase the original violation. Liberal democracy requires compassion, but it also requires the people’s ability to set rules for membership. Amnesty may sometimes be necessary as a practical settlement. It should not be elevated into a standing moral right.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The passage of time does not convert a legal violation into an entitlement, as the state must maintain the integrity of its rules to ensure they remain meaningful.
  2. 02Granting amnesty to those who successfully evade the law is unfair to millions of equally deserving people who remain abroad because they choose to respect immigration regulations.
  3. 03Establishing a fixed period after which status is regularized creates a perverse incentive that encourages more unauthorized migration and benefits smuggling networks.
  4. 04Democratic membership is an institutional status based on mutual consent, not merely a sociological reality created by a migrant's unilateral decision to stay.
  5. 05Granting full citizenship to those who bypassed legal channels fundamentally alters the political community without the explicit authorization of the existing electorate.
  6. 06Public trust in the immigration system requires that enforcement and border security be prioritized over broad, automatic legalization programs.
  7. 07A more balanced approach would offer narrow, case-by-case humanitarian relief for specific groups, such as those brought as children, rather than a universal right to amnesty.
