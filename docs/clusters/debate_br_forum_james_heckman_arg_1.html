Vanilla GPT

"While early childhood intervention is a vital tool for social mobility, it should be viewed as a complement to, rather than a substitute for, broader economic redistribution and institutional reform."

James Heckman is right about the central fact: opportunity begins long before kindergarten. By the time children enter school, class differences in vocabulary, health, self-regulation, and confidence are already large. A society that waits until adolescence to repair those gaps is not practicing equality of opportunity; it is economizing on children and then blaming schools, courts, and job-training programs for the consequences. The evidence from Perry, Abecedarian, and nurse home visiting is powerful precisely because it shows that early disadvantage is not destiny.

But the case for early intervention becomes less persuasive when it is framed as an alternative to redistribution rather than as a complement to it. Heckman’s “predistribution, not redistribution” formulation risks drawing too sharp a line between the quality of parenting and the material conditions under which parenting occurs. Love is not money, but neither is love independent of money. Parents who work irregular hours, face eviction, lack health insurance, live in dangerous neighborhoods, or suffer untreated depression are not simply deficient suppliers of “nurturing.” They are raising children under conditions that make consistent nurturing harder.

Indeed, some of the evidence Heckman cites points in this direction. The casino-income study did not merely teach parents better values; it increased family income, and family relationships improved. The mechanism may have been parenting, but the intervention was money. Similarly, expansions of the Earned Income Tax Credit, food stamps, Medicaid, and housing assistance are not just static transfers. They can reduce stress, improve nutrition and health, increase residential stability, and make it more possible for parents to read, talk, supervise, and plan. To say that “giving families more money is not the same as enhancing children’s environments” is true. But it does not follow that money is unimportant to those environments.

The same point applies to family structure. Children in stable two-parent families often do better, but policy cannot simply wish such families into existence. Low wages, mass incarceration, unstable employment, poor health, and weak prospects for less-educated men all shape marriage markets and family life. If we want more stable families, we need not only parenting programs but also jobs that pay enough to support a household, less destructive criminal justice policies, and health care that reaches parents as well as children. A home-visiting nurse can do a great deal; she cannot by herself reverse the collapse of work for less-skilled men.

There is also a question of scale. Perry and Abecedarian were intensive, carefully staffed, and small. Their successes should inspire policy, but they cannot simply be multiplied by congressional appropriation. Head Start’s more mixed record is a warning that quality matters enormously. If early childhood programs are underfunded, staffed by poorly paid workers, or reduced to test-prep for four-year-olds, they will not reproduce the results of the model programs. The lesson is not that we should abandon large-scale early education, but that we should be honest about what it requires: well-trained teachers, low child-adult ratios, attention to health and family support, and stable financing.

Nor should enthusiasm for the early years lead us to understate the importance of schools. Heckman is correct that many gaps appear before first grade, but schools can still widen or narrow them. A child who enters kindergarten behind is not helped by attending a chaotic elementary school, a segregated middle school, and a high school with inexperienced teachers and few advanced courses. Early investment “begets” later learning only if later institutions are capable of receiving and reinforcing it. The right conclusion is not early childhood instead of school reform, but a developmental strategy that links prenatal care, home visiting, preschool, elementary instruction, after-school supports, and pathways into work.

The focus on non-cognitive skills is salutary, but it too requires care. Perseverance, self-control, and motivation are real contributors to success. Yet when public policy becomes fascinated with “character,” it can drift toward asking poor children to adapt to unjust conditions rather than changing those conditions. A teenager may need grit, but he also needs a safe school, a summer job, a reliable bus route, and some reason to believe effort will be rewarded. Non-cognitive skills should not become a polite way of relocating responsibility from institutions to children.

The strongest version of Heckman’s argument is not that early childhood policy can substitute for redistribution, school improvement, or labor-market reform. It is that any serious egalitarian politics must begin earlier than our politics usually begins. That is a profound correction. But social mobility is produced by an ecology: family resources, parental time, neighborhood safety, health, schools, wages, and public institutions. Early childhood programs are among the best investments we can make. They will work best when embedded in a broader commitment to reduce poverty and strengthen the worlds in which parents are trying to raise their children.

The Scaling Problem Heckman Won't Quite Face

James Heckman's essay is a tour of impressive findings tied together with a confident policy moral. I want to dwell on a question the essay raises but never really answers: can the results of the Perry Preschool Project and the Abecedarian Project be reproduced at scale? Because if they cannot, the entire policy structure built on those two studies is wobbling on a very narrow base.

Consider the sample sizes. Perry randomized 58 children. Abecedarian randomized 111. These are not typos; they are the actual populations from which we are extracting eye-popping internal rates of return and on which the case for a major redirection of federal social policy is being built. Both studies were run by committed researchers in specific communities — Ypsilanti, Michigan and Chapel Hill, North Carolina — using carefully recruited staff and curricula designed by their inventors. The Nurse-Family Partnership, similarly, achieved its strongest results in trials supervised by its founder, David Olds.

What happens when programs leave the hothouse? Head Start is the natural test. It has been running at scale since 1965 and serves nearly a million children annually. The 2010 Head Start Impact Study — a large, randomized national evaluation released by HHS during the Obama administration — found modest initial gains in cognitive skills that largely faded by the end of first grade. There were some persisting effects on parenting practices and on certain socio-emotional measures, but the eye-popping ROI numbers Heckman cites from Perry simply do not show up in the program we actually run. Tennessee's state pre-K, evaluated by Vanderbilt researchers, shows a similar pattern of early gains followed by convergence.

Heckman knows this. His standard response is that Head Start is underfunded, uneven in quality, and not as intensive as Perry or Abecedarian. That may be true. But it is exactly the point. The interventions that produced the famous returns were exceptional in their intensity, staff quality, and duration. Perry involved daily classroom time plus weekly 90-minute home visits by certified teachers. Abecedarian ran year-round, full-day, from infancy through age eight, with a 3:1 infant-teacher ratio. There is no reason to believe that the typical American child care worker, paid little more than minimum wage and turning over at high rates, can deliver what the Perry teachers delivered. Heckman's evidence is, in effect, that a Michelin-starred meal is nutritious and delicious. He has not shown that we can serve it in a school cafeteria.

There is a further problem with the cited rates of return. Most have been calculated by Heckman and his collaborators themselves, using particular assumptions about discount rates, the monetization of crime reduction, and projections of lifetime earnings effects from small early differences. None of these moves are illegitimate, but they are sensitive to assumptions, and as the original samples age, the "treatment" and "control" arms involve dozens of people each, not thousands. A handful of unusually successful or unsuccessful individuals can shift the headline numbers materially. We should be reading these effect estimates with wider confidence intervals than the essay implies.

I am not arguing against early childhood investment. I am arguing for honesty about what we know. Here is what I think the evidence actually supports: (1) severe early deprivation has lasting negative effects, as the Romanian orphanage data devastatingly show; (2) high-intensity, high-quality programs delivered to small numbers of severely disadvantaged children can produce meaningful gains; (3) less intensive programs delivered at scale produce smaller and more uncertain effects, particularly on cognitive measures.

This more modest summary has different policy implications than Heckman's. It suggests we should be cautious about promising voters or legislators large macroeconomic returns from universal pre-K. It suggests we should target the most intensive interventions on the most disadvantaged families, as Heckman himself recommends, rather than diffusing resources thinly across the entire under-five population. It suggests we should be investing in rigorous evaluation of new programs at scale, not assuming Perry's results will replicate. And it suggests humility about how much social mobility any preschool program, however good, can deliver against headwinds like deindustrialization, residential segregation, mass incarceration, and a stagnant low-wage labor market.

There is a final irony. Heckman criticizes the official high school graduation statistics for laundering bad outcomes through the GED. He is right to. But the analytic standards he applies to NCES data should apply to small-sample randomized trials too. We should be asking whether long-run Perry effects are robust to coding choices, attrition, and the inclusion of a handful of outliers. Some of the technical literature suggests they are not as stable as the headline numbers imply.

Heckman has reoriented an entire field toward early childhood. That is a real contribution. But the case for redirecting billions of dollars of federal spending needs more than two beautifully run demonstrations from half a century ago. It needs evidence that we can actually do this at scale — and that evidence, on the current record, is much thinner than his essay suggests.

Vanilla Claude

"While Heckman’s case for early childhood investment is conceptually persuasive, his proposal overreaches by underestimating the difficulties of scaling boutique programs, creating a false dichotomy between parenting quality and material resources, and downplaying the political and cultural tensions inherent in family interventions."

Heckman's case for early childhood investment is powerful and, in its broad outlines, persuasive. The evidence from Perry, Abecedarian, and the Nurse-Family Partnership is genuinely impressive, and his insistence on non-cognitive skills corrects a real distortion in current education policy, which—under No Child Left Behind—has fetishized measurable test scores at the expense of the persistence, self-control, and sociability that we know matter for adult life. But I want to press on three points where I think the essay overreaches or papers over genuine difficulties.

First, the scaling problem. Perry enrolled 58 children; Abecedarian, 111. Both were boutique programs run by deeply committed researchers in the 1960s and 70s, with intensive home visits and unusually low child-to-teacher ratios. The leap from these demonstrations to the claim that "policy can matter" at a national scale is enormous. The closest thing we have to a scaled-up federal early childhood program is Head Start, and the picture there is sobering. The 2010 Head Start Impact Study, a randomized evaluation of roughly 5,000 children, found that initial cognitive and socio-emotional gains had largely faded by the end of first grade. Heckman cites Perry's IQ fadeout and reassures us that non-cognitive benefits persist, but the Head Start results raise the question of whether even those subtler effects survive when programs are run by ordinary administrators serving ordinary populations. A serious case for predistribution has to grapple with implementation, not just demonstration.

Second, the relationship between money and parenting is more entangled than Heckman allows. He repeatedly insists that "the scarce resource is love and parenting—not money," and he uses the Eastern Cherokee casino study to argue that income matters only through its effect on parenting behavior. But that study cuts both ways: parenting did improve when families received cash, which suggests that material resources and parenting quality are not cleanly separable. Greg Duncan and others have shown that exogenous income changes affect child outcomes in ways that look causal. To set "predistribution" against "redistribution" as though they were rivals is a false choice. Stressed, sleep-deprived, food-insecure parents working two jobs are not failing at love; they are failing at time. The EITC and the Child Tax Credit are arguably among the more effective "predistributional" tools we have, precisely because they relax the constraints under which parenting happens.

Third, Heckman's invocation of cultural sensitivity and the "sanctity of early family life" deserves more weight than he gives it. His own framing—that the "real determinants" of disadvantage are parenting quality, measured by things like words-per-hour spoken to a toddler—edges close to pathologizing poor and minority families. The Hart and Risley study, with its 42 families, has become a workhorse for this line of argument, but it is a slim empirical reed for a sweeping claim about what constitutes adequate parenting across class and culture. When Heckman writes that some program-induced changes "may run counter to the values of certain parents," he is acknowledging, almost in passing, a deep political problem: programs targeted at the parenting of the poor will be experienced, and resisted, as intrusions. The history of such interventions—from mid-century child welfare practices to contemporary disputes over home visiting—shows how quickly "helping kids" curdles into surveillance.

None of this undermines Heckman's central insight: early environments matter enormously, and the social returns to investing in them are likely high. But the policy implication is not "predistribution instead of redistribution." It is both, plus humility about what large-scale public programs can replicate from small, well-run experiments, plus seriousness about the political legitimacy of intervening in family life. Heckman has identified the target. He has not yet shown us a credible weapon.

"While early childhood intervention is essential, focusing primarily on parenting quality risks pathologizing the poor and ignores the structural inequalities—such as economic instability, lack of labor protections, and mass incarceration—that constrain a family's ability to provide a nurturing environment."

James Heckman’s essay is humane in purpose and impressive in evidence, but it also illustrates a danger that has long accompanied American debates about poverty: the tendency to translate structural inequality into the language of deficient family life. The essay begins with a compelling claim—birth is becoming fate—and ends with a policy agenda focused on improving parenting. That move is not wrong, exactly. Parenting matters. But it is incomplete and politically fraught.

Poor children are not disadvantaged only because they receive fewer words, less enrichment, or weaker socio-emotional training. They are disadvantaged because their parents are more likely to have low wages, unstable schedules, unsafe housing, untreated illness, exposure to violence, inferior transportation, and less bargaining power with employers and landlords. Their neighborhoods are more likely to contain environmental hazards and under-resourced schools. Their fathers, brothers, and cousins are more likely to be entangled with a criminal justice system that has expanded dramatically over the past generation. Their mothers are more likely to be judged by public agencies that combine help with suspicion.

When Heckman writes that the “proper measure” of child adversity is the quality of parenting, he risks shrinking this world of constraint into the intimate space of the home. The quality of parenting is not a free-standing input. It is shaped by time, money, health, safety, and respect. A parent working unpredictable hours at a low-wage job may not fail to read because she undervalues language. She may fail to read because she is exhausted. A parent who appears irritable may be depressed, threatened with eviction, or coping with domestic violence. A mother who misses a school meeting may not lack motivation; she may lack paid leave or a reliable bus.

The gender politics here matter. Early childhood policy often speaks of “parents,” but in practice it means mothers. Mothers are expected to be wage earners and intensive caregivers; poor mothers are expected to do both while under surveillance. More educated women, Heckman notes, spend more time in enrichment activities even though they work more. But this is partly because they can purchase substitutes for other labor: child care, prepared activities, safer neighborhoods, flexible work, and often a spouse with income. The ideal of intensive parenting is easier to meet when one has resources.

There is also a racial history that should make us cautious. American social policy has repeatedly pathologized black family life, from the Moynihan Report debates to welfare stereotypes of the “underclass” and the “welfare queen.” Heckman does not indulge those caricatures. His tone is careful, and he acknowledges diversity and the need for respect. Yet the policy implications can still be absorbed into an old narrative: poor communities, especially black communities, suffer because their family cultures are defective. Once that narrative takes hold, the public becomes more willing to fund behavior modification than economic justice.

The language of “non-cognitive skills” has a similar ambiguity. Perseverance, self-control, and motivation are valuable. But who decides what counts as appropriate self-regulation? A child who resists arbitrary authority may be labeled oppositional in school and courageous in a social movement. A teenager who distrusts institutions may be responding rationally to institutions that have repeatedly failed him. “Character education” can become a way of training poor children to endure conditions that affluent adults would never accept.

This does not mean we should reject early childhood intervention. On the contrary, we should expand it. But we should design it as support, not correction. High-quality child care, preschool, home visiting, and parental coaching can be liberating when they give families resources and relationships. They become troubling when they imply that experts must rescue children from their parents. The difference is not semantic. It affects staffing, governance, consent, and accountability.

A better early childhood agenda would begin with universality. Programs narrowly targeted to the poor often stigmatize participants and invite intrusive eligibility rules. Universal child care and preschool, funded progressively, would recognize that all families need support and that caregiving is a public good. Within universal systems, additional resources can flow to children with greater needs. This is how we avoid creating one program for the poor and another for everyone else.

We should also include paid family leave and predictable work schedules as early childhood policies. The first months of life matter, but the United States remains strikingly stingy in supporting parents during that period. If attachment, breastfeeding, maternal mental health, and early interaction are important, then enabling parents to spend time with infants without losing wages should be central. Likewise, labor standards that give parents advance notice of work hours may do more for bedtime routines than many parenting lectures.

Income supports should be part of the developmental toolkit. Heckman warns that giving families more money is not the same as enhancing children’s environments. True. But it can enhance those environments when it reduces chaos. The Earned Income Tax Credit, food assistance, housing support, and child care subsidies are not crude bribes. They are ways of creating the conditions in which parents can parent. The casino income example in Heckman’s own essay shows that money can improve supervision and engagement.

Finally, families affected by incarceration require special attention. If we worry about single-parent homes, we must discuss the policies that remove large numbers of men from poor communities and then return them with criminal records that limit employment and family stability. Early childhood policy that ignores mass incarceration treats symptoms while avoiding one of the causes of family stress.

Heckman has given us valuable evidence against genetic fatalism and against the narrowness of test-score policy. For that he should be applauded. But we should resist any conclusion that the main scarcity in poor children’s lives is love. Many poor children are fiercely loved. What is scarce is security: secure income, secure housing, secure health care, secure time, secure neighborhoods, and secure public respect. Early childhood programs should build on that love, not presume its absence. If birth is becoming fate, then our answer must be broader than parenting improvement. It must be a politics of care, equality, and shared responsibility for the conditions under which families raise children.

  3. 03The concept of 'non-cognitive skills' can be used to unfairly label rational responses to institutional failure as character flaws or lack of self-regulation.
  4. 04Early childhood policy should be designed as universal support rather than targeted correction to avoid stigmatization and intrusive surveillance of poor families.
  5. 05Effective developmental policy must include structural labor reforms, such as paid family leave and predictable scheduling, to give parents the time and security necessary for nurturing.
  6. 06Direct income supports and the mitigation of mass incarceration are necessary to create the baseline stability required for successful early childhood outcomes.
  7. 07The primary scarcity in the lives of poor children is not parental love or motivation, but the security of income, housing, and public respect.
Diversified DeepSeek

"James Heckman overstates the potential of early childhood intervention by downplaying the persistent influence of genetic factors and relying on small-scale, non-replicable studies that do not support a massive shift in social policy."

James Heckman’s essay is an impressively crafted brief for early intervention, but it overstates the case. He claims that “the evidence from epigenetics… suggests that the gene-environment distinction… is obsolete” and that interventions can produce lasting positive effects. This narrative, while hopeful, runs ahead of what the science actually supports. A more sober reading of the evidence suggests that while early enrichment can nudge some outcomes for the most deprived children, the magnitude and durability of these effects are modest, and genetic differences remain powerful determinants of life outcomes. We should be realistic about what government programs can achieve, lest we promise more than we can deliver and divert resources from other necessary approaches.

Heckman leans heavily on two small randomized experiments, Perry Presidential and Abecedarian, which he says demonstrate “substantial positive effects” persisting into adulthood. But these studies, for all their methodological virtues, involved minuscule samples and were conducted decades ago under conditions that are not easily replicated. Perry’s 58 treated children achieved notable reductions in crime and welfare use, but an initial IQ gain faded completely within four years. The lasting benefits were in non-cognitive domains, but the exact mechanisms remain fuzzy. Were the children truly more persistent and motivated, or were they simply connected to supportive networks that helped them navigate life? The fact that the Abecedarian program included a home-school teacher who helped parents find employment and navigate bureaucracies complicates the claim that the “treatment” was purely an early education intervention.

More fundamentally, Heckman does not adequately grapple with the large and well-replicated finding that heritability of cognitive ability is substantial. Behavioral genetic studies using twin and adoption designs consistently find that in the environments typical of modern Western societies, genes account for roughly 50 percent of the variance in IQ by adolescence and up to 70 percent in adulthood. The Perry participants were black children with very low IQs (the mean IQ at entry was around 70, near the threshold for intellectual disability). The fadeout of IQ gains suggests that the intervention could not permanently lift general intelligence beyond the children’s genetic potential. What it may have done is improve the expression of that intelligence through better social skills and expanded knowledge, which is valuable, but it is not the same as transforming raw ability.

The epigenetic findings Heckman cites are fascinating but far from conclusive. The Caspi study on the MAOA gene and antisocial behavior, for instance, was a landmark, but it has been subject to the same replication difficulties that plague candidate gene studies. Large-scale genome-wide association studies have since shown that the genetic architecture of complex traits involves thousands of tiny effects, not a few simple gene-environment interactions. The claim that environment “triggers” gene expression is true in a trivial sense—all development requires an environment—but it does not imply that environmental interventions can easily rewire deeply canalized developmental pathways. The Romanian orphanage studies show the catastrophic effects of profound deprivation, but the American children Heckman is concerned about are not raised in such conditions. Extreme deprivation causes permanent damage; it does not follow that moderate enrichment of moderately disadvantaged homes will yield proportionate benefits at scale.

Heckman’s cost-benefit comparisons are slick but questionable. Comparing the 6–10 percent rate of return on Perry to the 5.8 percent return on equities since World War II is an apples-to-oranges exercise. The equity return is a market outcome for diversified portfolios; the Perry return is an estimated internal rate of return based on a host of assumptions about future earnings, crime costs to victims, and the deadweight loss of taxation. Change a few assumptions—about the value of a statistical life or the counterfactual earnings of dropouts—and the return can vanish. Head Start, the large federal program modeled partly on these experiments, has shown mixed long-term effects in the most rigorous national randomized study, with early gains fading and some modest health and parenting benefits but little impact on ultimate educational attainment or employment. This is not to say Head Start is worthless, but it does caution against claiming that early intervention is a magic bullet.

A responsible policy approach should acknowledge the limits of our knowledge. We should fund more small-scale, varied experiments, but we should not pretend that a massive shift of resources to early childhood will by itself reverse inequality. A just society will also need to provide direct income support, adjust labor market institutions, and yes, offer later remediation for those who need it. The genetic lottery is real, and while we should run the best possible preschools, we must also learn to respect the diversity of human talents and build an economy that values those who, for whatever mix of genetic and environmental reasons, will never excel at the cognitive and non-cognitive tasks Heckman prizes.

  2. 02Behavioral genetics research consistently shows that heritability accounts for a substantial portion of the variance in cognitive ability, explaining why IQ gains from early interventions often fade over time.
  3. 03The epigenetic and gene-environment interaction evidence cited by Heckman is scientifically inconclusive and often fails to replicate in large-scale genome-wide association studies.
  4. 04The high rates of return calculated for early intervention programs are based on questionable assumptions and are not reflected in the mixed results of large-scale federal programs like Head Start.
  5. 05A realistic social policy must look beyond early intervention to include direct income support and labor market reforms, acknowledging that government programs cannot fully override the 'genetic lottery.'
Vanilla DeepSeek

"While early childhood intervention is vital, a truly effective policy agenda must treat income support and early education as complementary strategies rather than dismissing the role of material resources in shaping a child's environment."

James Heckman’s diagnosis of America’s skills divide is powerful in many respects. He is right that the accident of birth now predicts life outcomes with alarming force, that non-cognitive skills matter at least as much as test scores, and that gaps in both kinds of abilities appear distressingly early. Yet his prescription, while wrapped in the language of rigorous evidence, gives too little weight to the most direct way that families shape those early environments: through their material resources. In downplaying income, the essay risks constructing a policy agenda that is both incomplete and unnecessarily modest.

The claim that “giving money to poor families does not, by itself, promote social mobility across generations” is stated as a hard lesson from the War on Poverty. But it is belied by a growing body of experimental and quasi-experimental evidence that was already well established by 2012. Several recent studies exploiting the Earned Income Tax Credit, for instance, show that increases in family income produce measurable improvements in children’s cognitive test scores and eventually in school achievement. Dahl and Lochner, in a 2012 paper, demonstrated that a $1,000 increase in income from the EITC raised combined math and reading scores by roughly 6 percent of a standard deviation, with effects concentrated among the most disadvantaged. Other work links the EITC to improved infant health and reductions in maternal stress. These findings matter because they isolate income’s role from the “quality of parenting” that Heckman rightly emphasizes. The most plausible pathway, supported by research on maternal depression and parenting stress, is that extra income gives parents the psychological and logistical bandwidth to provide the nurturing, stimulating environments Heckman champions. Income is not a rival to parenting quality; it is one of its key determinants.

Heckman’s celebration of small-scale experiments such as Perry Preschool and Abecedarian also prompts caution. Those programs were intensive, expensive, and run by committed researchers under artificial fidelity. The per-child cost of Abecedarian has been estimated at roughly $14,000 per year in 2012 dollars—far above what any scalable public program is likely to sustain. When early childhood education is taken to scale in the real world, as with Head Start, the results have been far more modest. The rigorous Head Start Impact Study, which released its third-grade follow-up in 2010, found that initial cognitive gains mostly faded out by the end of first grade, and few differences persisted on non-cognitive measures either. While Heckman would rightly note that Head Start varies greatly in quality, that is precisely the point: translating small-scale triumphs into a national system that consistently delivers high-quality interactions is a profound implementation challenge. The essay’s own call for a “menu of high quality programs from which parents can choose” acknowledges this difficulty without fully confronting it.

A more balanced approach would treat early intervention and income support as complements, not substitutes. The potent logic that “skill begets skill” cuts both ways: early environments are shaped in part by the financial strains that crowd out the very attentive, language-rich parenting that better-off children receive. The 1996 welfare reform that Heckman cites did not simply cut checks; it combined time limits with work requirements and dramatic expansions in supports like the EITC, essentially blending redistribution with predistribution. The evidence suggests that its most successful elements were precisely those that lifted take-home pay and reduced deep poverty among working families. To dismiss post-hoc redistribution as incapable of promoting mobility is to ignore this recent history.

None of this is to say that Heckman’s emphasis on early, character-focused intervention is wrong. It is vital, and the Perry and Abecedarian results are genuinely impressive. But a strategy that exclusively targets the parent-child interaction while leaving the economic pressures on parents intact sets an unnecessarily high bar for itself. The “scarce resource” may well be love and engaged parenting, but poverty taxes both. If we are serious about closing the gaps that open before kindergarten, we should expand high-quality early learning and simultaneously ensure that families have enough income to escape the corrosive stress that makes consistent nurturing so hard. A just society cannot choose between predistribution and redistribution; it needs both.

Scaling these models nationally would require building infrastructure that does not currently exist. A modest but well-designed expansion of evidence-based home visiting programs, combined with quality improvements in existing preschools, might achieve real results. Promising universal preschool immediately, based on demonstrations that cost multiples of what universal programs could realistically spend, sets up expectations we cannot meet.

Heckman is right that early intervention is more effective than later remediation. But effectiveness in controlled settings does not guarantee effectiveness at scale. Sound policy requires us to bridge that gap thoughtfully rather than assume that demonstration results will repeat themselves in imperfect real-world implementations. We should invest in early childhood, but we should also invest in the institutional capacity to make that investment count.
