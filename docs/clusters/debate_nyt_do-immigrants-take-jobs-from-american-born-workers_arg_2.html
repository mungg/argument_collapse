"Immigration only threatens the economic stability of American-born workers when poorly designed legal frameworks and visa programs grant employers unfair leverage to depress wages and standards."

It's Not Immigrants. It's the Rules We've Built Around Them.

The question "do immigrants take jobs from Americans?" is poorly posed, because it treats immigration as a single phenomenon with a single effect. In reality, the labor-market impact of immigration depends almost entirely on the legal architecture surrounding it. A Salvadoran roofer working off the books, an Indian software engineer on an H-1B, a Filipino nurse on a green card, and a Canadian executive on an L-1 visa are not interchangeable inputs. They affect different labor markets in different ways, and our policy treats them inconsistently—often perversely.

Consider the undocumented workforce, roughly 8 million strong in the labor market. Their illegality is not a bug from the employer's perspective; it is a feature. Workers who cannot complain to OSHA, cannot unionize meaningfully, and cannot demand back wages without risking deportation are cheaper and more pliable than workers who can. This pushes down standards in construction, agriculture, meatpacking, and hospitality—industries where native workers once earned middle-class wages and now often do not. The harm here is real, but its source is the legal limbo, not the immigrants themselves. Legalization would do more to raise wages in these sectors than deportation ever could.

The H-1B program illustrates the same dynamic in a white-collar key. The visa ties the worker to a sponsoring employer, creating a captive labor pool that depresses wages and reduces mobility. Outsourcing firms have learned to game the lottery, importing workers specifically to replace American IT staff—as recent layoffs at Southern California Edison and elsewhere have shown. Again, the issue is not that talented foreigners come to America; it is that we've designed a program that hands employers leverage over them.

So when Frum points to post-recession job gains accruing to immigrants, the right response isn't "build a wall" or "open the borders." It's to ask which immigrants, in which legal status, in which industries—and what features of policy produced that pattern. Enforce labor standards against employers regardless of worker status. Legalize the long-settled undocumented. Replace employer-tethered visas with portable work authorization. Shift toward skill-based selection where the labor market genuinely needs it.

Immigration becomes a threat to working-class stability mainly when policy makes it one. Change the policy, and the supposed dilemma between welcoming immigrants and protecting native workers largely dissolves.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The undocumented status of millions of workers allows employers to bypass labor standards and unions, driving down wages in industries like construction and agriculture.
  2. 02The H-1B visa program creates a captive labor pool by tying workers to specific employers, which reduces mobility and allows companies to replace native IT staff with cheaper labor.
  3. 03Legalizing long-settled undocumented immigrants would raise industry standards and wages more effectively than deportation.
  4. 04Replacing employer-tethered visas with portable work authorization would eliminate the artificial downward pressure on white-collar salaries.
  5. 05Shifting toward skill-based selection and enforcing labor laws regardless of worker status would resolve the perceived conflict between immigrant and native-born labor.
Diversified Gemini

"The economic strain on American-born workers is caused by employers exploiting the lack of legal protections for immigrants to undercut labor standards, rather than by immigrants themselves."

The debate over whether immigrants "take" jobs from American-born workers relies on a deeply flawed premise. It treats jobs as objects to be stolen and paints immigrants as the perpetrators of this theft. In doing so, it entirely absolves the actual decision-makers in the labor market: employers. Immigrants do not take jobs; employers choose to hire them, often purposefully exploiting a broken immigration system to undercut labor standards for everyone.

When we look at the post-recession job growth highlighted by David Frum, we must ask why employers in certain sectors prefer immigrant labor. The answer rarely has to do with an absolute shortage of American workers. Instead, it is driven by the vast power imbalance between management and an increasingly vulnerable workforce. Millions of immigrants, particularly the estimated 11 million undocumented individuals living in the shadows of our economy, are easily exploited. They face the constant threat of deportation, making them far less likely to report workplace safety violations, demand overtime pay, or attempt to organize a union.

By hiring workers who possess no legal rights or bargaining power, unscrupulous employers drag down the wage floor for entire industries. This dynamic certainly hurts working-class American citizens, who find themselves unable to compete with workers effectively forced to accept sub-legal conditions. However, the solution to this problem is not to build higher walls or deport millions of people, which only drives the undocumented population further underground and increases their vulnerability.

The true threat to American economic stability is not immigration, but the systematic erosion of labor rights and workplace protections. If we want to protect working-class citizens, the most effective strategy is comprehensive immigration reform that includes a path to legal status. As the Obama administration's recent executive actions attempt to recognize, bringing workers out of the shadow economy forces employers to pay minimum wages and abide by labor laws.

When immigrants have the same rights, protections, and bargaining power as native-born citizens, they can no longer be used as tools for wage suppression. Rather than pitting native-born workers against immigrant workers in a race to the bottom, we should focus on holding employers accountable, enforcing labor standards, and ensuring that any job created in the United States pays a living wage, regardless of who is hired to do it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The preference for immigrant labor in certain sectors is driven by a power imbalance that allows employers to exploit vulnerable workers who fear deportation.
  2. 02Hiring workers without legal rights drags down the wage floor for entire industries, making it impossible for American citizens to compete with sub-legal working conditions.
  3. 03The systematic erosion of labor rights and workplace protections, rather than immigration itself, is the primary threat to American economic stability.
  4. 04Comprehensive immigration reform and a path to legal status would protect all workers by preventing employers from using immigrants as tools for wage suppression.
  5. 05Economic policy should focus on holding employers accountable and enforcing universal labor standards rather than pitting native-born workers against immigrants.
