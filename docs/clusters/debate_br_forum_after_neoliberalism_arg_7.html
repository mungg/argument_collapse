Vanilla DeepSeek

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network offers valuable policy alternatives, it overestimates the ability of economic expertise to dismantle neoliberalism without first addressing the discipline's internal biases and the political power structures that determine which ideas are implemented."

The authors of “Economics After Neoliberalism” offer a compelling vision of a discipline that has moved well beyond market fundamentalism and stands ready to supply the policy tools for a more inclusive prosperity. Their diagnosis is welcome, and the sheer range of ideas assembled under the EfIP banner—from wage boards to social safeguards in trade—demonstrates that economists are indeed generating proposals that take inequality and power seriously. Yet the essay invites a deeper question that its authors, for all their candor about the profession’s “bad habits,” never quite settle: if contemporary economics is so rich in resources for combating inequality, why has the neoliberal policy paradigm proved so durable, and why should we now trust economists to lead the way out of it?

The authors insist that neoliberalism is not the consistent application of modern economics but its “primitive, simplistic perversion.” This is a familiar move. It draws a sharp line between the profession’s sophisticated internal debates and the slogans deployed in policy circles. But it underestimates how much the structure of the discipline itself—not just its public-facing caricatures—has contributed to the intellectual climate that sustained deregulation, financialization, and hyper-globalization. The essay notes that the competitive equilibrium model is dominant only in introductory courses, that thoughtful economists quickly move beyond it. But those introductory courses shape the worldview of countless future policymakers, lawyers, and business leaders. If the first and often last impression students get of economics is that markets are efficient, and if the discipline’s cultural prestige is built on the very mathematical formalism that gives “markets work” arguments their apparent rigor, then the damage is not merely a matter of poor public relations. The authors themselves acknowledge that many economists share a bias toward market-based solutions and that first-best reasoning has justified policy excesses. That bias is not a superficial add-on; it is cultivated by a curriculum and incentive system that rewards internal technical elegance over institutional and political context.

Moreover, the empirical revolution that the authors celebrate—the shift toward applied work and credible causal identification—is a double-edged sword. Yes, it has yielded findings that complicate market idolatry: minimum wages do not kill jobs, trade shocks devastate communities, financial liberalization breeds crises. But empirical economics is rarely a neutral arbiter. For every study showing minimal employment effects of a higher minimum wage, there has been intense methodological debate. The authors cite work by Arindrajit Dube on wage boards, yet the broader minimum-wage literature in 2019 remains contested terrain, with reasonable economists reaching different conclusions. The very model of the policy “free lunch”—the idea that we can push toward justice without sacrificing efficiency—depends on a specific reading of often fragile empirical results. If the evidence were as unambiguously progressive as the essay implies, one wonders why the policy world has not already been reshaped by it. The answer, of course, is that technical debates are easily weaponized, and the authority of “sound economics” is claimed by all sides. When the authors assert that neoliberal policies are “supported neither by sound economics nor by good evidence,” they risk simply defining soundness in terms of their own preferred conclusions, a move that can further erode public trust rather than rebuild it.

This points to a deeper tension. The EfIP project is presented as a set of policy ideas to be plugged into the existing machinery of democratic deliberation. But the essay’s own political economy analysis, drawing on Ethan Kaplan’s work, shows that the machinery is broken: money distorts representation, turnout is stratified by class, and the policy process is captured by special interests. If the deck is so stacked, then the road from a clever, evidence-based proposal to enacted legislation is all but impassable without a prior shift in political power. The authors propose to counter asymmetries of power with smarter policies—unions and wage boards to rein in monopsony, restrictions on campaign contributions to increase accountability. But these very policies must first be adopted by the same political system that the power asymmetries have compromised. The circularity is glaring. The essay implicitly assumes that good ideas, once demonstrated by economists, will muster the political coalition needed to implement them. Yet that assumption has repeatedly failed under neoliberalism, not for lack of ingenious policy blueprints but because the balance of organized interests made such blueprints politically nonviable. A genuine break with market fundamentalism might require a more confrontational politics than the essay’s technocratic framing allows—a politics that challenges the legitimacy of concentrated wealth and corporate power, not merely one that designs better tax formulas or wage-setting procedures.

The authors are right that economists have an indispensable role in laying out trade-offs and highlighting institutional alternatives. But their essay often slides into a more expansive claim: that economics, properly applied, provides the intellectual architecture for inclusive prosperity. This too easily equips the discipline with a primacy it has not earned. The design of institutions is irreducibly normative, involving choices about distributional weights and democratic procedures that economics does not supply. In a second-best world “rife with market imperfections,” where power matters enormously, as the authors concede, the most urgent task is not to devise the optimal carbon price or contingent credit contract but to restructure the political economy that decides which expertise counts and whose preferences are heard. That task lies squarely in the domain of democratic mobilization and institutional reform, and economists who wish to contribute must be far more open than the essay suggests about the limits of their own analytical tool kit. They must join a broader conversation in which economic expertise is one voice among many, not the arbiter of what counts as sound policy. Until then, the suspicion that economics is just another name for neoliberalism will persist, no matter how many creative policy briefs the profession produces.

  2. 02Empirical economic evidence is rarely a neutral arbiter and is easily weaponized in policy debates, meaning 'sound economics' cannot be used to objectively settle normative political disputes.
  3. 03The authors' technocratic approach fails to resolve the circular problem that their proposed policies to rebalance power must be enacted by a political system already captured by the very interests they seek to constrain.
  4. 04Achieving inclusive prosperity requires a confrontational politics and democratic mobilization that goes beyond the analytical toolkit of economics, which should be one voice among many rather than the primary architect of social change.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While the authors correctly argue that economics provides the analytical tools to challenge neoliberalism, their vision for inclusive prosperity underestimates the political obstacles and the inherent limitations of economic expertise in achieving structural reform."

The Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman essay makes a compelling case that economics as a discipline has been grievously misidentified with the market fundamentalist ideology that has shaped policy since the 1980s. Their argument is both accurate and important: the profession contains far richer analytical resources than its popular reputation suggests, and many economists have long been critical of the policy conclusions attributed to the field. The EfIP project they describe offers substantive, evidence-based proposals that deserve serious engagement.

Yet the essay's central rhetorical strategy—dissociating economics proper from neoliberalism as a perversion of the discipline—leaves important questions unaddressed. The problem is not merely one of public misunderstanding. The disconnect between what economics knows and what policy actually gets implemented reflects deeper structural features of our political economy that the essay acknowledges but does not fully explore.

The authors correctly note that economists often fall back on "first-best" reasoning that ignores distributional consequences and institutional context. They are right that recent empirical work has complicated simple claims about markets, trade, and regulation. But the history of economic policy suggests that the gap between academic consensus and political practice does not arise primarily from insufficient public understanding. It arises from the fact that economic ideas are deployed in a political arena where some interests are far more organized than others.

Consider the minimum wage. The essay cites evidence that minimum wages do not reduce employment—evidence that was indeed building in the 1990s and 2000s. Yet despite this evidence, minimum wage policy remained politically contested for decades, with the federal minimum wage stagnant in real terms for most of the past fifty years. This was not because Americans lacked access to the relevant research. It was because the political coalition favoring a higher minimum wage lacked the resources and organizational capacity to prevail against determined business opposition.

The authors' emphasis on "power asymmetries" is therefore welcome, and their proposals for addressing these asymmetries—through unions, wage boards, campaign finance reform, and voting access—represent the essay's most important contribution. But the same political forces that have blocked minimum wage increases and sustained corporate tax competition will resist these reforms with equal vigor. The essay would benefit from a more explicit analysis of how progressive economic policies might actually be enacted in our current institutional context.

There is also a tension in the essay's approach to expertise and democracy. The authors argue that economics provides tools for clarifying trade-offs and informing democratic deliberation. This is a defensible and modest role for the discipline. But the essay sometimes slides toward a more ambitious claim—that economists can identify "policy free lunches" that improve both efficiency and equity without meaningful costs. Such claims deserve scrutiny.

The finding that minimum wages do not reduce employment is now reasonably well-established, though debate continues about the magnitude of effects in different contexts. But the policy proposals go well beyond minimum wages. The proposal for steering technological change toward "inclusive" innovations assumes that governments can successfully direct innovation in ways that preserve both prosperity and equity. This is an empirical question, not a theoretical certainty. The history of industrial policy is littered with well-intentioned interventions that failed to achieve their stated goals. Economic analysis can illuminate trade-offs and identify potential problems with different approaches, but it cannot guarantee that any particular intervention will produce its intended effects.

Similarly, the proposal for reform of international corporate taxation is clever and analytically sound. But it would require international coordination that has proven extraordinarily difficult to achieve. The OECD's ongoing efforts at base erosion and profit shifting have made progress, but major corporate tax havens remain outside effective reach of any unilateral or even multilateral reform.

The authors are right that economics offers powerful tools for institutional design and policy analysis. They are right that many dominant policy ideas are poorly supported by evidence. But the path from sound analysis to inclusive prosperity runs through a political system that the essay describes but does not adequately grapple with. Economics can inform that system, but it cannot substitute for the harder work of building political coalitions capable of demanding and sustaining reform.

What the essay offers, then, is best understood not as a blueprint but as a contribution to an ongoing conversation about what economic policy could look like if we took seriously what economics actually knows. The authors' fundamental point stands: the discipline contains resources for thinking systematically about markets, institutions, and distribution. The challenge lies not in developing better ideas but in creating the political conditions under which better ideas can prevail.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The gap between economic research and policy implementation is driven by political power and organized interests rather than a simple public misunderstanding of economic theory.
  2. 02The authors' focus on 'policy free lunches' risks overstating the ability of economists to guarantee outcomes in complex areas like industrial policy and technological innovation.
  3. 03Proposed solutions, such as international tax reform, require levels of political coordination that are historically difficult to achieve and go beyond mere institutional design.
  4. 04The primary challenge to inclusive prosperity is not a lack of better economic ideas, but the absence of the political coalitions necessary to enact and sustain them.
