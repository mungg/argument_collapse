"While experimentalist governance offers a pragmatic framework for sectoral innovation, it fails as a global climate solution because it ignores the massive financial costs for the Global South and risks becoming a tool for 'green imperialism' by the Global North."

David G. Victor and Charles Sabel offer a compelling, albeit highly sanitized, framework for overcoming the gridlock of global climate diplomacy. Their diagnosis of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) as a paralyzed institution, hamstrung by its requirement for universal consensus and the lowest-common-denominator pledges it produces, is largely accurate. Their proposed solution—"experimentalist governance," modeled on the Montreal Protocol and driven by sectoral innovation and "penalty defaults"—sounds seductively practical. However, in their rush to praise the pragmatic virtues of sectoral experimentation and technological substitution, the authors gloss over the single most insurmountable barrier to global climate action: the astronomical cost of the transition for the Global South, and the historical inequity embedded in who is being asked to pay for it.

Victor and Sabel point to the Multilateral Fund of the Montreal Protocol as a triumph of experimentalist governance. They correctly note that the fund paid essentially all the compliance costs for developing nations, ensuring that they could adopt ozone-friendly technologies without sacrificing their own economic development. But the authors dramatically understate the difference in scale between replacing chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and decarbonizing the entire global economy. The Multilateral Fund has disbursed roughly $4 billion since its inception in 1991. The International Energy Agency estimates that transitioning the energy systems of developing nations to zero-carbon alternatives will require trillions of dollars in investment every single year.

To suggest that the Montreal model can be seamlessly scaled up to tackle climate change ignores the fundamental political economy of global inequality. Ozone-depleting substances were a distinct, highly specific class of industrial chemicals used in a limited number of applications—refrigeration, aerosols, solvents. Fossil fuels, by contrast, are the lifeblood of the modern global economy. They are the foundation of transportation, agriculture, electricity, and basic infrastructure. For a nation like India or Nigeria, fossil fuels represent the cheapest and most accessible path to lifting millions out of poverty, electrifying rural villages, and industrializing their economies.

When Victor and Sabel advocate for "penalty defaults"—which they define as "draconian penalties" for governments and firms that drag their feet, including trade sanctions and exclusion from key markets—they are effectively endorsing a system of green imperialism. Who gets to define the terms of these penalties? Historically, and inevitably in the authors' framework, it will be the industrialized nations of the Global North, which have already grown rich by burning the vast majority of the carbon currently trapped in the atmosphere.

Imagine a scenario where the United States and the European Union, having successfully commercialized green steel and zero-carbon shipping technologies through "experimentalist" sectoral committees, decide to impose penalty defaults on developing nations that cannot afford these premium technologies. Tariffs and border carbon adjustments would be slapped on goods from the Global South, locking them out of Western markets because they cannot magically summon the capital to overhaul their heavy industries overnight. This is not "governance"; it is economic coercion masquerading as environmental stewardship.

The Paris Agreement, for all its structural flaws, explicitly recognizes the concept of "Common But Differentiated Responsibilities" (CBDR). It acknowledges that while all nations must act, wealthy nations have a historical obligation to lead and to provide finance and technology transfers to the developing world. Victor and Sabel dismiss this distinction, noting approvingly that Paris "did away with the distinction, codified in Kyoto... between developed countries, responsible for financing the costs of decarbonization, and developing countries, who were essentially exempted from all obligations." They view this erasure of historical responsibility as a feature, freeing the system to embrace "local initiative." From the perspective of the Global South, however, it is a profound betrayal. It effectively shifts the burden of a crisis created by the North onto the shoulders of the South.

Furthermore, the authors’ reliance on the private sector to drive this transition is naive. They cite Maersk’s coordination inside the International Maritime Organization (IMO) as a success story of a firm pooling knowledge and risk. But Maersk is a massive multinational corporation with the capital reserves to weather a multi-decade technological transition. What happens to the domestic industries in developing countries that lack such capital? If the primary driver of experimentalist governance is corporate innovation backed by Western regulatory penalties, the result will be a massive consolidation of global wealth. Western multinationals will develop the green patents, and the developing world will be forced to pay exorbitant licensing fees or face trade embargoes.

The authors write that "climate activism will focus on aggressive administrative action... to increase pressure on firms in key industries." But activism must also focus on global justice. If experimentalist governance is to be anything more than a tool for the Global North to maintain its economic hegemony while retrofitting its own industries, it must place massive, unconditional financial transfers at its core. Until the architects of climate policy grapple with the trillions needed to fund the energy transition in the Global South, comparing the climate crisis to the ozone hole will remain an exercise in intellectual escapism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Montreal Protocol is an inadequate model for climate change because the cost of decarbonizing entire economies is trillions of dollars higher than the relatively inexpensive phase-out of ozone-depleting chemicals.
  2. 02The use of 'penalty defaults' like trade sanctions would unfairly penalize developing nations that lack the capital to adopt expensive green technologies, effectively functioning as economic coercion.
  3. 03The authors' celebration of the removal of 'Common But Differentiated Responsibilities' ignores the historical obligation of wealthy nations to lead the transition and provide technology transfers to the Global South.
  4. 04Relying on private sector innovation risks a consolidation of global wealth where Western multinationals hold green patents while the developing world is forced to pay high licensing fees or face market exclusion.
  5. 05Experimentalist governance is intellectual escapism unless it places massive, unconditional financial transfers to the Global South at the center of its policy architecture.
