# Courts as the Last Line of Defense

The lead essay is largely correct in its diagnosis of the problem but dangerously complacent about the solution. Akbar and Theoharis write that "courts historically often endorsed public fears and hatreds," invoking Dred Scott and Korematsu as cautionary tales. But this history cuts both ways. Courts have also been the vehicle through which civil liberties have been vindicated when political branches failed. The very fact that Trump's travel ban was blocked by federal judges—some appointed by Republicans—demonstrates that the judiciary can serve as a check, even in moments of national hysteria.

The authors' critique of federal courts in terrorism cases is overstated. Yes, courts have shown deference to government assertions of national security. But this deference is not unlimited, and it has been contested. In cases like Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, the Supreme Court upheld the material support statute but left open questions about its application to independent political speech. Lower courts have occasionally reined in the most aggressive prosecutorial overreach. And the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, while not a model of transparency, has rejected significant numbers of government surveillance applications.

The more urgent problem is that courts are being asked to do too much with too little institutional support. Federal judges are appointed for life precisely because they are supposed to be insulated from political pressure. But the executive branch has systematically exploited this insulation to expand its own power. When the government says "national security," courts have often accepted this as a black box requiring no further inquiry. The answer is not to despair of the courts but to insist that they fulfill their constitutional function.

This means demanding that courts require the government to provide actual evidence—not just classified summaries—to justify surveillance, detention, and prosecution. It means insisting that defendants in terrorism cases receive the same due process protections as defendants in ordinary criminal cases. It means challenging the use of secret evidence, anonymous witnesses, and inhumane conditions of confinement.

Some will object that national security requires deference to the political branches. But this argument has been made to justify every abuse of civil liberties in American history, and it has always been wrong. The courts are not omniscient, and they cannot always distinguish genuine security threats from political demonization. But that is precisely why their independence matters. When courts reflexively defer to executive claims of national security, they abandon their constitutional role and become handmaidens to oppression.

The authors are right that the resistance to Trump's ban came too late and was too narrow. But the legal challenges to the ban are ongoing, and they offer a model for how courts can be engaged. The lawsuits filed by civil liberties groups have raised serious constitutional questions about discrimination on the basis of religion, about the procedural protections afforded to detained travelers, and about the statutory authority claimed by the executive. These questions deserve serious judicial attention, not reflexive deference.

What is needed is a sustained effort to rebuild the culture of civil liberties review in the federal courts. This means supporting organizations that litigate these issues, demanding that judicial nominees have real commitments to civil liberties, and building public pressure for judicial independence. The courts are not sufficient for the struggle against Islamophobia, but they are necessary, and we should not abandon them to the executive.
