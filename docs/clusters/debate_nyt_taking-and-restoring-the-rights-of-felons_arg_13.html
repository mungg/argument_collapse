"Once a felon has completed their sentence and re-entered society as a free individual, their constitutional right to own a firearm for self-defense should be fully restored."

The outrage over states restoring gun rights to non-violent and even some violent felons is fundamentally misplaced. It ignores the constitutional reality that the Second Amendment is not a second-class right.

If we accept that a person has served their time and is re-entering society, they possess an inherent right to self-defense. The world is a dangerous place, and an ex-felon living in a high-crime neighborhood has arguably a greater need for a firearm for home protection than a wealthy suburbanite.

The argument that violent felons will simply use legally acquired guns to commit more crimes defies logic. A criminal intent on committing armed robbery does not care about background checks or restoration certificates; they will procure a weapon on the black market. The only people taking advantage of the legal restoration of gun rights are those who are genuinely trying to live within the bounds of the law.

If an individual is a truly unredeemable monster who cannot be trusted with a basic tool of self-defense, then the justice system failed by releasing them from confinement. But once a person is allowed to walk the streets as a free individual, their constitutional rights must be fully recognized. We cannot selectively pick and choose which parts of the Bill of Rights apply to free men and women based on past mistakes for which they have already paid the price.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Second Amendment is a fundamental constitutional right that should not be treated as inferior to other civil liberties.
  2. 02Ex-felons living in high-crime areas have a legitimate and significant need for firearms to ensure their personal safety and home protection.
  3. 03Restricting legal access to guns does not deter recidivism, as criminals intent on committing crimes will obtain weapons through illegal channels regardless of the law.
  4. 04The legal restoration process primarily benefits individuals who intend to follow the law, rather than those who remain a threat to society.
  5. 05If an individual is deemed too dangerous to exercise their constitutional rights, they should remain incarcerated rather than being released with restricted liberties.
