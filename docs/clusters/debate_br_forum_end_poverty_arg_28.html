"The failures of ICT4D projects are not inherent to technology itself, but rather the result of poor project management and a lack of rigorous, user-centered design."

Kentaro Toyama’s essay is at its strongest when it recounts the quiet failures of telecenters across South Asia and Africa. His description of the Retawadi telecenter—a dirt-floored room, a boy typing “Alaska,” and a business model that hemorrhaged money—is a miniature tragedy of development misplaced. Yet Toyama draws the wrong lesson from these failures. The problem is not technology per se, nor even the “myth of scale.” The problem is that ICT4D practitioners have repeatedly ignored the mundane but essential disciplines of good project management: understanding user needs, iterating based on feedback, and building sustainable business models.

Consider the Retawadi telecenter again. Why did it fail? Toyama notes that the owner earned about twenty dollars a month while costs were a hundred. But this is a straightforward pricing or subsidy problem, not a philosophical argument against technology. In many rural areas, cybercafés in small towns thrive precisely because they have identified services that people will pay for: job applications, remittance transfers, family video calls, exam results. The Retawadi center offered typing lessons and occasional Internet browsing—activities that were likely supply-driven rather than demanded. A more market-oriented approach might have found a different niche.

The failures Toyama catalogues—poor electrical supply, lack of local content, inadequate training—are all failures of execution. They are not inherent properties of information technology. In the commercial world, similar challenges are met with offline-capable apps, local-language interfaces, and solar-powered charging. There is no reason such solutions cannot be deployed in development contexts, provided we treat ICT4D projects with the same rigor we would expect in a startup. Instead, too many projects are designed in distant labs and parachuted into villages, with no plan for long-term viability. That is an institutional failure, not a technological one.

Toyama’s magnifier thesis, while elegant, actually lets development professionals off the hook too easily. By framing technology as a mere amplifier, he implies that if underlying human capacity is weak, no amount of technology will help. But that fatalism ignores the possibility that a well-designed intervention can compensate for local deficiencies. For example, an agriculture app that uses pictograms and voice prompts rather than text can be used by illiterate farmers. A telemedicine system that connects community health workers with remote doctors can improve care even where local medical expertise is scarce. These are not cases of technology waiting on capacity; they are cases of technology bridging capacity gaps.

Of course, technology cannot replace a functioning primary school system or honest government. But no one in the ICT4D field seriously claims it can. What technology can do is deliver rapid, incremental improvements in specific domains—market efficiency, health information, financial inclusion—that cumulatively raise living standards. The real question is not whether technology can end poverty alone, but whether it can be one effective tool among many. On that score, the evidence from mobile money, supply chain management, and distance learning is more encouraging than Toyama’s essay admits. The failures he documents are less a verdict on technology than a mirror held up to a development industry that too often privileges good intentions over good design.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic failure of specific telecenters is a solvable problem of pricing and market-fit rather than a philosophical indictment of technology.
  2. 02Technical and logistical hurdles like poor electricity or language barriers are execution failures that can be overcome with better engineering and local adaptation.
  3. 03Toyama’s 'magnifier thesis' is overly fatalistic and ignores how well-designed technology can actively bridge capacity gaps, such as literacy or medical expertise.
  4. 04Technology should be viewed as one effective tool among many for incremental improvement rather than a standalone solution to poverty.
  5. 05The lack of success in many ICT4D initiatives reflects a development industry that prioritizes good intentions over sustainable business models and functional design.
