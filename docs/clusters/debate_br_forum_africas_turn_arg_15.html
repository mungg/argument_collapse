"The primary threat to Africa’s progress is not merely electoral fraud, but a constitutional design that concentrates excessive power in the presidency, making elections high-stakes, existential conflicts over resources and security."

Kenya’s crisis should force us to revise the standard story about African democracy. The problem is not simply that an election was stolen. The deeper problem is that the presidency was worth stealing.

Edward Miguel describes Kenya’s political opening with justified admiration: independent media, opposition rallies, energetic civil society, and a real possibility that an incumbent could lose. These are major achievements. But the violence after December 2007 revealed a constitutional disease common across the continent: excessive concentration of power in the executive. When control of the presidency means control of appointments, contracts, land administration, police, development funds, and protection from prosecution, elections become dangerously high-stakes events.

In such a system, citizens do not vote only for policies. They vote for access, security, and recognition. Ethnic appeals become rational, if destructive, because people believe “our” man in office will protect “our” people. This is not ancient tribalism. It is modern state competition under conditions of distrust. Ethnicity becomes politically explosive when state institutions distribute benefits unequally and fail to protect losers.

Kenya illustrates this painfully. The Rift Valley clashes cannot be understood apart from land. Colonial settlement, post-independence redistribution, migration, patronage, and historical grievance all shaped local claims. Politicians have repeatedly activated those claims around elections. To describe the violence as Kalenjin versus Kikuyu is accurate but incomplete. It was also about who belongs, who owns, who is protected, and who can use the state to settle local disputes. Until land grievances are addressed through credible institutions, electoral competition will continue to carry the risk of expulsion and revenge.

This is why “democratization” must be disaggregated. Multiparty elections are necessary but not sufficient. A country may have newspapers and opposition parties, yet still lack impartial courts, professional police, trusted land registries, independent electoral administration, and constraints on executive power. Freedom House scores capture important changes, but they cannot tell us whether a farmer in the Rift Valley trusts the police to protect him if his ethnic group loses an election.

The lesson extends beyond Kenya. Côte d’Ivoire’s collapse was tied to citizenship, land, migration, and the manipulation of identity. Zimbabwe’s disaster has involved executive domination, land seizures, politicized violence, and the destruction of electoral credibility. Nigeria’s elections have often been corrupted by the enormous rewards of office in an oil state. In each case, the formal act of voting occurs within a political economy that makes losing dangerous.

Miguel asks whether democracy may help explain Africa’s recent economic gains. Perhaps. More open politics can discipline rulers, expose corruption, and encourage better policies. But democracy can support growth only if political competition is institutionalized. Investors need to believe that contracts will survive a change in government. Citizens need to believe that losing parties are not excluded from public goods. Opposition leaders need to believe they can compete again if they lose today. Incumbents need to believe they can leave office without prison, exile, or dispossession—unless they have committed serious crimes through processes seen as fair.

This raises an uncomfortable issue: in some fragile states, immediate winner-take-all presidential elections may not be the best democratic form. Parliamentary systems, federal arrangements, proportional representation, decentralization, and constitutionally limited executives may better manage diversity. None is a magic solution. Decentralization can empower local bosses; federalism can harden ethnic boundaries; power-sharing can reward violence. But institutional design matters. The presidency should not be a national trophy with the entire state attached.

Kenya’s power-sharing agreement, brokered by Kofi Annan, was necessary to stop bloodshed. But it is not a substitute for constitutional reform. If the bargain merely divides ministries among elites, it may demobilize violence while preserving the system that produced it. A genuine settlement would include electoral commission reform, judicial strengthening, police accountability, land commissions with real authority, parliamentary oversight, and perhaps a rebalancing of power away from the presidency.

International actors have a role, but it must be precise. Donor pressure helped push Daniel arap Moi toward multiparty elections, yet donors too often treat elections as discrete events: fund observers, praise turnout, issue statements, and leave. The harder work is between elections. Voter registries, constituency boundaries, local courts, civic education, media freedom, campaign-finance rules, and security-sector neutrality all require sustained attention. Election day is the exam; institution-building is the education.

Miguel’s proposed rapid conflict prevention support is valuable but should include political triggers as well as economic ones. Rainfall drops and commodity-price collapses can increase conflict risk, but so can constitutional referenda, succession struggles, censuses, land adjudication, and close elections. A country approaching a high-stakes election with a biased electoral commission may need preventive diplomacy and institutional support as urgently as a drought-stricken country needs food aid. Conflict prevention should not be technocratic weather insurance alone.

The economic stakes are large. Kenya’s post-election violence disrupted transport to Uganda and beyond. A political crisis in one country can become a regional economic shock. Investors who watched Nairobi burn will reassess risk elsewhere. Tourism collapses quickly. Small businesses lose inventories. Workers flee. Children miss school. The costs of institutional failure are not abstract.

Yet one should not conclude that Africa is unsuited to democracy. That would be both wrong and dangerous. The problem is not too much democracy, but too little constitutionalism. Africans have repeatedly shown that they want accountable government. Long lines at polling stations, courageous newspapers, opposition organizing, and civil-society monitoring all testify to democratic demand. The task is to build states that can survive that demand without turning competition into combat.

Here Botswana again offers lessons, though not a model that can simply be copied. Its stability rests partly on diamonds and prudent leadership, but also on institutions that created a broader social contract. Drought relief, regular elections, consultation, and relatively clean administration helped citizens see the state as something other than a prize for one group. The essential point is not that Botswana is perfect; it is that legitimacy can be built.

Africa’s turn, if it comes, will be constitutional as much as economic. Paved roads and mobile phones can accelerate commerce, but they cannot by themselves decide who has the right to rule, who owns land, or who is protected by police. Commodity booms can finance budgets, but they can also finance repression. Aid can support services, but it can also be captured.

The central development question is therefore political: can African states make power less terrifying to lose? If they can, elections will become mechanisms of accountability rather than moments of existential danger. If they cannot, every boom will remain one disputed tally away from flames.
