"Victims of mass violence should primarily seek long-term compensation through the civil litigation system rather than government funds or private charities to ensure accountability and systemic reform."

When discussing compensation for victims of mass violence and terrorism, we must recognize that money serves a dual purpose: it aids the injured, but it also holds the negligent accountable. Bypassing the traditional tort system through special government funds or sweeping charity distributions strips victims of their most powerful tool for justice—the right to sue.

Consider the true origin of the September 11th Victim Compensation Fund. It is often remembered as a profound act of national generosity, but its primary legislative purpose was to protect the airline industry from financial ruin. To receive a payout from the fund, families had to sign away their right to sue the airlines, the security companies, and other entities whose failures allowed the hijackers to succeed. While this provided quick financial relief, it effectively slammed the door on public discovery, preventing depositions and trials that would have thoroughly exposed corporate negligence.

Civil litigation is not merely about greed or seeking a payday; it is one of the most effective regulatory mechanisms in American society. When victims of mass shootings or bombings are allowed to take their cases to court, they gain subpoena power. They can uncover whether a commercial property ignored security warnings, whether a landlord failed to provide adequate exits, or whether a gun manufacturer skirted the law. The threat of massive financial liability forces corporations to implement better security measures, ultimately preventing future tragedies.

Private charities and ad-hoc government funds offer no such structural corrections. They simply pay out cash and allow the institutions whose negligence contributed to the tragedy to escape public scrutiny. We must not let the desire for swift financial relief override the necessary pursuit of truth. While immediate charitable assistance for medical bills is essential, long-term compensation should be determined in the courtroom. It is the only way to ensure that the victims get their day in court, and that the systemic failures that allowed the tragedy to occur are exposed and remedied.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Government-administered funds often serve to protect negligent industries from financial liability by requiring victims to waive their right to sue.
  2. 02The civil litigation process provides victims with subpoena power to uncover corporate negligence and security failures that would otherwise remain hidden from the public.
  3. 03The threat of massive financial liability through lawsuits acts as a regulatory mechanism that incentivizes institutions to implement better safety measures to prevent future tragedies.
  4. 04Private charities and ad-hoc funds provide financial relief without addressing the structural failures or holding responsible parties accountable for their actions.
