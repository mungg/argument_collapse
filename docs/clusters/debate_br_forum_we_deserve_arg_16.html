The Teleology Wasn't the Problem

Evgeny Morozov's essay diagnoses the original sin of AI as its "teleological" orientation—its commitment to goal-pursuit, problem-solving, and instrumental reason—and locates the corrective in Hans Otto Storm's "eolithism" and a recovered pragmatist tradition of "ecological reason." It is an elegant intellectual move, but as someone who has spent two decades working on machine learning, I think it gets the technical history almost exactly backwards. Teleology is not what went wrong with AI. The contemporary problem is precisely that frontier AI systems have become alarmingly non-teleological, generative rather than goal-directed, and that the social institutions surrounding them have not adapted to this fact.

Consider Morozov's own example: the General Problem Solver. Yes, Newell, Simon, and Shaw built a system that did means-ends analysis on formal problems. But GPS was a failure as a general technology. It did not survive contact with real domains. The line of work Morozov treats as foundational—rules-based, expert-system, symbolic AI—collapsed in the late 1980s precisely because the world refused to behave teleologically. The neural network paradigm that replaced it is not, contra Morozov, just "teleology continued by other means." It is structurally different. A large language model is not trying to solve a problem in any meaningful sense. It is performing next-token prediction over a distribution of human-generated text. This is a profoundly different operation from means-ends analysis, and the differences matter.

Morozov does seem to half-recognize this when he writes that today's AI "hallucinates its way to an answer" and that LLMs "resemble the flexibility of the market more than the rigidity of bureaucracy." But he immediately recodes this as a continuation of the same instrumental-reason project, just dressed in postmodern clothes. This is an analytical mistake. Hallucination is a symptom of non-teleological operation. The model has no telos; it has only a learned distribution. When we then bolt teleological scaffolding on top—through reinforcement learning from human feedback, through prompting, through agent frameworks—we are explicitly trying to make these systems more goal-directed, because their native operation is too unmoored from goals to be useful. This is the opposite of the picture Morozov paints.

Why does this matter? Because Morozov's prescription—that AI should "amplify ecological reason" rather than "embody instrumental reason"—turns out to be an apt description of what frontier generative AI already does, and the result is not the democratic flowering he hopes for. His own example of his language-learning app makes the point against him. He stumbled, flâneur-like, on a feature; he linked services together in unexpected ways; he treated ChatGPT as an "experimental workshop"; he produced something idiosyncratic and unplanned. By his own account, this is eolithic bricolage. The fact that he is dissatisfied with this experience—that he finds it "individualistic," "alienated," "ecologically costly"—suggests that the problem is not insufficient eolithism but something else entirely: the political economy of the platforms, the energy footprint of the compute, the inability to share or pool the labor, the proprietary control over the weights.

These are real problems. But notice that they are not problems about teleology versus eolithism. They are problems about ownership, infrastructure, externalities, and labor. Morozov's framework conflates a political-economic critique (which I largely share) with a cognitive-historical critique (which I think is mistaken). The former does not depend on the latter.

There is also a technical-historical question Morozov elides. The most important precursor to deep learning is not the Cold War operations researcher's means-ends analysis but the connectionist tradition that AI's symbolic establishment marginalized for decades. Rosenblatt's Perceptron was attacked in Minsky and Papert's 1969 book on grounds that, in retrospect, were over-stated; backpropagation languished; LeCun, Hinton, and Bengio worked in relative obscurity for years before the 2012 ImageNet result. The line of research that produced ChatGPT was emphatically not the favored child of the Efficiency Lobby; it was a heterodox bet by researchers who were often dismissed by the symbolic AI mainstream and who attracted serious industrial investment only after empirical results forced the issue. To paint contemporary deep learning as the smooth continuation of a 1950s teleological project is to flatten a much more interesting and contested history.

What about Morozov's pragmatist alternative? I am sympathetic to the move toward Dewey—his theory of inquiry, his rejection of means-end dualism, his account of habit and experience are all rich resources. But Dewey is not a deus ex machina. Pragmatist ideas have in fact circulated within AI research for decades. The embodied cognition movement, the situated robotics tradition associated with Rodney Brooks (whose famous 1991 paper "Intelligence Without Representation" is practically a Deweyan manifesto), reinforcement learning's emphasis on agent-environment coupling, even the recent enthusiasm for "world models" in robotics—all these draw, often explicitly, on pragmatist and ecological-psychological sources. James Gibson's notion of "affordances" (itself a sibling of Storm's eolithism) is foundational to modern HCI. Morozov writes as though the AI community has been deaf to non-Cartesian alternatives; in fact, it has been engaging them, sometimes well and sometimes badly, for thirty years.

The genuine puzzle is why none of this has produced the democratic technology Morozov wants. The answer, I would submit, has very little to do with the philosophy of cognition and very much to do with the political economy of compute. Modern frontier AI requires vast capital expenditure on data centers, energy, and chips. The companies that can afford this are few. Government investment in alternatives has been chronically underfunded. There is no public-option foundation model because no public has been organized to demand one. None of this would change if every AI researcher tomorrow read Dewey instead of Heidegger. It would change if the political coalition for public technology infrastructure were strengthened—which is, ultimately, the same point I take Morozov to want to land on, but which his cognitive-historical framing obscures.

So I want to thank Morozov for an unusually rich essay while resisting his diagnosis. The eolithic flâneur and the goal-directed problem-solver are not the deep stakes of contemporary AI politics. The deep stakes are who owns the compute, who labels the data, who pays the externalities, and who decides what gets built. These are political questions, not cognitive ones. We do not need a new philosophy of intelligence. We need a new political coalition for technology. The Cold War metaphors are pretty, but they may be leading us away from the fight that is actually in front of us.
