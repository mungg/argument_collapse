"To manage the evolutionary certainty of antibiotic resistance, we must shift from a strategy of preservation to a resilient system of continuous drug development and diversified treatment methods."

Antibiotic resistance is not a problem that can be solved; it is an evolutionary certainty that must be permanently managed. The quest to “avoid a future where antibiotics are no longer useful” is a well-intentioned fantasy. Bacteria predate us by billions of years and will outlast every drug we invent. Our true goal must be to build a resilient system that manages a slow, perpetual flow of resistance while constantly cycling our arsenal—an approach grounded in humility, not hubris.

The fantasy of a final victory arises from a misunderstanding of biology. Resistance genes exist naturally in ancient bacteria, and horizontal gene transfer allows them to spread between species with shocking ease. Every introduction of an antibiotic selects for the few mutants that can survive it. So long as we use these drugs, resistance will increase. Our current strategy of trying to “preserve” our current drugs by restricting them is necessary but ultimately a delaying action. The pipeline of entirely new classes is critically thin, and even with lavish incentives, we cannot outpace evolution indefinitely.

A more honest strategy focuses on three layers of resilience. First, aggressive monetary support for truly novel drug research must be a permanent feature of public health funding, not a one-time surge. We must think in terms of a continuous rolling pipeline where new scaffolds are always entering the late stages of development, designed with a limited therapeutic lifespan in mind.

Second, we must make combination therapy the standard, not an exception. Using two or three drugs with different mechanisms simultaneously, the tactic that transformed HIV from a death sentence to a chronic condition, reduces the probability of a single bacterium surviving all three attacks. This would require commercial incentives to develop antibiotics specifically for use in such cocktail regimens.

Finally, we need to accept that infection control will increasingly rely on non-antibiotic methods: phage therapy, which uses viruses that prey on bacteria, remains a viable and neglected avenue worthy of massive investment. We should pursue vaccines against the most common hospital-acquired threats, like *Staphylococcus aureus*, with renewed vigor. A future without reliable antibiotics is a terrifying prospect, but a future that acknowledges their inherent fragility and diversifies accordingly is a future we can endure. The point is not to stop the clock—it’s to learn how to live in the moving current.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Resistance is an inevitable biological process that cannot be stopped, making the preservation of current drugs only a temporary delaying tactic.
  2. 02Public health funding must provide permanent, aggressive support for a continuous pipeline of novel drug research to replace failing antibiotics.
  3. 03Adopting combination therapy as the standard treatment would reduce the likelihood of bacteria surviving multiple simultaneous mechanisms of attack.
  4. 04Investment should be redirected toward non-antibiotic alternatives such as phage therapy and vaccines for hospital-acquired infections to reduce our reliance on traditional drugs.
