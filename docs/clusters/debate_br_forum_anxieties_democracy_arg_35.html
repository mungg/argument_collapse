"The current crisis of democracy is not a failure of legislative procedure or civic norms, but the inevitable result of representative institutions being captured by global capital and transformed into an oligarchy."

Ira Katznelson is right to notice the scaffolding around the Capitol and the metaphorical crumbling of the representative institutions inside. But by framing the current crisis as a loss of "background agreement" or a failure of legislative procedure, he fundamentally misdiagnoses the disease. The anxieties of democracy today are not the product of a breakdown in procedural norms, nor are they the result of citizens selfishly seeking direct participation over the "dignity of legislation." Rather, what we are witnessing is the inevitable consequence of a representative system that has been entirely captured by global capital.

Katznelson places immense faith in the legislature as the ideal arena where differences are managed and collective choices are produced. But this idealized vision of parliamentarianism assumes that the actors within the legislature are actually representing the diverse interests of the populace. In reality, the American legislature—and indeed, parliaments across the advanced capitalist world—has become a cartel of the wealthy. The "principle of non-convertibility" that Katznelson cites, where economic wealth is not supposed to translate into political power, has never been fully realized, and under the neoliberal consensus of the past forty years, it has been deliberately obliterated.

When Katznelson points to the New Deal as the savior of democracy in the 1930s, he glosses over the fact that the New Deal was a massive restructuring of the political economy, one that required severe constraints on capital. Today’s legislatures are structurally incapable of enacting a new New Deal, not because of arcane rules or a lack of civic virtue, but because both major political parties are funded by the very financial institutions and multinational corporations that benefit from the status quo. The Supreme Court’s *Citizens United* decision was merely the final nail in a coffin that had been constructed over decades of deregulation and privatization.

This brings us to Katznelson’s dismissal of populism. He lumps Occupy Wall Street and the Tea Party together as "movements [that] share the conviction that a mobilized people forces conclusions superior to those achieved by elites." He views this as a dangerous circumvention of representative democracy. But what Katznelson fails to understand is that these movements did not arise because people suddenly lost their taste for parliamentary debate. They arose because the parliament had already shut them out. When the financial crisis of 2008 destroyed the livelihoods of millions, the legislature’s response was to bail out the banks and leave the working class to drown. When the legislature acts as a shield for capital rather than a mediator of public interest, extra-parliamentary mobilization is not a threat to democracy; it is the last gasp of democracy trying to assert itself.

Katznelson warns that abandoning the legislature invites "Peronism" or illiberalism, citing Viktor Orbán. But the rise of right-wing nationalism is itself a symptom of the legislature's economic abdication. When representative institutions refuse to address staggering inequality, wage stagnation, and the precarity of modern labor, they create a vacuum. If the left does not fill that vacuum with a mass movement demanding a radical redistribution of wealth and power, the right will fill it with xenophobia and authoritarianism.

Therefore, the solution cannot be found in a return to the polite, compromising legislative norms of a bygone era. We cannot simply politely debate our way out of an oligarchy. Real democracy requires conflict, and it requires challenging the structural economic power that has hollowed out the state. We do not need a recommitment to the "physics of consent" within a rigged legislature; we need a fundamental transformation of the political economy to ensure that the representatives in the Capitol are beholden to the people on the streets, not the donors in the suites. Until we sever the umbilical cord between concentrated wealth and political power, the scaffolding will remain, and the rotunda will continue to decay.

  3. 03Populist movements and extra-parliamentary mobilization are not threats to democracy, but necessary responses to a representative system that has systematically excluded the interests of the working class.
  4. 04The rise of illiberalism and right-wing nationalism is a direct symptom of the legislature's failure to address economic inequality and the precarity of modern labor.
  5. 05Restoring democracy requires a radical transformation of the political economy to sever the link between concentrated wealth and political power rather than a return to polite parliamentary norms.
