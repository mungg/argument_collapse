Some Conflicts Should Not Be Endured

Mie Inouye writes movingly about the discomfort of organizing across difference and the value of staying in rooms where one would rather not be. There is much in her essay I admire — the honesty about her own motivations, the recovery of the Rainbow Coalition footage, the willingness to think with her own family rather than against it. But I want to register a sharp disagreement with the essay's central prescription. The injunction to cultivate "social endurance," to anticipate "annoyance and heartbreak," to interpret discomfort as "a process of becoming less racist, less classist, less transphobic," sounds wise in the abstract. In practice it has too often functioned as a rationale for asking the wrong people to absorb the wrong costs.

I write as a Black queer woman who has spent fifteen years in organizations that, to varying degrees, demanded my endurance. I have endured the white liberal who needed me to walk her through her own racism for the third time this year. I have endured the male comrade whose feminism evaporated whenever a woman of color disagreed with him about strategy. I have endured the well-meaning chapter chair who could not understand why "we're all here for the same reason" did not feel inclusive to those of us for whom the reasons are not, in fact, the same. Each of these endurances was sold to me as the price of solidarity. Each was, in some measure, the redistribution of emotional labor from those who could leave to those whose attachment to the cause made leaving harder.

This is not a denunciation of Inouye's argument. She is careful, and she explicitly does not endorse abusive conditions. But the language of endurance has a long, ambiguous history in left-of-center politics, and it tends to function asymmetrically. Bayard Rustin was asked to endure being closeted by his movement for decades. Ella Baker was asked to endure being passed over by Martin Luther King's circle. The women of SNCC were asked to endure being told that "the only position for women in the movement is prone." In each case, "endurance" was the name given to a coalition's refusal to do its own internal work, and the cost was paid by those whose other options were thinnest.

The essay's framing of deference politics inherits, I think, an analytic move that deserves more scrutiny. Olúfemi Táíwò's Elite Capture, which Inouye cites approvingly, made an important argument: that "centering the most marginalized" can become a rhetorical maneuver by which elite members of marginalized groups capture institutional space in the name of those whom they do not actually represent. This is true and worth saying. But the critique of deference politics has been picked up by a much broader set of voices, many of whom are less interested in elite capture than in dismantling any practice that asks privileged participants to defer to anyone, ever. The result is that "anti-deference" has come to mean, in some quarters, the restoration of the unmarked organizer — usually but not always white, male, and credentialed — to the position of authoritative judgment, with "epistemic humility" serving as a polite cover.

I do not think Inouye intends this. Her invocation of standpoint theory is careful, and she explicitly insists that organizing must "take seriously the essential insight of standpoint theory — that social location influences what we can know about the social world." But the essay's practical conclusions — endure, stay in the room, value conflict — apply with much less symmetry than the theory requires. The white PMC member who feels chastened by a conversation about race and decides to "stay" has, in most cases, suffered nothing. The Black or brown comrade who is asked to keep teaching that white member, week after week, often suffers a great deal. Both endurances are framed as solidarity. They are not the same.

What would a less symmetric account look like? It would start by recognizing that the costs of cross-difference organizing fall unevenly, and that solidarity practices have to account for that unevenness explicitly rather than wave at it through abstractions. The young white mother in the Bob Lee footage was lifted to her feet and asked to speak — yes. But Lee did that work as a leader with structural authority and political clarity, in a meeting that the Black Panthers had a strategic interest in organizing. He did not ask Black members of the Illinois Panthers to do extra labor to make white Appalachians comfortable. The Panthers brought their analysis to Uptown; they did not abandon it. The Rainbow Coalition was an offer of partnership conditional on a shared program, not an open-ended invitation to mutual transformation.

This conditionality is what I find missing in Inouye's framework. She presents endurance as a near-universal good, modulated only by the warning that organizations need not last forever. But endurance without conditions is just absorption. The Black women who left SNCC, the queer comrades who left the early gay liberation movements when those movements failed to grapple with race, the disabled organizers who have repeatedly told us that "showing up" is not a neutral expectation — these are the people whose leaving has often been the necessary precondition for movements to change. To valorize endurance without also valorizing the strategic exit is to load the dice in favor of those whose comfort the room is built around.

I want to suggest a different image. Instead of endurance as the master virtue, think of conditional commitment with structured accountability. I will stay in this organization as long as it is willing to do its own internal work, take direction from those it claims to represent, and accept that some kinds of harm are not raw material for my political education but reasons for me to leave. Solidarity organizations should make their own accountability practices legible enough that exit becomes a meaningful threat — not because we want people to leave, but because the credible possibility of departure is what disciplines coalitions to take their internal contradictions seriously.

This is not a counsel of fragility. The organizers I most admire, including many of my Black feminist forebears, were not fragile. They worked across enormous differences and absorbed enormous costs. But they did so within frameworks of accountability that they themselves had built, and they were willing to walk when those frameworks were violated. The Combahee River Collective Statement is a document of solidarity, but it is also a document of exit — the recognition that the existing organizations had failed Black lesbian socialists, and that a new formation was required.

So my friendly amendment to Inouye's essay would be this: cultivate endurance, yes, but cultivate it together with the capacities for accountability and for principled departure. Ask not only "how do we stay?" but "on what terms?" Make those terms explicit. Build organizations that can be left without catastrophe, because the credible possibility of being left is what holds them honest. And recognize that the asymmetry of cost is the thing solidarity has to address, not the thing it asks its most burdened participants to endure quietly. Otherwise "Solidarity Now" becomes another name for "patience, please."

  2. 02The critique of 'deference politics' can be co-opted to restore the authority of privileged organizers under the guise of 'epistemic humility.'
