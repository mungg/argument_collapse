Diversified Gemini

"While Mie Inouye’s framework of social endurance and epistemic humility provides a vital alternative to fragile deference politics in the climate movement, its emphasis on slow transformation must be accelerated to meet the urgent timeline of the climate crisis."

Mie Inouye’s reflection on the convergence of climate catastrophe, racial capitalism, and the mundane reality of organizing provides a necessary provocation for the climate movement. As we face an existential threat that requires a mass, multiracial, cross-class movement, the internal dynamics of our coalitions have never been more critical. Inouye’s critique of deference politics and her call for "social endurance" offer valuable lessons for climate organizers who are often paralyzed by the very divides she describes.

The climate movement has long struggled with the gap between the professional-managerial class (PMC) and the working class. The movement has historically been dominated by white, middle-class environmentalists who prioritize carbon taxes and conservation, while working-class communities—often communities of color—face the immediate brunt of toxic pollution, extreme weather, and economic precarity. When these groups attempt to coalesce, the result is often the fragile "deference politics" Inouye critiques, where the PMC guiltily defers to "frontline communities" in rhetoric, while maintaining control of the NGOs and funding streams in practice.

Inouye suggests that solidarity requires more than deference; it requires the visceral recognition that "this shit is killing you, too." For the climate movement, this is not an abstract metaphor. The climate crisis is the ultimate equalizer, even if its impacts are currently distributed unevenly. The PMC may be able to retreat to air-conditioned homes during a heatwave, but they cannot escape the collapse of global food systems, the proliferation of pandemics, or the cascading failure of insurance markets.

By applying Inouye’s framework, we can see that climate solidarity must be based on this shared, material vulnerability. We must move beyond the paternalistic idea that the privileged are "helping" the marginalized. As Fred Moten puts it, the coalition emerges from the recognition that "it's fucked up for you... in the same way that it's fucked up for us."

However, Inouye’s emphasis on "endurance" and the slow, temporal process of building solidarity through endless meetings feels dangerously misaligned with the ticking clock of the climate crisis. We do not have generations to slowly transform each other through productive conflict. We have a rapidly closing window to decarbonize the global economy.

Therefore, our "social endurance" must be fiercely targeted. We cannot afford to indulge in the "unrealistic conflict" of personality clashes and purity tests. The climate movement must adopt Inouye’s "epistemic humility"—recognizing that no single group has all the answers—but we must combine it with an unrelenting focus on taking and wielding state power. We need to stay in the room with people we disagree with, not just to learn and grow, but because we physically cannot stop the fossil fuel industry without them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The climate movement currently suffers from a divide between the professional-managerial class and working-class communities that is often masked by performative deference rather than true solidarity.
  2. 02True climate solidarity requires a visceral recognition of shared material vulnerability, moving away from the paternalistic notion of 'helping' toward a realization that the crisis threatens everyone.
  3. 03Inouye’s model of slow, temporal transformation through meetings is potentially misaligned with the rapid decarbonization timeline required to address the climate emergency.
  4. 04Social endurance in the climate context must be strictly disciplined to avoid 'unrealistic conflict' and purity tests that distract from the primary goal of wielding state power.
  5. 05Epistemic humility should be utilized as a tool for strategic cooperation with disagreeable allies because the fossil fuel industry cannot be defeated without a broad, functional coalition.
