"The current crisis in American democracy is not caused by populist movements or legislative gridlock, but by an entrenched political elite and an unaccountable administrative state that have ignored the will of the people."

Ira Katznelson’s "Anxieties of Democracy" is a perfect encapsulation of the establishment elite’s profound disconnect from the American people. Watching the Capitol undergo repairs, Katznelson frets that the legislature inside is paralyzed, thwarted by "partisan division" and a lack of "civic virtue." He decries movements like the Tea Party as dangerous, populist end-runs around the majestic "dignity of legislation." But from the perspective of millions of frustrated Americans, Katznelson has the story entirely backward. The legislature is not paralyzed because it lacks virtue; it is paralyzed because the people have finally recognized that the system is rigged, and they are demanding their country back.

Katznelson looks back to the mid-20th century as a golden era of democratic functionality, when a "background consensus" allowed representatives to easily manage conflict. But what was that consensus? It was a cozy cartel of professional politicians, bureaucrats, and corporate interests who agreed on a fundamental trajectory: an ever-expanding federal government, higher taxes, endless foreign interventions, and the slow erosion of traditional American values. For decades, the two parties engaged in a polite, managed conflict that ultimately delivered the same results—the managed decline of the middle class and the enrichment of the coasts at the expense of the heartland.

When Katznelson complains about "gridlock," what he really means is that the elite consensus has finally been challenged. The rise of the Tea Party in 2009 was not an attempt to destroy representative democracy; it was a desperate attempt to save it from an executive branch and a complicit Congress that were ramming through unpopular, transformative legislation like Obamacare and massive bank bailouts against the clear will of the majority.

Katznelson relies on Nadia Urbinati to argue that populism privileges "will over opinion" and seeks to sidestep normal liberal democracy. This is a patronizing dismissal of legitimate citizen grievances. The Tea Party did not seek to abolish the legislature; it sought to *elect better representatives*. Grassroots conservatives organized, campaigned, and voted to send people to Washington who would actually stand up to the uniparty machine. If those new representatives refuse to "compromise" (which is Washington-speak for surrendering to the leftward drift), that is not a failure of democracy; that is representation working exactly as intended.

The true threat to liberal democracy, which Katznelson barely touches upon, is the administrative state—the vast, unelected bureaucracy that now makes the vast majority of our laws. Congress has abdicated its constitutional duty, happily passing vague statutes and letting unaccountable agencies dictate the details of American life, from environmental regulations that kill coal jobs to healthcare mandates that violate religious liberty. The people are right to be anxious. They realize that their votes matter less and less because the real power has been shifted out of the hands of elected representatives and into the hands of technocrats and federal judges.

Katznelson warns of the risk of "Peronism," suggesting that demands for direct action might lead to authoritarianism. But what could be more authoritarian than an elite class that refuses to listen to the people? When the political establishment consistently ignores the demands of the working and middle classes regarding immigration enforcement, trade deals that ship jobs overseas, and a suffocating regulatory state, they should not be surprised when the people demand more forceful champions.

We do not need a return to the "policy neutrality" or the genteel "moral ambiguity" that Katznelson advocates. We need representatives who have the courage to draw hard lines and fight for the constitutional principles this country was founded upon. The scaffolding on the Capitol is fitting: the building needs repair, but the real rot is the entrenched political class inside. Democracy will not be saved by the experts managing our decline more smoothly, but by the people reclaiming their sovereignty.
