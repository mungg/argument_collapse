"While Peter Singer’s introduction to effective altruism is a valuable recruitment tool, the movement must evolve beyond its initial focus on global health to prioritize animal welfare, long-term existential risks, and a more transparent acknowledgment of its own epistemic uncertainty."

**Yes, And: Where Singer's Argument Needs to Go Next**

I write as someone broadly inside the effective altruism community — I have donated through GiveWell, attended EA Global, and argued with friends about cause prioritization on the relevant forums. Peter Singer's essay is, in many ways, the public face we want: clear, generous, recruiting. But it is also, I think, a slightly out-of-date face. The version of EA Singer describes is the version of five years ago, before the movement's center of gravity began to shift. A response essay is a chance to say what is changing and why it matters.

The clearest shift is away from global health and toward two other priorities: animal welfare and the long-term future. Singer mentions both in passing. He notes that animal suffering counts, and he lists "reducing risks of human extinction" as a cause that effective altruists debate. But the order of presentation in his essay — bed nets first, the rest as footnote — no longer matches the order of arguments inside the movement. Many of the people doing the most rigorous cause-prioritization work, at organizations like the Open Philanthropy Project, the Centre for Effective Altruism, and the Future of Humanity Institute, have concluded that on expected-value grounds, work to reduce risks from advanced artificial intelligence, engineered pandemics, or nuclear war may dominate global health by orders of magnitude, because the future contains enormously many potential lives. Whether one finds this argument compelling or troubling, it is now central, and any essay introducing EA that doesn't grapple with it is doing readers a disservice.

The shift toward animals is, if anything, even more practically important. By any honest accounting of suffering, the roughly 70 billion land animals slaughtered each year for food — most of them in conditions of extreme confinement and pain — represent a vast moral catastrophe that no extant human-welfare intervention can match in scale. The marginal cost of preventing severe suffering through corporate cage-free campaigns, broiler welfare reforms, or alternative-protein research appears, on current estimates, lower than the marginal cost of preventing equivalent human suffering through global health. Singer pioneered the philosophical case for animal consideration in 1975, and he would not deny any of this. But his essay leads with bed nets because bed nets are easier to sell. The movement has earned the right to a harder sell.

Second, I want to defend earning to give against the predictable critique that it is just dressed-up greed. Critics will say: shouldn't Matt Wage have become a public-defender attorney, a development economist, a teacher? In some cases yes — direct work, when one has rare skills for it, often dominates earning to give. The movement's own career-advice organization, 80,000 Hours, has been increasingly explicit about this: most high-impact people should not earn to give, because there are not enough good direct-work people to fill the roles. But for someone with no specific direct-work skills and a real comparative advantage in finance, the calculation is different. A donor giving $200,000 a year to AMF really does fund a great deal of bed-net distribution that would otherwise not happen. The system-level critique — that finance is harmful, that Wage shouldn't be there — has weight, but it cuts both ways: if the trade goes to someone else with Wage's seat, Wage's exit doesn't change the harm, only the donations.

Third, on epistemic humility. The most fair criticism of EA in 2015 is that it overclaims its certainties. Singer's essay slides between two registers: the radical Socratic challenge ("we should do the most good we can") and the technocratic recommendation ("therefore donate to AMF"). The first is hard to deny. The second is much harder to defend than we sometimes admit. Real cause prioritization is performed under enormous uncertainty about causal chains, about whether numbers from one context generalize to another, about long-run effects we cannot model. The movement is at its best when it openly displays its uncertainty — when GiveWell publishes the spreadsheets, when 80,000 Hours updates its recommendations, when forum debates surface the cruxes of disagreement. It is at its worst when it talks as if the leaderboard is settled.

If effective altruism continues to mature — taking animal suffering seriously, taking the long-term future seriously, and taking its own uncertainty seriously — it has a chance to become more than a giving guide. It can become an actual research program for figuring out what doing good consists in. That is a more ambitious and more interesting project than Singer's introductory essay quite reveals.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement's center of gravity is shifting toward long-termism, where reducing existential risks like advanced AI or engineered pandemics is seen as having a higher expected value than traditional global health interventions.
  2. 02Addressing the vast scale of suffering in industrial animal agriculture offers a more cost-effective opportunity for moral impact than many human-centric interventions.
  3. 03Earning to give remains a valid ethical choice for individuals with a comparative advantage in high-earning fields, despite the movement's increasing emphasis on the importance of direct work.
  4. 04Effective altruism must maintain epistemic humility by openly acknowledging the significant uncertainties and complex causal chains involved in determining the 'most' good.
  5. 05The movement should transition from being a simple giving guide to becoming a rigorous, ambitious research program dedicated to defining the nature of doing good.
