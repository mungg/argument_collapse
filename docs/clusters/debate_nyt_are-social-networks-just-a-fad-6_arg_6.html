Diversified DeepSeek

"Whether social networks are a fad depends on their function, with performance-based personal networks likely to decline while utility-based professional networks become permanent fixtures of modern life."

The answer depends entirely on which social network and for whom. For the casual user broadcasting vacation photos to a vast, loose collection of “friends,” the current model is probably a fad. It already feels like a digital treadmill that generates more anxiety than connection. The prompt’s mention of estranged family members painfully learning news through a screen highlights this hollow quality. That version of social networking—a performance of intimacy rather than the thing itself—will likely wither as people seek more authentic, private spaces. The mass exodus of teens to more ephemeral or niche platforms is a leading indicator.

But for professional life, social networks are no fad at all; they are the new resume and Rolodex combined. LinkedIn’s steady, unglamorous growth shows that when the value proposition is clear—jobs, contacts, business deals—users will build permanent habits. You don’t quit the platform that gets you hired. The distinction is between the social network as a glittering public square for personal life and as a utility for working life. The former may well be a bubble, while the latter is just modern networking. Treating them as one phenomenon is the real error. So Facebook may fade; LinkedIn, or something like it, will become as mundane and essential as email.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Casual social networking for a broad audience is a fad because it creates more anxiety and hollow performance than genuine connection.
  2. 02The migration of younger users to niche or ephemeral platforms indicates that the current mass-market model of personal social media is unsustainable.
  3. 03Professional social networks are not a fad because they provide clear, essential value as a modern replacement for resumes and business contacts.
  4. 04Utility-driven platforms like LinkedIn foster permanent habits because they function as essential tools for employment and career growth rather than mere entertainment.
