Vanilla Claude

"While reclaiming a constitutional 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is a vital diagnostic and rhetorical step for progressives, it will remain ineffective unless it is paired with aggressive institutional court reform and a more critical engagement with the racial exclusions inherent in that historical tradition."

Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet have produced one of the most bracing diagnoses of the contemporary liberal predicament I have read in a long while. Their core observation—that mid-century liberals tried to wall off both constitutional interpretation and economic policy from democratic politics, and that conservatives never accepted the bargain—is correct, and it explains an enormous amount about how we got here. The "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition they recover from the Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Dealers is a genuinely usable past, and their three-part program (reclaiming the First Amendment, re-fusing race and political economy, repoliticizing antitrust and corporate law) sketches the outlines of a constitutional politics worth fighting for. And yet I want to press on three points where their argument feels too confident, or too thin, given what the next few years look likely to demand.

First, the asymmetry problem. The essay treats the diffusion of constitutional argument into public debate as a kind of equalizer: if progressives just stop ceding the field to courts and lawyers, the people themselves can reshape what the Constitution means. But the conservative legal movement spent fifty years doing exactly this work *while also* capturing the federal judiciary and the Republican Party's judicial-selection apparatus. They did not merely make better arguments at town halls; they built the Federalist Society, packed lower courts, blockaded Merrick Garland, and rammed through three Trump appointees. Popular constitutionalism without institutional power is a sermon. The lead essay is right that not speaking about the Constitution cedes ground to courts—but speaking about it without the votes to confirm judges, the seats to expand the bench, or the procedural willingness to strip jurisdiction simply enriches the rhetorical environment in which a 6-3 majority continues to do whatever it likes. The Dobbs draft leaked earlier this month is a fair preview. The authors mention judicial reform mostly to dismiss restorationist nostalgia, but they say strikingly little about court expansion, term limits, or jurisdiction-stripping—all of which would be the muscle behind the rhetoric they propose.

Second, the historical tradition is more equivocal than the essay lets on. The Jacksonian anti-oligarchy tradition was inseparable from Indian removal and the herrenvolk democracy of the white republic; its "many" was conspicuously not everyone. The New Deal labor settlement, as the authors themselves know, was bought partly with the exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers, a concession to Southern Democrats that helped lock in Black economic subordination for another generation. Reconstruction is the cleanest precedent, and even there the political economy program (land redistribution, federal enforcement) was abandoned within a decade. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is real, but it is also a tradition of partial coalitions and racial bargains. Reviving it as constitutional argument requires confronting, not just incorporating, that history—otherwise the appeal to "American traditions of constitutional argument" risks reproducing the very compromises that made those traditions politically viable in the first place. The authors gesture at this in their section on race and the ACA, but the tension between coalitional politics and racial justice deserves more weight than it gets.

Third, the shield-versus-sword distinction is more porous than the essay suggests. The authors say conservatives wield constitutional political economy as a sword to strike down legislation, while progressives should use it as a shield to defend legislation. This is rhetorically attractive but probably unstable. If progressives genuinely believe that something like the ACA is constitutionally *required*, that wealth concentration violates the anti-oligarchy commitments of the Constitution, that the First Amendment imposes affirmative duties on the state to build countervailing worker power—then those claims have offensive implications too. They imply, for example, that a state's failure to expand Medicaid is itself unconstitutional, or that Taft-Hartley's restrictions on secondary boycotts are constitutional violations Congress must repeal. Once you say the Constitution *requires* a certain political economy, you have armed future courts—including hostile ones—to enforce that requirement on their terms. The Lochner Court was, in its own self-understanding, defending a constitutional political economy. The history the authors recover is precisely a history of legislators arguing against judicial supremacy, but that argument only worked when the elected branches were strong enough to face the Court down. It is not obvious that "departmentalism" is a stable equilibrium rather than a transitional posture en route to either court reform or court capitulation.

None of this is fatal to the project. The authors are right that liberals' technocratic retreat has been a strategic disaster, that the First Amendment has been weaponized in ways that demand a constitutional response and not just a doctrinal one, and that antitrust and corporate law are constitutional terrain whether or not we admit it. The PRO Act, H.R. 1, a robust wealth tax with backstops—these are exactly the kinds of measures progressives should be pushing, and pushing in explicitly constitutional terms. But the constitutional argument needs to be paired with the institutional argument it has been studiously avoiding. Make progressive politics constitutional again, yes. But also: reform the Court, or be prepared to watch the constitutional argument be answered by five robed men.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Popular constitutional arguments lack efficacy without the institutional power to back them up, necessitating measures like court expansion or jurisdiction-stripping to counter the current conservative judicial majority.
  2. 02The historical 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is morally compromised by its roots in racial exclusion and white populism, requiring a more rigorous confrontation with these failures than the lead essay provides.
  3. 03The distinction between using the Constitution as a 'shield' versus a 'sword' is unstable, as claiming a specific political economy is constitutionally required inevitably invites judicial intervention and offensive legal claims.
  4. 04The authors' proposal for 'departmentalism'—where branches of government compete over constitutional meaning—only works if the elected branches are strong enough to actively face down or reform a hostile Supreme Court.
