"While dethroning fossil capital is necessary, climate strategy must move beyond domestic state politics to address the geopolitical reality where fossil fuels are strategic assets in a competitive international system."

Táíwò’s essay is a welcome corrective to climate politics that imagines moral appeal or technological diffusion can substitute for power. But the power that matters is not only fossil capital’s power over domestic states. It is also the power of states in a competitive international system. Climate strategy must be geopolitical, or it will be naïve.

The climate crisis unfolds in a world of rivalry: the United States and China compete over technology, trade, security, and influence; Russia uses fossil exports and war as instruments of statecraft; petrostates seek to preserve revenue and relevance; Europe tries to reconcile decarbonization with industrial competitiveness and energy security; India seeks development space; many African and Latin American states seek infrastructure, finance, and bargaining power. In such a world, “dethrone fossil capital” is necessary but underspecified. Fossil fuels are not only commodities owned by corporations. They are strategic assets.

Russia’s invasion of Ukraine made this plain. Europe’s dependence on Russian gas constrained its politics, funded Moscow’s state, and forced a rapid rethinking of energy security. The resulting scramble included both accelerated renewables and new LNG infrastructure. Climate advocates were right to warn against lock-in, but any politically serious account must recognize why states panicked: energy shortages can topple governments, damage industry, and fracture alliances. A transition that cannot provide reliability and security will lose to fossil realism.

This does not refute Táíwò’s argument; it strengthens one part of it. The state is indispensable because only states can coordinate energy security at scale. But it also complicates the idea that the main obstacle is private fossil capital. In many cases the obstacle is a national security establishment that regards fossil fuels as insurance, a finance ministry that relies on fossil revenue, or a foreign policy apparatus that sees energy exports as leverage. Saudi Arabia, Russia, Iran, the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, and other producers are not merely homes to fossil firms. Their state projects are built around hydrocarbons. Even the United States, with its private oil majors, has long treated energy abundance as a pillar of geopolitical power.

A serious climate strategy must therefore make clean energy geopolitically superior to fossil energy. That means clean systems must be reliable, affordable, secure against coercion, and compatible with industrial strength. If decarbonization is framed only as sacrifice, it will fail in the face of hard-power politics. If it is framed as liberation from volatile fuel markets, petro-authoritarianism, air pollution, and fragile supply lines, it can win.

The good news is that renewables have geopolitical advantages. Sun and wind are widely distributed. Once built, they do not require continuous fuel imports. Electrification can reduce exposure to oil price shocks. Batteries, demand response, transmission, geothermal, hydro, nuclear, and other firm resources can increase resilience. A country with clean domestic power is less vulnerable to pipeline sabotage, shipping chokepoints, and cartel behavior. But these advantages do not materialize automatically. They require industrial policy, supply chain strategy, grid planning, and international cooperation.

China’s role is central. China dominates large parts of solar manufacturing, battery supply chains, and processing of critical minerals. It has also built enormous renewable capacity while continuing to use vast amounts of coal. For the United States and Europe, dependence on Chinese clean technology creates political tension: rapid decarbonization favors cheap imports; industrial strategy favors domestic manufacturing and allied supply chains; security hawks warn against dependence. Climate politics cannot wish this away. If clean energy becomes another front in zero-sum rivalry, costs may rise and deployment may slow. If supply chains remain concentrated, backlash may grow. The answer is neither free-trade complacency nor green protectionism alone. It is coordinated diversification: build manufacturing capacity in many regions, including the Global South, while preserving trade flows that speed deployment.

This is one reason a purely localist climate politics is inadequate. Municipal solar and public utilities are valuable, but the transition depends on copper from Chile and Peru, nickel from Indonesia, cobalt from Congo, lithium from Australia and South America, polysilicon supply chains, semiconductor equipment, shipping routes, and standards for global industry. These are geopolitical questions. If they are not governed democratically, they will be governed by great-power competition and corporate extraction.

Táíwò mentions the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty. The analogy is useful. Fossil fuels, like nuclear weapons, pose collective danger and are bound up with state power. But nonproliferation regimes work only when they address security concerns and inequalities. A fossil treaty that simply tells producers to stop will not succeed. It must include compensation, development alternatives, verification, and differentiated obligations. Wealthy producers should move first. Poorer countries should receive finance and technology. Petrostates need pathways to fiscal transition. Otherwise they will obstruct, cheat, or form counter-coalitions.

Climate clubs may also have a role. Groups of countries could agree on common industrial standards for steel, cement, aluminum, shipping, aviation fuels, methane, and clean technology supply chains, using trade access as an incentive. Done badly, this becomes green imperialism. Done well, it creates markets for low-carbon goods and prevents laggards from undercutting action. The design question is distributive: are poorer countries given finance and time to comply, or merely punished? A just climate club would include technology transfer, concessional finance, and representation for developing economies.

The military dimension cannot be ignored. Armed forces are large fossil consumers, and wars are ecological disasters. Yet defense establishments are often exempt from climate scrutiny. They also shape technological systems and infrastructure. A climate movement that ignores militarism misses both emissions and political power. At the same time, simply demanding demilitarization will not persuade states facing real threats. The near-term task is to force transparency, reduce unnecessary military activity, decarbonize bases and logistics where possible, and challenge threat inflation that justifies endless expansion. Long-term climate stability requires peace, but peace requires institutions capable of managing conflict.

Adaptation will also become geopolitical. As warming intensifies, states will respond to migration, food shocks, water stress, and disasters. The danger is “climate security” defined as border militarization and resource hoarding. Europe’s treatment of migrants, the United States’ southern border politics, and rising xenophobic parties already show the pattern. Fossil capital may fuel authoritarianism, as Táíwò argues, but climate impacts themselves can strengthen the right if states respond with fear. A humane geopolitical climate strategy must include migration rights, disaster finance, food cooperation, and conflict prevention. Otherwise decarbonization may coexist with fortress politics.

Where does this leave the state? More central than ever, but not as a unitary savior. States must be pressured by movements, disciplined by law, and coordinated internationally. The relevant arena is not only national elections but trade ministries, development banks, standards bodies, utility commissions, central banks, port authorities, military procurement offices, and multilateral negotiations. Climate politics must become fluent in these institutions without being captured by them.

Táíwò criticizes the Biden administration for relying on incentives to private capital rather than building a “big green state.” There is truth in that. But from a geopolitical perspective, the Inflation Reduction Act also represents an important shift: the United States finally treated clean energy as industrial strategy. Its flaws are real—too much reliance on private investors, too little public ownership, insufficient attention to demand reduction, and trade tensions with allies. But it marks a departure from the idea that climate policy is merely environmental regulation. Other states have responded with their own industrial plans. This competitive dynamic could become wasteful nationalism, but it could also accelerate decarbonization if linked to cooperation and equity.

The climate movement should therefore pursue a dual strategy: confrontation with fossil incumbents and construction of a clean geopolitical order. Confrontation means ending fossil subsidies, blocking new long-lived infrastructure, regulating methane, mandating phaseout, and holding companies liable. Construction means building grids, factories, ports, transit, storage, and resilient agriculture; securing fair mineral supply chains; financing Southern development; and creating international rules that reward decarbonization. One without the other is insufficient. Anti-fossil politics without construction risks scarcity and backlash. Construction without anti-fossil politics risks additive energy growth and continued emissions.

The strongest argument for Táíwò’s two-step view is that climate change will increasingly determine the conditions of all politics. The strongest amendment is that those conditions are geopolitical as well as ecological. If democratic forces do not shape the new energy order, authoritarians, militaries, and oligarchs will. A decarbonized world could still be unjust, coercive, and violent. But a world that fails to decarbonize will almost certainly become more so.

Dethroning fossil capital is a necessary slogan. The strategic program must be larger: build a secure, democratic, internationalist energy order capable of outcompeting fossil power while protecting those whom geopolitics usually sacrifices. Climate politics must learn to speak not only the language of justice and ecology, but also the language of power—then change what power is for.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fossil fuels are not just corporate commodities but are deeply embedded in the national security, fiscal revenue, and foreign policy apparatus of rival states.
  2. 02A successful transition requires making clean energy geopolitically superior to fossil fuels by ensuring it provides greater reliability, industrial strength, and security from coercion.
  3. 03The state is the only entity capable of coordinating energy security and industrial policy at the scale required to manage global supply chains and technological competition.
  4. 04International climate agreements, such as non-proliferation treaties or climate clubs, will only succeed if they address the security concerns and developmental inequalities of the Global South.
  5. 05Climate politics must engage with hard-power institutions like trade ministries and military procurement offices to prevent the transition from being governed by zero-sum rivalry or border militarization.
  6. 06A dual strategy of confronting fossil incumbents while simultaneously constructing a democratic, internationalist energy order is required to prevent a transition that is merely additive or remains unjust.
