"Charging pre-teens as adults is a legal and moral mistake because children lack the developmental maturity and culpability of adults, regardless of the severity of their crimes."

Charging a 12-year-old as an adult is an understandable emotional response to an unbearable act, but it is almost always a legal and moral mistake. The seriousness of the harm should determine how urgently the state intervenes, not whether it pretends a child has the judgment, identity and culpability of a grown person.

By 12, a child may know that stabbing is wrong. But adult criminal punishment rests on more than bare awareness of wrongfulness. It assumes a relatively formed character, capacity to weigh consequences, resistance to pressure and fantasy, and meaningful participation in one’s defense. Developmental science and common experience both tell us pre-teens are still highly impulsive, suggestible and dependent. The Supreme Court’s youth sentencing decisions in Roper, Graham and Miller, though involving older adolescents, recognize the same principle: children are less culpable and more capable of change.

That does not mean a violent child should be sent home with counseling and a warning. Juvenile courts can and should use secure placement, psychiatric evaluation, long-term treatment and close supervision when a child poses a danger. In the most serious cases, confinement may last for years. The public has a right to protection, and victims’ families deserve a process that treats the offense with gravity.

But adult court is poorly designed for children. It increases the risk of harsh sentences, adult incarceration, stigma and procedural confusion without necessarily improving safety. The “superpredator” panic that drove many transfer laws in the 1990s has not aged well; juvenile crime has fallen, and the premise that some children are essentially irredeemable is both empirically weak and ethically dangerous.

A pre-teen who kills or maims has done something grave. The law should say so plainly. But it should also say plainly that a child is still a child.

Vanilla Claude

"Charging pre-teens as adults is unjust and indefensible because children lack the cognitive and moral maturity required for adult criminal responsibility."

Charging a 12-year-old as an adult is almost never defensible, and the impulse to do so reflects prosecutorial convenience more than justice. The premise of adult criminal law is that the defendant possesses the cognitive and moral capacities of an adult: the ability to foresee consequences, control impulses, resist peer influence, and understand the gravity of harm. By every measure we have — developmental psychology, neuroscience of the still-maturing prefrontal cortex, and the Supreme Court's own reasoning in Roper, Graham, and Miller — children that young simply do not.

The Michigan and Milwaukee cases illustrate the point rather than rebut it. A boy who stabs a playmate and then sobs into a 911 phone that he wants to die is not a calculating adult criminal; he is a child in some kind of crisis whose actions are catastrophic but whose mind is unformed. The Wisconsin girls, reportedly egged on by a fictional internet bogeyman called Slender Man, displayed exactly the magical thinking and susceptibility to suggestion that distinguishes preadolescents from adults. Treating their delusion as adult premeditation is a category error.

That doesn't mean nothing should happen. The juvenile system can confine a dangerous child, mandate intensive psychiatric treatment, and supervise him for years — in serious cases, with the possibility of extended jurisdiction into the mid-twenties. What it won't do is brand a seventh-grader a murderer for life, drop him into adult prison where the rates of sexual assault and suicide for juveniles are notoriously high, and foreclose any plausible rehabilitation. Most states still permit transfer to adult court for teenagers accused of homicide; extending that machinery down to twelve crosses a line that ought to be bright.

If we believe children are different — and our drinking, voting, driving, and contract laws all say we do — we cannot suspend that belief the moment the crime becomes terrible.

The impulse to treat pre-teens as adults in the wake of horrific violence is understandable, but it collapses justice into vengeance. Developmental neuroscience, widely accepted by 2014, tells us that the adolescent brain—particularly the prefrontal cortex governing impulse control, risk assessment, and long-term planning—is a work in progress well into the mid-twenties. In a twelve-year-old, those structures are profoundly immature. The Supreme Court recognized this reality in *Roper v. Simmons* (2005) and *Graham v. Florida* (2010), ruling that juveniles cannot be sentenced to death or to life without parole for non-homicide crimes, because their diminished culpability and greater capacity for change make them constitutionally different from adults. To apply the same logic at the charging stage is not to excuse a stabbing; it is to align the machinery of the law with the facts of human development.

Charging a pre-teen as an adult abandons the foundational premise of the juvenile court, which was built to distinguish between a child’s terrible act and an irredeemable criminal character. The adult system is retributive and largely blind to rehabilitation, exposing a young defendant to adult prisons, solitary confinement, and a permanent felony record that forecloses education and employment. For a twelve-year-old, whose identity is still pliant, that is a life sentence by other means. Furthermore, a mechanism like “direct file,” where prosecutors unilaterally move a case to adult court, removes the judicial check that is supposed to weigh individual circumstances. The result is a system driven by media frenzy rather than diagnostic assessment.

There are rare cases where older adolescents commit crimes so calculated and pathological that the rehabilitative timeline of the juvenile system may be insufficient to protect the public. But that calculus strains credibility when applied to children barely out of elementary school. The Wisconsin girls and the Michigan boy, whatever their intent, are at an age where untreated trauma, mental illness, or manipulation by the Internet figures cited in the Milwaukee case are far more plausible explanations than full-fledged adult malice. The proper response is not to throw them into a punitive adult apparatus, but to deploy intensive, secure juvenile facilities equipped with forensic psychiatry and long-term therapy. To insist on treating them as juveniles is not to be soft; it is to insist that justice demands proportionality and a honest reckoning with what a child’s mind can fathom.

"Pre-teens should not be charged as adults because their biological immaturity and capacity for rehabilitation make adult legal standards both scientifically and ethically inappropriate."

The Michigan and Wisconsin cases are undeniably disturbing, and the instinct to respond with the harshest available punishment is understandable. Yet the evidence we have about adolescent development argues strongly against charging pre-teens as adults, even in the most horrifying circumstances.

By 2014, decades of neuroscience had confirmed what parents and teachers long suspected: the adolescent brain is fundamentally different from an adult's. The prefrontal cortex—responsible for impulse control, long-term consequence assessment, and rational decision-making—does not fully mature until the mid-twenties. A twelve-year-old, regardless of the atrocity of his act, lacks the neurological equipment to fully comprehend the magnitude of his choices in the way an adult would. This is not an excuse; it is a biological fact that should inform our legal responses.

The Supreme Court recognized this reality in a series of rulings striking down extreme juvenile sentences, with Justice Kennedy writing in Roper v. Simmons that juveniles possess "a lack of maturity and an underdeveloped sense of responsibility" that results in "diminished culpability." Courts have increasingly understood that treating children as if they possessed adult cognitive capacities produces neither justice nor public safety.

When we transfer pre-teens to adult systems, we expose them to violence, strip away educational opportunities, and often consign them to permanent criminal records that preclude legitimate futures—outcomes that serve no one. The juvenile system, flawed as it is, at least maintains the possibility of genuine rehabilitation and reintegration.

This does not minimize the suffering of victims' families or suggest that accountability is inappropriate. But justice for society means proportional responses that recognize moral differences between actors. A twelve-year-old who commits a terrible act remains a child who committed it.

The question of whether we should ever charge a pre-teen as an adult misses a deeper point: our binary system itself is the problem. This allows for intensive therapy, education, and gradual transition, while protecting the community through long-term oversight. Crucially, it rejects the false certainty that we can divine a child’s future dangerousness at the moment of arrest. The 12-year-old who called 911 saying “I want to die” is crying out for psychiatric intervention, not a sentencing grid. We should build a justice response that is as nuanced as the developmental science demands, leaving neither the child’s capacity for change nor the public’s safety to chance.

The sensible fix is blended sentencing and extended juvenile jurisdiction. Minnesota, Texas, and a handful of other states already permit dispositions that begin in juvenile facilities and convert to adult supervision if the offender, as he matures, demonstrates that he remains dangerous or fails to engage in treatment. A 12-year-old who kills could be committed until age 25, with periodic review, intensive mental health treatment, education, and a meaningful pathway out — but also a meaningful pathway to continued confinement if the underlying pathology persists. This respects both the developmental science the Supreme Court endorsed in Miller v. Alabama and the legitimate public concern that some children commit acts so grave they cannot simply be sent home at 21.

The Michigan and Milwaukee cases illustrate the need for this middle path. We don't yet know whether the 12-year-old stabber is suffering from psychosis, severe abuse history, or something else. We don't yet know whether the Wisconsin girls' Slender Man obsession reflects emerging schizophrenia or ordinary preadolescent magical thinking gone terribly wrong. The honest answer in all three cases is: we need time, expertise, and flexibility to find out.

Charging pre-teens as adults forecloses that inquiry by treating the verdict as if it answered the question of who the child is. It doesn't. We should build a system that can actually ask.
