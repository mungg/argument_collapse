Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that the campus protests against Israel crossed the line from protected political speech into actionable harassment because they systematically targeted and excluded Jewish students based on their identity."

In “The Right to Be Hostile,” Alex Gourevitch argues that the suppression of pro-Palestinian campus protests was an authoritarian overreach based merely on the “subjective feelings” of Jewish students. As a Jewish academic who has spent my life advocating for civil rights and holding complex, often critical views on Israel, I find Gourevitch’s abstraction of the last year’s events intellectually dishonest and deeply minimizing.

Gourevitch structures his argument around a sleight of hand. He equates the Gaza solidarity encampments with historical protests against the Vietnam War, apartheid South Africa, and for civil rights. In doing so, he frames the protests as fundamentally universalist—directed at a state policy or an institutional investment. From this premise, he argues that the discomfort of Jewish students is merely the inevitable byproduct of encountering sharp political disagreement.

But this framing ignores the specific, targeted nature of the hostility that was normalized on campuses. The protests were not merely anti-war or anti-Israel; they were intensely, systematically focused on "Zionists."

On modern university campuses, over 80% of Jewish students identify as Zionists, viewing a connection to the State of Israel as an integral part of their ethnoreligious identity. When protesters carry signs saying "Zionists are not welcome here," or physically block pathways asking individuals to state whether they are Zionists before allowing them to pass (as was documented at Columbia and UCLA), this is not a general critique of foreign policy. It is the targeted exclusion of a specific demographic minority from the public life of the university.

Gourevitch asks us to distinguish between "felt harm" and "actionable injury." He writes that a hostile environment requires "objective evidence." But what is more objective than a Jewish student being told they cannot enter an academic building unless they disavow a core tenet of their identity?

Imagine applying Gourevitch’s logic to any other minority group. If a right-wing student group set up an encampment demanding the deportation of undocumented immigrants, and specifically targeted Hispanic students, demanding to know their citizenship status before letting them enter the dining hall, would Gourevitch characterize this as an "in-your-face protest" that must be tolerated? Would he dismiss the Hispanic students' complaints as "self-reported feelings" born of a culture of "therapeutic consumerism"?

Of course not. We recognize intuitively that when political speech morphs into the policing of marginalized bodies in physical space, a line has been crossed.

Gourevitch minimizes the violent rhetoric of the protests, assuring us that "none of what I heard... amounted to threats of imminent and likely harm." But shouts to "Globalize the Intifada"—a term referencing a period of suicide bombings targeting civilians on buses and in cafes—cannot be hand-waved away as mere "impolite speech." When Jewish community centers (Hillels) are targeted for picketing, protesters are not attacking the State of Israel; they are attacking the institutional home of Jews on campus.

The application of Title VI was not a right-wing conspiracy led by Christopher Rufo, as Gourevitch conveniently suggests. It was a desperate attempt by Jewish students to remind universities that they, too, are entitled to equal access to their education. Gourevitch’s demand that we tolerate "hostility" is a demand that Jewish students accept being treated as second-class citizens on their own campuses.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The protests were not merely critiques of state policy but were directed at 'Zionists,' a term that applies to the vast majority of Jewish students for whom it is an integral part of their ethnoreligious identity.
  2. 02Physical actions, such as blocking pathways and demanding students disavow their identity to enter campus buildings, constitute objective discriminatory exclusion rather than just subjective discomfort.
  3. 03The use of violent rhetoric, such as calls to 'Globalize the Intifada,' and the picketing of Jewish community centers represent credible threats rather than mere impolite speech.
  4. 04Applying a standard of absolute tolerance to these protests creates a double standard that would never be accepted if other marginalized groups were being targeted and policed in physical spaces.
  5. 05The invocation of Title VI was a necessary measure to ensure Jewish students maintain equal access to education, rather than a right-wing conspiracy to suppress academic freedom.
Diversified Gemini

"The recent campus crackdowns were not an act of political suppression but a necessary fulfillment of the university's legal and moral mandate to ensure equal educational access under Title VI."

Alex Gourevitch’s impassioned defense of campus protests rests on a fundamental category error: he conflates the public square with the educational environment. In his attempt to shield the pro-Palestinian encampments of the past year from administrative discipline, Gourevitch waves away the application of Title VI of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 as a weaponization of “subjective feelings.” But a university campus is not Hyde Park. It is a federally funded educational institution, and the legal standards governing it are not designed to maximize political expression at all costs, but to ensure equal access to education.

Gourevitch argues that for a “hostile environment” to exist, there must be objective evidence of severe and systematic mistreatment, not just “feelings of unease.” He is correct on the legal standard, but entirely wrong on the facts of what occurred on American campuses over the last eighteen months. He sanitizes the reality of the encampments, portraying them as mildly inconvenient gatherings where the worst offense was an excess of noise.

In reality, the “disruption” was not merely spatial; it was discriminatory. At numerous institutions, student protesters physically blocked walkways and building entrances, explicitly denying access to students who refused to disavow “Zionism”—a core component of the ethnoreligious identity of the vast majority of Jewish students. When protesters create checkpoints on a campus quad, demanding ideological purity tests before allowing peers to pass to the library or the dining hall, they have not merely engaged in “in-your-face protest.” They have altered the terms and conditions of a minority group’s educational access.

Under Title VI, universities are legally obligated to prevent and remediate hostile environments based on race, color, or national origin. The Department of Education’s Office for Civil Rights has long held that when harassment is so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it effectively denies a student equal access to an institution’s resources, the university must act. Gourevitch dismisses the invocation of this standard as a capitulation to right-wing pressure and “donor-beholden administrators.” But the obligation to protect students from discriminatory harassment is a progressive achievement, born of the civil rights movement.

Gourevitch suggests that only "credible threats of imminent harm" should trigger university intervention. This is the standard for criminal assault, not civil rights compliance. If a group of white nationalist students set up an encampment in the center of a university, shouting racial slurs and blocking Black students from entering the student union, no serious academic would argue that the university should wait for an “imminent physical attack” before dismantling the tents. We would immediately recognize that the encampment itself constitutes a discriminatory deprivation of educational access.

The right to protest is essential to a democratic society. But universities are specialized environments with a specific mission. When political expression metastasizes into the monopolization of shared educational spaces and the targeted harassment of specific student demographics, administrators are not committing “McCarthyism” by stepping in. They are fulfilling their legal and moral mandate to ensure that the university remains open to everyone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The author incorrectly conflates the open public square with the specialized, federally regulated educational environment of a university campus.
  2. 02The Gaza encampments went beyond political expression by physically blocking access to campus facilities and imposing ideological purity tests on Jewish students.
  3. 03Title VI compliance requires universities to intervene when harassment is severe and pervasive enough to deny students equal access to resources, regardless of whether physical violence is imminent.
  4. 04The obligation to remediate a hostile environment is a civil rights achievement intended to protect marginalized groups from discriminatory deprivation of their education.
  5. 05Administrators have a duty to prevent political expression from metastasizing into the monopolization of shared spaces and the targeted harassment of specific demographics.
