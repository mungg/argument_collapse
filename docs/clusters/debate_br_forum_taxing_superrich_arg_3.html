Diversified DeepSeek

"A progressive wealth tax is a morally and democratically necessary tool to dismantle the political power of the superrich and protect the foundations of the American republic."

Saez and Zucman have performed an immense public service. In their essay, they strip away the fog of economic jargon to reveal a simple, inescapable truth: extreme wealth is not just a sign of market success; it is a political pollutant. Wealth is power, and when it accumulates beyond a certain threshold it corrodes the democratic foundations on which a free society rests. Their proposal for a progressive wealth tax, even at the radical end, is not an exercise in class envy—it is an urgent act of self-defense for the republic.

My purpose here is not to quibble with their technical apparatus, which is as robust as any that exists in contemporary public finance. Nor do I intend to spend much time rescuing the wealth tax from the familiar chorus of objections. Instead, I want to strengthen the democratic case they open with, and to argue that the moment we inhabit—here in March 2020, as markets lurch and the public purse is being opened to stave off a pandemic-driven collapse—makes the wealth tax not just desirable but morally obligatory.

Begin with Madison, whom the authors quote to great effect. When he wrote of reducing extreme wealth toward “a state of mediocrity,” he was not suggesting that everyone should be equally poor. He was articulating the classical republican anxiety that a permanent oligarchy would capture the instruments of government and govern in its own interest. That anxiety is no longer theoretical. A generation of political science research has mapped the ways in which policy outcomes in the United States track the preferences of the affluent while bearing almost no relation to the desires of the middle class and the poor. The mechanism is not simply campaign contributions, though those are pernicious enough. It is the capacity of the superrich to shape the intellectual climate, to fund think tanks, to own media platforms, to threaten primary challenges against legislators who stray. In short, to make the entire political class aware that the price of crossing the donor class is career death.

No tweak to campaign finance law will solve this. The Supreme Court’s jurisprudence, from Buckley to Citizens United, has effectively constitutionalized plutocracy by equating money with speech. Until that changes, we must attack the problem at its source: the vast pools of private capital that can be weaponized at will. The wealth tax is exactly such an attack. By steadily chipping away at fortunes above a billion dollars at rates that approach ten percent, it would over time reduce the stock of plutocratic ammunition. That is why, as Saez and Zucman note, a radical wealth tax raises less revenue in the long run than a moderate one: its purpose is not to fill government coffers but to shrink the number and power of the oligarchs.

The objection that this would harm economic growth deserves a reply. The authors point out—correctly—that the mid-twentieth century, when top marginal income tax rates exceeded ninety percent, was a period of robust broad-based growth. But more fundamentally, the growth argument assumes that the marginal dollar of wealth held by a centibillionaire does more for the economy than the same dollar distributed more broadly. That assumption has no empirical basis. What we know is that when capital is hyper-concentrated it chases rent-seeking rather than productive investment. The superrich lobby for patent extensions, regulatory moats, and financial engineering that do nothing to raise total factor productivity. A progressive wealth tax that compels billionaires to sell assets, pay taxes, and fund public goods would reallocate resources toward real innovation, infrastructure, education, and health. In doing so it would likely increase the economy’s productive capacity, not diminish it.

Implementation concerns are real but overstated. The authors’ ingenious proposal to allow payment in kind—shares of private companies—solves both the valuation and the liquidity problems at a stroke. If the owners of Cargill think the IRS has overvalued their business, let them hand over two percent of their equity and watch the market sort it out. Suddenly, the opacity around private fortunes disappears, replaced by the very price discovery that free-marketeers claim to revere. This is not a government takeover; it is the creation of a market where none currently exists, a beautifully Hayekian solution to a Hayekian complaint. And if the tax is confined to the top 0.1 percent, the administrative burden on the rest of us is zero.

As for the charge that the wealth tax is unconstitutional because it is a “direct tax” not apportioned by state population: that reading of the Constitution is a relic of a nineteenth century the Supreme Court has already abandoned. The Sixteenth Amendment cleared the way for an income tax without apportionment. A wealth tax, properly structured, is no more a capitation than the estate tax, which has been upheld repeatedly. Legal scholars from across the spectrum have argued that a wealth tax passes constitutional muster, and if a future court disagrees, that court will have revealed itself as a partisan check on democracy, not a neutral arbiter.

There is a final reason to act now, on this seventeen of March two thousand twenty. The economy is entering a sharp downturn, and the federal government is preparing to inject trillions of dollars of liquidity into the financial system through the Federal Reserve and fiscal stimulus. If history is any guide, a large portion of that liquidity will end up inflating asset prices, enriching those who already own large portfolios. The pandemic will likely widen the wealth gap, not narrow it. Imposing a wealth tax as the recovery takes hold would ensure that the costs of the crisis are not borne by the most vulnerable while the spoils again flow to the top. It would be a solemn assertion that we are all in this together—not just in rhetoric, but in the distribution of sacrifice and gain.

Saez and Zucman have given us the blueprint. The question is whether we have the collective will to build. If we do not, we should stop pretending that we live in a democracy and admit that we have settled for oligarchy with periodic elections. I prefer to believe that the American experiment is not yet finished, and that a wealth tax is one of the tools that can renew it.

  2. 02Because the Supreme Court has equated money with speech, the only effective way to curb plutocratic influence is to reduce the actual stock of private capital that can be weaponized against democracy.
  3. 03Hyper-concentrated capital encourages unproductive rent-seeking and lobbying for regulatory moats rather than the productive investment and innovation that drive broad-based economic growth.

  6. 06The economic fallout of the COVID-19 pandemic makes a wealth tax an urgent moral obligation to ensure the costs of the crisis are shared equitably rather than further enriching asset owners.

"A progressive wealth tax is a necessary tool to protect democratic equality by dismantling the dangerous concentration of political power held by the ultra-wealthy."

The Madison Doctrine: Why Extreme Wealth Is Incompatible with Democratic Equality

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman have rendered a valuable service by returning James Madison to the center of our tax policy debates. Their invocation of Madison's 1792 warnings about the political dangers of concentrated wealth should give every American pause. Here is the father of the Constitution himself, the man who more than any other architected our republican institutions, explicitly connecting extreme wealth inequality to the corruption of self-government. This is not radical left-wing propaganda; it is the foundational concern of the American political tradition.

The lead essay's central insight—that wealth is power, and extreme wealth is extreme power—demolishes the naive notion that economic inequality is merely a matter of private fortune. When Jeff Bezos can deploy his billions to influence elections, when the Koch brothers can fund a nationwide network of advocacy organizations, when a handful of families can shape the ideological landscape through their foundations and media investments, the pretense of political equality becomes a joke. We do not have one person, one vote in any meaningful sense when a single family can outspend millions of ordinary citizens combined.

What strikes me most forcibly about the Saez-Zucman analysis is their reframing of the question. Critics of progressive taxation typically ask: Is it fair to take more from the wealthy? But Madison asked a different question: Is it safe to allow such concentration of power? The fairness question admits of genuine disagreement; the safety question is empirical. Do democracies survive extreme wealth inequality? The historical record suggests they do not. The Roman Republic collapsed under the weight of aristocratic concentration. The French Revolution was triggered by feudal privilege. And Madison himself observed that "the same malignant aspect in republicanism may be traced in the inequality of fortunes."

I want to push the Saez-Zucman argument further along the democratic dimension. They correctly note that concentrated wealth distorts policy to favor the rich. But the problem runs deeper than policy outcomes. Extreme wealth inequality shapes what we can even imagine as politically possible. When the top 400 Americans own more than the bottom 60 percent of the population combined, when billions flow to think tanks and lobbying firms, when campaign finance requires hundreds of millions for competitive Senate races, the very horizon of political debate shifts rightward. Policies that would benefit 99 percent of Americans—single-payer healthcare, free public education, aggressive antitrust enforcement—become unmentionable not because they are unpopular but because the people who would benefit lack the resources to make their voices heard.

The authors' proposed solution—a progressive wealth tax starting at $50 million—is both more modest and more radical than it appears. It is modest in that it would affect fewer than 0.1 percent of families. It is radical in that it would begin to reverse four decades of bipartisan policy that has systematically transferred power upward. The authors are right that the estate tax is insufficient; waiting for billionaires to die is cold comfort when they are actively using their wealth to reshape society while alive.

I am less enthusiastic about the authors' "moderate" versus "radical" distinction. Yes, a 3 percent tax on billion-dollar fortunes would generate substantial revenue. But if the goal is democratic equality—if Madison was right that extreme wealth is inherently dangerous—then we should not flinch from more aggressive rates. The comparison to carbon taxation is instructive. We do not set carbon taxes at rates merely sufficient to "fill a gap" in the tax system; we set them at levels necessary to alter behavior and prevent catastrophic harm. Why should we treat the concentration of economic power any differently?

Some will object that such taxes would drive wealth flight or discourage entrepreneurship. The historical evidence, however, tells a different story. The highest peacetime top marginal income tax rates in American history—reaching 90 percent or more—coincided with the most broadly shared prosperity this country has ever known. The postwar economic boom, with its rising wages, expanding middle class, and democratized homeownership, occurred precisely when the tax system most aggressively constrained extreme wealth accumulation. The argument that high taxes on the rich destroy prosperity is refuted by the American experience itself.

There is, finally, a moral dimension that the authors touch on but could elaborate further. The superrich of today increasingly derive their fortunes not from creating value but from extracting it. Financial engineering, patent thickets, regulatory capture, monopoly pricing—these are the engines of contemporary billionaire wealth. When Mark Zuckerberg can exploit network effects and data monopolies to accumulate $60 billion while social media tears the social fabric apart, when pharma executives can price essential medicines beyond the reach of the dying, the case for confiscatory taxation strengthens not from envy but from justice. These fortunes are not merely the result of market rewards for value creation; they are often the product of political manipulation, regulatory failure, and social infrastructure that the wealthy did not create and do not pay for.

Madison understood this. He wanted to "withhold unnecessary opportunities from a few" and to use "the silent operation of laws" to prevent "an immoderate, and especially an unmerited, accumulation of riches." A progressive wealth tax is precisely such a law. It is time to stop treating extreme inequality as an unfortunate byproduct of success and to recognize it for what Madison knew it to be: a threat to the republic itself.

  3. 03Waiting for the estate tax to apply at death is insufficient because billionaires use their wealth to actively reshape and damage society while they are still alive.
  4. 04Wealth taxes should be set at aggressive, even 'radical' rates similar to carbon taxes in order to actively discourage the harmful behavior of extreme wealth accumulation.
  5. 05Historical evidence from the mid-twentieth century proves that high marginal tax rates on the rich are compatible with, and perhaps conducive to, broadly shared national prosperity.
  6. 06High taxes on the superrich are a matter of justice because modern billionaire fortunes are often derived from rent-seeking and value extraction rather than genuine value creation.
