What Standards Must Be Met for the U.S. to Kill an American Citizen? · Cluster 2 of 4

Main arguments in this cluster
Diversified Gemini

"The standards for the U.S. government's use of lethal force should be based on international human rights law and the laws of armed conflict rather than the citizenship status of the target."

The intense domestic outrage over the Justice Department white paper has largely focused on a remarkably parochial question: does the United States government have the right to kill an *American citizen* without trial? While the constitutional concerns regarding due process are valid, framing the debate exclusively around citizenship misses a far more profound moral and legal crisis.

Why should an American passport be the singular shield against extrajudicial execution? The standards for the use of lethal force outside of hot battlefields should be dictated by international human rights law and the laws of armed conflict, not by the nationality of the target.

The white paper suggests that outside of areas of active hostilities, like Afghanistan, the U.S. can use lethal force against suspected terrorists simply because they belong to Al Qaeda. This "global battlefield" theory allows the U.S. to fire Hellfire missiles into civilian areas in Yemen, Somalia, and Pakistan. When we do this, we frequently kill civilians and non-citizen suspects who are afforded no process whatsoever. The outrage over the targeted killing of Anwar al-Awlaki—a U.S. citizen—has overshadowed the fact that the vast majority of those killed by the drone program are foreign nationals who are equally deprived of any basic human rights before their deaths.

Under international human rights law, lethal force outside a recognized armed conflict can only be used when strictly necessary to protect against an immediate threat to life, and only when non-lethal means are unavailable. The white paper’s loose definition of "imminence" blatantly violates this international standard.

If the United States insists on conducting a covert, global drone war, the standard must be uniform and compliant with international law. We must abandon the fiction that human rights are a privilege reserved only for those holding U.S. citizenship. By focusing solely on the Fifth Amendment, we ignore the broader tragedy: the normalization of state-sponsored targeted killings across the globe, fundamentally undermining international humanitarian norms.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing exclusively on the due process rights of American citizens ignores the moral and legal crisis of extrajudicial killings of foreign nationals.
  2. 02The government's 'global battlefield' theory improperly justifies the use of lethal force in civilian areas outside of active war zones.
  3. 03The Justice Department's loose definition of an 'imminent threat' violates international standards that require lethal force to be a last resort used only when strictly necessary.
  4. 04The normalization of state-sponsored targeted killings undermines international humanitarian norms and the universal nature of human rights.
Diversified Claude

"The standards for targeted killing should be based on universal human rights and due process for all persons rather than being restricted to the narrow legal protections of American citizenship."

There is something deeply uncomfortable about treating American citizenship as the relevant moral variable in this debate. Why does the death of Anwar al-Awlaki provoke a constitutional crisis while the deaths of hundreds of Yemeni, Pakistani, and Somali nationals killed by the same drone program receive a fraction of the attention? If targeted killing is wrong, it is wrong because it kills human beings without adequate process, not because it occasionally kills the kind of human being who carries a U.S. passport.

That framing matters, because the citizenship-focused debate generates perverse legal arguments. Defenders of the program point out, correctly, that Americans who join enemy forces have historically been killable in combat. From this they conclude that the program is constitutionally sound. But this gets the analysis backwards. The proper question is not "can we kill Americans the way we kill foreigners?" It is "are we killing anyone, citizen or not, on the basis of standards we would accept if applied to ourselves?"

By that standard, the program fails badly. Signature strikes target individuals whose identities are unknown, based on behavioral patterns that fit a profile. The "imminent threat" standard, as redefined in the white paper, requires no actual imminence. The geographic scope of the conflict has no clear limit. The number of civilian casualties is contested precisely because the government counts military-age males in strike zones as combatants unless proven otherwise.

A serious framework for targeted killing — for citizens and non-citizens alike — would require: independent verification of intelligence; a meaningful, time-bound definition of imminence; published rules of engagement; transparent casualty accounting; and consequences for errors. None of this currently exists in robust form.

If we limit the conversation to American citizens, we will end up with a two-tiered system in which the procedural protections demanded for U.S. nationals serve as a fig leaf over the largely unconstrained killing of everyone else. The Constitution speaks of persons, not citizens, in the Fifth Amendment for a reason. The standards that should govern this program are universal, or they are not really standards at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing exclusively on the rights of citizens creates a perverse legal framework that ignores the moral weight of killing non-citizens without adequate process.
  2. 02The current program fails to meet acceptable standards because it relies on a redefined, non-urgent concept of 'imminent threat' and targets individuals based on behavioral profiles rather than confirmed identities.
  3. 03The government’s method of counting military-age males as combatants by default obscures the true scale of civilian casualties.
  4. 04A legitimate framework for lethal operations requires independent intelligence verification, transparent rules of engagement, and accountability for errors that apply regardless of the target's nationality.
  5. 05The Fifth Amendment’s protection of 'persons' implies that procedural safeguards against government killing are a universal requirement rather than a privilege of citizenship.
