Diversified Claude

"To prevent another Dust Bowl, the federal government must stop subsidizing environmentally destructive farming practices and instead allow farmers to utilize their own accumulated knowledge of soil stewardship."

Everyone wants to talk about federal programs, but the most important thing standing between us and another Dust Bowl is the accumulated knowledge of farmers themselves. After eighty years of trial and error, American producers know how to keep soil on the ground. The policy question is whether we let them practice that knowledge or whether we override it with subsidies that reward the opposite.

Visit a well-run dryland farm in western Kansas or the Texas Panhandle today and you will see no-till drilling into the previous year's stubble, diversified rotations that include cover crops, terraced contours, and shelterbelts inherited from the New Deal. These practices are not mandated; they make economic sense because they preserve the productive capacity of the land. The farmer who blows his topsoil into the next county is the farmer who goes broke.

The problem is that federal policy increasingly tilts the playing field against this kind of stewardship. The renewable fuel standard guarantees a market for corn at prices that make it rational to break out grassland that should never see a plow. Heavily subsidized crop insurance shifts the downside risk of farming marginal acres onto the taxpayer, encouraging cultivation in places that the market alone would leave in grass. Direct payments — finally on their way out, perhaps — historically rewarded base acres regardless of conservation practice.

If we want to prevent another Dust Bowl, the first step is to stop paying people to cause one. End the ethanol mandate, or at least uncouple it from corn. Tie crop insurance premium subsidies to compliance with strong conservation plans, including sodsaver provisions that deny coverage on newly broken native sod. Fully fund CRP and EQIP rather than treating them as the first thing to cut.

Beyond that, leave farmers alone. The men and women working the land have a stronger interest in its long-term productivity than any official in Washington. Drought will come — it always does on the Plains — but a farmer who is allowed to make sound decisions will weather it. A farmer whose decisions are distorted by Beltway politics may not, and the rest of us will breathe the consequences.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern farmers have already developed effective conservation techniques, such as no-till drilling and cover cropping, that make economic sense because they preserve land productivity.
  2. 02Current federal policies, like the renewable fuel standard and subsidized crop insurance, incentivize the cultivation of marginal grasslands that are highly susceptible to erosion.
  3. 03The government should reform agricultural policy by ending ethanol mandates and tying insurance subsidies to strict conservation compliance.
  4. 04Farmers have a greater personal stake in the long-term health of their land than federal officials and will make better ecological decisions if their choices are not distorted by political subsidies.
