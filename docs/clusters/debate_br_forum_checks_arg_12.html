"While the lead essay correctly identifies the flaws in traditional checks and balances, it goes too far in dismissing federalism, which currently serves as a vital practical defense against authoritarianism and a laboratory for progressive social reform."

Lisa Miller is right to warn against treating “checks and balances” as a magic phrase. But her skepticism toward federalism goes too far. In the current crisis, the decentralized structure of American government is not merely an antique obstacle to national progress. It is also one of the few practical defenses against an administration bent on centralizing power, intimidating opponents, and testing whether law still binds the president. Progressives should not romanticize state authority, but neither should they discard it. They need a theory of democratic federalism suited to both social reform and anti-authoritarian resistance.

The history Miller recounts is undeniable. “States’ rights” was the language of slavery, secession, Jim Crow, massive resistance, and opposition to civil rights enforcement. State variation has often meant racial hierarchy, threadbare welfare states, unequal schools, voter suppression, environmental sacrifice zones, and deadly gaps in health care. The federal government has repeatedly been necessary to secure equal citizenship. Any progressive federalism that forgets this history becomes morally unserious.

But there is a difference between venerating state sovereignty and using state institutions. The United States in 2025 is not governed from one center. Elections, policing, licensing, education, courts, public health, land use, utilities, labor enforcement, benefits administration, and much criminal law remain substantially state and local. That dispersion can frustrate egalitarian national policy. It can also frustrate authoritarian national policy. Whether federalism helps or harms democracy depends on who controls which institutions, what legal powers they have, and what values they serve.

Miller worries that praising federalism reinforces a constitutional structure that blocks national action. That is a real danger. But in the face of Trump’s second administration, it would be politically and morally perverse for blue states, cities, and state courts to say: because federalism once protected segregation, we will not use it now to protect immigrants, students, workers, reproductive freedom, climate policy, voting administration, and due process. No movement for justice has ever refused available terrain because that terrain had previously been occupied by its enemies.

Consider the role of states after Dobbs. The destruction of federal constitutional protection for abortion was a catastrophe, and federal legislation protecting reproductive freedom would be far preferable to a patchwork. Yet state constitutions, state ballot initiatives, state courts, and state legislatures became vital instruments for preserving access where possible. That did not solve the national problem, and it left millions subject to coercive laws. But for the people who could obtain care because their state protected it, the distinction mattered. Federalism was not justice; it was shelter.

The same is true of climate policy. National action is indispensable, and fragmented state policy cannot substitute for federal investment and regulation. But state renewable standards, emissions rules, public utility decisions, building codes, transit investments, and litigation against polluters can move markets and preserve capacity when Washington retreats. Similarly, states can raise minimum wages, expand Medicaid, support unions within legal limits, fund child care, enforce antidiscrimination law, and protect tenants. These policies are uneven, but uneven progress is not worthless. It can build models, constituencies, and proof of concept.

The civil rights lesson is also more complicated than a simple national-good, state-bad story. The federal government defeated Jim Crow only when national institutions were forced by movements, litigation, party realignment, geopolitical pressure, and mass organizing to act. For decades, federal institutions were themselves complicit: Congress refused anti-lynching laws, presidents temporized, the Supreme Court narrowed Reconstruction, and national parties accommodated segregationist power. Black freedom struggles used every level of government—federal courts, national legislation, local organizing, state challenges, municipal boycotts, and constitutional amendments. The problem was not decentralization alone; it was white supremacist control across institutions.

A democratic federalism should therefore be built around national floors and local ceilings. National power should guarantee baseline rights and social protections: voting access, nondiscrimination, labor rights, health care, environmental standards, reproductive liberty, due process, and equal protection. States should not be permitted to fall below those floors. But they should be able to exceed them. California should be able to regulate emissions more aggressively; Minnesota should be able to strengthen labor protections; New York should be able to protect tenants; Massachusetts should be able to expand health programs; Michigan should be able to reinforce voting rights. The old states’ rights tradition treated state sovereignty as a shield against national justice. Democratic federalism treats state authority as an instrument for expanding justice above a national minimum.

Miller may respond that the constitutional structure often prevents creation of those national floors. True. The Senate, filibuster, courts, and campaign finance system make national egalitarian legislation difficult. But that is an argument for reforming national institutions, not for dismissing subnational power. Indeed, state-level victories may be among the ways national reform becomes imaginable. Before national marriage equality, state litigation and legislation helped transform public expectations. Before national labor or welfare reforms, state experimentation exposed both possibilities and failures. Before federal marijuana reform became plausible, states changed facts on the ground. Policy diffusion is not a substitute for national democracy, but it can be a pathway toward it.

The lead essay’s emphasis on mass politics actually strengthens the case for federalism as a field of organizing. National politics is expensive, mediated, and often remote. State and local campaigns can cultivate leaders, unions, coalitions, and governing competence. School board fights, city council housing battles, county sheriff races, state supreme court elections, ballot initiatives, and legislative campaigns can be distorted by money and low turnout, but they are also places where ordinary people can build power. If progressives abandon those arenas because they are imperfect, organized reactionaries will gladly occupy them.

Of course, there are serious risks. Localism can empower homeowners against renters, police unions against accountability, business lobbies against wages, and affluent suburbs against regional equality. State governments can preempt progressive city policies. Rural minorities within blue states and urban minorities within red states can be ignored. State courts can be captured. A democratic federalism must be honest about these dangers. It should favor regional equity, state constitutional rights, participatory budgeting, fair districting, public financing, strong local journalism, and national protection against state-level authoritarianism.

But centralized power has dangers too. Miller’s essay is written in the shadow of a president who wants to bend the federal executive to his will. If one national election can place immigration enforcement, prosecution, federal grants, military authority, intelligence, labor regulation, education policy, and administrative expertise in the hands of an authoritarian faction, then decentralization is not merely obstruction. It is insurance. State officials can sue. State courts can interpret state constitutions. State agencies can refuse voluntary cooperation. State legislatures can fund legal defense networks. Governors can protect records. Localities can maintain services. None of this is sufficient, but sufficiency is not the standard for political tools in a crisis.

The right posture, then, is neither Newsom-style romanticism about the “United STATES” nor Miller’s suspicion that federalism mainly reinforces vetoes. Federalism is not a principle to worship. It is a set of institutions to democratize and deploy. Progressives should speak plainly: we support national power when it secures equal freedom and material dignity; we support state and local power when it expands those goods or resists national abuse; we oppose any level of government that entrenches domination.

This approach also helps answer the authoritarian appropriation of anti-elitism. Trump’s movement claims to speak for “the people” against distant institutions, but it often seeks to crush communities’ ability to govern themselves when they disagree with him. A democratic federalism can expose that fraud. If Washington threatens universities for disfavored speech, states can defend academic freedom. If federal agencies target immigrants without due process, local legal networks can respond. If national policy serves fossil fuel or tech oligarchs, states can regulate where they retain authority. The point is not that blue-state elites are automatically virtuous. It is that plural power gives democratic actors room to fight.

Miller is correct that the long-term goal must be a national democracy capable of acting for ordinary people. Universal health care, voting rights, climate stabilization, labor power, and anti-poverty policy require federal action. But the road to that democracy will run through states and cities as well as Congress. In the meantime, people need protection where they live.

The dead end is not federalism. The dead end is federalism without equality, localism without democracy, and national power without accountability. The task is to build a system in which no state can deny basic rights, no national administration can monopolize power, and every level of government can be made answerable to organized publics. That is not the old states’ rights tradition. It is democratic federalism, and in 2025 it is not optional.
