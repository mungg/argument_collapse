"While offshore balancing is a useful corrective to overreach in other regions, it is a dangerous strategy for East Asia because a purely maritime, over-the-horizon posture cannot maintain a stable balance of power against a rising China."

Stephen Walt’s advocacy of offshore balancing is a vital corrective to the hubris of the Bush years, but the strategy as he outlines it suffers from a significant regional blind spot: East Asia. Walt argues that the United States should maintain a significant air and naval presence in Asia primarily as a foundation for containing China if Beijing’s ambitions turn hegemonic. Yet he simultaneously insists that the United States should play hard-to-get, rely on regional allies, and intervene only when absolutely necessary. This uneasy combination underestimates both the speed and the character of China’s rise and overestimates the capacity of a purely maritime, over-the-horizon posture to preserve a stable Asian balance of power. A more coherent realist grand strategy for Asia would retain the permanently forward-deployed ground, air, and naval forces that have underpinned regional peace for five decades, because only a credible, in-theater deterrent can prevent the cascade of balancing behaviors that would make a conflict with China more, not less, likely.

Walt’s error is to treat Asia as an extension of the logic that he applies to Europe and the Persian Gulf. In Europe, he argues, there is no serious military threat and the European Union can handle its own security; offshore balancing there means near-total withdrawal. In the Persian Gulf, he wants a classic balance-of-power policy in which the United States intervenes only when a regional hegemon threatens to dominate, relying at other times on local states to balance each other. He then applies a similar template to Asia: a significant but reduced air and naval presence, with the understanding that Washington would commit forces only if China actually attempted to establish hegemony. But the Asian strategic environment is fundamentally different. China is not a distant, episodic threat like a resurgent Iraq; it is a rising great power with a dynamic economy, a rapidly modernizing military, and unresolved territorial disputes along its entire maritime periphery—Taiwan, the South China Sea, the East China Sea. Its neighbors, with the partial exception of Japan, lack the scale and the military capability to balance China on their own. South Korea, the ASEAN states, and Australia each face overwhelming asymmetries in population, economic size, and military potential vis-à-vis China. Walt’s assumption that they will “do more for us” if we seem reluctant is not a blank check; it is more likely to produce a stampede of bandwagoning as smaller states seek an accommodation with Beijing on unfavorable terms rather than risk being abandoned.

The historical record supports this concern. The post–World War II Asian order has not been a self-regulating balance of power. It has been a Pax Americana underwritten by bilateral alliance commitments and a permanent forward presence of approximately 100,000 troops, including substantial ground forces in South Korea and Japan. This presence has done more than deter Chinese aggression; it has suppressed the security dilemmas among America’s own allies. Japan, if left to its own devices, would face intense pressure to remilitarize, possibly going nuclear, in response to China’s growing power and North Korea’s missiles. A nuclear-armed Japan would trigger a corresponding response from South Korea and Taiwan, and the resulting arms race would destabilize the region in ways that no offshore fleet could control. Walt acknowledges this Danger only in passing, but his own logic implies that offshore balancing would encourage regional balancing against the United States. In Asia, it is far more likely to encourage regional balancing against each other, which the United States would then have to pacify at a much higher cost after the fact.

Moreover, China’s strategic trajectory is already being shaped by the perceived degree of American commitment. Beijing’s military doctrine focuses heavily on “anti-access/area denial” capabilities—submarines, ballistic missiles, and cruise missiles designed to push U.S. carrier battle groups away from its coasts. These capabilities are being developed precisely because Chinese planners believe that in a crisis over Taiwan, the United States might intervene from offshore bases. If the United States signals that it is moving to an offshore-balancing posture and reducing its forward presence, Chinese leaders will interpret that not as restraint but as a window of opportunity. The result could be a more assertive Chinese policy on Taiwan, greater coercion of the Philippines and Vietnam in the Spratlys, and an accelerated military buildup aimed at closing the window before the United States can reverse course. Far from reducing the danger of war, Walt’s prescription could make it a self-fulfilling prophecy.

A better grand strategy for Asia would combine the realism of selective engagement with the recognition that the region is now the principal theater of great-power competition. The United States should maintain its current forward-deployed forces, not reduce them, while also pressing its allies to assume greater responsibility for their own conventional defense as part of a broader burden-sharing agreement. The key is not to threaten abandonment but to transform the alliance system from a Cold War protectorate into a genuine partnership of capable democracies, with the United States providing the nuclear umbrella, intelligence, and high-end precision-strike capabilities, while Japan, Australia, South Korea, and others invest more in their own air-sea denial and ground forces. This is not offshore balancing; it is in-shore balancing, with American forces permanently positioned to shape the calculations of both Beijing and its neighbors. It also preserves the diplomatic leverage necessary to engage China economically and politically, offering Beijing a stable place in the regional order in exchange for peaceful resolution of disputes. A strategy of overt military retrenchment would forfeit that leverage.

Walt is right that hegemony is unsustainable and that the United States should avoid occupations in the Islamic world. But the Asian theater demands a different calibration. American primacy in Asia is not about imposing democracy or disarming proliferators; it is about sustaining a balance of power that has kept the world’s most populous and economically dynamic region at peace for an unprecedented period. Abandoning the forward infrastructure that makes that balance possible, in the hope that local powers will take up the slack, is a gamble that the United States cannot afford. The realist tradition that Walt invokes teaches that great powers do not rest their security on hope; they rest it on the careful husbanding of preponderant force where it matters most. In the first decade of a new century, that place is the Western Pacific. Offshore balancing should be applied where it fits; Asia is not one of those places.
