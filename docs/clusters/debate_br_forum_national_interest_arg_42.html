Diversified Gemini

"Stephen Walt’s strategy of offshore balancing is a cynical and historically blind form of isolationism that misdiagnoses the roots of terrorism and ignores the strategic necessity of promoting democracy to ensure long-term American security."

Stephen M. Walt’s prescription for American grand strategy, “offshore balancing,” is presented as a pragmatic and restrained alternative to the Bush administration’s policies. Yet, beneath its veneer of hard-nosed realism, Walt’s essay offers a deeply cynical and historically blind view of world affairs. Writing in early 2005, as Iraqis brave insurgent violence to cast their ballots in the first free elections in a generation, Walt dismisses the promotion of democracy as a “fool’s errand.” In doing so, he misunderstands both the true nature of the threats facing the United States and the ideological foundation of American power.

Walt’s fundamental error lies in his diagnosis of the roots of Islamic terrorism. He attributes the rise of al Qaeda and similar movements to American military presence in the Middle East and its support for Israel. This is a profound misreading of radical Islamism. Osama bin Laden and his ideological kin are not motivated primarily by a desire for American withdrawal or a two-state solution in the Levant; they are driven by a totalitarian vision of a restored caliphate and a violent rejection of modernity, pluralism, and secular governance. Appeasing them by abandoning our allies and retreating over the horizon will not satiate their hatred. It will merely signal weakness, validating their narrative that the United States is a “paper tiger” that lacks the stomach for a long struggle.

The neoconservative project, which Walt derides as “quasi-imperial,” is grounded in a recognition that the status quo in the Middle East—a status quo Walt seeks to maintain through “balance of power” politics—was the very swamp that bred the 9/11 hijackers. For sixty years, the United States pursued precisely the strategy Walt advocates: propping up local strongmen, relying on “regional allies” like Saudi Arabia and Hosni Mubarak’s Egypt to maintain stability, and ignoring the internal character of those regimes. The result was a toxic political culture in the Arab world, where state-sponsored media deflected domestic frustrations outward toward Israel and the United States, and where mosques became the only sanctioned outlets for political organizing.

To return to this strategy, as Walt suggests, is not realism; it is willful amnesia. The administration’s push for democratic transformation is not a utopian crusade; it is a strategic necessity. Democracies do not harbor transnational terrorist networks, they do not secretly develop weapons of mass destruction to blackmail their neighbors, and they provide legitimate avenues for the redress of grievances. Yes, the transition in Iraq has been immensely difficult and costly. But state-building is a generational endeavor. To declare it a failure less than two years after the fall of Baghdad is to display the very lack of strategic patience that emboldens our adversaries.

Furthermore, Walt’s recommendations regarding Israel are strategically incoherent and morally troubling. He advocates threatening to sever economic and military support for Israel unless it capitulates to American demands regarding the Palestinians. This approach ignores the reality that Israel is the only reliable, democratic ally we have in a volatile region. Israel faces the same existential threats from radical terrorism that we do. To abandon Israel or force it into concessions that jeopardize its security would not win us the affection of the Arab street; it would be interpreted by Hamas, Hezbollah, and their state sponsors in Tehran and Damascus as a monumental victory for terrorism.

Walt’s proposed “grand bargain” on nuclear weapons is similarly detached from reality. He suggests that if the United States shrinks its own nuclear arsenal and abandons plans to modernize it, rogue regimes like North Korea and Iran will suddenly feel less threatened and give up their own nuclear ambitions. This assumes that Pyongyang and Tehran are reacting rationally to the size of the U.S. stockpile. They are not. The Iranian mullahs and Kim Jong-il seek nuclear weapons to guarantee their own survival against internal dissent, to dominate their neighbors, and to project power independently of U.S. actions. A reduction in American warheads from 5,000 to 500 will not change the calculus of dictators who view nuclear capability as their ultimate trump card.

Ultimately, Walt’s “offshore balancing” is isolationism dressed up in academic jargon. It is a strategy of retreat. By pulling back our forces and relying on local actors to balance each other, we invite chaos. Power abhors a vacuum. If the United States steps back from the Middle East or East Asia, the resulting instability will not neatly balance itself out; it will spiral into regional wars that will eventually drag us back in, under far worse conditions and at a far higher cost.

America’s role as the world’s sole superpower is unprecedented, but it cannot be sustained by husbanding our strength and hiding behind our oceans. Primacy requires presence. It requires a willingness to shape the international environment, not merely react to it. It requires the moral clarity to distinguish between democratic allies and despotic regimes. Walt wishes to treat Israel the same way we treat “a Norwegian state, a Thai state, a Polish state,” ignoring the fact that Israel is surrounded by enemies sworn to its destruction. This bloodless, amoral calculation is the fatal flaw of his realism.

We are engaged in a global ideological struggle against a fanatical enemy. We cannot defeat this enemy by simply manipulating regional balances of power from afar. We must support the forces of liberty and reform within those regions, offering a compelling alternative to the nihilism of the terrorists. This requires the judicious but resolute application of American power, both hard and soft. It is a daunting task, but it is one we cannot shirk by retreating to our shores and hoping the world sorts itself out. The security of the United States depends on the success of liberty abroad, and no amount of realpolitik maneuvering can alter that fundamental truth.

  7. 07Maintaining American primacy requires an active presence and the moral clarity to support the forces of liberty against despotic regimes.
