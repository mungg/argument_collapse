**Don't Let a Crisis Become an Excuse for Bad Engineering**

Whenever drought tightens its grip on the West, the same megaprojects come back from the dead. Pipelines from the Mississippi or the Great Lakes. Towing icebergs from the Arctic. Massive new desalination plants up and down the Pacific coast. A revival of the long-dormant North American Water and Power Alliance, which would have plumbed Canadian rivers south to the Sun Belt. Each promises to solve scarcity by simply moving more water around.

We should resist these temptations. The history of Western water is largely the history of grand engineering projects whose ecological and fiscal costs ended up dwarfing their benefits. The Colorado River no longer reaches the sea. The Salton Sea is becoming a toxic dust bowl. The Owens Valley was drained to make Los Angeles bloom. The lower Colorado is so over-promised that Lake Mead and Lake Powell are both at historic lows even before climate change finishes its work. The lesson is not that we need more dams and pipelines; it is that we have already built too many.

Desalination deserves particular scrutiny. The Carlsbad plant under construction in San Diego County will be the largest in the Western Hemisphere, and it will produce water at roughly twice the cost of imported supplies, while consuming significant energy and producing brine discharge that damages near-shore ecosystems. As one tool in a portfolio, fine. As a substitute for conservation, it's a boondoggle that locks ratepayers into expensive water for decades.

The cheaper, smarter, and more sustainable path is what water planners call the "soft path": efficiency, recycling, stormwater capture, and demand management. Orange County's groundwater replenishment system already takes treated wastewater, purifies it to drinking standards, and recharges aquifers. Los Angeles, by some estimates, lets enough stormwater run off into the Pacific each year to supply hundreds of thousands of households; capturing even a fraction would meaningfully reduce imports. Singapore, with no hinterland at all, meets a large share of its needs through "NEWater" recycling.

These approaches share a virtue: they are modular, distributed, and reversible. A misjudged efficiency program wastes some money. A misjudged pipeline scars a continent for a century.

We should also stop building as if the climate of 1950 will return. Phoenix and Las Vegas continue to add subdivisions with grass lawns and decorative fountains. Real planning means tying new development to demonstrated water supply — something Arizona has done in part through its Assured Water Supply rules, which deserve to be tightened and copied.

The West's water future will not be saved by another dam. It will be saved by a thousand smaller adjustments — efficient appliances, drip lines, recycled effluent, captured rainwater, sensible zoning, and honest prices. The temptation to build our way out is exactly the trap that got us here.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical grand engineering projects have caused severe ecological degradation and fiscal burdens that outweigh their intended benefits.
  2. 02Large-scale desalination is an expensive, energy-intensive option that risks locking ratepayers into high costs while damaging marine ecosystems.
  3. 03Modular and distributed solutions like stormwater capture and wastewater recycling provide a more sustainable and less risky alternative to traditional infrastructure.
  4. 04Urban planning and development must be strictly tied to demonstrated water supplies and climate realities rather than outdated historical weather patterns.
  5. 05The water crisis is best managed through a combination of small-scale adjustments, including efficient appliances, sensible zoning, and honest pricing.
