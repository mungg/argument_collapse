China Is the Story; Russia Is the Distraction

For twenty years, American foreign policy has spent disproportionate attention on Russia — first to manage its collapse, then to expand NATO, then to "reset" relations, and now to handicap Putin's third term. Meanwhile, the actual transformation of the international system has been happening in East Asia, and we have been late to almost every important question it poses.

The numbers tell the story plainly. China's economy is roughly five times the size of Russia's and growing at three or four times the rate. China's military budget, officially around $100 billion and probably higher in reality, dwarfs Russia's. China holds more than a trillion dollars in U.S. debt; Russia holds a fraction of that. China is the largest trading partner of nearly every country in Asia, including American treaty allies. China is building a blue-water navy, asserting expansive claims in the South China Sea, and developing anti-access capabilities specifically designed to push the U.S. Navy out of the western Pacific.

What is Russia doing on a comparable scale? Selling gas to Germans and arms to Venezuelans. Vetoing Security Council resolutions on Syria — a country whose strategic importance to the United States is, frankly, modest. Hectoring its neighbors. None of this is trivial, but none of it reshapes the global balance of power.

The question is not whether Russia matters — of course it does, on nuclear weapons, on Iran, on Europe's energy security. The question is whether it deserves the bandwidth it currently consumes in Washington's strategic mind. The answer is no. The Obama administration's "pivot to Asia," announced last fall, is the correct instinct. It needs to be more than rhetoric.

Putin's reelection is a story for the regional press. The bigger story this week, as always, is in Beijing — where a leadership transition is approaching, the Politburo is debating economic rebalancing, and the implications for American power in the twenty-first century are being written. That is where our attention belongs.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01China's economic and military growth significantly outpaces Russia's, making it a much more formidable global power.
  2. 02China's strategic actions, such as its naval expansion and economic influence in Asia, directly challenge American power in ways that Russia's regional activities do not.
  3. 03Russia's current international influence is largely limited to regional issues and obstructionism that do not fundamentally reshape the international system.
  4. 04The U.S. government's 'pivot to Asia' is the necessary strategic correction to address the reality that the twenty-first century will be defined by Beijing rather than Moscow.
