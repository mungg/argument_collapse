"Heartbreak should be treated as a legitimate injury that requires structured self-care, the setting of boundaries, and a healthy reframing of the narrative."

Heartbreak should be treated less like a personal failure than like an injury: real, painful and deserving of care. The first task is to stop reopening the wound. That usually means limiting contact, putting away the digital reminders and resisting the late-night forensic investigation of old texts. Social media makes grief feel interactive; it is often healthier to make it temporarily boring.

The second task is not to “get over it” on command. People recover by metabolizing loss, not by pretending it is trivial. Let yourself be sad, but give sadness some structure: take walks, sleep, eat decently, keep appointments, and see friends even when you are poor company. Exercise and routine are not clichés; they help restore a sense of agency when rejection has made life feel externally controlled.

It also helps to tell the story carefully. A breakup can become evidence that one is unlovable, or it can become evidence that one attachment ended. Those are very different conclusions. Talk to people who will comfort you without turning your former partner into a cartoon villain. If the pain becomes immobilizing or dangerous, seek professional help.

On Valentine’s Day, ignore the marketing. Love is larger than romance, and recovery begins by remembering that.

I distrust the modern impulse to make heartbreak productive. “Learn from it.” “Grow from it.” “Become your best self.” Fine, perhaps, eventually. But in the early days, heartbreak does not need a lesson plan. It needs permission to be useless.

"The most effective way to handle heartbreak is through intense physical exertion and exercise."

The most underrated cure for heartbreak is physical exertion. I do not mean this glibly. When a relationship ends, the body is awash in cortisol, adrenaline, and the strange ache of withdrawal from another person's pheromones and presence. Researchers like Helen Fisher have shown, via fMRI scans of the recently dumped, that romantic rejection lights up the same brain regions as cocaine craving. You are, quite literally, in withdrawal.

Treat it that way. Run until your lungs burn. Lift something heavy. Swim laps until your arms shake. Exercise floods the brain with endorphins and BDNF, restores sleep, and gives the obsessive mind something to do besides replay the final argument. It also returns to you a sense of agency over a body that may feel, in the wake of breakup, like it has been borrowed and given back damaged.

There is a second benefit, less often discussed. Heartbreak collapses your sense of self. You spent months or years calibrating yourself to another person, and now that scaffolding is gone. Physical practice rebuilds you from the ground up — not as the person someone else loved or stopped loving, but as a body that can do things. Sign up for a martial arts class. Train for a 10K. The discipline matters less than the daily evidence that you are still here, still capable, still becoming.

Skip the wine. Skip the ice cream. Lace up your shoes. Your future self will thank you.

Let yourself grieve. That is the single most important piece of advice I can give anyone in the throes of heartbreak, and it is the one most often ignored. After a week or two of permitted sadness, gently begin reintroducing structure: a morning walk, a standing dinner with a friend, a project that demands your hands. The Victorians understood this with their year of mourning followed by a return to society. We have lost that wisdom and replaced it with either suppression or wallowing, neither of which heals.

On Valentine's Day specifically? Make plans. Not heroic plans — modest ones. A friend, a meal, a movie. The day's symbolism is only as powerful as you allow it to be.

"To effectively handle heartbreak, a person must move beyond mere distraction and engage in the process of truthfully re-narrating their life story."

Heartbreak is often described as the loss of another person, but it is also the collapse of a story. You thought you knew what the last months meant. You thought you knew where the next years were going. Then a sentence is spoken — “I don’t love you,” “I met someone,” “I can’t do this” — and the plot tears down the middle.

That is why advice that begins and ends with distraction is inadequate. Distraction is useful; nobody should spend every hour staring at the ceiling. But eventually the heartbroken person has to become a narrator again. The question is not only “How do I stop hurting?” It is “What happened to me, and how can I tell it without lying?”

There are two tempting lies. The first is that the lost relationship was perfect and its ending proves life is ruined. The second is that the other person was worthless and therefore the love meant nothing. Both stories are understandable. Both are prisons. The first keeps you worshiping the past; the second requires you to despise your own tenderness.

A better practice is to write the story in stages. First, write the raw version, the one full of accusation, longing, self-pity and contradiction. Do not send it. A week later, write the factual version: what was said, what was done, what you know and what you do not know. Later still, write the version in which you are neither villain nor saint. What did you ignore? What did you give? What did you need? What did you learn about your own capacity for attachment?

Therapy can help because a good therapist is trained to hear the story without needing to become one of its characters. Friends help too, though one should be careful not to turn every dinner into a deposition.

The goal is not to produce a neat moral. Some endings remain shabby. Some questions remain unanswered. But if you can tell the truth without making the lost person your god or your devil, the breakup begins to shrink. It becomes a chapter, not the title of the book.
