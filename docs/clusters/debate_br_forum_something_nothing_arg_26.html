"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies the severe flaws in the Afghan government and security forces, his conclusion that the war effort is hopeless is premature because a more limited, decentralized strategy can still prevent a Taliban victory and secure American interests."

Nir Rosen has written a bracing indictment of American strategy in Afghanistan, and anyone inclined to defend President Obama’s decision to send additional troops must begin by conceding much of his description. The Afghan police are often corrupt and incompetent. The Karzai government’s legitimacy has been gravely damaged by fraud. The United States has too often confused kinetic activity with political progress. Many officers recite counterinsurgency language without practicing counterinsurgency. And no serious person should believe that Afghanistan in 2010 resembles Iraq in 2007 in any simple or comforting way.

Yet Rosen moves too quickly from these truths to the conclusion that the effort is hopeless. He is right that counterinsurgency is difficult, costly, and frequently abused as a slogan. He is wrong to imply that because the United States cannot create a modern centralized Afghan state, it cannot achieve a more limited but still important objective: preventing the Taliban from regaining decisive momentum while creating conditions under which Afghan politics can become less violent and less hospitable to transnational jihadists.

The question before policymakers is not whether Afghanistan can be transformed into a coherent liberal democracy. It cannot, at least not by foreign troops on any timetable relevant to American politics. The question is whether a Taliban victory or near-victory would make the region more dangerous, demoralize Afghans who have taken risks since 2001, and strengthen the perception that the United States can be bled into retreat by insurgent persistence. On those questions, Rosen is too dismissive.

He argues that al Qaeda is already in Pakistan and that the Taliban are not primarily interested in global jihad. Both claims are substantially true but insufficient. The Taliban’s relationship with al Qaeda may be pragmatic rather than ideological, but pragmatic alliances can be deadly. The pre-2001 Taliban did not need to share every al Qaeda ambition to provide it sanctuary. The movement’s leaders refused to surrender bin Laden after the embassy bombings and again after September 11 except on terms the United States could not accept. Perhaps the Taliban have learned from that disaster. Perhaps they would restrain al Qaeda next time. But “perhaps” is a thin reed on which to rest American security policy, especially when Pakistan’s borderlands already provide sanctuary and a Taliban resurgence in Afghanistan could deepen and widen that sanctuary.

Moreover, the Pakistan problem is not an argument for abandoning Afghanistan; it is an argument for seeing Afghanistan and Pakistan together. A Taliban triumph in southern and eastern Afghanistan would encourage Pakistani Taliban factions, intensify pressure on Islamabad, and embolden elements within Pakistan’s security establishment that still regard Afghan Taliban groups as useful proxies. The United States cannot solve Pakistan’s internal contradictions by pacifying Helmand, but neither can it ignore the cross-border ecology of militancy.

Rosen’s strongest criticism is that the American partner is unworthy. He is devastating on the police: drug use, extortion, illiteracy, cowardice, and predation. But this is precisely why a shift in strategy is necessary. For years the United States under-resourced training, tolerated warlords, relied on air power, and treated Afghanistan as an economy-of-force theater while Iraq consumed attention. The resulting institutions are rotten in part because they were built hastily and supervised poorly. That does not mean they are beyond improvement. The Afghan National Army, while imperfect and ethnically imbalanced in some units, has often performed better than the police and enjoys more public respect. Police reform is harder because police live among the people and touch daily life. But difficulty is not impossibility.

A serious counterinsurgency campaign should not mean simply adding more Afghan police. It should mean triage and partnership: identify units that can be mentored, remove abusive commanders, embed trainers for longer periods, pay salaries reliably, create local complaint mechanisms, and connect security to dispute resolution. It should also mean abandoning the fantasy that Kabul can administer every valley. Here Rosen’s critique should refine COIN rather than destroy it. Population protection in Afghanistan must be locally adapted. Some areas may require tribal or community defense arrangements; others may require district-level bargains; still others may be left to indirect influence. A centralized state is the wrong model, but a patchwork political order backed by national resources may be attainable.

Rosen writes as if the only alternatives are maximal state-building and withdrawal. There is a middle course: a population-security campaign in strategically important areas combined with political decentralization and negotiations from a position of strength. The additional troops authorized by Obama can buy time for that approach. They can push insurgents away from key population centers, secure roads, reduce reliance on air strikes, and give Afghan officials—where competent ones exist—a chance to function. The troops are not a magic ingredient. But without security, there is no politics at all except Taliban night letters and police shakedowns.

The objection, of course, is that foreign soldiers themselves generate insecurity. Rosen’s vignettes show this. A child kicked by a policeman, villagers detained on the way to prayer, a student imprisoned because of a ringtone—these incidents accumulate into occupation. But the answer is not necessarily fewer troops. Sometimes fewer troops mean more raids, more air strikes, more dependence on abusive local strongmen, and more inability to protect those who cooperate. McChrystal’s restrictions on air power and night operations may frustrate soldiers, but they reflect an essential truth: the population will not support a government whose foreign backers kill civilians with impunity. If additional troops allow commanders to substitute presence and discrimination for standoff firepower, they may reduce the very harms Rosen deplores.

Rosen is also unfair to the soldiers he observes. He quotes their profanity and contempt, their references to “Hajis,” their impatience with Afghan fear. Those attitudes are real and corrosive. But his own account shows men trying, under terrible conditions, to do more than survive. Westby listens to elders, postpones a mission after Afghan police deaths, corrects abuses, and tries to distinguish innocent villagers from insurgents. These are not trivial acts. Counterinsurgency depends on thousands of such acts, and while they cannot substitute for strategy, neither should they be dismissed as theater. The military is capable of learning, even if slowly. The field manual did not transform the Army overnight, but it has changed incentives and language in ways that matter.

Still, defenders of the escalation must accept limits. If by the end of 2010 there is no evidence of improved security in key districts, better Afghan unit performance, reduced civilian casualties, and credible local governance, then the strategy should be reconsidered. “More time” cannot become an all-purpose excuse. Nor should the United States subordinate its policy to Karzai’s survival. Aid should be conditional, corruption should be confronted even when politically inconvenient, and reconciliation should be pursued with Taliban elements willing to break from al Qaeda and accept basic rules of political competition. The goal should be an Afghanistan that is inhospitable to international terrorist organizations, not an Afghanistan remade in America’s image.

Rosen warns against a cult of generals and celebrity strategists. On that point he is entirely right. No general, however disciplined or intellectually fashionable, can repeal Afghan history. But humility does not require fatalism. The United States helped create the present disorder through neglect, overreliance on warlords, civilian casualties, and strategic distraction. It has obligations as well as interests. A rapid withdrawal may satisfy critics of empire, but it could leave behind Afghans who believed American promises and a region more dangerous than before.

The Obama strategy deserves support only if understood as limited, conditional, and political. It should not be sold as victory. It should be sold as an attempt to prevent defeat while narrowing aims: protect key communities, improve Afghan security forces where possible, reduce the Taliban’s leverage, negotiate local settlements, and keep al Qaeda from regaining open sanctuary. Rosen has shown how easily such a strategy can fail. He has not shown that failure is inevitable.
