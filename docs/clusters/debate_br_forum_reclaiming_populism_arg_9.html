The View from Outside: Populism, Empire, and the Global Conjuncture

Adom Getachew is right to insist that the recent setbacks of the left — Morales in Bolivia, Corbyn in Britain, Sanders in the United States — must be read together, in a global frame, rather than as discrete national stories. Her own scholarship on anticolonial worldmaking has done as much as any recent work to remind English-language readers that the great political questions of the twentieth century were never simply national, and that the architects of the postcolonial world understood the construction of democratic peoples to be inseparable from the construction of a more just international order. I want to take up Getachew's invitation to think comparatively and push it further than her brief essay can. The argument I want to make is that the global lens does not just clarify the recent defeats of the left; it also clarifies the limits of the "reclaiming populism" framing itself, by revealing that populism as a category has done very different political work in different parts of the world, and that the contemporary American debate has not adequately reckoned with this variation.

Consider, first, the Latin American case, which is where contemporary scholarly debates about populism began and which provides the deepest theoretical archive for thinking about left-populist politics. The "pink tide" governments of the 2000s — Chávez and Maduro in Venezuela, Lula and Rousseff in Brazil, Kirchner and Fernández in Argentina, Morales in Bolivia, Correa in Ecuador, Vázquez and Mujica in Uruguay — are routinely lumped together as instances of left populism, but they were in fact quite different projects with quite different fates. Bolivia under Morales was perhaps the most genuinely transformative of these projects, because it was rooted in indigenous social movements that long predated MAS as an electoral vehicle and that maintained an autonomous political life alongside it. The 2019 ouster of Morales was, as Getachew rightly says, a coup — but the conditions for that coup were created in part by the erosion of MAS's social-movement base over the course of its time in office, as the party-state apparatus increasingly substituted itself for the movements that had originally made it. This is the recurring pathology of left populism in Latin America: a movement comes to power on the strength of autonomous popular organization, and then proceeds to absorb or marginalize that organization in the name of the leader and the state. By the time the right counter-mobilizes, the movement's defenses have been thinned by years of incorporation.

Venezuela is the most dramatic example. Chávez came to power in 1999 with the support of an extraordinarily mobilized urban poor, and he initiated genuine redistribution that pulled millions out of poverty. But the institutions he built — the Bolivarian Circles, the communal councils, the various participatory mechanisms — were always in tension with the personalist character of his rule, and that tension became fatal under Maduro, who inherited the personalism without the charisma or the oil revenues. Today Venezuela is in catastrophic crisis, and while a great deal of that crisis is the result of external sabotage (sanctions, opposition intransigence, U.S. interference), the internal vulnerabilities are also real and structural. The lesson is not that left projects in Latin America cannot succeed; it is that the populist form has specific liabilities — over-centralization, leader-dependence, the tendency to substitute state for movement — that have repeatedly undone left projects even when they began with genuine democratic energy.

The right-populist wave in Latin America that has followed the pink tide tells another part of the story. Bolsonaro's victory in Brazil, Macri's in Argentina (before Fernández's recent return), Duque's in Colombia, Piñera's continued presence in Chile: these are not simply the result of Latin American electorates rejecting the left. They are the result of right-wing political projects that have learned populist techniques — the moralized binary, the charismatic leader, the appeal to authentic national identity against foreign and elite contamination — and deployed them with considerable skill. The recent Latin American experience does not vindicate the project of reclaiming populism for the left. It complicates it.

The Indian case complicates it further. Narendra Modi's BJP has been since 2014 one of the world's most successful right-populist projects, and it has been so on the basis of a Hindu-nationalist redefinition of "the people" that explicitly excludes Muslims, who are recast as foreign elements requiring discipline. The Citizenship Amendment Act of December 2019, the National Register of Citizens, the abrogation of Article 370 in Kashmir, and the brutal handling of the Delhi pogroms in February 2020 are all instantiations of a populism in power. The Indian left's response — to the limited extent that an organized Indian left exists at the national scale — has not been to reclaim populism for itself but to defend a more capacious, secular, pluralist conception of the Indian nation that has its roots in the anticolonial movement and in the constitutional vision of Ambedkar and Nehru. This is significant: in India, the most serious opposition to right populism has come not from a counter-populism but from a defense of constitutional pluralism understood as a substantive political project. The shaheen bagh protests, which sustained a remarkable women-led occupation against the CAA for months, were not populist in form. They were pluralist, deliberative, and explicitly anti-majoritarian. They are now being dispersed by lockdown and police repression, but the political vocabulary they articulated is worth studying.

The European case adds another dimension. The Corbyn defeat in Britain has been read in many ways, and I do not want to litigate it in detail. But I want to note that Corbyn's project was always less populist in form than its critics suggested. It was, more accurately, a traditional social-democratic project — public ownership of utilities, redistribution, increased welfare spending — that happened to be led by a figure the British press could caricature as a populist outsider. The defeat in December 2019 was driven less by the failure of populism than by the unresolvable contradiction over Brexit, which fractured the coalition that Corbyn had assembled in 2017. The Labour seats that fell were almost entirely Leave-voting constituencies, and they fell because Labour could not satisfy them on Brexit without losing its Remain-voting urban and youth base. The lesson here is not about populism per se; it is about the specific difficulty of holding cross-cleavage coalitions together when an issue cuts across class lines, as Brexit did.

Compare this to the more genuinely populist projects elsewhere in Europe. Syriza in Greece came to power in 2015 on an explicitly left-populist program, was crushed by the Troika within months, and ended its term implementing the very austerity it had been elected to oppose. Podemos in Spain articulated an explicitly Laclauian left-populist theory and has spent the years since adjusting itself downward into a junior coalition partner in a center-left government. La France Insoumise has stalled at around twenty percent of the vote and has struggled to translate its mobilizational energy into durable institutional power. The Five Star Movement in Italy, which began as a left-adjacent populist project, has ended up in coalition first with the far right and then with the center-left, demonstrating exactly the kind of ideological promiscuity that the populist form invites. None of this is encouraging for the proposition that populism is the form left projects should adopt.

The American case must be understood in this comparative context. The Sanders campaign was not, I would argue, a populist project in the strictest sense, though it sometimes deployed populist rhetorical tropes. It was, more accurately, a social-democratic project that was trying to build a mass base for policies — Medicare for All, free public college, a Green New Deal — that have majority support in opinion polls but have struggled to find political vehicles. Its failure in the Democratic primary cannot be explained by any single factor: the consolidation of moderate support after South Carolina, the long-standing affection of older Black voters for Biden, the COVID-19 disruption of the campaign's final phase, the structural disadvantages Sanders faced with party elites and donors. But one comparative observation is worth making: in countries where social democracy has succeeded, it has done so on the basis of institutional infrastructure — unions, parties, mutual aid societies, cooperatives — that the United States simply does not possess in comparable form. The American labor movement organizes around eleven percent of the workforce; in Sweden it organizes around seventy percent. No campaign, populist or otherwise, can substitute for that institutional gap. Sanders's project, in retrospect, was attempting to build social democracy without the institutional preconditions that social democracy historically required.

This is where the international frame is most clarifying. The crisis of social democracy in the global North and the crisis of left populism in the global South are not separate phenomena. They are linked manifestations of the same underlying transformation: the rise, over the last forty years, of a global capitalist order in which capital is mobile, labor is fragmented, the state is hollowed out, and the institutional forms that the twentieth-century left built — industrial unions, mass parties, redistributive welfare states — have been systematically dismantled or weakened. No national project, no matter how well led, can fully address this transformation on its own. What is needed, as Getachew rightly insists, is a renewed internationalism — not just rhetorical solidarity but concrete coordination across borders to constrain capital, regulate finance, share medical and technological capacity, and confront climate change as the global emergency it is.

The COVID-19 pandemic has clarified this need with brutal force. The competition for ventilators and protective gear, the hoarding of vaccine research, the wielding of sanctions to deny medicine to Iran and Venezuela, the immigration restrictions imposed under public health pretexts, the absence of any meaningful global coordination — all of these are manifestations of the same nationalist enclosure of what should be transnational solidarities. The countries that have done best epidemiologically have done so by drawing on regional and global expertise (Taiwan, South Korea, Vietnam, New Zealand), and the countries that have done worst have done so by closing themselves off rhetorically while remaining structurally exposed to the global circuits they depend on (the United States above all). The Trump administration's response — pulling funding from the WHO, blaming China, doubling down on sanctions, expelling asylum seekers — is the populist response to a global crisis, and it is failing on every dimension simultaneously.

What would an alternative look like? An internationalist left politics adequate to 2020 would, I think, have several features. It would treat the WHO and other multilateral institutions not as inadequate to current needs (which they are) but as worth defending and strengthening rather than abandoning. It would push for immediate suspension of sanctions that impede pandemic response and for debt relief for countries in the global South that cannot otherwise mount fiscal responses to the crisis. It would advocate compulsory licensing of vaccines and treatments to ensure global access at affordable prices. It would treat migration not as a crisis to be managed but as a constitutive feature of the world we live in, requiring rights-based frameworks rather than enforcement-based ones. It would understand climate, pandemic, and migration as a single complex of global commons problems requiring coordinated rather than nationalist responses.

This is not populism. It is something older and, I think, more useful: it is internationalism. The tradition that runs from the First International through the anticolonial movements to the contemporary global justice movement has consistently understood that the construction of democratic peoples within nations and the construction of solidarity across nations are not separate projects but a single project. The American left, in its current preoccupation with reclaiming populism, has perhaps lost touch with this tradition. The global frame Getachew rightly insists on is most powerful when it reminds us that the resources for a left politics adequate to our moment are international, and that the parochialism of contemporary American political debate — even when that debate is conducted in the name of "the people" — is itself part of what needs to be overcome.
