Diversified GPT

"To ensure surrogacy is ethical and safe, the international community and the United States must establish enforceable legal standards and oversight to address the exploitation and legal uncertainty inherent in cross-border arrangements."

The most troubling surrogacy cases are not usually those arranged between relatives in Boston or Chicago after months of counseling. They are the cross-border arrangements in which affluent foreigners travel to poorer countries because the price is lower, the law is looser and women have fewer realistic alternatives. If we want ethical surrogacy, we must confront the global inequality built into the industry.

India has become a prominent destination for commercial surrogacy, and other countries have also attracted intended parents seeking lower costs. Defenders say these arrangements can provide life-changing income to women. That can be true. But the ethical question is not simply whether a woman receives more money than she otherwise might. It is whether she has meaningful bargaining power, full medical information, freedom of movement, independent advice, control over pregnancy decisions and continuing care if something goes wrong.

International surrogacy also creates risks for children and intended parents. Conflicting nationality, parentage and adoption laws can leave families stranded. A country may treat the woman who gives birth as the legal mother; another may not recognize the intended parents; a consulate may hesitate to issue travel documents. When a child’s legal status depends on gaps between legal systems, adults have designed a dangerous arrangement.

The United States should not pretend this is only a foreign problem. American intended parents and agencies help create demand. One practical step would be to require that U.S. courts and immigration authorities recognize foreign surrogacy arrangements only if minimum protections are documented: independent counsel for the surrogate, informed consent in her own language, comprehensive medical insurance, no coercive residence requirements, transparent compensation, and legal parentage rules that prevent abandonment. Agencies arranging foreign surrogacy for Americans should be licensed in the United States and liable for misconduct abroad.

At the international level, surrogacy needs something like the Hague adoption framework: not a blanket endorsement, but common rules to prevent trafficking, baby-selling and legal limbo. Countries that permit surrogacy should agree on parentage documentation, citizenship safeguards, agency oversight and post-birth care. Countries that prohibit commercial surrogacy should still cooperate to protect children already born.

None of this eliminates the deeper moral discomfort. Wealthy people hiring poorer women to gestate children will always raise questions about commodification and exploitation. But ignoring the practice, or pushing it from one jurisdiction to another, makes matters worse. Ethical surrogacy cannot be built on the ability of money to cross borders faster than law. It requires enforceable standards that follow the transaction wherever it goes.

  3. 03The United States should mandate that foreign surrogacy arrangements meet specific ethical protections, such as independent counsel and medical insurance, as a condition for legal recognition.
  4. 04Surrogacy agencies should be licensed and held legally liable for misconduct occurring in their international operations.
  5. 05An international framework, similar to the Hague adoption convention, is necessary to establish common rules that prevent human trafficking and ensure the welfare of children born through surrogacy.
Diversified Claude

"To prevent the exploitation and practical harms inherent in the cross-border surrogacy trade, the international community must implement a coordinated regulatory framework with mandatory safety and ethical standards."

The most urgent reforms are not philosophical but practical, and they concern the cross-border trade. Whatever one thinks about surrogacy in principle, the system as it currently operates between rich countries and poor ones is producing predictable and preventable harms.

Consider what happened in Thailand earlier this year. An Australian couple commissioned twins through a Bangkok surrogate, learned that one had Down syndrome, and reportedly took home only the healthy child, leaving baby Gammy with the surrogate. The story prompted Thailand to begin shutting the industry down. Around the same time, an Australian man was discovered to have commissioned a child in Thailand whom he subsequently abused, raising questions no agency had thought to ask. India, the largest market, has been tightening rules and may soon bar foreign single people and same-sex couples entirely. Nepal, Mexico, and Georgia are emerging as new hubs, each less regulated than the last.

The pattern is clear. Wherever commercial surrogacy goes underground or operates in jurisdictions with weak oversight, the same problems recur: surrogates who do not fully understand the contracts they sign, payment structures that put all risk on the carrier, no screening of intended parents, abandoned children, citizenship limbo, and clinics that cut medical corners because their clients have already left the country.

A serious response requires international coordination, not just national rules. The Hague Conference on Private International Law has been working since 2011 on a framework analogous to the inter-country adoption convention. That work should be accelerated and adopted. At minimum, such a convention should require: background checks on intended parents, including criminal and child welfare screening; independent legal representation for the surrogate; minimum compensation tied to local cost of living; medical standards including limits on embryo transfer to prevent dangerous multiple pregnancies; and a guarantee of citizenship for the child in the intended parents' country before the pregnancy begins.

Receiving countries — the United States, Australia, the United Kingdom, Israel — have the most leverage here. They can refuse to recognize parentage or grant passports for arrangements that did not meet international standards. That alone would push the market toward compliance.

None of this answers the deeper question of whether wombs should be for hire at all. But while that debate continues, real women and real children are being processed through an industry that no one is regulating in earnest. The first task is to stop the bleeding. The philosophical reckoning can follow.

  3. 03International regulations must include mandatory background checks for intended parents, independent legal representation for surrogates, and guaranteed citizenship for the resulting children.
  4. 04Wealthy receiving countries should use their leverage by refusing to recognize parentage or grant passports for surrogacy arrangements that fail to meet international ethical standards.
  5. 05Practical regulatory reforms must take priority over the philosophical debate regarding the morality of surrogacy to protect the women and children currently at risk in the industry.
Diversified Gemini

"To ensure surrogacy is safe, ethical, and protective of all parties, the international community must adopt a comprehensive, binding treaty modeled on the Hague Convention on Intercountry Adoption."

The recent and tragic "Baby Gammy" case in Thailand—where an Australian couple allegedly abandoned a surrogate-born infant with Down syndrome while taking his healthy twin sister home—has laid bare the chaotic and dangerous reality of international commercial surrogacy. We are currently operating in a global Wild West. Because domestic laws vary so wildly, intended parents simply cross borders to find the cheapest and least regulated markets. To ensure surrogacy is safe and ethical, we desperately need a comprehensive international treaty modeled on the Hague Convention on Protection of Children and Co-operation in Respect of Intercountry Adoption.

Right now, cross-border surrogacy creates a host of legal nightmares. Children are frequently left stateless or parentless due to conflicting laws between the surrogate’s home country and the intended parents' nation. Surrogates in developing countries are subjected to opaque contracts, substandard maternal healthcare post-delivery, and severe social stigma, while intended parents are vulnerable to extortion and fraud by unregulated, fly-by-night agencies.

An international treaty would establish universal minimum standards for cross-border surrogacy arrangements. First, it must mandate comprehensive medical and psychological screening for both the surrogate and the intended parents, ensuring that no party is being coerced and that parents are fit to raise a child. Second, the treaty must guarantee independent legal representation for the surrogate, paid for by an independent fund rather than the intended parents, to avoid conflicts of interest.

Third, an international framework must resolve the legal status of the child at the moment of birth. A Hague-style convention would require signatory nations to mutually recognize parentage orders, ensuring that no child is left stateless or trapped in legal limbo because a country refuses to recognize the surrogacy contract. Finally, the treaty must mandate comprehensive escrow accounts to ensure that the surrogate’s medical expenses are covered long after the birth, mitigating the physical risks she assumes.

We cannot stop the demand for biological children, and piecemeal national bans only drive the industry underground or into the next vulnerable developing nation. Only a binding, internationally enforced regulatory framework can bring commercial surrogacy out of the shadows and protect the most vulnerable parties involved.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01An international treaty would prevent children from being left stateless or parentless by requiring signatory nations to mutually recognize legal parentage at birth.
  2. 02A global framework would protect surrogates from exploitation by mandating independent legal representation and guaranteed coverage for long-term medical expenses.
  3. 03Universal standards would mitigate risks for intended parents and surrogates by requiring comprehensive medical and psychological screenings for all parties involved.
  4. 04A binding international regulatory framework is necessary because national bans merely shift the industry to less regulated and more vulnerable developing nations.
Diversified MiniMax

"To ensure that surrogacy is safe and ethical, the international community must develop a comprehensive legal framework and diplomatic convention to regulate cross-border arrangements."

Surrogacy has become a genuinely global phenomenon, with prospective parents routinely crossing borders to engage surrogates in other countries. This reality demands an international legal framework that currently does not exist—and urgently needs to be built.

The current situation is untenable. India, which emerged as a major surrogacy destination, has moved toward restricting commercial surrogacy for foreigners amid concerns about exploitation. Thailand, after high-profile cases involving same-sex parents and other arrangements that caught international headlines, has similarly tightened rules. The Ukraine has become a destination for others, while the United States operates a patchwork of state laws with no federal coherence. Intended parents find themselves in a dizzying landscape where the legality of their arrangement depends entirely on where they happen to be.

This creates concrete harms. Children born through international surrogacy arrangements sometimes face citizenship gaps or immigration complications. Surrogates in developing countries often lack basic legal protections. And the absence of consistent standards allows the worst actors to operate with impunity.

What would international coordination look like? At minimum, nations should agree on baseline protections for surrogates—requirements for informed consent, medical standards, and prohibition of the most exploitative practices. Agreements on parentage recognition would prevent children from becoming legal non-entities while their parents litigate jurisdiction questions. And mechanisms for cross-border enforcement would give all parties certainty about their rights and obligations.

The Hague Conference on Private International Law has begun work on some of these issues, but progress is slow. What is needed is a serious, sustained diplomatic effort to develop a comprehensive international convention on surrogacy. The children, surrogates, and intended parents caught in this legal vacuum deserve better.

  3. 03International agreements are necessary to establish baseline protections for informed consent, medical standards, and the prohibition of exploitative practices.
  4. 04A unified framework for parentage recognition is required to prevent children from becoming legal non-entities during jurisdictional disputes.
