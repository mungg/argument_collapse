"The best way to combat online piracy is through a combination of international law enforcement cooperation, globalized legal distribution models, and domestic legislation that prioritizes due process and proportionality."

The debate over SOPA and PIPA has been too national in its imagination. Online piracy is a global problem, and that is precisely why American legislation that tries to project power through domestic intermediaries is both tempting and dangerous. A foreign site can injure American rights holders while remaining beyond the easy reach of American courts. But if every country responds by ordering Internet companies to block disfavored foreign sites, the open global network will become a patchwork of national blacklists.

The best alternative is a combination of international enforcement, lawful global distribution and restraint at home. The United States should continue to use trade negotiations, mutual legal assistance treaties and cooperation with foreign law enforcement to target criminal piracy operations. Many rogue sites are not romantic communities of sharing; they are profit-making enterprises that exploit creators while contributing little to culture. Their operators should face real consequences, and countries that shelter them should face diplomatic pressure.

But enforcement cannot succeed if the legal market remains artificially territorial. Consumers experience the Internet as global, while entertainment licensing often remains divided by country, window and platform. A person who can read about a new American television show instantly but cannot legally watch it for months is being pushed toward piracy. Studios and publishers should make worldwide digital availability a priority. Regional licensing practices may have made sense in an older distribution world; online they increasingly function as an invitation to infringement.

Domestic law should focus on due process and proportionality. Any remedy aimed at a foreign piracy site should require a court, a clear evidentiary standard, notice where possible and a remedy tailored to the unlawful conduct. Cutting off advertising and payment services may be appropriate in some cases. Seizing domains, blocking DNS, or imposing broad duties on platforms should be approached with extreme caution. The United States should not create tools it would condemn if used by less democratic governments.

Finally, creators and users need a better social compact. Schools should teach that creative labor has value, while copyright owners should acknowledge fair use, libraries, education and transformative expression as legitimate public interests rather than loopholes. The public will support enforcement when it sees copyright as balanced.

Piracy will not be eliminated. The realistic goal is to reduce its commercial scale while preserving the architecture and freedoms that make the Internet valuable. That requires international cooperation, better markets and narrower laws than SOPA or PIPA.
