"A post-neoliberal economic framework must move beyond mere carbon pricing to deeply integrate ecological limits and climate justice into every facet of economic policy, from labor and industry to ownership and growth."

The lead essay identifies many failures of neoliberalism, but it still places prosperity at the center and ecology near the margins. Climate change appears in the opening list of crises and in the discussion of carbon pricing, but the full implications of ecological breakdown are not integrated into the economic vision. Economics after neoliberalism must be climate economics in the deepest sense: not merely the pricing of carbon, but the reorganization of production, consumption, investment, and politics around planetary limits.

The neoliberal era failed on climate in a particularly revealing way. The atmosphere is the textbook externality: emitters impose costs on others, including future generations, without paying for them. Economists have long known the standard answer: put a price on carbon. Yet the world has not done so at anything like the necessary scale. This failure is not simply intellectual. It is political. Fossil fuel companies had power. Consumers were locked into carbon-intensive infrastructure. Governments feared backlash. International cooperation was difficult. The benefits of action were diffuse and long-term; the costs were concentrated and immediate. Market failure interacted with power failure and democratic failure.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman argue that economists should move beyond first-best reasoning. Climate is the area where this lesson is most urgent. A uniform global carbon price may be elegant, but elegance is not enough. Decarbonization requires a portfolio: regulation, public investment, industrial policy, research and development, procurement, transit, building standards, renewable energy mandates, grid modernization, just transition funds, and changes in land use. Prices matter, but they operate within systems. If people have no public transit, they cannot respond much to gasoline prices. If renters do not control insulation or heating systems, they cannot make efficient investments. If utilities are regulated to favor old assets, clean energy is slowed. If communities depend on coal employment, climate policy without replacement livelihoods will generate resistance.

The emergence of the Green New Deal in American politics reflects this reality. It links decarbonization with jobs, justice, and public investment. Critics complain that this mixes climate policy with social policy. But politically and economically, the linkage is a strength. Climate action will transform the economy. The question is whether that transformation will be planned democratically and equitably or imposed chaotically by disaster and market adjustment.

A post-neoliberal economics should therefore reject the idea that climate policy is a narrow environmental correction. It is macroeconomic policy, labor policy, industrial policy, housing policy, transportation policy, and foreign policy. It will shape where people live, what work they do, how energy is produced, who owns infrastructure, and how costs are distributed.

Consider ownership. If the transition to renewable energy simply creates new private monopolies, extracts rents through patents and regulated utilities, and leaves communities with little control, it will reproduce existing inequalities. Public investment should secure public returns. Community solar, public power, cooperative ownership, and green banks can help ensure that the gains from decarbonization are broadly shared. When public money funds innovation, the public should not merely socialize risk and privatize reward.

Labor is equally central. Fossil fuel workers and communities cannot be treated as collateral damage. They did not create the climate crisis by themselves; they worked in the energy system society built. A just transition must include wage replacement, pensions, retraining, local investment, and, where possible, guarantees of good jobs. But we should be honest: “retraining” alone is not enough. A fifty-five-year-old coal worker cannot be answered with a coding boot camp. Communities need institutions, tax bases, schools, health care, and new industries.

At the same time, green jobs must be good jobs. Solar installation, building retrofits, care work during climate disasters, transit construction, and ecological restoration should not become low-wage precarious labor. Labor standards, project labor agreements, apprenticeship programs, and union rights should be built into climate policy. This is where the lead essay’s support for wage boards and unions intersects directly with decarbonization.

Climate also forces economists to rethink growth. The authors seek inclusive prosperity, and in a world of poverty, growth cannot be dismissed. Billions of people need more energy, housing, sanitation, transportation, and health care. But rich countries must ask what kind of growth they pursue. More private jets, larger SUVs, planned obsolescence, and energy-intensive luxury consumption are not morally equivalent to better schools, health care, clean water, and public transit. GDP aggregates what should be distinguished. An economy can grow while degrading the conditions of life.

This does not mean embracing austerity for ordinary people. On the contrary, ecological politics will fail if it is experienced as scarcity imposed on those already insecure. But prosperity should be redefined: less waste, more time, better public goods, healthier communities, shorter commutes, cleaner air, durable products, and secure care. Some sectors should expand—renewable energy, education, health, care, transit, housing retrofits. Others should contract—fossil fuels, pollution-intensive luxury consumption, perhaps some forms of advertising-driven overconsumption. Economics can help manage this structural shift, but only if it stops treating all market output as presumptively good.

International justice is unavoidable. Rich countries bear disproportionate responsibility for historical emissions. Poor countries are more vulnerable to climate harms and still need development. A fair transition requires climate finance, technology sharing, and space for green industrial policy in the global South. Intellectual property rules should not prevent diffusion of clean technologies. Trade rules should allow countries to support domestic green industries while avoiding a destructive subsidy race. Border carbon adjustments may be necessary, but they must be designed with development concerns in mind.

The authors’ discussion of innovation policy is especially relevant. Technological change is directed by incentives. Public policy already shapes energy technology through subsidies, military spending, procurement, and research. We need mission-oriented public investment on a much larger scale: storage, grids, zero-carbon industry, agriculture, carbon-neutral fuels where necessary, and adaptation. But we should beware of technological salvationism. There is no guarantee that future negative emissions or geoengineering will rescue us from delayed action. Economics should incorporate uncertainty and catastrophic risk more seriously than standard cost-benefit analysis often does.

Discounting is a moral issue masquerading as a technical parameter. How much do we value the lives and welfare of future generations? A high discount rate can make severe future climate damages look small today. But young people marching for climate action are not making a spreadsheet error. They are asserting a claim of justice. Economics can clarify implications of different discount rates; it cannot determine the ethical answer.

Democracy is also at stake. Climate disasters can empower authoritarian responses, border militarization, and eco-apartheid. Alternatively, climate action can rebuild democratic capacity through public works, local planning, and shared purpose. The direction depends on institutions. If decarbonization is designed by technocrats and imposed through regressive prices, backlash will grow. If it is built through visible public benefits—jobs, transit, clean air, lower energy bills, resilient communities—it can strengthen democracy.

The lead essay’s greatest insight is that markets are not natural; they are structured. Climate change demands that we structure them differently and, in some domains, rely less on them. We need carbon prices, but also bans, standards, public ownership, planning, and investment. We need efficiency, but also resilience. We need innovation, but also restraint. We need prosperity, but prosperity must be compatible with a livable planet.

Economics after neoliberalism cannot be merely more inclusive. It must be ecological. Otherwise it will offer a fairer distribution of an economy whose material foundations are collapsing.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Climate policy should be treated as a comprehensive macroeconomic and industrial strategy rather than a narrow environmental correction.
  2. 02Decarbonization efforts must prioritize democratic and public ownership models to ensure that the gains from the green transition are equitably shared rather than captured by private monopolies.
  3. 03A 'just transition' requires linking climate action with labor protections, such as union rights and community-level investments, to ensure fossil fuel workers are not treated as collateral damage.
  4. 04Economists must redefine prosperity to distinguish between socially beneficial growth and ecologically destructive consumption, moving away from the assumption that all GDP growth is inherently good.
  5. 05International economic policy must incorporate climate justice by providing technology sharing and financial support to the global South, acknowledging the disproportionate historical responsibility of wealthy nations.
  6. 06Economic modeling of climate change must replace technocratic cost-benefit analysis and high discount rates with ethical considerations regarding catastrophic risk and the rights of future generations.
  7. 07Effective climate action requires a diverse portfolio of interventions, including public investment, regulation, and planning, because market prices alone cannot overcome structural and political barriers to decarbonization.
