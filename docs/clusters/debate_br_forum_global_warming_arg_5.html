"Climate change should be addressed as a problem of risk management and insurance rather than a debate over scientific certainty, necessitating immediate policy action to mitigate potentially intolerable consequences."

Kerry Emanuel’s essay is powerful because it does not rest on the most easily caricatured claims about global warming. He does not say that every consequence will be bad, or that every temperature record is beyond dispute, or that the science has no uncertainties. Indeed, the most important word in the essay may be “insurance.” Emanuel asks us to think about climate change not as a morality play between certainty and denial, but as a problem of risk management under conditions of deep uncertainty. That is exactly the right frame.

We do not need to know with mathematical precision how much Greenland will melt by 2100, how the frequency of hurricanes will change, or which agricultural regions will gain and lose in order to justify action. We know enough already: greenhouse gases are accumulating because of human activity; the planet is warming; ice and sea level are responding; and the possible consequences include not just gradual inconvenience but abrupt, costly, and irreversible shifts. In ordinary life, we insure our houses not because we are certain they will burn down, but because the loss would be intolerable if they did. The atmosphere is the one house that all nations share.

The debate in the United States has too often been staged as if the only serious question were whether climate change is a “hoax” or a “catastrophe.” Emanuel’s essay usefully escapes that trap. He acknowledges that warming may bring some benefits: lower heating bills, longer growing seasons in high latitudes, fewer cold-related deaths, and perhaps CO2 fertilization of some crops. These points should not be dismissed. But they are also not decisive. Averages conceal distribution, and distribution is the heart of climate politics. A longer growing season in Canada does not compensate a farmer in the Sahel for drought, a family in Bangladesh for storm surge, or a coastal city for the loss of its protective wetlands. The fact that some people may gain from a destabilized climate is not a moral argument for destabilizing it.

Nor is the comparison with past ice ages reassuring. Human beings survived enormous climatic swings, but they did not do so while maintaining modern coastal cities, ports, irrigation systems, insurance markets, electricity grids, or an interconnected global economy of more than six billion people. Civilization is not simply the biological survival of Homo sapiens. It is the persistence of institutions, settlements, and infrastructures built around a relatively stable Holocene climate. If climate shifts helped weaken societies in Mesopotamia, the Maya lowlands, or the American Southwest, then the lesson is not that humans always adapt easily. It is that social complexity can be vulnerable to climatic stress, especially when water, food, and political legitimacy are already strained.

Emanuel is also right to emphasize the hydrological cycle. Much of the public discussion imagines global warming as though the earth were merely being placed under a slightly warmer lamp. But warming is not just a change in average temperature. It is a change in evaporation, condensation, snowpack, soil moisture, runoff timing, and extreme precipitation. It means more water vapor in the air, heavier downpours, and potentially longer dry spells. For human beings, water is often where climate becomes fate. We can tolerate a degree or two in average temperature more readily than we can tolerate the collapse of seasonal snowmelt, repeated crop failures, contaminated floodwaters, or conflicts over rivers already overdrawn.

The same is true of sea level. The six-to-sixteen-inch estimate for the next century may sound manageable, but it is not the whole story. First, sea level rise is not experienced on an abstract global shoreline. It is experienced in storm surges, saltwater intrusion, erosion, and the loss of protective marshes. A few inches can matter if they arrive on top of a hurricane tide. Second, the possibility of larger changes from ice-sheet dynamics is precisely the sort of low-probability, high-consequence risk that should command attention. Emanuel candidly notes that our understanding of ice physics is poor. That is not a reason for complacency. If a doctor says, “I do not fully understand the mechanism, but there is a plausible risk that this behavior could destroy vital organs,” the prudent response is not to demand perfect prediction before acting.

The difficult question is not whether climate change is real, but whether we can stop it. The answer depends on what “stop” means. We cannot stop all warming. The climate system has inertia; oceans store heat; carbon dioxide remains in the atmosphere for a long time; and the world is still building fossil-fuel infrastructure at a rapid pace. Even a heroic effort beginning today would not return the atmosphere to its preindustrial state. But if “stop” means prevent the worst outcomes, limit the magnitude of change, and reduce the odds of dangerous surprises, then yes, we can. Not easily, not cheaply, and not by one policy alone. But we can.

The first step is to put a price on carbon. At present, the market treats the atmosphere as a free waste dump. Coal, oil, and natural gas prices reflect extraction, transport, and refining, but not the climatic risk imposed on everyone else. That is a textbook externality. Whether through a carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system, emissions must carry a cost. A tax has the advantage of simplicity and price certainty; cap-and-trade has the advantage of fixing total emissions and may be more politically acceptable in some settings. The design matters less than the principle: emitting carbon should no longer be free.

The second step is efficiency. The cheapest ton of carbon is often the one never burned. Buildings leak heat; appliances waste electricity; cars remain less efficient than they could be; industrial processes discard usable energy. The United States in particular has lived for decades with artificially cheap energy and land-use patterns that assume endless oil. Better building codes, appliance standards, vehicle standards, combined heat and power, and urban planning are not glamorous, but they can buy substantial reductions while saving money over time. Conservation should not be confused with deprivation. A refrigerator that uses less electricity but keeps food just as cold is not an ascetic triumph; it is intelligent engineering.

Third, we must decarbonize electricity. This is where the hardest political choices begin. Coal is abundant and cheap, especially in the United States, China, and India, but conventional coal combustion is incompatible with a stable climate if used indefinitely at expanding scale. Carbon capture and storage may prove important, but it must be demonstrated, regulated, and made economical. Natural gas can help as a bridge but is still a carbon fuel. Wind and solar are promising and should be expanded, but intermittency and storage remain challenges. Nuclear power cannot be wished away. It raises real concerns: waste, cost, proliferation, and public trust. Yet a serious climate policy must compare risks, not seek purity. If nuclear energy can displace large amounts of coal under strict safeguards, it deserves a place in the discussion.

Fourth, we must invest heavily in research and development. The energy system is vast, capital-intensive, and slow to change. We need better batteries, lower-cost solar cells, advanced biofuels that do not compete destructively with food or forests, safer nuclear designs, carbon capture, smarter grids, and perhaps technologies not yet imagined. Public investment has historically played a crucial role in transformative technologies, from aviation to computing. Climate policy should unleash private markets, but markets alone will underinvest in basic research whose benefits are widely shared.

Fifth, adaptation is unavoidable. Even if we mitigate aggressively, some warming is already committed. Coastal planning, flood defenses, drought-resistant crops, water pricing, public-health systems, early-warning networks, and disaster preparedness will save lives. But adaptation is not an alternative to mitigation. It is the other half of prudence. To say “we will adapt” while continuing unlimited emissions is like saying we will improve firefighting while handing out matches in a drought-stricken forest.

The international dimension is the most difficult. The United States cannot solve the problem alone, but neither can it credibly demand restraint from others while refusing its own. Kyoto was flawed: it excluded binding targets for developing countries and became a symbol in American politics rather than a durable solution. But the American rejection of Kyoto did not solve those flaws; it deepened mistrust. A workable regime must recognize both responsibility and development. Rich countries put most of the existing stock of industrial carbon into the atmosphere. Poor countries, understandably, seek growth. The bargain must therefore be: wealthy nations reduce first and fastest, help finance cleaner development, and support technology transfer; developing nations accept increasingly serious commitments as their capacities rise. China and India cannot be permanently outside the system, but they cannot be expected to freeze themselves in poverty to solve a problem largely created by others.

Some will object that the costs are too high. But compared to what? The cost of rebuilding after disasters? The cost of relocating coastal infrastructure? The cost of military instability in regions stressed by water and food insecurity? The cost of losing ecosystems that provide services we scarcely know how to price? The Stern Review, released last year, has already pushed this debate by arguing that delayed action may be far more expensive than early mitigation. Economists will dispute discount rates and damage functions, as they should. But common sense suggests that waiting until the climate system provides unambiguous catastrophe as proof is a ruinous strategy. By then, the relevant carbon will already have been emitted.

Others will object that uncertain science cannot justify coercive policy. But all public policy deals in uncertainty. We set interest rates without knowing the next recession. We inspect food without knowing which shipment will cause illness. We fund militaries without knowing the next war. What matters is not certainty but probability, severity, reversibility, and available alternatives. Climate change scores high on severity, low on reversibility, and increasingly high on available alternatives. That is a strong case for action.

The most important political change required is psychological. Climate change is slow by the standards of television news and fast by the standards of geology. Its causes are ordinary: driving, heating, manufacturing, eating, building. Its victims are dispersed across space and time. These features make it easy to evade responsibility. There is no villain with a smoking gun; there are billions of tailpipes and smokestacks. Yet moral responsibility does not disappear because it is shared. If anything, shared responsibility is the definition of politics.

Can we stop global warming? We can no longer stop the fact of it. But we can still stop our sleepwalking. We can stop treating the atmosphere as an open sewer. We can stop pretending that uncertainty is ignorance. We can stop building a civilization whose prosperity depends on changing the chemistry of the air faster than human institutions can respond. The science does not tell us exactly what to do; that is a task for politics, economics, and ethics. But the science does tell us enough to know that delay is itself a decision. It is a decision to gamble with other people’s coastlines, harvests, water supplies, and futures.

Emanuel’s essay is measured, not apocalyptic. That is why it should be alarming. The case for action no longer depends on speculative nightmares. It rests on observations already made, mechanisms already understood, and risks any prudent society would insure against. We do not need panic. We need seriousness. And seriousness begins by admitting that the climate problem is not a distant environmental cause. It is the central infrastructure challenge of the twenty-first century. If we start now, we can still shape its scale. If we wait for certainty, we will have chosen the most reckless experiment of all.
