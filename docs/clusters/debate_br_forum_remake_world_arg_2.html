"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies the constitutive link between slavery and capitalism, his framework of 'racial capitalism' risks understating the fundamental distinctiveness of the plantation system as a form of social organization based on property-in-persons rather than contract."

Walter Johnson's essay is a major contribution to the debate on slavery and capitalism, and it deserves to be read carefully by anyone interested in the history of the modern world. Yet in its emphasis on the global and structural dimensions of racial capitalism, it may understate the distinctiveness of the plantation system and the challenge it posed to conventional categories of political economy.

Johnson's central claim is that the history of Manchester never happened without the history of Mississippi—that capitalism and slavery were constitutively intertwined, not accidentally connected. This claim has substantial support, especially when we consider the Atlantic economy as a whole. The profits of the slave trade funded industrialization, the cotton of the South fed the mills of the North and England, and the financial instruments developed for the slave trade were adapted for other purposes.

Yet the plantation system was not simply a regional variant of industrial capitalism. It was a distinct form of social organization, one that combined commercial logic with the control of labor through property-in-persons in ways that fundamentally differed from the wage labor of industrial capitalism.

The key difference is in the mechanism of labor control. The wage worker is formally free; she sells her labor power for a wage, and the employer cannot compel her to work beyond the terms of the contract. The enslaved worker, by contrast, is property; the owner can compel labor through force, and the worker has no legal standing to refuse. This difference in labor control is not merely a matter of degree; it is a difference in kind.

This difference matters for understanding the political economy of slavery. The plantation system was not simply a more efficient or more profitable form of capitalism; it was an alternative to capitalism based on the control of labor through property rather than contract. The "free labor" that Northern industrialists championed was not merely a different form of labor but a different conception of the worker—as a legal subject with rights rather than as property.

Johnson is right that we should not separate the history of the plantation from the history of the factory. But we should also recognize that they were not the same. The plantation was capitalism with a racial twist—the control of black labor through property-in-persons rather than through the market. This twist was not incidental but essential, and it shaped the development of capitalism in ways that continue to affect us today.

The contemporary resonance is clear. The carceral system in the United States, with its disproportionate incarceration of Black Americans, its use of prison labor, and its denial of voting rights, is not simply a legacy of slavery; it is a continuation of the plantation logic under conditions of formal freedom. The worker in the prison is not enslaved, but she is not free in any robust sense either. She is compelled to labor, her rights are severely restricted, and her exploitation contributes to the profits of private corporations. This is not capitalism with slavery appended; it is a specific form of labor control that echoes the plantation.

Johnson's argument is thus not only historical but political. The critique of racial capitalism is a critique of the present as well as the past, and it points toward a transformation that would not merely reform the existing system but fundamentally alter the terms of labor control itself.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The mechanism of labor control in the plantation system represents a difference in kind, not just degree, from the wage labor of industrial capitalism.
  2. 02The plantation system functioned as an alternative to capitalism by replacing market-based labor with the control of labor through property rights.
  3. 03The 'free labor' ideology of Northern industrialists was based on a fundamentally different conception of the worker as a legal subject with rights.
  4. 04The logic of the plantation system persists today in the American carceral system, which utilizes specific forms of labor compulsion and rights restrictions that echo slavery.
  5. 05A political critique of racial capitalism must aim to fundamentally alter the terms of labor control rather than merely reforming the existing system.

"While Walter Johnson’s synthesis of 'racial capitalism' is a powerful political framework for seeking justice, his broad definition of the term risks sacrificing the analytical precision needed to distinguish the unique proprietary nature of slavery from wage-based industrial capitalism."

Walter Johnson's essay is a major intervention in the debate on slavery, capitalism, and justice, and its synthesis of the Du Boisian tradition deserves wide attention. Yet in its emphasis on the global and structural dimensions of racial capitalism, it may understate the distinctiveness of the plantation system and the challenge it posed to conventional categories of political economy.

Johnson argues that the history of capitalism makes no sense separate from the history of the slave trade. The plantation and the factory were nodes in a single network, and the profits of the slave trade funded the industrial revolution. This argument is powerful, but it raises questions about what we mean by "capitalism" and whether the concept is adequate to the task Johnson assigns it.

If capitalism is defined broadly, as any economy organized through markets and the pursuit of profit, then the plantation was certainly capitalist. But if capitalism is defined more narrowly, as an economy organized through wage labor and the market exchange of labor power, then the plantation was not capitalist but something else. The enslaved worker was not a seller of labor power; she was property, and the master's claim to her labor was not contractual but proprietary.

Johnson acknowledges this distinction but argues that it should not be overemphasized. The plantation was commercially oriented, and its operations depended on market relationships; the enslaved were not only workers but capital. The plantation was thus "capitalist" in important respects.

Yet there is a risk in this framing. If "capitalism" can include both the wage labor of industrial capitalism and the slave labor of the plantation, then the concept becomes so broad that it loses its analytical power. What is gained by calling both systems "capitalist"?

Perhaps the answer is that "racial capitalism" is not so much an analytical concept as a political one. It is a way of linking the history of slavery to the history of capitalism in order to make claims about the present. If capitalism was constitutively intertwined with slavery, then the racial hierarchy that slavery created is not a residue that can be eliminated by equal opportunity reforms.

This is a powerful political claim, but it should be distinguished from a neutral historical analysis. The question "was slavery capitalist?" is not only a historical question; it is also a political question, and its answer depends on what we want the concept of capitalism to do.

Johnson's essay is valuable precisely because it insists on this political dimension. The critique of racial capitalism is not only a matter of historical understanding; it is a framework for political action. By showing that the system is historically contingent, that it was made and can be unmade, it offers hope that another world is possible.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defining capitalism broadly enough to include both the plantation and the factory may render the concept so expansive that it loses its specific analytical power.
  2. 02The relationship between a master and an enslaved person was fundamentally proprietary rather than contractual, creating a distinct economic logic from the market exchange of labor power found in conventional capitalism.
  3. 03The term 'racial capitalism' functions more effectively as a political tool for linking historical slavery to modern systemic inequality than as a neutral historical or economic category.
  4. 04The answer to whether slavery was capitalist depends less on objective historical data and more on the specific political goals one wants the concept of capitalism to achieve.

If capitalism means simply the pursuit of profit through market exchange, then the plantation was capitalist—but so was the household economy of the ancient world, and so is almost every economy that has ever existed.

Walter Johnson's essay is a major contribution to the literature on slavery and capitalism, and its synthesis of the Du Boisian tradition deserves wide attention. Yet in its emphasis on the global and structural dimensions of racial capitalism, it may understate the distinctiveness of the plantation system and the challenge it posed to the categories of liberal political economy.

Johnson argues that slavery was not an anomaly or a residue of pre-capitalist social relations but an integral part of the capitalist system. The plantation and the factory were not separate worlds but nodes in a single network, and the history of Manchester cannot be separated from the history of Mississippi.

This argument is powerful, but it raises questions about what we mean by "capitalism." If capitalism is defined broadly, as any economy organized through markets and the pursuit of profit, then the plantation was certainly capitalist. But if capitalism is defined more narrowly, as an economy organized through wage labor and the market exchange of labor power, then the plantation was not capitalist but something else.

The plantation was not organized through wage labor; it was organized through property-in-persons. The enslaved worker was not a seller of labor power but a piece of property, and the master's claim to her labor was not contractual but proprietary. This is not a subtle distinction; it is a difference in kind.

This difference matters for understanding the political economy of slavery and its legacy. The plantation system was not simply a less efficient or more brutal form of capitalism; it was an alternative to capitalism based on the control of labor through property rather than contract. The "free labor" that Northern industrialists championed was not merely a different form of labor but a different conception of the worker—as a legal subject with rights rather than as property.

Johnson's argument is thus not only historical but philosophical. The critique of racial capitalism is a critique of the categories of liberal political economy, which assume that labor is freely exchanged in markets and that workers are legal subjects with rights. These assumptions were never true for all workers, and they remain not true for workers today who are compelled to labor by poverty, incarcerated in prisons, or denied legal standing as undocumented immigrants.

The contemporary implications are significant. The carceral system in the United States, with its use of prison labor, its denial of voting rights, and its disproportionate incarceration of people of color, is not simply a legacy of slavery; it is a continuation of the plantation logic under conditions of formal freedom. The worker in the prison is not enslaved, but she is also not free in the sense that liberal political economy assumes.

Johnson's essay points toward this critique, but it could be developed further. The relationship between the plantation and the factory, between slave labor and wage labor, between racial capitalism and the liberal order that claims to supersede it—these are questions that deserve more sustained attention.

  2. 02Defining capitalism too broadly risks eliding the specific challenge that the plantation system posed to the liberal political economy's conception of the worker as a legal subject.
  3. 03The 'free labor' ideology of Northern industrialists was not just a variation of labor management but a competing philosophical conception of human rights and legal standing.
  4. 04The logic of the plantation persists today in the carceral system, where labor is extracted from individuals who are denied the status of free legal subjects assumed by liberal economic theory.
