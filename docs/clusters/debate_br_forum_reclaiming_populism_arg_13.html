"To be effective in the wake of the COVID-19 pandemic, a reclaimed left populism must move beyond national electoral strategy and embrace a democratic internationalism that recognizes the global interconnectedness of labor, health, and power."

The most important sentence in Adom Getachew’s essay may be the one that insists the defeat of left projects in the United States, Britain, and Bolivia be considered “in a global frame.” Without that frame, the discussion of populism risks becoming another argument about national electoral strategy: whether Bernie Sanders should have done more outreach to older black voters, whether Jeremy Corbyn was undone by Brexit or the press, whether Evo Morales misjudged the referendum and the military, whether the Working Families Party can move Democrats left. These questions matter. But the pandemic has clarified that the crisis of democracy is not only national, and neither can be the reconstruction of “the people.”

COVID-19 is a planetary event passing through a radically unequal world. It has not abolished borders; it has revealed their violence. It has not made all humans equally vulnerable; it has shown how vulnerability is distributed by race, class, gender, citizenship, disability, and imperial power. It has not suspended geopolitics; it has intensified competition over masks, ventilators, tests, medicines, information, and blame. In such a moment, reclaiming populism cannot mean only building a better national majority. It must mean asking whether the language of the people can be stretched beyond the nation-state without losing political force.

That is not an abstract concern. The dominant right-wing response to the pandemic is already a form of nationalist populism. It says: our people were endangered by foreign contamination, globalist elites, open borders, and disloyal cosmopolitans. It transforms public health into border theater. It turns supply shortages into proof that other nations cheated us. It treats migrants as disease carriers. It uses China as both geopolitical rival and racial symbol, making Asian people everywhere vulnerable to suspicion and abuse. It promises protection through exclusion.

A merely domestic left populism can oppose the cruelty of this politics while accepting too much of its structure. It may say: no, the true people are workers, not billionaires; the true enemy is corporate greed, not immigrants; the nation should care for all its residents, not just the rich. That is a necessary correction. But if the imagined people remain primarily national, then internationalism becomes an add-on, a moral clause after the main program. In times of scarcity, add-ons are the first things abandoned.

The pandemic makes such abandonment both immoral and irrational. A virus anywhere can become a virus everywhere. A shortage of medical supplies in poor countries is not a distant tragedy; it is part of a global system of risk. Sanctions that restrict access to medicine are not ordinary foreign policy tools; in a pandemic, they are instruments of mass vulnerability. Deportations can spread disease across borders. Debt burdens can prevent governments from protecting public health. Patent rules and pharmaceutical monopolies can determine who lives. If left populism cannot make these facts central to its account of the people, it will be inadequate to the age.

Getachew’s essay gestures toward this with welcome clarity. The United States is not simply one national community among others trying to protect itself. It is an imperial power whose decisions shape the life chances of people far beyond its borders. Its sanctions affect more than twenty countries. Its competition for medical supplies can outbid poorer states. Its immigration enforcement can export danger. Its political rhetoric can inflame racism globally. Its central bank and fiscal decisions can reverberate through the world economy. A U.S. left that speaks only in the language of domestic redistribution will miss the scale of responsibility.

This is one lesson to draw from the fall of recent left projects. Corbynism, Sandersism, and the MAS in Bolivia are very different phenomena. One operated in a parliamentary system shadowed by Brexit; one in the Democratic primaries of a two-party presidential system; one in a country marked by indigenous mobilization, resource nationalism, and military intervention. But each faced the difficulty of building popular sovereignty under conditions of global constraint. Financial markets, media systems, commodity dependence, trade regimes, diplomatic pressure, and transnational capital do not politely stop at the edge of the demos. A national people may elect a left government, but that government immediately confronts forces organized beyond the nation.

Right-wing populists understand this in distorted form. They blame globalists, migrants, international institutions, and foreign competitors. Their answer is sovereignty as domination: walls, tariffs, police, extraction, militarism, and executive power. Liberals answer with a defense of rules-based international order, but that order has often meant austerity, unequal trade, intellectual property regimes, border externalization, and humanitarian language masking hierarchy. The left needs a third answer: democratic internationalism.

Democratic internationalism begins from the fact that popular self-government is impossible when economic life is governed by unaccountable transnational power. Workers cannot govern their conditions if corporations can move production to evade labor rights. Communities cannot govern their futures if debt payments dictate budgets. Public health cannot be democratic if medical knowledge and supplies are monopolized. Climate policy cannot be national alone when emissions, extraction, and ecological damage are globally organized. Migrants cannot be treated as outsiders to democracy when their displacement is often produced by wars, trade policies, climate change, and economic arrangements in which powerful states are implicated.

A left populism adequate to this reality would define its antagonists internationally: not “foreigners,” but tax havens, pharmaceutical monopolies, fossil fuel corporations, arms dealers, authoritarian oligarchs, debt enforcers, border profiteers, and states—including the United States—that maintain global hierarchy. It would define its people not as a culturally unified nation, but as the workers, caregivers, migrants, debtors, patients, prisoners, and communities whose lives are subordinated to profit and empire. This is harder to communicate than “America First” or even “Medicare for All,” but difficulty is not an excuse for evasion.

The immediate COVID-19 agenda offers concrete starting points. The United States should lift or suspend sanctions that impede access to medical supplies and financial support. It should halt deportations and immigration raids. It should include undocumented workers and mixed-status families in health care and economic relief. It should support global efforts to produce and distribute tests, protective equipment, and treatments according to need rather than purchasing power. It should back debt relief for poorer countries facing public health emergencies. It should reject vaccine nationalism before it becomes entrenched. It should treat anti-Asian racism as a political danger, not merely a matter of civility.

These demands are not foreign policy luxuries. They follow from a basic understanding of public health and justice. They also help clarify what is wrong with the Trump administration’s posture and with Democratic efforts to outflank Trump on China. Criticizing the Chinese government’s handling of the outbreak, secrecy, or repression is legitimate. Turning China into a racialized object of blame is dangerous. When Biden releases an ad implying Trump was too soft on China, he may hope to neutralize Republican attacks, but he participates in a spiral that harms Asian Americans and narrows the space for international cooperation. A left internationalism must be able to criticize states without demonizing peoples.

The domestic racial dimensions of the pandemic reinforce the point. Black communities in Chicago and Milwaukee are dying at horrifying rates not because the virus selected them, but because the United States has organized health, housing, labor, and environment through racial inequality. Prisons and jails are becoming sites of infection because the carceral state has concentrated vulnerable people in cages. Essential workers are disproportionately women, immigrants, and people of color because care and service labor have been historically devalued. These are domestic expressions of a global system that sorts people into differential exposure to death. The same logic that renders a black bus driver disposable renders a garment worker in Bangladesh, a nurse in the Philippines, a migrant farmworker in California, or a sanctioned patient in Iran disposable.

This is why the language of essential work should be internationalized. The pandemic has revealed a global care chain. Nurses move across borders. Migrant workers harvest food. Women perform paid and unpaid care in households, hospitals, and elder facilities. Remittances sustain families and economies. Protective equipment is manufactured through international supply chains. The national economy is not a container; it is a node in a web of labor. A politics that says “our workers first” misdescribes the world. Many of “our” workers are from elsewhere, support people elsewhere, and labor alongside supply chains that bind their fate to others.

Of course, politics still happens through states. Hospitals need funding from governments; relief checks are issued by treasuries; labor law is national; elections determine executive power. A planetary people cannot simply vote in a planetary parliament. This is the strongest objection to internationalist populism: it may be morally expansive but institutionally vague. National welfare states were built because workers could pressure states; global solidarity often lacks comparable levers.

The objection is serious. But the conclusion should be institutional invention, not national retreat. Unions can organize across supply chains and support international labor standards. Cities can form networks around public health, climate, and migrant rights. Parties can coordinate platforms on debt, taxation, climate, and trade. Social movements can target multinational corporations. States governed by the left can renegotiate trade agreements, support global public goods, and refuse sanctions. Public banks and development institutions can be redesigned. International law can be used not only to protect investors but to protect labor, health, and climate. None of this is easy. But neither was building national labor law or universal health systems.

The Green New Deal debate already points in this direction. Climate change is the slow pandemic, a global emergency whose burdens fall first on those least responsible. A Green New Deal confined to national job creation and infrastructure would be insufficient. It must include climate finance, technology sharing, just transitions for the global South, and repair for communities harmed by extraction. The same applies to pandemic recovery. If stimulus spending rebuilds fossil fuel dependence, hardens borders, and rescues corporations without changing ownership or labor power, the crisis will deepen ecological and social breakdown. If recovery is linked to care, decarbonization, public health, and global solidarity, it can become the first step toward a different order.

What then of populism? I am ambivalent. The word carries democratic energy because it insists that ordinary people, not oligarchs, should rule. It can puncture the false neutrality of technocracy and expose the class power hidden in “responsible” policy. But populism also tends to seek a people coherent enough to act, and coherence is often purchased through exclusion. The nation supplies a ready-made boundary. Internationalism troubles that boundary. A “planetary populism” may sound inspiring, but it risks becoming too diffuse to organize.

Perhaps the answer is to think of populism as one register within a broader internationalist politics. At the local and national level, movements must make claims in the name of the people: the people need hospitals, not corporate bailouts; tenants need homes, not evictions; workers need power, not praise; black communities need life, not abandonment; migrants need protection, not detention. But these claims should always be linked outward. The people whose lives matter do not stop at the border. The antagonists who block democracy do not stop there either.

This outward linkage also changes domestic strategy. It prevents the left from defining working-class interest in nationalist terms. It challenges the idea that jobs must be protected by militarized borders or hostility to foreign workers. It insists that public health includes prisons, detention centers, reservations, colonies, and sanctioned countries. It treats anti-racism and anti-imperialism as central to economic democracy. It asks American workers to see themselves not as victims of the world, but as potential participants in a global majority against oligarchy and abandonment.

Such a politics will face accusations of utopianism. But the supposedly realistic politics of the last decades has produced a society unable to provide masks to nurses, health care to the sick, savings to workers, or safety to prisoners. It has produced supply chains efficient in profit terms and fragile in human terms. It has produced global institutions that protect capital more reliably than life. It has produced nationalist leaders who can close borders but not organize testing. If this is realism, we need better dreams.

Getachew is right that the crisis will not automatically create the transformation we seek. Catastrophe can breed solidarity, but it can also breed hoarding, scapegoating, and authoritarianism. The right has a simple story ready: outsiders endangered us, traitors weakened us, strong borders will save us. The center has another: competent managers will restore normalcy. The left must offer a story equal to the crisis: normal was the emergency; our lives are interconnected; the people who sustain the world should govern it; no one is disposable, whether in Chicago, Milwaukee, Guayaquil, Lagos, Tehran, Wuhan, or a detention center at the U.S. border.

Reclaiming populism is worthwhile only if it helps tell that story. If it narrows our imagination to a better-protected nation, it will fail. If it names a global majority against the forces that sacrifice life to profit and power, it may yet become part of a democratic internationalism adequate to the pandemic age.
