"The left should abandon rather than reclaim populism because its inherent logic of a unified people against a corrupt elite is antithetical to pluralistic democracy and prone to authoritarianism."

Adom Getachew’s thoughtful lead essay opens a crucial debate about the future of populism on the left. But it also, inadvertently, demonstrates why the term should be abandoned, not reclaimed. In an era when right-wing populists from Narendra Modi to Viktor Orbán to Donald Trump have used the rhetoric of “the people” to assault democratic institutions, minority rights, and the rule of law, the pursuit of a left-led, multiracial populism risks reinforcing the very political style it claims to oppose. The language of populism is not a neutral tool that can be easily separated from its exclusionary and authoritarian tendencies. Even when espoused by progressives with the best of intentions, it carries a logic that is antithetical to the pluralist, institutionally mediated democracy we should be defending. Instead of reclaiming populism, the left would do better to articulate a robust, pluralistic democratic vision that refuses the binary of the pure people versus the corrupt elite—a vision that embraces the messy, often frustrating work of coalition-building, compromise, and institutional reform.

The first problem with the populist frame is its constitutive ambiguity about who “the people” are. Getachew, following Michael Denning, notes that “every politician is a populist” in the sense that all democratic politics must appeal to some conception of the people. This is true as a matter of formal description, but it elides the qualitative distinction between a politics that treats the people as a diverse collection of citizens with competing interests and one that invokes a singular, unified people whose will is claimed to be self-evident and indivisible. The liberal democratic tradition, for all its flaws, has generally sought to accommodate pluralism through institutional mechanisms: separation of powers, federalism, judicial review, free press, and the protection of individual and minority rights. These mechanisms are designed precisely to prevent any single faction from claiming to embody the authentic voice of the people. Populism, by contrast, tends to regard such institutions as obstacles to the expression of popular sovereignty. The populist leader claims a direct, unmediated connection to the people, bypassing intermediaries like parties, courts, and the media. This is not a bug of populism; it is its defining feature. To think that a left populism can adopt that same structure without replicating its pathologies seems to me wishful thinking.

The historical record bears this out. The first great left-populist experiment in the Americas was the Mexican Revolution, whose Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) built its rule on a rhetoric of the “revolutionary people” while constructing a one-party state that crushed dissent, co-opted unions and peasant organizations, and systematically foreclosed genuine pluralism. In Argentina, Juan Perón’s populism did deliver real material gains for the working class, but it did so through a corporatist structure that concentrated power in the executive, persecuted opponents, and cultivated a cult of personality around the leader—a template that would later be used by right-wing populists as well. In Venezuela, Hugo Chávez’s Bolivarian Revolution, for all its genuine social achievements, gradually eroded the independence of the judiciary, the media, and the opposition, leaving a political system so hollowed out that his successor could preside over economic catastrophe and massive repression while still claiming the mantle of “the people.” These episodes are not simply deviations from an otherwise pure left-populist ideal; they are the working-out of a political logic that concentrates authority in the executive, delegitimizes dissent, and treats institutional constraints as anti-democratic.

Getachew might reply that the populism she and the Working Families Party are reclaiming is different: a pluralist, multiracial, democratic populism that eschews the strongman style. But can one have populism without the strongman? The very structure of a political appeal to a unified “people” against a “corrupt elite” tends to demand a focal point of identification—a leader who embodies the popular will. Even when the leader is not a Chávez or a Morales, the movement’s energy flows toward the top of the ticket. The symposium Getachew describes was animated by the prospect of a Sanders presidency. Sanders himself is a man of genuine principle, but the campaign’s structure was unmistakably leader-centric. When he lost, the “political revolution” was suddenly thrown into question. This is the inherent fragility of a politics that leans too heavily on the figure of the populist tribune. Contrast this with the approach of a more institutionally minded left, one that focuses on building durable organizations—unions, community groups, cooperative enterprises, independent media—that do not rise or fall with a single electoral cycle. That kind of left does not need to claim to speak for the whole people; it advances the interests of working people through the slow, cumulative work of organizing and legislating.

A second danger of the populist frame is its tendency to oversimplify conflicts into a moral binary of the virtuous people and the corrupt elite. Real democratic politics is far more complicated. The working class is not a monolithic bloc; it is riven by race, gender, region, religion, and sector. Employers and political elites are not a unified cabal; there are real conflicts between, say, finance capital and industrial capital, or between anti-immigrant nationalists and cosmopolitan globalizers. A politics that frames every issue as a struggle between the people and the elite is poorly equipped to navigate these complexities. It can encourage a kind of political Manichaeism that sees compromise as betrayal and treats allies who are not “of the people” as enemies. In the United States, this dynamic has a particular racial valence. Historically, the invocation of “the people” in American politics has often been code for white people—the “producer” class arrayed against both parasitic elites and racialized Others. The original Populist Party of the 1890s contained both interracial solidarity currents and virulent white supremacist strands, and it was the latter that ultimately triumphed in the Jim Crow South. Today’s left populists are, of course, consciously anti-racist. But the rhetorical structure of “us versus them” can still activate deep-seated nativist sentiments, especially when the “elite” is portrayed as cosmopolitan, globalist, or beholden to foreign interests. The Sanders campaign struggled with this tension; its emphasis on class unity sometimes made it difficult to address the specific concerns of Black voters in the South, who did not see themselves simply as part of an undifferentiated “working class” but as a community with a distinct history and political agenda.

The liberal alternative, which Getachew’s essay implicitly criticizes, is not the bloodless centrism of the pre-Trump era. It is a pluralist democratic socialism—or social democracy, if you prefer—that embraces the goals of economic justice but pursues them through a politics of coalition, compromise, and institutional transformation. This is a politics that takes seriously the Madisonian insight that factions are inevitable and that the only way to prevent tyranny is to multiply them, creating a system in which many different interests must negotiate and bargain. Such a politics does not speak of “the people” but of working families, organized labor, civil rights organizations, environmentalists, and faith communities, each with their own perspectives and priorities. It does not promise to overthrow the establishment but to reform it, step by step, through legislative action, regulatory enforcement, and judicial appointments. It is, admittedly, less exciting than the romantic vision of a people rising as one. But it is also more resilient, less prone to the authoritarian temptation, and better suited to governing a deeply diverse society.

Getachew is right that the COVID-19 crisis has exposed the catastrophic failures of our current political economy and that universal health care now appears an obvious response. But she also warns that the fallout could “deepen the rightward trend of domestic and global politics.” That warning should give us pause. One reason right-wing populism has been so successful is that it offers simple, emotionally satisfying narratives of national betrayal and redemption. A left populism that mirrors that structure, even with progressive content, risks reinforcing the same cognitive habits—the distrust of institutions, the impatience with procedure, the longing for a savior—that the right has so successfully exploited. We would do better to offer a different paradigm entirely: not the politics of the angry People, but the politics of patient, pluralistic, institutional reconstruction. That means defending and improving the democratic institutions we have—Congress, the courts, the administrative state—rather than deriding them as corrupt barriers to the popular will. It means rebuilding the mediating institutions—unions, civic associations, local parties—that have atrophied over the past half-century. And it means crafting a public language that is capacious enough to acknowledge difference, conflict, and uncertainty, rather than subsuming them under a mythic unity.

In practice, this might look like a New Deal without the populist rhetoric—or rather, with a rhetoric that was always more pluralist than we remember. Franklin Roosevelt’s “economic royalists” speech was an outlier; much of the New Deal’s appeal was technocratic, experimental, and coalitional. It worked not because Roosevelt channeled a unified popular will but because he assembled a broad electoral coalition—urban machines, Southern segregationists, Midwestern farmers, industrial workers—and then delivered concrete policies that benefited each component, often at the cost of messy compromises. That coalition was deeply flawed, especially in its accommodation of white supremacy, and any contemporary equivalent would have to be rigorously anti-racist. But the lesson is that durable progressive change does not require a unified “people.” It requires a smart, organized, and patient political strategy that can win elections, pass laws, and build institutions over the long haul.

The left does not need to reclaim populism. It needs to reclaim the neglected virtues of democratic pluralism: patience, reciprocity, institutional craftsmanship, and a deep appreciation for the fact that in a free society, the people will never speak with one voice. That is not a weakness to be overcome by the right kind of leader or the right kind of rhetoric. It is a permanent condition of democratic life, and the only safe politics is one that builds that insight into its very structure.
