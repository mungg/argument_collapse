Diversified DeepSeek

"While the lead essay correctly identifies reproduction as a technologically and socially mediated process, it fails by uncritically centering biological and genetic relatedness as the primary goal of family-making."

The lead essay opens with an evocative piece of history: the infant incubator as a “queer little shop” invention, and it closes with a stirring call for solidarity across the women whose stories it tells. In between, it presents a thesis that deserves to be taken seriously: that all reproduction, even the most apparently natural, is always technologically and socially mediated. This is a powerful anti‑naturalist tool, one that serves well to challenge the hegemony of what the essay calls the “School of Great Experience.” But I want to argue that the essay’s relentless centering of biological reproduction—its assumption that the ultimate goal of these technologies is the production of a genetic child—unwittingly reinforces a different kind of normativity. By treating the desire for a biologically related child as a universal and unquestioned good, the essay risks marginalizing forms of family‑making that do not revolve around genes, gestation, or the fantasy of a “mixture of us two.”

My point is not to shame the people whose stories are told. Their longing is real, and their experiences of homophobia, transphobia, and medical gaslighting are appalling. But the political framework into which those stories are inserted tells a story about what reproduction means. Over and over, the essay suggests that the deep wound is the inability to produce a child that is “biologically” yours. N and K, exhausted by people asking “Why don’t you just adopt?” respond with a line that the essay treats with sympathy: “We cannot have a biological child that is both of ours.” B, though she considered adoption, ultimately recoiled from foster‑parent training because it taught her to guard against attachment; she wanted an experience of parenting that started without the rehearsal for loss. S freezes her eggs as an insurance policy against a future in which she may not find a partner; the eggs represent genetic material saved for later use. Even micha cárdenas, whose work is the most politically radical in the essay, banks sperm in the shadow of transphobic violence, framing the production of a biological child as an act of resistance against a “genocidal” world.

What is missing from this picture is any robust account of families formed through adoption, foster care, step‑parenting, communal care, or other forms of non‑biogenetic kinship. The essay mentions these possibilities only to dispatch them. B’s ten‑week training is presented as a trauma machine. Adoption is dismissed by N as a thing that straight couples don’t get hectored about. The analysis never pauses to ask whether the intense pull toward genetic connection might itself be a cultural product—a modern inheritance of the same ideology of blood that underwrites everything from monarchies to anti‑miscegenation laws. Queer and feminist theory have long offered resources for thinking otherwise. Kath Weston’s *Families We Choose* (1991) documented the rich networks of care that gay and lesbian people built in the shadow of the AIDS crisis, networks that often had nothing to do with biological procreation. Judith Butler’s *Antigone’s Claim* (2000) challenged the Oedipal family as the necessary horizon of psychic life. These traditions suggest that the desire for a genetic child is not an irrational given, as the essay concludes, but a desire shaped by a society that continues to treat blood relation as the gold standard of kinship. When the lead essay treats that desire as beyond politics, it naturalizes the very thing it otherwise seeks to denaturalize.

There are also more immediate, practical reasons to be uneasy about the essay’s bio‑focalism. The foster‑care system in the United States is a disaster, but its disaster is a product of austerity, racism, and a punitive family‑regulation system, not of an inherent flaw in non‑biological parenting. A reproductive justice framework that takes Dorothy Roberts seriously would demand the transformation of that system rather than its abandonment. Similarly, adoption can be ethically fraught, particularly transracial and transnational adoption, but it is still a way that hundreds of thousands of families are formed. To treat adoption as a poor substitute for “real” reproduction is to send a painful message to adopted people and their parents, a message that directly contradicts the essay’s own anti‑naturalist commitments.

What might a different essay look like? It would still listen carefully to the women described here, but it would place their stories alongside those of foster parents, adoptive parents, step‑parents, and people who have chosen not to have children at all but who devote their lives to caring for other people’s kids. It would ask not just how we can make biological reproduction more accessible, but how we can make all acts of care—whether or not they produce a genetic legacy—socially honored and materially supported. It would take Firestone’s critique of the biological family to its logical conclusion, questioning why the parent‑child dyad, with its intense psychological investments, remains the default container for human vulnerability. Firestone herself called for the “diffusion of the child‑rearing function” among many adults in a collective household. That vision has been all but forgotten in our current moment, when single parents like B are left to finance IVF alone while their friends lend them money. Why are those friends not co‑parents? Why is the two‑parent nuclear family, biological or not, the only model we can imagine?

The Boston Review essay admirably shows that the natural is a cover story for inequality. But the genetic family, too, can be a cover story—for a culture that fears the messiness, the risk, and the profound social responsibility of raising a child who is not “your own.” A genuinely queer reproductive politics would loosen the grip of that story, opening space for children to be claimed not by the accident of gametes but by the ongoing work of care. Until we are ready to have that conversation, our assisted reproduction will remain what it so often is today: a highly expensive, technologically sophisticated way of reproducing the most conventional of family forms.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay treats the desire for a biologically related child as an irrational, universal given rather than a cultural product shaped by a society that privileges blood relations.
  2. 02By dismissing adoption and foster care as traumatic or inferior alternatives, the essay reinforces the very naturalistic normativity it claims to challenge.
  3. 03The focus on biogenetic kinship ignores a rich history of queer and feminist theory that advocates for 'chosen families' and the diffusion of childcare responsibilities beyond the biological parent-child dyad.
  4. 04A truly radical reproductive politics should prioritize the social work of care and communal child-rearing over the technological reproduction of the conventional nuclear family form.
  5. 05The essay's framework overlooks the structural failures of the foster care and adoption systems, which a reproductive justice lens should seek to transform rather than abandon.
