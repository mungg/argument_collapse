Diversified Claude

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has failed because it imposes enormous compliance costs and damages American capital markets without effectively preventing corporate fraud or financial crises."

Sarbanes-Oxley is the cautionary tale of legislation written in panic. A decade in, the costs are enormous, the benefits are speculative, and the damage to American capital markets is finally becoming visible.

Start with the price tag. Compliance with Section 404 alone runs into millions of dollars per year for mid-sized public companies, and total annual SOX compliance costs across U.S. issuers have been estimated at well over $1 million per company on average — with smaller filers bearing a wildly disproportionate share relative to their size. That money does not vanish; it is diverted from R&D, hiring, and capital investment into armies of internal auditors, outside consultants, and documentation that few investors ever read.

Look at the IPO market. The number of companies going public in the United States is a fraction of what it was in the late 1990s, and the share of global IPOs happening in New York has collapsed relative to London and Hong Kong. Surveys of venture-backed CEOs routinely cite SOX compliance as a major reason to stay private, sell to a strategic acquirer, or list abroad. We have effectively raised the toll for accessing public equity markets — and the result is fewer public companies, less retail-investor access to growth firms, and more concentration of ownership among private-equity insiders.

And what did we buy with all this? The post-SOX era has produced Bear Stearns, Lehman, AIG, MF Global, the Madoff fraud, and a parade of restatements. The premise of SOX — that prescriptive internal-controls documentation and executive certifications would deter fraud — has not been borne out. Determined fraudsters sign certifications anyway; Madoff filed flawless paperwork for years.

The worst aspects are precisely the most onerous: Section 404(b)'s external-auditor attestation, the PCAOB's expanding and unaccountable rulemaking, and the criminalization of ordinary disclosure judgments. Congress finally exempted smaller filers in the JOBS Act, an implicit admission that the regime was indefensibly costly. But the same logic should apply more broadly. SOX has not prevented the crises it was sold to prevent; it has simply taxed the honest companies that would never have committed fraud in the first place.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The financial burden of compliance, particularly Section 404, diverts millions of dollars away from productive investments like R&D and hiring.
  2. 02The law has stifled the U.S. IPO market by incentivizing companies to remain private, sell to acquirers, or list on foreign exchanges to avoid regulatory hurdles.
  3. 03The act has failed its primary purpose of deterring fraud, as evidenced by major financial collapses and scandals like those of Lehman Brothers and Bernie Madoff.
  4. 04The regulatory regime disproportionately penalizes honest companies through onerous requirements like external-auditor attestations and the criminalization of disclosure judgments.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has failed because it imposed staggering compliance costs that stifled the U.S. public equity market while failing to prevent major financial collapses or address systemic risks."

By almost any measure that matters, Sarbanes-Oxley has failed. It was sold to the public as a shield against the kind of fraudulent accounting that vaporized Enron and WorldCom, yet a decade later the statute looks less like a shield and more like a straitjacket that did nothing to stop the 2008 meltdown. Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers imploded not because of hidden off-balance-sheet partnerships but because of reckless leverage and toxic mortgage securities — risks that SOX’s narrow focus on audit committees and internal controls never touched. If a law cannot prevent the largest financial collapse since the Great Depression, calling it a success requires a breathtakingly low bar.

The compliance burden has been staggering, and it fell most heavily on the wrong shoulders. Small and mid-sized public companies, the engines of innovation and job creation, were forced to divert millions into Section 404 audits of internal controls, often duplicating work already done. The predictable result: a hollowing out of the U.S. public equity market. IPOs dried up, many firms went private or stayed private, and a generation of growth companies sought capital in less regulated foreign venues. This was not a minor side effect; it was a structural shift that undermined the very market transparency SOX claimed to protect.

Meanwhile, the foreign competition that critics warned about materialized vividly. London, Hong Kong, and other exchanges siphoned off listings that might have come to New York, in part because foreign issuers recoiled from SOX’s extraterritorial reach and its criminalization of honest mistakes. The law transformed corporate governance into a checklist exercise, where boards spent more time ticking boxes than exercising judgment. And for all that, the scandals never stopped — they simply migrated to areas the law ignored. SOX gave us an expensive audit culture while leaving systemic risk, executive compensation excess, and the shadow banking system completely untouched. Ten years on, it has proven to be an expensive failure, not a safeguard.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The law's narrow focus on internal controls and audit committees failed to address the reckless leverage and toxic assets that caused the 2008 financial crisis.
  2. 02The high cost of Section 404 compliance has burdened small and mid-sized companies, leading to a decline in IPOs and a shift toward private or foreign capital markets.
  3. 03The act's extraterritorial reach and criminalization of errors have driven international listings away from U.S. exchanges to competitors like London and Hong Kong.
  4. 04Sarbanes-Oxley reduced corporate governance to a checklist exercise that prioritizes box-ticking over the exercise of sound business judgment.
  5. 05The statute failed to stop corporate scandals, which simply migrated to unregulated areas like executive compensation and the shadow banking system.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has failed because it imposed staggering compliance costs on businesses while failing to prevent major financial collapses."

**The Verdict After a Decade: Guilty**

Ten years after Sarbanes-Oxley became law, the evidence is damning. This legislation — rushed into existence by a panicked Congress after Enron and WorldCom — was always more about political theater than genuine reform. The results confirm that suspicion.

Consider the record. Bear Stearns collapsed in March 2008. Lehman Brothers followed in September of that year. These were not small companies hiding accounting tricks; they were pillars of the American financial system, and they fell spectacularly while SOX was fully in force. The law did nothing to stop them. It could not stop them, because the core problems — excessive leverage, opaque derivatives, and a federal Reserve that failed to use its regulatory authority — were simply beyond SOX's reach. We enacted a law targeting accounting fraud and called it financial reform. That is the fundamental failure.

Meanwhile, the costs of SOX compliance have been staggering. Smaller public companies routinely spend millions of dollars annually on auditors, consultants, and internal staff dedicated solely to Section 404 compliance. This is money that does not go to research, hiring, or investment in growth. It goes to paperwork and attorneys. For smaller firms, SOX has become a barrier to going public at all, pushing entrepreneurs toward private equity or foreign exchanges where the regulatory burden is lighter. The law has effectively rationed access to public capital for smaller businesses.

The irony is that genuine accountability did not require a mandate. Companies that wanted to signal integrity to investors could have adopted internal controls voluntarily. SOX forced that choice on everyone, including companies where the risk of fraud was negligible. We created a compliance industrial complex that benefits lawyers and consultants far more than shareholders.

After ten years and two massive financial crises, it is time to admit that SOX achieved the appearance of reform while accomplishing far less than its proponents promised.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The law failed to prevent the collapse of major financial institutions like Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers because it ignored systemic issues like excessive leverage and opaque derivatives.
  2. 02The high cost of Section 404 compliance diverts essential capital away from research, hiring, and growth investment.
  3. 03The regulatory burden of the law acts as a barrier to entry for smaller companies, discouraging them from going public or driving them to foreign exchanges.
  4. 04The mandate creates a 'compliance industrial complex' that benefits auditors and consultants rather than providing genuine protection for shareholders.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has failed because it imposes staggering compliance costs that distort capital markets while failing to address the substantive risks and incentive structures that lead to corporate collapse."

**An Expensive Shield That Misses the Real Fight**

Sarbanes-Oxley represents a classic regulatory mistake: it addressed the last crisis rather than thinking carefully about the next one. Ten years later, American businesses have paid enormous compliance costs, and we have very little to show for it in terms of the risks that actually materialized.

The compliance burden of SOX has been staggering and poorly targeted. For larger companies, the costs are absorbed and largely passed on to consumers and shareholders. For smaller public companies, Section 404 can consume meaningful portions of operating budgets. This is not merely an inconvenience — it is a structural distortion of capital markets. When compliance costs are high relative to company size, the rational choice for many entrepreneurs is to stay private longer, to seek foreign listings, or to sell to larger companies that can absorb the overhead. SOX has made American capital markets less accessible to the businesses that need them most.

More fundamentally, the law created a compliance culture that distracts from genuine corporate governance. Companies now have elaborate systems designed to satisfy auditors and regulators, and they spend enormous energy on documentation. Whether any of this actually produces better decision-making, more honest reporting, or more responsible risk-taking is unclear. What is clear is that Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers had excellent audit firms, presumably compliant internal controls, and boards populated by experienced directors — and they still failed spectacularly.

The deeper problem is that SOX treats fraud as the only threat worth addressing. But corporate governance also requires attention to incentive structures, risk culture, and the relationship between executive compensation and risk-taking. These are harder things to regulate, and they cannot be fixed with a checklist. A decade of expensive compliance has given us the appearance of accountability without its substance. That is the real failure — not that SOX was wrong, but that it was too narrow and too shallow to matter where it counted most.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The high cost of compliance creates a structural barrier that discourages smaller companies from entering American public capital markets.
  2. 02The law fosters a checklist-driven compliance culture that prioritizes documentation over genuine improvements in corporate decision-making and risk-taking.
  3. 03The failure of major firms like Lehman Brothers demonstrates that compliant internal controls do not prevent spectacular corporate collapses.
  4. 04By focusing narrowly on accounting fraud, the law ignores more critical governance issues such as incentive structures and executive compensation.
