"The Araki Yasusada hoax must be understood as a strategic response to the marginal and prestige-driven economics of the contemporary American poetry market, which demands novelty but offers few paths for unknown poets to achieve it."

I want to focus on a dimension of the Yasusada affair that Marjorie Perloff touches but does not develop: the relationship between literary hoaxes and the economics of the poetry market. This context is essential for understanding why Kent Johnson might have chosen this particular strategy.

Contemporary American poetry operates in a market so marginal that most published poets cannot support themselves through their writing alone. The prestige economy — who publishes where, who is reviewed in which venues, who is invited to which universities for readings — matters far more than any material compensation. In this economy, novelty is at a premium. A new voice must be recognizably different from existing voices while also fitting within recognizable categories.

Johnson faced a particular problem: he was an unknown poet seeking attention in an over-crowded field. His previous work, including the anthologies The Third Wave and Beneath a Single Moon, suggested interests in cross-cultural poetry and Buddhist themes. But these interests, however genuine, would not automatically distinguish him in a market where every serious poet has cultivated some specialty.

The invention of Yasusada solved several problems at once. First, it provided a way to publish experimental poetry without the burden of personal authorship. The poetry could be as radical as Johnson wished — employing the "new sentence," incorporating obvious anachronisms, refusing to resolve its own ambiguities — but it would not be read as the work of Johnson himself. Second, it positioned Johnson as a "translator," a role that carries different prestige than "poet." Translators are seen as cultural mediators, as figures who have access to traditions inaccessible to ordinary readers. Third, the Hiroshima frame gave the work an urgency and significance that pure experimentation would lack. This was not a formal exercise; this was testimony.

None of this excuses Johnson's choices, but it helps explain them. The poetry market is not a neutral arena where merit is recognized; it is a system of preferences and exclusions that shapes what can be published and read. Johnson worked within that system, manipulating its preferences to get his work noticed. Whether this manipulation is "criminal" or "brilliant" depends on how one weighs the competing values of individual integrity and systemic critique.

Perloff compares Johnson to James McPherson, whose Ossian poems were accepted as authentic Gaelic translations for decades. But McPherson was not competing in a marginal market; he was a young Scottish poet seeking recognition within a literary culture that valued antiquarian knowledge. The stakes were different. Johnson, operating in late-twentieth-century America, faced a market that had no room for him unless he could make himself remarkable. The hoax was his way of making himself remarkable.

I am not arguing that economics excuses anything. But any analysis of the Yasusada affair that ignores the material conditions of poetry publication is incomplete. The hoax is not simply a meditation on authenticity; it is also a comment on the absurdity of a literary culture that demands continuous novelty while providing no means for achieving it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The invention of Yasusada allowed Kent Johnson to bypass the burden of personal authorship and publish radical experimental poetry that would have otherwise been ignored.
  2. 02Adopting the persona of a 'translator' granted Johnson the prestige of a cultural mediator with exclusive access to a foreign tradition.
  3. 03The Hiroshima framing provided the work with a sense of moral urgency and historical significance that formal poetic experimentation alone could not achieve.
  4. 04The hoax serves as a systemic critique of a literary culture that prioritizes identitarian positioning and 'newness' over individual merit.
  5. 05Unlike historical hoaxes like Ossian, the Yasusada affair is specifically a product of a late-twentieth-century market that forces poets to use extreme measures to become remarkable.
