A Historian's Footnote: We Have Been Here Before

Dan Breznitz writes as if his case for diversified innovation strategy and skepticism about the Silicon Valley monoculture were a new intervention against a settled orthodoxy. As a matter of intellectual history, it is not. The arguments he makes have been made before—by Friedrich List in the 1840s, by the German historical school in the late nineteenth century, by Alexander Gerschenkron on late development, by Chalmers Johnson on MITI, by Robert Wade and Alice Amsden on East Asia, by Ha-Joon Chang on infant industries, by Mariana Mazzucato on the entrepreneurial state. The fact that Breznitz can publish his essay as a fresh contribution tells us something important about the amnesia of American policy discourse. The tradition of political-economy thinking about industrial development that flourished in the mid-twentieth century has been so thoroughly displaced by market-fundamentalist economics that arguments which would have been commonplace in 1955 now feel like daring revisions.

This matters in two ways. First, it means we have a richer body of thought to draw on than Breznitz acknowledges. Second, it means we have a sobering historical record about how hard the alternatives are to execute. Let me take each in turn.

The richer body of thought. Breznitz's stages-of-production framework rhymes with a long line of thinking about technological capabilities and the ladder of comparative advantage. List argued in the 1840s that Britain's preaching of free trade to Germany was a kind of "kicking away the ladder"—an attempt to deny to later developers the protective policies Britain itself had used. Gerschenkron argued in the 1950s that late developers face qualitatively different conditions than early developers and need correspondingly different institutional arrangements (universal banks for Germany, the state for Russia). Hirschman wrote about backward and forward linkages in ways that anticipate Breznitz's stages. Amsden's account of South Korea—Asia's Next Giant, 1989—analyzed exactly the kind of stage three "learning" that Breznitz attributes to Taiwan, showing how Korean firms moved from licensed production to original equipment manufacture to original brand manufacture through deliberate state guidance. Wade's Governing the Market made the parallel case for Taiwan with great empirical rigor. Chang's Kicking Away the Ladder synthesized the historical evidence that today's rich countries all used the very policies they now forbid to developing ones.

This is a serious intellectual tradition, and it has been marginal in American policy discourse for decades, partly because the discipline of economics drifted away from political economy after the 1960s and partly because the policy world fell under the spell of the Washington Consensus from the 1980s. The result is that we now rediscover, with each generation, things we used to know. Mazzucato's The Entrepreneurial State (2013) made waves by pointing out that the iPhone's key technologies came from public research—a fact that would have been entirely unsurprising to anyone reading economic history in 1965. Breznitz's stages framework is genuinely useful, but it is most powerful when read as part of this older tradition rather than as a stand-alone insight.

The sobering record. The same historical literature gives us reason to be cautious about implementing Breznitz's recommendations in the contemporary American context. The East Asian developmental states succeeded under conditions that are difficult to reproduce: Cold War geopolitics that gave the U.S. reason to tolerate their managed trade; insulated, meritocratic bureaucracies built on Japanese colonial and Confucian foundations; tight elite networks that allowed policy coordination among state, banks, and firms; weak labor and farm interests that could be subordinated to industrial policy; and crucially, a permissive global trade regime before the WTO that allowed them wide policy latitude. None of these conditions obtains in the United States today. American political institutions are not configured for the kind of patient, technocratic industrial policy that produced TSMC. We have a fragmented federal system, an adversarial relationship between Congress and the executive, capture-prone regulatory agencies, and a politics that demands visible short-term wins.

The American history of industrial policy is not actually one of pure laissez-faire—DARPA, the NIH, the land-grant universities, the agricultural extension service, the postwar manufacturing extension partnership, the Department of Energy national labs, and so on, are all examples of state-led innovation infrastructure—but it has been industrial policy without the dirigiste coordination capacity that East Asia mobilized. We have done well at funding basic research and at large mission-oriented projects (Manhattan, Apollo, Warp Speed). We have done less well at the patient, sectoral, capability-building industrial policy that Breznitz wants. The reasons are deep in our political institutions, not in our intellectual menu.

This gives me three concerns about Breznitz's program. First, the framework risks understating how much of what produced Taiwanese or Italian success was institutional and historical rather than strategic. You cannot order an industrial cluster the way you order a policy paper. Brenta has the cluster it has because of centuries of leather-working, postwar regional politics, the persistence of small-firm ownership, and a particular Italian relationship between regional governments and industry. Trying to teleport this to Cleveland is not a strategic choice; it is a wish.

Second, the framework can flatter the policymakers who adopt it. "We are pursuing stage three innovation" sounds more rigorous than "we are trying to figure out what works." But the stages framework is a description of outcomes, not a recipe for getting them. Saying that Taiwan succeeded at stage three innovation does not tell us how to do stage three innovation, any more than saying that Silicon Valley succeeded at stage one tells us how to replicate it. The honest answer in both cases is that success depended on a long historical sequence of institution-building and luck, much of which is not replicable.

Third, by framing the issue as "Silicon Valley got it wrong," Breznitz somewhat misidentifies the opponent. The opponent of better American industrial policy is not the Silicon Valley evangelist who thinks every city should be Palo Alto. It is the broader political-economic regime—forty years of financialization, antitrust abdication, public investment austerity, and deregulation—within which Silicon Valley triumphalism is just one symptom. Fixing it requires changes much further upstream than choosing a different innovation stage to specialize in.

I want to end on a positive note. Breznitz's essay is the kind of writing we need more of: empirically rich, historically attentive, willing to challenge orthodoxy. My quibble is mainly with framing. The argument is more powerful when situated within the rich tradition of political-economy thinking about development, and more honest when paired with acknowledgment of how hard the institutional preconditions for his program are to build. The fact that this conversation is happening at all, in venues like Boston Review, is a sign of intellectual health. The fact that we have to keep re-discovering arguments that were standard fare in our grandparents' generation is a sign of how much ground we have to recover.

Vanilla Claude

"While Breznitz correctly identifies that cities should pursue diverse stages of innovation beyond the Silicon Valley model, his framework overlooks the institutional, political, and labor-market structures required to actually achieve inclusive prosperity."

Breznitz's essay performs a valuable service by puncturing the cargo-cult mentality that has gripped local economic development since the 1990s. Walk into almost any mayor's office from Columbus to Cairo and you will find the same PowerPoint slides: incubators, accelerators, "innovation districts," tax breaks for venture capital, a science park named after some local notable. The implicit theory is that if you assemble enough of the visible furniture of Silicon Valley, the magic that produced Google and Facebook will somehow materialize. Breznitz is right that this almost never works, and that even when something like it does work—as in Israel—the social results are often dismaying.

Where I want to push back, or at least complicate, is in three areas: the cleanness of the four-stage taxonomy, the political economy of choosing a stage, and the question of what the Silicon Valley model actually is.

First, the four stages. Breznitz presents Shimano, Giant, MediaTek, Brenta footwear, and Shenzhen assembly as exemplars of distinct innovational "stages," each capable of supporting inclusive prosperity if cultivated correctly. The framework is illuminating as a corrective—it forces us to see that Apple's industrial designers and TSMC's process engineers and Foxconn's logistics planners are all doing innovation, not merely executing on Cupertino's ideas. But in practice these stages bleed into one another. Giant did not stay a stage-three improver; its work on carbon fiber frames is, by any reasonable definition, novel product development. MediaTek's reference designs are arguably as inventive as anything Qualcomm produces. The Pearl River Delta's "stage four" prowess increasingly drives stage-one decisions upstream, because firms like DJI emerged precisely because Shenzhen's assembly ecosystem made it cheap to iterate on drone prototypes. The danger of a four-stage taxonomy is that policymakers, who love nothing more than a checklist, may treat the stages as discrete bins to be chosen between rather than as overlapping capabilities that reinforce each other.

Second, the political economy. Breznitz is admirably blunt that to excel at one stage you must forgo others, and that public-private leadership is required to make these choices. But the essay is curiously quiet about who, in a place like Youngstown or Saint-Étienne, is supposed to do the choosing. Taiwan's Industrial Technology Research Institute, the Israeli Innovation Authority, and the Brenta footwear consortium are products of unusual institutional histories: developmental states emerging from existential security threats, regional craft traditions with centuries of accumulated tacit knowledge, post-authoritarian industrial planning capacity. When American cities try to copy these models, they typically run into the same problem they encountered with Silicon Valley emulation—you cannot conjure the institutional substrate by force of will. The U.S. lacks a national innovation agency on the ITRI model; its community colleges are starved of operating funds; its industrial extension service (the Manufacturing Extension Partnership) operates on a budget that would embarrass a mid-sized Taiwanese ministry. Telling Cleveland to "become a stage-three hub" without addressing this institutional deficit risks producing yet another round of disappointment.

Third, on what Silicon Valley actually is. Breznitz frames the Valley as a stage-one VC-and-exit machine, and for the post-1995 Valley this is largely accurate. But the deeper history is more interesting and more supportive of Breznitz's own argument than he acknowledges. The Valley that produced Fairchild, Intel, Hewlett-Packard, and even the early Apple was itself a stage-two-and-three hub: it had fabs, it had production engineering, it had thousands of mid-skill manufacturing jobs, and it had massive sustained public investment via Defense Department procurement. The thing we now call "Silicon Valley"—an asset-light, software-and-finance complex producing extreme inequality—is what the original Silicon Valley turned into after it offshored its production capacity. So the cautionary tale is not just "don't copy Silicon Valley"; it is "don't let your successful stage-one cluster hollow out its connections to the other stages," which is exactly what Schwinn did and what the U.S. semiconductor industry mostly did before TSMC's belated invitation to Arizona.

This sharpens Breznitz's policy conclusions in a useful way. The "tech teens" argument is the strongest part of the essay, and it deserves more emphasis than it gets. Companies in the five-to-fifteen-year range, with revenue, real customers, and local roots, are systematically underserved by both public policy and private capital, which are calibrated to the bimodal distribution of seed-stage moonshots and Fortune 500 giants. The Small Business Innovation Research program, the Manufacturing Extension Partnership, and procurement-based instruments like Operation Warp Speed are tools that can be expanded without waiting for Washington to develop ITRI-grade planning capacity. They are the kind of unglamorous instruments that Breznitz's argument actually calls for.

One last note: Breznitz says little about the labor side of inclusive prosperity. Taiwan and Germany sustain mid-skill jobs not only through stage-three innovation but through vocational training systems, sectoral wage bargaining, and labor-market institutions that the United States has largely dismantled. A community that becomes a stage-three hub without union density or wage standards will discover that "good jobs at all skill levels" do not automatically follow from innovation capacity. Innovation policy and labor policy must be designed together, or the inclusive part of inclusive prosperity will once again go missing.

  3. 03The modern Silicon Valley model is a cautionary tale of what happens when a successful innovation cluster offshores its production and hallows out its connections to other stages of development.
  4. 04Focusing on 'tech teens' and existing unglamorous policy tools like procurement and industrial extension is more practical than attempting to build new national innovation agencies.

Vanilla GPT

"While disaggregating innovation into different stages is a useful framework, achieving inclusive prosperity requires moving beyond technical innovation models to implement a political economy that actively protects workers and prevents wealth extraction."

Dan Breznitz is right to insist that innovation is not synonymous with venture-backed invention, and that the Silicon Valley model is a poor guide for places seeking broad-based prosperity. In fact, the most useful part of his argument is not the criticism of Silicon Valley itself, which many people now share, but the disaggregation of innovation into different stages. Once we see innovation as design, engineering, production, maintenance, scaling, and continuous improvement—not only as the launch of a new platform or app—we can begin to imagine economic strategies that are less glamorous but more democratic.

Still, the hardest question is not whether there are many forms of innovation. There are. The harder question is how to make any of them inclusive under contemporary capitalism. The danger is that policymakers will replace one fetish with another: instead of fetishizing startups and venture capital, they may fetishize “advanced manufacturing,” “clusters,” “supply chains,” or “ecosystems” without confronting the institutions that determine who captures the gains. A region can be innovative in production and still generate precarious work, low wages, environmental damage, and political dependency. The lesson to draw from Silicon Valley’s failures is not simply that we should innovate at other stages of production. It is that innovation policy must be joined to labor policy, social policy, competition policy, and democratic planning.

Consider Silicon Valley itself. Its early history was not only a story of entrepreneurs and venture capitalists; it was also a story of federal procurement, Cold War spending, university research, immigration policy, and a dense regional labor market. Public money helped create the technological base, while private firms captured much of the upside. Over time, as the region shifted toward software platforms, network effects, intellectual property, and financialized exits, the distance between winners and everyone else widened. Housing costs exploded, service workers were pushed into punishing commutes, and local governments competed to host firms whose tax strategies often minimized their local contribution. The problem was not innovation as such. The problem was a political economy that allowed innovation to become a mechanism for extraction.

That same risk exists in the alternative models Breznitz describes. Taiwan’s semiconductor ecosystem is rightly admired, and the United States has much to learn from it. But the lesson is not simply “build fabs.” Semiconductor production requires enormous capital investment, patient coordination, technical training, supplier depth, and reliable demand. It also requires decisions about water, energy, land use, and the distribution of public subsidies. If the United States pays foreign or domestic firms billions of dollars to locate plants in Arizona, Ohio, or Texas without demanding strong labor standards, technology diffusion, supplier development, and local training, it may get impressive facilities without durable regional prosperity. A fab can be a cathedral in the desert unless it is embedded in a broader institutional system.

The same is true of stage four production and assembly. Shenzhen’s rise is one of the most important economic stories of the last forty years. Its capacity to prototype, source, assemble, and scale at astonishing speed is a genuine form of innovation. But the Pearl River Delta also grew through migrant labor systems, long hours, dormitory work regimes, and environmental costs that should not be treated as incidental. To call production innovation “inclusive” because it employs millions risks setting the bar too low. Employment matters enormously, especially compared with jobless growth. But inclusion must also mean voice, mobility, safety, bargaining power, and the ability to build a stable life.

This point matters for American regions trying to recover from deindustrialization. Many communities do not just lack startups; they lack institutional power. They have lost anchor firms, union density, vocational systems, local banks, technical schools, and the civic associations that once connected business decisions to community welfare. When a city or state chases the next big employer with tax abatements, it is often negotiating from desperation. The resulting jobs may be better than nothing, but they rarely rebuild the local capacity to shape economic development. Amazon’s HQ2 competition was an extreme example of this dynamic, but smaller versions happen constantly. Public officials offer subsidies; firms promise jobs; the public receives little guarantee of long-term commitment.

Breznitz’s emphasis on “tech teens” is therefore especially promising. Firms that are past the fragile startup phase but not yet dominant are often more rooted in place and more likely to need exactly the kinds of public goods that governments can provide: testing facilities, export assistance, workforce training, procurement contracts, and help meeting regulatory standards. Operation Warp Speed’s support for vaccine-related manufacturing showed that public procurement can do more than buy finished goods; it can create capabilities. The same principle could apply to heat pumps, grid equipment, batteries, medical supplies, public transit, building retrofits, and low-carbon industrial materials. Decarbonization is not merely an environmental imperative. It is a chance to organize demand for useful production.

But public support should come with public obligations. If taxpayers help firms scale, taxpayers should not merely hope that benefits trickle down. Contracts can require domestic or regional production where appropriate, prevailing wages, apprenticeship programs, neutrality toward union organizing, profit-sharing, limits on stock buybacks, and commitments to local supplier development. Public equity stakes or revenue-sharing arrangements may also be justified when the state assumes major technological or market risk. This is not hostility to business. It is recognition that innovation is collective, and that collective investment should produce collective returns.

The United States has some institutional models to build on. Community colleges, land-grant universities, manufacturing extension partnerships, national laboratories, and sectoral training initiatives can all support non-Silicon Valley innovation. Germany’s Fraunhofer Institutes are often invoked for a reason: they help translate research into industrial capability, especially for small and medium-size firms. The American Manufacturing USA institutes created over the last decade point in a similar direction, though they remain too small relative to the scale of the challenge. The problem is not an absence of examples; it is the weakness and fragmentation of the American developmental state.

A further challenge is that local strategy cannot be purely local. Breznitz’s examples are place-based, but they succeed in relation to national and global systems. Taiwan’s achievements depended on national industrial policy, export discipline, public research institutions, and geopolitical positioning. Italian industrial districts depended on craft traditions, regional institutions, and access to high-value European markets. Israeli high tech depended on military research, immigration, and deep integration with U.S. capital markets. Local leaders matter, but they cannot overcome every macroeconomic force. Exchange rates, trade rules, antitrust enforcement, health care costs, infrastructure, and federal research spending all shape what localities can plausibly do.

This is especially important because globalization did not merely “open entry points”; it also created hierarchies. Lead firms often control branding, standards, data, customer relationships, and intellectual property, while suppliers compete on thinner margins. A region specializing in production engineering or components may do well, but it may also become vulnerable to the purchasing power of dominant multinationals. Apple’s supply chain, for example, contains extraordinary manufacturing knowledge, but Apple captures a disproportionate share of profits because it controls design, software, brand, and ecosystem. Any strategy focused on middle stages of innovation must ask how local firms will avoid being permanently squeezed.

That brings us back to competition policy. If a small number of platform companies, pharmaceutical giants, agribusiness firms, or defense contractors dominate markets, local innovation ecosystems will struggle to flourish independently. Antitrust enforcement, open standards, interoperability, limits on exclusionary contracting, and public options in key sectors can all create space for smaller firms and regions. Innovation policy without competition policy may simply subsidize future acquisition targets for incumbents.

The most attractive vision in Breznitz’s essay is of innovation as a collective civic project. That vision is badly needed. For too long, American economic development has oscillated between two inadequate models: laissez-faire faith that entrepreneurs will save us, and subsidy-driven recruitment of footloose corporations. Neither builds the thick local capacities required for shared prosperity. Communities need institutions that can diagnose their strengths, coordinate investment, train workers, share technical knowledge, and adapt over time. They need patience, because capabilities accumulate slowly. And they need humility, because not every place can or should pursue the same niche.

Yet humility should not become resignation. The pandemic exposed the fragility of supply chains built only for cost minimization. It revealed shortages in masks, testing supplies, ventilator components, pharmaceutical inputs, and semiconductors. It also showed that government can move quickly when it chooses to: funding research, guaranteeing markets, coordinating production, and absorbing risk. The question now is whether that emergency capacity can be turned into a durable democratic capacity.

Silicon Valley’s greatest error was not that it celebrated invention. Invention is necessary and often magnificent. Its error was to present one narrow, financialized version of invention as the whole of innovation and then to confuse private fortunes with public success. A better innovation politics would value the machinist improving a production process, the nurse helping redesign care delivery, the engineer making batteries safer, the technician maintaining a water system, and the small manufacturer adapting a component for a new use. But valuing them rhetorically is not enough. We must build institutions that give them power, security, and a share of the gains.

The future of inclusive prosperity will not be found by copying Silicon Valley, but neither will it be found by copying Taiwan, Germany, Italy, Israel, or Shenzhen wholesale. It will require communities to discover where they can contribute to solving real problems—and governments willing to make markets, discipline capital, and protect workers. Innovation can be a road out of Cobalt. But only politics can decide who gets to travel that road, who pays the tolls, and who owns what is built along the way.

"While Breznitz correctly identifies diverse stages of innovation beyond the Silicon Valley model, his proposal fails to account for the deep-seated political and institutional barriers that prevent policymakers from adopting these alternative strategies."

The Stages Are Right, the Politics Are Missing

Dan Breznitz has done the field of economic development a real service by offering a vocabulary—novelty, design, second-generation, production—that lets us escape the suffocating monoculture of "be the next Silicon Valley." His Taiwanese and Italian examples are genuinely illuminating, and the contrast between Giant and Schwinn ought to be taught in every business school that still treats outsourcing as an unmitigated good. But I want to register a worry: Breznitz writes as though the central problem is intellectual, a matter of policymakers being seduced by the wrong model. I think the problem is mostly political, and that any serious effort to implement his program will run into obstacles he largely sidesteps.

Consider the politics of "tech teens." Breznitz is right that five-to-fifteen-year-old firms with fifty employees are the workhorses of local economies, and right that they sit in a blind spot between venture capital and corporate-incentive packages. But why is that blind spot there? It is not because nobody noticed. It is because the political economy of state and local development policy rewards visible, photogenic interventions: ribbon-cuttings at new Amazon HQ2s, splashy "innovation district" launches, science parks named after sitting governors. A program that quietly capitalizes a hundred mid-sized firms over a decade generates no such payoff for the politicians who fund it. Foxconn in Wisconsin was a fiasco on the merits, but it made perfect political sense. Until we change the incentives facing elected officials, the Cobalt-style boondoggles will keep happening regardless of how persuasive Breznitz's taxonomy is.

A related problem: Breznitz's most successful cases—Taiwan, the Italian industrial districts, Japan's component giants—all rest on institutional substrates that took decades to build and that involved degrees of state coordination American polities find culturally hard to accept. The Industrial Technology Research Institute did not appear because someone in Taipei read a good policy memo. It is the product of a developmental state with a particular history, a particular relationship between bureaucratic elites and industry, and a particular tolerance for picking winners. The Brenta shoe cluster reflects centuries of accumulated craft knowledge plus a postwar Italian regional politics of small-firm cooperation that, frankly, we do not have. Telling Cleveland to become Brenta is only marginally less utopian than telling it to become Palo Alto.

I also think Breznitz lets his enthusiasm for stages two through four make stage one sound easier to dispense with than it really is. Yes, novelty is overrated in popular discourse. But novelty is also where the largest rents accrue, and a country that does only second-generation innovation eventually finds itself paying license fees and IP royalties to the country that does the first. Taiwan is a magnificent example of stage three excellence, but it is also a country whose semiconductor preeminence rests on ASML in the Netherlands and on tools and IP from the United States. The strategy works, but it works within a global division of labor that depends on someone doing the front-end work. The United States's mistake was not pursuing stage one innovation; it was pursuing stage one innovation while letting stages two through four atrophy. Both/and, not either/or.

The deepest tension in the essay, though, concerns inequality. Breznitz uses Israel to show that VC-led stage one growth produces inequality, and Taiwan to show that stage three growth produces broad-based prosperity. Fair enough as a first cut. But Taiwan's relative equality also reflects high union density in certain sectors, an aggressive land reform in the late 1940s and 1950s, near-universal health care, and a labor market structure quite unlike the American one. Even if Cleveland successfully reinvented itself as a stage three hub tomorrow, the resulting good jobs would land in a labor market with weak unions, employer-tied insurance, and dramatic regional cost-of-living differentials. The same production strategy can yield very different distributional outcomes depending on the surrounding institutions. Innovation policy is necessary but nowhere near sufficient for inclusive prosperity.

None of this is to dismiss the argument. I think Breznitz is right that the menu of innovation strategies is broader than the Valley evangelists admit, right that incremental innovation is undersung, and right that public-private R&D ecosystems of the Taiwanese kind deserve emulation. What I want to add is that the binding constraints on adopting his program are political and institutional, not cognitive. Policymakers don't pursue the Silicon Valley dream because they haven't heard of the alternatives. They pursue it because it offers them political cover, because the donor class wants it, and because the institutional capacities needed for the alternatives have been allowed to wither. Rebuilding those capacities—public R&D agencies with real autonomy, vocational education systems with industry buy-in, financing instruments that reward patient growth—is a generation-long project.

So I would amend Breznitz's conclusion. Yes, we have more choices than Silicon Valley evangelists admit. But choices require choosers, and the choosers in American local economic policy are operating in a system that systematically discourages the very strategies Breznitz recommends. The essay's policy advice is excellent. The political theory needed to make it stick is the next book.

Diversified MiniMax

"While Breznitz correctly identifies diverse innovation paths, historical industrial patterns suggest that achieving inclusive prosperity requires building long-term institutional capacity for adaptation rather than just selecting a specific innovation stage."

Breznitz's framework invites historical comparison, and the comparison is illuminating. The concern that regions can specialize in ways that leave them vulnerable to economic shocks is not new; it is a recurring theme in the history of industrialization.

Manchester in the nineteenth century was the world's leading center of cotton textile manufacturing, dependent on cotton grown in American South and sold in global markets. When the American Civil War disrupted cotton supplies, the resulting "cotton famine" devastated the region, throwing hundreds of thousands of workers into poverty. The lesson seemed clear: over-reliance on single industries and external supply chains creates vulnerability. Yet Manchester eventually recovered, not by replicating the Silicon Valley model but by transitioning to other forms of production and by building diversified service sector employment.

The Rust Belt story is similar but more tragic, because the transition came later and was less successful. American industrial cities did not fail because they lacked innovation; they failed because of specific policy choices: theNAFTA-driven reorientation of production toward Mexico, the failure to invest in workforce transition programs, the decline of labor protections, and the ideologically driven hostility to industrial policy. Cleveland and Detroit did not lack ideas about how to diversify their economies; they lacked the public investment and policy support needed to implement those ideas.

What does this history suggest for Breznitz's framework? First, it suggests that over-specialization is genuinely risky, and that communities should seek diversification within and across innovation stages rather than putting all their eggs in a single basket. Second, it suggests that public investment is essential for transition—that when industries decline, the question is not whether government should intervene but how it should intervene. Third, it suggests that the timeline for successful transition is measured in decades, not years, and that political systems oriented toward short-term electoral cycles will struggle to implement the kind of sustained strategy that successful transition requires.

Breznitz is right that the Cobalt model of resource extraction followed by abandonment is not a path to inclusive prosperity. But the alternative is not simply to make smarter choices about which innovation stage to pursue. It is to build institutions capable of managing ongoing economic change—not because change can be prevented, but because communities that can adapt successfully will survive and those that cannot will decline.

The history of industrialization offers grounds for cautious optimism: most regions that have suffered major economic shocks have eventually recovered, provided they had the institutional capacity and public support to adapt. The challenge for contemporary policymakers is to build those capacities before the shocks arrive, not after.

  3. 03Successful economic transitions require public investment and a long-term strategic timeline that transcends short-term electoral cycles.
  4. 04The primary goal for policymakers should be building institutional resilience that allows communities to manage and adapt to ongoing economic change.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While Breznitz correctly identifies the flaws of the Silicon Valley model, his proposed four-stage framework underestimates the immense difficulty of deliberately cultivating specific innovation capabilities and ignores the political and economic barriers to implementing alternative models."

Breznitz is right that the Silicon Valley model has been oversold to local policymakers. But in correcting one set of myths, he risks constructing another. His four-stage framework is analytically elegant, yet it understates how difficult it is for any community to deliberately cultivate stage-specific capabilities, and it glosses over what may be the real obstacle to inclusive innovation: not the absence of better models, but the political economy that makes those models so hard to implement.

The Israel example is compelling but not entirely fair. By 2021, Israel had achieved remarkable things beyond high-tech prosperity. Its start-up density per capita remained the highest in the world, and companies like Mobileye and Jerusalem's own tech ecosystem had created genuine wealth. The inequality is real, but so are the tens of thousands of middle-class jobs in cybersecurity, biotech, and enterprise software that didn't exist in 1968. Breznitz frames Israel's trajectory as a cautionary tale, yet the country navigated from a post-socialist economic backwater to a global innovation powerhouse in a single generation. That is not nothing, even if the distribution of its benefits was uneven.

The more interesting question is whether Taiwan's model is actually exportable. Breznitz presents it as a paradigm to emulate, but Taiwan's success in stages two and three emerged from highly specific conditions: a government that made industrial policy a national priority for decades, a geographic position that made it indispensable to global supply chains during the Cold War, and a society that invested heavily in technical education before the high-tech boom. The Industrial Technology Research Institute was not created in a vacuum; it reflected a state-led development philosophy rooted in Taiwan's particular circumstances. Other regions have tried similar approaches—Brazil's attempt to build a domestic aircraft industry through Embraer, South Korea's cultivation of Samsung and Hyundai—with mixed results. The lesson may be less "follow Taiwan's stages" and more "understand your specific position in global networks and build from there."

More fundamentally, the four-stage framework assumes a clarity of strategic choice that most local economies do not possess. Breznitz argues that communities should "foster particular capacities—to the exclusion of others." But this implies a degree of coordination between public and private sectors that is exceptionally rare. The firms that know which stage they occupy are making that calculation moment by moment, responding to global competitive pressures, not following a government blueprint. Giant knew to pivot to carbon fiber frames because Schwinn abandoned it; that was opportunistic adaptation, not stage-based planning. The Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company became a pure-play foundry because it recognized an opening in the early 1990s, not because policymakers had designated "stage three" as the national priority. Strategy emerges from market signals and firm-level capabilities, not from stages that scholars impose retrospectively.

There is also the challenge of political sustainability. Breznitz calls for investment in "collective goods"—education systems, shared production facilities, collaborative public spaces. These are precisely the investments that have proven hardest to sustain in an era of fiscal austerity and short-term electoral cycles. The community colleges, technical schools, and regional development agencies that once supported stage-two and stage-three industries have been hollowed out across the United States and Europe. Restoring them would require not just money but a political consensus that markets alone cannot provide the coordination needed for inclusive growth. Breznitz acknowledges this at the end of the essay, but the difficulty deserves more weight than a closing paragraph suggests.

The "tech teens" concept is the most promising idea in the essay and deserves fuller treatment. Breznitz is right that the focus on start-ups and giants misses the companies that actually anchor local tech ecosystems. But the mechanisms that sustain tech teens—patient capital, patient customers, institutional networks that reward longevity over exit—are exactly what VC-dominated markets have eroded. Creating them requires not just policy tweaks but a willingness to question the financialization of innovation itself. That is a harder argument than "learn from Taiwan," and it may be the one we most need to hear.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies that communities should look beyond the Silicon Valley model for inclusive growth, his framework overlooks the necessary symbiosis between innovation stages and the critical role of political governance in ensuring equitable outcomes."

Dan Breznitz’s sweeping critique of the Silicon Valley model is both timely and necessary. In the wake of COVID-19, as communities scramble to rebuild, his warning against chasing shimmering mirages of high-tech start-ups and venture capital is a vital antidote to the boosterism that has drained local coffers from Kansas to Kazakhstan. His framework—distinguishing innovation that yields inclusive prosperity from the winner-take-all dynamics of stage-one novelty—offers a granular and hopeful map for economic renewal. Yet in his eagerness to debunk myths, Breznitz at times underplays the symbiosis among the four stages he describes, and skates too quickly over the political economy of their implementation. Without addressing these tensions, his otherwise compelling blueprint risks replacing one kind of cargo-cult thinking with another.

Breznitz’s account of Israel’s high-tech miracle is a case in point. He rightly shows that an obsessive focus on NASDAQ exits and foreign VC widened Israel’s gulf between a “geek elite” and the rest, leaving a fifth of households in relative poverty. But the problem was not stage-one innovation itself; it was the way Israel’s innovation agency, for all its foresight, embedded its tech sector almost entirely within U.S. financial circuits. Israeli firms innovated, then handed profits and intellectual property over to overseas investors. That extractive design is a political choice, not an inherent feature of novel-product development. Taiwan, which Breznitz celebrates for its stage-three prowess, has also attracted vast foreign capital into its semiconductor foundries. Yet Taiwan’s model—with state-led research institutes, local ownership structures, and dense supplier networks—kept the gains circulating domestically. The lesson is not to abandon stage one for later stages but to demand that the fruits of any innovation stage be anchored locally through deliberate policy. Tech teens, which Breznitz champions as better rooted in community, could thrive in stage one too, if given patient capital and public procurement, as Operation Warp Speed demonstrated. The SiO2 success in Alabama was a stage-one breakthrough in material science, funded by the government, that simultaneously expanded local employment. The villain is not novelty but the exit-obsessed venture model.

A second strain in Breznitz’s argument is that communities must choose which stage to specialize in, cultivating “particular capacities—to the exclusion of others.” This makes strategic sense in a global division of labor, but it understates how stages feed on one another in the medium term. The ascent of MediaTek, a Taiwanese stage-three champion, relied on stage-one breakthroughs in integrated-circuit design from the wider industry, along with advances from global giants like Qualcomm and ARM, whose early R&D was supported by U.S. public investment. If every region had abandoned stage-one research in favor of incremental improvement, the technological frontier would stall—harming even those who specialize in making existing products better and cheaper. The United States’ belated panic over losing semiconductor fabrication capacity is illustrative: by allowing stage-three capabilities to wither, America jeopardized its own stage-one design firms, which depend on close collaboration with cutting-edge fabs. Regions do need to focus, but the most resilient ecosystems—like Taiwan’s or even Silicon Valley’s own early decades—maintain a strategic mix across stages, often with public institutions bridging the gaps. The binary “pick your stage” framing may mislead local leaders into creating another kind of brittle monoculture.

Then there is the question of what “inclusive prosperity” actually means across Breznitz’s successful examples. He praises the Pearl River Delta’s stage-four innovation for creating millions of jobs and lifting migrants out of rural subsistence. That is no small achievement. But by 2021, the region had become synonymous with grueling working conditions, wage suppression, and rampant labor-rights violations that critics have likened to a new form of exploitation. Shenzhen may have offered a path out of deeper poverty, but the quality of its inclusion is heavily contested. Brenta’s shoe-design ecosystem, by contrast, relies on highly skilled craftspeople and has secured a premium position in global luxury supply chains—yet it serves only a narrow, artisanal labor market. Taiwan’s stage-three model has kept inequality relatively low, but it has done so in a society with a distinct history of state-led development, universal education, and small-firm networks that are not easily replicable. Breznitz is right that stage-two and stage-three innovation can support a wider range of skills, but his case studies hint that context—especially labor protection regimes, education systems, and the political will to redistribute—determines whether “inclusive” means decent wages for many or a slightly larger middle class amid stark divides.

Breznitz is on firmer ground when he calls for robust public goods: collaborative spaces, shared production facilities, and active networks that stitch industries into communities. Yet the political conditions for such investment are fragile. The Biden administration’s early pushes for industrial policy have been beset by partisan gridlock, and the Trump administration’s last-minute plea to TSMC to build fabs in Arizona came with no coherent plan for training workers or developing the supplier base that makes Taiwanese foundries so agile. Brenta’s artisanal revival and Taiwan’s institute-led partnerships each emerged from decades of sustained, often undemocratic, government steering. Today’s local leaders may lack the patience or power to replicate those conditions, especially when they are pressured to show quick wins to taxpayers and bond markets.

Ultimately, Breznitz’s essay succeeds in its primary mission: smashing the myth that Silicon Valley is the one true path. But the response should not be to then elevate stage two, three, or four as magic bullets. The deeper insight—which the essay gestures toward but does not fully explore—is that the governance of innovation trumps its stage. Equity, local ownership, reinvestment of profits, and the creation of high-road employment depend on deliberate institutional design, not just on finding the “right” niche in the global production chain. As communities scarred by deindustrialization and pandemic disruptions weigh their next moves, they would do well to heed Breznitz’s call to look beyond high-tech hype. But they must also resist the lure of any single-stage template, and instead build the messy, adaptive, and deeply political ecosystems that let innovation of many kinds serve the many, not the few.

  2. 02Breznitz’s recommendation that regions specialize in one stage to the exclusion of others risks creating brittle economic monocultures, as different stages of innovation often depend on one another for long-term resilience.

Diversified MiniMax

"While Breznitz’s four-stage innovation framework provides a vital roadmap for local prosperity, policymakers must tailor their investments to specific stages based on regional capabilities and institutional strengths rather than attempting to replicate the Silicon Valley model."

The most valuable contribution Breznitz makes is the four-stage framework for understanding innovation, and I want to explore its implications for policymakers who must actually make choices about where to invest scarce public resources.

Stage one innovation—novelty creation—is genuinely important but extraordinarily difficult to support through policy. The history of "picking winners" in technology policy is largely a history of failure, with governments missing transformative innovations while lavishing support on technologies that never achieved commercial viability. The United Kingdom's support for the Ferranti Mark 1 computer, Japan's attempt to dominate the fifth-generation computer project, the European Union's various attempts to build a competitor to GPS—all are cautionary tales. Yet without public support for research that has no immediate commercial application, the pipeline of new technologies will dry up.

The solution, I suggest, is not to fund applied research that duplicates private sector efforts but to fund basic research at universities and public institutions, allowing the private sector to commercialize discoveries that flow from this foundation. The United States has done this reasonably well through the NIH, DARPA, and basic research funding at the National Science Foundation. The problem is that this support has been eroded over decades while resources have been redirected toward corporate welfare and tax breaks for the wealthy.

Stage two innovation—design, prototype development, and production engineering—may be the most promising target for place-based industrial policy. The Italian clusters Breznitz describes show that specialized capabilities in these stages can generate sustained competitive advantage and inclusive employment. But these clusters do not emerge spontaneously; they require institutions that transmit tacit knowledge, that provide specialized training, and that enable collaboration among firms that might otherwise compete rather than cooperate. In the United States, these institutions have atrophied as policy has focused on "innovation" in the abstract rather than on the specific capabilities that enable particular industries to flourish in particular places.

Stage three innovation—second-generation improvement of existing products and components—is where most employment in manufacturing actually occurs, and it is where the United States has suffered the greatest losses. The semiconductor industry is the paradigmatic case: American firms still design cutting-edge chips, but the fabrication has migrated to Taiwan, along with the associated employment and the knowledge that comes from producing complex products. Rebuilding stage three capabilities will require not just investment but institutional change—workforce development programs, apprenticeships, and the kind of public-private collaboration that existed in the mid-twentieth century but has been largely dismantled.

Stage four innovation—production and assembly—is unlikely to return to high-wage economies unless wages are kept low, automation is deployed extensively, or trade policy creates effective barriers to offshoring. Breznitz is right that China has achieved remarkable capabilities in stage four, but replicating these capabilities in the United States would require accepting either low wages, massive public subsidies, or a significant change in trade regime. None of these options is politically easy.

The honest policy conclusion is that different communities should pursue different stages based on their existing capabilities, their institutional strengths, and their political constraints. Some regions will excel at stage one; others will find their comparative advantage in stages two or three. But this diversity of strategy requires a policy environment that supports experimentation and learning rather than imposing a single Silicon Valley template on everyone. That is Breznitz's most important insight, and it deserves to be taken seriously.

  2. 02Stage two innovation offers the most promising target for place-based industrial policy, provided that governments rebuild the institutions that transmit specialized tacit knowledge and foster firm collaboration.
  3. 03Reclaiming stage three innovation requires a shift from abstract innovation policy toward rebuilding the workforce development programs and public-private partnerships necessary for complex manufacturing.
  4. 04Stage four innovation is difficult to sustain in high-wage economies without politically challenging trade barriers, massive subsidies, or increased automation.
  5. 05Effective economic policy requires a diverse environment that encourages regional experimentation and learning instead of imposing a uniform technological template.
