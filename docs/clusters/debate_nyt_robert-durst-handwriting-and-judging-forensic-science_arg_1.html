The Durst case illustrates both the promise and the danger of forensic evidence. A similarity between the handwriting on two envelopes may be relevant, but it should not be treated as a scientific trump card. Handwriting comparison depends on judgments about slant, spacing, letter formation and habits of abbreviation. The larger problem is that American forensic science grew up mostly inside law enforcement, not inside the scientific research culture. Many techniques became courtroom staples before they were subjected to rigorous validation. The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report made this point forcefully: with the exception of nuclear DNA analysis, many forensic disciplines lacked strong empirical foundations. Hair microscopy, bite-mark comparison, tool marks, fingerprints and handwriting have too often been presented in language far more certain than the underlying studies justify.

Making forensic science dependable requires changing both institutions and courtroom habits. Crime laboratories should be independent from police and prosecutors, or at least insulated from investigative pressure. Analysts should receive only the information needed to perform the test, not a narrative about the suspect’s supposed guilt. There is a temptation, watching the Durst case unfold alongside a documentary's dramatic flourishes, to treat handwriting analysis as junk and move on. That would be a mistake. The right question is not whether forensic disciplines are perfect, but whether they are useful, and what institutional arrangements make them more or less reliable.

Handwriting comparison is a good test case. Unlike bite-mark analysis, which compares an impression on flesh that distorts and heals, handwriting comparison operates on stable physical artifacts. Studies by Moshe Kam and others in the 1990s found that trained document examiners outperformed laypeople in distinguishing genuine from simulated writing, sometimes substantially. That is not nothing. It does not mean any individual examiner's opinion in any individual case should be treated as gospel — but it does mean the discipline has empirical support that, say, hair-fiber identification largely lacks.
