"To make forensic science dependable and professional, the field must transition from a law enforcement-driven culture of subjective certainty to a scientifically rigorous framework defined by institutional independence and transparent, validated standards."

The Durst case illustrates both the promise and the danger of forensic evidence. A similarity between the handwriting on two envelopes may be relevant, but it should not be treated as a scientific trump card. Handwriting comparison depends on judgments about slant, spacing, letter formation and habits of abbreviation. Those judgments can be useful, especially when combined with other evidence, but the field has not always established clear error rates, uniform standards or the limits of what an examiner can responsibly say.

The larger problem is that American forensic science grew up mostly inside law enforcement, not inside the scientific research culture. Many techniques became courtroom staples before they were subjected to rigorous validation. The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report made this point forcefully: with the exception of nuclear DNA analysis, many forensic disciplines lacked strong empirical foundations. Hair microscopy, bite-mark comparison, tool marks, fingerprints and handwriting have too often been presented in language far more certain than the underlying studies justify.

Making forensic science dependable requires changing both institutions and courtroom habits. Crime laboratories should be independent from police and prosecutors, or at least insulated from investigative pressure. Analysts should receive only the information needed to perform the test, not a narrative about the suspect’s supposed guilt. Blind testing and regular proficiency exams should be routine, and their results should be public enough to build confidence. Accreditation should be mandatory, not aspirational.

Equally important, forensic testimony must become more modest and more quantitative. Examiners should not say that a mark, print or handwriting sample “matches” to the exclusion of all others unless the science can support that claim. They should explain the basis for their conclusion, the quality of the samples, known error rates where available, and the possibility of disagreement among competent analysts. Judges, applying Daubert or similar standards, should demand validation studies rather than deferring to tradition.

Defense lawyers also need fair access to evidence, experts and laboratory notes. A forensic system is not reliable if only one side can afford to test its claims. Finally, scientific research should be funded outside the pressures of individual prosecutions, so that methods can be tested before liberty depends on them.

Forensic science can help courts find the truth. But it becomes dangerous when subjective expertise is dressed up as certainty. The goal should not be to exclude forensic evidence categorically, but to make its strengths, weaknesses and limits visible to juries.

Three reforms would do the most good. First, every pattern-matching discipline needs published, peer-reviewed validation studies establishing how often examiners are right, wrong, and inconclusive on realistic samples. The National Commission on Forensic Science, paired with NIST's scientific role, can drive uniform standards—but only if courts stop reflexively admitting techniques under Frye-era precedents and start applying Daubert seriously.

Whether Durst is convicted or acquitted matters less than whether the case finally pushes judges to demand evidence that forensic evidence works.

Finally, judges must act as rigorous gatekeepers, refusing to admit testimony that overstates certainty or implies a level of accuracy without quantitative backing. The Durst trial will likely turn on a jury’s gut reaction to expert theater. The Durst handwriting dispute illustrates the central problem with much of forensic science: courts have allowed disciplines to sound more scientific than they really are. Handwriting comparison may be useful as an investigative tool, and an experienced examiner may notice similarities that a layperson would miss. It is whether the method has been tested, how often it is wrong, what standards govern it and whether the examiner can explain the limits of the conclusion.

For years, forensic testimony has too often been presented in absolutes: “matches,” “identifications,” “to the exclusion of all others.” That language is dangerous unless the underlying science can support it. The 2009 National Academy of Sciences report was blunt about this problem. Aside from DNA analysis, many forensic fields had not been subjected to the rigorous empirical testing expected in other scientific disciplines. Bite marks, hair microscopy, tool marks, fingerprints and handwriting all vary in the strength of their research base, but they share a history of courtroom acceptance preceding scientific validation.

Making forensic science more dependable begins with humility. Examiners should not be permitted to testify that handwriting “proves” guilt. At most, they should describe observed similarities and differences, explain the known limits of the method and, where possible, give jurors information about error rates. If reliable error rates do not exist, that absence should be disclosed rather than hidden behind professional jargon.

Second, forensic work should be blind whenever possible. If a handwriting examiner is told that police believe Robert Durst is the killer, or is shown only two suspect samples instead of a larger comparison set, the analysis is vulnerable to confirmation bias. Context management is not an insult to examiners; it is a recognition of human psychology. Doctors, scientists and auditors all use blind procedures because good intentions do not eliminate bias.

Third, laboratories and practitioners need mandatory accreditation, certification, proficiency testing and written protocols. A field cannot call itself professional if two experts can examine the same evidence, reach opposite conclusions and neither can point to a common, validated standard for resolving the disagreement.

Finally, judges must stop outsourcing their gatekeeping role to tradition. “We have always admitted this evidence” is not a scientific argument. Forensic evidence should be admitted only to the extent that it has been validated. The goal is not to help defendants or prosecutors. It is to make verdicts depend less on courtroom mystique and more on reliable knowledge.

It would be a mistake to treat the controversy over handwriting in the Durst case as proof that forensic science is useless. Criminal investigations often depend on physical traces: fingerprints, fibers, documents, bullets, blood, digital records. The public is right to worry about exaggerated claims, but it should also worry about a reform movement that throws away practical expertise because it does not look like laboratory physics.

Handwriting examination is not magic. It is also not astrology. People develop habits in spacing, slant, letter formation, pressure, punctuation and spelling. The infamous “cadaver” note in the Berman case, with its misspelling of “Beverly,” is precisely the sort of document that investigators would naturally compare with known writings. If an examiner says only, “these features are consistent with the same writer,” that may be a fair contribution. If the examiner says, “there is no possible doubt,” that is overreach.

The task, then, is calibration. Forensic science should be made more dependable by matching the strength of testimony to the strength of the method. DNA can often support very strong statistical claims because it rests on population genetics and measurable probabilities. Handwriting usually cannot. That does not mean handwriting evidence should be excluded in every case. It means it should be presented as one piece of circumstantial evidence, alongside motive, opportunity, statements, travel records and other facts.

Professionalization requires national standards. Examiners should be trained, certified and tested. Reports should identify the specific features compared, the quality of the samples, any limitations and the level of conclusion reached. Courts should forbid grandiose terms like “individualization” unless the field has demonstrated the scientific basis for them. But courts should not demand an impossible standard of perfection. Juries routinely evaluate eyewitnesses, informants and confessions, all of which can be unreliable. Properly limited forensic testimony may be more transparent than many kinds of evidence already admitted every day.

The Durst case is unusual because it is wrapped in celebrity, television and a long, strange history. But the broader lesson is ordinary: forensic expertise should inform judgment, not replace it. A competent justice system can admit evidence while also explaining its weaknesses. Dependability will not come from pretending that every forensic discipline is DNA. Nor will it come from pretending that experience has no value. It will come from standards, candor and proportion.

The phrase “forensic science” covers very different enterprises, and reform should start by admitting that. It should not convert a visual comparison into a theatrical declaration of guilt.

The path forward is a national research agenda. Forensic disciplines need validation studies using realistic samples, blind examiners and known ground truth. How often do qualified handwriting examiners falsely identify a writer? How often do they falsely exclude? These questions are answerable, but only if government funds independent research rather than leaving professional guilds to validate themselves.

Standards must follow evidence. If research shows that a technique is reliable only under certain conditions, testimony should be limited to those conditions. If research shows that a method performs poorly, courts should exclude it or allow only very modest claims. Experts should speak plainly: what they examined, what similarities and differences they found, what studies support their inference, and what uncertainty remains.

The National Commission on Forensic Science has an opportunity to push the field toward evidence, transparency and restraint. The aim should not be to rescue every traditional technique or to discredit every examiner. It should be to build a system in which forensic conclusions are as strong—and only as strong—as the science behind them.

Forensic science will not become dependable until judges become less credulous. The courtroom has been the place where many forensic techniques acquired legitimacy, even when the laboratory had not yet earned it. That is backwards.

Under Daubert in federal court and similar standards elsewhere, judges are supposed to ask whether expert testimony rests on reliable methods: Has it been tested? Has it been peer reviewed? Are there standards? What is the known or potential error rate? Yet in criminal cases, judges have often admitted familiar forensic disciplines because earlier courts admitted them. Fingerprints, handwriting, firearms comparison and other pattern evidence became routine before being subjected to the kind of empirical scrutiny that would be expected in civil litigation over pharmaceuticals or engineering.

The result is a double standard. Corporate defendants often succeed in excluding speculative expert testimony. Criminal defendants, whose liberty or lives are at stake, face experts who speak in the language of certainty about methods with thin validation. That imbalance should trouble conservatives and liberals alike. The rule of law requires that expert evidence be more than tradition.

The Durst handwriting issue is a useful test. A judge should not simply ask whether handwriting experts have testified for a century. The judge should ask what the examiner proposes to say. If the testimony is that certain features are similar and that the document cannot be excluded as Durst’s writing, that may be admissible if the method is explained and cross-examination is allowed. If the testimony is that the examiner can identify the writer to a practical certainty, the court should require scientific proof that such certainty is justified.

Judges should also police the language of expertise. Words like “match,” “consistent with,” and “identification” can mislead jurors unless carefully defined. Experts should be barred from implying a statistical confidence they cannot calculate. Written jury instructions should explain that some forensic comparisons are subjective and that disagreement among qualified examiners may reflect real limitations in the discipline, not merely hired-gun behavior.

The National Commission can recommend better procedures, but commissions do not decide admissibility. Judges do. If judges demand validation, laboratories and professional organizations will have an incentive to produce it. If judges continue to admit weak claims, reform will remain optional.

Forensic science has helped solve serious crimes and exonerate innocent people. The question is not whether science belongs in court. It does. The question is whether courts will insist on science rather than accept its costume. The gatekeeper must start keeping the gate.

"To make forensic science more dependable, the legal system must move away from deferring to practitioner experience and instead require that expert testimony meet rigorous standards of empirical validation and scientific scrutiny."

The Robert Durst case is being treated as a referendum on handwriting analysis, but it should really prompt a broader question: why do American courts admit expert testimony that would never survive scrutiny in any other empirical field?

In medicine, a new diagnostic test is required to demonstrate sensitivity, specificity, and reproducibility before clinicians may rely on it. In engineering, a structural calculation must be backed by published methods and validated assumptions. In epidemiology, a causal claim must clear journal review and replication. In forensic science, by contrast, an examiner may walk into court and declare that two signatures were written by the same person, basing the conclusion on training, experience, and a methodology that has often never been subjected to controlled testing at all.

The mechanism that allows this is Federal Rule of Evidence 702 and the Daubert framework, which empower judges to act as gatekeepers screening out unreliable expert testimony. In theory, Daubert demands consideration of testability, error rates, peer review, and general acceptance. In practice, judges are not scientists, are reluctant to disturb long-standing forensic disciplines, and frequently default to admitting evidence on the basis of "general acceptance" within a small community of practitioners who all trained one another. The result is a closed loop: the discipline is reliable because its practitioners say it is, and the practitioners say it is because the discipline has long been admitted in court.

Breaking this loop requires changes in three places. First, judicial education. Judges should receive serious training in evaluating scientific claims, perhaps through a dedicated science advisory office attached to the federal courts. Second, an external validation requirement: forensic disciplines seeking admissibility should have to point to independent, published studies of their accuracy, not just to internal proficiency tests run by friendly labs. Third, a willingness by appellate courts to actually reverse trial judges who admit shaky evidence. Right now, abuse-of-discretion review essentially insulates admission decisions from meaningful scrutiny.

Durst may be convicted or acquitted. The handwriting on those envelopes may indeed be his — common sense suggests as much, given the misspelling — but conviction should rest on evidence whose reliability has been demonstrated, not asserted. Forensic science will become more dependable when courts stop accepting "trust the examiner" as a substitute for "show your work." Until then, every conviction built on weakly validated techniques is a conviction the legal system may someday have to revisit, as the FBI is now doing with hundreds of hair-comparison cases. That is not a sustainable model of justice.

The weakness in forensic evidence is not always the science itself, but the gatekeepers who let it in. For more than two decades, the Supreme Court’s Daubert ruling has instructed federal judges to serve as active screeners, evaluating the methods behind expert testimony. Yet judges repeatedly admit techniques like handwriting analysis—the very evidence front and center in the Durst trial—without demanding the rigorous validation the law theoretically requires. Handwriting comparison often comes into court as a “specialized knowledge” rather than scientific knowledge, a loophole that softens the judicial scrutiny. Many document examiners cannot answer these questions with specifics drawn from methodologically sound research. Yet judges accept generalized statements about training and experience and allow the testimony.

The fix is twofold. First, judicial education must improve. The National Judicial College and the Federal Judicial Center offer some programs on forensic evidence, but attendance is voluntary and sporadic. Every judge who presides over criminal cases should receive mandatory, regularly updated training on the scientific method and the specific limitations of each forensic discipline. Second, trial courts should be required to appoint a neutral expert in forensic science when a party challenges the admissibility of a technique like handwriting analysis. A court-appointed expert can walk the judge through the empirical literature without the filter of advocacy, making gatekeeping less of a rubber stamp.

Once admitted, testimony must be bounded. Even if an examiner may testify to similarities, the witness should be barred from using absolute language like “match” or “identification.” Instead, judges should insist on plain-English descriptions of the findings' limits and require the jury to be instructed that handwriting analysis is not infallible. That modest procedural reform would not impair the prosecution’s ability to present relevant evidence, but it would blunt the misleading aura of certainty that so often overwhelms the truth-seeking process.
