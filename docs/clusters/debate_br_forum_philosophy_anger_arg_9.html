"While anger has valid moral reasons that do not simply vanish, the rule of law and public institutions are necessary to prevent these reasons from devolving into endless, destructive private grudges and revenge."

Agnes Callard’s essay makes most sense if we read it as a challenge to private morality. When one person wrongs another, the victim’s anger naturally reaches backward and outward: backward because the wrong cannot be undone, outward because the wrongdoer must somehow be made to answer. Callard argues that the backward reach becomes the grudge and the outward reach becomes revenge. Her conclusion is that anger’s moral function cannot be separated from its moral danger.

This is persuasive as a description of anger before law. But the most important human response to the problem she identifies is neither Stoic detachment nor sentimentalist purification. It is legal and political institution-building. The rule of law exists precisely because anger has reasons that do not know how to stop.

A legal order begins by taking the victim seriously but not literally. It acknowledges that the victim has been wronged, that the past matters, and that the offender must answer. But it refuses to let the victim’s anger set the measure of response. This refusal is not a denial that anger has reasons. It is a recognition that anger’s reasons are partial, personal, and potentially endless. Law does not abolish vengeance; historically, it often grows out of efforts to limit vengeance. It says: not never, but no more than this; not anyone, but this person; not whenever, but after proof; not by any means, but by specified means; not forever, but for a term.

That is why Callard’s Argument for Grudges is both true and politically incomplete. Yes, if someone assaulted me ten years ago, it remains true today that he assaulted me. In that sense I may still have a reason to be angry. But public justice must distinguish between the persistence of a reason and the authority of that reason to govern action. Many reasons persist without retaining practical priority. I may always have reason to mourn a loss, but not always reason to cancel every obligation in order to mourn. I may always have reason to distrust someone who lied, but not always reason to oppose every good thing that person later does. The law’s job is to prevent one permanent reason from swallowing the whole practical world.

Statutes of limitation, rules of evidence, proportional sentencing, parole, bankruptcy discharge, and doctrines of finality all embody this thought. They are morally imperfect. Sometimes they protect the powerful, silence victims, and produce grotesque injustice. But their underlying problem is real: if every wrong remains permanently actionable in the same way, social life becomes impossible. The past must be remembered, but it cannot be allowed to exercise unlimited jurisdiction.

This point applies even more strongly to revenge. Callard describes revenge as making the wrongdoer live by the principle he used against the victim. But public punishment, at its best, rejects that symmetry. The murderer is not murdered by the victim’s family in the street. The thief is not robbed. The rapist is not raped. The fraudster is not defrauded. Instead, the community imposes a response that aspires to be proportionate, impersonal, rule-bound, and publicly justified. Of course punishment still hurts. A prison cell is not a seminar in moral accountability. But the difference between retaliatory imitation and legal punishment is morally significant.

It is fashionable, especially after Foucault, to regard this distinction with suspicion. Callard herself invokes Foucault’s claim that modern punishment turns from the body to the soul, from spectacle to discipline. There is truth in that suspicion. Prisons can be cruel, arbitrary, racialized, criminogenic, and hidden from public conscience. The fact that punishment is legal does not make it just. But Foucaultian unmasking can become too easy. To say that prison is coercive is not yet to say that it is merely revenge. To say that rights are suspended is not yet to say that punishment and crime are the same. A lawful deprivation of liberty after due process may be unjust in many cases, but it is not identical to kidnapping.

The distinction matters because without it we lose the vocabulary needed to reform institutions. If all punishment is revenge, then the difference between a lynching and a trial becomes administrative rather than moral. But that difference was paid for in blood. The history of criminal procedure—rights to counsel, confrontation, appeal, impartial juries, bans on cruel punishment—can be understood as a long, incomplete effort to rescue accountability from rage. It has failed often. It has failed especially for Black Americans, immigrants, the poor, and other vulnerable groups. Yet the remedy for those failures is not to declare purification impossible. It is to demand that institutions better perform the limiting function they claim.

Callard might say that these institutions merely conceal the same logic. The state takes over vengeance from the victim, cools it, bureaucratizes it, and calls it justice. There is something to this. Anyone who has watched public appetite for harsh sentencing, sex offender registries, or humiliating perp walks knows that the punitive state can become a machine for socially approved anger. But institutions can also discipline anger by requiring reasons that anger itself would not supply: evidence, consistency, proportionality, attention to mitigating circumstances, concern for the offender’s future, and awareness of social causes.

Take mitigation. Anger sees the wrong and the wrongdoer. Law asks about intent, capacity, coercion, age, mental illness, provocation, necessity. These questions do not deny the victim’s injury. They widen the frame. Anger’s moral vision is often telescopic: it sees the offense in sharp focus and blurs everything else. Law, at its best, forces refocusing. It asks not only “What did he do to you?” but “What exactly happened, what did he know, what could he have done, what response is fair, what response will protect the community, and what response will not degrade us?”

In April 2020, as governments impose emergency restrictions in response to COVID-19, we can see the importance and fragility of this institutional discipline. Fear and anger produce demands: punish hoarders, arrest violators, shame the reckless, close borders, blame officials, blame foreigners, blame neighbors. Some enforcement is necessary. But emergencies are precisely when anger’s partial reasons become dangerous. We need rules, transparency, sunset clauses, oversight, and proportionality not because people have no reason to be angry or afraid, but because they do.

Callard’s essay is strongest when it exposes the inadequacy of purely therapeutic responses to injustice. “Let it go” is often an immoral instruction. Some wrongs should be carried, recorded, and answered. But law teaches that answering is not the same as avenging. Indeed, the victim’s legitimate grievance may require removing the power of response from the victim. That removal is painful. It can feel like dispossession: first I was harmed, and now the state takes over my injury. Legal systems should acknowledge that pain and give victims voice. But voice is not veto, and anger is not sentence.

The same is true in politics. Groups that have suffered historic injustice have reasons to remember and to demand repair. Those reasons do not expire simply because the beneficiaries of injustice prefer a clean slate. But democratic politics must convert grievance into claims capable of public justification: voting rights, equal protection, reparations, institutional reform, labor protections, anti-discrimination law. The conversion is always imperfect. It cools something that may deserve to burn. Yet without conversion, politics becomes feud.

Callard concludes that “we can’t be good in a bad world.” I would put it differently: we cannot be privately innocent in a bad world, which is why we need public forms. Institutions do not make us pure. Courts, legislatures, schools, unions, churches, and public health agencies are human and compromised. But they allow us to distribute moral tasks that individuals cannot safely bear alone. A victim should not have to preserve the memory of wrong through an eternal grudge. A community should remember through records, rituals, compensation, and law. A victim should not have to teach the wrongdoer through revenge. A community should hold accountable through fair procedure.

The danger today is twofold. On one side are those who use civility, closure, and forgiveness to protect wrongdoers from consequences. On the other are those who treat anger’s intensity as self-validating and institutions as mere obstacles to justice. Callard’s essay is a useful warning to both. To the first, it says: anger has reasons, and they do not vanish when you become uncomfortable. To the second, it says: anger’s reasons may be endless and bloody.

The rule of law is not the fantasy that anger can be purified. It is the practice of admitting that anger is too morally serious to be left in charge of itself.
