"New York City should establish an independent inspector general for the NYPD, modeled after Los Angeles’s system but tailored with broader analytical capacity and stronger protections for independence."

New York need not reinvent the wheel when it comes to police oversight. The experience of Los Angeles, however imperfect, offers a concrete reference point that should inform the current debate. Since 2001, the Los Angeles Police Department has operated with an inspector general inside the Police Commission, an office created in the aftermath of the Rampart corruption scandal and reinforced by a federal consent decree. That IG has auditing authority over departmental policies, the ability to review use-of-force investigations, and a mandate to report publicly on compliance with the consent decree’s terms. It is not a paragon of unchecked independence—the Police Commission appoints the IG, and the mayor appoints the commissioners—but it has added a layer of scrutiny that did not exist before and has contributed to meaningful, if uneven, improvement in the LAPD’s disciplinary systems and training curricula.

Applying this model to New York requires tailoring, not transplanting. The LAPD IG’s office is relatively small and its focus was initially dictated by a specific court-ordered reform agenda. The NYPD, three times the size of the LAPD and with a far more complex intelligence apparatus, would need an IG with broader analytical capacity and a charter that is not solely wedded to a consent decree—because no such decree yet exists, although the Floyd litigation may produce one. That charter could be defined by the City Council to focus the IG’s work on policy audits of stop-and-frisk, surveillance operations, and other programs that carry high risks of disparate racial or religious impact. The key is to avoid creating a redundant complaint‑intake sink; the IG must be forward-looking, not mired in retrying individual stops.

Critics worry that an IG would become a political cheerleader or a bureaucratic obstructionist. The LA experience shows those risks are real but manageable. The IG’s independence depends on a secure budget line and a fixed term not tied to the mayor’s election cycle. Moreover, an IG that publishes data‑driven reports—comparing, say, stop outcomes across precincts and adjusting for local crime rates—can gain a reputation for non‑partisan rigor that insulates it from the charge of being an anti‑police shop. The LAPD IG has survived multiple police chiefs and mayors because its findings, while sometimes uncomfortable, are grounded in facts, not editorials.

For New York, the lesson is not that an IG is a magic wand—Los Angeles still struggles with racial disparities in stops and use of force—but that a properly chartered oversight office can prevent the most egregious systemic breakdowns and provide the public with a credible, institutional voice inside the closed world of police policymaking. The mayoral candidates who talk about an inspector general should spend less time sloganizing and more time visiting the West Coast to study what has worked, what has failed, and how to build a New York IG that is at least one notch more independent than the LA original. Anything less risks giving the city another toothless commission, and nothing would be worse than an oversight body that breeds only cynicism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Los Angeles experience demonstrates that an inspector general can provide a necessary layer of scrutiny that improves police disciplinary systems and training curricula.
  2. 02A New York inspector general should focus on forward-looking policy audits of high-risk programs like stop-and-frisk and surveillance rather than redundant individual complaint processing.
  3. 03To ensure non-partisan rigor and independence, the office requires a secure budget, fixed terms for leadership, and a mandate for data-driven public reporting.
  4. 04While not a total solution for police misconduct, a properly chartered oversight office prevents systemic breakdowns by providing a credible institutional voice within police policymaking.
