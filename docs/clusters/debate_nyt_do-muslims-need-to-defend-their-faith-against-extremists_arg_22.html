"Muslims should defend their faith against extremism by reclaiming and restoring the intellectual depth, traditional scholarship, and ethical disciplines of classical Islam."

The cure for the Islamic State is not a thinner Islam. It is a better-rooted one.

A striking feature of many modern extremists is their impatience with inherited religious authority. They are not humble students of law, ethics and spirituality. They are young men with slogans, translated quotations, battlefield videos and contempt for centuries of scholarship. They treat the Qur’an as a weapons cache and Islamic history as a costume drama. In that sense, ISIS is not traditional Islam revived; it is modern ideological rage dressed in antique clothing.

Muslims should defend their faith by restoring the disciplines that extremists despise: learning, restraint, reverence, disagreement and character. Classical Islamic jurisprudence did not make every angry man a judge, soldier and executioner. It developed rules, authorities, limits and procedures. Sufi traditions cultivated humility and purification of the ego — precisely the opposite of the swaggering brutality seen in ISIS propaganda. Sunni and Shiite scholars, despite deep disagreements, long understood that declaring other Muslims unbelievers is a grave and dangerous act. ISIS turns takfir into a hobby.

This does not mean the past was perfect or that every medieval ruling should be imported into the twenty-first century. But a civilization that produced al-Ghazali, Ibn Rushd, Rumi, Maimonides writing in Arabic, Ottoman plural arrangements, Indian Muslim poetry and West African scholarship cannot be reduced to masked men in pickup trucks. The extremists win when both they and their anti-Muslim critics agree that violence is the most “serious” form of Islam.

The response should therefore be educational. Mosques need qualified teachers, not merely charismatic speakers. Young Muslims should learn Arabic, law, history and ethics from people formed by tradition, not YouTube algorithms. Muslim institutions should be independent enough to criticize tyrants and extremists alike. Sermons should teach adab — moral comportment — as much as identity politics.

Western audiences should also understand that demanding a “reformation” as shorthand for secularization may miss the point. The Muslim teenager tempted by ISIS does not need to be told that religion is backward. He needs to encounter religious authority deeper, wiser and more demanding than the recruiter’s fantasy.

Muslims should defend Islam, yes — by making it harder for ignoramuses with guns to impersonate scholars.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern extremism is characterized by a rejection of inherited religious authority and centuries of scholarship in favor of ideological rage.
  2. 02Classical Islamic traditions, such as jurisprudence and Sufism, provide necessary frameworks for restraint, humility, and legal procedure that extremists lack.
  3. 03Reducing Islam to violence validates the extremists' own narrative and ignores the rich, pluralistic history of Islamic civilization.
  4. 04The solution to radicalization is an educational approach that prioritizes qualified teachers and traditional learning over charismatic speakers and online algorithms.
  5. 05Countering extremism requires offering young Muslims a religious authority that is more intellectually demanding and spiritually grounded than the simplistic fantasies of recruiters.
