"Contrary to the skepticism expressed in the lead essay, a substantial increase in targeted and accountable foreign aid is essential to solidify Africa’s fragile economic and social gains."

Edward Miguel’s lead essay rightly challenges both the naïve boosterism of some aid enthusiasts and the glib pessimism of aid skeptics, but his own proposal for “rapid conflict prevention support” and his overall stance on foreign assistance deserve a sharper pushback. He understates the transformative potential of well-designed aid, particularly in health and education, and he underestimates the degree to which aid can — and already has — served as a catalyst for the very institutional strengthening that Africa needs. The question is not whether aid has worked everywhere or at all times, but how to make it work better. On that score, recent successes suggest that a substantial increase in targeted, accountable aid is not only desirable but essential to solidify Africa’s fragile gains.

Miguel’s reading of the macro data is too sweeping. He points out that aid fell sharply after the Cold War with no apparent effect on African growth rates, inferring that aid is not a decisive factor. But this natural experiment is far muddier than he acknowledges. The Cold War’s end unleashed a wave of political reforms and economic stabilization programs, plus the onset of democratization, all of which might have offset whatever negative growth effect a sudden aid reduction would have caused. More fundamentally, much Cold War–era aid was strategic, not developmental, flowing to corrupt regimes and feeding elite consumption rather than public goods. Its withdrawal may have been roughly growth-neutral because it was never doing much good in the first place. The interesting period is the one since the late 1990s, when aid has been increasingly channelled through more transparent mechanisms and better-aligned with recipient-country priorities. On that score, evidence is mounting that recent aid has had measurable impacts on health, school enrollment, and agricultural productivity — exactly the human-capital foundations on which long-run growth depends.

Take the health sector. Miguel himself mentions the international campaign to fund anti-retroviral drugs, but he underplays its significance. By 2008, major initiatives such as PEPFAR and the Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria have placed millions of Africans on life-saving treatment and drastically reduced malaria deaths through the distribution of insecticide-treated bed nets. These are not just humanitarian achievements; they are economic investments. When parents live to raise their children and farmers survive to plant the next season, entire communities benefit. The spillover effects on labor supply, school attendance, and social stability are profound but appear only slowly in GDP statistics. To dismiss such aid because it does not immediately lift national income is to miss the forest for the trees.

The same holds for education. The reintroduction of school fees in the 1980s and 1990s depressed enrollment exactly when the HIV pandemic was decimating the teaching profession. Since 2000, large aid flows, combined with domestic political will, have supported the abolition of primary school fees in countries such as Uganda, Kenya, and Malawi. Enrollments have surged. Yes, quality remains a major challenge — overcrowded classrooms and underpaid teachers are real. But the first step is getting children into school; quality interventions can follow. Aid, carefully targeted, has been a crucial enabler of that first step.

Miguel’s own creative idea of rapid conflict prevention support (RCPS) is, tellingly, a form of aid. He envisions it as insurance, not as traditional infrastructure funding. But why stop there? The same logic that makes weather-indexed transfers useful for preventing conflict could be extended to other shocks — health emergencies, terms-of-trade collapses. And the institutional machinery required to deliver such insurance transparently and at scale does not magically exist; it must be built, often with external assistance. A more robust aid architecture that puts cash directly into the hands of the vulnerable, conditions it on results, and strengthens domestic accountability could be the very thing that turns fragile states into stable polities.

The lesson is not that aid is a silver bullet, but that the binary debate Miguel describes — Sachs versus Easterly — is sterile. Both sides cherry-pick evidence. The real question is what kind of aid, under what conditions, produces sustainable public goods. And here, the last decade offers reasons for qualified hope. If the U.S. and its partners expand proven health and education programs, and if they couple that with the sort of risk insurance Miguel advocates, Africa’s turn may become more than a passing moment. Aid, properly conceived, is not charity; it is global public finance for the world’s poorest. And right now, Africa needs more of it, not less.
