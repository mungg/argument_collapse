"While the lead essay correctly critiques the 'natural' as a tool of oppression, feminists must shift focus toward the political economy of reproductive assistance to ensure it is governed by public rights rather than corporate interests."

The lead essay is right to distrust “nature.” Appeals to nature have justified too much suffering to deserve feminist deference. They have been used to deny pain relief in childbirth, to shame women who cannot or do not breastfeed, to condemn queer families, to naturalize women’s unpaid labor, and to cast motherhood as destiny rather than work. Yet I want to linger over a danger that the essay identifies but does not fully confront: if all reproduction is assisted, and if assistance is increasingly supplied by corporations, clinics, insurers, data platforms, and employers, then the most urgent feminist question may not be whether reproduction is natural. It may be who owns the assistance.

Consider egg freezing as an employee benefit. The story of S complicates the easy critique. It matters that LGBT employees pushed for benefits that did not define infertility through heterosexual intercourse. It matters that some women who freeze eggs are not careerist caricatures but people facing breakups, illness, uncertainty, or the brute mismatch between reproductive aging and the timing of partnership. To mock them is lazy and sexist. Still, the existence of real benefits for real people does not make the corporate form innocent.

When a technology enters the workplace as a “perk,” it carries the workplace’s values with it. Silicon Valley did not become famous for respecting human limits. It became famous for free dinners that keep employees at the office, laundry services that reduce errands, wellness programs that individualize stress, and a culture in which devotion to work masquerades as self-actualization. In that setting, egg freezing is not simply a gift of time. It is also a management tool for reorganizing time around the needs of firms.

A generous reproductive politics would ask why a thirty-four-year-old employee needs to purchase a biological option on motherhood while her employer offers haircuts and bike repair but not, in many cases, robust childcare, predictable hours, or a culture in which parents are not penalized. The problem is not that women freeze eggs. The problem is that the workplace increasingly offers women technologies for adapting their bodies to corporate schedules rather than reorganizing work around human dependency.

This is the old problem of liberal feminism in new biomedical form. Women are told they can enter male-designed institutions if they manage the inconveniences of embodiment privately. Delay pregnancy. Pump milk in a closet. Answer email in labor. Return before the baby sleeps through the night. Hire another woman, usually poorer and often a woman of color or immigrant, to perform the care work that professional success renders impossible. The promise is equality, but the model of equality is still the worker unencumbered by caregiving.

The lead essay’s phrase “all reproduction is assisted” can illuminate this structure, but it can also blur distinctions we need to preserve. Assistance from a midwife is not the same as assistance from an employer seeking retention in a competitive labor market. Assistance from paid leave is not the same as assistance from a fertility clinic selling expensive hope with uneven success rates. Assistance from a grandmother is not the same as assistance from a contractual surrogacy arrangement brokered across lines of class and nationality. If everything is assistance, we must become more precise, not less, about the political economy of assistance.

Reproductive technologies are not developed in a vacuum. They emerge from markets, professional incentives, patent regimes, liability concerns, and cultural fantasies. IVF clinics advertise success rates in ways that can be difficult for patients to interpret. Egg freezing is often marketed as empowerment despite limited guarantees, especially for women who freeze later in their thirties. Genetic screening can be offered as reassurance while expanding expectations that responsible parents should optimize embryos. Apps that track fertility transform intimate bodily rhythms into data. Employers and insurers decide which interventions count as medically necessary. Pharmaceutical companies profit from hormonal protocols. None of this means the technologies are bad. It means they are embedded in institutions that feminists should not romanticize.

The essay rightly rejects criticism that turns from capitalism to women’s choices. But we need criticism that can do both things at once: defend women from moralizing while scrutinizing the systems that profit from their constrained choices. A woman can be fully justified in freezing her eggs and still be participating in a labor regime that should be changed. A queer couple can be right to use IVF and still be subject to a medical system that extracts high fees because law and insurance lag behind family diversity. A single woman can rationally pursue genetic screening after miscarriage and still be drawn into a culture that treats uncertainty as personal failure.

What would a less corporate reproductive strategy look like? First, benefits should be portable and public. No one’s ability to preserve fertility, treat infertility, take parental leave, or secure legal parentage should depend on working for the right company. Employer-based benefits always produce insiders and outsiders. They also allow companies to brand themselves as feminist without surrendering the deeper prerogatives that make caregiving difficult.

Second, paid leave and childcare should be treated as reproductive technologies. This may sound odd, but it follows directly from the lead essay’s best insight. If reproduction is assisted by social arrangements, then a public childcare center assists reproduction as surely as a cryotank does. A living wage assists reproduction. Housing assistance assists reproduction. Clean water assists reproduction. Protection from domestic violence assists reproduction. A schedule that allows parents to attend a pediatric appointment without losing pay assists reproduction. The hierarchy that treats high-tech medicine as innovation and social support as expense is itself ideological.

Third, we need informed consent that includes social information, not only medical risk. Patients considering egg freezing should know the physical burdens, likely success rates by age, storage costs, and future costs of thawing, fertilization, and transfer. They should also know that the technology cannot substitute for workplace rights, partner equality, or social support. IVF patients should receive counseling that neither pushes them toward endless cycles nor treats stopping as failure. Queer and trans patients should not have to educate their doctors, and clinics should not be allowed to operate as though every patient is a married heterosexual woman with a husband in the waiting room.

Fourth, reproductive justice must include the workers who make assisted reproduction possible. Nurses, embryologists, lab technicians, egg donors, surrogates, cleaners, receptionists, and care workers all belong in the story. The lead essay centers patients, understandably. But a politics of assistance must ask whose labor is hidden behind the miracle. This is especially important when reproduction crosses class and national boundaries. Wealthy intended parents may experience surrogacy as liberation while gestational carriers experience it as one of few available ways to support their own families. Again, the point is not to condemn everyone involved. It is to refuse sentimental stories in which technology dissolves inequality.

Finally, feminism should resist the conversion of every structural problem into an individualized reproductive project. The question “Why not just have the baby?” is cruel when directed at S, but it also reveals a social failure. Why is timing so unforgiving? Why is partnership so determinative? Why does motherhood still threaten professional standing? Why is paid leave so scarce? Why do women bear the psychological burden of planning around institutions that refuse to plan around them?

The lead essay wants the political to catch up with the technological. I agree, but I would put it more sharply: the political must discipline the technological. Without public power, reproductive technology will expand in the image of the market—unequal, individualized, profitable, and wrapped in the language of choice. With public power, it could become part of a broader reproductive commons: health care, leave, childcare, legal recognition, bodily autonomy, and safe communities.

Nature is not the refuge. But neither is the marketplace. Feminism needs better reproductive strategies, and those strategies must begin by taking assistance away from the benevolence of bosses and placing it within the realm of rights.
