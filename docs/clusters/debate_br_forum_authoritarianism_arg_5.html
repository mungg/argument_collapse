Sub-arguments
  1. 01The term 'moderate' lacks a stable or clear definition, making it an elusive and impractical guide for political strategy.
  2. 02National mandates for moderation are unenforceable because no central authority can control the diverse actions of local candidates, activists, or voters.
  3. 03Voters experience candidates as individuals with personal stories rather than as bundles of policy positions, meaning the same platform can be received differently depending on the messenger.
  4. 04Relentless poll-testing and ideological calibration undermine the authenticity that voters crave and that is necessary to capture public attention.
  5. 05Recruiting and sustaining candidates is difficult when they are pressured to run on uninspiring, 'middle-of-the-road' platforms rather than their authentic convictions.
  6. 06Voters are generally not ideologically consistent and prioritize finding trustworthy leaders who can solve their problems over finding candidates who fit a specific point on the left-right spectrum.
Diversified MiniMax

"The failure of the Harris campaign demonstrates that Democrats must prioritize authentic candidate narratives and genuine records of commitment over calculated ideological moderation or poll-tested presentation."

Bonica and Grumbach make a compelling case that the 2024 election demonstrated the failure of the moderation strategy. I want to examine what this failure tells us about the specific failure mode of the Harris campaign.

Harris did not just moderate on policy. She moderated on presentation. She presented herself as a reasonable, responsible candidate who would not make waves. This presentation failed not because voters prefer extremism but because voters do not trust candidates they perceive as calculating.

The authors correctly identify that authenticity matters. Authenticity is not about policy positions; it is about presentation and track record. A candidate who has consistently fought for something—anything—will be more credible than a candidate who has triangulated to every passing wind.

Harris's problem was not that she was too liberal or too moderate. It was that she was associated with an administration that voters blamed for their economic difficulties, and she did not clearly differentiate herself from that administration. She was not authentically anything except the presumably next in line.

The lesson for future candidates is not about ideology but about narrative. Candidates need stories that explain who they are and why they are running. These stories cannot be poll-tested into authenticity. They can only come from genuine commitment to something.

The authors' recommendations point in this direction. Anti-corruption requires a candidate who has fought corruption. Anti-establishment requires a candidate who has challenged the establishment. Turnout requires a candidate who inspires enthusiasm.

All of these requirements point to the same underlying need: candidates who stand for something. The debate between moderate and progressive is a distraction from this more fundamental requirement.

Democrats should focus less on ideology and more on authenticity. This means recruiting candidates with genuine records of fighting for something, not just candidates who can win focus groups.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Harris’s failure was rooted in a presentation of 'reasonableness' that voters perceived as calculating rather than authentic.
  2. 02Authenticity is derived from a candidate's consistent track record and personal story rather than their specific placement on a left-right policy spectrum.
  3. 03Harris was unable to establish a distinct identity because she failed to differentiate herself from an unpopular administration, appearing only as a 'next in line' politician.
  4. 04The recommendations of the lead essay—such as anti-corruption and anti-establishment platforms—can only succeed if championed by candidates with the genuine credibility to lead those specific fights.
  5. 05The traditional debate between 'moderate' and 'progressive' ideologies is a distraction from the more fundamental electoral requirement of inspiring voter trust through conviction.
