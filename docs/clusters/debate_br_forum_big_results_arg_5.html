"The behavioral economics approach to development, while well-intentioned, mistakenly prioritizes small-scale psychological nudges over the large-scale structural and institutional changes that historically drive societal transformation."

It is striking that Glennerster and Kremer’s vision for improving lives in the developing world relies so heavily on the notion that the poor need nudges, incentives, and cleverly designed defaults. This approach has a distinctly ahistorical flavor. When today’s rich countries confronted illiteracy and epidemic disease in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, they did not turn to behavioral economics. They did not give farmers lentils to immunize their children or place free chlorine dispensers at the village well. Instead, they passed compulsory schooling laws, built massive public school systems, invested in sanitation infrastructure, and eventually created welfare states that provided broad entitlements. The history of development suggests that big structural change, not small psychological tweaks, is what ultimately transforms societies. The essay’s enthusiasm for nudges, however well intentioned, represents a narrowing of ambition that should trouble anyone who cares about global poverty.

The implicit message of the behavioral development agenda is that poor people are poor decision-makers. We are told that they procrastinate, fail to value the future, and are fazed by trivial inconvenience. That may be true in a descriptive sense, but it is also true of affluent populations—and yet we do not organize the entire American health system around patient procrastination. We regulate, mandate, and build. The Progressive Era reformers who fought for mandatory schooling did not wring their hands about whether parents were present-biased; they argued that education was a public good and a right, and they built the legal and fiscal machinery to make it universal. The result was a dramatic rise in enrollment and literacy over several decades, not by nudging one parent at a time but by changing the institutional environment. Why, when addressing the poorest countries today, do we reach for the smallest possible lever?

Part of the answer, I suspect, is a loss of faith in the capacity of states and in the politics of large-scale reform. Proving that chlorinating water at the source reduces diarrhea is easier and more satisfying than wrestling with a corrupt water utility. Running an RCT on conditional cash transfers is more manageable than overhauling a Ministry of Education. The behavioral approach offers a kind of technocratic escapism: we can sidestep messy political battles and still feel we are making a difference. But the danger is that we end up with a archipelago of successful micro-projects while the underlying determinants of poverty—inequality, weak governance, extractive economic structures—remain untouched. The essay’s triumphant “two dollars per diarrhea case averted” could, perversely, relieve pressure on governments to provide the piped water and sanitation that would make home chlorination unnecessary.

Historical analogy also calls into question the generalizability of the behavioral patterns. When industrial jobs became available in nineteenth-century Britain, parents sent their children to work but also increasingly to school once the Factory Acts and economic shifts made educated labor more valuable. They responded not to lentils but to deep changes in the returns to education. The lead essay cites a study from India showing that call-center recruitment improved girls’ nutrition and schooling. That finding actually supports the rational, structural view: when the economy genuinely rewards education, families invest. The small incentives that the essay lionizes are most effective precisely where the returns are not yet apparent or the constraints are purely psychological. But if the structural payoff isn’t there, a free uniform will only buy temporary attendance, not lasting human capital.

Another uncomfortable historical echo is the colonial legacy of trying to change the habits of subject populations through incentives and disincentives. Colonial administrators were forever trying to induce hygiene, settlement, or labor with small rewards and punishments, while studiously avoiding the fundamental issue of colonial exploitation and dispossession. Today’s behavioral interventions, however benevolent, risk repeating that pattern: we focus on the behavioral deficits of the poor while leaving in place international economic arrangements that entrench their poverty. The World Bank and USAID could embrace chlorine dispensers and school uniform subsidies without ever addressing agricultural subsidies, trade barriers, or tax avoidance that bleed developing countries of resources.

To be clear, I am not arguing against giving bed nets away. If it saves lives and costs little, do it. But let’s not fool ourselves that this constitutes a development strategy. The great transformations—in East Asia, in Scandinavia, in Latin America’s more successful countries—were characterized by big pushes: land reform, universal public education, industrial policy, public health infrastructure. Behavioral economics can refine programs at the margins, but it cannot substitute for the political and economic project of building inclusive institutions. By celebrating small results so enthusiastically, Glennerster and Kremer risk making the margins the center of the conversation, and letting the big changes slide further out of view.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical evidence from developed nations shows that universal progress in health and education was achieved through mandates, public infrastructure, and welfare states rather than behavioral tweaks.
  2. 02Focusing on the behavioral deficits of the poor serves as a form of technocratic escapism that allows policymakers to avoid the difficult political work of reforming corrupt or weak state institutions.
  3. 03Micro-interventions risk relieving pressure on governments to provide essential public services, such as piped water and sanitation, by offering cheaper but less permanent substitutes.
  4. 04The behavioral approach ignores the fact that people respond most effectively to deep economic shifts and genuine returns on education rather than small, artificial incentives like lentils or free uniforms.
  5. 05Prioritizing behavioral interventions can distract from addressing global systemic issues such as trade barriers, tax avoidance, and extractive economic structures that entrench poverty.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While randomized controlled trials and behavioral nudges can improve specific program outcomes, they offer a hubristic and incomplete solution to poverty that risks diverting attention from essential structural and systemic reforms."

Glennerster and Kremer’s essay brims with confidence, presenting a cascade of randomized trial results as if they add up to a new, rigorously proven consensus about development. The trouble is, they don’t. For all their intellectual elegance, randomized controlled trials (RCTs) of small behavioral nudges offer a profoundly incomplete picture of poverty. The danger of the essay is not that it is wrong about particular programs—chlorine dispensers may indeed be better than social marketing—but that it encourages a hubristic belief that we can solve deep-seated problems with tweaks to choice architecture, when the real constraints are structural and systemic.

RCTs, for all their virtues in establishing causality, have well-known limitations. They estimate the average effect of a program in a specific time and place, among a population that volunteered or was selected. Extrapolating from a handful of trials in Kenya and India to a global policy prescription is a leap of faith, not a scientific deduction. Angus Deaton has pointed out that RCT results are local in space and time, and that we often lack any theory of why an effect occurred. When a study finds that free uniforms increase attendance, we cannot be sure whether it is because of reduced financial burden, a psychological endowment effect, or something else entirely. Without understanding mechanisms, we risk policy by rote imitation, applying lentils-for-immunization in contexts where it may be ineffective or even counterproductive.

The essay’s behavioral narrative—that the poor are poor decision-makers, prone to procrastination and present bias—is invoked with a sweeping brush. Yet the evidence is compatible with quite different stories. For example, if a mother does not buy chlorine because she has no cash on hand and faces a choice between chlorinating water and buying food, her behavior is not a psychological failure but a rational response to extreme scarcity. The fact that small subsidies boost uptake could just be evidence that even tiny costs are prohibitive when you live on a dollar a day. Yes, the essay nods to cash constraints, but it then emphasizes procrastination and convenience as if they are a universal cognitive flaw. This framing risks blaming the poor for not being savvy consumers, when in truth their decision environment is radically different from that of a Swiss nurse deciding where to place sanitizer.

Another concern is the neglect of general equilibrium effects and supply-side dynamics. An RCT can show that conditional cash transfers boost school enrollment, but it cannot show what happens to labor markets when a generation of newly educated youth enters them, or whether schools become even more overcrowded and learning deteriorates. The lead essay optimistically assumes that if demand is stimulated, the supply will adapt. Yet we know that many of the world’s schools and clinics are dysfunctional—teachers absent, drugs out of stock, workers demoralized. Pumping up demand without fixing the quality of services could lead to disillusionment or even worse outcomes. Indeed, the essay’s own admission that we know little about how abolishing user fees affects health-worker motivation is a crack in the edifice. Those informal fees that “creep back” when official fees are removed are a symptom of deeper institutional rot that a nudge cannot heal.

The iterative process of testing small tweaks may produce a parade of micro-successes while the big challenges go unaddressed. After a decade of nudging, we may have more children in school and more bed nets hanging, but still face stagnant economic transformation, weak governance, and profound inequality. The development mantra “context is everything” is not refuted by a few cross-country similarities in consumer behavior; it is underscored by the fact that the most successful development stories—South Korea, Botswana, Costa Rica—were not built on lentils and chlorine dispensers but on broad-based public investment, industrialization, and institutional reform. Behavioral economics can improve the edges of programs, but it cannot substitute for a political and economic strategy.

None of this means RCTs are useless or that the particular programs described are not good. It does mean that we should resist the seductive idea that we now have a general, scalable, low-cost toolkit for development. Glennerster and Kremer are right that small changes can yield big results, but only in the narrow sense of increasing uptake of specific technologies. The larger result—a healthier, better-educated, more prosperous society—requires changes that are anything but small. We need to think about industrial policy, tax systems, urban planning, and the quality of public institutions. Behavioral insights can be a useful complement, but presenting them as a transformative new wave is misleading and risks diverting energy and funding from more fundamental reforms. Let’s celebrate the chlorine dispenser, but keep our eyes on the prize of comprehensive development.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The results of randomized controlled trials are often local and lack a theoretical understanding of mechanisms, making it scientifically risky to extrapolate them into global policy prescriptions.
  2. 02Framing poverty as a result of psychological failures like procrastination ignores the reality that the behavior of the poor is often a rational response to extreme resource scarcity.
  3. 03Focusing on stimulating demand through nudges neglects critical supply-side issues, such as institutional corruption and the poor quality of public services.
  4. 04Micro-successes in behavioral uptake do not account for general equilibrium effects, such as how labor markets or school quality will react to increased participation.
  5. 05Historical evidence suggests that successful national development depends on broad-based public investment and institutional reform rather than small-scale behavioral tweaks.
