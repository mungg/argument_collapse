"The government should not impose broad restrictions on where civilian drones can fly and film, but should instead rely on updated existing laws to punish specific harmful conduct like harassment and trespass."

The fear of civilian drones is understandable, but the proposed cure — broad government restrictions on where private citizens may fly and film — could be worse than the disease. In the United States, we have generally not treated public visibility as a privacy violation. If you walk down a street, attend a protest, sunbathe on an unfenced beach or leave your curtains open, other people may see you. Cameras did not change that rule. Neither should model aircraft with better cameras.

That does not mean anything goes. A drone operator who trespasses, stalks, harasses, endangers aircraft, peers into bathrooms or bedrooms, or repeatedly hovers outside a home should face legal consequences. Existing doctrines of nuisance, trespass, harassment, voyeurism and reckless endangerment can be updated and enforced. The FAA also has a legitimate role in preventing collisions and keeping drones away from airports or sensitive airspace. Safety regulation is not censorship.

But we should be cautious about inventing a new privacy right that allows people to veto photography from the air. Drones will not be used only by paparazzi and nosy neighbors. They may be used by a local paper documenting pollution, by a homeowner inspecting storm damage, by a civil liberties group monitoring a police raid, or by a farmer checking irrigation. If every image that captures a person outdoors requires consent, then the powerful will have a new tool for hiding from scrutiny.

The history of photography is a history of moral panics. Handheld cameras, telephoto lenses and camera phones all made it easier to record strangers. Each created abuses; each also expanded public knowledge. The right response was not to presume that the outdoors had become private property, but to punish genuinely invasive or harmful conduct.

The harder problem is government surveillance, not civilian hobbyists. Police drones raise Fourth Amendment questions because the state can combine aerial observation with databases, license plate readers and long-term monitoring. That deserves strict rules. But private filming in public should remain presumptively lawful. If you are outside or visible through an open window, you should not assume perfect privacy. You should, however, be protected against harassment, trespass and peeping. That balance is less satisfying than a blanket rule, but it is freer and more workable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public visibility has historically not been treated as a privacy violation, and the introduction of drone technology should not change the principle that being outdoors or visible through a window does not guarantee privacy.
  2. 02Existing legal doctrines regarding nuisance, voyeurism, and reckless endangerment are sufficient to address drone abuses without creating overly restrictive new privacy rights.
  3. 03Broad restrictions on aerial photography would allow powerful individuals to hide from public scrutiny and hinder beneficial uses of drones by journalists, farmers, and civil liberties groups.
  4. 04The history of photography shows that the appropriate response to new recording technologies is to punish invasive behavior rather than presuming that public spaces have become private.
  5. 05The primary focus of drone regulation should be on limiting government surveillance and police power rather than restricting the activities of private citizens and hobbyists.
