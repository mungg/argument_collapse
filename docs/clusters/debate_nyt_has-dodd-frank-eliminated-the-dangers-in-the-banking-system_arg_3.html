"Dodd-Frank has failed to eliminate systemic banking dangers because it relies on regulatory discretion rather than market discipline and fails to end the expectation of government bailouts."

Dodd-Frank has not eliminated dangers in the banking system, but neither would the favored progressive remedy of breaking up the banks. The central problem is that Washington still wants a financial system in which private actors take risks while public officials decide, during emergencies, which losses are too dangerous to allow. As long as markets believe the government will rescue important firms, risk will be mispriced.

Dodd-Frank is an enormous statute with some sensible components, especially higher capital standards and more attention to resolution planning. But it also relies heavily on regulatory discretion. It assumes that the Federal Reserve, the FDIC and other agencies can monitor complex institutions, anticipate risks, design stress tests, supervise living wills and manage failures more wisely than they did before 2008. That may be an improvement over the previous regime, but it is not the same as market discipline.

The living-will rejection illustrates the problem. Six years after passage, regulators are still telling major institutions that their plans for bankruptcy are not credible. If those firms cannot explain how they would fail, then investors and creditors should not be allowed to assume a rescue. Yet the very existence of a special orderly liquidation authority may reinforce the belief that giant firms will be handled politically, not through ordinary bankruptcy. Officials can insist that shareholders and creditors will bear losses, but in a real panic the pressure to prevent contagion will be immense.

Breaking up banks is an appealing expression of frustration, but it is an imprecise tool. The crisis involved mortgage originators, government-sponsored enterprises, investment banks, insurers, ratings agencies, money market funds and global capital flows. Simply making today’s largest banks somewhat smaller would not prevent bad lending, asset bubbles or runs in short-term funding markets. It could also push risky activity into less regulated corners of finance.

A better approach would emphasize much higher, simpler capital requirements and a credible bankruptcy process for financial firms. Equity is not a bureaucratic guess about safety; it is a real buffer against losses. If large banks had to fund themselves with substantially more common equity and long-term debt, they would be less fragile and less able to privatize gains while socializing losses. At the same time, Congress should reform bankruptcy law so that failing financial companies can be restructured quickly without resorting to bailouts or ad hoc rescues.

Regulators should also distinguish between Wall Street giants and community banks. One of Dodd-Frank’s weaknesses is that compliance burdens often fall on smaller institutions that did not cause the crisis and lack armies of lawyers. A safer system should not mean entrenching the biggest firms because only they can afford the regulatory apparatus.

Dodd-Frank made the system more supervised, but not necessarily disciplined enough. The goal should be clear rules, thick capital cushions and a genuine expectation that failure means failure. Without that, danger remains, whether banks are large, small or merely renamed.

"Dodd-Frank has failed to eliminate the dangers in the banking system because it relies on complex regulatory oversight rather than restoring the credible threat of market failure."

Dodd-Frank has not eliminated the dangers in the banking system because it attacked complexity with more complexity. It created a vast supervisory architecture, but it did not restore the one discipline that matters most in capitalism: the believable threat of failure.

The rejected living wills are revealing. Six years after passage, the biggest institutions still cannot convince regulators that they can go through bankruptcy without destabilizing the system. That means creditors, counterparties and managers have every reason to assume that, in a real panic, Washington will improvise again. The statute may say taxpayers will not bear losses. But during a crisis, officials will face the same terrible choice they faced in 2008: risk a cascade or provide support. Markets understand this.

Dodd-Frank also entrenches the idea that some firms are special. The Financial Stability Oversight Council designates systemically important institutions. Regulators subject them to bespoke rules. The Orderly Liquidation Authority gives government officials broad discretion to seize and restructure a failing firm. These mechanisms may be intended to prevent bailouts, but they also signal that large firms are in a category apart from ordinary businesses. Once a company is treated as systemic, it is hard to believe its creditors will ever be treated as ordinary risk-takers.

Meanwhile, the law has imposed heavy compliance costs on smaller banks that did not cause the crisis. Community banks spend more time and money on paperwork, examinations and legal uncertainty. That burden encourages consolidation — exactly the opposite of what critics of too big to fail should want. The largest banks can hire armies of lawyers and compliance officers. A small bank in a rural county cannot.

The alternative is not simply to “break up the banks” by political decree. Size matters, but the deeper problem is leverage and implicit guarantees. A better reform would be simpler and tougher: require much more genuine equity capital; limit reliance on runnable short-term debt; create a specialized bankruptcy process for large financial firms; make clear that shareholders, managers and unsecured creditors will take losses; and remove discretionary rescue powers that invite lobbying and panic-time exceptions.

A banking system can be regulated without being micromanaged. The goal should be rules that are understandable in advance and credible in a crisis. Dodd-Frank’s thousands of pages have produced an elaborate negotiation between giant banks and giant regulators. That may reduce some risks, but it does not end too big to fail. If anything, it makes the public more dependent on regulators’ judgment — the very judgment that failed so badly before.
