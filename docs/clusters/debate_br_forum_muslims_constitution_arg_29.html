"While the lead essay correctly identifies systemic flaws in post-9/11 counterterrorism, its call to dismantle the entire security apparatus is a dangerous overreaction that ignores the necessity of reform and the progress made through incremental policy improvements."

The lead essay in this forum arrives like a cold bucket of water, insisting that we see the Muslim Ban not as an aberration but as the culmination of a fifteen-year, bipartisan project. It is an important argument, and it is correct in its particulars: the surveillance excesses, the overbroad material support prosecutions, the opaque No Fly List, and the aggressive CVE programs all predate Donald Trump and were advanced under both Republican and Democratic administrations. Yet I find myself resisting the essay’s conclusion that the entire pre-Trump settlement is so rotten that it must be uprooted wholesale. That is a dangerous counsel of despair, one that risks discarding the genuine, if imperfect, progress made since 9/11 and leaving us with no coherent framework for protecting Americans—including American Muslims—from actual terrorist violence.

Let us be concrete about what the Obama years actually looked like. Yes, the government continued to surveil, to prosecute, and occasionally to overreach. But it also implemented significant course corrections that the lead essay barely acknowledges. The Department of Justice under Eric Holder and Loretta Lynch issued guidance limiting racial profiling by federal law enforcement. The administration created a process, albeit flawed, for Americans to petition for removal from the No Fly List. It released thousands of documents on the drone program and codified some targeting standards in a presidential policy directive. It ended the use of torture and black sites, and it reduced the Guantánamo population from 242 to 41. These were incremental, hard-fought reforms that required pushing against a recalcitrant Congress and a risk-averse intelligence community. To dismiss the pre-Trump era as merely a kinder gentler version of the same bigotry is to flatten a complex record into a morality play.

The central analytic error of the lead essay is that it collapses three distinct things: the fact of a disproportionate impact on Muslims, the presence of anti-Muslim sentiment in society, and the deliberate character of government policy. The fact that counterterrorism measures inevitably focus on Muslim communities is not, by itself, proof of Islamophobia. It is a reflection of the uncomfortable reality that the most lethal terrorist threats to the United States in the post-9/11 era have indeed arisen from a violent fringe that invokes Islam. This is not to indict the faith or its millions of peaceful adherents; it is to note that any rational allocation of investigative resources will take threat vectors into account. The FBI also investigates white supremacist extremists, but the scale and nature of jihadist networks has required a different set of tools. The question is not whether we should focus resources where the threat is, but whether the tools we use are lawful, proportionate, and subject to robust oversight. On all three counts, I agree with the lead essay that we have fallen short—but the remedy is reform, not abolition.

Consider the material support statute which the authors paint as a device for thought-crime. The Supreme Court upheld it because the government demonstrated that even nonviolent support provided to a designated terrorist organization can legitimately advance its military capabilities. That logic is debatable, but it is not facially unreasonable. The real abuse lies in how the statute has been paired with aggressive entrapment tactics, creating prosecution in cases where no real threat existed. That is a critical problem of investigative practice and prosecutorial discretion, not a problem that requires gutting the underlying law. A better approach would be to impose strict guidelines on undercover operations, require independent review before a sting goes forward, and limit the use of paid informants who target vulnerable individuals. These reforms would protect innocent Muslims—and innocent people of all backgrounds—without leaving a gaping hole in the legal framework against actual financing of terror.

The lead essay’s treatment of the courts also paints with too broad a brush. It is true that judges have been deferential to national security claims, but not monolithically so. In *United States v. Mehanna*, the First Circuit overturned a conviction on certain counts precisely because the government had stretched the material support concept to cover independent advocacy. In *Al-Haramain v. Obama*, a district court found that the government had violated the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act by illegally surveilling an Islamic charity. The judiciary has pushed back when the government overreaches, albeit inconsistently. The very fact that federal courts in Washington State and the Ninth Circuit have blocked Trump’s travel ban—despite the administration’s apocalyptic rhetoric—shows that the rule of law is not a dead letter. To portray the courts as wholly supine erases the very openings that lawyers and advocates have used to protect rights.

Perhaps the most troubling aspect of the lead essay is its treatment of the Countering Violent Extremism program. The authors dismiss it as “marking certain forms of religious practice or critique of U.S. policies as criminally dissident.” There have indeed been troubling instances where CVE pilots veered into profiling, but the core idea—that communities themselves can play a role in preventing radicalization—is not inherently sinister. Many Muslim community organizations have developed impressive resilience programs that support at-risk youth, promote mental health, and teach critical thinking about extremist propaganda. To cede that entire space to the FBI is a mistake. A reformed CVE, designed and led by Muslim communities with full transparency and no law-enforcement intelligence gathering, could be an empowering tool, not a surveillance trap.

I share the lead essay’s moral urgency, but I worry about the political consequences of its maximalism. The American public will not accept a position that treats all counterterrorism efforts as illegitimate. If we demand the repeal of material support laws and the end of surveillance programs without offering a credible alternative for preventing attacks, we cede the argument to the hardliners, who will paint us as naive and out of touch. That dynamic is precisely what Trump exploited. The smart response is not to abandon the center, but to occupy it with a serious, reform-minded agenda that takes security seriously while fiercely defending civil liberties. We need a new national commission, similar to the Church Committee, to conduct a comprehensive review of post-9/11 authorities and recommend a framework that balances security and rights. We need to give the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board real teeth. We need a statutory right of action for citizens and residents to challenge their placement on watchlists in court, with access to the evidence against them, as much as national security permits.

The airport protests were a moving display of decency, and they demonstrated that broad majorities reject Trump’s crude scapegoating. But the durable majority will not march for the abolition of the counterterrorism apparatus. It will march for a system that is tough, smart, and just—one that defeats the terrorists without becoming like them. That is the America we should be fighting for: not a nation that has abandoned the field, but one that has finally learned to fight with clean hands.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The pre-Trump era was characterized by hard-fought, meaningful reforms—such as ending torture and limiting racial profiling—rather than a monolithic system of bigotry.
  2. 02Disproportionate impacts on Muslim communities are a reflection of rational threat-vector analysis rather than inherent Islamophobia, as the most lethal post-9/11 threats have emerged from a violent fringe invoking Islam.
  3. 03The legal issues surrounding material support statutes and surveillance are problems of investigative practice and prosecutorial discretion that should be addressed through strict guidelines rather than total abolition.
  4. 04The judiciary has not been entirely supine, as evidenced by various court rulings that have successfully checked government overreach and protected civil rights.
  5. 05Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs are not inherently sinister and can be transformed into empowering, community-led tools for resilience if decoupled from law enforcement intelligence gathering.
  6. 06Adopting a maximalist, abolitionist stance on counterterrorism risks alienating the public and ceding the political center to hardliners who exploit fears of insecurity.
