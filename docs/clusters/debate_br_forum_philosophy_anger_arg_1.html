"Contrary to the view that anger is inherently corrupting and vengeful, a virtue-ethics perspective suggests that anger can be educated and moderated through practical wisdom to serve as a constructive moral response."

The debate Agnes Callard describes between Stoics and sentimentalists is, in one sense, a debate Aristotle would have found oddly abstract. Should we eradicate anger, or preserve it as moral perception? Is anger essentially vengeful, or can it be purified? Aristotle’s answer would begin elsewhere: the question is not whether to have anger, but whether to be angry at the right things, toward the right people, in the right way, at the right time, for the right length of time, and for the right reasons.

Callard might reply that this is precisely the fantasy she attacks. To speak of right anger seems to imagine a purified passion stripped of grudges and vengeance. But the virtue-ethical position need not deny anger’s dangerous tendencies. It says instead that human passions are educable. They are not raw forces with one inexorable logic. Nor are they detachable instruments of reason. They are part of character, and character can be formed well or badly.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges claims that if the fact that you wronged me gives me reason to be angry, and that fact remains forever, then I have reason to be angry forever. A virtue ethicist should resist the assumption that emotional reasons operate in this timeless way. The virtuous person is not an archivist of every reason that has ever applied. She is someone whose perception is responsive to the salience of reasons in a concrete situation.

A past wrong remains a fact, but its significance changes with apology, restitution, repentance, punishment, time, and the needs of the present. If a friend betrays me and later sincerely repents, I do not pretend the betrayal never happened. But the question “What does this call for now?” has a different answer. Perhaps it calls for caution, a changed friendship, or forgiveness with memory. Perhaps it calls for permanent separation. In some cases it may still call for anger. But it does not automatically call for anger of the same intensity forever.

Callard says amends cannot alter the fact that the wrong occurred. True. But virtue does not require metaphysical erasure. It requires practical wisdom. Practical wisdom asks how the past should be integrated into a life aimed at human flourishing. A person who remains as angry on Thursday as on Tuesday after full repair may be accurately tracking one fact while ignoring others: repentance, vulnerability, the value of peace, the claims of other relationships, the cost of obsession, and the possibility that the wrongdoer is more than the wrong. This is not rational steadfastness. It is defective attention.

The point is not that one should “move on” quickly. Some wrongs are shattering. The demand that victims forgive on schedule is itself a vice, often born of cowardice or convenience. Aristotle’s mean is not moderation in the sense of tepidness. Sometimes the right anger is fierce, public, and enduring. The virtue of mildness does not mean never being angry; it means not being ruled by anger’s excess or deficiency. There is a vicious deficiency of anger: servility, moral numbness, lack of self-respect. There is also vicious excess: irascibility, bitterness, vindictiveness. The mean is not halfway between them; it is what the situation truly deserves.

Callard’s Argument for Revenge also looks different from the standpoint of virtue. She argues that revenge makes the wrongdoer’s principle into a rule: since he made my bad his good, I make his bad mine. But the virtuous person does not let the vicious person legislate the terms of response. If you act from greed, why should greed set the pattern of my action? If you humiliate me, why should humiliation become our shared language? To answer vice by adopting its principle is not accountability. It is moral dependence.

A courageous soldier does not become cowardly in order to teach cowardice a lesson. A truthful person does not answer lies by lying back. A just person does not answer injustice by becoming unjust in turn. This does not mean wrongdoers face no consequences. Justice may require punishment, restitution, exclusion, or public condemnation. But these must be shaped by the virtues of justice, courage, temperance, and practical wisdom—not by the wrongdoer’s maxim reflected back at him.

Callard is right that “teaching someone a lesson” is central to anger. But good teaching is not mimicry. If a student cheats, a teacher may fail him, report him, or require him to redo the work. The teacher does not cheat the student. The lesson is not “I can do to you what you did.” It is “Here is the standard you violated, and here are the consequences that uphold it.” Punishment, when virtuous, is not the victim’s bad becoming good. It is the community’s good being defended through proportionate response.

The difficulty is that human beings often enjoy punishment too much. Callard’s warning lands here. Even when punishment is justified, the punisher may take vicious pleasure in the offender’s pain. A parent disciplining a child, a judge imposing sentence, an activist exposing misconduct, a citizen denouncing corruption—all can slide from justice into cruelty. Virtue ethics does not deny this. It makes the slide central to moral education. The question is not merely “Was the act permitted?” but “What kind of person am I becoming by acting and feeling this way?”

That question is largely absent from Callard’s framework. She speaks of anger as morally corrupting, but the corruption appears almost automatic, as though the victim’s soul must be stained by either rage or acquiescence. Virtue ethics offers a more graduated picture. Character can be wounded, tempted, trained, strengthened, distorted, repaired. Anger may be part of moral growth when it awakens self-respect or solidarity. It may be part of moral decline when it becomes identity. It may be unavoidable at first and yet gradually transformed by habituation, reflection, and community.

Callard’s account of oppressed people is therefore too tragic. Oppression can indeed deform character. It can breed fear, hatred, suspicion, and servility. But it can also call forth courage, patience, loyalty, practical intelligence, and righteous resistance. None of this justifies oppression; the virtues developed under oppression are often costly and should not have been demanded. Still, to say simply that oppressors make the oppressed morally worse misses the way human beings sometimes become morally magnificent under terrible conditions.

Think of people who hid Jews during the Holocaust, or of civil rights workers facing mobs and jail, or of dissidents under authoritarian regimes, or of nurses and doctors now facing COVID-19 with inadequate protection. These people may feel anger. They may also cultivate steadiness, mercy, humor, and resolve. Their moral lives are not reducible to the corrupting logic of anger. Bad worlds make goodness harder; they do not make it impossible.

Callard’s story of her grandmother and the German girl is moving because it shows conflicting virtues and wounds. The grandmother’s anger expressed loyalty to the murdered and vigilance against forgetting. It also risked injustice toward a child. The granddaughter’s openness expressed generosity and innocence, but perhaps also ignorance of history’s weight. A virtue-ethical reading does not force us to say both were corrupt. It asks what practical wisdom would have required in that impossible moment: perhaps protecting the survivor’s memory while refusing inherited hatred; perhaps teaching the child why the game hurt without making the German girl an enemy. Such wisdom is rare, but naming it matters.

One reason it matters is that moral ideals guide formation. If we tell ourselves that anger’s essence is revenge, we may become fatalistic about our vindictiveness. If we tell ourselves that purified anger is easy, we may become hypocrites. Virtue ethics offers a harder middle path: anger is natural, sometimes appropriate, always dangerous, and subject to cultivation. We should neither eradicate it like a disease nor enthrone it as justice’s engine. We should train it.

How is anger trained? By habits of truthful speech rather than fantasy; by friends who can say “you are right to be angry” and also “do not do that”; by rituals of apology and restitution; by laws that limit retaliation; by stories that honor courage more than vengeance; by practices of forgiveness that do not erase accountability; by attention to bodily signs of escalation; by education in history so that memory is not left to private rage alone. Such training is imperfect, but it is not fictional. It is civilization at the scale of character.

Callard has done us a service by showing that anger cannot be made innocent by terminology. But the conclusion should not be that every morally serious response to wrong is a form of corruption. The better conclusion is older: passions become virtuous only when shaped by practical wisdom. We can be good in a bad world, though never easily, never without scars, and never by pretending that anger will educate itself.

"The essay argues that anger is not a morally necessary or rational response to injustice, as there exists a viable alternative of disciplined, non-hostile concern that opposes wrongdoing without seeking the suffering of the wrongdoer."

Agnes Callard presents the anti-anger position as if its serious version requires an almost superhuman detachment: Socrates serenely drinking hemlock, the Extreme Stoic placing no value on children, life, or bodily safety. Most of us, she says, cannot inhabit that position. We are vulnerable to injustice; our blood boils. Therefore we must permit some anger, while recognizing that anger always carries a taste for blood.

This framing gives away too much to anger. It assumes that the only alternatives are ordinary rage, morally compromised but necessary, and an inhuman invulnerability that has transcended attachment altogether. But there is another possibility, familiar in Buddhist, Christian, Stoic, and other moral traditions: deep concern without hatred, fierce opposition without the wish to retaliate, grief and courage without anger’s fantasy that another’s suffering will answer one’s own.

Callard knows that philosophers such as Martha Nussbaum and Owen Flanagan try to identify anger-like states that lack vengeance. She thinks these are fictions produced by renaming. Perhaps some are. But lived moral practice offers examples that are not so easily dismissed. A parent may stop a child from running into traffic with urgency, fear, even sharpness, but without anger. A doctor may fight a disease relentlessly without hating the patient’s body. A civil rights activist may confront a sheriff, accept arrest, and expose brutality without making the sheriff’s pain the goal. These are not cases of indifference. They are disciplined forms of concern.

One might object that injustice differs from danger or disease because it involves agency. A disease does not insult me; a thief does. True. But the fact that wrongdoing involves agency does not require that my response be hostile. It requires that I see the wrongdoer clearly as a person acting badly. Seeing clearly may intensify compassion rather than anger. A person who steals out of greed, fear, vanity, or cruelty is not merely a threat. He is someone whose own moral life is disordered. That disorder may need restraint, confrontation, punishment, or separation. But none of these requires that I adopt his suffering as my good.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges rests on the thought that the original reason for anger never disappears: the wrong occurred, and it ought not to have occurred. But this treats anger as though it were the only faithful apprehension of moral reality. Why not say instead that the permanent truth of wrongdoing gives permanent reason for sorrow? Or vigilance? Or remembrance? Or renewed commitment? The pastness of wrong does not dictate anger in particular. It dictates that the wrong not be denied.

If someone murders my friend, it will forever be true that my friend was murdered. If anger is the only adequate response to that fact, then I have reason to be angry forever. But if love, mourning, reverence, and the pursuit of justice are also adequate responses, then anger loses its monopoly. The question becomes not “Does the fact remain?” but “Which response best honors the fact?” Sometimes anger may be psychologically inevitable. That does not make it morally necessary.

The Buddhist tradition is especially useful here because it begins from the mind’s vulnerability. It does not deny that anger feels like moral vision. It says precisely that this feeling is part of anger’s danger. Anger narrows attention. It simplifies the wrongdoer. It converts pain into accusation and accusation into permission. It often tells the truth about one thing while lying about the whole. The person who has wronged me really did wrong me; anger is often right about that. But anger then says: because he wronged me, his pain matters less. That is where corruption begins.

Callard argues that revenge “teaches a lesson” by making the wrongdoer live under the principle he used against the victim. But this lesson is pedagogically suspect. If I answer cruelty with cruelty, the wrongdoer may learn only that cruelty is indeed how power speaks. If I humiliate the humiliator, he may learn resentment, not humility. If I steal from the thief, I may prove that theft spreads. Revenge often claims to educate, but its curriculum is imitation.

There are better lessons. To require restitution teaches that property and trust matter. To require public acknowledgment teaches that truth matters. To impose lawful restraint teaches that the community will protect the vulnerable. To forgive under the right conditions may teach that the wrongdoer is not identical with his worst act. These lessons may be painful. They may include punishment. But their point is not to make the wrongdoer’s bad my good. Their point is to restore his relation to the good, if possible, and to protect others if not.

Callard might respond that I am smuggling anger back in under other names. But the distinction can be tested from within experience. When I am angry, I rehearse the injury. I imagine confrontations. I want the offender to feel exposed, diminished, afraid, or ashamed. When I am compassionately opposed, my attention is different. I may still act forcefully, but I do not feed on images of the other’s suffering. I am oriented toward stopping harm, telling truth, and securing repair. The difference is not always stable, and self-deception is common. But instability is not fiction. Chastity, courage, patience, and generosity are also unstable; we do not conclude that they do not exist.

The example of Socrates is therefore misleading if it suggests that freedom from anger requires not caring about anything external. Socrates cared intensely about justice, the city, his companions, and the condition of souls. What he denied was that another person’s wrongdoing could make him worse unless he joined it. One need not accept that doctrine in its strongest form to see its power. It draws a line between being harmed and being morally conscripted. Callard thinks such conscription is unavoidable: to be wronged is to be pulled into anger or acquiescence. Socrates represents the refusal of that forced choice.

The refusal is difficult. It may be impossible in full for most of us. But moral ideals often exceed ordinary ability. The fact that perfect courage is rare does not mean cowardice is necessary. The fact that perfect truthfulness is rare does not mean every lie is rational. The fact that angerlessness is hard does not mean anger is morally required.

Callard is right to warn against acquiescence. There is a counterfeit peace that is merely fear, laziness, or self-erasure. Marginalized people have too often been told to abandon anger by those who benefit from their silence. Any anti-anger ethic that counsels passivity in the face of abuse is morally bankrupt. But the great non-anger traditions do not counsel passivity. Śāntideva’s attack on anger is not an invitation to tolerate injustice; it is a discipline for acting without hatred. Martin Luther King Jr., drawing on Christian agape and Gandhian nonviolence, did not ask Black Americans to accept segregation. He asked them to resist it in a way that refused to make the enemy’s degradation the goal. Whether or not one thinks King always succeeded, the aspiration is not imaginary.

Indeed, nonviolent resistance exposes something Callard’s framework obscures: anger and acquiescence are not exhaustive. There is also noncooperation, witness, courage, solidarity, sacrifice, and love. These are not soft emotions. They can be more demanding than anger because they require sustained action without the intoxicating energy of hatred. Anger often burns hot and then seeks discharge. Compassionate resolve can endure.

What about Callard’s claim that oppressed people are made morally worse by oppression? Here the anti-anger position can agree in part. Oppression harms the soul; it can implant fear, hatred, envy, and despair. But it does not follow that anger is the necessary price of resisting those harms. On the contrary, anger may be one of oppression’s victories. The oppressor injures my body, narrows my opportunities, and then, if I allow hatred to rule me, colonizes my mind. To refuse hatred is not to excuse him. It is to deny him that final conquest.

This point must be stated carefully. No outsider has standing to demand serenity from the oppressed. The person who suffers has the first claim on our attention, not our moral correction. Still, if we are asking philosophically what response is best, we should not identify dignity with anger. Dignity may express itself as rage, but also as composure, prayer, organizing, humor, mourning, mutual aid, or disciplined refusal.

Callard’s essay is valuable because it strips away euphemism. Many forms of “righteous anger” are indeed revenge in respectable dress. But she too quickly concludes that the only honest project is to manage anger’s corruption. The more radical project is to train attention so that injustice is seen without hatred. This is not purity in the sense of untouched innocence. It is purification in the older sense: the slow burning away of impulses that bind us to what we oppose.

We cannot undo Monday’s theft. We can remember it, repair it, learn from it, protect against its repetition, and perhaps forgive it. None of that requires pretending that the theft did not matter. It requires believing that the thief’s suffering cannot redeem the theft. If anger tells us otherwise, then anger is not moral clarity. It is one more broken thing in a broken world.

"While anger is a complex and messy emotion, it is not rational to maintain it indefinitely because moral facts and relationships can be transformed through apology, restitution, and reform."

Agnes Callard is right to suspect that much of the philosophical debate about anger has become too tidy. It is implausible that we can simply keep the inspiring, justice-sensitive, protest-bearing part of anger while discarding everything that makes anger frightening. Anyone who has actually been angry knows that the wish to “hold accountable” and the wish to hurt are not separated by a clean glass wall. They bleed into each other. The person who says “I only want him to understand what he did” often also wants him to suffer in the process of understanding it.

But Callard’s central argument depends on a slide. She moves from the claim that the original wrongdoing remains true forever to the claim that the victim therefore has the same reason to be angry forever. That does not follow. The pastness of a wrong is not morally irrelevant, but neither is it the whole moral situation. An apology, restitution, punishment, reform, and changed relationship do not erase the theft on Monday. But they may change what the theft now means, what it calls for, and what emotional response is fitting.

Consider the example of theft. On Tuesday I am angry because you stole from me. On Wednesday you return the object, compensate me, apologize sincerely, and promise never to do it again. Callard says that on Thursday I still have the same reason to be angry: you stole, and you ought not to have stolen. But the reason I had on Tuesday was not merely the eternal proposition “a theft occurred.” It was a richer practical situation: you stood in an uncorrected relation to me as someone who had wronged me; the stolen object was gone; your will toward me was unknown or hostile; the moral meaning of the event was still unsettled. By Thursday, much of that has changed.

This is not because apology is magic. Many apologies are false, manipulative, self-protective, or premature. Restitution can be partial. Trust can remain broken. Some wrongs are so grave that no gesture could restore the relationship. But if we stipulate—as Callard does—that the apology is sincere, restitution complete, compensation adequate, and future conduct reliable, then the object of anger has changed. The old fact remains, but the present relation is different. And emotions are not only about bare facts; they are about facts under descriptions that matter to action.

This is why grief, regret, gratitude, shame, and anger all have temporal structures. If someone once saved my life but has since become cruel to me, I may still have reason for gratitude, but not necessarily the same reason, or the same amount, or the same expression. If I regret a childish betrayal, my regret may persist in one form after I apologize and repair the damage, but it should not remain unchanged if reconciliation occurs. To say that reasons for emotions survive as long as their triggering facts remain true would make emotional life unintelligible. We would be required to feel every fitting emotion forever: gratitude for every kindness, grief for every loss, shame for every mistake, anger for every insult. Since human beings cannot do that, the theory would make ordinary moral maturity look like irrational forgetfulness.

Callard anticipates this by saying that anger is “not a desire to fix something but a way of grasping the fact that it is broken.” Yet even if anger is a way of grasping brokenness, brokenness itself can be transformed. A repaired vase is not an unbroken vase, but it is not simply a broken vase either. A reconciled friendship is not an untouched friendship, but neither is it identical to a friendship still ruptured. If I continue to treat the repaired relation as though no repair has taken place, I am not being more faithful to the past. I am refusing to acknowledge another moral fact.

The same problem appears in the argument for revenge. Callard says that revenge takes the wrongdoer’s principle—your bad is my good—and turns it back upon him. In doing so, the victim “teaches a lesson.” But this account assumes that the wrongdoer’s act really expressed a principle fit to govern the relationship. Often it did not. Theft may express greed, cowardice, desperation, contempt, addiction, confusion, or opportunism. Even when it expresses a maxim, the maxim may be one the thief does not endorse on reflection. If I steal your wallet, it does not follow that I have legislated a principle permitting you to steal mine. More importantly, your adopting my bad principle does not hold me responsible for it; it reproduces it.

There is a difference between accountability and imitation. A parent who punishes a child for lying need not lie to the child. A court that sentences a kidnapper need not kidnap him. A friend who confronts betrayal need not betray in return. Indeed, much of moral accountability consists in refusing the wrongdoer’s terms. “You treated me as an object; I will not treat you as one.” “You used fear; I will not.” “You humiliated me; I will answer you without humiliation.” These responses are not weak evasions of accountability. They may be more exacting than revenge, because they demand that the wrongdoer face a standard he violated rather than a mirror image of his own violation.

Callard is also too quick to equate anger’s “logical” conclusion with endless vengeance. Emotions have internal tendencies, but they do not contain complete action plans. Fear may prompt flight, but courage does not require denying fear; it requires perceiving what fear discloses and then acting well. Desire may prompt possession, but love may discipline desire into patience. Anger may prompt retaliation, but it can also prompt speech, protest, withdrawal, demand, organization, punishment, or reform. It is not a philosopher’s fiction to distinguish anger’s perception of wrong from revenge’s project of harm. The distinction is fragile, but real. Civilization depends on many fragile but real distinctions.

The current emergency, as I write in April 2020, makes this especially vivid. People are angry—at governments that delayed, employers that endangered workers, profiteers who hoarded, citizens who treated public health as optional. Some of that anger is not merely understandable; it is morally necessary. But it matters enormously whether anger becomes a demand for truth, protection, compensation, and institutional change, or whether it becomes the indiscriminate search for enemies. The difference between public accountability and scapegoating is not a semantic trick. It is the difference between justice and another round of damage.

Callard’s most provocative claim is that oppression makes the oppressed morally worse. Here again she captures something real but states it too starkly. Injustice can deform the moral lives of its victims. It can make trust dangerous, tenderness costly, suspicion adaptive, and rage habitual. It can teach people to see enemies everywhere because enemies have in fact been everywhere. No sentimental account of victimhood should deny this. But to call this simply “moral corruption” risks flattening the distinction between wound and vice. If someone limps because I broke his leg, his limp is not a failure of character. If he becomes guarded because betrayal was constant, his guardedness may be a scar, a strategy, a burden, perhaps sometimes a danger—but not straightforwardly corruption.

The deeper question is whether being morally damaged always means being morally worse. Trauma may constrict some capacities while enlarging others. Victims of injustice can acquire moral knowledge that the comfortable lack: knowledge of humiliation, hypocrisy, danger, and endurance. That knowledge may come with anger, and anger may sometimes tempt revenge. But it may also sharpen perception. The oppressed are not morally purified by oppression; Callard is right about that. But neither are they morally degraded in the same ledger on which their oppressors are degraded. The asymmetry matters.

Callard wants to force us to abandon fantasies of innocence. That is a valuable ambition. There is too much moral talk, especially in politics, that divides the world into the spotless and the damned. Yet the way out of innocence is not to say that every response to wrong is itself wrong. It is to recognize that moral life is temporal, relational, and reparative. We live after damage. We remember what cannot be undone. But we also ask what can now be made, restored, limited, learned, or forgiven.

The wrong done on Monday remains forever. But Thursday is not Tuesday. If the thief has truly repented and repaired, then to remain just as angry is not fidelity to moral fact; it is neglect of moral change. The challenge is not to purify anger into something bloodless. It is to educate anger so that it remains answerable to the whole truth: not only the truth that wrong occurred, but also the truth that human beings can sometimes answer for wrong without being forever reduced to it.

**The Extreme Stoic Has More Friends Than Callard Thinks**

Near the end of her essay, Callard reaches for a figure she calls the Extreme Stoic, who "places no value on anything the world can remove from himself" and so can be entirely anger-free. She names Socrates, says she knows no modern thinker who represents this position, and treats it as a kind of asymptotic limit that the rest of us cannot reach. "Most of us are neither willing nor able to achieve the kind of detachment that this immunity from wrath requires."

I want to push back on this framing in two ways. First, the Extreme Stoic is not as extreme as Callard makes him out to be, nor as friendless. Second, what the contemplative traditions actually recommend is not the elimination of moral response but a transformation in what we take ourselves to be defending when we respond. Once we see this, Callard's claim that we cannot purify our response to injustice looks less like a deep discovery about morality and more like a parochial conclusion drawn from a single tradition's vocabulary.

Begin with the Buddhists. Callard nods to Śāntideva, but she treats Buddhist anti-anger as a maximalist outlier. In fact, the Buddhist analysis of anger is detailed, sophisticated, and has been refined over two millennia of meditative and ethical practice. The Buddhist does not deny that wrongdoing happens or that it should be opposed. The bodhisattva is enjoined to act vigorously against suffering and injustice. What the Buddhist denies is that the affective state called *dveṣa* (hatred, aversion) is required for or even helpful to such action. On the contrary: the Buddhist holds that hate is a kind of cognitive distortion, one that fuses an accurate perception ("this is harmful and should stop") with an inaccurate one ("the being causing this harm is a fixed, separate self maximally identified with this harmful action and deserving of suffering in return").

Disentangle the accurate from the inaccurate, the Buddhist says, and you get something that looks a lot like what Nussbaum calls transition anger: clear-eyed recognition of the wrong, fierce motivation to address it, but without the colonizing grudge-life Callard describes. Callard says this purified response is a "philosopher's fiction." But it is not a fiction; it is the explicit, documented result of long contemplative training, attested by countless practitioners and refined into specific techniques (loving-kindness, equanimity, analytic meditation on the causes of harmful action).

Callard's response would presumably be: yes, but those people have given up the moral seriousness of anger. They no longer hold wrongdoers responsible in the colonizing, intimate way that anger requires. Exactly so. The Buddhist's reply is: that intimate colonization is not in fact required for holding-responsible, and the fact that it feels required to those raised in our particular moral grammar reflects a feature of our culture, not of morality. Holding-responsible can be done from a position of clarity rather than from a position of grievance. Indeed the Buddhist would say it is done *better* from clarity, because the brooding fantasy-life of the grudge-holder distorts perception and motivates disproportionate response.

Now consider what Callard says about Socrates. She notes that he died anger-free. She does not mention that he also held that no one does wrong willingly, that injustice harms its perpetrator more than its victim, and that the appropriate response to being wronged is therefore something like pity rather than anger. This is not a quirky aside; it is the cornerstone of the Socratic ethical position. And it has had enormous downstream influence — through the Stoics, through Augustine, through much of Christian ethics, and through modern figures like Tolstoy, Gandhi, and Martin Luther King Jr., all of whom are quite recognizably modern thinkers and quite recognizably not in Callard's roster.

King is an especially interesting case because his nonviolent practice is sometimes glossed as if it required suppressing anger. It did not. King wrote movingly about the role of righteous indignation in the civil rights movement, but he also wrote with equal care about *agape* — a form of love that is not affective sentiment but a disciplined will toward the good of the other, including the oppressor. King's claim was precisely that one could hold white supremacists fully accountable for their crimes while refusing the colonizing grudge-life of vengeance. He thought this was not only possible but morally necessary, because vengeance would corrupt the movement and reproduce the very logic of oppression it sought to overcome. King is, in Callard's terms, a near-Extreme Stoic, and he is anything but acquiescent.

So we have a tradition stretching from Socrates through the Stoics, through Buddhism, through Christian agape, through modern nonviolence — a tradition that says: yes, respond to wrong, fiercely if necessary; no, you do not have to do so by making the wrongdoer's bad your good. This is not one philosopher's fiction. It is a vast and well-attested human possibility.

Why, then, does Callard treat it as marginal? I think because she is taking the phenomenology of anger in our particular culture as evidence about the structure of moral response as such. In our culture, anger does often have the structure she describes: it does colonize, it does fantasize vengeance, it does cling. But that is a cultural fact, not a transcendental one. Cultures that have trained their members differently produce different phenomenologies.

Here Callard might object that her Arguments for Grudges and Revenge are arguments about rationality, not about culture. The wrong remains a wrong; therefore the reason to be angry remains; therefore the anger is rational. But this argument proves too much. By the same logic, every reason-giving fact in the world would generate undiminished affective response forever. The fact that my friend died ten years ago is unchanged; the fact that I have a body is unchanged; the fact that suffering exists is unchanged. We do not think it is irrational to feel grief differently at ten years than at one week, or to feel embodied joy without constant amazement. Reasons for affective states are temporally and contextually structured. To say "once you have reason X, you have it forever" is to confuse the persistence of a propositional content with the appropriateness of a constant affective intensity.

The Buddhist and the Stoic make a subtler claim: the original anger-response was itself based on a partly mistaken construal of the situation. When you trained yourself out of that construal, you trained yourself out of the anger. Not by repression but by understanding. The wrong remains; what changes is your relation to it, because you now see it more accurately — as the product of causes and conditions, as an event that does not require you to construct a vengeful counter-self in response.

Callard concludes that "we can't be good in a bad world." This is a striking line, and it may be true on some construal. But the construal that makes it true is one in which goodness is defined by maintaining a particular affective response to wrong, and that response is described as ineliminably vengeful. Change either part of that definition — and the contemplative traditions give us serious resources for doing so — and the conclusion dissolves.

I do not want to suggest the path is easy. Of course it is not. Callard is right that most of us, most of the time, get angry in the colonizing way she describes. But "most of us, most of the time" is not the same as "rationality requires." The Extreme Stoic is not a fantasy figure; he is a recognizable human achievement, partial and imperfect but real, and the traditions that cultivate him have given us more guidance than Callard's essay acknowledges. Before concluding that we cannot be good in a bad world, we should look harder at the people who, by ordinary lights, manage something pretty close to it.

"Agnes Callard’s claim that anger is inherently corrupting and rationally requires eternal grudges is based on a flawed, static view of reasons that ignores the communicative and relational nature of moral repair."

Agnes Callard’s provocative lead essay draws a great deal of its unsettling power from a single, deceptively simple move: she treats the reasons for anger as if they were frozen in time, immune to the very forms of moral repair that our practices of accountability make possible. Once I steal from you, she argues, the fact of the theft is forever fixed in the past, a permanent gap between what I did and what I ought to have done. Nothing I do later—apology, restitution, sincere promise—can erase that fact. So, she concludes, your reasons for being angry remain intact; you have a reason to be angry forever. The Argument for Grudges thus appears to fall out of a few unremarkable premises. But this line of reasoning conflates having a reason with having a sufficient or undefeated reason, and in doing so it drains anger of its essentially communicative nature—a nature that Callard’s own sensibility might otherwise lead her to appreciate.

What the Argument for Grudges misses is that the reasons for anger are not exhaustively backward-looking. Yes, anger is a way of grasping that something is broken, but it also makes a demand: that the wrongdoer recognize the wrong, disavow it, and take responsibility. This is the grain of truth running from Adam Smith through Strawson: the reactive attitudes like anger treat their targets as members of a moral community, capable of answering for what they’ve done. When I apologize with full sincerity, make restitution, and credibly commit to change, I am not trying to make the past disappear. I am answering your demand. In doing so, I give you a new kind of reason: a forward-looking reason to let the anger go, not because the past was not terrible, but because the moral relationship has been repaired insofar as it can be. The fact that the theft was wrong does not change, but its significance for our ongoing relationship shifts. My amends alter the normative landscape between us, and your anger, if it is to remain rational, must be responsive to that shift.

Callard anticipates this reply and implicitly dismisses it by insisting that the original reasons remain. But this is like saying I have a reason to grieve a lost loved one forever because the loss is permanent. That is true in a thin sense: I will always have some reason to feel the pain of the absence. But it doesn’t follow that interminable, unmodified grief is rationally required, or even rationally fitting. The passage of time, the integration of the loss into a meaningful life, and the other values I pursue all give me reasons to modulate my grief. Similarly, your anger, if it is part of a healthy moral psychology, is not a static reaction but a dynamic attitude that must answer to the full range of practical reasons, including the reason to move forward with someone who has done the hard work of making amends. The Argument for Grudges works only if we accept an implausibly atomistic picture of reasons, one that ignores how reasons for reactive attitudes are essentially relational and temporally extended.

The deeper problem with Callard’s approach is that it subtly relocates the location of moral corruption. Her analysis makes anger’s rational endpoint a kind of self-perpetuating fixation, and then she argues that such fixation is both rationally grounded and morally corrupting. But if anger genuinely contains an internal orientation toward repair—if what it fundamentally seeks is not blood but acknowledgment—then the “dark side” of grudge-bearing and disproportionate revenge is not anger’s logical conclusion but a pathological deviation. This is not to retreat into a philosopher’s fiction of purified indignation. It is to recognize that emotions are not inferential machines whose outputs are fully determined by a single unchanging premise. They are shaped by habituation, by normative ideals, and by a cultivated responsiveness to the complex moral reality of other people. The fact that many people in fact cling to vengeful grudges no more shows that such clinging is rationally required by anger than the prevalence of crippling anxiety shows that anxiety rationally demands paralysis.

Callard’s Argument for Revenge is similarly contestable. She portrays revenge as a way of holding the wrongdoer responsible by converting the one-off opposition of interests into a rule: I make your bad my good. But this is a deeply partial picture of what holding responsible involves. A far more central and widely practiced form of accountability is what we might call the moral demand for recognition. You hold me responsible not by inflicting symmetrical harm but by insisting that I confront what I have done, by withdrawing trust until I earn it back, by demanding that I internalize the wrongness of my action. These forms of holding responsible are perfectly responsive to the backward-looking fact of the wrongdoing without requiring that you, the victim, transform your own good into my suffering. Revenge, as Callard describes it, is one possible expression of the angry impulse, but to present it as the central mechanism of moral accountability stacks the deck in favor of the conclusion that morality is inescapably corrupt.

The result is that Callard’s crisis—the claim that victims are inevitably morally damaged, whether by anger or by acquiescence—rests on a false dilemma. She assumes that any anger worth the name carries a permanent, quasi-rational orientation toward perpetuating harm, and that any attempt to steer clear of this orientation is either an idle fantasy or a form of acquiescence. But there is a vast middle ground that has been the subject of careful philosophical attention at least since the virtue-ethical tradition took up the idea of tempering the passions. We can be sensitive to injustice, motivated to protest and resist, and yet cultivate habits of anger that do not demand limitless payback. The fact that this is psychologically difficult and often imperfectly achieved does not make it fictional. It makes it an aspiration, one that the very moral communities Callard draws on—the Jewish tradition with its emphasis on justice and remembrance, the anti-racist and feminist movements that transform anger into organized political action—have long tried to realize.

None of this denies the profound insight Callard brings: our moral lives are marked by tragedy, by the impossibility of a perfectly clean response to being wronged. Her grandmother’s anger was not pristine, and her own childhood game was not innocent. But the lesson to draw from this is not that being wronged makes us worse people in a way that is equivalent to the corruption of the wrongdoer. It is that moral life forces us to navigate ineliminable tensions—between backward-looking protest and forward-looking repair, between the demand for justice and the pull of mercy—without the comfort of a pure, unambivalent stance. That is not corruption; it is the condition of finite, relational creatures who must live with one another after harm. Framing it as corruption risks treating the very difficulty of the moral life as a kind of contamination, rather than as the terrain on which moral maturity is forged. And that, in the end, is a more hopeful conclusion than Callard’s, not because it denies the darkness she sees, but because it refuses to mistake difficulty for defeat.

"Agnes Callard’s claim that morality is inherently corrupting is based on logical slippages that incorrectly equate moral accountability with irrational grudges and vengeful obsession."

Agnes Callard has written a bracing essay, and its most important service is to embarrass a certain kind of philosophical hygiene — the move by which both Stoics and sentimentalists discreetly invent purified surrogates ("transition anger," "righteous indignation") to do anger's moral work without anger's stains. She is right that this is suspicious, and right that grudges and revenge cannot be waved away as mere psychological tics. Still, I think her two central arguments — for Grudges and for Revenge — depend on slippages that, once noticed, deflate the more vertiginous conclusion that we cannot be good in a bad world.

Start with the Argument for Grudges. Callard reasons: the reason you had to be angry on Tuesday was that I wronged you, that fact is unchangeable, therefore you have that same reason to be angry forever. But "you wronged me" is not, strictly, what anger responds to. Anger responds to standing in a certain live relation to a wrongdoer: someone who has done me harm and has not yet acknowledged it, repaired it, or recommitted to the norms between us. When I apologize, pay restitution, and promise reform — and you believe me — the historical fact of the theft remains, but the relational fact (an unrepaired breach between us) does not. Compare grief: the death of someone I love is permanent, yet grief that did not attenuate over years would not count as a more faithful response to the fact; it would count as pathological mourning. Reasons for emotions track not bare events but the present significance of those events. Callard writes that "anger... is a way of grasping the fact that it is broken." Exactly — and once it is, in the relevant sense, no longer broken, the grasping ceases to be apt. Her argument equivocates between the historical and the relational reading of "what you were angry about."

The Argument for Revenge has a similar shape. Callard says holding someone accountable means turning their wrong into "a principle and rule for our interactions" — and she glosses this as "making my bad your good." But these are two different things. To turn a wrong into a principle is to insist: you may not do this; if you do, there are consequences; the norm you violated still binds us. That can be done through protest, demand for repair, withdrawal of trust, sanction, even punishment — none of which requires me to reorganize my own welfare around your harm. Vengeance does require that reorganization: the avenger's good consists in the wrongdoer's suffering. Holding accountable does not. A judge who sentences a thief is not better off because the thief is worse off; she is doing something else entirely, namely upholding a norm. Callard's vivid phenomenology — the colonized fantasy life, the mental effigy, the "uncanny intimacy" — describes vengeful obsession beautifully, but it does not show that this is what moral accountability *is*. It shows what happens when accountability is privatized and emotionalized rather than handed off to institutions, friends, or time.

Her own anecdote actually cuts against her view more than she lets on. Her grandmother, full of warranted historical anger, treats a German girl who had wronged no one as if she were continuous with the perpetrators. Callard is honest enough to call this not innocent on either side. But notice what the example actually illustrates: the grudge here does not faithfully track moral reality; it overgeneralizes, transferring guilt across persons. If grudges were the rational extension of accurate moral perception, we would expect them to discriminate. Instead they smear. That is evidence that grudges are not the lucid emotional inference Callard's argument makes them out to be, but a familiar cognitive distortion — one whose pull we should acknowledge without dignifying as valid reasoning.

This leaves the deepest layer of her essay — the Nietzsche-Girard-Foucault thought that morality and violence are mutually implicated — with real force but a smaller scope than she wants. Yes: any practical regime of holding-accountable will involve coercion, exclusion, sometimes suffering inflicted in the name of the good. There is no antiseptic morality. But "the institutions of accountability carry traces of the violence they regulate" is a much weaker claim than "the only rational response to injustice is grudge and revenge, so victims of oppression are morally damaged by their own moral responses." The first is a sober observation about non-ideal ethics. The second sounds like an insight only because Callard has built it by collapsing accountability into vengeance and apt protest into eternal anger.

**Transition Anger Is Not a Philosopher's Fiction**

Agnes Callard's provocative essay accuses both Stoics and sentimentalists of bad faith: in trying to carve off the "moral side" of anger from its "dark side," she says, we have invented philosopher's fictions like "indignation" and "transition anger" that name nothing real. The real thing — what actually surges in us when we are wronged — is, on her view, ineliminably tied to grudge-bearing and to the desire to make another's evil our good. Purifying anger, she concludes, is impossible, and the project of doing so distracts us from the genuine moral crisis: we cannot respond rightly to being treated wrongly.

This is a powerful argument, and I want to take its central provocation seriously. But I think Callard mistakes a feature of one phenomenology of anger for the structure of anger as such, and in doing so she underestimates the resources Nussbaum's "transition anger" actually has.

Consider Callard's Argument for Grudges. The reasons that originally justified anger — that I stole from you, that I ought not to have — are not erased by my apology and restitution. They remain true forever. Therefore, she concludes, you have reason to be just as angry on Thursday as on Tuesday. Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever.

But notice the equivocation here between "reason to be angry" and "reason to be angry to the same intensity, in the same form, indefinitely." It is true that the past wrong remains a wrong forever. What is not obvious is that the appropriate affective response to "you wronged me two years ago, you apologized sincerely, you made restitution, and you have lived as a friend to me since" is the same affective response as to "you just wronged me." The fact picked out as the object of anger changes over time even if the underlying historical event does not. The fact is no longer "this person is in unrepented violation of moral relations with me" but "this person did, on one occasion, violate moral relations with me, and has since done everything possible to repair them." These are different facts, and they warrant different responses.

Callard might reply that the past wrong itself, as such, remains a reason. But this proves too much. By the same logic, gratitude should also be eternal and undiminished: you saved my life in 1995, that fact is unchanging, therefore I owe you the same intensity of gratitude forever. We do not actually think this. Gratitude can be deep and lasting without being constantly felt at the same pitch. Reasons for affective states are not like reasons for beliefs; they are temporally textured.

The Argument for Revenge fares no better. Callard offers a strikingly elegant gloss: revenge takes the principle implicit in your wronging me — "your bad is my good" — and turns it back on you as a rule, "educating" you into moral accountability. The image is compelling. But it is also a description of one particular cultural-psychological enactment of holding-responsible, not of holding-responsible as such. The Greek tragedies Callard's tradition reaches for are full of this logic; so is the honor culture of the Mediterranean and the Pashtun and the antebellum South. But there are also cultures, and individuals within every culture, where holding-responsible takes other forms: refusing to do business with a cheat, telling the truth about what happened, naming and shaming, withdrawing trust, refusing to pretend. None of these requires the intimate, colonizing reciprocity Callard describes.

What Callard has done, I think, is take Strawson's reactive attitudes and read into them the maximal version: not just resentment but the brooding, fantasy-life-colonizing resentment of the wronged who cannot move on. Anyone who has been wronged knows that state. But anyone who has been wronged also knows other states: the state of feeling the wrong cleanly, naming it, and then turning back to one's own life; the state of refusing to forgive while also refusing to be consumed; the state of letting time soften an injury without erasing its truth. These are not philosopher's fictions; they are ordinary human achievements. They are what Nussbaum is pointing at with "transition anger" and what therapeutic, religious, and political traditions across the world cultivate.

Callard's response will be that these "achievements" are achievements only because we have suppressed the rational response, which is endless vengeance. But this confuses a phenomenological claim with a normative one. The mere fact that endless vengeance is psychologically possible, and that some humans default to it, does not show it is what reason requires. Reason often requires us to weigh many considerations, including the moral status of the wrongdoer-after-repentance, the costs of grudge-keeping to one's own flourishing, the duty not to colonize one's psychic life with another's evil, and the recognition that human beings are, in fact, capable of moral repair.

There is also something curious about Callard's portrait of the wrongdoer in the original example. By Wednesday, the thief has not merely returned the goods but compensated, apologized sincerely, offered gifts, and promised reform — and the victim believes the apology is sincere. Callard insists the historical fact "you stole" is unchanged. True. But she elides the changed fact: this person, who stole, has become a person-who-stole-and-fully-repented. To be angry with them on Thursday as on Tuesday is to refuse to update one's representation of who they now are. This is not rational tenacity; it is a failure to track the moral world as it currently is.

I do not want to deny the dark phenomenology Callard describes. Grudges colonize. Revenge has its own seductive logic. The wronged often are, as she says of her grandmother, "full of anger" — and this anger is not innocent. Callard's point that long-term oppression morally damages the oppressed is important and underappreciated. It deserves the prominence she gives it.

But the conclusion she draws — that this damage reflects the rational structure of anger, that the alternative to corruption is acquiescence, that purification is a fiction — does not follow. Between the eternal vengeance of the unhealed grudge and the supine acquiescence of the doormat lies a vast territory of human moral practice: protest, repair, mourning, forgiveness (sometimes), accountability-without-cruelty, and yes, transition anger. To call all of this a "philosopher's fiction" is to insult the actual lives of people who have done the difficult work of being wronged without being deformed by it.

Callard ends by saying we can't be good in a bad world. Perhaps. But the bad world she describes is, in part, a function of the maximalist picture of anger she has chosen to defend. Loosen that picture, attend more carefully to the actual phenomenology of moral repair, and the world looks less hopeless. It does not look innocent — Callard is right that innocence is not on offer — but it looks like a world in which transition anger names something real: the residue of injury, felt and acknowledged, that does not have to harden into bloodlust to remain morally serious.

The deepest mistake in Callard's argument is, I think, the assumption that the alternative to vengeance must be erasure. Either you keep the wrong alive by hating the wrongdoer, or you betray the wrong by letting it go. This binary is false. Memory is not the same as resentment. Knowing what happened is not the same as nursing one's wrath to keep it warm. There is a way to carry an injury that neither denies it nor lets it govern one. Calling that way a fiction is what allows the argument for limitless violence to look plausible. It is not plausible. It is one option, and not the one reason recommends.

  2. 02Reasons for emotions are temporally textured and do not require constant intensity, much like how the unchanging fact of a past favor does not require eternal, high-pitch gratitude.
  3. 03Callard’s 'Argument for Revenge' describes only one specific cultural-psychological enactment of holding others responsible rather than the universal structure of moral accountability.
  4. 04The ability to acknowledge a wrong and seek accountability without being consumed by bloodlust or nursing a grudge is an ordinary human achievement rather than a philosopher’s fiction.
  5. 05To remain as angry after a sincere apology as before it is a failure to rationally track the moral world as it currently exists.
  6. 06The alternative to vengeance is not the erasure of memory or acquiescence, but a middle ground of protest, repair, and accountability-without-cruelty.

"Agnes Callard’s claim that anger inevitably leads to moral corruption is based on a flawed, static view of morality that ignores the rational capacity for relational repair and forgiveness."

Agnes Callard presents a deeply provocative and ultimately despairing vision of human morality. By arguing that anger cannot be divorced from endless grudges and boundless vengeance, she arrives at the bleak conclusion that responding to injustice inevitably corrupts us. In Callard’s view, because the historical fact of an injustice cannot be erased, the rational justification for anger remains forever. Therefore, she claims, victims of oppression are morally compromised by the very act of holding their oppressors accountable. While her argument is elegant in its relentless logic, it relies on a fundamentally static view of human relationships and a misunderstanding of what moral anger actually tracks. We can, in fact, be good in a bad world, provided we understand anger not as a permanent ledger of historical facts, but as a dynamic, relational demand for moral equality.

The hinge of Callard’s thesis is what she calls the “Argument for Grudges.” She asks us to imagine a theft on Monday, followed by complete restitution, apology, and behavioral change on Wednesday. Callard posits that if it was rational to be angry on Tuesday, it remains rational to be angry on Thursday, because the apology does nothing to alter the fact that the theft occurred. “Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever,” she writes.

This argument makes a category error regarding the object of our moral emotions. As Peter Strawson famously delineated, our reactive attitudes—like resentment and indignation—are responses to the *quality of will* expressed by another person, not merely to the material harms they cause or the historical timeline of their actions. When someone steals from me, the reason for my anger is not merely the absence of my property. My anger is a response to the communicative content of their action: the thief’s behavior asserts that my rights do not matter, that my boundaries are permeable, and that I am of lesser moral status.

When the offender returns the property, compensates me, and sincerely apologizes, they do not change the past, but they absolutely change the present moral reality. A sincere apology is a retraction of the original insult. It is a communicative act in which the offender disavows their previous quality of will and publicly aligns their values with my own, acknowledging my equal moral standing. Therefore, the reason for my anger *has* been addressed. The historical fact of the theft remains, but the ongoing moral threat—the disrespect and the assertion of unequal status—has been neutralized. It is entirely rational to let go of anger at this point, not because we have developed amnesia, but because the relational breach that the anger was protesting has been repaired. By treating anger as a response to a static historical event rather than a dynamic relational stance, Callard ignores the rational mechanism of forgiveness.

Callard’s “Argument for Revenge” suffers from a similar reductionism. She argues that revenge is simply how we hold one another accountable: because the offender made our bad their good, we must make their bad our good. She claims this adversarial embrace is the logical endpoint of moral responsiveness. But this conflates holding someone accountable with the infliction of suffering for its own sake.

True moral accountability is not a zero-sum transfer of pain; it is a demand for recognition. When we hold someone accountable, we force them to reckon with the reality of our personhood and the consequences of their actions. If punishment is involved, its morally rational aim—distinct from sadism or bloodlust—is to bring the offender to an understanding of the harm they caused, ideally leading to their reintegration into the moral community. Callard describes the angry person forcing their own thinking "down [the offender's] throat," but she misidentifies the goal. The goal of righteous indignation is not to eternally enslave the offender in a flipped hierarchy, but to establish a mutual understanding of justice that was absent during the offense.

The most troubling consequence of Callard’s framework is her conclusion regarding victims of systemic injustice. She argues that because anger inherently contains the seeds of vengeance and grudges, long-term oppression inflicts long-term moral damage on the oppressed. In her words, the oppressors make the oppressed "morally worse people."

This is a profound misreading of the function of anger for marginalized groups. Far from being a corrupting force, anger in the face of systemic degradation is often a vital assertion of one’s own humanity. In her seminal 1981 essay "The Uses of Anger," the poet and essayist Audre Lorde articulated how the anger of Black women in a racist society is not a moral failing, but an essential tool for survival and clarity. Lorde distinguished clearly between hatred, which seeks the destruction of the other, and anger, which demands change and the rectification of a distorted relationship.

To label the defensive, boundary-asserting anger of the oppressed as “moral corruption” shifts a heavy philosophical burden onto the vulnerable. It pathologizes the very emotional mechanism by which marginalized people resist internalizing the degradation forced upon them. When Callard’s grandmother—a survivor of unimaginable trauma—reacted with anger to a German girl, she was not necessarily executing a perfectly rational moral deduction about collective guilt. She was reacting from a place of profound trauma. We can hold deep compassion for the psychological scars of oppression without conceding that eternal vengeance is the logically sound conclusion of moral injury. Trauma may make letting go of anger incredibly difficult, but that is a psychological reality, not a proof of Callard’s philosophical rule.

Callard challenges us to step inside our own moral theories and face the darkness she believes is baked into them. But the darkness she sees is a shadow cast by an incomplete theory. If we strip away the possibility of genuine relational repair, if we deny the moral power of apologies, and if we view accountability only as a mirroring of cruelty, then of course we are left with nothing but bloodlust and cynicism. But human morality is vastly richer than this. Anger is the alarm bell that alerts us to a fire; it is entirely rational to turn the alarm off once the fire has been extinguished. We are not doomed to be corrupted by our injuries. Through the demanding, rational work of accountability, restitution, and forgiveness, we can indeed be good in a bad world.
