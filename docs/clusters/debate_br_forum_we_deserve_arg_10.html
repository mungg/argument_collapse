"Evgeny Morozov’s critique of AI as irredeemably neoliberal and instrumental is overly dismissive of the practical, democratic efforts currently being made by 'realists' to reform and reappropriate the technology."

Evgeny Morozov’s “The AI We Deserve” is a thrilling intellectual ride, tracing the lineage of artificial intelligence from Cold War bureaucracy to the market-shaped generative models of today, and proposing a utopian alternative rooted in what he calls ecological reason. He invites us to imagine a different history, one in which computing nurtured curiosity and play rather than instrumental problem-solving. Yet for all its erudition and verve, the essay ultimately preaches to a choir already convinced that neoliberal technoscience is irredeemable. In doing so, it badly misrepresents the realist position it dismisses, foreclosing the very democratic possibilities that are already taking shape right under our noses.

Morozov paints the debate as two camps: realists, who accept AI as it is and seek only policy tweaks, and refuseniks, who denounce it as hopelessly tainted. This is a caricature. The most thoughtful “realists” are anything but complacent. They include researchers, activists, and policymakers fighting for open-source models, public AI infrastructure, algorithmic transparency, and democratic governance of data. Far from accepting the status quo, they are engaged in a pitched battle against the concentration of power Morozov decries. By framing their work as mere “pragmatic adjustments,” he obscures the genuinely transformative potential of these efforts. When the European Union passes the AI Act with its risk-based framework, or when cities like Barcelona push for municipal data trusts, these are not cosmetic reforms; they are contests over the very institutional architecture of AI.

Morozov’s alternative is seductive but politically weightless. He conjures a hypothetical “Humanity Lobby” that might have challenged the Efficiency Lobby in the 1950s, and he points to the Latin American experiments of the early 1970s as glimpses of a better path. Yet those experiments ended in coups, economic strangulation, and defeat. They are inspiring precisely because they were crushed, but they offer no roadmap for today’s democratic struggles within actually existing capitalist states. Morozov is right that technology’s emancipatory potential requires a political project, but he fails to grapple with what that project might look like in, say, contemporary Germany or South Korea. Instead, he gestures toward a vague “radical political project” that seems to require a wholesale rupture with neoliberal capitalism—a rupture that, however desirable, is not on the immediate horizon. In the meantime, we need strategies that can curb Big Tech’s power, build public alternatives, and embed democratic oversight while we work toward deeper transformation.

The realist approach is not an abandonment of radical ambition; it is a recognition that institutions matter and that building countervailing power requires engaging the messy mechanics of regulation, procurement, and standards. The open-source movement in AI, for instance, challenges corporate control not by refusing to build models but by creating commons-based alternatives. Publicly funded AI research, such as the projects under way at the French National Centre for Scientific Research or the Alan Turing Institute in the UK, is actively shaping what counts as legitimate AI development. These initiatives are not trapped within instrumental reason; they are, in fact, exploring exactly the kinds of open-ended, curiosity-driven inquiry Morozov valorizes.

Moreover, Morozov’s own language app anecdote inadvertently undermines his thesis. He describes using ChatGPT to build a personalized learning tool, a process he likens to eolithic flânerie. Yet this experience was enabled by the very commercial, market-based AI he criticizes. The tools were privately owned, subscription-based, and energy-intensive—and yet they allowed him to exercise precisely the kind of playful, non-instrumental intelligence he champions. This suggests that the boundary between instrumental and ecological reason is not as rigid as he implies. Generative AI, for all its embeddedness in capitalism, is genuinely flexible and can be appropriated for purposes its creators never intended. That is a feature, not a bug, and it opens space for collective reappropriation. Imagine a public-interest version of the same capabilities, funded by governments and governed by user communities—an app store for the common good, if you will. That is a realistic, achievable goal, not a utopian fancy.

The deeper flaw in Morozov’s argument is his equation of AI’s origins with its eternal destiny. He chastises the early AI pioneers for their teleological narrowness and their ties to the military-industrial complex, and he suggests that these founding sins irreparably contaminate the entire field. This is a genetic fallacy. Technologies are not forever defined by the intentions of their creators; they are reshaped by the uses to which they are put and the institutions that govern them. The internet began as a Pentagon project, yet it became a medium for social movements, collaborative knowledge production, and democratic deliberation. Nuclear research was born of the Manhattan Project, but nuclear medicine saves lives every day. The task is not to dream of a pristine origin but to fight over the direction of development.

None of this is to say that Morozov’s critique lacks value. His excavation of alternative intellectual traditions—Hans Otto Storm’s eolithism, the Bariloche Foundation’s numerical experimentation—is a genuine contribution. It reminds us that the history of computing could have been otherwise, and that imagination is a vital political resource. But by setting this alternative up as a utopian horizon against which all present efforts are found wanting, he risks encouraging a posture of purist withdrawal. The realists he scorns are the ones doing the hard work of building counter-institutions, of litigating, legislating, and organizing. They know that the AI we deserve will not be handed down by a benevolent Humanity Lobby; it will be won through the unglamorous, never-finished labor of democratic struggle. That struggle is already under way. It deserves more than a dismissive wave of the hand.
