"A truly democratic AI agenda must move beyond metaphorical 'ecological reason' to confront the material environmental costs of computation, such as energy, water, and mineral consumption."

Morozov’s essay uses “ecological reason” to describe a form of intelligence grounded in context, indeterminacy, play, and mutual adjustment with the world. It is a suggestive phrase. But in an age of climate breakdown, AI’s ecological question cannot remain metaphorical. The first test of any democratic AI agenda is whether it confronts the material ecology of computation: energy, water, minerals, land, e-waste, and the unequal geography of environmental harm.

Generative AI has made the physicality of the digital harder to ignore. Training frontier models requires massive data centers packed with specialized chips. Running them for millions of users requires continuing electricity and cooling. The exact numbers are often opaque, and companies have incentives to emphasize efficiency gains while hiding total growth. But the broad trend is clear enough: AI is accelerating demand for compute at precisely the moment when societies must decarbonize grids, conserve water, and allocate scarce infrastructure wisely. The fact that a single query may consume only a modest amount of energy compared with other activities does not settle the matter. At scale, and under a business model that encourages endless generation, even marginal costs become collective burdens.

Morozov’s language-learning app is charming, and his self-critique is important. Do we need every learner generating bespoke stories and audio anew, each through paid APIs, when shared resources could serve many people? This is not a small design question. It points to the absurdity of a computational economy organized around individualized abundance. We are building systems that can generate infinite custom text, images, code, voices, and video. But the planet does not have infinite energy, water, or mineral capacity. A democracy must ask not only “Can AI do this?” but “Is this use worth its material cost, compared with other uses?”

That question is largely absent from the dominant AI race. Companies compete to make models bigger, faster, more multimodal, and more deeply integrated into everyday workflows. Investors reward growth. Cloud providers build data centers. Governments fear falling behind China or losing industrial advantage. Users are encouraged to treat generation as frictionless. The result is a familiar pattern: private actors capture convenience and revenue while environmental costs are diffused across communities, grids, watersheds, and future generations.

The geography matters. Data centers are built somewhere. They draw on local electricity systems and water supplies. Chip supply chains require mining, fabrication, shipping, and disposal. The burdens do not fall evenly. Communities already exposed to industrial pollution, water stress, or weak labor protections are more likely to bear hidden costs. The “cloud” is not in the sky; it is in places where land is cheap, regulation is negotiable, and political power may be limited. A democratic AI politics must make those places visible.

Morozov contrasts the Efficiency Lobby with a Humanity Lobby. Environmental justice demands that the Humanity Lobby include the people living next to the infrastructure of AI and those extracting its materials. It is not enough to democratize access to models if the costs of access are imposed on others. Nor is it enough to build public AI systems if they reproduce the same energy-intensive habits as private platforms. A public bad is still a bad.

What would an ecologically serious AI agenda look like? First, mandatory transparency about energy use, water use, carbon emissions, and hardware supply chains. Companies developing and deploying large models should disclose not only training emissions but inference costs, data center locations in meaningful terms, water impacts, and mitigation strategies. Public agencies should not procure AI systems without environmental reporting.

Second, compute should be treated as a scarce public resource, not an endlessly expandable commodity. This does not mean banning frivolous uses by decree. It means designing incentives and institutions that favor reuse, caching, smaller models, domain-specific systems, and non-generative alternatives when they suffice. If a database lookup can answer a question, it is wasteful to summon a giant language model. If a shared educational resource can serve thousands of learners, it is wasteful to regenerate near-identical materials for each user. If a local model can perform a task adequately, it may be preferable to a frontier model reached through a distant data center.

Third, AI evaluation should include sufficiency, not just performance. The benchmark culture of AI rewards marginal gains on abstract tasks. Ecological democracy would ask: what is the smallest, least resource-intensive system that meets a legitimate need? In medicine, law, climate adaptation, and accessibility, more capable systems may justify substantial costs. In advertising, spammy content generation, speculative financial optimization, or synthetic engagement bait, they likely do not. A society capable of ecological reason must discriminate between needs and manufactured demand.

Fourth, public investment should prioritize AI for decarbonization and adaptation, but without techno-solutionist fantasies. Machine learning can help with grid management, materials discovery, weather forecasting, building efficiency, biodiversity monitoring, and climate-risk mapping. Yet these tools are useful only when embedded in political commitments to reduce emissions, protect communities, and change infrastructure. AI cannot substitute for transmission lines, heat pumps, public transit, wetland restoration, or fossil fuel phaseout. Indeed, unchecked AI growth could compete with electrification for clean power. A data center boom powered by renewable energy still uses renewable energy that might otherwise decarbonize homes, transport, or industry.

Fifth, democratic control over data center siting and resource use is essential. Local communities should have a voice in whether and how facilities are built. Water-stressed regions should not be pressured into hosting thirsty infrastructure for distant consumers. Utilities should not offer sweetheart deals that shift costs onto residents. If AI is becoming critical infrastructure, its physical footprint must be governed like infrastructure, not waved through as innovation.

This environmental frame also clarifies the limits of Morozov’s eolithism. Play, experimentation, and open-ended creativity are precious. But they are not free. The Stone Age wanderer’s stonefield did not require a hyperscale data center. Generative AI does. The metaphor of the workshop must be joined to the politics of the mine, the power plant, the river, and the landfill. Otherwise ecological reason becomes a privileged experience purchased through ecological unreason elsewhere.

There is also a moral hazard in the rhetoric of AI abundance. Silicon Valley often presents generative AI as a universal assistant that will unlock creativity for all. But abundance at the interface can produce scarcity in the world. Infinite images may mean more energy demand. Infinite text may mean more data storage, more spam, more moderation labor. Infinite personalization may mean fewer shared resources and more isolated consumption. A culture of “why not generate it?” is incompatible with a politics of limits.

None of this requires rejecting AI. It requires rationing attention and resources democratically. Some uses of AI are clearly worth their costs: helping disabled people communicate, improving public health, supporting climate science, translating vital information, reducing dangerous labor, aiding education where teachers remain central. Other uses are socially trivial or actively harmful. The market will not reliably distinguish between them because it recognizes willingness to pay, not public value. That is why Morozov’s call for a political project is indispensable.

The AI we deserve must be low-carbon, water-conscious, repairable, reusable, and governed by communities as well as users. It must encourage shared infrastructures rather than endless individualized generation. It must treat compute as something to be justified, not merely consumed. And it must remember that the ultimate test of intelligence is not whether a machine can answer any prompt. It is whether a society can recognize when not to prompt at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current AI business model of individualized abundance and frictionless generation creates massive collective environmental burdens that are often hidden from the public.
  2. 02Democratic AI politics must make the physical geography of AI visible, acknowledging that data centers and supply chains disproportionately harm vulnerable communities.
  3. 03Mandatory transparency regarding energy use, carbon emissions, and hardware supply chains is a necessary first step for any ecologically serious AI policy.
  4. 04Compute should be treated as a scarce public resource, prioritizing smaller, reusable, or non-generative models over resource-intensive frontier systems.
  5. 05Societies must distinguish between AI applications that serve legitimate public needs, such as medicine or climate science, and those that merely generate manufactured demand or social harm.
  6. 06Local communities must be granted democratic control over the siting of data centers and the allocation of local resources like water and electricity.
  7. 07The 'eolithic' playfulness and creativity celebrated in AI must be tempered by a politics of limits to ensure that individual experimentation does not come at the cost of global ecological unreason.
