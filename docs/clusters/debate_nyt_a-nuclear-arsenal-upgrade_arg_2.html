"The United States must pursue a comprehensive modernization of its nuclear triad and command systems to ensure its deterrent remains credible against evolving global threats."

The United States can safely deter nuclear war only if its arsenal remains credible to adversaries and reassuring to allies. That means modernization cannot be reduced to laboratory maintenance or symbolic disarmament. It must include replacement of aging submarines, bombers, missiles, warheads and command systems. The nuclear force the country relies on today was largely built during the Cold War. Extending its life indefinitely is not a strategy; it is deferred risk.

Russia has violated the spirit, and in some cases allegedly the letter, of post-Cold War restraint. It has seized Crimea, threatened nuclear use in rhetoric, invested in new nuclear systems and relied on escalation threats to compensate for conventional weakness. North Korea has conducted repeated nuclear and missile tests. China is modernizing its forces. In that environment, unilateral American restraint will not make nuclear weapons disappear. It may instead convince allies that they need their own.

The triad remains valuable because each leg complicates an adversary’s planning. Submarines provide survivability. ICBMs force any attacker to contemplate striking hundreds of targets on the American homeland, a massive and unmistakable act. Bombers are visible, recallable and useful for signaling in a crisis. Eliminating one leg might save money, but it would also reduce flexibility and resilience.

The proposed long-range standoff weapon should not be dismissed as a “more usable” nuclear bomb. Its purpose is to ensure that air-delivered nuclear deterrence remains viable against advanced air defenses. If bombers cannot credibly reach targets, the bomber leg becomes a gesture rather than a capability. Similarly, updated gravity bombs and delivery systems are needed for NATO deterrence, where allies live under the shadow of Russian nuclear and conventional power.

Of course modernization should exclude reckless innovation. The United States should not seek exotic new warhead designs, resume nuclear explosive testing or blur nuclear and conventional command systems in ways that invite confusion. It should maintain strong civilian control, rigorous safety procedures and arms control channels with Moscow. But deterrence is not maintained by speeches about abolition. It is maintained when potential aggressors believe there is no winning a nuclear confrontation.

A trillion dollars over several decades is a large sum, but it must be measured against the consequences of deterrence failure. Modernization should be disciplined and reviewed, but it should be real. The safest nuclear weapon is one that never has to be used because no one doubts it will work.

Diversified MiniMax

"The United States must proceed with a comprehensive modernization of its nuclear triad to ensure that its deterrent remains credible, reliable, and capable of preventing catastrophic conflict."

The United States faces a strategic crossroads. With aging weapons systems approaching the end of their operational lifespans, modernization is not optional—it is a security imperative. The current nuclear triad of land-based missiles, submarine-launched warheads, and strategic bombers provides the foundation for credible deterrence, and letting those capabilities atrophy would be reckless. A trillion-dollar investment over decades represents sound insurance against catastrophic risk.

Critics who question such spending often underestimate what deterrence actually requires. Nuclear peace has held for seven decades not because nations were virtuous, but because the costs of nuclear conflict were unthinkable. That credibility depends on forces that adversaries cannot dismiss as unreliable or vulnerable. Modernizing the arsenal means replacing systems that date to the 1970s and 1980s with reliable alternatives that maintain the confidence of both American planners and potential adversaries. Uncertainty about American resolve or capability invites miscalculation.

The Hillary Clinton versus Barack Obama disagreement over smaller, targeted weapons reflects a genuine policy tension worth examining carefully. The case for such weapons rests on providing more usable options to deter lower-level conflicts without escalation. The case against centers on lowering the threshold for nuclear use and potentially destabilizing crisis scenarios. Both concerns have merit. But the appropriate response is not to abandon targeted capabilities entirely—it is to ensure they are designed and deployed with clear limits.

Modernization should proceed with several non-negotiable principles. First, maintain the triad—the combination of delivery systems creates redundancy that no adversary can counter simultaneously. Second, cap warhead numbers through New START and future negotiations rather than expanding the arsenal. Third, invest heavily in command and control systems that ensure presidential authority remains secure and unambiguous. Fourth, resist calls to develop new categories of weapons that blur the line between conventional and nuclear use.

Deterrence works when it is credible, comprehensible, and survivable. A modernized arsenal keeps those properties intact. The cost is significant, but the cost of failing to deter nuclear conflict dwarfs any budget allocation.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States must prioritize and fully fund the comprehensive modernization of the entire nuclear triad to maintain a credible deterrent against evolving global threats."

The debate over smaller nuclear weapons is a dangerous distraction from the fundamental challenge: the entire U.S. nuclear triad is going obsolescent at the same time. Over the next 15 years, the nation must replace its ballistic missile submarines, build a new long-range bomber, acquire a new land-based ICBM, and recapitalize the nuclear command-and-control infrastructure. The estimated $1 trillion price tag over 30 years is not for exotic new weapons; it is the unavoidable cost of maintaining a safe and credible deterrent that has prevented great-power war for seven decades.

Critics fixate on new low-yield warheads, but these are a minor component of the modernization plan. The real question is whether we replace our Ohio-class submarines with the Columbia class and buy the B-21 bomber. If we fail to do so, we will not have a smaller, more ‘reasonable’ arsenal—we will simply have none. The triad’s resilience depends on a diverse mix of platforms, each with different vulnerabilities, so that no single enemy breakthrough can neutralize our deterrent.

The focus on low-yield weapons misses the strategic logic. Even if we build no new warheads, the mere process of modernizing delivery systems requires integrating the existing stockpile into new platforms. The B61-12 life extension is necessary to maintain the safety and reliability of a bomb that is already deployed in Europe. It is not a new weapon; it is a critical recapitalization of an old one. Similar stories apply to other warheads.

Historically, nuclear modernization has been a bipartisan effort. It must remain so. We cannot let partisan disagreements over narrow technical choices derail the entire enterprise. Adversaries are watching. Russia is modernizing its own triad and increasing the tempo of nuclear exercises. China is expanding. North Korea has just conducted its fifth nuclear test. A United States that hesitates is a United States that invites miscalculation.

The next president, whether Democrat or Republican, will inherit a modernization program that is long overdue. Delay will only drive up costs and increase risk. The time for action is now. Let’s fund it, and let’s have the serious strategic discussion about the specific mix later. But above all, let’s not allow the perfect to be the enemy of the necessary.
