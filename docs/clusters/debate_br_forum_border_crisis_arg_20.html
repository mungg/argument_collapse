Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that Ypi’s vision of global class solidarity is a utopian and electorally disastrous strategy that ignores the fact that a sustainable welfare state requires the bounded community and social trust provided by the nation-state."

Lea Ypi’s “The Real Border Crisis” presents a beautifully written, theoretically elegant, and entirely unworkable vision for the future of progressive politics. By framing the modern border as nothing more than an instrument of global capital designed to divide the international working class, Ypi resurrects a classic Marxist internationalism that sounds inspiring in the seminar room but has proven disastrous at the ballot box. In her eagerness to denounce the “exclusionary tendencies” of social democratic parties, she fundamentally misunderstands the nature of the welfare state and the sociological foundations of human solidarity.

Ypi takes aim at figures like Sahra Wagenknecht in Germany and Keir Starmer in the UK, accusing them of capitulating to right-wing hegemony by acknowledging the trade-offs between high levels of immigration and the maintenance of domestic social safety nets. She dismisses the idea that uncontrolled immigration creates distributive and cultural conflicts as a mere "idealized account" or a reactionary myth. But the tension between open borders and a generous welfare state is not a right-wing conspiracy; it is a structural reality recognized by economists, sociologists, and, most importantly, the working-class voters whom the left claims to represent.

A welfare state is essentially a massive system of social insurance. Like any insurance system, it relies on boundaries. It requires a defined pool of contributors and beneficiaries, bound together by a strong sense of mutual obligation and shared fate. This mutual obligation does not materialize out of thin air, nor is it forged solely through abstract "struggles for political representation," as Ypi suggests. It is historically grounded in the nation-state. Citizens are willing to pay high taxes to support the unemployed, the sick, and the elderly because they see those beneficiaries as part of their extended community—people who share their laws, their institutions, and their historical burdens.

When migration happens at a pace and scale that outstrips a society’s capacity to integrate newcomers, this delicate fabric of trust begins to fray. Harvard sociologist Robert Putnam and others have long documented how rapid, unmanaged demographic changes can lead, in the short to medium term, to a decline in social trust. People withdraw from civic life, support for wealth redistribution drops, and communities become atomized. Ypi dismisses these cultural and social dynamics as mere superstructures of class conflict. But culture and community are not simply window dressing for economics; they are the lived realities of citizens. When a working-class neighborhood experiences rapid demographic turnover, the resulting strain on local schools, housing markets, and healthcare services is not a "narrative" constructed by elites. It is a material reality that people experience every day.

Ypi argues that the left should stop playing "catch" with the right and instead build a counter-hegemonic project based on global class solidarity. She points to the "Make Amazon Pay" campaign as a blueprint for this new internationalism. It is a noble example, but it is entirely insufficient to address the scale of the challenge. Transnational labor organizing is incredibly difficult, hindered not just by language and geography, but by vastly different stages of economic development. An Amazon warehouse worker in Leipzig and an Amazon warehouse worker in Mumbai may both be exploited by the same multinational corporation, but their immediate material interests regarding global labor mobility are not identical. In a world of vast economic disparities, opening borders completely—or even significantly loosening them—inevitably increases the labor supply in developed nations, putting downward pressure on the wages and bargaining power of the domestic working class.

Historically, labor unions understood this. From the mid-20th century onwards, the strongest labor movements were those that operated within tightly regulated national labor markets. The idea that we can simply replace national borders with global class solidarity is a utopian leap of faith that asks the most vulnerable domestic workers to bear the brunt of globalization's disruptions.

Furthermore, Ypi’s proposed political strategy—ignoring the electoral consequences of open-border advocacy in the hopes of shaping a long-term "counter-hegemonic" discourse—is a recipe for perpetual political irrelevance. She blithely states that "in a contest on ethnocentrism and immigrant scapegoating, the left will never win against the right," and suggests that short-term electoral losses are an acceptable price to pay for maintaining ideological purity. But in democratic politics, you cannot help the vulnerable if you do not hold power.

We are writing in late 2025, a time when far-right parties have made unprecedented gains across Europe, capitalizing almost entirely on the progressive left’s refusal to address border anxiety seriously. When mainstream social democratic parties vacate the space of border enforcement, they do not usher in an era of Enlightenment cosmopolitanism. They hand the keys of the state directly to the radical right. The "pragmatic" shifts by leaders like Starmer or the Danish Social Democrats are not betrayals of left-wing values; they are necessary recalibrations to save the social democratic project from extinction. By proving that they can manage borders competently and humanely, these parties neutralize the right's most potent weapon and secure the political capital needed to actually build housing, fund healthcare, and empower trade unions.

Ypi is absolutely right to condemn the grotesque commodification of citizenship, where oligarchs buy "Golden Visas" while desperate refugees drown. The hypocrisy of a system that welcomes the capital of the global rich while criminalizing the bodies of the global poor is a moral stain on liberal democracies. We should aggressively tax and regulate these pathways, end citizenship-by-investment schemes, and crack down on the employers who exploit undocumented labor to undercut wages.

But correcting the hypocrisies of the border does not mean abolishing the logic of the border itself. True solidarity requires a foundation of trust, and trust requires a bounded community. If the left follows Ypi's advice and abandons the nation-state in pursuit of an abstract global proletariat, it will not usher in an era of Enlightenment universalism. It will simply watch from the opposition benches as the right dismantles the welfare state, unencumbered and unopposed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A generous welfare state functions as a social insurance system that relies on a defined pool of contributors and a shared sense of mutual obligation historically grounded in the nation-state.
  2. 02Rapid, unmanaged migration can fray the fabric of social trust and place material strains on local infrastructure, which are lived realities for citizens rather than mere elite narratives.
  3. 03Global class solidarity is an insufficient substitute for national borders because workers in different stages of economic development often have conflicting immediate material interests regarding labor mobility.
  4. 04Abandoning border enforcement in favor of ideological purity leads to political irrelevance and hands power to the far right, whereas pragmatic border management secures the political capital necessary to enact progressive domestic policies.
  5. 05While the commodification of citizenship for the rich is a moral stain that must be corrected, addressing these hypocrisies does not necessitate abolishing the fundamental logic of the border.
