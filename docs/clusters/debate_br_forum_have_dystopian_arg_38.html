"Daron Acemoglu’s proposal to redirect AI toward human-centric tasks is based on a flawed historical analogy of the post-war economic boom that ignores the unique demographic and physical factors of that era."

Daron Acemoglu builds his argument for redirecting artificial intelligence on a specific historical foundation: the "thirty glorious years" following World War II, a period of shared prosperity that he attributes to technology naturally creating "new tasks" for human workers. By attempting to use this era as a template for 21st-century technology policy, Acemoglu commits a grave error of economic history. The post-war economic boom was a demographic and geopolitical anomaly, a once-in-a-civilization event that cannot be recreated by simply tweaking the algorithms of modern AI or modifying the tax code.

Acemoglu asks why technology in the mid-twentieth century fueled wage growth and created new tasks, admitting, "We don’t know for sure." But economic historians do know. The period from 1945 to 1975 was defined by the massive rebuilding of a devastated Europe and Japan, which created an insatiable, artificial global demand for American manufacturing exports. Domestically, the United States was experiencing the Baby Boom, driving unprecedented demand for housing, appliances, and infrastructure. Simultaneously, the GI Bill democratized higher education, creating a massive, one-time leap in the human capital of the workforce.

Furthermore, the technologies that matured during this era—electricity, the internal combustion engine, modern plumbing, commercial aviation, and the interstate highway system—were fundamental transformations of the physical world. As economist Robert Gordon has extensively documented, these inventions drove a century of rapid productivity growth that culminated in the post-war era. These physical technologies inherently required massive amounts of human labor to manufacture, install, and maintain. They didn't just "create new tasks" conceptually; they required millions of human hands to physically build out the modern world.

Digital technology, and artificial intelligence in particular, is fundamentally different. It operates in the realm of information, where the marginal cost of reproduction is effectively zero. Once an AI model is trained to diagnose a disease, review a contract, or optimize a logistics network, it can be deployed globally without the need to hire thousands of workers to "install" it. The nature of software is scalability without proportional labor. Acemoglu’s desire to force AI into the mold of a 1950s physical manufacturing technology is essentially asking software not to act like software.

Because Acemoglu misdiagnoses the cause of the post-war boom, his solutions for our current malaise are misaligned. He believes that if the government just directs research funding properly, AI will magically spawn new, high-paying jobs for the masses, just as the post-war era did. But we are now operating in a mature, low-growth economy with an aging demographic profile, burdened by decades of accumulated debt and infrastructural decay. In this environment, the "excessive automation" Acemoglu decries is actually a demographic necessity. As birth rates plummet across the industrialized world and the ratio of retirees to active workers skyrockets, we *must* automate vast sectors of the economy simply to maintain our current standard of living.

Instead of fighting a quixotic battle to recreate the mid-century manufacturing economy in the digital age, we must adapt our social institutions to the realities of a software-driven, low-labor-demand future. This means accepting that the era of rapid, shared wage growth driven by mass employment is likely over. We must sever the link between basic survival and traditional employment. We need institutions that provide housing, healthcare, and education independent of a person's value in the labor market. Yearning for a return to the *Trente Glorieuses* by artificially handicapping artificial intelligence is not a strategy; it is an exercise in historical nostalgia.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The post-war 'thirty glorious years' were driven by unique geopolitical anomalies and physical infrastructure needs that cannot be replicated in the digital age.
  2. 02Unlike the physical technologies of the mid-twentieth century, digital software and AI are inherently scalable without requiring proportional increases in human labor.
  3. 03Acemoglu’s critique of 'excessive automation' fails to account for the demographic necessity of automation in an aging society with a shrinking workforce.
  4. 04Rather than trying to force AI to create traditional jobs, society should adapt by decoupling basic survival and essential services from labor market participation.
