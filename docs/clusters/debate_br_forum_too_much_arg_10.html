"The problems identified in modern mothering and gender inequality are not failures of feminist theory or individual choice, but are structural pathologies produced by a capitalist economic order that refuses to organize itself around human need."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay begins with mothers, but it should end with capitalism. The pathologies she identifies—competitive parenting, maternal dependency, workplace penalties, contempt for poor mothers, the privatization of care—are not simply mistakes in feminist theory or failures of individual women’s nerve. They are produced by an economic order that depends on care while refusing to organize itself around human need.

Capitalism requires workers who arrive each day fed, clothed, socialized, educated, emotionally repaired, and physically sustained. It requires new generations of workers and consumers. It requires care for the sick, elderly, disabled, and young. Yet much of this labor is unpaid or poorly paid, performed disproportionately by women, and treated as outside “the economy.” Care feminism’s achievement was to expose that lie. The danger Hirschmann sees is real: once care is praised as women’s special contribution, women may be pushed back into unpaid dependency. But the answer is not to tell women to become more individualistic. The answer is to reorganize social reproduction.

Hirschmann criticizes stay-at-home mothers who withdraw from public life after receiving expensive educations. But paid labor is not identical with public contribution. Much paid work is socially useless or harmful; much unpaid work is necessary. The recent financial crisis should make this obvious. The trader devising opaque instruments may earn millions while creating systemic risk. The woman caring for a child, a dying parent, or a disabled neighbor may earn nothing while sustaining life. If feminism responds to this by urging women to seek more places at the trading desk, it has accepted the wrong measure of value.

Of course women need independent income and power. Economic dependence on husbands is dangerous, and the breadwinner-homemaker model gives men structural authority. But we should ask why the only reliable route to independence is full participation in a labor market designed around the unencumbered worker. Why must equality require that everyone sell labor on terms set by employers, then scramble privately to meet care needs? Why not reduce working hours, provide universal services, and distribute care across society?

The American “time bind,” described by Arlie Hochschild, is not a private scheduling mishap. It is a political failure. Parents are expected to work long hours or unpredictable shifts. Schools close before workdays end. Childcare is expensive. Health insurance is tied to employment. Paid leave is limited or nonexistent for many workers. Low-wage workers may be punished for tending to a sick child. Professional workers may be rewarded for total availability. Under these conditions, families search for private solutions: a mother leaves work, a grandmother fills the gap, an immigrant nanny is hired, children are left in questionable care, or everyone becomes exhausted. Then commentators debate whether mothers have chosen correctly.

Hirschmann is right that employer “family-friendly” policies can leave gender unchanged. But that is because they are partial accommodations within an economy still governed by profit, competition, and masculine career norms. A firm may offer flexibility but penalize those who use it. A university may provide parental leave but reward the father who writes an article during it. Real reform requires enforceable social rights, not discretionary perks: paid family leave, public childcare, universal health care, living wages, predictable schedules, paid sick days, and pensions that recognize caregiving. These policies would not solve everything, but they would shift care from private sacrifice to collective responsibility.

The fear that public support for care invites public surveillance is serious. Poor mothers know this better than anyone. Welfare reform in 1996 transformed assistance into discipline, demanding wage work even when jobs were unstable and childcare inadequate. But the lesson is not to avoid public provision; it is to fight for universal, non-stigmatizing provision. Public schools are not perfect, yet their universality gives them a legitimacy welfare lacks. Social Security has been politically durable partly because it is broad. Programs for “poor mothers” are vulnerable to racism and moralism; programs for all children and caregivers are harder to demonize.

A socialist feminist politics of care would start from dependency as universal. Every person is dependent at some points; many are dependent throughout life; all are interdependent always. The fiction of the independent male worker has been sustained by women’s unpaid labor and by the underpaid labor of poorer women, often women of color and immigrants. When professional women “lean in” by hiring domestic workers, they may gain individual power while leaving the hierarchy of care intact. That is not liberation; it is redistribution of drudgery downward.

Hirschmann’s critique of privileged mothers is therefore best understood as a critique of class reproduction. Affluent parents use family resources to secure advantage for their children. They call it love. They lobby schools, purchase enrichment, excuse misconduct, and transform childhood into résumé production. This is not care in the democratic sense; it is accumulation by familial means. But again, the pressure comes from inequality. When the penalties for falling are severe, parents hoard opportunity. A more equal society would make it easier for parents to raise children as citizens rather than competitors.

What about men? Hirschmann says women should insist that men do more, and she is right. But bargaining inside heterosexual couples is shaped by wages, work hours, unemployment risk, childcare costs, and cultural expectations. If his job pays more and offers health insurance, her demand that he cut back may threaten the family’s security. If childcare costs nearly equal her wages, his claim that her job is irrational has economic force even if the economy itself is gendered. Men should be challenged morally, but moral challenge without structural change leaves many women negotiating with weak cards.

A collective politics would strengthen those cards. Comparable worth efforts, stronger unions, higher minimum wages, universal childcare, and health care independent of employment would reduce the economic logic that pushes women home. Nontransferable paid leave for fathers would help establish male care as normal. Shorter workweeks would make it possible for all adults to combine paid work and care. Public investment in eldercare and disability services would reduce the hidden burdens that fall on daughters and wives. None of this requires idealizing the state; it requires democratizing it.

Care feminism should not retreat. It should become more materialist. Instead of saying “care is valuable” and asking for recognition, it should ask who controls the conditions under which care is performed. Who has time? Who has money? Who has immigration status? Who has benefits? Who is watched? Who is praised? Who is exhausted? Who profits when care is unpaid? Who profits when it is underpaid?

Hirschmann concludes that women’s power, not care itself, will enable equality. But power for what? If women gain access to the same overwork, competitiveness, and disregard for dependency that men have long enjoyed, equality will be shallow. The goal is not for women to become equally free of care. It is for no one to be free of responsibility and no one to be trapped by it. Care must become a principle for reorganizing work, welfare, citizenship, and time.

Mothers do not care too much. Society cares too little, then blames mothers for the distortions that follow. Some mothers respond by smothering their children; some by sacrificing themselves; some by policing poorer women; some by buying other women’s labor. These are symptoms of a privatized care regime. Feminism should neither romanticize them nor scold them in isolation. It should build a politics in which raising children, tending bodies, and sustaining relationships are no longer treated as women’s private problem but as the common work of a decent society.
