Diversified Gemini

"The Democratic Party's failure to mobilize the working class is rooted in profound cultural alienation rather than a lack of anti-corruption messaging, requiring the party to make genuine concessions on cultural issues to win back voters."

The autopsy of the 2024 Democratic defeat offered by Bonica and Grumbach correctly diagnoses the failure of triangulation, but it completely misidentifies the barrier to working-class mobilization. The authors argue that Democrats can bypass polarization by framing the struggle around "anti-corruption," believing that this will unite disaffected rural conservatives and urban progressives. This fundamentally misunderstands the nature of modern cultural polarization.

The divide between the Democratic Party and the working-class (particularly in rural and exurban America) is not rooted in a belief that Democrats are insufficiently opposed to corruption. It is rooted in profound cultural alienation. Over the last decade, the Democratic Party has become the cultural home of the highly educated, urban professional class. The language the party uses, the cultural signifiers it adopts, and the implicit moral judgments it makes are profoundly off-putting to non-college voters.

When a progressive candidate talks about "un-rigging the system," the rural working-class voter does not hear a promise to rein in Wall Street. They hear the condescension of a professional class that wants to regulate their energy jobs out of existence, mandate their cultural values, and tax their livelihoods. The authors claim we need to recruit "authentic, working-class candidates," but they fail to realize that true working-class candidates often hold cultural views that the progressive base of the Democratic Party finds unacceptable.

You cannot bridge the cultural divide with economic or procedural populism alone. To win back the regions that have swung wildly to the populist right, Democrats must be willing to make real concessions on cultural issues. They must tolerate candidates who are pro-gun, who favor strict border enforcement, and who actively reject the avant-garde cultural theories of the academic left. Bonica and Grumbach want the aesthetic of working-class authenticity without the ideological discomfort it brings to the progressive coalition. That is a fantasy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The divide between Democrats and non-college voters is driven by the party's identity as the home of the urban professional class and its associated cultural signifiers.
  2. 02Economic or procedural populism, such as anti-corruption framing, is insufficient to overcome the perception of professional-class condescension toward rural livelihoods and values.
  3. 03Authentic working-class candidates often hold cultural views—such as being pro-gun or favoring strict border enforcement—that the current progressive base finds unacceptable.
  4. 04To be electorally viable in rural and exurban areas, the Democratic Party must tolerate ideological diversity and reject the cultural theories of the academic left.
