"While referendums can provide democratic legitimacy, they should not be used to outsource leadership on vital national issues unless they are governed by strict rules that ensure informed judgment rather than momentary rupture."

Referendums can give democratic legitimacy to decisions that ordinary politics cannot settle, but they are a poor substitute for governing. The Colombian vote shows the danger: a peace agreement painstakingly negotiated over years was rejected by a tiny margin with low turnout, leaving the future of war and reconciliation hostage to weather, turnout machines and protest votes. Britain’s vote on the European Union similarly compressed questions of trade, sovereignty, migration and constitutional identity into a single yes-or-no choice, after a campaign in which claims were often emotional, speculative or misleading.

That does not mean “the people” should be distrusted. It means popular consent must be treated as more than a one-day tally. Representative democracy exists because many national decisions require expertise, bargaining, compromise and accountability over time. Legislators can amend, revisit and be removed for the consequences of their choices; a referendum result is blunt, often irreversible and hard to interpret beyond the slogan printed on the ballot.

If governments use referendums on vital issues, they should set demanding rules in advance: clear questions, neutral public information, adequate deliberation time, campaign transparency and perhaps turnout or supermajority thresholds for constitutional or irreversible changes. Referendums should ratify broad settlements, not outsource leadership. The people’s voice deserves respect, but it is most trustworthy when institutions help it become informed judgment rather than momentary rupture.

"Governments should not use referendums for vital national decisions because they represent an abdication of representative duty that replaces nuanced deliberation with dangerous, binary gambles."

The lurch toward government by referendum is less a triumph of direct democracy than a dangerous abdication of representative duty. The stunning rejection of the Colombian peace accord and Britain’s vote to leave the European Union share a common thread: both asked citizens to ratify enormously complex, multidimensional pacts with a blunt binary question, stripping away the nuance that legislative negotiation and public deliberation are meant to provide. When a nation’s most vital interests are at stake, plebiscites tend to magnify passion at the expense of prudence.

A referendum transforms a living, amendable set of compromises into an all-or-nothing gamble. The Colombian deal, painstakingly assembled over four years to end a half-century of bloodshed, was reduced to a single “yes” or “no” that left no room for the kind of iterative improvement that parliamentarians might have achieved. Similarly, the Brexit campaign revealed how easily a complex web of trade, migration and regulatory ties could be condensed into emotionally charged slogans about sovereignty and £350 million a week for the National Health Service — a figure later disowned by its proponents. In both cases, vital truths were buried under a flood of misinformation and tribal loyalties, and once the votes were tallied, the damage was instantaneous and likely irreversible.

Low turnout further erodes the moral authority of a referendum result. In Colombia, just 40 percent of eligible voters participated, meaning a minority of a minority imposed a decision that will shape the nation’s future for generations. That is not the voice of the people; it is the voice of the most motivated, often angriest, slice of the electorate. Worse, referendums are frequently hijacked by unrelated grievances. The Brexit ballot became a protest against austerity, immigration and a perceived London elite, while the Colombian “no” was driven partly by resentment of the government rather than a sober assessment of the accord’s terms.

The architects of modern constitutional democracy understood that cooling mechanisms — separated powers, bicameralism, judicial review — were necessary to refine and enlarge public views. Referendums bypass those filters, handing immense power to prime ministers or presidents who call them for tactical advantage. David Cameron offered a referendum not because EU membership was a burning constitutional question but to manage internal party divisions, and it backfired catastrophically. That is not responsible governance; it is gambling with a nation’s destiny.

Some will protest that the people are wise and that elites have no right to override their verdict. Yet democratic legitimacy depends not merely on counting heads but on processes that encourage informed deliberation, protect minority rights and allow course corrections. A vote cast in a fog of deceptive advertising, with no mechanism to adjust the outcome afterward, is a poor substitute for the slow, frustrating work of representative institutions. When vital issues are at stake, leaders should lead — by debating, amending and legislating — not by tossing a fuse-laden question to an unprepared public and hoping for the best.

Referendums occupy an uneasy place in democratic theory. They promise unmediated legitimacy — what could be more democratic than letting citizens decide? — yet they also strip complex questions of the deliberation, amendment and compromise that representative institutions are designed to provide. The Colombian and British votes illustrate the dangers. Voters were asked to render binary verdicts on documents (a 297-page peace accord, a treaty relationship four decades in the making) that few could have read, much less weighed against alternatives. Turnout in Colombia barely cleared the legal threshold; Brexit passed by under four points. In both cases, a thin and contingent majority produced an irreversible-feeling result.

That does not mean referendums should be abolished. They are appropriate where the question is genuinely constitutional — secession, sovereignty pooling, fundamental rights — and where the choice is concrete rather than aspirational. The Scottish independence referendum of 2014 worked reasonably well because the alternatives were defined and the campaign was long.

The rule of thumb should be: use referendums to ratify, not to design. Require supermajorities or turnout thresholds for consequential changes. And never use them, as David Cameron did, to manage a party-political problem.

"Governments should only use referendums for clear, limited questions with an informed and engaged electorate, while leaving complex policy dilemmas to representative institutions."

Referendums give voters a direct say on pivotal issues, but their legitimacy hinges on two prerequisites: an informed, engaged electorate and a clear, binary choice. When both are present, the outcome can reinforce democratic legitimacy, as happened in the 1991 Colombian constitutional referendum. In contrast, the 2016 Colombian peace accord vote and the United Kingdom’s Brexit referendum illustrate the risks of disregarding those conditions. In each case, the question was complex, the electorate was divided, and turnout was modest—about 40 % in Colombia and roughly 72 % in the UK. Low participation erodes the claim that the result reflects the will of the whole people, and complex trade‑offs are poorly captured by a simple yes or no.

Governments should therefore reserve referendums for clear, limited questions—such as a territorial status change—where the stakes are transparent, the campaign provides balanced information, and mechanisms exist to protect minority rights if the majority chooses narrowly. For intricate policy dilemmas that require balancing competing interests, representative institutions are better suited to deliberate and implement nuanced solutions. In short, the people’s voice should be trusted when it is both well‑informed and sufficiently broad; otherwise, the risk of a costly, irreversible mistake outweighs the democratic payoff.

The recent shocks in Colombia and the United Kingdom have laid bare a fundamental flaw in the modern political reliance on direct democracy. Representative democracy was designed precisely to filter the public will through institutional deliberation, negotiation, and expertise. When leaders bypass these democratic structures to ask the public a binary question on a vastly complex issue, they are not acting heroically; they are often abdicating their responsibilities, with destabilizing results.

The primary problem with referendums is the problem of complexity. A 297-page peace accord detailing transitional justice, political integration, and rural reform cannot realistically be reduced to a simple "Yes" or "No." Similarly, unwinding decades of economic, legal, and political integration with the European Union is a monumental task that resists binary reduction. Voters rarely have the time or specialized knowledge to evaluate the fine print. Consequently, complex policy decisions become vulnerable to emotional appeals, demagoguery, and outright misinformation campaigns.

David Cameron gambled the British economy to settle an internal Conservative Party dispute and misjudged the depth of working-class frustration. In Colombia, Juan Manuel Santos submitted a carefully negotiated end to a half-century of bloodshed to a popular vote to secure a mandate, only to be ambushed by 40 percent turnout and an opposition that successfully framed the complex compromise as a surrender. In both cases, the blunt instrument of direct democracy failed the broader national interest.

While referendums feel inherently pure, utilizing them for vital, highly technical national decisions is a dereliction of legislative duty. We elect representatives to study intricate issues, negotiate painful compromises, and make the hard choices—and we hold them accountable in regular elections. Abdicating the most consequential decisions to a single, highly volatile day of voting leaves nations at the mercy of the prevailing political winds. Parliaments and congresses should do the jobs they were elected to do, reserving the referendum solely for the clearest constitutional questions.

Referendums are the wrong tool for the wrong job, and the past few months have made the case more forcefully than any political scientist could. This is precisely why representative democracy exists. We elect legislators not because they are wiser than us in some aristocratic sense, but because the division of labor in a complex society demands that some people spend their days reading 300-page treaties while the rest of us go to work. A trade deal, a peace settlement, a constitutional reform — these are intricate packages whose clauses interact. Reducing them to a ballot question forces voters to express a verdict on the whole when they may object only to a part, or to vote on the basis of mood, protest, or the latest tabloid headline.

There is also the turnout problem. Colombia's referendum drew only about 40 percent of eligible voters, meaning a transformational decision about war and peace was made by roughly a fifth of the electorate. Low turnout is the norm in plebiscites that lack the personality-driven drama of a general election, and it systematically advantages whichever side is angrier or better organized — rarely the side of cautious compromise.

None of this is to say the people's voice should be ignored. It speaks loudly through general elections, where parties offer platforms, leaders are held accountable, and citizens weigh bundles of issues against each other. That process is messy, but it builds in the deliberation, expertise, and revisability that referendums strip away. When David Cameron called the Brexit vote, he treated a constitutional question as a tool to manage his party's internal politics; Colombia's President Santos staked a national settlement on a single afternoon's mood. Both gambles were reckless precisely because referendums make reckless gambling easy. Vital decisions deserve institutions that slow us down, not ones that speed us up.

"Referendums should only be used to decide vital national issues if they are carefully designed as part of a broader deliberative process rather than treated as a simple instrument of pure democracy."

The right question isn't whether referendums should decide vital national issues, but under what conditions they can do so legitimately. Treated as a magic instrument of pure democracy, they fail; treated as one carefully designed step in a broader deliberative process, they can succeed.

Three design features separate the good referendums from the bad. First, the threshold should match the stakes. Constitutional changes in many countries require supermajorities, or majorities in multiple regions, or a minimum turnout. Brexit was decided by 52 percent on a single afternoon, with no requirement that the winning side present an actual plan for implementation. Colombia's peace deal failed on a turnout of 40 percent. Decisions that bind future generations should require more than a transient plurality of the motivated.

Second, the question must be answerable. "Should the United Kingdom remain a member of the European Union or leave the European Union?" sounds clear but conceals dozens of distinct trade-offs: the single market, the customs union, freedom of movement, the Irish border, financial passporting. A well-designed referendum either ratifies a specific, drafted agreement — as the Good Friday Agreement was ratified in 1998 — or chooses among concrete alternatives. It does not delegate the hard part to politicians after the fact.

Third, the campaign environment must allow real deliberation. The Brexit campaign featured industrial-scale misinformation from both sides, and Britain's libel-friendly press has no equivalent of an FEC or a fact-checking body with teeth. Ireland's Citizens' Assembly model — convening randomly selected citizens to study an issue for months before it goes to a public vote — is a useful complement, marrying mass democracy with informed deliberation.

By these criteria, the Colombian and Brexit referendums look less like indictments of direct democracy than indictments of how leaders chose to deploy it. President Santos staked a generational settlement on a low-turnout up-or-down vote. David Cameron called a referendum to settle a quarrel inside the Conservative Party, with no required plan for the morning after. Both used the people's voice as a tactical instrument, then were surprised when it spoke back.

The lesson is not that voters cannot be trusted with vital decisions. It is that the architecture surrounding such decisions — thresholds, question design, deliberative infrastructure — matters as much as the vote itself. Build the architecture badly, and even the right answer arrives by accident. Build it well, and the people's voice becomes something more than a coin flip.

"Governments should continue to use referendums for vital national decisions, but only if they implement structural safeguards like turnout thresholds and supermajority requirements to ensure outcomes reflect a stable and broad public mandate."

The experience of 2016—Brexit’s 52–48 shock and the Colombian peace referendum’s astonishing defeat on 40 percent turnout—has exposed a deep flaw not in the principle of direct democracy, but in how we practice it. Referendums are not inherently disastrous; when well designed, they can legitimize constitutional choices and settle great national questions. But the crude all-or-nothing votes we have just seen show that without proper safeguards, referendums become loaded dice for demagogues and minority rule. The solution is not to abandon the people’s voice, but to discipline it.

Consider the glaring structural failings. In Colombia, a pivotal decision about ending half a century of bloodshed was decided by a simple majority with no turnout threshold, meaning a mobilized “No” campaign could effectively veto the process with just over 50 percent of only 40 percent of eligible voters. In Britain, a once-in-a-generation constitutional rupture was triggered by a simple majority on a 72 percent turnout—a figure that looks respectable until you realize that 37 percent of the electorate permanently determined the country’s trajectory for the other 63. In both cases, fleeting campaign dynamics and accidental turnouts produced outcomes that would be unthinkable under even basic democratic guardrails.

The remedy is straightforward and practiced successfully in other democracies. A binding referendum on fundamental national questions should require both a minimum turnout—say, 50 percent—and a supermajority of at least 60 percent for change. This would prevent narrow, transient majorities from imposing irreversible transformations, while still allowing clear public mandates to prevail. Had such thresholds existed, Brexit would have failed 52–48, and in Colombia the quorum rule would have voided the result entirely, pushing negotiators back to the table without sidelining the public’s concerns. Advisory referendums, meanwhile, could remain unweighted, providing a democratic temperature check while leaving final decisions to parliaments where compromise is possible.

The Swiss have long understood this. Their frequent referendums work not because the people are infallible, but because the system is built to filter out extremism: multiple votes, supermajorities for constitutional amendments, and ample time for information to sink in. The Anglo-Colombian approach of springing an unstructured binary choice on an unprepared public is a recipe for buyer’s remorse and political chaos. We should not give up on the people’s voice—we should finally start taking it seriously enough to build institutions that channel it responsibly.
