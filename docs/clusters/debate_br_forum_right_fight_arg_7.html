"The integration of local police into a national domestic-intelligence network risks normalizing a permanent security apparatus that distorts democratic governance and treats ordinary citizens as data points."

Daniel Richman asks how we can build an effective domestic intelligence network that is also democratically accountable. That question is sensible on its own terms, but it accepts too much of the post-September 11 consensus. Before deciding how local police should participate in the “War on Terror,” we should ask whether the war frame has already distorted domestic governance. The danger is that by making local police partners in national intelligence, we will normalize a permanent security apparatus whose targets expand far beyond terrorism.

The lead essay is careful, far more careful than most official statements. Richman rejects crude federal commands, criticizes immigration enforcement, and sees community trust as essential. Yet the structure he proposes still moves in one direction: more intelligence, more information flows, more local participation, more recognition that “every felony arrest is an intelligence opportunity.” That phrase reveals the problem. Once ordinary policing becomes a tributary of national security, the boundaries of criminal justice shift. The person arrested for a minor offense becomes a source. The neighborhood dispute becomes a data point. The mosque meeting becomes context. Political anger becomes something to be assessed.

The United States has been here before. During World War I, the American Protective League converted patriotic zeal into surveillance and harassment. During the Red Scares, dissent was treated as subversion. During the civil rights and Vietnam eras, federal and local agencies spied on activists, infiltrated organizations, and disrupted lawful politics. Each episode had its own justification. Each was defended as necessary to protect the nation. Each later looked shameful. The lesson is not merely that we need better intergovernmental design. The lesson is that fear repeatedly produces overbroad definitions of threat.

Since September 11, the category of terrorism has already shown its elasticity. Immigration violators have been detained under suspicion without evidence of involvement in terrorism. Charities have been shut down or stigmatized. Protesters have found themselves corralled into “free speech zones” at political events. The Justice Department has sought expanded surveillance powers. The government has asserted extraordinary detention authority. Overseas, the Abu Ghraib revelations have shown what can happen when officials convince themselves that emergency requires exceptional methods. Domestic policing is not the same as military detention in Iraq, but the same moral hazard exists: when the mission is defined as preventing catastrophe, almost any intrusion can be redescribed as prudence.

Richman argues that local police may moderate federal zeal because they need community trust. Sometimes they will. But local police departments are also institutions of coercion with their own histories of racial control. For many black, Latino, immigrant, and poor communities, local policing has never been a gentle, accountable enterprise. The language of community policing can mask aggressive street control. Gang databases, drug sweeps, stop-and-frisk tactics, and quality-of-life enforcement have often fallen disproportionately on marginalized groups. Adding counterterrorism to this mix does not create accountability; it gives local coercion a national-security halo.

Nor should we assume that local political accountability protects dissenters. Local majorities can be frightened, punitive, and indifferent to minorities. In some places, opposition to federal counterterrorism policy has produced civil-liberties resolutions. In others, politicians may compete to appear tougher than Washington. A Muslim community without electoral power may find local police more intrusive, not less. Federalism is a structure, not a virtue.

The premise that we need a “national domestic-intelligence network” deserves more scrutiny. Of course the government must investigate credible threats of violence. Of course agencies should share specific information about suspects, plots, weapons, financing, and travel. But an intelligence network is not merely a set of telephone numbers. It becomes an institutional appetite. It requires databases, analysts, liaison officers, reporting protocols, and sources. It seeks patterns. It rewards collection. It tends to blur the difference between what is dangerous and what is merely different.

The “connecting the dots” metaphor has encouraged this appetite. If only we had collected and shared more before September 11, the argument goes, we might have stopped the attacks. Perhaps. But dots are not self-identifying. A society of nearly 300 million people produces infinite dots: foreign travel, flight training, religious conversion, political speeches, money transfers, immigration irregularities, library use, chemical purchases, internet searches, odd behavior. Collecting more can make analysis harder. It can also draw innocent people into suspicion. The problem before September 11 was not simply too little surveillance. It was institutional failure, misinterpretation, and perhaps imagination. More domestic intelligence is not automatically the cure.

A better approach would narrow the counterterrorism mission rather than embed it everywhere. Terrorism should be treated primarily as serious crime: conspiracy, murder, bombing, material support for violence, financing of armed groups, weapons offenses. Investigations should require individualized suspicion. Intelligence about foreign organizations should be handled by federal agencies under strict rules. Local police should receive alerts and report specific threats, but they should not be encouraged to map communities, monitor ideology, or treat ordinary arrests as intelligence opportunities.

We should also invest in non-police forms of safety. Communities are more likely to resist violent extremism when they are politically included, economically stable, and free to argue openly. Foreign policy matters too, though domestic officials often prefer not to say so. Anger over wars, occupations, dictatorships, and civilian casualties can be exploited by extremists; suppressing discussion of that anger will not make it disappear. The answer to radical ideas is often more speech, more political participation, and more credible justice, not more surveillance.

Local governments that oppose federal policies should not be dismissed as passing “empty resolutions.” Symbolic politics can matter. When a city declares that it values civil liberties, immigrant residents hear something important. Of course resolutions should be matched by policy: limits on intelligence gathering, refusal to enforce civil immigration law, transparency about task-force participation, and protection for protest. But public dissent from national policy is not a distraction. It is part of democratic accountability.

Richman is right that some civil-liberties debates focus too much on particular Patriot Act provisions while missing older powers such as grand-jury subpoenas. But the conclusion should be broader skepticism toward surveillance powers, not acceptance that institutional structure will do most of the work. Administrative subpoenas, grand-jury subpoenas, national-security letters, material-witness warrants, immigration detention, undercover operations, and informants all require tighter limits. The more tools government has, the more important it is not to create a culture in which use of those tools is presumed patriotic.

The country faces real risks. The Madrid bombings were horrific. Al Qaeda and similar groups have declared their desire to kill civilians. No responsible critic can simply say “do nothing.” But the choice is not between passivity and a domestic intelligence web. We can strengthen port security, aviation security, emergency response, public health, financial investigations tied to evidence, international police cooperation, and targeted criminal investigation. We can improve translation and analysis within federal agencies. We can fix watchlists and visa systems without making local police into intelligence collectors. We can protect vulnerable sites without treating vulnerable communities as suspect.

The central question is what kind of society we are trying to defend. If the answer is a constitutional democracy with space for dissent, privacy, religious life, and immigrant belonging, then the means of defense must reflect that answer. A permanent, locally embedded intelligence network may promise security, but it risks changing the ordinary relationship between government and governed. The police officer becomes not only a protector and law enforcer but an intelligence sensor. The resident becomes not only a citizen or neighbor but a possible data source. That transformation should not be accepted as inevitable.

Richman’s essay is an important warning against federal arrogance. But we need an equally strong warning against security creep. The right fight is not how to make domestic intelligence more locally accountable. The right fight is how to prevent domestic intelligence from becoming the organizing logic of local policing. Target violence. Investigate crimes. Share specific threat information. But do not build a national habit of watching communities and call it trust.
