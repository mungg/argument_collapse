Diversified Claude

"While Daniel Richman correctly identifies the necessity of local police in domestic intelligence, the current federal-local partnership is operationally flawed due to one-way information sharing, secretive task force structures, and the erosion of community trust."

I served for many years as a senior officer in a large municipal police department, and I want to respond to Daniel Richman's essay from inside the institutions he describes. Richman gets a great deal right. He is correct that the federal government cannot run a domestic intelligence network without local police, correct that the costs of post-9/11 federal initiatives fall disproportionately on cities, and correct that immigration enforcement work by local officers wrecks the community relationships on which effective policing depends. I want to extend his account with some operational realism.

First, on information flow. The single most important fact about the post-9/11 intelligence relationship between the FBI and local police departments is that information flows one way: up. Local officers feed information into JTTFs, which feed it into Bureau headquarters, which feed it into the broader intelligence community. What comes back to the local department is, in most cases, nothing. JTTF detectives are double-hatted federal task force officers, sworn to federal secrecy, and the relevant Bureau procedures prohibit them from sharing case information with their home departments without specific authorization. The promise of "two-way information sharing" that Richman invokes is, as a practical matter, mostly aspirational. Until the federal government solves this — by establishing classified communications down to the precinct level, by pre-clearing more municipal officials, by changing Bureau culture about local sharing — the partnership Richman describes will remain lopsided.

Second, on the JTTF model. Richman is generally positive about JTTFs as "good vehicles for operational coordination." From where I sit, JTTFs have significant pathologies that he understates. The detectives we assign to JTTFs disappear from our own intelligence picture. We learn, sometimes from the newspaper, that "our" officers participated in raids in our city that we knew nothing about. Local chiefs are placed in the position of having to defend operations in their cities that they were not consulted on and cannot describe. When something goes wrong — a wrongful arrest, a community uproar — the JTTF withdraws into federal secrecy and the local department is left holding the bag. This is not a basis for sustainable cooperation. The price of municipal participation in JTTFs has to include real consultation and the right for chiefs to know, at a minimum, what is being done in their cities in their names.

Third, on community policing. Richman is too kind to the community-policing model as a guarantor of restraint. Community policing in many departments is, as he half-acknowledges, more of a grant-writing posture than an operational reality. Where it is real, it is fragile, and it is the first thing to break under counterterrorism pressure. The same officer who has spent three years building trust at the local mosque cannot also be the officer who shows up to take down a "material witness" in the middle of Ramadan. We have already seen this. The Dearborn quote Richman cites — "people would not work with us" — is not hypothetical. It is happening. We are watching years of community-policing investment erode in real time because federal counterterrorism tactics treat that investment as a free good.

Fourth, on intelligence units. Richman discusses past abuses by police intelligence bureaus — Denver, NYPD, others — and assumes that consent decrees and modern professional standards will prevent recurrence. I am less confident. Many departments dismantled their political intelligence units in the 1970s and 1980s after litigation, and have spent the past two decades operating without them. We are now standing them back up, often hastily, often without clear policy frameworks, often staffed by officers with no intelligence-analysis training. The NYPD has built out an intelligence and counterterrorism capability that exceeds anything most federal agencies field; smaller departments are attempting similar things at smaller scale with far less professionalism. The risk of locally generated abuses — bad targeting, sloppy informant handling, retaliatory uses of intelligence files — is real and growing.

Fifth, on the funding politics. Richman is right that the cities have legitimate grievances about both the level and distribution of homeland-security dollars. But the operational truth is messier than the policy debate. A great deal of homeland-security funding has gone to equipment we did not particularly need — armored vehicles, decontamination suits, regional intelligence centers — rather than to the people-intensive work that actually generates leads. The administrative requirements attached to federal grants are byzantine and require staff we do not have. And the fact that funds cannot be used for personnel costs means that, in real terms, much of the money cannot be converted into the kind of street-level intelligence work that counterterrorism actually requires. The right reform is not just more money to the cities, but more flexibility in what the money can be spent on.

Sixth, on what we should actually do. Richman's policy bottom line — that the federal government should resist using local police as immigration enforcers, should fund cities directly, should give them more voice in policy — is sensible as far as it goes. I would add three things. One, formalize the role of local civilian oversight in JTTF operations through agreed protocols on city-side review and notification, with classified annexes if necessary. Two, develop a national professional standard for police intelligence units, including training, retention rules, audit requirements, and minimization standards comparable to what federal agencies must follow. Three, build a clear legal regime around the use of immigration tools by joint federal-local teams, including bright-line prohibitions on coercive recruitment of informants through threats of removal.

The deeper insight in Richman's essay is that effectiveness and accountability are not in opposition in counterterrorism work. They are mutually reinforcing. An intelligence system that burns its own information sources by alienating the communities it depends on is not just unjust — it is operationally bad. The chiefs I know understand this in their bones. The challenge is that the federal counterterrorism enterprise, three years after 9/11, still does not seem to understand it. Until it does, the partnership Richman wants to build will remain an uneasy and incomplete one, and the costs of that incompleteness will fall, as he rightly notes, on cities and on the immigrant communities within them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The promise of two-way information sharing is currently aspirational because federal secrecy protocols prevent Joint Terrorist Task Force (JTTF) officers from sharing intelligence with their home departments.
  2. 02The JTTF model creates institutional friction by excluding local police chiefs from operations conducted in their own jurisdictions, leaving them to face community backlash for actions they did not authorize.
  3. 03Federal counterterrorism tactics, particularly the use of local police for immigration enforcement, are actively destroying the fragile community-policing relationships required to generate intelligence leads.
  4. 04The rapid re-establishment of local police intelligence units lacks the professional standards and policy frameworks necessary to prevent a recurrence of past civil rights abuses.
  5. 05Federal homeland security funding is often wasted on unnecessary equipment because grant restrictions prevent cities from spending money on the personnel-intensive work essential for intelligence gathering.
  6. 06To create a sustainable network, the federal government must formalize local civilian oversight of task forces, establish national standards for police intelligence units, and prohibit the coercive use of immigration threats to recruit informants.
