"The behavioral economics approach to development, while empirically robust in its small-scale results, is a paternalistic framework that pathologizes the poor and ignores the structural and political causes of global poverty."

In "Small Changes, Big Results," Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer advocate for the use of randomized evaluations and behavioral economics to address global poverty. Their argument is elegant and seemingly empirical: small barriers prevent the poor from making rational investments in their future, and equally small interventions—like removing a tiny fee or offering a bag of lentils—can trigger massive improvements in health and education. While the data they present is robust within its own parameters, their overarching framework is steeped in a paternalism that strips the poor of their political agency. By framing poverty as a problem of cognitive bias and poor decision-making, the behavioral economics revolution dangerously legitimizes an unequal power dynamic between Western experts and the global South.

The fundamental premise of the behavioral intervention is that the "expert" knows what is best for the "subject." The researchers—usually sitting in well-funded universities in the Global North—determine the optimal behavior: children should be in school, water should be chlorinated, vaccines should be administered. When the poor fail to act according to these externally imposed priorities, the researchers diagnose them with "procrastination" or an inability to "make small upfront investments." The poor are thus reduced to irrational actors needing to be nudged, bribed, or tricked into doing what is good for them.

This framing is profoundly ahistorical and apolitical. It completely ignores the highly rational reasons why marginalized communities might resist or ignore public health or educational interventions. When rural populations avoid a government clinic, is it truly because they are procrastinating, or is it because decades of marginalization, ethnic discrimination, and systemic abuse have taught them to distrust state institutions? When families do not prioritize sending a girl to a school that teaches a curriculum entirely disconnected from her daily survival, is that an irrational cognitive failure, or a deeply rational assessment of the school's actual utility? By reducing complex social behavior to universal psychological quirks, economists erase the lived political reality of the poor.

Furthermore, the very nature of the randomized controlled trial turns the developing world into a vast, unconsenting laboratory. In the medical trials that RCT advocates so frequently cite, there are strict ethical protocols regarding informed consent, equipoise, and the right of patients to understand the experiment they are participating in. In the social science RCTs deployed in development, entire villages or districts are frequently subjected to manipulated incentive structures—cash transfers withheld from one group to serve as a control, or subsidies dynamically altered to test price sensitivity—often without the subjects’ full knowledge of the overarching experiment. The poor become mere data points for academic publications, their communities engineered by outside actors who bear no long-term political accountability for the consequences of their interventions.

Consider the much-touted lentil incentive for immunization in Rajasthan. Glennerster and Kremer present this as a brilliant triumph of behavioral design. But take a step back and examine the power dynamic: wealthy foreign donors and researchers are using food rations to compel impoverished mothers to subject their children to medical procedures. While the goal—immunization—is objectively beneficial, the method relies on leveraging the sheer desperation of the poor. A wealthy American might need an automatic enrollment "nudge" to save for a 401(k), but they do not need to be bribed with basic sustenance to vaccinate their children. They have access to accessible, trusted pediatricians. Leveraging hunger to achieve a public health metric is not a sustainable model for human dignity; it is an exercise in coercion masked as a "nudge."

This focus on the micro-behavior of the poor also serves a very convenient ideological function for the global elite. If the reason children are dying of diarrhea is because their parents fail to properly use a chlorine dispenser, then the burden of poverty rests on the individual choices of the poor. This narrative completely shifts the focus away from the macro-structural injustices that produce poverty in the first place. It asks nothing of the global economic order. It demands no changes to unfair trade tariffs, no debt forgiveness, no halt to corporate tax evasion, and no massive transfer of wealth to build permanent, modern infrastructure like piped water systems. It allows the international community to feel it is "solving poverty" for two dollars a case, without ever having to surrender power or privilege.

Glennerster and Kremer ask, "How can we feel so different and be so much alike?" implying that because Americans and Kenyans both procrastinate, we are all part of the same universal human family. But this sentimental conclusion obscures the massive chasm in power. We may all suffer from the same cognitive biases, but when a Western economist makes a bad decision, they lose out on some retirement interest. When a Kenyan farmer makes a bad decision, their child dies. True development is not about designing clever psychological traps to make the poor behave "better." It is about empowering them with the same structural resources, democratic rights, and economic security that allow the wealthy to survive their own irrationalities. Until we address the imbalance of power, behavioral economics will remain a sophisticated tool for managing the poor, rather than liberating them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'nudge' framework reduces the poor to irrational actors who must be manipulated by Western experts, stripping them of their political agency.
  2. 02Behavioral interventions ignore the historical and political reasons, such as systemic discrimination or institutional distrust, that may lead marginalized communities to resist certain programs.
  3. 03The use of randomized controlled trials in developing nations often turns vulnerable communities into unconsenting laboratories for academic research without long-term accountability.
  4. 04Using basic necessities like food as incentives for health behaviors is a form of coercion that leverages desperation rather than upholding human dignity.
  5. 05Focusing on the micro-behaviors of individuals conveniently shifts the burden of poverty onto the poor themselves, absolving the global elite of the need to address macro-structural injustices like unfair trade and debt.
  6. 06True development should focus on providing the poor with the same structural resources and democratic rights as the wealthy rather than designing psychological traps to manage their behavior.
