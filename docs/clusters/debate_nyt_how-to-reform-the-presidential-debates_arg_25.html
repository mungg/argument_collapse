Diversified Gemini

"To improve the substance of presidential debates, party committees should replace polling-based entry criteria with a requirement that candidates submit detailed, publicly vetted policy papers to earn their spot on stage."

The debate stage should be a privilege earned through rigorous policy work, not merely a reward for name recognition or a temporary bump in national polls. The current criteria for debate entry—relying entirely on polling averages—virtually guarantees a superficial debate because it incentivizes candidates to focus on outrage and stunts to grab headlines.

If the Republican and Democratic National Committees want substantive debates, they must change the price of admission.

To qualify for the debate stage, candidates should be required to submit comprehensive, detailed policy papers on key issues (e.g., a federal budget proposal, a healthcare plan, a foreign policy doctrine) to the party committee weeks in advance. These papers should be made public and reviewed by independent economic and policy analysts.

The debates themselves would then be structured around these submitted plans. Moderators would not ask vague questions about "fixing the economy"; they would ask specific questions about the math in Candidate X’s published tax plan.

If a candidate is unwilling or unable to produce a serious policy document, they have no business occupying space on a presidential debate stage. By tying debate access to actual policy generation, the parties would weed out the vanity campaigns and the reality-TV stars. It would force campaigns to hire policy experts early on and commit to a platform, elevating the entire discourse of the primary season.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Current polling-based entry requirements incentivize candidates to prioritize headline-grabbing stunts and outrage over serious policy discussion.
  2. 02Requiring comprehensive policy papers would allow moderators to shift from vague questioning to rigorous interrogation of a candidate's specific proposals and data.
  3. 03Tying debate access to policy generation would effectively filter out vanity candidates and reality-TV stars who lack serious governing platforms.
  4. 04A policy-based entry requirement would force campaigns to invest in policy expertise early in the primary season, elevating the overall quality of political discourse.
