"Veganism can only be good for everyone if we shift from viewing it as an individual moral choice to a public health imperative that requires systemic restructuring of the food system."

Reading Tara Parker-Pope's recent writing on this topic, I am struck by how the conversation consistently focuses on the individual — his or her discipline, means, medical history, and commitment level. This focus on individual capacity is not wrong, but it systematically avoids the structural forces that determine who even gets to face the question in the first place.

If we shift from the individual to the population level, a different picture emerges. In the United States in 2012, obesity rates have reached alarming levels, type 2 diabetes is approaching epidemic status, and diet-related chronic disease accounts for a massive and growing share of healthcare expenditure. These are not primarily problems of individual moral failure or lack of discipline. They are problems of a food system that makes calorically dense, nutritionally poor foods cheaper and more accessible than whole plant foods, and that positions animal products as the centerpiece of normal eating.

A genuinely useful question about veganism would ask not just "is it good for individuals?" but "how can a food system be restructured so that plant-based eating is the accessible, affordable, culturally normalized default rather than the province of the privileged and the committed?" This is a fundamentally different inquiry — one that acknowledges that the current conditions under which veganism "works" are conditions that most people do not have.

What would it take to make veganism genuinely good for everyone, or at least available to everyone as a viable option? It would require subsidizing whole plant foods, restructuring agricultural policy away from commodity crop overproduction, investing in food infrastructure in underserved communities, and funding the kind of nutritional education that is currently available only to those who can afford dietitians. None of this is utopian — several countries are already experimenting with variations on these approaches. But it requires us to stop treating veganism as an individual moral project and start treating it as a public health and justice imperative that demands systemic change.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current focus on individual discipline and medical history ignores the structural forces that determine who has access to plant-based diets.
  2. 02Widespread health crises like obesity and diabetes are products of a food system that prioritizes cheap, calorically dense animal products over whole plant foods.
  3. 03Making veganism a viable option for the general population requires significant policy changes, such as subsidizing plant foods and reinvesting in underserved food infrastructures.
  4. 04The current conditions that allow veganism to be successful are largely restricted to the privileged, making it an inaccessible choice for most people.
