Vanilla Claude

"While reclaiming the term 'populism' is a valid defensive move against elite criticism, the American left must prioritize rebuilding dense social and labor organizations over mere rhetorical mobilization to achieve lasting political power."

Adom Getachew is right to want to rescue something from the wreckage that the word "populism" has become in our political vocabulary. Read enough op-eds and you would think populism were a kind of weather event — a storm that occasionally lashes liberal democracies from outside, against which the only response is shelter. Jan-Werner Müller's influential definition, with its emphasis on the "anti-pluralist" claim to exclusive representation of the real people, has done much to entrench this view, as has the journalistic habit of using "populist" as a near-synonym for "authoritarian nationalist." The result, as Getachew and Michael Denning point out, is a kind of category error: democratic politics necessarily involves the constitution of a people, and the suggestion that one can do politics without ever invoking that "we" is itself a partisan position, usually one that benefits whoever already enjoys disproportionate institutional power. Reclaiming the concept is therefore worth attempting.

But I want to register two worries about the project as Getachew frames it, both of which I think the symposium and the broader left will need to face squarely in the months ahead. The first concerns the analytic burden the word "populism" is being asked to carry. The second concerns whether the structural conditions for a left populism in the United States actually exist, or whether the model on offer — the Chicago Teachers' Union, the Working Families Party, the Sanders insurgency — describes something both more promising and more limited than "populism" suggests.

Start with the conceptual problem. To say that every politician must address and constitute a people is true but thin. It does not yet tell us what distinguishes populism from, say, ordinary democratic mobilization, or from socialism, or from civic republicanism. Ernesto Laclau, the theorist most responsible for left populism's intellectual rehabilitation, located populism in a specific discursive operation: the construction of a chain of equivalences among unmet popular demands and their unification against a frontier — an oligarchy, an elite, a regime. By this measure populism is not merely the appeal to a people; it is a particular way of constructing antagonism. That is a real and useful concept. But it also means populism is essentially formal: it tells us how to mobilize, not what for. Hugo Chávez and Donald Trump both fit Laclau's schema. So did Father Coughlin and Huey Long; so did the Tea Party and Occupy. If the term picks out a strategy rather than a content, then "reclaiming" populism amounts mostly to insisting that left projects, too, may legitimately use this strategy. That is a defensive move against a smear, not yet a positive program.

The trouble is that the smear has bite for reasons that go beyond elite squeamishness. The original American Populists, whose iconography the contemporary left periodically reaches for, are a genuinely instructive case. In the 1880s and 1890s the Farmers' Alliances, the Knights of Labor, and at moments the Colored Farmers' Alliance built a remarkable interracial agrarian movement that came closer to threatening the bipartisan settlement than anything since Reconstruction. The North Carolina Fusion government of 1894–98, in which Populists and Republicans (including many Black voters) governed together, briefly gave the lie to the idea that Southern politics had to be the politics of white supremacy. And then it was destroyed, by the Wilmington coup of 1898 and the disenfranchisement campaigns that followed. Tom Watson, the Georgia Populist who in the early 1890s had appealed openly for Black-white agrarian solidarity, ended his life as one of the most rabid race-baiters and antisemites in American public life. The Populist moment shows that interracial mass politics from below is possible, and that it has a historic tendency, under pressure, to collapse along racial lines. Any serious left "reclamation" has to start there, not from a Denning-style insistence that populism is just a name for democratic politics as such. It is striking that Getachew's introduction gestures at white supremacy as a "challenge," but the deeper point is that white supremacy has been the specific solvent of every previous interracial popular alliance in U.S. history. The mechanism by which that solvent works — the rapid abandonment of working-class whites by elites who suddenly remember their racial solidarity in moments of fusionist threat — is not a residual problem to be managed. It is the central problem.

This brings me to the structural worry. The populist projects Getachew describes — the CTU-WFP-DSA alignment in Chicago, the Sanders campaign nationally, Corbyn's Labour, Morales's MAS — vary enormously in their political-economic contexts, but they share a feature that distinguishes them from their nineteenth- and twentieth-century antecedents. They are not, except in pockets, embedded in dense organizations of working-class life. The Populists had the Alliances and their cooperatives, with hundreds of thousands of dues-paying members and an extraordinary network of lecturers. Mid-century social democracy had mass unions, working-class press, party clubs, fraternal orders. MAS in Bolivia, perhaps uniquely among recent left projects, did build on something like that base — the cocaleros, the neighborhood juntas of El Alto, the indigenous federations — which is part of why it had such durability before November's coup. Sanders 2020 was, by contrast, a campaign organization that tried to function as a movement. The "political revolution" it called for was supposed to bring new constituencies into being through the act of voting. When older Black voters in South Carolina and across the South declined the invitation, the campaign discovered, painfully, that there was no organized social infrastructure it could fall back on to recruit them on a different timetable. Sanders's loss is being narrated as a failure of message — too socialist, not socialist enough, too focused on class, insufficiently attentive to race — but a more sobering reading is that the campaign was attempting to do, in six months of primaries, what the Farmers' Alliances took fifteen years to build, and at a moment when the social materials for that kind of construction are far thinner.

The CTU's trajectory is, in this respect, an exception that proves the rule. It is precisely because the union spent a decade and a half rebuilding rank-and-file power, fusing community organizations to its bargaining campaigns, training a generation of organizers through the Caucus of Rank-and-File Educators, and then leveraging that base into electoral interventions, that Chicago looks the way it does today. The election of Brandon Johnson, the Socialist Caucus on City Council, Jeanette Taylor's path from hunger striker to alderwoman — these are real achievements, and they suggest a model. But the model is, properly speaking, less "populist" than syndicalist-democratic: it builds power from particular workplaces and neighborhoods outward, rather than constructing a discursive "people" against an "elite" and hoping that the social referent of the term will appear when called. The difference matters. The CTU's victories are difficult to scale not because they lack the right rhetoric but because most American workers do not belong to anything like the CTU, and the legal and political conditions for building such institutions have grown harder, not easier, over the past forty years.

This is where I find Getachew's framing of the COVID moment most useful and most worth pushing on. She rightly cites Nikhil Singh: the pandemic will not produce the transformation we want but will set the terms for it. The terms it is setting, however, do not obviously favor populist mobilization of any stripe. Crises in their acute phase tend to produce rally-round-the-flag effects that benefit incumbents — note the recent polling rebound for governors of both parties, and indeed for Trump in March before his April collapse. They license dramatic expansions of executive and surveillance power that are easier to install than to dismantle. They atomize the very forms of assembly — picket lines, mass meetings, door-knocking, congregational worship — through which populist movements have historically constituted themselves. And they hand to incumbents a quantum of fiscal and monetary intervention that, until February, the bipartisan consensus held to be impossible. The CARES Act's $2.2 trillion is, among other things, an enormous demonstration that "how will you pay for it" was always a political question and never an economic one. But the demonstration is being given by Mitch McConnell and Steven Mnuchin, not by Bernie Sanders, and the political beneficiary of having proven Modern Monetary Theory correct in practice may well be the right rather than the left. Getachew acknowledges this risk; I would press it harder. The historical record on crises and left advance is at best mixed: the Depression produced the New Deal in the U.S. but fascism in much of Europe; the 1970s stagflation crisis produced neoliberalism, not its opposite. Which way COVID cuts will depend on prior organization, and prior organization is what the left most conspicuously lacks.

So where does this leave the project of "reclaiming populism"? I want to suggest a friendly amendment rather than a refusal. The most important work the symposium can do is to disentangle three things that "populism" tends to fuse: the rhetorical act of constituting a people, the strategic construction of antagonism against an oligarchy, and the patient organizational work of building the institutions through which a multiracial working class can act for itself. The first is necessary but cheap. The second is powerful but combustible, and historically as available to the right as to the left. The third is the precondition for the other two to amount to anything, and it is the one for which our current vocabulary — "populism," "movement," "campaign" — is least suited. The Chicago example matters because it shows that this third kind of work is still possible. The defeats of Morales, Corbyn, and Sanders matter because they remind us how far short of what is needed even the most ambitious electoral expressions of left populism have so far fallen. Reclaiming populism is worth doing; rebuilding the institutions that could make populism mean something is the larger task.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The term 'populism' is conceptually thin and describes a formal strategy of mobilization rather than a specific political program, making it equally available to the right as to the left.
  2. 02Historical American populist movements demonstrate that interracial alliances are highly susceptible to collapsing under the pressure of white supremacy, which remains the central obstacle for any modern multiracial project.
  3. 03Modern left-wing insurgencies like the Sanders campaign failed because they lacked the deep, organized social infrastructure that sustained historical populist and social democratic movements.
  4. 04The success of the Chicago Teachers Union suggests that a 'syndicalist-democratic' model rooted in workplace and neighborhood power is more effective and durable than constructing a 'people' through discourse alone.
  5. 05The COVID-19 pandemic is more likely to benefit incumbents and the right wing than the left because the crisis atomizes the forms of physical assembly necessary for movement building.
