"The Occupy movement must reject the idea that American institutions merely need better alignment with their ideals and instead recognize that the current capitalist system is fundamentally incompatible with democracy."

The lead statement speaks in the language of national ideals, and that is understandable. It is also part of the problem. Occupy Wall Street has become compelling precisely because so many Americans no longer believe that the country’s institutions can be brought “into better alignment” with their stated principles. The gap is not accidental. It is not a temporary lapse. It is built into the current organization of economic and political life.

For thirty years, both major parties have accepted the broad framework of market discipline, deregulation, weak labor, low taxes on capital, and deference to finance. There have been differences, sometimes important ones, but the direction is hard to miss. The repeal of Glass-Steagall, the growth of derivatives markets, the expansion of executive compensation, the attack on welfare, the decline of unions, the outsourcing of manufacturing, and the conversion of higher education into a debt-financed private burden all occurred under Democratic as well as Republican administrations. The story is not simply that conservatives won. It is that the political class as a whole made peace with a form of capitalism that treats democratic life as a secondary concern.

The lead essay invokes emancipation, suffrage, and civil rights as examples of America correcting itself. But those examples also show the limits of liberal self-congratulation. Slavery ended through war, not polite institutional adjustment. Women won the vote after decades of agitation that respectable opinion often condemned. Civil rights activists faced police dogs, bombs, murders, and federal surveillance. In each case, the excluded had to make the country ungovernable in its old form before reform became thinkable.

Occupy’s refusal to issue a tidy platform is therefore not a weakness. It is an acknowledgment that the crisis is deeper than any single demand. If the movement announced tomorrow that it wanted a marginal tax increase, a jobs bill, and tougher bank regulation, Washington would know exactly how to handle it: praise the passion, negotiate the details, water down the substance, and return to business. The force of Occupy lies in its rejection of the premise that the existing system is basically legitimate and merely in need of repair.

What does that rejection mean? It need not mean a fully worked-out blueprint for socialism, though some occupiers are socialists and should not be asked to hide it for the sake of respectability. It does mean naming capitalism as a political system, not just an economic one. Workplaces are governed hierarchically. Investment decisions that shape communities are made by owners and managers seeking returns, not by residents seeking decent lives. Public officials compete to attract private capital by lowering taxes, weakening regulation, and disciplining labor. The result is a democracy that stops at the factory gate, the bank lobby, and the bond market.

The financial crisis exposed this structure with unusual clarity. When ordinary people default on debts, they are lectured about responsibility. When large banks collapse after years of reckless speculation, the state mobilizes to save them. The distinction is not moral; it is power. The banks were “systemically important.” The unemployed are not. Occupy’s encampments have reversed that judgment symbolically. They say that the homeless, indebted, jobless, and precarious are also systemically important.

Some liberals want to convert Occupy into an auxiliary of the Democratic Party for 2012. That would be a mistake. To be sure, elections matter. A Republican administration backed by the Tea Party would likely deepen austerity, attack labor, and weaken regulation further. But movements that become campaign arms lose the ability to tell the truth. Occupy should pressure Democrats, not serve them. It should make demands that reveal the inadequacy of the official debate: debt cancellation, public employment, a moratorium on foreclosures, free or radically cheaper higher education, public banking, stronger rights to organize, and democratic control over investment.

The movement should also expand beyond encampments. Occupying parks has created visibility, but the more strategic occupations may be of homes, schools, workplaces, and public institutions threatened by austerity. When a family is evicted from a foreclosed home while the bank that holds the mortgage received public support, the contradiction becomes concrete. When students occupy administrative buildings to protest tuition hikes and debt, the politics of the future becomes visible. When workers and occupiers act together, the slogan of the 99 percent gains organizational weight.

The danger is not that Occupy is too radical. The danger is that it will be domesticated before it discovers its own strength. The language of American ideals can be useful if it invites people in. But it can also soften the movement’s critique, making it sound as though the country simply forgot to live up to promises that everyone shares. The powerful do not merely forget. They benefit. They lobby, invest, discipline, and rule.

If Occupy is to have a future, it must remain a disruption. Its task is not to reassure America that its principles are intact. Its task is to force the question of whether democracy can coexist with concentrated economic power. That question cannot be answered by better rhetoric or procedural reform alone. It requires a struggle over ownership, labor, debt, and the allocation of social wealth. The encampments have opened a door. The next step is to walk through it without asking permission.
