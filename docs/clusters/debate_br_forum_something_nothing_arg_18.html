"The United States should shift from a military-centric occupation of Afghanistan to a strategy of restraint and 'decent politics' that addresses the political grievances fueling extremist recruitment."

Rosen ends with the argument that the roots of anti-American violence lie not in an inexplicable hatred of freedom but in grievances produced by American power: support for occupation, dictatorships, humiliation, and war. This is the part of his essay that many readers will find most provocative, perhaps even offensive. After September 11, to speak of grievances can sound like excusing murder. It is not. Explanation is not exoneration. If the United States cannot distinguish between understanding why a movement gains sympathy and justifying its crimes, it will continue to fight symptoms while feeding causes.

The Afghanistan debate has been trapped between two impoverished moral vocabularies. The first is the language of resolve: terrorists attacked us, the Taliban sheltered them, and therefore withdrawal means weakness. The second is the language of exposure: America is an empire, its stated ideals are hypocrisy, and therefore resistance to it is predictable. Rosen leans toward the second, but his reporting complicates both. The Americans on Highway 601 are not cartoon imperialists; many are brave and some are humane. The Taliban are not merely national liberators; they intimidate civilians and enforce a cruel social order. The Afghan police are not simply puppets; they are also poor men dying in large numbers. The villagers are not waiting to be liberated by either side; they are trying to survive.

A serious policy must begin from this complexity. It should also begin from a hard question: what is the relationship between American military action and American safety? Since 2001 the United States has treated military pressure as the central instrument against jihadist terrorism. It overthrew the Taliban, invaded Iraq, expanded detention and interrogation systems, used air strikes and raids across multiple countries, and built a global architecture of counterterrorism cooperation. Some of this was necessary. Al Qaeda is real, and governments have a duty to disrupt those planning mass murder. But the military frame has also enlarged the conflict. It has allowed a small network of extremists to present itself as the armed vanguard against a superpower at war with Muslims.

Rosen may understate al Qaeda’s danger when he describes its members as “a few hundred angry, unsophisticated Muslim extremists.” The 9/11 plot was sophisticated enough to kill thousands. The Madrid and London bombings showed that the threat is not imaginary. Failed and disrupted plots remind us that vigilance is required. But Rosen is right that al Qaeda’s strategic hope is to provoke overreaction. It cannot conquer the United States. It can tempt the United States into wars that drain resources, radicalize new recruits, alienate allies, and confirm the story that America respects no sovereignty but its own.

Afghanistan is now the test of whether America can escape that trap. The Taliban’s original refusal to break with al Qaeda made war in 2001 justified. But eight years later, the mission has blurred. Are American troops in Helmand protecting New York from al Qaeda, protecting Kabul from the Taliban, protecting Afghan villagers from both Taliban and police, protecting NATO credibility, protecting women’s rights, protecting supply routes, or protecting the reputation of presidents and generals? When a war has too many purposes, failure in any one of them becomes a reason to continue.

Rosen’s account shows how moral purpose is dissipated on the ground. A doctrine premised on protecting civilians becomes a raid on an empty mosque. A campaign for legitimate government becomes partnership with police who steal sheep and arrest students for ringtones. A struggle against global terrorism becomes confusion over whether Peshawar is Quetta. A promise of development becomes an irrigation problem caused by a road repair. These are not anecdotes in the margins; they are the war.

Yet one should be careful with the phrase “root causes.” Poverty alone does not produce terrorism; most poor people do not become terrorists. American support for Israel or Arab dictatorships does not make anyone fly planes into buildings. Ideology matters, religious authority matters, organizational networks matter, and individual moral choices matter. But grievances shape the audience in which extremist ideology seeks legitimacy. When images of civilian casualties circulate, when prisons become symbols of abuse, when corrupt rulers are seen as American clients, al Qaeda’s narrative becomes easier to sell even to those who dislike al Qaeda’s fanaticism.

What would it mean to address roots without appeasing murderers? First, end unnecessary wars. The Iraq invasion remains the clearest example of strategic self-harm: justified by false claims about weapons and insinuations about terrorism, it became a recruiting symbol for jihadists and a source of immense Iraqi suffering. Afghanistan risks becoming a different but related error: a once-justified intervention transformed into an open-ended attempt to reorder a society through force. The United States should reduce its military footprint and narrow its aims to preventing transnational terrorist sanctuary.

Second, restore the distinction between criminals and civilizations. The rhetoric of a “war on terror” has too often blurred into a perceived war on Islam. Guantánamo, Abu Ghraib, extraordinary rendition, and indefinite detention have damaged American legitimacy. Closing Guantánamo has proven politically difficult, but the principle remains essential: suspects should be charged, tried, transferred, or released under lawful procedures. Torture and cruel treatment are not only immoral; they are strategic gifts to enemies.

Third, change the political context in which anti-American militancy recruits. This includes a more even-handed approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. Supporting Israel’s security need not mean tolerating settlement expansion or Palestinian dispossession. It includes reducing support for friendly autocrats who crush peaceful opposition and thereby leave violent opposition as the most visible alternative. It includes treating Muslim publics as political communities, not as terrain on which extremists and moderates compete for American approval.

Fourth, invest in intelligence and policing rather than occupation. The most effective counterterrorism work is often quiet: tracking money, sharing information, infiltrating networks, securing ports and aviation, and prosecuting conspirators. These tools lack the drama of troop surges but are better suited to a dispersed threat. Military force should be reserved for situations where targets are clear, risks to civilians are minimized, and political consequences are weighed honestly.

Fifth, support Afghan civilians without making them instruments of war. Humanitarian aid, education, agriculture, and health programs should continue where possible, but not as bribes for allegiance. Afghans can tell when assistance is a tactic. Respect requires patience, local consent, and willingness to fund peaceful areas as well as violent ones. If Americans truly care about Afghan women, for example, they should support Afghan women’s organizations, legal aid, and asylum pathways—not merely cite women’s suffering when justifying escalation.

Rosen’s essay sometimes seems to imply that if the United States corrected its imperial excesses, al Qaeda’s appeal would wither. That is too optimistic. Apocalyptic movements can survive the loss of popular sympathy; small conspiracies can still kill. But reducing the pool of passive sympathy and outrage matters. So does depriving extremists of their most effective propaganda: the sight of foreign soldiers breaking down doors in Muslim villages to prop up corrupt governments.

The United States cannot undo September 11 by remaking Afghanistan, and it cannot redeem its errors by staying until the errors look purposeful. It can, however, choose a different kind of strength: lawful, restrained, politically intelligent, and less addicted to military solutions. Rosen is right that decent politics would do more than another escalation. Decent politics will not eliminate danger. But it may stop us from manufacturing enemies faster than we can kill them.
