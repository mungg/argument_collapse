# Religious Liberty as the Constitutional Compass

The lead essay makes a compelling case that American Islamophobia is structural and bipartisan. But its proposed framework for resistance—what it calls the "six key misapprehensions"—is, I think, too focused on critiquing existing policies rather than articulating a positive vision. What is missing is a clear statement of the principles that should guide our resistance.

I want to suggest that religious liberty offers the best constitutional framework for opposing anti-Muslim discrimination. This might seem counterintuitive. Religious liberty is often invoked by conservatives to defend discrimination against LGBTQ people, and many of the same people who invoke religious liberty are also Trump supporters. But this does not mean that religious liberty is the wrong framework for defending Muslims. It means that religious liberty has been hijacked by bad actors, and we should reclaim it.

The First Amendment provides that "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof." This means that the government cannot favor one religion over another and cannot burden religious practice without adequate justification. Both of these principles have been violated by the War on Terror. The government has surveilled mosques, criminalized religious practice (through the material support ban), and subjected Muslims to heightened scrutiny based on their religion.

But religious liberty is not just a legal principle; it is a political commitment. A society that takes religious liberty seriously cannot single out one religion for special surveillance, cannot criminalize charitable giving to religious causes abroad, and cannot treat the adherents of one religion as presumptive security threats. This is not because Muslims are special but because religious liberty is universal. It applies to Muslims, Christians, Jews, Hindus, and everyone else.

This framework has advantages over the "Islamophobia" framework. It does not require accepting the premise that all anti-Muslim sentiment is irrational prejudice, which many Americans reject. It does not require accepting the premise that Islam is a monolithic ideology rather than a diverse religion with many interpretations. And it offers a positive vision for what American law should look like—one grounded in the principle that the government cannot discriminate on the basis of religion.

The Khans, whom the lead essay discusses, were celebrated for invoking the Constitution. This was appropriate. But the Constitution does not merely protect liberty and equal protection; it also protects religious exercise. The Khans, as observant Muslims, were exercising their religion when they attended the Democratic National Convention and spoke about their son. Their exercise of religion should be protected, not scrutinized.

A coalition for religious liberty would bring together Muslims, Christians, Jews, and others who share a commitment to the principle that the government cannot single out religions for special treatment. It would challenge the surveillance of mosques, the material support ban, the border questioning of Muslims, and the Countering Violent Extremism program—all on religious liberty grounds. And it would demand that the government treat all religious communities with the same respect, even when it disapproves of their beliefs or practices.

The lead essay is right that Trump's ban must be opposed. But we also need to build a positive vision of religious equality that can sustain a movement over the long term. The First Amendment offers that vision, and it is the best constitutional hook for the work ahead.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Religious liberty provides a positive, principled vision for resistance rather than just a critique of existing policies.
  2. 02The government’s surveillance of mosques and criminalization of religious practices like charitable giving constitute direct violations of the First Amendment's free exercise and establishment clauses.
  3. 03Framing the issue through religious liberty is more politically effective than the 'Islamophobia' framework because it relies on universal principles rather than debates over the nature of prejudice.
  4. 04A religious liberty framework allows for the building of a broad, multi-faith coalition of Muslims, Christians, and Jews united by the shared principle that the state cannot single out any religion for special scrutiny.
  5. 05Reclaiming religious liberty from conservative actors who use it to justify discrimination allows it to serve its true purpose of protecting all religious adherents from government overreach.
