"While the lead essay correctly identifies the targeting of Muslims, it fails to emphasize that the post-9/11 security infrastructure represents a universal constitutional crisis that threatens the civil liberties of all Americans."

Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis’s essay, “Islam on Trial,” is a necessary, albeit incomplete, corrective to the historical amnesia that has characterized the first few weeks of the Trump administration. As thousands flood the airports to protest the newly signed Executive Order banning travel from seven Muslim-majority nations, the authors rightly point out that this draconian measure did not emerge from a vacuum. It is, as they argue, the weaponization of an existing infrastructure built by both the Bush and Obama administrations. However, by framing this crisis almost exclusively through the lens of Islamophobia and anti-Muslim prejudice, Akbar and Theoharis risk missing the broader, more insidious constitutional crisis at hand. The tragedy of the post-9/11 era is not simply that the state has targeted Muslims; it is that in the process of targeting Muslims, the United States has systematically dismantled the civil liberties that protect every single American.

The architecture of surveillance and preemptive prosecution that the authors detail is not merely a tool of racial or religious subjugation; it is a profound assault on the First and Fourth Amendments of the United States Constitution. When we examine the “material support” statute, which Akbar and Theoharis rightfully condemn, we must understand it fundamentally as a First Amendment issue. Under the guise of national security, the government has criminalized speech, association, and charitable giving. In *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* (2010), the Supreme Court ruled that even teaching international law to a designated foreign terrorist organization constitutes a crime. This ruling effectively erased the distinction between protected political advocacy and criminal conduct. While Muslims currently bear the brunt of this jurisprudential disaster, the legal precedent is a loaded weapon lying on the table, ready to be used against any political dissident, environmental activist, or anti-war protester the government deems a threat.

Similarly, the massive expansion of the surveillance state, revealed in stark detail by Edward Snowden in 2013, represents the near-total collapse of Fourth Amendment protections against unreasonable searches and seizures. Akbar and Theoharis argue that the “brunt force” of this surveillance fell on Muslim communities, and they are correct in terms of targeted harassment. The NYPD’s Demographics Unit, which mapped out where Muslims ate, shopped, and prayed, is a shameful testament to this reality. But the underlying legal authority—the FISA Amendments Act, Section 215 of the Patriot Act, and Executive Order 12333—relies on the mass, indiscriminate collection of metadata and communications from *all* citizens. By exceptionalizing the Muslim experience, the authors inadvertently minimize the universal danger of a government that believes it has the inherent right to monitor the digital lives of its entire population.

The “chilling nature” of surveillance that the authors describe is real, but it is a chill that threatens the very foundation of a free society. If journalists cannot protect their sources, if activists cannot organize without fear of infiltration, and if citizens cannot communicate privately, democracy itself begins to wither. The fact that Muslim communities were the testing ground for these authoritarian tools does not change the fact that the tools themselves are fundamentally incompatible with a constitutional republic. We must view the targeting of Muslims not as an isolated pathology, but as the canary in the coal mine for American liberty.

Furthermore, Akbar and Theoharis’s critique of the judicial system is trenchant but needs broader historical context. The “tremendous judicial deference to overzealous prosecutors” is a hallmark of times of national panic. From the suspension of habeas corpus during the Civil War to the Espionage Act prosecutions of World War I, to the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II upheld in *Korematsu*, the courts have consistently abdicated their role as a check on the executive branch during times of perceived crisis. The federal courts’ current deference to the national security state is the latest chapter in this dark tradition. However, the remedy is not simply to expose the anti-Muslim bias within the system, but to demand a reinvigoration of judicial independence and a strict adherence to constitutional limits, regardless of the defendant’s identity or the nature of the alleged crime.

The authors also touch upon the No Fly List, noting its secrecy and lack of due process. This is perhaps the clearest example of how the administrative state has circumvented constitutional protections. The government has created a system where an individual can be deprived of a fundamental right—the right to travel—based on secret evidence, without notice, and without a meaningful opportunity to challenge the designation. The fact that this list has been used to coerce Muslims into becoming informants is despicable. Yet, the existence of such a list should horrify every American, regardless of faith. It represents a parallel system of quasi-criminal punishment that operates entirely outside the boundaries of the Bill of Rights.

While Akbar and Theoharis correctly identify the hypocrisy of liberals who champion figures like Khizr Khan while ignoring the structural violence inflicted upon Muslim communities, their proposed solution—a vague call to "challenge these practices"—lacks the necessary constitutional grounding. We cannot simply rely on public outcry to rein in the national security state. Protests at airports are powerful symbols, but they do not rewrite statutes or overturn bad jurisprudence.

What is required is a comprehensive legislative and legal strategy to dismantle the post-9/11 security architecture. We must demand the repeal of the Patriot Act, strict legislative limits on the material support statute to protect First Amendment activities, and the end of bulk data collection. We must push for judicial nominees who are skeptical of executive power and who understand that the Bill of Rights is not a suicide pact, but rather the very thing that makes the nation worth defending.

The authors are right to warn us that Trump’s presidency represents an urgent opportunity to understand and contest these practices. But if we frame this fight solely as a defense of marginalized communities against an Islamophobic administration, we concede too much ground. The battle against the surveillance state, preemptive prosecution, and secret watchlists is a battle for the soul of the American legal system. The rights of Muslim Americans are inextricably linked to the rights of all Americans. If the Constitution cannot protect a Muslim charity worker from arbitrary surveillance and prosecution today, it will not protect the rest of us tomorrow. We must defend these rights not just because it is a moral imperative for a marginalized group, but because the alternative is a post-constitutional America.
