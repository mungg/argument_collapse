It is too easy to turn a few campus controversies into a sweeping indictment of an entire generation. Today’s students are not uniquely fragile, nor are older Americans consistently brave defenders of dissent. Every era has its orthodoxies. What is distinctive now is that students have grown up amid social media, economic insecurity and a sharper public vocabulary for racism, sexual violence and mental illness. They are often less willing than their predecessors to treat speech as an abstract exercise detached from power and harm.

That instinct has real moral force. Universities have too often told marginalized students to endure humiliation in the name of “debate” while doing little to ensure that they were full participants in campus life. A request for a trigger warning is not always censorship; it can be a modest notice that allows a veteran or assault survivor to prepare for difficult material. A “safe space” can mean a necessary refuge in an institution where some students still feel isolated.

But the same impulse becomes dangerous when it tries to punish, defund or silence views rather than answer them. The Wesleyan newspaper episode is troubling precisely because student journalism exists to test arguments in public. A bad essay should be met with better essays, reporting, protest and criticism—not with efforts that make editors fear publishing controversial opinions. If students learn that the proper response to offensive speech is institutional suppression, they will carry a thin-skinned politics into workplaces, media, government and civic life.

The answer is not to sneer at students, but to teach liberal habits more deliberately. Colleges should protect protest and protect speakers; support vulnerable students and insist that discomfort is part of education. Future leaders will need empathy, but also resilience. A democratic culture depends on both.

"While there is a concerning trend of students using therapeutic language to suppress speech, it is premature to judge an entire generation's future leadership based on the actions of a vocal minority."

The premise of the question deserves a little scrutiny before we draw conclusions about an entire generation. Yes, there have been notable flashpoints recently — the Wesleyan Argus controversy over a column critical of Black Lives Matter, the disinvitations of speakers like Condoleezza Rice at Rutgers and Ayaan Hirsi Ali at Brandeis, the spread of trigger warnings, the campaign at Mizzou. Greg Lukianoff and Jonathan Haidt argued in The Atlantic two months ago that "vindictive protectiveness" is reshaping campus discourse. But Pew's polling this year also showed that 40 percent of millennials think government should be able to prevent speech offensive to minorities — a higher share than older cohorts, but still a minority. We are talking about a tendency, not a transformation.

Even granting the trend, the framing of "intolerance" obscures what is actually happening. Today's students did not invent the impulse to police speech. The campus speech codes of the 1980s and 1990s, the Hays Code, McCarthyism, and the long tradition of community pressure against unpopular ideas all predate them. What is distinctive now is who is doing the pressuring — students themselves, often from the left — and the therapeutic vocabulary they use. The shift from "this speech is dangerous to the republic" to "this speech is harmful to me" reflects a real change in moral grammar, one rooted in the broader expansion of concepts like trauma and microaggression.

That deserves criticism, and Obama was right to offer it. A university that cannot tolerate Christina Hoff Sommers or a sophomoric op-ed is failing at its core function. Students who graduate believing argument is a form of violence will struggle in workplaces, courtrooms, and legislatures where it isn't.

But the leap from "some loud undergraduates" to "future leaders of this country" is too fast. Most twenty-year-olds in any era hold opinions they will revise. The more honest worry is whether faculty and administrators — the adults — have the nerve to defend unfettered inquiry when students demand otherwise. The generation will be shaped by that answer.

"The current campus debates over speech are not a rejection of the First Amendment, but rather a necessary and historically common renegotiation of the boundaries between free expression and the responsibility to prevent harm."

The uproar at Wesleyan and the broader campus conversation about trigger warnings and safe spaces has been cast as a generational retreat from the First Amendment. But to frame it simply as “intolerance” misses a deeper, more familiar story. Today’s students are not rejecting free speech; they are contesting its boundaries and demanding that it be paired with a responsibility to avoid foreseeable harm—a tension that has defined every generation’s free-speech fights.

The current flashpoints echo earlier battles. In the 1990s, campus speech codes were the alleged threat to liberty, yet that generation went on to lead a vibrant democracy. Before that, civil rights activists were accused of being too sensitive when they challenged racist rhetoric as harmful. What looks like coddling is often a moral awakening to the injuries that language can inflict on marginalized people. When a student requests a trigger warning before a graphic depiction of sexual assault, she is not silencing the professor; she is asking for the cognitive space to engage without being ambushed by trauma. That is a strategy for inclusion, not censorship.

Of course, some student activism has veered into illiberal territory. Attempts to defund the Wesleyan student newspaper for publishing a provocative essay crossed a line from protest to prior restraint. Yet it is a mistake to extrapolate from the angriest voices on a few campuses to an entire generation. Most young people are navigating a difficult balancing act: they online, where unmoderated hate speech is rampant, while also learning in diverse classrooms that their words have consequences. They are experimenting with norms that make rigorous debate possible for those who historically had the most to fear from “uncomfortable ideas.” As future leaders, they will not abandon free expression; they will—like every generation before them—renegotiate its limits based on the knowledge that speech can both liberate and wound. That negotiation, however messy, is a sign of democratic vitality, not its collapse.

Vanilla MiniMax

"Characterizing the current generation of students as intolerant of free speech misinterprets their actions, which are often rooted in a coherent moral framework regarding harm and community norms rather than a desire for censorship."

The debate about free speech on college campuses raises legitimate concerns but risks oversimplifying complex generational and cultural shifts. While trigger warnings and safe spaces have become more common, characterizing an entire generation as intolerant of free speech misreads both the phenomenon and the young people it purports to describe.

Trigger warnings, at their most basic, represent an attempt by individuals to control their exposure to potentially distressing content—an exercise of autonomy rather than an attack on speech itself. Most students encountering trigger warnings are not demanding that the triggering content be banned, but simply requesting advance notice so they can make informed choices about their engagement. This is not fundamentally different from content warnings that have long appeared in educational settings.

Safe spaces, similarly, often reflect students creating voluntary communities with shared norms rather than demanding institutional censorship. The line between choosing one's associations and demanding conformity is blurry. That young people seek spaces of relative comfort among peers is hardly novel—what might be new is the explicit articulation of that desire in these terms.

The more troubling incidents—efforts to disinvite speakers, calls for professor discipline over controversial ideas—do deserve scrutiny. But these tend to be the exceptions that generate headlines rather than the rule. Such behaviors reflect a particular political moment where intensity on both left and right sometimes overwhelms norms of civil discourse. This is not exclusively a youth problem.

What does seem true is that this generation approaches questions of speech, harm, and community differently than its predecessors. Having come of age in a world of social media and heightened awareness of systemic inequality, many young people genuinely believe that some speech causes concrete harm and that communities have legitimate interests in setting norms. Whether one agrees with that view or not, it emerges from a coherent moral framework rather than simple intolerance.

The Wesleyan incident, rather than epitomizing suppression, actually demonstrated that student journalists could rally in defense of editorial independence. That response received far less attention than the initial controversy, but it suggests institutional cultures remain capable of protecting expressive freedom even under pressure.

If this generation has different attitudes toward speech boundaries, perhaps the appropriate response is sustained engagement rather than alarm. These young people will indeed become future leaders, and they will learn that democratic society requires negotiating disagreement. The skills they develop navigating these tensions—argumentation, empathy, the willingness to revise positions—may serve them well. College has always been a place where discomfort with ideas can be transformed into deeper engagement with them.

The current panic over a "coddled" generation of college students often misses the underlying motivation behind the push for safe spaces and trigger warnings. When Wesleyan University students petitioned to defund their campus newspaper over a provocative op-ed, critics understandably saw a generational abandonment of the First Amendment. However, to understand what this means for our future leaders, we must recognize that these students are not acting out of malice, but out of a deeply felt—if sometimes clumsy—commitment to inclusivity.

Today’s undergraduates are part of the most diverse generation in American history. For many of them, the drive to police offensive speech stems from an acute awareness of historical marginalization. Trigger warnings and safe spaces initially emerged to protect students who had experienced genuine trauma, serving as tools of empathy in environments that have not always been welcoming to minorities, women, or LGBT individuals. In this light, the impulse to shield peers from deeply offensive rhetoric suggests that our future leaders will be highly attuned to equity and psychological well-being.

Yet, the danger lies in conflating emotional comfort with intellectual safety. When the desire to protect marginalized voices morphs into the censorship of unpopular ideas, it undermines the foundational purpose of a university. The Wesleyan incident is troubling not because students protested an article they found offensive—protest is a hallmark of free expression—but because they sought to dismantle the platform that published it. If students graduate believing that institutional power should be used to silence uncomfortable viewpoints, they will be ill-equipped to navigate the messy realities of democratic governance.

As these young people transition into leadership roles, their profound empathy will undoubtedly be an asset to the country. But if they cannot separate ideological disagreement from personal harm, their effectiveness will be severely limited. A functioning pluralistic society requires the resilience to confront hostile ideas with better arguments, rather than shutting down the forum entirely. Our task is not to dismiss this generation as overly fragile, but to guide them in reconciling their pursuit of justice with the indispensable friction of free and open debate.

The most troubling thing on campus today is not that students are too sensitive. It is that some have begun to treat disagreement as a form of harm and institutional punishment as the proper answer to bad argument. That shift matters because universities are supposed to be the place where citizens learn the difference between rebutting an idea and suppressing it.

The Wesleyan episode is a useful example. A student newspaper published an essay critical of aspects of Black Lives Matter. Many students found it ill-informed or offensive. They had every right to say so, to write letters, to boycott, to publish counterarguments, to demand better editing. But the campaign to cut the newspaper’s funding moved from criticism to coercion. A newspaper that has existed for generations should not face financial strangulation because it printed an unpopular opinion. If that becomes the norm, student editors will learn the safest lesson: avoid controversy.

Trigger warnings and safe spaces are not all the same thing. A professor who alerts students that a novel contains sexual violence is not necessarily betraying liberal education. A campus group that reserves a room for students to recover after a racist incident is not abolishing the First Amendment. But the language surrounding these practices increasingly blurs discomfort, offense, trauma and danger. Once offense is treated as injury, the case for restricting speech becomes much easier to make.

This does say something about the formation of future leaders. Not that today’s students are uniquely weak; every generation has tried to silence something. Earlier students demanded loyalty oaths, bans on communists, restrictions on obscenity or crackdowns on antiwar radicals. But it does suggest that a portion of this generation is being educated into a cramped understanding of pluralism. They are learning to value diversity of identity while sometimes mistrusting diversity of thought.

That is a dangerous combination for a democracy. Future legislators, judges, executives, editors and nonprofit leaders will face ideas they consider not merely mistaken but cruel. They will be tempted to use rules, budgets, licensing and professional norms to push those ideas out of respectable life. The better habit is harder: answer speech with speech, protect even foolish expression, and trust that adults can hear ugly claims without being destroyed by them.

Universities should not sneer at students’ moral seriousness. But they should insist that moral seriousness includes intellectual courage. If campuses cannot model that, the country will be poorer for it.

Abolitionists, suffragists, civil rights demonstrators, labor organizers and gay rights activists all needed free speech because the respectable majority often considered their demands dangerous or indecent. Before diagnosing an entire generation, we should ask how much evidence we really have. The current panic about intolerant students rests heavily on a handful of vivid anecdotes: a disinvited speaker here, a trigger warning controversy there, the fight over the Wesleyan Argus. These incidents deserve attention, but they do not prove that millions of young Americans have turned against free speech.

College campuses have always produced moral crusades, linguistic fashions and theatrical protests. In the 1960s, student activists occupied buildings and shouted down administrators. In the 1980s and 1990s, fights over political correctness and speech codes produced claims very similar to those we hear now. Long before that, universities excluded radicals, atheists, women, black students and suspected homosexuals with far less hand-wringing about open inquiry. If today’s students sometimes behave censoriously, they did not invent censorship.

There is also a selection problem. We notice elite campuses because journalists graduated from them and like writing about them. Wesleyan, Yale, Oberlin and Columbia become stand-ins for “this generation,” even though most young people attend community colleges, regional public universities or no college at all. Many are working jobs while studying, worrying less about microaggressions than debt, rent and whether their degree will lead to employment. It is reckless to infer national character from the loudest debates at the most visible schools.

That said, the language of harm on campus is worth scrutinizing. When students describe a newspaper op-ed, a visiting lecturer or a classroom text as making them “unsafe,” they sometimes expand the concept of safety beyond usefulness. Physical safety and equal access to education are fundamental. Emotional insulation from bad ideas is not. Universities should make that distinction clearly.

But older commentators should make distinctions too. A trigger warning is not censorship. A protest is not censorship. A demand is not automatically censorship. Students have free speech rights as well, including the right to say that a speaker is wrong, bigoted or undeserving of a platform. The line is crossed when they seek to use institutional power to prevent others from speaking or publishing.

So what does this say about future leaders? Probably that they will resemble past leaders: principled in some moments, hypocritical in others, shaped by institutional incentives more than generational essence. The task is not to lament “kids today.” It is to build campus rules and habits that protect expression while teaching why protection is necessary even when speech offends.

The question asks what campus speech controversies reveal about young people. They may reveal more about institutions. Universities have spent decades marketing themselves as protective communities while also promising radical personal transformation. They recruit students with brochures celebrating belonging, diversity and support, then seem surprised when students ask administrators to enforce those promises.

This does not excuse every demand made in the name of safety. It explains why the demands arise. Students today arrive on campus after years of adult supervision, anti-bullying campaigns, online reputation management and high-stakes competition for credentials. They also live inside social media, where offense spreads instantly, outrage is rewarded, and a local argument becomes a national morality play before anyone has time to think. The result is a campus politics that is fast, emotional and administrative: identify harm, mobilize peers, demand an official response.

Free speech suffers in that environment because it is slow. It asks people to tolerate the existence of bad arguments long enough to answer them. It requires faith that open debate will produce correction, or at least that suppression is more dangerous than error. That faith is hard to sustain when students believe institutions have ignored racism, sexual assault or homophobia for years. To them, calls for “more debate” can sound like delay.

Still, the faith is necessary. A university newspaper cannot do its job if a single controversial article can trigger a funding crisis. A classroom cannot do its job if professors fear that teaching disturbing material will be treated as misconduct. A student cannot become intellectually mature if every encounter with a hostile view is converted into a complaint process.

The answer is to separate support from censorship. Universities should provide counseling, cultural centers, disability accommodations and clear protections against harassment. They should also adopt strong free-expression rules that prevent administrators and student governments from punishing protected speech. Those commitments are not contradictory. They tell students: you belong here, and so do arguments you may hate.

As future leaders, today’s students will inherit a country that is diverse, unequal and angry. They will need more than sensitivity; they will need resilience, restraint and a principled respect for the rights of adversaries. But older generations should not merely scold them. Adults built the therapeutic, bureaucratic and digital world in which these habits formed. If we want different leaders, we need to teach and model a better kind of freedom now.
