"While the diagnosis of extreme wealth inequality is correct, a federal annual wealth tax is less effective and more problematic than a comprehensive reform of capital income, inheritance, and unrealized gains taxation."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman have done an enormous public service by forcing Americans to look directly at the tax privileges of the very rich. Their broad diagnosis is largely correct: the contemporary tax system often treats labor income more harshly than accumulated capital; the estate tax has been hollowed out; the corporate tax has been weakened; and the richest households can defer or avoid taxable income in ways unavailable to ordinary workers. Yet the remedy they emphasize—a federal annual wealth tax—is not the best way to solve the problem. If the aim is to tax the superrich effectively, reduce dynastic inequality, and fund public goods, we should focus less on annual valuation of wealth and more on comprehensive taxation of capital income, inheritances, and unrealized gains.

The distinction matters because the case for a wealth tax often conflates two propositions. The first is that billionaires are undertaxed. That is true. The second is that the best way to tax them is to assess their net worth every year and apply a percentage levy. That is much less clear.

Start with the Buffett example. Saez and Zucman point out that Warren Buffett’s economic income is far larger than his taxable income because Berkshire Hathaway retains earnings rather than paying dividends. This is a genuine problem. But it is not obvious that an annual wealth tax is the most direct solution. A more natural solution is to tax unrealized capital gains for the very wealthy, either annually on a mark-to-market basis for publicly traded assets or at least at death and upon major gifts for all assets. If Buffett’s Berkshire shares rise in value because the company accumulates profits, that appreciation can be taxed. If valuation is easy because the shares are publicly traded, then mark-to-market taxation is straightforward. If valuation is difficult because the asset is private, tax can be deferred with an interest charge until sale, transfer, or death. This would attack the core avoidance strategy: indefinite deferral.

The United States already has the conceptual tools for such a system. We tax capital gains when realized. We can broaden realization events, eliminate step-up in basis at death, strengthen gift taxation, and impose look-through rules on trusts. We can require billionaires to pay annual tax on tradable assets and impose a “retrospective capital gains tax” on nontradable assets when eventually sold. None of this is administratively simple, but it is more closely tied to actual income than a wealth tax and less vulnerable to the claim that government is taxing assets whose value may later collapse.

This concern is not merely theoretical. We are writing in March 2020, at a moment of extraordinary market volatility. Equity values are swinging wildly as the coronavirus pandemic spreads and governments scramble to respond. Suppose a founder’s stake is valued at $5 billion on December 31, taxed accordingly, and then falls by half in March. Of course, wealthy people can bear losses better than anyone else. But an annual wealth tax, unlike an income tax, taxes the stock of wealth even when the return is low or negative. Over time, that may be intended. Saez and Zucman frankly argue for deconcentration. But if the goal is tax justice—taxing people according to economic gains—then taxing actual gains is more principled.

The valuation problem is also more serious than Saez and Zucman allow. They are right that many assets of the very rich have observable market prices. Publicly traded stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and real estate can be valued. But the hard cases will not be marginal in practice. Wealthy taxpayers will rearrange their affairs to create hard cases. They will hold assets through partnerships and trusts; they will place wealth in private companies; they will create debt instruments, derivatives, family limited partnerships, and cross-border structures designed to reduce reported net worth. Tax law is not applied to static taxpayers. It is applied to taxpayers who respond.

The estate tax offers a warning. It has been weakened by politics, but also by decades of planning: valuation discounts, grantor retained annuity trusts, dynasty trusts, charitable vehicles, and other devices. A wealth tax would face this planning every year. Saez and Zucman respond with in-kind payment: if the taxpayer objects to the IRS valuation, let her pay in shares, which the government can auction. This is ingenious, but it creates its own difficulties. Would the government really auction minority stakes in private family firms every year? Would buyers receive information rights? Would the sale of small stakes in illiquid companies produce fair value or fire-sale discounts? Would private equity firms use the process to acquire strategic footholds? Would family businesses restructure to avoid precisely this threat? A clever mechanism is not the same as a stable tax system.

There is also the constitutional risk. A federal wealth tax would immediately be challenged as an unapportioned direct tax. Perhaps it would survive. Eminent scholars disagree. But the uncertainty is not trivial. A president who made a wealth tax the centerpiece of a fiscal agenda could spend years defending it in court only to see it struck down. Meanwhile, more clearly constitutional reforms—higher income tax rates, capital gains reform, estate tax reform, corporate minimum taxes, financial reporting rules—might have been enacted and implemented.

Supporters sometimes answer that constitutional anxiety is a counsel of timidity. But political capital is finite. The question is not whether we should challenge entrenched wealth. We should. The question is how to do so in a way that actually collects revenue and changes behavior. A tax on unrealized gains of billionaires, an end to stepped-up basis, a restored estate tax with tight anti-avoidance rules, and stronger corporate taxation would be formidable. They would also be harder to caricature as a tax on “wealth” in the abstract, because they are taxes on income, transfers, and profits—categories already deeply embedded in American law.

What might an alternative agenda look like?

First, tax capital gains at the same rate as ordinary income for high-income taxpayers. The preferential rate on capital gains is one of the great inequities of the code. If a nurse’s wages are taxed more heavily than a billionaire’s stock gains, the system cannot be defended.

Second, require mark-to-market taxation for publicly traded assets held by the ultra-wealthy. If a household has, say, more than $50 million or $100 million in net worth, annual appreciation on publicly traded securities should be taxable whether sold or not. Losses could be credited or carried forward. This would end the game by which billionaires borrow against appreciated stock while avoiding realization.

Third, impose a retrospective tax with interest on gains from non-publicly traded assets when sold, transferred, or bequeathed. This would reduce the advantage of holding private assets without requiring the IRS to establish a precise annual value for every family business.

Fourth, eliminate stepped-up basis at death. The current rule, which can permanently erase income tax on appreciation, is indefensible. Death should not be the ultimate tax shelter.

Fifth, rebuild the estate and gift tax. Exemptions should be lower, rates higher, and avoidance vehicles curtailed. The taxation of inheritances directly targets dynastic wealth and does so at a moment when liquidity is often created by transfer or can be planned for.

Sixth, strengthen corporate taxation, including minimum taxes and anti-profit-shifting rules. Saez and Zucman are right that the corporate tax is blunter than it was in the 1950s because stock ownership is more dispersed and international. But it remains a crucial backstop. If profits are not taxed at the entity level, wealthy shareholders can defer personal tax indefinitely.

Seventh, fund enforcement. This is perhaps the least glamorous but most important reform. The IRS cannot police billionaires with antiquated technology and depleted staff. Every serious progressive tax agenda requires information reporting, audit capacity, and international cooperation.

This agenda would not satisfy those whose primary goal is to reduce billionaire wealth as such. Saez and Zucman compare extreme wealth to carbon emissions: a negative externality to be reduced, not merely a source of revenue. I understand the analogy, but I am uneasy with it. Carbon emissions are harmful because they physically destabilize the climate. Wealth is more complicated. Some fortunes reflect monopoly, rent extraction, and political manipulation. Others reflect innovations that consumers value. The problem is not every large fortune in itself; it is the legal and political environment that allows fortunes to translate into domination and dynastic privilege.

That means taxation should be paired with non-tax reforms. If billionaires have too much political power, strengthen campaign finance rules, lobbying disclosure, antitrust enforcement, labor rights, and public financing of elections. If executives extract rents, reform corporate governance and empower workers. If drug companies abuse patents, change patent and procurement law. A wealth tax may reduce concentrated resources, but it is a blunt substitute for institutions that prevent wealth from buying power in the first place.

There is another political risk. A wealth tax can sound simple: two cents on every dollar above $50 million. That simplicity is part of its appeal. But when the actual law becomes a dense web of valuation rules, exemptions, anti-avoidance provisions, and litigation, disappointment may follow. Opponents will highlight every dispute as proof of failure. By contrast, reforms to capital gains and inheritances are less rhetorically dramatic but may prove more durable.

None of this should comfort defenders of the status quo. The current tax system is indefensible. The richest Americans should pay far more, and their ability to defer tax indefinitely should end. But a wealth tax is not the only way to get there, and it may not be the strongest way. The most important objective is to tax capital as effectively as we tax labor. That means following income as it accrues, preventing gains from disappearing at death, taxing inheritances robustly, and ensuring that corporations cannot serve as permanent shelters.

Saez and Zucman have moved the debate in the right direction by making billionaire undertaxation impossible to ignore. The next step is to design reforms that can survive courts, administrators, markets, and taxpayers’ lawyers. A wealth tax is one path. A comprehensive capital-income and inheritance tax agenda may be a better one.
