"The United States should modernize its nuclear arsenal by prioritizing a secure, survivable retaliatory capability while excluding new weapons that lower the threshold for nuclear use or create dangerous ambiguity."

Modernization should begin from a clear premise: the purpose of American nuclear weapons is to deter nuclear attack on the United States and its allies, not to make nuclear war more usable. A safer modernization would therefore preserve a secure, survivable retaliatory capability while avoiding new weapons that blur the line between conventional and nuclear war.

Some investment is unavoidable. Much of the arsenal and its supporting infrastructure dates from the Cold War, and deterrence depends not only on warheads but on submarines, command-and-control systems, early-warning satellites, secure communications and a competent scientific workforce. The most important leg of the triad is the ballistic-missile submarine force, because it is the hardest to find and destroy. Replacing aging Ohio-class submarines is expensive but defensible. Life-extension programs for existing warheads are also reasonable if they improve safety, security and reliability without adding new military missions or new explosive capabilities.

But modernization should exclude a rush toward smaller, more “tailored” nuclear weapons or weapons advertised as more accurate and therefore more thinkable. The danger is not only technical; it is political and psychological. If presidents, adversaries or allies come to believe that a limited nuclear strike can be controlled, the threshold for nuclear use may fall. The B61 bomb upgrade and especially a new nuclear-armed cruise missile deserve skepticism for this reason. Cruise missiles are also destabilizing because an adversary cannot easily know whether an incoming cruise missile is conventional or nuclear. In a crisis, ambiguity can invite catastrophic miscalculation.

The United States can deter nuclear war with fewer weapons than it now plans to maintain. New START still permits 1,550 deployed strategic warheads, more than enough to devastate any attacker. Rather than recapitalizing every delivery system on a Cold War timetable, Washington should prioritize survivability, safety and command integrity, while delaying or canceling systems whose main rationale is bureaucratic symmetry among the services.

Finally, modernization must be paired with restraint. That means pursuing further negotiated reductions with Russia when possible, maintaining a moratorium on explosive testing, strengthening nonproliferation commitments, and making clear that nuclear weapons exist only for extreme circumstances. Deterrence is strongest when it is credible, disciplined and narrow. A trillion-dollar rebuild that makes nuclear arms seem more usable would not be modernization; it would be regression.

Modernization is unavoidable: warheads, delivery systems, and command-and-control infrastructure built in the 1980s are aging out, and letting them decay quietly is itself a policy choice with safety implications. But "modernization" must not become a euphemism for expanding what the arsenal can do. The proposed B61-12 guided gravity bomb and the new long-range standoff (LRSO) cruise missile cross precisely that line. Lower-yield, more accurate weapons blur the firebreak between conventional and nuclear war, and a stealthy nuclear cruise missile is indistinguishable in flight from its conventional cousin — an invitation to catastrophic misidentification in a crisis. Former Secretary of Defense William Perry is right to warn against the LRSO.

A defensible program would prioritize life-extension of existing warheads, replacement of the Ohio-class submarines (the most survivable and therefore most stabilizing leg of the triad), and a serious overhaul of early-warning and command-and-control systems, which the 2014 Pentagon reviews showed to be in alarming disrepair. It would reconsider, not automatically replace, the Minuteman III ICBMs, whose fixed silos invite launch-on-warning postures that shorten presidential decision time to minutes. And it would shrink, not enlarge, the role of "usable" tactical weapons.

Deterrence does not require parity in every category or novel battlefield options; it requires a secure second-strike capability and adversaries who believe we possess one. Spending a trillion dollars to recreate a Cold War posture — or worse, to lower the threshold of use — buys less safety, not more. Clinton's skepticism is warranted.

The trillion-dollar plan to modernize the American nuclear arsenal has opened a necessary debate, but it is one defined by a dangerous false choice. The core mission remains the deterrence of nuclear war. That mission is not advanced by pursuing smaller, more targeted nuclear weapons—it is profoundly undermined. A responsible modernization must focus on maintaining a safe, secure, and reliable deterrent that makes the use of any nuclear weapon unthinkable, not more tempting. It should therefore exclude new, more usable nuclear capabilities like the proposed air-launched cruise missile and any new low-yield warheads, while concentrating investment on the essential triad’s life-extension and the often-neglected human and cyber systems that prevent accidental war.

The central paradox of nuclear deterrence is that stability rests on the shared belief that any nuclear exchange would be catastrophic. Fielding more “discriminate” low-yield weapons, as President Obama’s plan implicitly allows, chips away at this foundation. Proponents argue that smaller weapons are needed to counter limited nuclear strikes from Russia or other adversaries, providing options short of all-out war. Yet this is escalation control as a mirage. In the fog of a crisis, a battlefield nuclear detonation would shatter the long-standing taboo, making the leap to strategic exchange not less but dramatically more likely. By signaling that a nuclear war could be “manageable,” the United States would lower the psychological threshold for use everywhere, eroding the very discipline that has kept the nuclear peace for over seventy years.

Hillary Clinton’s reported opposition to the new cruise missile aligns with this prudent understanding. The Long-Range Standoff weapon is a classic example of a capability that creates more problems than it solves. A penetrating, nuclear-tipped missile that blurs the line between conventional and nuclear strikes would rattle adversarial early-warning systems and feed fears of a disarming first strike. This compels potential foes to adopt hair-trigger postures, increasing the risk of accidental war. Instead of a new weapon that invites crisis instability, a sound modernization would upgrade the command, control, and communications architecture to insure against cyber intrusion and guarantee that leaders are never forced to decide on retaliation in isolated haste.

Critically, modernization cannot be divorced from cost. A trillion dollars spent on new war-fighting tools would drain resources from conventional forces, diplomacy, and nonproliferation efforts that do the daily work of preventing conflict. The safest, most credible nuclear arsenal is one that is unquestionably capable of devastating retaliation but is not designed to fight a “limited” nuclear war. That means refurbishing aging submarines, bombers, and land-based missiles to remain safe and reliable, while forgoing destabilizing new warheads or delivery vehicles. By excluding the pursuit of smaller, supposedly more usable nuclear options, the United States can stick to its deterrence goals without chasing a mirage of controlled escalation that would only make nuclear war more conceivable.

The trillion-dollar modernization plan raises fundamental questions about what role nuclear weapons should play in American defense strategy. Modernization is inevitable—aging systems must be replaced—but the character of that modernization matters enormously.

First, we should resist the temptation to build more usable, "tactical" nuclear weapons. The argument for smaller, more targeted warheads assumes we could wage limited nuclear war with impunity. This is dangerously wrong. Every use of a nuclear weapon, regardless of yield, crosses a moral and strategic threshold that invites escalation. The deterrence value of existing weapons already deters major attacks; additional low-yield options add little security while increasing accident risk.

Second, modernization should prioritize systems that are unambiguous in their purpose: strategic deterrence. This means the submarine leg of the triad remains essential—ballistic missile submarines are the most survivable deterrent and the hardest to decapitate in a first strike. The bomber leg provides flexibility and redundancy. But we should be skeptical of ground-launched cruise missiles, which blur the line between conventional and nuclear missions and complicate crisis decision-making.

Third, modernization must proceed alongside continued arms control efforts. The New START treaty, which expires in 2021, provides verification mechanisms we cannot afford to lose. Modernization without diplomatic engagement risks a renewed arms race, particularly with Russia, which squanders resources both nations desperately need.

Finally, modernization budgets must be scrutinized fiercely. The nuclear enterprise has suffered from cost overruns and schedule delays. Every dollar spent on unnecessary capability is a dollar not spent on conventional readiness or deficit reduction.

Deterrence works when adversaries believe we have reliable, second-strike capability. It fails when we convince ourselves nuclear weapons solve problems they cannot. The appropriate modernization is modest, survivable, and unambiguously strategic—not a suite of more usable weapons that lower the nuclear threshold.

The debate over the proposed trillion-dollar modernization of the United States nuclear arsenal hinges on a profound strategic question: what types of weapons best deter nuclear war without inadvertently making it more likely? As the nation prepares to replace its aging triad of intercontinental ballistic missiles, submarines, and strategic bombers, the controversy over developing smaller, more targeted nuclear-tipped cruise missiles highlights the delicate balance of modern deterrence.

On one side of the debate is the argument that a credible deterrent requires flexible, survivable options. Proponents of the current administration’s plan, which includes the new Long-Range Standoff (LRSO) cruise missile, argue that adversaries like Russia have adopted doctrines that might contemplate limited nuclear use. To deter such a scenario, the United States must possess weapons that an adversary believes would actually be used in a proportional response. If the U.S. arsenal consists only of massive, high-yield strategic weapons, adversaries might gamble that an American president would be self-deterred from retaliating against a limited strike. From this perspective, modernizing with advanced, targeted capabilities ensures that deterrence remains absolute across all possible rungs of the escalation ladder.

Conversely, a robust chorus of arms control experts and policymakers—including recent skepticism expressed by Hillary Clinton—warns that these tailored capabilities are profoundly destabilizing. The core concern is that smaller, more accurate nuclear weapons lower the threshold for nuclear use. By making nuclear weapons appear more "usable," military planners might be tempted to view them as just another tactical option rather than weapons of absolute last resort. Furthermore, because adversaries cannot easily distinguish between an incoming conventional cruise missile and a nuclear-armed one, the deployment of such dual-use delivery systems dramatically increases the risk of miscalculation. In the fog of a crisis, an adversary might detect a conventional strike and assume a nuclear attack is underway, triggering a full-scale atomic response.

An appropriate modernization strategy must navigate carefully between these competing risks. Upgrading the arsenal should prioritize the safety, security, and reliability of existing forces, focusing heavily on modernizing early warning systems, secure command and control networks, and the highly survivable submarine fleet. These elements form the undisputed backbone of strategic stability.

However, policymakers must remain exceedingly cautious about introducing capabilities that blur the line between conventional and nuclear warfare. The ultimate goal of the nuclear arsenal is to ensure it is never deployed in combat. A modernization program that secures the core triad while rigorously debating the necessity of new "usable" tactical weapons offers the safest path toward maintaining a robust deterrent without sparking a new arms race.

The phrase “nuclear modernization” sounds antiseptic, as if the United States were updating bridges or air-traffic software. But the issue is whether to spend roughly a trillion dollars sustaining and refining weapons capable of destroying cities and threatening civilization. The right answer is not a better nuclear arsenal. It is a smaller one, moving deliberately toward abolition.

The standard defense of nuclear weapons is that they deter nuclear war. That claim cannot be dismissed; the bomb has shaped great-power caution since 1945. But deterrence is also a gamble. It assumes rational leaders, perfect information, reliable technology and permanent luck. The historical record is full of close calls, false alarms, accidents and misread signals. A system that must work flawlessly forever is not safe.

Modernizing for safety and security is one thing. No one should want aging warheads or vulnerable command systems. But the current plan goes far beyond custodianship. It aims to preserve all three legs of the triad for decades, develop new delivery systems and, in some cases, make weapons more precise and supposedly more usable. That is not disarmament by another name. It is recommitment.

The United States should exclude new nuclear cruise missiles, new low-yield options and any weapons designed to fight a “limited” nuclear war. It should cancel plans that expand the role of nuclear weapons and declare that the sole purpose of the arsenal is to deter nuclear attack. It should adopt a no-first-use policy, take missiles off high alert and reduce deployed warheads below New START levels, inviting Russia to reciprocate but not allowing Russian obstruction to dictate every American moral choice.

Critics will say this is naïve because Russia, China and North Korea will not follow. But American overinvestment in nuclear weapons has never persuaded others to abandon theirs. In fact, it often strengthens the argument of hard-liners abroad who claim that nuclear arms are the currency of power. A serious modernization would modernize American thinking. It would recognize climate change, pandemics, cyberattacks and loose nuclear material as security threats at least as urgent as building a new generation of doomsday machines. The United States should maintain the minimum force necessary while leading, visibly and credibly, toward a world in which nuclear weapons are stigmatized, reduced and eventually eliminated.

The trillion-dollar modernization plan is a strategic and fiscal mistake that the next administration should pare back substantially. The United States already possesses roughly 4,500 warheads in its active stockpile, deliverable by a triad of submarines, bombers, and intercontinental ballistic missiles. No plausible adversary doubts our capacity to incinerate them many times over. What deters nuclear war is not the freshness of our warheads but the certainty of retaliation, and that certainty is overdetermined.

The most dangerous element of the current plan is the new B61-12 guided gravity bomb and the proposed Long-Range Standoff (LRSO) cruise missile. A cruise missile launched in a crisis cannot be identified as nuclear or conventional by the country it overflies; the ambiguity invites catastrophic miscalculation.

Mrs. Clinton's reported skepticism about smaller, more targeted weapons is therefore well-placed, and it sits awkwardly with President Obama's otherwise admirable Prague agenda. A sensible modernization would do three things. Deterrence does not require parity in every category or a bespoke warhead for every contingency. Pouring a trillion dollars into rebuilding every leg of the triad while developing more usable weapons does the opposite: it signals to Russia and China that we intend to fight, not merely deter, a nuclear war.

At the same time, it should pursue arms control, crisis communication channels with Russia and China, and policies that reduce reliance on launch-on-warning. The safest arsenal is not the largest or the most versatile. It is the one least likely to be used by mistake, miscalculation or misplaced faith in controllable nuclear war.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modernization must focus on upgrading communication, warning, and command systems to withstand cyber attacks and satellite failures that could lead to rushed or erroneous decision-making.
  2. 02The U.S. should prioritize the most stabilizing delivery systems, such as submarines and bombers, while re-evaluating the necessity of land-based missiles that create pressure for rapid launch.
  3. 03New weapons that blur the line between conventional and nuclear conflict, such as nuclear-armed cruise missiles or low-yield warheads, should be excluded to avoid lowering the threshold for nuclear use.
  4. 04Deterrence should be maintained through a survivable force capable of a devastating response rather than an elaborate menu of tactical strike options.
  5. 05Safety and stability should be enhanced through rigorous personnel training, independent reviews of launch procedures, and active pursuit of arms control and crisis communication channels.
