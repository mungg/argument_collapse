Diversified Claude

"The Araki Yasusada hoax is not a brilliant poststructuralist provocation but a shameful act of literary fraud that exploits the suffering of Hiroshima survivors for aesthetic consumption."

**Stop Calling It Brilliant**

Marjorie Perloff's essay strains throughout to maintain a tone of high-toned analytic disinterest, but underneath the Foucault and the Pound parallels and the dutiful citations of Tanikawa Shuntaro, what it amounts to is an extended apology for a particularly nasty piece of literary tourism. I do not think Kent Johnson has "performed an invaluable service." I think he has done something shabby, and the fact that he has done it cleverly does not redeem it.

Let us be clear about what Johnson actually wrote. He wrote, in the first person, a poem in which the speaker stumbles upon the severed head of his own daughter, killed by the bomb dropped on Hiroshima. He wrote letters from a hospital ward of a man dying of radiation-related cancer. He wrote the man's grief for his wife and infant daughter, both incinerated on August 6, 1945. And he affixed to these inventions a biography of an actual category of human being—the *hibakusha*—and submitted them to journals as the discovered manuscripts of such a man.

Perloff treats this as a clever bit of poststructuralist play, a "perfect simulacrum," an "invaluable" provocation against essentialism. I find the description grotesque. There is a difference between Macpherson inventing an ancient Gaelic bard and an American poet inventing a recently dead survivor of a still-living atrocity. There is a difference between Pound's *Homage to Sextus Propertius*—which Perloff repeatedly invokes as a parallel—and Johnson's Yasusada. Pound did not pretend to be Propertius. He signed his own name. He layered his frigidaires into Latin elegy openly, as Ezra Pound, and dared the reader to follow. That is irony. Johnson's procedure is not irony. It is impersonation.

Perloff's defense, when she offers one, is that Johnson seeded his text with clues—the scubadivers, the impossible chronology of Barthes, the misnamed Hiroshima University—so that any sufficiently attentive reader would catch on. This is the standard alibi of every clever hoaxer: I left signals; you should have noticed. But the signals were not noticed. They were not noticed by *American Poetry Review*, by *Conjunctions*, by *Grand Street*, by *Stand*, by the anonymous Wesleyan reader, by Ron Silliman staying up nights with the poems. If the clues were so unmistakable that the work was always already declaring itself as fiction, then the editors would have read it as fiction. They didn't. They read it as testimony. Which means either Johnson's clues were too faint to function as he claims they were intended, or Johnson was happy to have them go unnoticed for as long as the credit kept accruing. Either way the alibi fails.

The deeper offense, though, is not against the editors. It is against the witnesses. There exists a real literature of Hiroshima—Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko, the tanka poets in Nakano's anthology whose lines Perloff somewhat condescendingly dismisses as "more notable for their subject matter than for their poetic quality." These writers paid for the right to say what they say. They paid in radiation sickness, in the loss of children, in decades of survivor's burden. To produce a more "sophisticated" Hiroshima poetry—more elliptical, more Spicer-inflected, more pleasingly fragmented for the contemporary American avant-garde—and to do so by puppeting a fictional survivor is to treat their actual suffering as raw material for a literary career move. It does not matter how skilled the puppetry is. Skill is not the question.

Perloff almost glimpses this when she observes that Yasusada's poems make "no demand" on the American reader, that they offer a manageable, aestheticized Hiroshima that lets us "concentrate on the effectiveness of Johnson's fiction" rather than wrestle with responsibility. But having registered the point, she fails to follow it. The reason Yasusada makes no demand is precisely that he isn't real. Real survivors make demands. Their language is sometimes flat, sometimes polemical, sometimes embarrassing in its directness ("Help me help me / Water / Water / Somewhere / Someone"). It refuses the elegance the literary marketplace would prefer. Johnson's Yasusada gives the marketplace the elegance it wants—Spicer with a death count attached. That is not a critique of Orientalism. That is Orientalism's most refined product.

I will grant Perloff this: the affair does expose something about the American poetry economy that deserves exposure. The journals' uneven appetite for contemporary Japan—voracious for hibakusha testimony, indifferent to Shiraishi or Tanikawa—is genuinely damning, and her catalogue of *APR*'s translation choices is the most useful part of her essay. The editors are not blameless. They have constructed an economy of curated otherness in which Yasusada was a predictable arrival.

But the diagnosis that they are complicit does not absolve the perpetrator. A confidence man who exploits a community's weakness for sentimental narratives is not engaged in social criticism, even if his fraud incidentally reveals the weakness. He is engaged in fraud. The lesson is not that we should celebrate his ingenuity. The lesson is that he ought not to have done it, and that the editors and readers who fell for it ought to read more carefully, and that we should now spend our attention on the actual living Japanese poets whose absence from our magazines made Johnson's fabrication thinkable in the first place.

Perloff would agree with that last sentence, I think. I wish her essay had gotten there sooner, and without quite so much admiration for the man who made the detour necessary.

  3. 03By creating a 'sophisticated' version of Hiroshima poetry that caters to avant-garde tastes, Johnson treats the actual trauma of the hibakusha as mere raw material for a career move.

  6. 06The literary community should shift its focus away from celebrating Johnson’s ingenuity and toward engaging with actual contemporary Japanese poets.
Diversified GPT

"The Araki Yasusada affair should be judged as a moral fraud and a breach of the literary contract rather than celebrated as a theoretical achievement or dismissed as a harmless hoax."

It is tempting to treat the Yasusada affair as an elegant theoretical event: the author-function imploding, translation unmasked as fiction, authenticity exposed as a commodity. But there is a less glamorous word for what happened: fraud. I use it not in the sense of criminal prosecution, which would be absurd, but in the ordinary moral sense. Editors were told that poems came from a Japanese Hiroshima survivor’s notebooks. They did not. Payments were made, space was allocated, readers were misled, and symbolic value was obtained under false pretenses.

Poetry operates on a fragile economy. The sums are small, but the stakes are not. A page in American Poetry Review, Grand Street, Conjunctions, Stand, or Aerial is a form of currency. It brings reputation, invitations, possible book publication, academic attention, and influence. When a fabricated identity is used to secure that space, someone else is displaced. Perhaps an actual translator of Japanese poetry. Perhaps an American poet submitting under his own unfashionable name. Perhaps no one identifiable, but the principle remains: scarcity gives deception weight.

Marjorie Perloff argues persuasively that calling the deception “criminal” exaggerates the matter. Yet the demand for return of payment is not merely comic. It expresses the fact that publication is a contract, even when informal. The editor agrees to present work to readers under certain conditions. The contributor agrees that the representation is truthful unless otherwise signaled. Pseudonyms complicate but do not abolish this contract. “George Eliot” did not claim to be a newly discovered Japanese survivor of atomic bombing. “Araki Yasusada” did.

Some defenders will say that the editors should have known. Indeed they should have. The anachronisms were glaring. But editorial gullibility does not vindicate authorial deception. If I leave my door unlocked, theft remains theft. If a journal fails to check a biography, the fabrication remains fabrication. Responsibility is not a zero-sum game.

Still, the editors’ responsibility is considerable. The Yasusada materials were not submitted as ordinary lyric poems. They came with a complex apparatus of translation, biography, archival discovery, and historical witness. Such apparatus should trigger verification. The more a submission asks readers to rely on extraliterary claims, the more those claims matter. If a poem is printed simply as “by Kent Johnson,” readers judge it one way. If it is printed as “from the notebooks of Araki Yasusada, Hiroshima survivor,” they judge it through another frame. The frame was part of the product.

This is why aesthetic defenses are insufficient. A beautiful forged signature is still forged. The poem may survive as literature; the publication event remains compromised. We can admire “Telescope with Urn” and still object to the way its pathos was financed by a nonexistent daughter’s ashes. We can analyze the project as conceptual art and still ask why the concept required deceiving small magazines rather than declaring itself.

The answer, of course, is that declaration would have weakened the effect. “Here are poems by an American writer pretending to be a Japanese survivor” would not have produced the same awe, the same hush, the same thrill of discovery. The hoax needed belief. It converted the moral capital of Hiroshima into literary capital. That conversion is the troubling fact.

Perloff’s strongest contribution is to show that the conversion succeeded because American readers desired this particular product. Yasusada combined victimhood with avant-garde legitimacy. He allowed editors to publish an “other” who sounded uncannily like the most advanced version of themselves. This is not merely a multicultural problem, though multicultural pieties played a role. It is also a market problem. Literary markets reward novelty, marginality, and compelling backstory. The unknown survivor with secret notebooks is almost too perfect a commodity.

The small-press world often imagines itself outside the market because it is poor. That is a mistake. It has markets of prestige rather than money, scarcity rather than sales, symbolic capital rather than profit. Hoaxes flourish in such markets because the rewards are reputational and the safeguards weak. A commercial publisher may have lawyers and fact-checkers; a little magazine has enthusiasm and a post office box. Yet little magazines also shape taste. Their informality does not absolve them.

What should happen now? First, the record should be corrected everywhere the poems appeared. Not buried apologies, but clear notices in subsequent issues. Second, any book publication should identify the work as pseudonymous fiction or literary hoax, not as translation. Third, editors should disclose what they know about the submitter’s role. If Kent Johnson was agent, author, or intermediary, the distinction matters. Mystification may be part of the artwork, but journals are not obliged to protect it after being used.

Fourth, the affair should lead to better practices for translated work. Source texts, permissions, biographical verification, consultation with language experts: these are not bureaucratic insults but basic respect. They protect writers, translators, editors, and readers alike.

Finally, we should resist two bad endings. One bad ending is punitive hysteria, in which the author is denounced as a criminal and the poems are treated as poison. The other is avant-garde self-congratulation, in which the deception is celebrated as a brilliant exposure of bourgeois authenticity and everyone enjoys being scandalized. Both endings avoid accountability. The first refuses to read; the second refuses to judge.

Yasusada has value now as evidence: evidence of desire, carelessness, ingenuity, and misuse. Whether the poems have lasting literary value is a separate question, one time may answer. But the episode has already taught us something about the economy of trust. In poetry, where almost no one is paid what labor deserves, trust is one of the few currencies left. To counterfeit it is not a game without victims.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The fabrication of a Hiroshima survivor's identity was a form of deception used to obtain symbolic value and prestige under false pretenses.
  2. 02By using a fake identity to secure limited publication space, the author displaced actual translators and poets who operate within a fragile literary economy.
  3. 03The hoax succeeded by exploitative conversion of the moral capital of historical tragedy into literary capital for personal gain.
  4. 04Editorial gullibility and the failure to spot glaring anachronisms do not absolve the author of the responsibility for deliberate fabrication.
  5. 05The incident highlights a market problem where literary prestige is unfairly awarded to 'authentic' backstories that satisfy the desires of the Western avant-garde.
  6. 06To restore the economy of trust, journals must correct the record, disclose the author's true role, and implement stricter verification standards for translated works.
  7. 07The literary community should avoid both punitive hysteria and avant-garde self-congratulation, instead holding the author accountable for counterfeiting trust.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Araki Yasusada hoax is ethically problematic because it fabricates a survivor's voice, effectively prioritizing aesthetic sophistication over the integrity of actual trauma testimony."

A different perspective on the Yasusada affair focuses not on its literary significance but on its ethical implications for the study of trauma. The Hiroshima bombing is not merely a historical event; it is a wound that continues to shape Japanese consciousness and international relations. Kent Johnson's decision to invent a hibakusha poet deserves scrutiny on grounds that go beyond literary politics.

The ethics of representing trauma are complex. Survivors of horrific events have a right to tell their stories, but they also have a right to the integrity of their testimonies. When Johnson invented Yasusada, he was not merely creating a fictional poet; he was fabricating a survivor's voice. This fabrication has consequences for actual survivors and their communities.

Consider what actual hibakusha have said about their experiences. Hideko Tamura Snider, whom Perloff mentions briefly, writes in One Sunny Day of her family's loss and grief without political commentary. Seishi Toyota, the tanka poet, describes his radiation sickness as "karma and life-long work." These testimonies are particular, grounded in individual experience, resistant to generalization. They do not provide the kind of transcendent meaning that Johnson gives to his invented survivor.

Johnson's Yasusada is a better victim than any actual survivor could be. He combines trauma with avant-garde sophistication; he speaks in fragments that invite interpretation; his suffering is refracted through multiple traditions without ever being reduced to simple horror. This is, in a sense, what the literary establishment wanted: trauma that is aesthetically legible, politically correct, and formally challenging.

But real trauma is rarely aesthetically legible. Real survivors do not write poems that satisfydesires for refined suffering. They write poems that are awkward, repetitive, too close to the event, or sometimes not at all. The hibakusha poetry tradition Perloff describes is often more notable for its documentary value than its artistic achievement. This is not a criticism; it is the nature of testimony.

By inventing a hibakusha poet, Johnsonimplicitly criticizes actual survivors for failing to meet aesthetic expectations. His Yasusada is better trauma than reality provides. This is the ethical dimension that Perloff's essay, for all its sophistication, does not fully address.

The response might be that Johnson's hoax is a parody of multiculturalist expectations, a way of exposing how those expectations distort our understanding of trauma. But parody requires recognition, and there is no evidence that Johnson's readers recognized the parody. They embraced Yasusada as authentic, not as a critique. The hoax thus becomes a practical endorsement of the very expectations it might have mocked.

I do not wish to be precious about this. Literary hoaxes have a long history, and most of them cause no real harm. But the Hiroshima bombing is not just any historical event, and fabricating survivor testimony is not just any literary game. The victims of that bombing deserve better than to have their suffering used as raw material for sophisticated literary play, even when that play is intended as critique.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fabricating a survivor's voice violates the right of actual victims to the integrity and particularity of their own historical testimonies.
  2. 02The fictional Yasusada functions as a 'better victim' by providing the literary establishment with an aesthetically legible and formally challenging version of trauma that real survivors rarely produce.
  3. 03By creating a more sophisticated poetic persona than actual hibakusha, the author implicitly criticizes real survivors for failing to meet modern aesthetic expectations.
  4. 04The hoax fails as a parody of multiculturalism because readers embraced the persona as authentic rather than recognizing it as a critique of their own expectations.
  5. 05Using the specific tragedy of the Hiroshima bombing as raw material for literary play is an inappropriate use of profound human suffering.
Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada hoax is not an 'invaluable service' to literary theory but a grotesque and unethical appropriation of historical trauma that mocks the sanctity of human suffering."

Marjorie Perloff’s dissection of the Araki Yasusada affair is as sharp as we have come to expect from one of our most distinguished critics of contemporary poetry. She deftly traces the ways in which the American literary establishment, hungry for a very specific flavor of victimhood, was easily duped by an exoticizing hoax. Yet, for all her brilliant textual analysis, Perloff arrives at a conclusion that is deeply, chillingly wrong. To declare that Kent Johnson has performed an “invaluable service” by inventing a fake Hiroshima survivor is to elevate a clever literary parlor game above basic human decency.

Perloff writes that calling this impersonation a “criminal act” is “absurd,” citing the long history of literary pseudonyms from James Macpherson’s Ossian to the present. But Araki Yasusada is not Ossian. Ossian was a romantic fabrication of an ancient, mythic past. The atomic bombing of Hiroshima is not a myth, nor is it ancient history. It is an event of unimaginable horror that occurred in living memory, leaving hundreds of thousands of real people dead and many more suffering from the agonizing, lingering effects of radiation sickness. To appropriate this specific, historical trauma—to invent a dead daughter whose ashes are placed in an urn, or a mad daughter with a severed head—is not a victimless exercise in Foucauldian discourse theory. It is a grotesque act of grave-robbing.

What Perloff misses in her otherwise astute critique of the poetry market’s demand for the “authentic” is the ethical dimension of that authenticity. When a reader encounters a poem by Toge Sankichi or any actual *hibakusha* (survivor), the moral weight of the text derives not just from its linguistic arrangement on the page, but from the blood and ash out of which it was forged. We read such testimonies with a reverence born of our shared humanity and our recognition of the author's real suffering. By synthesizing this suffering into a pastiche of Jack Spicer and Roland Barthes, Johnson has not merely tricked a few gullible editors; he has mocked the very concept of historical witness.

Perloff rightly points out that the Yasusada poems function as a kind of “New Orientalism,” playing to American guilt and fantasies of Zen-like stoicism. But why does she then let the perpetrator off the hook? If the poems are indeed Orientalist fantasies—and they clearly are—then the act of writing and submitting them under the guise of a Japanese victim is a profound act of cultural imperialism. It is the literary equivalent of yellowface minstrelsy, updating the old stereotypes for a postmodern, MFA-educated audience. The fact that the editors of *American Poetry Review* and *Stand* were fooled does not excuse the malice or the sheer arrogance of the deception.

To excuse this hoax because it "challenges many dubious notions" about authorship and the "New Sentence" is to say that it is perfectly fine to exploit the Holocaust or the Middle Passage if it helps us make a neat point about literary theory. In the end, the Yasusada affair does not teach us anything we didn't already know about the gullibility of editors or the constructed nature of the author. What it does teach us, sadly, is that in the hyper-competitive, theory-addled world of the contemporary avant-garde, nothing is sacred—not even the ashes of Hiroshima.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike mythic literary fabrications of the past, impersonating a Hiroshima survivor exploits an event of unimaginable horror that remains within living memory.
  2. 02The moral weight of survivor testimony is derived from real suffering, and synthesizing that trauma into a stylistic pastiche devalues the concept of historical witness.
  3. 03The hoax constitutes a form of cultural imperialism or 'literary yellowface' by using a fake Japanese persona to update Orientalist stereotypes for a postmodern audience.
  4. 04Prioritizing the debunking of literary theories over the respect due to victims of mass tragedy reflects a chilling lack of basic human decency within the avant-garde.
