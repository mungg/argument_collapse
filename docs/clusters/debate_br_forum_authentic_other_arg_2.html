Diversified MiniMax

"Marjorie Perloff’s critique of the Yasusada hoax is intellectually inconsistent because it unfairly indicts multiculturalism for a failure that actually stems from a lack of scholarly rigor and an ethnocentric preference for avant-garde aesthetics over authentic testimonial poetry."

Let me offer a dissenting view on Marjorie Perloff's otherwise masterful analysis. Her essay performs a certain sleight of hand: it begins as a critique of Kent Johnson's hoax but ends up indicting the literary establishment, multiculturalism, and ultimately the very possibility of cross-cultural literary exchange. This trajectory is intellectually suspect and practically dangerous.

Perloff wants to have it both ways. On one hand, she praises Johnson's poetic achievement — "a brilliant job in inventing a world" — while on the other hand condemning his moral choices. But can we separate the two? Is it possible to recognize artistic brilliance while deploring its deployment? The essay never convincingly answers this question because it cannot decide who is really on trial: Johnson or his readers.

The critical weakness is Perloff's deployment of Edward Said's Orientalism framework. Said was describing a specific historical relationship between Western imperial powers and their colonial subjects. Can that framework apply to the contemporary American poetry scene? The power dynamics are not equivalent. American poets reading Japanese poetry are not exercising imperial domination; they are attempting, however clumsily, to engage with another literary tradition. The fact that they do so imperfectly — with more enthusiasm than knowledge — does not make them Orientalists in Said's rigorous sense.

More damaging is Perloff's treatment of multiculturalism. She writes that "the New Multiculturalism, far from countering the Orientalism Said decried as long as twenty years ago, has turned out to be its inadvertent promoter." This is a serious accusation, but the essay provides no evidence that multiculturalist critics have promoted Orientalist stereotypes. Indeed, the Yasusada affair might better be read as a failure of multiculturalism: a situation where editors sought cultural diversity without doing the scholarly work that genuine multicultural engagement requires. The failure is not of multiculturalism but of its superficial imitation.

What would genuine engagement look like? It would mean, at minimum, verifying basic facts about the culture in question. It would mean reading actual Japanese poetry rather than projecting fantasies onto invented poets. It would mean treating Japanese writers as full participants in contemporary literary culture rather than as exotic suppliers of spiritual wisdom. These are the obligations Perloff herself articulates at the essay's close — and they are sound obligations. But they are obligations of multiculturalism, not its antithesis.

I also question Perloff's dismissal of the "hibakusha" poetry tradition. She quotes several tanka that she finds "more notable for their subject matter than for their poetic quality." This judgment is too quick. The constraints of tanka form applied to nuclear horror produce a tension that can be genuinely powerful. Toge Sankichi's work is more sophisticated than Perloff allows. The issue is not that Hiroshima poetry lacks aesthetic merit; the issue is that Perloff's standards for that merit are ethnocentric — she values the fragmented, ironic, Language-poetry-inflected Yasusada poems over the direct testimonial mode of actual hibakusha writers.

Johnson's hoax, whatever its ultimate significance, has created a situation where the most discussed Japanese-related poetry in American journals turns out to be a fabrication. That is both a commentary on the poetry world and a lost opportunity for genuine cultural exchange. Perloff's essay helps us understand how we got here, but its moral framework is too comfortable. The guilt is not all on one side.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Perloff fails to reconcile her praise for Kent Johnson’s artistic brilliance with her condemnation of his moral deception, leaving it unclear whether the author or the audience is the primary target of her critique.
  2. 02The application of Edward Said’s 'Orientalism' framework is inappropriate here because the power dynamics of the contemporary American poetry scene do not equate to the imperial domination Said originally described.
  3. 03The Yasusada affair represents a superficial imitation of multiculturalism rather than a failure of the movement itself, as genuine multicultural engagement would require the very scholarly verification Perloff advocates.
  4. 04Perloff’s dismissal of actual 'hibakusha' poetry as aesthetically inferior reveals an ethnocentric bias that privileges fragmented, Language-poetry styles over the powerful directness of traditional Japanese forms.
  5. 05By framing the hoax as an indictment of cross-cultural exchange, Perloff overlooks the shared responsibility of editors and critics to treat international writers as full participants in contemporary culture rather than exotic sources of wisdom.
Diversified MiniMax

"Marjorie Perloff’s critique of the Yasusada hoax is flawed because she mischaracterizes the poems as simple Orientalist fantasies and wrongly blames multiculturalism for a deception that actually succeeded by exploiting the very lack of genuine multiculturalist inquiry."

Marjorie Perloff's essay is compelling in its detail but, I would argue, ultimately flawed in its central argument. Her claim that the Yasusada hoax reveals a new form of Orientalism propagated by multiculturalism is both too broad and too narrow — too broad in its critique of multiculturalism generally, too narrow in its refusal to examine the hoax's own textual strategies.

Let me address the narrower point first. Perloff describes Johnson's poems as "a perfect recipe for a new Orientalism," but she never seriously examines whether the poems actually function in Orientalist ways. The essay quotes several poems at length — "Telescope with Urn," the mad daughter poem, the renga fragments — but it does not analyze their rhetoric in detail. Instead, it asserts that the poems satisfy "our longing for a Japan rather like that of Barthes's Empire of Signs."

Is this longing actually satisfied? The poems are full of dislocations and tensions that resist easy consumption. "Telescope with Urn" juxtaposes astronomical discourse with Zen iconography in ways that are not harmonized but held in productive tension. The mad daughter poem moves from horror to bathos in a single stanza, undercutting the elegiac register with the flat statement "Squatting, I pulled the turnip up by the root." The renga fragments include lines like "The voices of the sorority girls sing of fucking in a plaintive way" that refuse any comfortable Orientalist coherence.

These are not the techniques of traditional Orientalism, which works through idealization and stabilization of difference. Johnson is doing something more complex: he is creating a Japan that is both available for projection and resistant to it. The question is whether this complexity is lost on readers who want the fantasy, or whether it remains available as a critical resource.

The broader point concerns multiculturalism. Perloff implies that the literary establishment's embrace of Yasusada represents the failure of multiculturalist criticism, but this conclusion does not follow. The editors who were taken in by the hoax were not following the advice of multiculturalist scholars; they were gratifying their own desires for exotic authenticity. Genuine multiculturalist criticism would have asked precisely the questions that went unasked: What is the actual state of Japanese poetry? Who are its major practitioners? What forms and traditions do they work with? These questions were not asked because the editors wanted a fantasy, not a relationship.

Johnson's hoax, I would suggest, is better understood as a failure of the very multiculturalist critique that Perloff invokes. The hoax worked because it satisfied a desire for the authentic Other that multiculturalist criticism has been trying to dismantle for decades. Johnson understood this desire and fed it, but in doing so he made visible its persistence and its power. The hoax is thus not a vindication of Orientalism but a symptom of multiculturalism's unfinished work.

Perloff's essay, for all its learning, does not adequately reckon with this complexity. It prefers the comfort of assigning blame to the discomfort of analysis.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Yasusada poems utilize dislocations, tensions, and jarring linguistic registers that resist the stabilization and idealization typical of traditional Orientalism.
  2. 02The hoax's success was not a failure of multiculturalist theory but rather a result of editors prioritizing their own desires for exotic authenticity over actual multiculturalist scholarship.
  3. 03Genuine multiculturalist criticism would have prevented the hoax by asking the specific questions about Japanese poetic traditions and contemporary practitioners that the editors ignored.
  4. 04The hoax serves as a symptom of multiculturalism’s unfinished work by exposing the persistent power of the Western desire for an 'authentic Other.'
