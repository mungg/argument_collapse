"To stabilize Egypt, regional allies and neighbors must shift from Western-led conditional aid to a model of direct Arab investment and regional economic partnership."

The best thing Egypt’s neighbors and allies can do is stop treating the country as a ward of Washington or Brussels and start treating it as the central Arab economy it is. Egypt will not be stabilized by Western consultants, conditional grants or photo opportunities. It needs Arab capital, regional trade and confidence from countries whose own prosperity is tied to Egypt’s success.

The Gulf states should take the lead. Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait and the United Arab Emirates have the financial resources to prevent a disorderly economic slide. They should provide deposits to Egypt’s central bank, invest in infrastructure and help finance fuel and food imports during the transition. This is not charity. A collapsing Egypt would export instability through migration pressures, ideological radicalization and regional uncertainty. A recovering Egypt would be a huge market, a source of skilled labor and a political anchor for the Arab world.

But Gulf aid should not be designed to purchase Egyptian politics. Backing one faction against another — the military, the Muslim Brotherhood, liberals or remnants of the old regime — would deepen polarization. The better model is visible, practical investment: electricity generation, ports, housing, public transportation and agricultural supply chains. Egyptians need to see that the revolution has not led only to shortages and inflation. Large labor-intensive projects can put people to work quickly and restore a sense of national momentum.

Turkey also has a role. Its recent experience combining electoral politics, private enterprise and a large Muslim-majority society gives it credibility in Egypt that many Western governments lack. Turkish firms can invest in manufacturing, construction and retail. Turkish officials can share lessons on encouraging small and medium-sized businesses, managing tourism and expanding exports. This should not become a lecture about “the Turkish model,” which Egyptians rightly resent, but a partnership between two major regional economies.

Western governments should support this regional approach by guaranteeing loans, encouraging private investment and easing trade barriers. They should also avoid sudden punitive moves that could accelerate Egypt’s economic panic. Human rights and democratic standards matter, but sanctions that damage ordinary Egyptians would be counterproductive.

Egypt’s problem is not simply a shortage of money; it is a shortage of confidence. If Arab and regional partners demonstrate that Egypt remains investable, open and central to the Middle East’s future, they will do more for stability than any number of diplomatic statements.

  3. 03Turkey should leverage its unique credibility to partner with Egypt on manufacturing, small business management, and export expansion.
  4. 04Western governments should pivot their role toward guaranteeing loans and easing trade barriers rather than imposing punitive sanctions that harm ordinary citizens.
  5. 05The primary goal of regional partners should be to restore global confidence in Egypt's status as an investable and central Arab economy.

"Egypt's allies should support the nation's economic recovery and stability by prioritizing practical cooperation and respect for sovereignty over paternalistic political conditions and donor-driven reform plans."

Egypt’s allies can help most by doing less of what they usually do. The country does need economic support, but it does not need another round of paternalistic advice, political conditionality and donor-driven reform plans. Egyptians have just overthrown an autocrat long praised abroad as a source of stability. They are entitled to suspicion when the same foreign governments now arrive with instructions for how their revolution should be managed.

The first priority should be to stop the bleeding in ways that respect Egyptian sovereignty. Tourism is one of the quickest sources of foreign currency and employment, so foreign governments should calibrate travel warnings carefully and avoid exaggerating risks for political reasons. Airlines, tour operators and cultural institutions can help restore confidence by returning to Cairo, Luxor, Aswan and the Red Sea resorts as conditions permit. Millions of Egyptians depend directly or indirectly on tourism; reviving it would matter more than many aid conferences.

Second, allies should return money that belongs to Egyptians. Assets connected to Mubarak-era corruption should be traced, frozen and repatriated through credible legal processes. This is not only financially useful; it is morally important. Egyptians should not be asked to borrow from international institutions while wealth taken under the old regime sits in foreign banks or real estate.

Third, trade access is better than charity. Europe and the United States should lower barriers to Egyptian exports, especially in sectors that employ large numbers of workers. Regional neighbors should make it easier for Egyptian labor, goods and services to circulate legally. If allies want Egypt to recover, they should buy from Egyptians, invest with Egyptians and hire Egyptians.

Fourth, outside powers should avoid using aid as a weapon in Egypt’s internal political struggle. The new parliament includes Islamists because Egyptians voted for them. Foreign governments may disagree with some of their views, and they should speak clearly about universal rights, but threatening collective punishment would feed nationalist resentment and weaken the very liberals outsiders claim to support. Engagement should be broad and pragmatic.

Finally, military aid deserves rethinking. Egypt’s stability will not come from more weapons. If foreign governments insist on continuing security assistance, they should at least separate it from urgent economic help and make the accounts public. The revolution was about dignity as well as bread. Allies who remember both will be more useful than those who remember only their own strategic anxieties.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Foreign governments and institutions should revive the tourism sector by calibrating travel warnings carefully and restoring travel services to the region.
  2. 02Allies should trace, freeze, and repatriate assets connected to Mubarak-era corruption to provide Egypt with its own rightful financial resources.
  3. 03Western and regional powers should lower trade barriers and increase investment to prioritize economic integration over charitable aid.
  4. 04Outside powers must avoid using financial aid as a political weapon or a means of collective punishment against the democratically elected parliament.
  5. 05Security assistance should be decoupled from economic aid and made transparent to ensure that stability is not prioritized over the dignity of the Egyptian people.

"The economic recovery and stability of Egypt depend primarily on deep strategic integration and aggressive investment from its Arab neighbors in the Gulf rather than conditional aid from Western institutions."

When discussing how to salvage Egypt’s battered economy, the conversation almost inevitably turns to Washington, Brussels, and the International Monetary Fund. But this Western-centric view ignores the most crucial actors in Egypt's immediate future: its Arab neighbors. The ultimate salvation of the Egyptian economy will not come from conditional Western aid packages, but from deep, strategic economic integration with the cash-rich nations of the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC). If allies want to help Egypt get back on its feet, the Gulf states must take the lead.

Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and the United Arab Emirates have a massive, existential stake in Egyptian stability. As the most populous Arab nation and a historical cultural heavyweight, a collapsing Egypt would send shockwaves throughout the Middle East, threatening the security of the Gulf itself. Fortunately, the GCC countries possess the sovereign wealth necessary to plug Egypt's immediate financing gaps without the paternalistic political conditions often attached to Western money.

However, Gulf allies must move beyond simply depositing billions into Egypt’s Central Bank to prop up the currency, though such short-term measures have been necessary. The long-term solution requires transitioning from aid to aggressive foreign direct investment. Egypt desperately needs infrastructure, energy development, and jobs. The Gulf has excess capital looking for profitable regional markets. By investing heavily in Egyptian real estate, manufacturing, and agriculture, Arab allies can jumpstart the economy from within.

Simultaneously, Egypt must signal to its regional allies that it is open for business. The chaotic political environment under the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces has spooked regional investors. The newly elected parliament needs to swiftly pass legislation protecting foreign investments from arbitrary expropriation and red tape. Furthermore, the Gulf can assist by absorbing more of Egypt’s highly educated but unemployed youth into their own labor markets, increasing the flow of remittances back to Cairo. By treating Egypt not as a charity case for the West, but as a vital partner for regional Arab capital, neighbors can ensure both economic recovery and long-term geopolitical stability.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Gulf Cooperation Council nations have a direct existential and security interest in preventing the collapse of the Arab world's most populous nation.
  2. 02The Gulf states possess the necessary sovereign wealth to provide immediate financial support without the restrictive political conditions imposed by Western donors.
  3. 03Allies must transition from short-term currency stabilization to long-term foreign direct investment in Egypt's infrastructure, manufacturing, and agriculture sectors.
  4. 04Egypt needs to implement legislative reforms to protect foreign investments and reduce bureaucracy to attract regional capital.
  5. 05Gulf nations can bolster the Egyptian economy by absorbing more Egyptian workers into their labor markets to increase the flow of remittances.

"To support Egypt’s recovery and stability, allies should provide practical, coordinated assistance focused on maintaining public order and preventing economic collapse rather than pushing for immediate ideological or fiscal reforms."

Egypt’s friends should begin with a hard truth: no economic plan will succeed if the state itself appears to be dissolving. Since the revolution, Egyptians have faced not only political uncertainty but also crime, labor unrest, pressure on the currency, sectarian tensions and insecurity in places such as Sinai. Tourism and investment depend on confidence, and confidence depends on order. Helping Egypt maintain stability is therefore not a reactionary goal; it is a precondition for any democratic transition.

The priority should be support for basic state functions, especially in ways that do not revive the old police state. The United States and Europe can offer training in community policing, crowd control that avoids lethal force, forensic work and judicial procedure. The point should be professional law enforcement under law, not the rebuilding of the feared Interior Ministry as it existed under Mubarak. Border security and counterterrorism cooperation in Sinai are also legitimate concerns for Egypt, Israel and Gaza, and should be handled quietly and respectfully.

Second, allies should help Egypt keep food and fuel available. Bread prices have political meaning in Egypt, and sudden shortages would be explosive. Emergency financing for wheat imports, fuel needs and the protection of the poorest households would buy time for a more legitimate government to make longer-term reforms. Pushing immediate austerity in the name of fiscal virtue would be a mistake.

Third, outside powers should maintain relationships with all centers of authority, including the military council, the newly elected parliament and emerging presidential candidates. Some will find this distasteful, but isolating the army while it still controls the transition could produce exactly the confrontation that would frighten investors and citizens alike. Engagement should be firm: a clear transfer to civilian rule, respect for peaceful protest and protection of minorities. But firmness is different from humiliation.

Finally, Egypt’s neighbors should avoid turning the country into an arena for regional rivalry. Gulf money, Turkish advice, American aid and European trade can all help, but not if each is tied to a competing ideological project. Egypt is too large and too important to be treated as a prize.

The best assistance now is practical, coordinated and modest in its ambitions: prevent economic free fall, preserve public order, protect the peace with Israel and give Egyptians the breathing space to build politics that can endure.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Allies should prioritize professionalizing Egypt's law enforcement and judicial procedures to restore the public order necessary for tourism and investment.
  2. 02International partners must provide emergency financing for food and fuel to prevent social unrest and avoid the destabilizing effects of immediate austerity measures.
  3. 03Outside powers should maintain diplomatic engagement with all centers of authority, including the military, to ensure a peaceful transition to civilian rule without triggering a confrontation.
  4. 04Regional and global actors must coordinate their aid efforts to avoid turning Egypt into a battleground for competing ideological projects or regional rivalries.

"Egypt’s allies should provide immediate, large-scale economic stabilization and market access without imposing intrusive political conditions or austerity measures that could jeopardize the country's democratic transition."

Egypt’s allies should resist the temptation to treat the country’s economic crisis as a reward-and-punishment exercise. The fall in tourism, the flight of capital, the pressure on foreign reserves and the widening budget deficit are not problems that can wait until Egypt writes a constitution, elects a president and settles every dispute between the army, liberals and Islamists. If the economy continues to deteriorate, every political faction will become more radical and less willing to compromise.

What is needed is a short-term stabilization package tied to broad rules, not intrusive micromanagement. The United States, Europe, the Gulf states and international financial institutions should assemble bridge financing large enough to reassure markets and keep Egypt from burning through reserves to defend the pound. The I.M.F. loan under discussion could be part of this, but it should be cushioned by grants, debt relief and concessionary lending so that Egyptians do not experience the transition as a foreign-imposed austerity program.

Conditions should be limited and public: transparent budgeting, protection of the electoral process, civilian oversight of economic decisions and disclosure of how emergency funds are used. Donors should not try to pick winners among the Muslim Brotherhood, liberals, the military council or remnants of the old order. Nor should they insist that Egypt slash food and fuel subsidies overnight. Those subsidies are inefficient, but abrupt cuts in a country already anxious about bread prices would be economically and politically reckless.

Allies can also help by opening their own markets. Europe should expand access for Egyptian agricultural and manufactured goods. The United States should consider converting portions of its assistance from military hardware into education, job training and small-business credit. Gulf governments, which have an interest in a stable Egypt, should move beyond pledges and deposit funds transparently with the central bank or invest in job-producing infrastructure.

Finally, stability cannot mean restoring the old bargain: foreign money in exchange for domestic repression and regional predictability. That bargain collapsed in Tahrir Square. Egypt’s friends should help prevent bankruptcy, but they should also accept that a more representative Egypt may speak in a different voice. Economic recovery will be strongest if Egyptians believe they are being helped to complete their revolution, not being paid to abandon it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Economic stabilization must be prioritized immediately because a deteriorating economy will radicalize political factions and hinder the formation of a stable government.
  2. 02International aid should be structured as a combination of bridge financing, grants, and debt relief to prevent the transition from being perceived as a foreign-imposed austerity program.
  3. 03Financial assistance should be tied to broad transparency and democratic protections rather than micromanagement or the picking of political winners.
  4. 04Allies should avoid demanding abrupt cuts to food and fuel subsidies to prevent further economic and political instability.
  5. 05Western and Gulf nations should support long-term growth by opening their markets to Egyptian goods and shifting aid toward education, job training, and infrastructure investment.
  6. 06Foreign support must respect Egypt's new political reality by moving away from the old model of trading financial aid for domestic repression.
