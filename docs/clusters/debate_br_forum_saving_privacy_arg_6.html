"While surveillance reform is necessary, a digital bill of rights should focus on strengthening democratic oversight and legal accountability rather than promoting encryption technologies that render lawful, warrant-based state inquiry impossible."

Reed Hundt’s essay admirably captures the sense of crisis that has gripped the digital world since Edward Snowden’s revelations. Yet his prescription—a digital bill of rights that empowers individuals through encryption and decentralized networks—overlooks the legitimate and enduring need for effective, lawful surveillance in a dangerous world. The fundamental question, as Hundt poses it, is “Who rules?” But the answer cannot simply be the sovereign individual wrapped in cryptographic armor; it must be a democratic state that balances security with liberty through transparent processes, not technological barriers erected against itself. The encryption absolutism that Hundt flirts with threatens to undermine the very capacity for governance that his own due process framework requires.

Let us be clear: the state’s surveillance excesses exposed by Snowden demanded reform, and the ongoing legislative and judicial debates over bulk collection are healthy and necessary. The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board’s split decision and the conflicting court rulings demonstrate that reasonable people can disagree on the scope of necessary surveillance. But Hundt moves from demanding oversight to advocating tools that would make much of that oversight impossible to execute even under the strictest warrant procedures. Tor hidden services and block-chain-based pseudonymity offer near-perfect anonymity. If such technologies become widely adopted, as Hundt hopes, law enforcement’s ability to investigate child exploitation, drug trafficking, terrorism, and cybercrime will be severely crippled. The resulting impunity would harm the very rule of law that Hundt invokes.

The issue is not whether encryption works—it does—but whether a democratic society can afford to have vast swaths of human interaction rendered opaque to any inquiry. Hundt’s analogy to the Masonic lodge is telling: secret societies have always existed, but when they become the norm rather than the exception, the social fabric frays. Trust in public institutions erodes, and the space for collective judgment diminshes. A government that cannot, with judicial authorization, penetrate the communications of foreign terrorist cells or domestic criminal rings abdicates its primary duty to protect its citizens. The question is not whether the watchmen should be watched—they must be—but whether they should be blindfolded. Hundt’s insistence that law enforcement can simply use undercover officers and traditional policing ignores the scale and speed of digital threats. In a world where a single encrypted message can trigger a coordinated attack, the state’s access through warrants must be technically feasible and timely.

The call for government to actively promote encryption standards rather than undermine them is sound in many respects; weak encryption hurts everyone, including businesses and individuals. But between forbidding backdoors and actively incubating privacy tools designed to frustrate all lawful access, there is a vast middle ground. That middle ground should focus on what Hundt calls the “crossing point” between digital and analog worlds, ensuring that legal process remains robust and that companies are capable of responding to lawful orders with actual data. Instead of championing technologies that make judicial orders meaningless, a digital bill of rights should strengthen the institutions that oversee surveillance: the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court needs adversarial proceedings; the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board needs permanent, binding authority; and whistleblowers need clearer protections to expose wrongdoing without resorting to massive disclosures.

Hundt’s vision of sovereign individuals forming private digital networks risks Balkanizing the public sphere. Democracy thrives not in encrypted enclaves but in the open clash of ideas, where speakers must stand behind their words and be accountable to their fellow citizens. Anonymity has a place—in whistleblowing, in resistance to tyranny—but as a universal default it fosters irresponsibility and makes collective deliberation impossible. The goal of privacy policy should be to protect individuals from arbitrary or mass surveillance, not to guarantee invisibility. A reformed, transparent, and legally constrained surveillance framework can achieve that balance without handing the state an electronic master key or, conversely, handing every potential malefactor an impenetrable shield.

Ultimately, the question of rule is not resolved by empowering individuals against the state but by making the state answerable to individuals through democratic accountability. The path forward lies in stronger laws, better oversight, and robust encryption standards that respect legal process—not in a retreat into cryptographic solitude.
