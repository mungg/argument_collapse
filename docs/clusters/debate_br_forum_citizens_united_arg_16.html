Inequality, Not Just Dependency

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “dependency corruption” illuminates one dimension of our campaign finance crisis, but it leaves another, equally profound dimension in shadow. By focusing on the conflict between the dependence on funders and the dependence on voters, Lessig frames the problem largely as a structural misalignment that distracts well-meaning representatives. What this framing understates is the sheer inequality of political voice that concentrated wealth produces — an inequality that would corrupt democratic outcomes even if every lawmaker were a saint immune to the subtle psychology of dependence. The real scandal is not just that Congress listens to donors more than to voters; it is that the donor class is so radically unrepresentative of the public as a whole, and that its preferences systematically diverge from those of the majority on crucial economic issues.

Consider the demographic profile of the donor class. Studies by the Center for Responsive Politics and academic researchers consistently show that campaign contributors are overwhelmingly wealthy, white, male, and older than the general population. The median income of a donor who gives more than $200 is many times the national median. These donors are not just citizens with strong views; they constitute a distinct economic elite. And as political scientists such as Larry Bartels and Martin Gilens have documented, the policy preferences of this affluent slice correlate much more strongly with congressional votes than the preferences of the poor and even the middle class. When the interests of the well-off diverge from those of the majority — on issues like financial regulation, tax policy, and labor law — it is the rich who tend to get their way. This is not merely a dependency that might distract a lawmaker from her voters; it is a class bias embedded in the very structure of private campaign finance.

Lessig’s iceberg theory of influence is useful here, but it needs to be supplemented with a class analysis. The credible threat of massive independent spending is wielded disproportionately by corporations and wealthy individuals. After Citizens United, a single billionaire can credibly threaten to spend millions to defeat a senator who votes against his interests, even if that billionaire does not live in the senator’s state. The dependency this creates is not a neutral “economy of influence”; it is a plutocratic economy in which the voices of the few drown out the voices of the many. To call this institutional corruption is not wrong, but it is too sanitized. The Framers were not merely anxious about dependencies; they were explicitly worried about the influence of “the rich and the well-born,” and they built a Constitution designed to prevent the concentration of wealth from translating directly into political power.

The remedies Lessig proposes — vouchers and small-donor matching — are steps toward a more egalitarian system. But they do not go far enough to break the grip of private wealth. A voucher of $50 per citizen would indeed inject billions into campaigns, but it would not prevent the Koch brothers or George Soros from spending additional hundreds of millions independently. The result could be a two-tier system: public-funded candidate campaigns, and a shadow campaign of unlimited independent expenditures still dominated by the wealthy. To truly address the inequality of voice, we need to consider more aggressive interventions, such as a constitutional amendment that would permit reasonable limits on both contributions and expenditures, or a system of complete public funding that bans private money from elections altogether. Lessig flirts with such ideas but ultimately retreats to the politically palatable.

Inequality and dependency are two sides of the same coin. But by foregrounding dependency, Lessig risks making the problem seem technocratic — a matter of tinkering with incentives. The deeper challenge is distributive. Democracy cannot survive as a genuine contest of ideas when economic power translates so directly into political clout. That is not just a corruption of an institution; it is a betrayal of the democratic promise of equal citizenship. Any reform agenda that fails to center economic inequality will leave the fundamental structure of plutocracy intact.
