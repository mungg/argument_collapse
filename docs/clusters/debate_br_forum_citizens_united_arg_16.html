"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies 'dependency corruption' as the central threat to American democracy, his proposed solutions are insufficient because they focus too narrowly on campaign finance and judicial doctrine rather than broader structural reforms of Congress."

**Lessig Is Right About the Disease, Too Cautious About the Cure**

Lawrence Lessig's essay is the most lucid statement available of why *Citizens United* is a constitutional disaster, and his concept of "dependency corruption" deserves to enter the standard vocabulary of campaign-finance debate. But for all its analytic precision, the essay pulls its punches at exactly the moment when bolder thinking is needed. Lessig writes as if the central problem is that Justice Kennedy misread the First Amendment, and as if the appropriate response is to push Congress toward voucher systems and Fair Elections matching funds. These are good ideas. They are nowhere near sufficient.

Start with what Lessig gets exactly right. The framers' explicit design for the House was a "dependence on the people"—a phrase Lessig draws from Federalist 57 and that he could have multiplied tenfold from the founding-era record. Dependence is not corruption; *misdirected* dependence is. When 67 percent of John Kerry's senatorial money came from outside Massachusetts, his electoral dependence on Massachusetts voters was already attenuated by his fundraising dependence on a national donor class. Multiply that by 535 members and you have a Congress whose center of gravity has drifted away from the geography it represents. Lessig's "economy of influence" frame captures this drift better than the older language of "bribery" ever could.

But here is where Lessig holds back. He treats the problem as a defect in legal doctrine that could be corrected by a future Supreme Court willing to recognize a broader definition of corruption. That is a comforting thought for a law professor. It also misreads the political economy of the situation. The Roberts Court is not going to overturn *Citizens United* in the foreseeable future, and the political coalition that produced the five-justice majority is not going anywhere. Worse, the donor class that benefits from *Citizens United*—and that benefited from the pre-*Citizens United* regime almost as much—has every incentive to make sure that future judicial appointments preserve the new dispensation. Lessig's hope that Kennedy's opinion "is not likely to survive very long" is, I'm afraid, wishful.

If the doctrinal route is blocked, what remains? The voucher and matching-fund proposals Lessig endorses are a beginning, but they share a quiet flaw: they assume that flooding the system with small-donor money will swamp the influence of big money. The math does not really work. The Ackerman-Ayres voucher scheme would, at $50 per voter, generate roughly $7 billion per cycle—a lot. But corporate political spending plus high-net-worth individual spending is already in that range, and after *Citizens United* it will rise. Independent expenditures by corporations and Super-PAC-like vehicles will not be displaced by public financing; they will operate alongside it, in many cases dominating the airwaves in the weeks before elections, when public-financed candidates have already spent their war chests.

The real lesson of *Citizens United*, then, is that we cannot regulate our way out of dependency corruption through campaign finance alone. We need structural reform of how Congress itself operates. Some options Lessig does not seriously discuss:

First, ban congressional fundraising during legislative sessions, as several states do for their legislators. If a Senate Banking Committee member cannot accept money from financial-services PACs while financial-reform legislation is on the calendar, the most blatant form of dependency is at least disrupted in time. This rule does not implicate *Citizens United* at all, because it regulates the conduct of legislators, not the speech of donors.

Second, lengthen House terms to four years, with half elected every two. The two-year House cycle, which the framers thought would tether representatives to the people, now tethers them to a permanent fundraising treadmill. The average House member spends, by various estimates, between a quarter and a half of working hours dialing for dollars. A four-year cycle would, ironically, restore something closer to the framers' vision by reducing the *frequency* of dependence on funders.

Third, dramatically tighten the revolving door. The lobbying industry now functions as a deferred-compensation system for former members and senior staff. The promise of a million-dollar K Street job is itself a form of dependency corruption that operates entirely outside the campaign-finance system Lessig analyzes. A long, strictly enforced cooling-off period would do more to break the dependency than another round of contribution limits.

Fourth, fund congressional staff and research capacity at levels comparable to the executive branch. One reason Congress is so dependent on lobbyists is that lobbyists provide the analytical work that members' offices cannot afford to do themselves. Starve the legislative branch of expertise and you guarantee that expertise will be supplied by interested parties. This too is a dependency, and one entirely curable by appropriation.

Lessig's framework, in other words, points toward a much more ambitious reconstruction of legislative dependence than the campaign-finance fixes he ultimately recommends. By treating *Citizens United* as the central trauma, he risks limiting his audience's imagination to the narrow channel of contribution limits and public financing. The Court's decision is bad, but the dependency corruption it exacerbates was already pervasive under the pre-2010 regime. McCain-Feingold did not save us from it. BCRA was, in many respects, a system for routing the same money through different channels. We need to think bigger.

One last point on rhetoric. Lessig is, as always, careful to insist that members of Congress are "decent souls" and that the corruption he describes is institutional rather than personal. This is generous and probably accurate. It is also strategically self-defeating. The reform coalition that produced the Pendleton Act, the direct election of senators, and the campaign-finance reforms of the 1970s did not succeed by reassuring the public that politicians were nice people captured by structures. It succeeded by making people angry. Lessig's institutional framing risks letting individual members off the hook for choices they make every day—choices to hold high-dollar fundraisers with industries they regulate, choices to spend their evenings at K Street townhouses rather than constituent meetings. These are not impersonal forces. They are decisions. A reform movement worth having will name them as such.

*Citizens United* is the title, but it is not the whole story. Lessig has given us the right diagnosis. We need a more aggressive treatment plan than he is willing to prescribe.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Supreme Court is unlikely to overturn Citizens United in the near future, making doctrinal legal corrections a futile primary strategy.
  2. 02Public financing and voucher systems are mathematically inadequate to swamp the massive volume of corporate and high-net-worth independent expenditures authorized by the Court.
  3. 03Meaningful reform requires changing the internal operations of Congress, such as banning fundraising during legislative sessions and lengthening House terms to reduce the constant need for money.
  4. 04Addressing the 'revolving door' and increasing funding for internal congressional research capacity are essential to breaking the dependency on lobbyists for expertise and future income.
  5. 05The movement for reform must move beyond polite institutional framing and hold individual politicians morally and politically accountable for their personal choices to prioritize donors over constituents.
