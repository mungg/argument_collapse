Diversified DeepSeek

"Nancy Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism is fundamentally flawed because it relies on a narrow, elite perspective that ignores how race and class structure the practice and politics of mothering."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay is a bracing and necessary indictment of a particular form of elite mothering, but as an analysis of care feminism and its political consequences, it is marred by an almost total neglect of race and class. Her narrative is set in the gilded corridors of private universities, investment banks, and summer trips to Paris, and from that narrow vantage point she draws universal conclusions about the failures of care theory and the choices of women. The result is a diagnosis that makes sense only if one imagines that the paradigmatic mother is wealthy and white.

The first thing to notice is that the “care feminism” Hirschmann takes as her target was never a unified doctrine, and its dominant voices—Gilligan, Ruddick, Tronto—have been concerned as much with the global division of care labor, with poor women and women of color who perform care work for wages, as with the stay-at-home mothers of the professional class. The exploitation of domestic workers, the racialized history of welfare reform, the way that care work is both sentimentalized when done by white mothers and criminalized when done by Black mothers on public assistance—these are not marginal footnotes to care theory; they are at its center for many thinkers. Hirschmann, by focusing exclusively on an affluent subset of women, lets this entire dimension disappear. She mentions welfare mothers only to note that they were excoriated for not working, an observation that is correct but undeveloped: what does it say about care feminism that it could be deployed to shame poor women into low-wage labor while celebrating the “choice” of wealthy women to stay home? The answer is not that care feminism caused this hypocrisy, but that a feminism untethered from an analysis of race and class will always be vulnerable to such co-optation.

Hirschmann’s students, who condemn welfare mothers as bad examples while idealizing their own future domesticity, are not simply victims of a flawed theory; they are enacting a racial script that has defined American motherhood since slavery. The image of the good mother—selfless, homebound, economically dependent on a male breadwinner—was built in part by contrasting it with the Black mother, who was cast as either a neglectful breeder or a “mammy” whose labor belonged to white children. When Hirschmann’s students insist that good mothers stay home and that poor mothers should work, they are drawing on a deep well of racialized ideology that no amount of care theory, by itself, will empty. To address that, one needs not a critique of care but a critique of the racial state.

The same blindness afflicts Hirschmann’s proposed remedy. She urges women to gain economic power through paid work in order to force men to share care, but for many women of color, working outside the home is not a route to leverage; it is a historical necessity and often a source of further exploitation. Black women have always had high labor-force participation, yet they have not been able to turn that into marital bargaining power, in part because their wages have been so low and their partners have often been shut out of good jobs. Telling them to “care less” would be an absurdity; their care has already been devalued and policed by the state for centuries. The feminist task is not to counsel yet more sacrifice for the sake of abstract gender equality but to build alliances across race and class that fight for all women’s right to care and to be cared for, on terms of dignity.

Hirschmann is on firmer ground when she points out the flaws in the rhetoric of “choice.” But that rhetoric is a product of a consumer-capitalist culture, not of care feminism per se. The way to counter it is not to demand that all women march into the workplace but to create conditions in which choice is real—including the choice to stay home without penury and the choice to work without being a bad mother. A universal childcare system, a living wage, and a social safety net that does not punish single parents would do more to undermine the self-righteousness of privileged opt-outers than any number of stern lectures about political responsibility. When care is truly valued across the economy, the distinctions between “worthy” and “unworthy” mothers may at last begin to erode.

Hirschmann’s essay is an honest attempt to wrestle with a genuine paradox. But until it reckons with the ways in which race and class structure both the practice of mothering and the meaning of care, it will remain a narrative about the anxieties of the comfortable, rather than a roadmap to a more just world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Care feminism is not a unified doctrine and has historically centered the exploitation of poor women and women of color, a dimension Hirschmann ignores by focusing on affluent mothers.
  2. 02The hypocrisy of Hirschmann’s students reflects a long-standing racial script in America that idealizes white domesticity while criminalizing or devaluing the mothering of Black women.
  3. 03Hirschmann’s solution of gaining leverage through paid work is ineffective for women of color, for whom labor has historically been a necessity that did not translate into marital bargaining power.
  4. 04The rhetoric of 'choice' is a product of consumer capitalism rather than care theory, and it can only be countered by systemic changes like universal childcare and a living wage rather than individual careerism.
  5. 05A truly effective feminist analysis must move beyond the anxieties of the comfortable to address how the state differentially polices care based on racial and economic status.
