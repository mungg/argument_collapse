"The United States must prioritize a strategy of nuclear nonproliferation and material security over regime-change ambitions and unilateral military dominance to prevent the existential threat of nuclear terrorism."

Stephen Walt’s essay ranges widely, but its most urgent argument concerns nuclear terrorism and nonproliferation. On this issue, he is exactly right to insist that American grand strategy must distinguish between terrible problems and existential ones. Conventional terrorism is frightening and politically destabilizing, but a nuclear detonation in an American city would be a catastrophe of another order. Preventing that outcome should rank above regime-change ambitions, rhetorical crusades, and marginal gains in military dominance.

The United States has spent enormous energy since September 11 on Iraq, aviation security, color-coded alerts, and reorganizing bureaucracies. Some of that was necessary. Much of it has been misdirected. The most dangerous materials on earth—highly enriched uranium and separated plutonium—remain too widely dispersed and unevenly secured. The Soviet collapse left a legacy of weapons, scientists, facilities, and materials requiring sustained attention. Research reactors in many countries still use bomb-grade uranium. Smuggling networks, while often amateurish, need only succeed once. Compared with the cost of the Iraq war, accelerating the global lockdown of nuclear materials would be cheap. Compared with the consequences of failure, it is indispensable.

Walt’s critique of preventive war is especially persuasive in the nuclear context. Invading countries to prevent possible future acquisition of weapons is a blunt instrument that can easily make proliferation worse. Iraq is the obvious case. The absence, so far, of discovered stockpiles has damaged American credibility and will make it harder to mobilize support when intelligence warnings are genuine. Iran and North Korea have watched the fate of a non-nuclear Iraq and drawn conclusions. If the United States announces that it may overthrow hostile regimes before they acquire nuclear weapons, then such regimes have an incentive to acquire them quickly and secretly. Preventive war is supposed to stop proliferation; it may instead create a race between inspectors and insecurity.

The better approach is a bargain combining pressure, incentives, and changes in American nuclear policy. Walt is right that the Non-Proliferation Treaty rests on reciprocity. Non-nuclear states agreed not to acquire nuclear weapons. Nuclear states agreed to pursue disarmament and facilitate peaceful nuclear cooperation. That bargain has frayed. Article IV’s promise of access to the fuel cycle now creates a loophole: enrichment and reprocessing can be justified as civilian activities while positioning a state near weapons capability. But efforts to restrict that loophole will be viewed as discriminatory unless the nuclear powers, especially the United States, take their own obligations seriously.

Washington cannot credibly tell others that nuclear weapons are unnecessary and illegitimate while exploring new nuclear options and maintaining an arsenal far beyond any plausible deterrent requirement. The United States does not need thousands of operationally deployed warheads to deter attack. Its conventional superiority is unmatched, and a survivable nuclear force of much smaller size would be more than sufficient to retaliate against any state foolish enough to strike America or its allies. By reducing numbers, ratifying and supporting test restraints, abandoning new nuclear earth-penetrator fantasies, and narrowing the declared role of nuclear weapons, the United States would not disarm unilaterally. It would strengthen the political foundation for stronger rules.

Some critics dismiss this as moral theater. Iran and North Korea, they say, will not abandon nuclear ambitions because America trims its arsenal. That is partly true. Pyongyang’s motives include regime survival, blackmail, prestige, and perhaps domestic politics. Tehran’s motives include security, regional status, technological pride, and factional competition. American reductions alone will not change those calculations. But nonproliferation is not only about the hardest cases. It is about maintaining a global coalition. To isolate Iran, we need Europe, Russia, China, India, and many nonaligned states to believe that the rules are fair enough to enforce. To persuade countries to forgo enrichment and accept fuel assurances, we need them to trust that they are not being asked to accept permanent technological subordination while the nuclear powers modernize indefinitely.

The Libyan example supports Walt’s case for tailored diplomacy. Libya did not abandon its programs because of one factor alone. Sanctions, interdiction, fear after Iraq, desire for international rehabilitation, and negotiations all mattered. The lesson is not that force works or that carrots work; it is that specific regimes make specific calculations. A.Q. Khan’s network also shows the value of intelligence cooperation and interdiction. The Proliferation Security Initiative, if grounded in law and broad participation, can help. So can stronger export controls, financial tracking, and criminal penalties for illicit trade.

Iran requires a patient multilateral strategy. The European negotiations with Tehran have been imperfect, but they are preferable to threats that unite Iranian factions. The objective should be verifiable limits that prevent Iran from acquiring a weapons capability while offering civilian nuclear cooperation under conditions, economic integration, and security assurances. The United States should be at the table, not sniping from outside. Refusing to talk to adversaries is not toughness; it is self-denial. At the same time, Iran must understand that concealment and violation will carry costs, including Security Council action if evidence warrants. Diplomacy without pressure is pleading; pressure without diplomacy is posturing.

North Korea is even harder. The regime has already announced nuclear capabilities and has a record of brinkmanship. Still, the six-party framework is the right venue because North Korea’s neighbors have the greatest leverage. China does not want collapse or war, but it also does not want a nuclear crisis that could encourage Japanese or South Korean nuclearization. A package involving security assurances, energy assistance, phased dismantlement, intrusive verification, and normalization steps remains the least bad path. Military options are appalling, given Seoul’s vulnerability and the risk of escalation. Containment may be necessary, but containment should be paired with an open diplomatic exit.

Walt’s proposed nonaggression assurances deserve serious consideration. Many Americans recoil at pledging not to attack odious regimes. But such pledges are not gifts; they are tools. If a regime’s strongest argument for nuclear weapons is fear of U.S.-sponsored overthrow, reducing that fear in exchange for verifiable nuclear restraint serves American interests. We made arms-control agreements with Stalin’s successors and Mao’s China. We can make conditional security arrangements with regimes we dislike.

The most important immediate task, however, is securing materials. A terrorist group need not build a sophisticated thermonuclear weapon to cause catastrophe. A crude gun-type device using highly enriched uranium would be difficult but not impossible for a capable group if it obtained the material. Radiological dispersal devices are less catastrophic but still disruptive. The first line of defense is accounting for, consolidating, converting, and guarding material everywhere. This requires money, access, scientific cooperation, and presidential attention. It should be treated with the urgency of a war plan.

Domestic measures matter too: radiation detection at ports, improved intelligence integration, emergency response planning, and consequence management. But detection is a last line, not a strategy. Once material is in motion, the odds worsen. The best bomb is the one never assembled because the material was never stolen.

Walt’s broader offshore-balancing argument may be debated, but on nuclear danger his hierarchy of threats is sound. The United States has limited attention, even if it has vast power. Every month consumed by dreams of transforming Iraq is a month not spent securing fissile material, repairing alliances for nonproliferation, and building enforceable bargains. A mature superpower would organize its policy around the gravest dangers, not the most ideologically satisfying targets.

Preventing nuclear terrorism will require humility as well as strength. It will require cooperation with Russia despite disagreements, with China despite rivalry, with Muslim governments despite mistrust, and with international agencies despite their imperfections. It will require the United States to accept constraints on its own nuclear posture in order to strengthen constraints on others. That is not appeasement. It is strategy.
