"To be truly transformative, campus anti-racist movements must expand their focus beyond student sensitivity and diversity initiatives to include solidarity with campus workers and a critique of the university's economic labor structure."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay should be read as an invitation to widen the frame. The dominant media story about campus protest has focused on student sensitivity, safe spaces, Halloween costumes, resignations, and elite universities. Kelley asks us to look instead at the university as an economic and political institution. That shift is essential. But we should push it even further: no movement against campus racism will be transformative unless it becomes a movement with campus workers.

The university is not only a site where students experience alienation. It is a workplace. It depends on janitors, dining hall workers, groundskeepers, clerical staff, graduate assistants, adjuncts, security guards, nurses, librarians, and maintenance crews. Many of these workers are black, Latino, immigrant, female, or otherwise positioned at the bottom of the institution whose diversity brochures celebrate inclusion. A university can recruit black students and hire a chief diversity officer while contracting out food service to keep wages low. It can hold a panel on racial justice in a building cleaned by workers who cannot afford rent near campus. It can fund a new center while graduate workers lack secure health care.

This is why the UNC protesters’ demand for a $25 minimum wage and equalized administrator pay is so important. It may sound utopian, but it clarifies the structure of the institution. Universities are full of people who speak the language of justice while accepting vast internal inequality as normal. Presidents and top administrators earn salaries that would have seemed extravagant a generation ago. Coaches at large athletic programs can become the highest-paid public employees in their states. Meanwhile adjuncts piece together courses across campuses, graduate students teach undergraduates while carrying debt, and service workers are told there is no money.

Kelley is right that diversity and inclusion can be absorbed by neoliberal management. The easiest way for a university to respond to protest is to add administrators, workshops, and statements. These may help in some cases, but they often leave untouched the labor regime that makes the university run. A more radical anti-racist politics would ask: Who cooks? Who cleans? Who teaches introductory courses? Who patrols? Who profits from construction? Whose neighborhoods are changed when the university expands? Who carries the debt? Who gets health insurance? Who gets tenure? Who gets called “staff” in a tone that means disposable?

The history of black freedom struggle provides models for this broader approach. The civil rights movement was never only about lunch counters and voting booths; it was also about jobs, wages, land, housing, and dignity. The 1963 March on Washington was a march for jobs and freedom. Martin Luther King Jr. died in Memphis supporting sanitation workers. Black student activism at its best has understood that campus issues are connected to labor and community struggles. The challenge now is to rebuild those connections under conditions of debt, precarity, and professional anxiety.

Students often have more power than they realize, but less than they imagine. They can embarrass administrations, generate media attention, occupy buildings, and disrupt ceremonies. Yet without workers, their leverage is limited. The Missouri football team’s threatened strike revealed this dramatically. Suddenly the moral claims of Concerned Student 1950 were joined to an economic threat. The university could ignore black pain more easily than it could ignore the possible cancellation of a football game. That lesson should not be lost. Power concedes when routines break and revenue is at risk.

Graduate students occupy a particularly important position. They are students and workers, consumers and laborers, aspiring professionals and precarious employees. Many have been drawn to Harney and Moten’s undercommons because the official university offers them professionalization without security. But graduate student radicalism can become self-referential if it does not connect to adjuncts, clerical staff, and service workers. The undercommons should not be only a space for brilliant conversations among the overeducated. It should become a meeting ground where the people who make the university possible can think and act together.

This also changes how we understand trauma. Workers experience institutional harm too, though not always in the language of campus climate. Racism may appear as a supervisor’s discipline, a schedule that ignores childcare, a security practice that treats black workers as suspects, a wage that forces a second job, or a benefits policy that leaves illness untreated. A politics centered only on student feelings can miss these injuries. A politics centered only on class can miss the specific forms of racial degradation students and workers face. The task is to hold both.

What might this mean practically? Student demands should include living wages, fair contracts, protections for immigrant workers, childcare, graduate employee rights, adjunct equity, and community benefit agreements around expansion. Anti-racist campaigns should support union drives. Faculty should refuse to treat labor issues as separate from intellectual life. Black studies and ethnic studies programs should teach university political economy alongside culture and history. Protests over racist incidents should ask not only for apologies but for budgets: Who is paid, who is hired, who is retained, who is policed?

Kelley’s phrase “love, study, struggle” can guide this work if love means solidarity beyond one’s own status group. Students must love workers enough not to use them as scenery in their own dramas. Faculty must love students enough to risk professional comfort. Workers must be recognized not as service providers but as co-inhabitants of the institution and co-creators of any just university.

The university will not liberate us. But a campus movement rooted in labor solidarity can make it a less comfortable place for exploitation. It can also teach students the most important lesson they may learn: institutions change when those who make them run decide to run them differently.
