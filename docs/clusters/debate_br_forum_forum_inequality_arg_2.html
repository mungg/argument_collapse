"David Grusky’s proposal for 'institutional repair' is based on a misdiagnosis of market outcomes as market failures and is less effective than traditional tax-based redistribution for reducing inequality."

David Grusky's essay misdiagnoses the sources of rising inequality and, on the basis of that misdiagnosis, prescribes the wrong remedies. His argument rests on a conceptual sleight of hand: he redefines market outcomes he dislikes as evidence of "market failure" and then calls for institutional intervention to correct the supposed failure. This move is intellectually convenient but ultimately unconvincing. The real question is not whether we can find ways to intervene in markets but whether such intervention will reduce inequality sustainably or simply create new distortions while reducing economic output.

Grusky's account of educational bottlenecks illustrates the problem. He argues that the college premium persists because elite universities ration slots rather than expanding supply to meet rising demand. This is true as far as it goes, but it conflates a legitimate market phenomenon—scarcity of prestigious credentials—with market failure. Elite universities produce not just education but signaling. Their degrees convey information to employers about worker quality that is difficult to obtain through other means. If every university were expanded to meet demand, the signaling function of elite credentials would be diluted, and employers might resort to other screening mechanisms that are less reliable or more costly. The "bottleneck" is partly a response to genuine information problems in labor markets, not simply a rent-seeking distortion.

Moreover, Grusky's proposed remedy—opening access to higher education—does not automatically lead to the outcomes he predicts. If we dramatically expand the number of college graduates, we may simply inflate credential requirements for jobs that do not genuinely require college-level skills. Employers might respond to a larger pool of college-educated workers by raising requirements for positions that were formerly open to non-college graduates. This credential inflation has already occurred to some degree; many jobs that once required only a high school diploma now require a bachelor's degree, even when the actual work has not changed substantially. Expanded college access might therefore reduce inequality only modestly while imposing substantial costs in the form of delayed labor market entry and wasted resources on credentials that add little value.

The CEO pay analysis is similarly problematic. Grusky treats current compensation practices as evidence of rent-seeking, but the empirical literature is far more contested than his confident tone suggests. Some economists argue that CEO pay reflects genuine competition for scarce managerial talent. Others identify inefficiencies. The debate cannot be resolved by asserting that board members are inherently conflicted. Boards do face incentive problems, but they also face pressure from institutional investors, media scrutiny, and shareholder voting. The question is whether the distortions are severe enough to justify the costs of intervention. Grusky does not seriously engage with the counterarguments or the empirical evidence on the efficiency of executive compensation.

The deeper flaw in Grusky's argument is its implicit embrace of market fundamentalism in a new guise. He assumes that if only markets were truly competitive, inequality would be much reduced. This assumption is empirically questionable. Highly competitive economies generate substantial inequality. Competition rewards those who possess scarce skills, take risks, or simply happen to be in the right place at the right time. The dispersion of outcomes in competitive markets can be wide even when those markets function well. Grusky treats "market failure" as the explanation for inequality, but much inequality may simply reflect the normal operation of competitive processes.

If this is correct, then "institutional repair" along the lines Grusky proposes is unlikely to achieve the dramatic reduction in inequality he envisions. We might break up educational bottlenecks and reform corporate governance, only to discover that inequality persists because competitive markets generate dispersed outcomes. The remedy then would be more aggressive redistribution, which is precisely the tax-based agenda Grusky dismisses.

None of this means we should abandon all effort to make markets more competitive or to correct genuine distortions. But it does mean that Grusky's sharp dichotomy between market failure and normal market outcomes is too simple. We should be humble about our ability to identify market failures and humble about the costs of intervention. The tax system offers a more reliable and politically robust pathway to reduced inequality because it operates on the final distribution of income rather than trying to engineer the complex processes that generate income in the first place.

Grusky warns that relying on tax policy means accepting "rough-and-ready redistribution" as a pragmatic concession to entrenched power. But this framing is too defeatist. Progressive taxation is not merely a fallback position; it is a legitimate and effective tool for reducing inequality. It can raise substantial revenue for public investments in health, education, and infrastructure that benefit low- and middle-income families. And it can directly reduce after-tax inequality by asking the wealthy to contribute more to shared purposes. This is not second-best policymaking; it is a sound approach to a genuinely difficult problem.

  4. 04Highly competitive markets naturally generate significant inequality, meaning that making markets more competitive will not necessarily eliminate the dispersion of wealth.
