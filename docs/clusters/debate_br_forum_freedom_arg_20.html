The Right's Freedom Is Not a Pose

Aziz Rana's essay is a sophisticated piece of intellectual history, but its central move is to treat the right's account of freedom as essentially derivative — a cultural residue of settler conquest and imperial primacy, propped up by racial fantasy and now exhausting itself. As a conservative who believes the language of liberty belongs as much to my tradition as to any other, I find this diagnosis tendentious and self-serving. The left did not lose the freedom argument because settlers stole Indigenous land; it lost because, in the twentieth century, the left's preferred remedies came increasingly to look like, and often were, expansions of bureaucratic power over ordinary people's lives. The right's appeal to freedom is not a fragile relic. It is a response to a real and continuing experience of being governed by institutions one did not choose and cannot meaningfully influence.

Start with the substance of what conservatives mean by freedom. Rana is right that there is a positive and a negative tradition. He prefers the positive — freedom as collective self-rule, as participation in shared institutions. Conservatives, including most American conservatives, hold to a more modest and arguably more defensible vision: freedom as the absence of arbitrary coercion, the security of person and property, the right to associate or not associate, to worship or not worship, to speak or stay silent, to raise one's children according to one's own conscience. This is not "extreme individualism" or "market fundamentalism." It is the classical liberal inheritance, refined through Anglo-American common law, that allowed enormous numbers of people of every background to build lives of dignity over the past two centuries.

Rana repeatedly conflates this tradition with white nationalism. The conflation is rhetorically convenient but historically dishonest. The principles of limited government, due process, freedom of conscience, and economic liberty were not invented by settlers to dispossess Indians. They were imported from a Britain that had no settler colonies, by people whose theoretical commitments — Locke, Sidney, Trenchard, the radical Whig tradition — were forged against monarchy. To say that these ideas became entangled with conquest is true; to say they are reducible to conquest is to throw out the moral inheritance of every dissenter, religious minority, abolitionist, and yes, eventually civil rights leader who ever appealed to them. Frederick Douglass did not appeal to "solidaristic collective self-rule" when he wrote his Fourth of July oration. He appealed to the Constitution and the Declaration, read against their hypocritical practitioners.

Second, consider the empirical record of the alternative Rana is offering. He wants a society where freedom means democratic codetermination in workplaces, robust public provision of pensions and health care, decriminalized borders, and dramatically reduced military spending redirected to social investment. This is roughly the program of European social democracy. Whatever its merits — and I would grant it some — let us be honest that it has not delivered greater freedom in any sense an ordinary person would recognize. Western European countries have less innovation, less entrepreneurship, higher youth unemployment, declining religious liberty, increasingly aggressive speech regulation, and demographic trajectories that suggest deep civic exhaustion. Their citizens are more taxed, more surveilled, and increasingly less able to dissent from prevailing elite opinion. The fact that Americans look at this model and prefer their own messier, more dynamic, more contentious freedom is not false consciousness. It is judgment.

Third, Rana misreads the recent populist surge. He suggests that Trump-era nativism is "fragile" because it can no longer make good on its old promises of mastery for white Americans. The reality is more interesting. The populist right has gained ground precisely because ordinary people across racial lines — Hispanic voters most notably, who have shifted toward Republicans in recent cycles — have correctly perceived that the managerial-progressive class has contempt for their values, their work, their communities, and their religious traditions. When parents object to ideological curricula in their children's schools, they are not reveling in domination; they are exercising the most basic parental freedom. When workers worry about open borders depressing wages, they are not channeling settler racism; they are doing the arithmetic that economists from Borjas to Card have argued over for decades. Rana's framework cannot see these voters except as confused or malevolent, which is why his political project keeps losing them.

Fourth, on empire and foreign policy. Here I share more ground with Rana than he might expect. The post-Cold War project of nation-building in Iraq and Afghanistan was a tragic misadventure, and conservatives of the older, more prudential school — George Kennan, Robert Taft, Pat Buchanan, and more recently figures like J.D. Vance — have long warned that imperial overreach corrodes republican virtue at home. A $900 billion defense budget is excessive; the F-35 program is a scandal; permanent forward deployments in dozens of countries make Americans less safe and less free. But the answer is not the universalist anti-imperialism Rana implies, with its presumption of open borders and global redistribution. The answer is a more restrained, more genuinely national foreign policy oriented to protecting the actual American republic — its borders, its industrial base, its citizens' security — rather than projecting power on behalf of an abstract "liberal order."

Fifth, on institutions. Rana wants stronger unions and expanded voting access. Reasonable people can debate the merits of the PRO Act, but its central provision — overriding state right-to-work laws — would deny workers the freedom not to fund a union they do not support. This is illustrative: left "freedom" projects routinely require coercive uniformity that ordinary Americans, when polled, do not want. Right-to-work laws exist in twenty-seven states because voters there preferred them. To dismantle that preference in the name of "collective self-rule" is to substitute the rule of one collective (a union local) for another (a state electorate). Whatever one thinks of the tradeoff, calling it freedom requires a serious argument, not an assertion.

Finally, let me concede what Rana gets right. Concentrated corporate power is a real threat to ordinary people's autonomy. The financialization of the American economy, the consolidation of monopolies, and the offshoring of industrial capacity have eroded the conditions for the small-property, small-business, self-employed independence that mid-century Americans took for granted. On this, a genuine populist conservatism and a serious left can find common cause, as figures like Oren Cass and Marco Rubio have begun to articulate. Where we differ from Rana is in believing that the answer is not more administrative state, more federal mandates, and more redefinition of freedom to mean participation in left-approved institutions. It is, rather, restoring the conditions under which families, churches, small communities, and yes small businesses can flourish without being squeezed by either Walmart or Washington.

The right's freedom is not a relic. It is the lived intuition of millions of Americans that they should be left alone to live their lives, and that intuition deserves better than Rana's reduction of it to settler nostalgia.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The right’s vision of freedom as the absence of arbitrary coercion and the security of private conscience is a defensible classical liberal inheritance that is distinct from, and not reducible to, white nationalism or conquest.
  2. 02The left’s decline in the freedom debate is due to its reliance on bureaucratic remedies that ordinary people perceive as a loss of personal autonomy rather than a gain in collective self-rule.
  3. 03European-style social democracy, which the lead essay advocates, has historically resulted in economic stagnation and increased state surveillance rather than genuine human liberation.
  4. 04The recent populist surge among diverse demographics is a rational response to the perceived contempt of a managerial-progressive class toward traditional values and local communities.
  5. 05True freedom is better served by restoring the independence of families, churches, and small businesses than by expanding the administrative state or mandating participation in left-aligned institutions like unions.
  6. 06While imperial overreach is a valid concern, the solution is a restrained national foreign policy focused on protecting the American republic rather than a universalist anti-imperialism that includes open borders.
