"Stephen Walt’s strategy of offshore balancing is a flawed imperial project that fails to address the systemic problems of American primacy, which should instead be replaced by a complete dismantling of global hegemony in favor of common security and international equality."

Stephen Walt’s “In the National Interest” purports to offer a hardheaded realist alternative to the Bush administration’s reckless imperialism, but the analysis remains trapped within the very logic it claims to transcend. Walt’s offshore balancing may be less bloody and less provocative than preventive war, yet it is still a strategy for managing an empire—an empire of bases, clients, and hegemonic privilege—rather than a genuine alternative to the structure of American global domination. By accepting the premise that the United States should remain the world’s only superpower and that its grand strategy should preserve primacy, Walt sidesteps the deeper question that the disasters of the past four years should force us to ask: whether primacy itself is the problem, not merely a clumsy management of it. A truly responsible foreign policy would begin with dismantling the military-industrial imperium, repudiating the bipartisan consensus on global intervention, and investing instead in a just and sustainable America at peace with the world.

Walt’s indictment of the neoconservative project is meticulous and often persuasive, but he never confronts the structural forces that produce overreach. He treats the invasion of Iraq as a mistake in judgment—a failure to follow the wise path of multilateral containment—as if the war were merely a policy blip that a different administration could have avoided. This misses the systemic nature of American imperialism. The United States did not stumble into global empire because George W. Bush happened to be president; it acquired an empire over the course of the twentieth century, driven by the imperatives of a capitalist economy that requires access to markets, resources, and cheap labor, and by a national-security establishment whose institutional interests are served by a constant state of military alert. Offshore balancing would still require the United States to police the Persian Gulf, maintain air and naval supremacy in Asia, and stand ready to intervene when local balances fail. That posture requires the same archipelago of overseas bases, the same aircraft carriers, the same vast defense budgets, the same cozy relationship between the Pentagon and defense contractors that produced the Iraq debacle. Walt’s strategy is a slightly less bellicose version of the same imperial mission, not a break from it.

The moral and political costs of this posture are written in the lives of ordinary people across the Global South. Walt worries about anti-Americanism as a condition that complicates U.S. foreign policy and aids the recruitment of terrorists. But he never asks what it is that makes American power so hated in the first place, beyond the specific “mistakes” of the Bush years. The truth is that America’s post–Cold War dominance has been exercised not for the abstract good of a liberal order but to enforce an economic system that widens global inequality, to prop up authoritarian regimes that serve American interests (from the Saudi monarchy to the Egyptian dictatorship), and to impose a regime of sanctions and debt that has killed hundreds of thousands in Iraq before a single American tank rolled across the border. Offshore balancing would continue to treat the Persian Gulf as a field of strategic “interests” to be secured by force, indifferent to the democratic aspirations of the people who live there. It would continue to arm Israel without requiring an end to the occupation. It would continue to define “vital interests” in terms of resource control and strategic denial, not in terms of human rights, egalitarian development, or ecological survival. Anti-Americanism is not a mysterious pathology; it is a rational response to a global system organized around American power and wealth. You cannot cure it by being a kinder hegemon; you must stop being a hegemon.

Walt’s recommendations also betray a technocratic fantasy that image management can legitimate an illegitimate position. His call for a public-diplomacy campaign that sells a “good product” assumes that the product—American primacy—is fundamentally good, and that the only problem is that foreigners have been sold a distorted story. This is the language of Madison Avenue, not of political morality. No amount of Arabic-language broadcasting or cultural exchange will persuade a Palestinian whose home has been bulldozed, an Iraqi whose family was killed by smart bombs, or a Central American whose economy was destroyed by structural adjustment, that American primacy is working in their interest. True legitimacy cannot be manufactured by spin doctors; it must be earned by policies that serve the common interests of humanity—aggressive action on climate change, cancellation of crushing debt, a foreign policy that subordinates military force to the rule of international law and the authority of a democratized United Nations. Walt’s essay gestures toward multilateralism, but only as a “buddy system” to reassure other states that America will use its power with restraint, not as a genuine commitment to sharing power and accepting the constraints of a global democratic polity. The United Nations in his scheme remains a tool of American reassurance, not a vehicle of global governance.

What would a genuine alternative look like? It would begin with a dramatic reduction of the military budget, redirected toward a global green new deal that retires the foreign debt of impoverished nations and funds sustainable development. It would close the vast majority of overseas bases, including those in Europe and Asia that Walt himself wants to keep in some form, and convert the Department of Defense into a Department of Common Security focused on disaster relief, peacekeeping under UN command, and the dismantling of weapons of mass destruction everywhere, including America’s own arsenal on a fixed, verifiable timetable. It would replace the bilateral alliance system with a strengthened, inclusive collective security architecture through which no single power can dominate the decision to wage war. It would end arms sales to authoritarian regimes and impose a strict code of conduct on American corporations operating abroad. Most importantly, it would abandon the premise that American leadership is a global good and instead work to build a world where no nation, including the United States, enjoys the power to impose its will on others.

Walt’s offshore balancing is an improvement over the war-without-end doctrine of the Bush administration, but it is an improvement in the sense that a chronic illness is an improvement over a gunshot wound. It still leaves the patient fundamentally sick. America’s national interest—understood as the well-being of its people, the integrity of its democracy, and the health of the planet—is not served by managing a global empire more adroitly. It is served by letting go of the empire altogether. The real choice is not between offshore balancing and selective engagement, but between the hubris of primacy and the humility of a country that learns to live as one nation among many.
