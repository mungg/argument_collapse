"While Nir Rosen’s critique of the Afghan war provides a valuable corrective to overly optimistic narratives, his sweeping pessimism and dismissal of counterinsurgency (COIN) are based on flawed historical and strategic assumptions."

Nir Rosen's essay on Afghanistan represents a particular genre of American foreign policy criticism that deserves careful examination. The author combines genuine expertise, on-the-ground reporting, and a coherent analytical framework to reach conclusions that will satisfy those already skeptical of American intervention but will likely fail to persuade those with different starting assumptions. This is not a criticism of Rosen's journalism, which is often excellent, but an observation about the limits of his analytical lens.

Rosen's central argument—that counterinsurgency in Afghanistan is attempting "something from nothing"—rests on several empirical claims that warrant scrutiny. His assertion that "Afghanistan has never had" a centralized state is historically contestable. The Durrani Empire of Ahmad Shah Durrani, which at its height encompassed much of modern Afghanistan, Pakistan, and parts of Iran and India, represented a significant centralized state structure. The subsequent fragmentation of Afghanistan into regional power centers reflected not permanent cultural imperatives but the impact of British and Russian imperial competition.

More importantly, the assumption that state-building requires replicating Western institutional forms misunderstands what effective governance in Afghanistan might look like. The most stable periods in Afghan history involved not strong centralized states but functional accommodations between Kabul and regional power holders. A federal or confederal structure that acknowledged Afghanistan's diversity might prove more durable than efforts to impose uniform bureaucratic governance.

Rosen's treatment of the Afghan security forces also requires nuance. The failures he documents—corruption, drug use, cowardice—are real, but they represent conditions that might be improved rather than permanent characteristics. The argument that "we can't expect it to change their activities" if we "take drug dealers and gangbangers from the streets of D.C." conflates structural obstacles with fixed limitations. Afghan police who served under effective leadership and in supportive institutional environments have demonstrated competence; those abandoned to corrupt commanders and impossible conditions have predictably failed.

The author's dismissal of COIN doctrine as requiring soldiers to be "Wyatt Earp and Mother Theresa" reveals a misunderstanding of what counterinsurgency actually requires at the tactical level. Effective COIN does not ask individual soldiers to simultaneously pursue enemies and provide social services. Rather, it requires coordinated military, economic, and political efforts with soldiers playing specific roles within that broader framework. The complaint that "once you get down to the rifle squad, COIN does not make any sense" mistakes tactical limitations for strategic impossibility.

Rosen's critique of the Iraq Surge analogy is largely valid but overstated. He correctly notes that conditions in Afghanistan differ fundamentally from those in Iraq, that the Surge succeeded partly because insurgency dynamics had already exhausted themselves, and that direct replication of Iraqi approaches will fail. However, this observation argues for adapting COIN doctrine to Afghan conditions, not abandoning population-centric operations entirely.

The essay's treatment of the al-Qaeda threat is particularly problematic. Rosen's suggestion that September 11 "had little impact on the American economy or way of life" ignores substantial economic disruption, significant ongoing security expenditures, and profound changes in American behavior and psychology. The threat posed by terrorist networks with global reach cannot be dismissed as mere imperial anxiety; it reflects genuine strategic concerns about weapons proliferation, critical infrastructure vulnerability, and the demonstrated willingness of violent extremists to inflict mass casualties.

Rosen is correct that the original justification for Afghanistan intervention—denying al-Qaeda safe haven—has been stretched beyond recognition. The addition of regime change, democratization, and nation-building to American objectives has confused strategy and diluted focus. But this observation argues for clarifying and narrowing American objectives rather than abandoning the effort entirely.

The author never explicitly recommends withdrawal, perhaps recognizing that such a position remains politically untenable. Yet his analysis points consistently in this direction, creating tension between what he argues and what he implies. A more honest essay would explicitly state its conclusions and defend them directly.

For policymakers, Rosen's essay provides valuable corrective to optimistic narratives about COIN success. But its sweeping pessimism offers equally poor guidance. The path forward requires neither naive optimism nor despairing surrender but rather honest assessment of what might be achieved at acceptable cost and willingness to adjust objectives accordingly.

"Nir Rosen’s critique of the American counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan is overly fatalistic and fails to recognize that historical challenges and institutional flaws can be overcome through adaptation and sustained commitment."

Nir Rosen has written a damning indictment of American counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan, and while his argument contains much that is compelling, it ultimately suffers from a fatalistic determinism that blinds him to the possibilities for change. His central claim—that the United States is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—rests on a series of assumptions about Afghan history and society that, while containing truth, are presented as unchangeable certainties rather than as conditions that American policy might meaningfully alter.

The core of Rosen's critique concerns the impossibility of creating a centralized state in Afghanistan, given that "Afghanistan has never had one." This historical observation is accurate but strategically incomplete. The absence of centralized statehood in Afghanistan's past does not mean such a state cannot emerge in its future, particularly with sustained international investment and support. Indeed, the very concept of modern statehood is a relatively recent development across the globe, and many nations that now possess functioning governments once seemed equally incapable of achieving them.

Rosen is right to note that the American military establishment faces institutional challenges in implementing true counterinsurgency doctrine. His observation that "the bureaucracy is still dominated by old-school army thinking" deserves serious attention. However, his conclusion—that therefore the United States should abandon its efforts—overlooks the possibility of meaningful institutional reform. The appointment of General Stanley McChrystal, whatever Rosen's reservations about his background, represented a genuine attempt to bring COIN expertise to the highest levels of command. The military's ability to adapt has been underestimated before.

The author's dismissal of the Afghan security forces as hopelessly incompetent also requires scrutiny. Rosen cites Sergeant Dyer's contempt for police who "were still wearing their man jammies" as evidence of fundamental inadequacy. But this anecdote, while colorful, hardly constitutes systematic analysis. The Afghan National Army and Police have shown signs of improvement, however gradual. Writing off these forces entirely ensures their failure by removing any incentive for reform or performance.

More troubling is Rosen's treatment of the al-Qaeda threat. He writes that "the attacks themselves had little impact on the American economy or way of life," which mischaracterizes both the immediate and long-term consequences of September 11. The economic disruption was substantial, the psychological impact on the American public was profound, and the policy responses—while sometimes misguided—reflected genuine security concerns rather than mere imperial overreach. To minimize the threat posed by terrorist networks with global reach is to ignore the lessons of that day's carnage.

Rosen's argument that al-Qaeda is "already ensconced in Pakistan" and therefore Afghanistan's importance is diminished actually strengthens the case for a robust American presence. If terrorist networks have safe havens in nuclear-armed Pakistan, then preventing them from establishing additional bases in Afghanistan becomes more critical, not less. The strategic depth that a Taliban-controlled Afghanistan would provide to extremist movements cannot be dismissed as irrelevant.

The author is correct that the Iraq Surge's success cannot be directly replicated in Afghanistan. The urban-rural demographics differ, the sectarian dynamics are distinct, and the historical context varies significantly. But this observation argues for adapting COIN doctrine to Afghan conditions, not abandoning counterinsurgency altogether. The principles of population protection, legitimate governance, and economic development remain valid even if their application must differ from the Iraqi model.

Rosen's essay would be more persuasive if it offered an alternative policy framework rather than primarily cataloging failures. The implicit suggestion seems to be American withdrawal, but this conclusion is never explicitly stated or defended. What would follow from abandoning Afghanistan to Taliban control and reduced American involvement? The author raises concerns about al-Qaeda's presence in Pakistan but does not explain how withdrawal would address this challenge.

The American mission in Afghanistan is indeed difficult, perhaps more difficult than its architects initially understood. Rosen deserves credit for illuminating the gap between COIN theory and practice, between strategic documents and field realities. But difficulty is not impossibility, and the consequences of failure—greater terrorist safe havens, regional destabilization, and the abandonment of an Afghan population that has already suffered enormously—deserve more consideration than Rosen's despairing analysis provides.

  4. 04Minimizing the impact of the September 11 attacks and the threat of al-Qaeda ignores the profound economic and security consequences of allowing terrorist safe havens to expand.

"While Nir Rosen provides valuable on-the-ground reporting of the failures in Afghanistan, his conclusion that counterinsurgency is fundamentally impossible and nonsensical is an overly deterministic dismissal of a strategy that can be improved through institutional adaptation."

Nir Rosen's account of counterinsurgency in Afghanistan represents the kind of granular, on-the-ground journalism that American policy debates desperately need but rarely receive. His detailed observations from Helmand province provide invaluable perspective on the gap between strategic theory and tactical reality. Yet despite the essay's many compelling passages, Rosen ultimately falls into a trap common among disillusioned observers: throwing out the counterinsurgency baby with the institutional bathwater.

The essay's most valuable contribution lies in its documentation of theAfghan security forces' failures. The anecdote about police failing to wear body armor because "it was hot and heavy" captures a fundamental problem that strategic documents consistently understate. These are not merely training deficiencies that additional instruction will remedy; they reflect deeply embedded cultural attitudes toward authority, risk, and professional responsibility that may prove resistant to American mentoring.

Rosen is also correct that the Iraq Surge analogy has been applied too mechanically to Afghanistan. The Sunni Awakening succeeded partly because Sunni tribes had already been defeated and exhausted by sectarian warfare. The American military could position itself as a relatively benign actor compared to Shia militias. No such dynamic exists in Afghanistan, where the Taliban have not been decisively defeated and where Pashtun populations view the American presence with deep ambivalence at best.

However, the essay's central argument—that counterinsurgency "doesn't make sense" because it asks soldiers to be "Wyatt Earp and Mother Theresa"—misunderstands what successful COIN actually requires. The solution to soldiers' primary concern with survival is not to abandon population-centric operations but to restructure those operations to reduce risk while maintaining pressure on insurgents. The village stability platforms and embedded training teams that Rosen dismisses as inadequate represent movement in this direction, however imperfect.

More fundamentally, Rosen's assertion that "the American military and policy establishments are incapable of doing COIN" conflates current institutional weaknesses with permanent incapacity. The military has proven capable of significant adaptation when properly directed and resourced. The establishment of Army Brigade Combat Teams restructured for counterinsurgency, the development of specialized cultural training programs, and the genuine learning that occurred during the Iraq Surge all demonstrate institutional flexibility that Rosen's determinism cannot accommodate.

The essay's treatment of the al-Qaeda threat is particularly problematic. Rosen argues that September 11 "had little impact on the American economy or way of life" and suggests that terrorist networks pose limited danger to a "vigilant superpower." This analysis ignores both the immediate economic disruption caused by the attacks and the ongoing threat assessment that informed subsequent American policy. Whether one agrees with specific policy responses or not, the underlying threat assessment cannot be dismissed as mere imperial anxiety.

Rosen correctly identifies that the Taliban are primarily motivated by nationalist concerns rather than global jihadist ideology, and that their alliance with al-Qaeda reflects convenience more than ideology. But this observation cuts both ways. If the Taliban are primarily concerned with expelling foreign occupiers and establishing Islamic governance, then a credible American drawdown timeline might indeed create space for accommodation. But the author never makes this argument explicitly, instead suggesting that American withdrawal would merely strengthen terrorist networks.

The essay's weakest section concerns the August 2009 operations in Helmand. Rosen describes these as resulting in "at best a stalemate," but this characterization requires scrutiny. The Marines' presence in previously Taliban-controlled territory, whatever its tactical limitations, did secure populations that had lived under insurgent shadow governance. The fact that the Taliban "merely slipped away" rather than being destroyed is a function of COIN's long-term orientation rather than evidence of failure.

Rosen concludes by suggesting that "addressing these problems at their roots"—meaning American imperial excesses and support for Israel and dictators—would do more for everyone than military intervention. This argument, while popular in certain intellectual circles, fails to engage with the actual strategic choices facing American policymakers. Reducing support for allies or withdrawing from global commitments would not eliminate terrorist grievances, many of which predate current American policies and reflect deeper ideological commitments than mere policy disagreements.

The essay provides valuable corrective to triumphalist narratives about COIN success, but its sweeping condemnations ultimately serve poorly as policy guidance. Afghanistan's challenges are real, but they are not insurmountable. The path forward requires honest acknowledgment of difficulties alongside sustained commitment to adaptation and reform.

"Nir Rosen’s critique of the Afghan war is analytically flawed because it relies on anecdotal failures to draw sweeping, fatalistic conclusions about the impossibility of success in Afghanistan."

Nir Rosen has produced an essay that will satisfy critics of American foreign policy and infuriate its supporters, but its analytical framework is too crude to support the weight of conclusions it draws. The author's willingness to document failure in granular detail is valuable, but his tendency to derive sweeping policy conclusions from selected anecdotes produces an account that is less than the sum of its parts.

The essay's central claim—that America is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—rests on a static conception of Afghan society that historical evidence does not support. Afghanistan has never possessed strong centralized institutions, true, but neither had Iraq before 2003, yet the surge in American investment and effort produced measurable improvements in Iraqi security and governance, however imperfect and fragile. The assumption that Afghan society is somehow more resistant to change than other societies reflects Orientalist assumptions that Rosen's leftist sympathies should make him suspicious of.

Rosen's account of the Afghan security forces documents genuine failures but extrapolates from them to sweeping conclusions about institutional impossibility. The drug test results and equipment shortages he cites are real problems, but they reflect conditions that might be addressed through different approaches rather than permanent features of Afghan security institutions. The author's anonymous expert who asks whether we can "expect it to change their activities" if we take drug dealers through an eight-week program ignores the possibility of longer-term interventions, different recruitment standards, or alternative institutional designs.

The comparison with Iraq produces insight but also analytical distortion. Rosen correctly notes that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum, but he understates the degree to which American actions contributed to those conditions. The Awakening did not occur spontaneously; it emerged from sustained American military pressure, diplomatic engagement, and economic investment. The lesson for Afghanistan is not that COIN cannot work but that it requires different timelines and methods.

The author's institutional critique of American military culture deserves endorsement, but his conclusion that the institution is "incapable" of doing COIN goes beyond what the evidence supports. The military has demonstrated capacity for adaptation when properly directed. The development of specialized COIN training programs, the restructuring of brigade combat teams, and the genuine learning that occurred during Iraq operations all suggest institutional flexibility that Rosen's determinism cannot accommodate.

More problematically, the essay's treatment of the al-Qaeda threat suggests analytical bias. Rosen writes that September 11 "had little impact on the American economy or way of life" and suggests that terrorist networks cannot pose grave dangers to a vigilant superpower. This characterization ignores substantial economic disruption, permanent changes in security posture, and the demonstrated capacity of small groups to inflict disproportionate harm. The question of whether al-Qaeda represents an existential or even severe threat to American security remains legitimately debatable, but Rosen's treatment dismisses the threat more than it analyzes it.

The author's suggestion that American withdrawal might create space for negotiated accommodation with the Taliban deserves development that the essay does not provide. If the Taliban are primarily motivated by nationalist concerns rather than global jihadist ideology, and if their alliance with al-Qaeda reflects convenience rather than shared commitment, then American departure might indeed create conditions for political settlement. But Rosen never makes this argument explicitly or defends it against obvious objections.

The implicit recommendation of withdrawal that emerges from Rosen's analysis requires engagement with implementation questions. How would withdrawal be sequenced? What residual American presence, if any, would remain? How would regional actors be managed? What provisions would protect Afghan civilians who had collaborated with American forces? These practical questions cannot be avoided by appealing to the impossibility of success.

For all its limitations, the essay provides valuable corrective to optimistic narratives about American progress in Afghanistan. The specific failures Rosen documents—corrupt police, civilian casualties, electoral fraud, institutional dysfunction—are real and require acknowledgment. But honest assessment of difficulty need not collapse into fatalism about possibility. The path forward requires neither Rosen's despair nor official optimism but rather clear-eyed recognition of what can be achieved and what it will cost.
