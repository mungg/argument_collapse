"The most effective way to curb prescription drug abuse and accidental deaths is through a coordinated public health education campaign and the implementation of community harm reduction strategies focused on drug mixing and disposal."

The tragic speculation surrounding Whitney Houston’s death highlights a critical, often misunderstood facet of the prescription drug epidemic: the fatal synergy of drug mixing. The most effective way to curb these accidental deaths is through a massive, coordinated public health education campaign and the widespread implementation of community harm reduction strategies, particularly regarding the dangers of combining central nervous system (CNS) depressants.

When we look at toxicology reports in prescription drug fatalities, we rarely see a single drug acting alone. The vast majority of these accidental deaths are the result of combining opioid painkillers (like Vicodin or OxyContin) with anti-anxiety benzodiazepines (like Xanax or Valium) and alcohol. All three of these substances act as depressants on the respiratory system. A patient may take a prescribed dose of a painkiller, have a glass or two of wine, and take a sleeping pill or anxiety medication, completely unaware that this combination can cause them to simply stop breathing in their sleep.

We need an aggressive public awareness campaign, similar to the anti-smoking or drunk-driving campaigns of previous decades, to educate the public on the lethal dangers of mixing these substances. Warning labels on pill bottles are currently too small, too filled with medical jargon, and easily ignored. Pharmacies should be required to provide clear, brightly colored warnings about the specific dangers of mixing CNS depressants with alcohol.

Additionally, we must remove the excess supply of these medications from America's medicine cabinets. Millions of people hold onto leftover pills "just in case," creating a ready supply for curious teenagers or struggling family members. We need permanent, no-questions-asked prescription drug take-back boxes located in every pharmacy and police station in the country, not just temporary drug take-back days twice a year.

By shifting our focus toward educating patients about the lethal arithmetic of combining prescriptions with alcohol, and providing easy ways to dispose of leftover medication, we can empower communities to protect themselves. We can drastically reduce the number of accidental overdoses without interfering with the prescribing practices of doctors treating legitimate medical needs.
