"To realize the democratic potential of AI, we must move beyond corporate-controlled platforms and establish public, civic infrastructures—such as AI workshops in libraries—that foster collective 'ecological reason' rather than just individual efficiency."

Evgeny Morozov’s most valuable move is to refuse the cramped choice between resignation and refusal. The dominant debate about AI too often oscillates between “adopt it, regulate it, and compete” and “reject it as a poisoned artifact of empire and capital.” Morozov asks a harder question: what kinds of intelligence have been excluded by the institutional history of computing, and what would it mean to build technologies that nourish those forms rather than merely automate goal pursuit? That question is not nostalgic. It is urgently practical.

The phrase “ecological reason” is useful because it names something many people have felt in their own encounters with generative AI. The technology is neither simply a calculator nor simply a fake person. It is often most interesting when used not to complete a predefined task but to widen the space of possible tasks. Ask it for a list of ways to organize a neighborhood meeting, a set of metaphors for explaining a medical diagnosis, a translation that preserves tone rather than literal structure, or a set of alternative framings for a local budget fight, and it can help people see paths they had not imagined. Its strongest democratic promise lies less in “solving” politics than in helping citizens pose problems more richly.

But Morozov is right that this promise is truncated by the institutional home generative AI has been given. The most advanced systems are not public workshops. They are rented appliances. The user enters alone, pays per query or through a subscription, and receives a personalized output from a proprietary infrastructure whose costs are largely hidden. The model of intelligence here is not the town meeting, the library, the classroom, the union hall, or the public laboratory. It is the platform marketplace. Even when the interaction feels playful, it is shaped by metering, surveillance, data extraction, and dependency.

If we take Morozov’s argument seriously, the policy response cannot stop at transparency mandates or procurement rules. Those are necessary but insufficient. We need public institutions capable of making AI into civic infrastructure. The analogy should not be “a government ChatGPT,” if that means merely a public-sector chatbot answering tax questions. The better analogy is the public library: a staffed, trusted, locally embedded institution that gives people access not only to information but to tools, training, community, and a measure of autonomy from private gatekeepers.

Imagine a network of public AI workshops housed in libraries, universities, community colleges, municipal offices, and labor education centers. Their purpose would not be to maximize “AI adoption.” It would be to let people experiment with computational tools in relation to their own projects: tenants analyzing lease patterns, teachers adapting materials for multilingual classrooms, small farmers comparing climate adaptation strategies, disability advocates designing accessible public information, local historians building community archives, patients preparing better questions for doctors, workers scrutinizing scheduling algorithms. Such workshops would combine technical support with democratic facilitation. The point would be not only to produce outputs but to cultivate judgment.

This matters because ecological reason is not a private mental faculty. It is social. Morozov’s Stone Age flâneur is a useful provocation, but democratic intelligence rarely emerges from solitary wandering alone. It emerges when people bring partial experiences into contact: a nurse knows one thing about a hospital workflow, a patient another, a janitor another, a billing specialist another. AI can help visualize, summarize, translate, simulate, and reframe those perspectives. But only institutions can gather them, legitimate them, and turn them into collective capacity.

That is why the examples from Chile and Argentina are so important. Their lesson is not that Project Cybersyn should be recreated with modern dashboards, nor that planning is inherently emancipatory. It is that information technology can be placed inside political projects that ask citizens and workers what futures they want. In contemporary terms, the democratic question is not simply whether an algorithm is explainable. It is whether the people affected by a system can contest the goals it serves.

For instance, consider health care. A realist AI agenda says: use models to summarize patient records, reduce physician paperwork, detect risk, and improve efficiency. That may be worthwhile. But a democratic ecological agenda asks different questions. Why are records so unreadable in the first place? Which burdens are being shifted onto patients? What kinds of knowledge are excluded from clinical encounters? Could AI help patients compare treatment paths, understand uncertainty, document symptoms, and participate more fully in decisions? Could it help nurses and care workers identify systemic failures rather than merely accelerate throughput? The same technology can either deepen managerial control or expand participatory knowledge.

The same distinction applies in education. The narrow question is whether students will cheat. The broader question is whether schools will use AI to intensify assessment culture or to create more time for exploration. A school district could deploy AI to generate worksheets, grade essays faster, and monitor students more closely. Or it could use AI to help students compare explanations, interrogate sources, stage debates, translate family histories, prototype projects, and reflect on how tools shape thought. The latter requires teachers with time, autonomy, and training. It cannot be purchased as a subscription add-on.

The hard part is funding and governance. Public AI cannot mean handing public money to the same firms under friendlier branding. It must include public ownership or stewardship of crucial layers: datasets, evaluation regimes, compute capacity, open models where appropriate, and domain-specific tools. There are already hints of such possibilities in open-source AI, national research clouds, university consortia, and nonprofit model development. But without a political coalition, these fragments will remain subordinate to corporate infrastructure.

A “Humanity Lobby,” to use Morozov’s phrase, would need to include teachers, librarians, unions, patient groups, artists, disability advocates, local governments, environmental organizations, and public-interest technologists. Its demand should be simple: if AI is becoming a general-purpose infrastructure, then the public must have institutions capable of shaping it for public ends. Not merely auditing it after deployment. Not merely begging companies to behave. Shaping it.

This also means accepting some limits. Ecological reason is incompatible with the fantasy of frictionless personalization at infinite scale. A democratic AI system might deliberately encourage reuse, sharing, and slower deliberation. It might tell a user: “Someone in your community already built a similar resource; would you like to adapt it?” It might privilege local knowledge over generic optimization. It might measure success by collective learning rather than engagement, productivity, or revenue. Such design choices will look inefficient from the standpoint of the market. That is precisely the point.

Morozov’s essay risks sounding abstract because “ecological reason” is a large philosophical banner. But its implications are concrete. Put AI in libraries, not only apps. Fund civic compute, not only corporate clouds. Treat datasets as public and communal resources where possible, not merely proprietary assets. Evaluate systems by whether they expand participation and judgment, not only by benchmark performance. Give workers and citizens authority over the goals embedded in automation.

The AI we deserve will not arrive from better prompts or kinder CEOs. It will require institutions that let people use computation to discover, debate, and revise their ends together. That is a democratic project before it is a technical one. Morozov is right: the question is not whether machines can be intelligent in our place. It is whether we can become intelligent enough, collectively, to build machines that do not make us smaller.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current 'platform marketplace' model of AI truncates its potential by framing users as isolated consumers and subjecting them to private surveillance and data extraction.
  2. 02Public institutions like libraries and universities should serve as the primary sites for AI engagement, providing tools and training that prioritize community autonomy over private gatekeeping.
  3. 03True democratic intelligence is social and emerges when AI is used to gather and synthesize the diverse, partial experiences of different citizens into collective capacity.
  4. 04The democratic value of AI lies in its ability to help citizens 'pose problems' and contest the goals of systems, rather than simply automating predefined tasks for managerial efficiency.
  5. 05A 'Humanity Lobby' consisting of diverse social groups must advocate for public ownership or stewardship of essential AI layers, including datasets and compute capacity.
  6. 06Democratic AI design should prioritize collective learning and the reuse of resources, even if these choices appear 'inefficient' from a market-driven perspective.
