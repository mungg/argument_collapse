"The United States should modernize its nuclear arsenal to ensure safety, reliability, and presidential control while rejecting the development of smaller, more usable weapons that lower the threshold for nuclear conflict."

Modernization should begin with a simple distinction: keeping nuclear weapons safe, secure and reliable is not the same thing as making them more usable. The United States has a legitimate interest in replacing aging submarines, bombers, missiles, command systems and warheads whose components were designed decades ago. A deterrent that adversaries doubt, or that presidents cannot control with confidence in a crisis, is not stabilizing. But modernization becomes dangerous when it drifts from stewardship into mission expansion.

That is why smaller, more targetable nuclear weapons deserve special skepticism. Their advocates argue that a president needs more credible options than massive retaliation. If Russia used a low-yield weapon in Europe, for example, Washington would not want to choose between surrender and city destruction. That concern is real. Yet the answer is not to make nuclear war appear more manageable. The most important feature of nuclear weapons is not their yield but the threshold they cross. Once the United States uses a nuclear weapon, even a “small” one, escalation control becomes a theory, not a guarantee.

An appropriate modernization should therefore include hardened and redundant command-and-control systems, secure communications with submarines and bombers, rigorous warhead surveillance, and replacement of delivery platforms where delay would create genuine vulnerability. It should also include cybersecurity, insider-threat protections and accident-prevention measures, which are less dramatic than new warheads but more directly related to nuclear safety.

It should exclude new nuclear missions, new battlefield nuclear weapons and designs advertised as more precise or more flexible. The United States already has a devastatingly credible deterrent. Its ballistic missile submarines alone can survive an attack and impose unacceptable costs. That fact, not a menu of low-yield options, is the core of deterrence.

The United States should also pursue modernization in a way consistent with arms control. Extending New START, preserving verification, reducing excess deployed warheads and keeping open channels with Moscow are not favors to adversaries. They are tools for avoiding miscalculation. Deterrence is not strengthened by an arms race that produces more weapons than any rational strategy requires.

The right goal is a smaller, safer and unquestionably survivable arsenal. Modernize the parts that keep the deterrent reliable and under presidential control. Reject the parts that blur the line between deterring nuclear war and preparing to fight one.
