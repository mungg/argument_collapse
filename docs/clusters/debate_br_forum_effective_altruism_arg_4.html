**What Effective Altruism Misses About Being a Person**

Peter Singer's essay has the curious quality of presenting a vision of the moral life that almost no one, including Singer himself, actually lives, and treating this as a feature rather than a bug. "Living a fully ethical life involves doing the most good we can." A "minimally acceptable ethical life" requires donating a substantial portion of one's spare resources. The rest is concession to human frailty: of course we cannot really be perfect, of course we may love our own children more, of course we leave ourselves "time and resources to relax." But the bar is set, and our lives are measured against it, and we are all by definition falling short.

I want to push back on the picture of ethics this implies. Singer treats morality as fundamentally a matter of maximizing impersonal goodness, with our particular attachments, projects, and identities appearing as obstacles to be managed — partiality "accepted" only because it would be "unrealistic" to require otherwise. But this gets the structure of ethical life almost exactly backwards. Our particular attachments are not concessions wrung from a more demanding impartial morality; they are the substance out of which moral life is built. To be a person at all is to stand in relations — to parents, children, friends, neighbors, fellow citizens, colleagues — that come with their own obligations, intelligible only from within them. The view from nowhere, from which all suffering counts equally and the question is simply how to minimize the aggregate, is not the deepest moral standpoint. It is, in many ways, a refusal of the moral standpoint, because it refuses the particularity that makes obligations recognizable.

Bernard Williams made this point decades ago in his criticism of utilitarianism, and the point still stands. A man who calculates whether saving his drowning wife is permitted by impartial morality has, as Williams put it, "one thought too many." He is not a better husband for the calculation; he has failed at being a husband, and at being a person, in a basic way. Singer's framework, despite all its qualifications about loving one's own children, cannot really accommodate this. Within his system the love is always provisional, always answerable upward to the demand to do the most good. The "spare" resources are whatever is left after our partialities have taken their permitted bite. But what counts as "spare" is precisely the question, and it cannot be answered without a substantive account of what a human life is for.

The arts question is a useful case. Singer says David Geffen could have found better uses for his $100 million than renovating a concert hall. On a narrowly consequentialist calculus, this is obvious — the marginal dollar spent on bed nets prevents more suffering than the marginal dollar spent on Lincoln Center acoustics. But why should the question be settled by that calculus? Music, scholarship, scientific curiosity, civic monuments, sacred architecture — these are among the things that make a human civilization rather than a managed welfare zone. A world that had eliminated malaria but built no concert halls and wrote no symphonies would not be a flourishing world. It would be a kept world. Singer concedes that the arts will be worthy "in a world that had overcome extreme poverty," as if culture were a luxury to be unlocked only after triage. This is exactly the philistinism that great moral traditions — religious and secular — have always pushed back against. There has never been a society so prosperous that suffering was absent, and on Singer's logic there never will be one in which it is permissible to build a cathedral.

I do not want to deny what is right in effective altruism. Most middle-class Westerners give too little and too thoughtlessly. We could do enormous good with money we currently spend on trivialities. A movement that nudges us toward giving more, and toward giving more carefully, is on balance a gift. But this is a moral revival, not a moral revolution. It does not require us to accept that ethics is fundamentally a maximization problem, or that our local loves are concessions to weakness, or that the value of a human life can be cashed out in dollars per QALY. We can give generously and remain people, embedded in the rich particularities that make giving — and being given to — meaningful in the first place. Singer's framework cannot quite see why this matters. Most of us can, and should hold onto the sight.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Personal relationships and local obligations are the primary substance of ethical life, not obstacles or concessions to be managed by an impartial moral framework.
  2. 02The requirement to justify personal loyalties through a universal calculus alienates individuals from the very relationships that make them human.
  3. 03Defining 'spare' resources for donation requires a substantive understanding of what constitutes a meaningful human life, which a purely quantitative approach lacks.
  4. 04Prioritizing basic survival over cultural achievements like the arts reduces human civilization to a managed welfare zone rather than a flourishing society.
  5. 05One can advocate for more thoughtful and generous giving without reducing ethics to a mathematical maximization problem or viewing local loves as moral weaknesses.

**Charity Begins at Home, and So Does Solidarity**

Peter Singer is one of the most influential moral philosophers alive, and he has spent his career arguing that we owe distant strangers what we owe our own. The argument has the force of pure logic on its side: a child is a child, suffering is suffering, geography is morally arbitrary. I want to suggest, with some hesitation, that the argument is wrong — or at least that it proves much less than Singer thinks it does, and that the movement it has spawned is shallower than it appears.

The communitarian tradition, from Aristotle through Burke to MacIntyre and Sandel, has insisted that human beings are not free-floating moral atoms but creatures situated in particular communities — families, neighborhoods, religious congregations, nations — and that our obligations are differentiated by these situations. A father owes his children what he does not owe his neighbor's children. A citizen owes his fellow citizens what he does not owe foreigners. A parishioner owes the elderly widow down the pew what he does not owe an equally needy stranger across the world. This is not parochialism dressed up as principle. It is the recognition that moral obligations track the relationships that make us who we are, and that to deny these obligations is to deny something essential about human personhood.

Singer waves at this concern with a brief paragraph about why one's own children may take priority — but only, he says, because it would be "unrealistic" to require otherwise and because children "thrive in close, loving families." Notice the structure of the concession. Partiality is permitted only on consequentialist grounds; it is a tactic in service of the impartial aggregate. The communitarian view is the opposite: partiality is the basic moral fact, and impartial concern is a derivative virtue, real and important but always embedded in and constrained by particular obligations.

Why does this matter for effective altruism? Because the movement, in its purer forms, ends up corroding precisely the local institutions and loyalties that make sustained moral life possible. Consider Singer's stricture against giving to one's alma mater or local church or community arts center, on the grounds that the marginal dollar would do more good distributed as bed nets in Africa. By this logic, virtually every form of giving that builds and sustains a community — the local food bank, the parish school, the synagogue building fund, the volunteer fire department, the friends of the public library — is suboptimal and should be redirected. But these institutions are the connective tissue of civil society. They are how, in Tocqueville's phrase, Americans have historically formed themselves into a people capable of self-government and mutual care. To hollow them out in the name of impartial maximization is to trade a durable, locally rooted ethical culture for a thin globalist transfer regime.

Religious traditions have understood this. The tithe, which Singer mentions approvingly, was historically given to the church or the local poor — that is, to the community of which one was a part. The "10 percent" was not a calculation of aggregate utility; it was an act of belonging. To recast it as a payment into an optimizing fund that distributes anonymously to wherever the spreadsheet points is to misunderstand what the practice was for. Religious giving builds the church that visits the sick, buries the dead, raises the children, and holds the community together. EA-style giving does some good things, but it does not do that, and it cannot.

There is also a political dimension. A society in which the privileged discharge their moral obligations by writing checks to faraway causes, rather than by participating in the messy, frustrating work of their own polity, is a society that is hollowing out from the middle. Singer's young people are encouraged to take high-paying finance jobs and donate to AMF. They are not encouraged to coach Little League, sit on the school board, run for city council, or take care of their aging parents — though every one of these matters more, in the texture of an actual life and a livable society, than a donation that they will never see at work.

I do not deny that we have obligations to distant strangers. Of course we do. But these obligations are added to, not substituted for, our particular ones. The trouble with Singer's framework is that it forgets this, and the trouble with effective altruism is that it institutionalizes the forgetting. A better ethics begins at home — and from there, reaches outward.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moral obligations are naturally differentiated by one's specific situation in communities like families and nations, rather than being a calculation of aggregate global suffering.
  2. 02Singer’s concession to parental partiality is insufficient because it treats personal love as a mere tactic for achieving impartial results rather than a fundamental moral fact.
  3. 03The movement's focus on maximizing marginal utility threatens to hollow out the local institutions, such as churches and community centers, that serve as the vital connective tissue of a self-governing society.
  4. 04Traditional practices like tithing are acts of communal belonging and solidarity that cannot be replaced by anonymous, spreadsheet-driven global transfers.
  5. 05Effective altruism encourages a detached form of morality where the privileged write checks to distant causes instead of engaging in the essential, hands-on work of maintaining their own local polities and families.
