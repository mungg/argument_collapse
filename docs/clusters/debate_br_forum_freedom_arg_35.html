Diversified Gemini

"To create a truly emancipatory vision of freedom for the 21st century, the Left must expand Aziz Rana’s critique of settler-colonial domination to include ecological justice and the recognition of our interdependence with the biosphere."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" provides a vital historical mapping of how the American conception of liberty became intertwined with domination, exclusion, and imperial expansion. By illuminating the "zero-sum" nature of settler-colonial freedom, Rana effectively dismantles the mythology of the rugged, self-sufficient individual, revealing that this independence was paid for by the expropriation of others. However, as we stand on the precipice of irreversible climate catastrophe in 2023, Rana’s framework, while necessary, remains incomplete. It is not just marginalized human populations that have been subjugated to fuel this exclusionary freedom; it is the Earth itself. To forge a truly emancipatory and solidaristic vision of freedom for the 21st century, the Left must expand Rana’s critique to encompass ecological justice, recognizing that true solidarity must extend to the non-human world and to future generations.

Rana correctly identifies that for early American settlers, "the great bounty of North America was a seemingly inexhaustible land base for economic prosperity." He focuses heavily on the human cost of this expansion: the genocide and displacement of Indigenous nations. But this expansion was also fundamentally an act of ecological devastation. The settler concept of freedom—defined as self-mastery, property ownership, and the right to extract wealth without limit—viewed nature not as a web of interdependent relationships, but as a dead repository of resources waiting to be commodified. The forests were timber, the plains were real estate, and the ground was a bank of fossil fuels.

This paradigm of extraction did not end with the closing of the frontier; it merely scaled up and went global, fueled by the carbon economy. The mid-century middle-class prosperity that Rana discusses—the very prosperity that cemented the individualistic, "free on my own" cultural narrative—was entirely dependent on the hyper-extraction and burning of cheap fossil fuels, backed by the global reach of the U.S. military. The "American Way of Life," often conflated with freedom itself, is structurally designed around ecological deficit spending: sprawling suburbs, car-dependent infrastructure, and massive, disposable consumption.

Therefore, Rana’s diagnosis of the right wing's "market fundamentalism" and "extreme individualism" must be understood as not just a threat to democratic institutions, but as an existential threat to planetary survival. The right-wing insistence on deregulation and the dismantling of the state is, in practice, a demand for the freedom to pollute, the freedom to extract, and the freedom to externalize the catastrophic costs of climate change onto the global poor and future generations.

Rana argues that the Left must respond by building institutions that foster "inclusive solidarity, mutual respect, and common concern," pointing primarily to labor unions and voting rights. While democratizing the workplace is crucial, a labor movement that remains wedded to the fossil fuel economy will ultimately fail. If collective self-rule in the workplace simply means workers democratically managing the extraction of coal or the production of gas-guzzling vehicles, we are merely democratizing the engine of our own destruction.

An ecological vision of freedom requires us to radically redefine "economic independence and material success." We must recognize, as Rana suggests, that "no one can be free on their own," but we must extend that interdependence beyond human society to the biosphere. Freedom cannot mean the limitless capacity to consume. True freedom in an era of ecological limits means security: the security of clean air, clean water, a stable climate, and sustainable food systems.

This requires a massive, state-led project of institutional transformation—a Green New Deal. We must build new institutions that coordinate the rapid decarbonization of the economy, replacing extractive industries with regenerative ones. This project aligns perfectly with Rana’s goal of creating "practical improvements to people’s daily lives" and shifting the cultural narrative. By establishing a federal jobs guarantee tied to green infrastructure, care work, and ecological restoration, we can provide the material security workers need, decoupling the right to a livelihood from the necessity of planetary destruction.

Furthermore, Rana’s critique of American empire and foreign policy takes on an even more urgent dimension when viewed through an ecological lens. The U.S. military is the single largest institutional consumer of hydrocarbons in the world. Its global footprint is largely designed to secure access to the very fossil fuels that are destroying a habitable future. Redirecting the $900 billion defense budget, as Rana advocates, is not just a matter of anti-imperial ethics; it is a matter of basic planetary survival. Those funds must be redirected toward global climate finance, technology transfer, and reparative policies for the Global South, which suffers the most severe consequences of a crisis it did the least to cause.

Similarly, Rana’s discussion of immigration must address climate migration. The "international economic pressures" and U.S. policies that generate migration patterns are increasingly exacerbated by climate-driven droughts, superstorms, and agricultural collapse. A solidaristic approach to borders must include recognizing climate refugees and taking responsibility for the ecological debt the U.S. owes the rest of the world.

Aziz Rana has pointed the way forward by demanding we abandon the zero-sum, extractive logic of the past. But the ultimate test of our solidarity will not just be how we share power in the workplace or the voting booth. It will be whether we can build a society that understands its profound dependence on the natural world. Reclaiming freedom means escaping the tyranny of endless extraction and embracing the deep, liberating responsibility of planetary stewardship.
