"While American citizens who join enemy forces can be lawful military targets, the executive branch must be subject to independent judicial oversight and strict criteria to ensure these killings do not violate constitutional due process."

The Constitution does not make citizenship a battlefield immunity card. An American who joins an enemy force and takes an operational role in attacks against the United States may be a lawful military target, just as a citizen fighting in a conventional enemy army would be. But citizenship does mean the executive cannot be the sole author, interpreter and judge of the rules for killing him.

At minimum, several standards should be required. First, the government must show that the citizen is not merely a propagandist, sympathizer or former associate, but a senior operational member of Al Qaeda or an associated force covered by the 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force. Second, the threat must be truly imminent — not “someday dangerous,” but tied to ongoing planning or command responsibility for attacks where waiting would materially increase the danger. The white paper’s apparent dilution of imminence is the most troubling feature of the policy.

Third, capture must be infeasible, not merely inconvenient or politically awkward. The government should have to explain why arrest, extradition, cooperation with local authorities or other less lethal means cannot reasonably work. Fourth, the operation must comply with the laws of war: necessity, distinction, proportionality and a reasonable effort to avoid civilian casualties.

The hard question is process. A full criminal trial is not always possible before a strike in Yemen or Pakistan. But due process is not satisfied by an internal executive branch memo. Before targeting a citizen, the administration should have to present classified evidence to an independent court or a specially designated federal judge, much as sensitive surveillance applications are reviewed. In the rare case where urgency makes prior review impossible, prompt after-the-fact review and notification to Congress should be mandatory.

The president needs power to defend the country, but a power to kill citizens based on secret legal standards and undisclosed evidence is too dangerous to leave unchecked. Due process may be adapted to war, but it cannot be erased by it.

Most of the commentary on the white paper focuses on the wrong question. We argue about whether the executive's procedures are sufficient, as if procedure were the heart of the matter. Once you accept that we are in a global, permanent armed conflict against an enemy of executive definition, the targeted-killing memos follow with grim logic. Of course the executive can kill enemy combatants. Of course citizenship doesn't immunize battlefield enemies. The legal infrastructure forces these conclusions. So the meaningful debate is not about white-paper procedures but about whether Congress will reclaim its constitutional responsibility to define the conflict.

Congress should repeal and replace the 2001 AUMF with a narrower, time-limited authorization specifying which groups it covers and which it does not. It should require executive reporting on targeted-killing criteria. It should mandate that the addition of new organizations to the target list requires fresh congressional authorization. Without that structural fix, debating the precise definition of "imminence" in a Justice Department memo is like rearranging furniture in a burning house. The fundamental issue is who decides when the country is at war, with whom, and for how long. The Constitution gives that power to Congress. Congress has surrendered it. Until that abdication is reversed, every refinement of executive procedure is window dressing on an unaccountable power.
