"To address the crisis of legitimacy and effectiveness in modern democracy, we should move beyond traditional elected representation and adopt sortition—the random selection of citizens for legislative assemblies."

Ira Katznelson’s magisterial essay asks whether representative democracy can be saved. But what if the premise is wrong? What if the problem is not the weakness of representation, but the impossibility of scale? The nation-state legislature—a few hundred people claiming to embody millions—was always a compromise between democratic aspiration and practical necessity in a large territory. Today, the march of technology and the evolution of democratic thought make possible something far more radical: the replacement of elected legislatures with randomly selected citizens’ assemblies, augmented by expert testimony and professional facilitation. This is not a call to tinker with representation; it is a call to transcend it.

The case for sortition—governance by lottery—is ancient, rooted in Athenian democracy, but it has been revived in recent decades by thinkers like John Burnheim and the late Robert Dahl. The core insight is devastating: election, by its nature, selects for ambition, wealth, and demographic narrowness, while a random sample, if truly representative, mirrors the population’s interests and perspectives far more accurately. A legislature composed of 600 randomly selected citizens, serving staggered terms with adequate compensation and training, would remove the distortions of campaign finance, the pathologies of the permanent campaign, and the distortions of gerrymandering. It would end the reign of career politicians and break the nexus of lobbying. Most importantly, it would restore democratic legitimacy by giving every citizen an equal probability of actually making the laws, transforming passive spectators into potential participants.

Katznelson might object that this is a “plebiscitary” or “populist” temptation that abandons the deliberative virtues of representation. But the evidence from existing mini-publics suggests otherwise. The British Columbia Citizens’ Assembly on Electoral Reform in 2004 brought together 160 randomly selected residents who spent a year learning about electoral systems, hearing from experts, and deliberating across all the partisan and ideological lines that plague Canada’s legislatures. They produced a carefully reasoned recommendation for a single transferable vote system that won strong support from the participants and nearly passed a provincial referendum. Similar experiments in Ireland, with the Convention on the Constitution (2012–2014), demonstrated that ordinary citizens, when given time, information, and a serious mandate, are more than capable of tackling complex constitutional questions with a nuance and civic spirit that professional politicians often lack. The recommendations on marriage equality that emerged from that process helped pave the way for the historic 2015 referendum.

The objection that such assemblies lack accountability misunderstands how accountability works in mass democracy. The accountability of elected representatives to a diffuse public is largely fictional, mediated by party labels, interest-group scorecards, and media spin. In contrast, a citizens’ assembly is directly accountable to the whole community through the transparent publication of its deliberations; its members return to ordinary life and live under the laws they helped craft. Rotating membership ensures constant refreshment of perspectives. Moreover, such assemblies need not replace all legislative functions. They could be integrated into a hybrid system where they set broad policy frameworks or handle particularly contentious issues, while representative bodies continue to manage routine governance. The aim is not to destroy representation but to correct its worst defects by adding a new, more legitimate foundation.

The current anxieties Katznelson describes—about money, about drift, about the loss of civic virtue—would be radically diminished in a sortition-based system. There would be no campaign expenditures, because there would be no campaigns. Legislative activity could not be captive to the short-term electoral calculus, because members would not face reelection. The deliberative quality would rise as citizens, freed from party discipline, would engage earnestly with the evidence. And the experience of serving in government would itself be a powerful force for civic education, spreading democratic competence through the population as participants returned to their communities.

Is this realistic? By August 2015, the idea is gaining traction in academic circles and local experiments, though national implementation remains distant. But so did universal suffrage once seem distant. The point is not to predict when it will happen, but to insist that our horizon of democratic reform be not limited to patching a broken model. Katznelson worries that alternatives to representation will lead to illiberalism. But if that alternative is chosen randomly from among us all, bound by a constitution and rights, and charged with deliberation, it may well be the most liberal, most democratic possibility of all. The Capitol’s scaffolding will come down. Perhaps it’s time to build a different building altogether.
