"The widespread condemnation of Donald Trump’s comments demonstrates that American society has made significant progress in its standards regarding the treatment of women, even if political reactions remain inconsistently applied."

The reaction to Donald Trump’s comments says something more complicated than “America is hopelessly sexist.” It suggests that standards have changed substantially, perhaps more than many critics admit. A presidential nominee was caught speaking in a crude and indefensible way about women, and the condemnation was swift, broad and bipartisan. That is evidence of progress, not merely evidence of backwardness.

No decent person should defend Trump’s words. They were ugly, boastful and demeaning. If taken literally, they described conduct that would be assault. The country was right to recoil. But the very fact that so many voters recoiled matters. Republican leaders withdrew endorsements. Conservative newspapers that had never backed a Democrat found new reason to repudiate him. Evangelical supporters struggled to explain themselves. Even many people who still planned to vote for Trump did not say the comments were acceptable; they said they were weighing them against other concerns, such as the Supreme Court, abortion, trade, immigration or Hillary Clinton’s record. One may dislike that calculation, but it is not the same as indifference to women.

There is a danger in treating this episode as proof that little has changed. In the past, powerful men routinely survived far worse behavior with little public scrutiny. Sexual harassment in the workplace was once treated as a joke, a woman’s burden or the price of ambition. Marital rape was not even recognized in law for much of American history. Women were excluded from many professions and political institutions. Today, a secretly recorded conversation from eleven years ago can shake a presidential campaign because public norms have shifted. That is not a small development.

At the same time, the reaction exposed weaknesses in the way Americans discuss women’s dignity. Some of Trump’s defenders used the phrase “locker room talk,” which unfairly smeared men and trivialized the seriousness of the words. Some opponents, however, seemed less interested in consistent moral standards than in partisan advantage. The Clinton camp and its allies were eager to make Trump answer for his treatment of women, but many Democrats spent the 1990s minimizing allegations involving Bill Clinton. Conservatives are hardly innocent either; some discovered feminist language only when it served their anti-Trump case. The public should be wary of selective outrage.

Progress for women is real when standards apply across party lines, across class lines and across institutions. If Trump’s comments are disqualifying because they reveal contempt for women, then similar contempt should matter when it appears in entertainment, workplaces, universities, families and among politicians of every ideology. If we are serious, we should teach boys and men that respect is not a slogan, and we should judge leaders by character as well as policy.

Trump revealed that the culture has moved far enough to punish a kind of male swagger once excused as harmless. He also revealed that moral judgment in politics is often inconsistent. The lesson should not be despair. It should be a demand for equal standards, honestly applied.

  3. 03The defense of Trump's comments as 'locker room talk' and the selective outrage from both political parties reveal that moral standards for women's dignity are often compromised for partisan advantage.

"The widespread and bipartisan condemnation of Donald Trump’s offensive comments demonstrates that society has made significant progress in establishing severe consequences for predatory behavior that was once tolerated."

To understand what the reaction to Donald Trump’s 2005 "Access Hollywood" tape reveals about the progress of women, we must look at history. It is tempting, in the heat of a vitriolic election season, to view the existence of the tape as proof that society remains fundamentally hostile to women. But the actual response to the tape—the immediate, overwhelming, and bipartisan revulsion—demonstrates precisely the opposite. It shows us how far we have come.

Consider the reality of the American workplace and political sphere just a few decades ago. In the "Mad Men" era of the 1960s, or even the corporate culture of the 1980s, powerful men routinely engaged in the kind of predatory behavior Trump described, often with absolute impunity. So-called "locker room talk" and the nonconsensual grabbing of women were frequently dismissed as boys being boys, a mere hazard of being a woman in the public sphere. When women did speak up, they were historically the ones punished, silenced, or pushed out of their industries.

Even in the 1990s, during the scandals surrounding Bill Clinton, the cultural instinct to blame the women involved or brush off allegations of harassment as private, irrelevant matters was remarkably strong.

Yet last week, when the tape of Trump boasting about using his celebrity to sexually assault women surfaced, the cultural immune system kicked in with unprecedented speed. Dozens of elected officials in his own party fled his campaign. Conservative media figures who had defended his every previous gaffe found themselves unable to stomach this one. The public consensus, across almost all major institutions, was swift: boasting about sexual assault is utterly disqualifying for the highest office in the land.

This is what progress looks like. It does not mean that sexism has been eradicated, nor does it mean that predatory men no longer exist. Progress is not the absence of bad behavior; it is the presence of severe, inescapable consequences for that behavior.

The fact that Donald Trump’s words from 2005 are causing his campaign to implode in 2016 is a testament to the tireless work of feminist activists, educators, and everyday women who have spent the last half-century shifting the cultural baseline. They have successfully moved the goalposts of acceptable behavior. Trump is discovering, in real-time, that the patriarchal passes handed out freely in the 20th century have expired in the 21st. We should take heart in this outrage. It is the sound of a society that has finally learned to say "enough."

  4. 04True progress is defined not by the total absence of sexism, but by the presence of inescapable social and political consequences for sexist actions.

Diversified GPT

"The widespread condemnation of Donald Trump’s comments as disqualifying rather than merely embarrassing demonstrates that the women’s movement has successfully shifted public moral standards toward recognizing and rejecting sexual predation."

The most striking thing about the response to Donald Trump’s comments is not that some people defended him. In a polarized country, some people will defend almost anything said by their side’s nominee. The more important fact is that the comments were widely understood as disqualifying, not merely embarrassing. That is a sign of progress.

It is worth remembering how dramatically public standards have changed. For much of American history, sexual aggression by powerful men was treated as a private matter, if it was acknowledged at all. Women who complained risked humiliation, job loss, or disbelief. Today, a secretly recorded conversation from 2005 can upend a presidential campaign because the public has learned to hear the difference between vulgarity and predation. Trump did not just use dirty words. He bragged that fame allowed him to touch women without consent. The backlash shows that many Americans now recognize that distinction.

That said, I am wary of turning the episode into a sweeping indictment of all men, all conservatives, or all Trump voters. Some voters who continue to support him are making a terrible moral compromise, but not all of them are endorsing sexual assault. Many are voting through the lens of party, courts, immigration, trade, or distrust of Hillary Clinton. That does not excuse their choice, but it does complicate the claim that the tape simply reveals a nation comfortable with misogyny.

The response also exposes a need for consistency. Democrats who are rightly appalled by Trump should not minimize allegations against men on their own side. Republicans who suddenly found their moral voice after the tape should ask why earlier attacks on women—Megyn Kelly, Carly Fiorina, Alicia Machado—were not enough. If the standard is that women should not be demeaned, harassed, or treated as props, then it has to apply even when the offender is politically useful.

The “locker room talk” defense was especially revealing because so many men rejected it. That matters. Progress for women requires not only women speaking up but men refusing to treat degradation as a bonding ritual. Fathers should teach sons that masculinity does not require conquest, humiliation, or entitlement. Employers should make clear that status never grants permission. Voters should not reward leaders who confuse dominance with strength.

Still, the reaction gives reason for guarded optimism. A culture does not change all at once. It changes when excuses stop working as well as they used to. In this case, the old excuses—boys will be boys, it was just talk, powerful men are like that—met real resistance. That resistance is not enough, but it is not nothing. It is evidence that the women’s movement has altered the moral expectations of public life.
