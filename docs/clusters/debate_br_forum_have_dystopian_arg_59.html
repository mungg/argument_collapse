The Democratic Crisis Is the Crisis

Daron Acemoglu's essay treats the threats to democracy and the threats to labor as parallel problems, two arms of the same AI octopus. I want to suggest a sharper claim: the democratic crisis is the master problem, and everything else—including the labor-market disruption he describes—follows from it. Get democracy right and the rest is tractable. Get democracy wrong and no amount of cleverness about labor-augmenting AI will save us.

Consider the asymmetry between the two challenges. A misdirected AI labor market produces lower wages, more inequality, and political resentment—all serious harms. But these harms are reversible. The 19th-century industrial economy went through similar disruptions and was eventually disciplined by union organizing, progressive taxation, and the regulatory state. The harms were real, but the political system that produced the remedies remained functional. People could vote, organize, publish, and protest. The feedback loop between popular grievance and political response, however imperfect, worked.

A democratic crisis is different. If the institutions through which we make collective decisions are themselves degraded—captured by oligarchs, polluted by disinformation, surveilled into self-censorship, or simply broken by polarization—then we lose the ability to respond to anything, including the labor-market problems Acemoglu describes. Democracy is not just one valuable thing among many; it is the meta-institution through which we adjudicate everything else. Damaging it makes all our other problems insoluble.

The evidence that AI is doing exactly this damage is, I am afraid, mounting fast. Acemoglu mentions the Cambridge Analytica scandal and Facebook's algorithmic amplification of polarizing content. These are not isolated incidents; they are features of an attention economy that has emerged in the last fifteen years. Recommendation algorithms optimize for engagement, which empirically correlates with outrage, fear, and tribal identity. The result is information environments in which calm, fact-based deliberation cannot compete with rage and conspiracy. The January 6 attack on the U.S. Capitol was not produced by AI alone, but it would have been unimaginable without the recommendation systems that channeled tens of millions of Americans into self-reinforcing alternative-reality bubbles over the preceding five years.

Surveillance is the other half of this story, and the more alarming half. We are now living through the construction, in plain sight, of an infrastructure for monitoring every aspect of human activity. The components are familiar: facial recognition in public spaces, license-plate readers, smart-home devices, cellphone location tracking, social-media monitoring, biometric employee surveillance, and—coming fast—aggregation services that fuse these data streams into comprehensive profiles. China's social-credit system is the most visible example, but the United States is building something not entirely unlike it through the private sector, with very little democratic discussion. The capabilities now exist, in commercial form, to track individuals' political opinions, religious practices, sexual behavior, medical conditions, and personal relationships at granular scale.

Why does this matter for democracy? Because the democratic citizen presupposed by liberal political theory is one who can think, speak, and act without being monitored by powerful actors who may retaliate. Once that condition fails, the social fabric on which democracy depends—the spaces of free conversation, dissent, association, and political experimentation—begins to wither. People do not need to be jailed for their views; they simply need to know that their views are being recorded and could be used against them. This produces a chilling effect that operates through self-censorship rather than overt repression. We can already see this in surveys of journalists, activists, and ordinary citizens who report changing their behavior in response to perceived surveillance.

Acemoglu sees this, but I want to push the implications further than he does. The first implication is that the labor-market agenda has to be subordinated, where conflicts arise, to the democratic agenda. If we have to choose between an AI policy that produces marginally more good jobs and one that produces a marginally healthier information ecosystem, we should choose the latter. The labor market can be fixed through ordinary politics; a degraded politics cannot be fixed at all.

Second, the platform problem is the locus of the democratic crisis, and it requires interventions far more aggressive than anything Acemoglu proposes. The current platform regime—a small number of advertising-funded firms operating recommendation algorithms whose details are trade secrets, shielded by Section 230, and accountable to no one—is structurally incompatible with a functioning democracy. Reforms that have been seriously discussed and should be on the table include: (1) significant amendment of Section 230 to make platforms liable for the specific design choices that amplify particular content; (2) interoperability requirements that allow users to take their social graphs to alternative providers, breaking the network effects that protect incumbents; (3) bans on micro-targeted political advertising; (4) data-portability and data-minimization mandates akin to GDPR but stronger; (5) algorithmic transparency requirements, including the right of independent researchers to audit recommendation systems; (6) outright bans on certain manipulative design patterns, like infinite scroll and engagement-maximizing notifications, in contexts where minors are involved; (7) a public-option social network funded through fees on commercial platforms.

These reforms will be opposed ferociously by the platform companies, which is the third implication: the political economy of reform requires breaking the power of those companies. Acemoglu notes that a handful of firms dominate AI research and the public sphere. He treats this as a problem to be worked around through norm change and government redirection. I think it has to be confronted directly through antitrust. The pending FTC case against Facebook, the Google antitrust suit, the various state actions—these are not distractions from the AI debate; they are at its heart. A platform sector with five or six dominant firms cannot be effectively regulated; their resources for lobbying, litigation, and regulatory capture are too great. Breaking them up is a precondition for the rest of the agenda.

Fourth, surveillance specifically requires legal regimes that do not yet exist. The Fourth Amendment was written for an era of physical searches and has been ill-adapted to an information economy in which the most sensitive personal data is held by third parties and accessed by the state through subpoena rather than warrant. We need a comprehensive privacy statute on the European model, but stronger: hard limits on the collection and retention of biometric data, bans on facial recognition in public spaces (with narrow, court-supervised exceptions), strict purpose-limitation requirements for any commercial data use, and a private right of action for violations.

Fifth, electoral integrity requires updated infrastructure: paper ballots, risk-limiting audits, public funding of campaigns, restrictions on coordinated inauthentic behavior on platforms, and serious cybersecurity investments in state and local election systems. Acemoglu rightly notes that AI threatens democracy in ways we are only beginning to understand. The defensive infrastructure has to be built before the next wave of attacks.

Acemoglu's essay is appropriately worried about democracy but treats the democratic agenda as parallel to the economic one. I think it has to be primary. The good news is that the agenda is fairly concrete; the bad news is that it requires confronting powerful incumbents who have so far succeeded in blocking even modest reforms. Whether democracy survives the AI transition depends less on what we do about jobs than on whether we can find the political will to discipline the platforms and reclaim the public information environment they have privatized.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike labor-market harms, which are historically reversible through political action, a democratic crisis destroys the very mechanisms of collective decision-making and reform.
  2. 02AI-driven recommendation algorithms and the attention economy have fundamentally degraded the information environment required for calm, fact-based democratic deliberation.
  3. 03The pervasive infrastructure of AI surveillance creates a chilling effect that undermines the free thought and association essential for a liberal democratic citizenry.
  4. 04Addressing the democratic crisis requires aggressive structural interventions, such as reforming Section 230 and mandating algorithmic transparency, rather than just shifting research norms.
  5. 05Breaking the monopolistic power of dominant tech giants through antitrust action is a necessary precondition for any effective regulation of AI's impact on society.
  6. 06New legal regimes and privacy statutes are required to protect citizens from the unprecedented data collection and biometric monitoring enabled by AI.
