The Port Huron Statement and the Question of Accountability

Hayden's defense of the Port Huron Statement's contemporary relevance is thoughtful, but it sidesteps a question that becomes increasingly urgent as time passes: what responsibility do the Statement's authors bear for how their vision was implemented, distorted, or abandoned?

The SDS that produced the Port Huron Statement underwent dramatic transformation in the years following its publication. By 1969, SDS had split into multiple factions, some of which embraced political violence. The revolutionary Marxism that displaced participatory democracy as the dominant New Left ideology was in many respects a reaction against the perceived limitations of the original Port Huron vision. The Weathermen, the Plumbers, and other radical factions argued that participatory democracy was insufficient—that genuine transformation required more disciplined organizations with clearer ideological commitments.

Hayden does not engage this history honestly. He traces a straight line from Port Huron to Stonewall to Occupy, as if the movements that actually challenged the system were simply variations on a theme. But the 1970s and 1980s were decades of defeat for the left, characterized by conservative retrenchment and the demonization of the movements that had come before. The Port Huron vision did not simply continue; it was challenged, rejected, and largely forgotten.

This raises uncomfortable questions. Were the original Port Huron authors naive about the difficulties of sustained progressive politics? Did their emphasis on process over substance contribute to the movement's fragmentation? Did their failure to develop concrete strategies for seizing state power leave a vacuum that more authoritarian organizations filled?

Hayden's answer is that the Port Huron message is "timeless but not dogmatic." This is too convenient. Timeless messages are by definition applicable to all times, which means they apply to no specific time with any precision. The Port Huron Statement said many things, but it did not adequately prepare its adherents for the challenges they would face.

This is not a reason to dismiss the Statement entirely. Every historical document reflects its moment and cannot anticipate subsequent developments. But it is a reason for humility. The activists of the 1960s made mistakes that we are still living with. We will make mistakes that future generations will have to correct. The appropriate response is not celebration but critical engagement—learning from successes and failures alike.

Hayden ends with the claim that participatory democracy is "the political expression of the universal human need to be involved in the joint-creation of our community." Perhaps. But need does not guarantee satisfaction. Humans also have needs for security, belonging, and meaning that participatory democracy alone cannot fulfill. The task is not to find a single framework that answers all these needs but to build movements that can hold multiple purposes in tension. That is harder than Hayden suggests.
