"The crisis identified by the Occupy movement is fundamentally a moral failure of the American soul, requiring a spiritual and ethical repentance that translates into specific policies of social restoration."

There is a religious undertone in the lead essay’s appeal to American principles: confession, repentance, renewal. We have sinned against our ideals; perhaps we can be restored. I welcome that language, though I would give it a sharper edge. The crisis Occupy has named is not only institutional or economic. It is moral. We have built a society that praises success while abandoning solidarity, celebrates consumption while tolerating loneliness, and measures worth by market price.

This does not mean that markets are evil or that wealth is inherently suspect. A decent society needs enterprise, creativity, and reward. But markets are dangerous when they escape moral limits. They teach useful habits—discipline, responsiveness, efficiency—but they also encourage us to see one another as competitors, consumers, costs, or opportunities. The financial crisis was a moral failure before it was a technical failure: a failure of restraint, truthfulness, stewardship, and responsibility.

The anger at Wall Street is therefore justified. Mortgage brokers sold loans they did not expect borrowers to repay. Banks packaged risk in forms few understood. Rating agencies blessed securities they should have scrutinized. Executives pursued short-term gains and left others with long-term losses. But the moral failure was not confined to finance. Politicians promised benefits without taxes, wars without sacrifice, homeownership without equity, and growth without limits. Citizens, too, were encouraged to confuse credit with prosperity. The culture of leverage was spiritual as well as financial.

Occupy’s encampments have been most moving when they have offered an alternative practice of community: feeding strangers, sharing supplies, holding open discussions, treating the homeless as participants rather than nuisances, creating libraries and medical tents. These practices matter. They remind us that democracy is not only a set of procedures but a way of recognizing one another. In a society increasingly sorted by income, education, neighborhood, and ideology, public encounter is itself a form of repair.

Yet Occupy also needs moral discipline. Anger can clarify, but it can also intoxicate. The language of “the 99 percent” should not become permission to dehumanize the 1 percent. Greed is real, but so is the human capacity for conversion. Some wealthy people have supported higher taxes, philanthropy, and reform. Some struggling people have acted irresponsibly. Moral categories do not map neatly onto income brackets. A movement grounded in justice must resist the temptation to replace one hierarchy of contempt with another.

The lead essay’s invocation of abolition and civil rights is appropriate because those movements combined moral witness with institutional struggle. They appealed to conscience, but they also changed law. They demanded repentance, but not as sentiment. Repentance meant emancipation, voting rights, desegregation, enforcement. What would repentance mean now?

It would mean, first, refusing to sacrifice the vulnerable to austerity. Budgets are moral documents. Cutting food assistance, unemployment insurance, Medicaid, schools, or housing support while protecting low taxes for the wealthy is not fiscal prudence; it is a statement about whose pain counts. Deficits matter, but deficit reduction that begins with the poor is morally backwards.

Second, repentance would mean debt mercy. The biblical tradition understood that debt can become bondage. Student loans that cannot be discharged, mortgages far exceeding home values, medical debts, payday loans, and fees imposed on the poor all trap people in a future already claimed by creditors. Debt forgiveness and restructuring are not merely economic tools; they are acts of social restoration.

Third, it would mean honoring work. No one who works full time should live in poverty. Care work—raising children, tending the elderly, supporting the disabled—should be valued rather than treated as invisible. Immigration status should not be used to create a subclass of exploitable labor. The dignity of work is a principle nearly everyone praises and too few institutions protect.

Fourth, it would mean limiting the political power of wealth. The right to be heard cannot depend on the size of one’s donation. Campaign finance reform, lobbying restrictions, and transparency are moral reforms because they protect the equal worth of citizens.

Finally, repentance would mean recovering a sense of enough. Extreme inequality is not only harmful because the poor have too little; it is harmful because a culture of limitless accumulation corrodes the rich as well. When status depends on ever greater wealth, no amount satisfies. The common good becomes an abstraction. The Sabbath wisdom that human beings need limits—on work, consumption, domination, and debt—has political significance.

Occupy is often described as secular, radical, or unruly. It is all of those in parts. But it also resembles an old form of public moral protest: people gathering in the square to say that the emperor’s accounts do not add up, that legality is not justice, that a society can lose its soul while increasing its gross domestic product. Those who care about moral and religious traditions should not stand aside because the movement’s language is unfamiliar. They should enter the conversation and deepen it.

The future will not be occupied by policy alone. It will be shaped by what we admire, what we tolerate, and whom we recognize as neighbors. If Occupy helps America remember that economic life is answerable to moral judgment, it will have already done a great service. If it can turn that judgment into mercy, accountability, and shared sacrifice, it may help us become not only more democratic, but more decent.
