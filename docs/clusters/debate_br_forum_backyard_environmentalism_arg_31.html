"The proposed rolling-rule regime is a flawed framework that threatens to institutionalize existing power imbalances and strip marginalized communities of the legal protections provided by centralized federal standards."

Reading “Beyond Backyard Environmentalism,” one cannot help but be struck by the profound optimism Archon Fung, Charles Sabel, and Bradley Karkkainen exhibit regarding the capacity of marginalized communities to engage in the highly technical, resource-intensive process of environmental deliberation. As an advocate for the environmental justice movement—a movement that has spent the last two decades fighting the disproportionate siting of toxic facilities in minority and low-income neighborhoods—I find their vision of a “rolling-rule regime” deeply troubling. It is a framework that threatens to institutionalize the very power imbalances we have been struggling to dismantle.

The authors paint a picture of a new democratic regulatory reorientation where “ordinary citizens” join forces with experts from corporations and government to determine “what kinds of activity are productive, yet acceptably sustainable.” They cite the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) and the Massachusetts Toxics Use Reduction Act (TURA) as examples of how information disclosure empowers local units to negotiate better outcomes. But they gloss over a critical reality: information is only power if you have the resources, time, and political capital to wield it.

Consider a low-income community of color in Louisiana’s “Cancer Alley” or on the South Side of Chicago. Under the current, albeit flawed, command-and-control system, these communities have a clear legal baseline. If a chemical plant is violating its Clean Air Act permit, grassroots organizations can sue under citizen-suit provisions. The standards are fixed, objective, and enforceable by a federal judge.

Now, introduce the authors’ “performance-based” architecture. The community is invited to sit at a table with representatives from the chemical plant and state regulators to establish “minimally acceptable levels of performance” and “plausible targets for improvement.” Who sits at this table? On one side, you have corporate environmental engineers, public relations experts, and industry lawyers whose full-time job is to maximize the firm’s profitability while navigating this new regulatory flexibility. On the other side, you have community residents who are juggling multiple jobs, lacking formal training in toxicology or hydrology, and trying to protect their children's health.

The authors suggest that these citizens will become “deep users,” engaging in joint responsibility and transforming their identities. But this assumes a level playing field that simply does not exist. In deliberative settings, power dictates whose knowledge is considered valid. When a community member testifies about the cluster of asthma cases on their block, it is often dismissed as anecdotal. When the corporation presents a polished, mathematically complex risk assessment showing that the “tolerable activities” are perfectly safe, it is accepted as objective science. The rolling-rule regime, by moving away from hard federal limits, forces vulnerable communities into continuous, exhausting negotiations where they are structurally disadvantaged.

Furthermore, the authors argue that this system provides “mutual accountability.” But accountability requires teeth. If a local community agrees to a pollution reduction plan with a factory, and the factory fails to meet its targets, what is the recourse? The authors mention that “local criticism and national scrutiny disciplines laggards.” This is breathtakingly naive. Multi-billion dollar corporations are not disciplined by local criticism from marginalized neighborhoods; they are disciplined by the threat of massive fines and factory shutdowns. Removing the rigid enforcement mechanisms of centralized command in favor of “continuous improvement” plans gives polluters an endless series of second chances.

The environmental justice movement was born out of the recognition that decentralization and local control often lead to environmental racism. When zoning and environmental decisions are devolved to the local level, the path of least resistance is inevitably the neighborhood with the least political power. Centralized federal standards, for all their inefficiencies, were a necessary corrective to local prejudices and economic pressures.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen acknowledge the risk of “haplessly parochial” localism, but they believe centralized pooling of information will correct it. However, data pooling does not solve the political problem. Knowing that a community in Texas is being exposed to higher levels of benzene than a community in Massachusetts is useful information, but it does not, in itself, stop the exposure.

We do not need to move “beyond” backyard environmentalism; we need to ensure that everyone has a backyard worth protecting. True environmental democracy is not about forcing citizens to become unpaid, quasi-expert regulatory negotiators. It is about providing strong, uncompromising federal guarantees that no community will be sacrificed for industrial progress. Until the deep inequalities of wealth and power in this country are addressed, replacing hard rules with flexible deliberation will simply provide a progressive-sounding cover for the continued exploitation of the most vulnerable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Information disclosure only empowers those with the existing resources, time, and political capital to interpret and act upon technical data.
  2. 02The shift from fixed legal baselines to flexible deliberation structurally disadvantages low-income residents who lack the professional expertise and funding of corporate entities.
  3. 03Replacing rigid enforcement mechanisms with 'continuous improvement' plans removes the threat of meaningful penalties and gives polluters an endless series of second chances.
  4. 04Decentralization and local control historically lead to environmental racism by allowing industry to target neighborhoods with the least political power.
  5. 05True environmental democracy requires strong, uncompromising federal guarantees rather than forcing vulnerable citizens into exhausting and unequal regulatory negotiations.
