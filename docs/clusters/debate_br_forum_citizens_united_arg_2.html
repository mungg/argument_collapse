Vanilla GPT

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies 'dependency corruption' as a systemic threat to democracy, the constitutional solution should not involve empowering the government to suppress independent political speech, but rather focusing on architectural reforms that incentivize broader participation."

Lawrence Lessig is right to insist that the central problem in American campaign finance is not bags of cash changing hands. The more serious problem is the daily, lawful dependence of elected officials on a tiny class of funders whose priorities are not identical with those of the public. Any account of corruption that begins and ends with bribery will miss most of what citizens actually experience as corrupt: the phone calls returned, the meetings granted, the amendments quietly inserted, the issues never brought to a vote. A Congress may be composed mostly of honorable people and still operate within a dishonorable structure.

But I am less persuaded that Citizens United is the best place to locate the constitutional cure for that disease. The decision is troubling, and Justice Kennedy’s confidence that independent expenditures cannot corrupt is too breezy. Still, the First Amendment problem is not an afterthought. When government prohibits a corporation, union, or nonprofit association from spending money to distribute a film about a presidential candidate, it is regulating political advocacy at the core of democratic debate. That should make even campaign-finance reformers uneasy.

The awkward fact in Citizens United is that the plaintiff was not General Motors or ExxonMobil but a nonprofit ideological corporation that wanted to distribute a documentary attacking Hillary Clinton. The statute did not merely regulate contributions to candidates; it prohibited certain election-season communications funded from a corporate treasury. To say that Citizens United could have spoken through a PAC is not a complete answer. PACs are burdensome, regulated vehicles, and the right to speak often includes the right not to speak through a government-designed substitute. If a conservative nonprofit can be silenced near an election, so can the Sierra Club, the ACLU, the NRA, or a small incorporated publisher of political documentaries.

Lessig’s answer is to distinguish suppression of speech from regulation aimed at “dependency corruption.” That distinction is morally powerful, but constitutionally slippery. If Congress may restrict independent political spending because it produces public distrust or creates a dependency on funders, then the question becomes: which speakers may Congress silence, and when? Corporations? Unions? Wealthy individuals? Media corporations? Advocacy nonprofits funded by wealthy donors? A great many speakers participate in “economies of influence.” The danger of a broad anti-corruption rationale is that it can become a license for incumbents to decide which concentrations of political power are too “distorting” to be heard.

That is why the Court’s narrowness, though unsatisfying, has a democratic logic. Quid pro quo corruption is not the only morally relevant corruption, but it is a relatively administrable constitutional boundary. Bribery and coordinated contributions can be regulated because they are tied directly to candidates and officeholders. Independent advocacy is different, not because it is harmless, but because prohibiting it requires the state to ration political persuasion. Once we move from “this payment bought that official act” to “this spending creates an unhealthy political dependency,” courts must judge the legitimacy of political influence itself. That judgment is hard to make without favoring some speakers over others.

Lessig’s Lochner analogy therefore cuts both ways. He is right that the Court should not simply announce, without evidence, that access and influence will not damage public faith in democracy. The assertion is implausible. Americans plainly believe that money matters too much in politics, and Congress’s miserable approval ratings cannot be separated from the perception that organized money receives privileged treatment. But Lochner also warns against judges constitutionalizing their own theory of proper political economy. If the Court should not impose laissez-faire economics on legislatures, neither should it too readily bless legislative attempts to equalize political influence by suppressing disfavored speakers.

The deeper problem is that both sides are tempted by an impossible aspiration: a politics cleansed of unequal influence. Representative government has always involved organized interests, wealthy patrons, partisan newspapers, labor unions, churches, trade associations, and ideological movements. Madison did not think factions could be eliminated; he thought their effects could be controlled through institutional design. The question is not whether some citizens will have more influence than others. They will. The question is whether our institutions can prevent that inequality from hardening into dependence.

On that point Lessig’s proposed remedies are far more promising than his constitutional critique. Small-donor public financing, matching funds, and democracy vouchers attack dependency without banning speech. They change the incentives of candidates rather than silence independent speakers. If members of Congress could raise competitive sums by appealing to large numbers of ordinary citizens, they would spend less time cultivating lobbyists, PACs, and wealthy contributors. The New York City matching-funds model, whatever its imperfections, suggests that public matching can diversify the donor pool and make small contributions politically meaningful. A national system along those lines would do more to restore trust than another round of prohibitions vulnerable to judicial invalidation.

Disclosure also deserves more defense than Lessig gives it. He is right that disclosure cannot reveal every threat, wink, or implied promise. The “iceberg” problem is real: a corporation or union need not spend millions if candidates know it credibly could. But secrecy makes the problem far worse. Voters, journalists, shareholders, and competitors should be able to know who is financing political messages. Disclosure will not eliminate dependency corruption, but it can discipline it. The Court in Citizens United upheld disclosure requirements by a lopsided vote, and reformers should exploit that opening aggressively.

There are also corporate-governance reforms that do not require banning speech. If corporations are to spend treasury funds in elections, shareholders should have meaningful notice and perhaps approval rights. Political spending by managers may not reflect the views of investors, employees, or consumers. Requiring internal accountability would respect the First Amendment interest in political association while reducing the agency problem created when corporate officers deploy other people’s money for political ends.

Finally, Congress can tighten rules on coordination. The formal line between independent and coordinated spending is too easy to manipulate. Lessig is convincing when he describes a world of nods and winks. If “independent” expenditures are functionally planned with campaigns, they should be treated as contributions and limited accordingly. That is not censorship; it is enforcement of the very distinction on which Citizens United relies.

Lessig’s most important contribution is to shift attention from villainy to architecture. We do not need to believe that members of Congress are crooks to see that their dependence on funders deforms representation. Yet the remedy should be architectural as well. Build alternative funding channels. Require sunlight. Police coordination. Empower shareholders and members of associations. Reduce the amount of time candidates spend dialing for dollars.

Citizens United may make reform harder, and the Court’s complacency about corruption is unwarranted. But a democracy worth defending must be cautious about empowering Congress to prohibit political advocacy, even advocacy financed by corporations. The better path is not to make some speakers quieter. It is to make representatives less dependent on the few who can afford to speak the loudest.

Vanilla Gemini

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies 'dependency corruption' as a systemic crisis in American democracy, his proposal to solve it by allowing the government to restrict political speech is constitutionally dangerous and inferior to structural funding reforms."

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “institutional corruption” offers a remarkably lucid vocabulary for the unease that most Americans feel when they look at Washington today. By separating the venal, *quid pro quo* bribery of a Rod Blagojevich from the systemic, legal dependencies that shape congressional behavior, Lessig accurately captures why a body composed largely of well-intentioned public servants routinely produces policies that the public actively dislikes. The dependency on funders has indeed eclipsed the intended dependency on voters.

Yet, while Lessig’s diagnosis of the disease is flawless, his critique of the Supreme Court’s refusal to permit speech restrictions as the cure is fundamentally flawed. Lessig laments that the Court in *Citizens United* rejected “dependency corruption” as a justification for banning independent corporate political expenditures. He argues that if Congress can ban bribery, it should be able to ban systemic dependency. But this leaps over a massive constitutional chasm, conflating the regulation of a transaction with the regulation of political speech.

If the Supreme Court were to adopt Lessig’s broad definition of institutional corruption as a compelling state interest sufficient to override the First Amendment, the cure would quickly prove worse than the disease. Allowing Congress to restrict political speech on the grounds that certain speakers create an "improper dependency" hands incumbents a tailor-made tool to silence their most well-funded critics. The First Amendment sets an intentionally high bar—*quid pro quo* corruption—because any broader definition, such as the “distorting effects” rationale from the now-overruled *Austin* decision, inevitably allows the government to decide whose voices are legitimate and whose are improperly loud.

Lessig attempts to frame Justice Kennedy’s majority opinion as an exercise in “First Amendment Lochnerism,” accusing the Court of substituting its own factual assumptions about public trust for those of the legislature. But this analogy misrepresents the architecture of constitutional law. *Lochner* involved the Court inventing unenumerated economic rights to strike down labor regulations under a basic rational-basis standard. *Citizens United*, by contrast, deals with the explicitly enumerated right to free speech, which necessarily triggers strict scrutiny. When a legislature bans political speech—even speech funded by a corporate treasury—the burden is entirely on the government to prove that the ban is narrowly tailored to a compelling interest.

Kennedy did not need to prove that the public would retain faith in democracy; rather, the government failed to prove that banning independent political documentaries was the only constitutional way to save that faith. Strict scrutiny places a heavy thumb on the scale of free expression. If we abandon that standard to defer to congressional fact-finding on what constitutes “corruption,” we invite majorities to perpetually rewrite the rules of political discourse to their own advantage.

Lessig is certainly correct about the "iceberg theory"—the credible threat of a massive independent expenditure by a corporation or union will absolutely influence candidate behavior. The implicit threat of a million-dollar ad campaign against a climate-change bill is a powerful deterrent. But in a free society, threatening to vigorously and publicly criticize a legislator’s record is not a loophole; it is the essence of democratic accountability, even if we find the wealth behind the megaphone distasteful. We cannot make it illegal to threaten to criticize a politician.

Where Lessig and I find absolute agreement is in his concluding prescription. Because the First Amendment rightly prevents us from artificially lowering the ceiling of political speech, our only constitutional recourse is to raise the floor. The structural alternative to censorship is subsidization.

If we wish to break the dependency on wealthy, out-of-district funders, we must create a counter-economy of influence that makes candidates dependent on the voters they actually represent. Lessig points to the Fair Elections Now Act and the Ackerman-Ayres democracy voucher system. These are not mere fallback positions; they are the optimal solutions. A system that matches small-dollar donations or grants voters democracy vouchers changes the incentive structure of campaigning without requiring a single censorious ban on independent expenditures. It forces politicians to spend their time courting thousands of constituents rather than a handful of corporate executives.

As we navigate this first election cycle after *Citizens United*, the surge of outside spending is undeniably alarming. But the path out of this crisis does not run through a reversal of First Amendment protections or a nostalgic return to the patchwork regulations of McCain-Feingold. It requires us to stop trying to silence the few and start financing the voices of the many. Lessig’s "dependency corruption" is the defining political crisis of our time, but we must cure it with more democracy, not less speech.
