"While Lawrence Lessig’s concept of 'dependency corruption' is a useful diagnosis, addressing the crisis of Citizens United requires a more radical structural response—including a constitutional amendment—to confront the fundamental inequality of corporate economic power in American democracy."

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “dependency corruption” is persuasive as far as it goes, but it does not go nearly far enough. The crisis exposed by Citizens United is not simply that members of Congress depend on campaign contributors rather than voters. It is that American democracy has long coexisted uneasily with enormous private concentrations of economic power, and the Court has now made it harder to pretend that the two can be neatly separated. Campaign finance is one channel through which economic power becomes political power. It is not the only one, and it may not even be the most important.

Lessig’s essay is strongest when it rejects the narrow quid pro quo view of corruption. Anyone who has watched Congress handle financial reform after the worst economic crisis since the Depression knows that influence rarely appears as a criminal bargain. It appears as expertise supplied by the regulated industry, as former staffers becoming lobbyists, as fundraisers hosted by those with business before committees, as “moderate” exemptions that just happen to protect organized interests, and as the quiet knowledge that certain votes will bring expensive retaliation. The used-car dealer exemption from consumer financial-protection legislation is exactly the kind of episode that makes citizens conclude, reasonably, that Washington listens more closely to organized money than to ordinary people.

But the word “corruption” can obscure as well as reveal. It suggests that there is a pure institution being contaminated by an external influence. In reality, the American state and corporate capitalism have grown together for more than a century. Tariffs, railroads, land grants, defense contracts, banking law, tax policy, intellectual property, agricultural subsidies, energy policy, and financial regulation have all been arenas in which government constituted markets and markets reshaped government. The corporate form itself is a legal creation, a bundle of privileges—limited liability, perpetual life, entity status—that enables capital to accumulate and act. When the Court says corporations must be free to spend treasury funds in elections, it is not protecting some pre-political natural speaker. It is extending political rights to entities made powerful by law.

Lessig cites Austin’s concern with “immense aggregations of wealth” accumulated through the corporate form and unconnected to public support for political ideas. That concern deserves more emphasis than he gives it. The problem is not only dependency; it is political inequality. A corporation with billions in revenue can project its preferences into the electoral arena on a scale that ordinary citizens cannot approach. Even if no candidate feels dependent, the public sphere is altered. Some views become ubiquitous because they are profitable to powerful entities; others struggle to be heard because their beneficiaries are poor, dispersed, or not yet born. Climate policy is the obvious example. Future generations cannot contribute to campaigns. Low-income communities exposed to pollution cannot match the resources of energy companies. The “marketplace of ideas” is a cruel metaphor when some speakers own the marketplace.

For that reason, I worry that Lessig’s preferred remedy—small-dollar public financing or democracy vouchers—while necessary, will be insufficient. Such systems would help candidates rely less on large donors, and they should be enacted. But independent expenditures after Citizens United could still drown out publicly financed campaigns. A candidate may be able to raise enough through vouchers to run a credible campaign, but if a handful of corporations or trade associations can spend many times that amount defining her as anti-jobs, anti-American, or corrupt, the dependency remains. She may not need their money, but she must still fear their power.

Disclosure is also inadequate. Knowing that an advertisement was paid for by a coal company or a Wall Street-funded committee may help voters evaluate the message, but disclosure does not equalize communicative power. A megaphone does not become less loud because it has a label. Worse, the path around disclosure is already visible: money can move through nonprofits and trade associations that obscure the original source. Even if Congress tightens disclosure rules, sophisticated actors will look for ways to hide, bundle, and launder political money. The issue is not merely sunlight; it is structure.

The more honest solution is constitutional change. If the Court reads the First Amendment to disable democratic control over corporate electoral spending, then the Constitution must be amended to make clear that the people may regulate such spending. This is not a step to be proposed lightly. Amendments are difficult, and they should be. But the difficulty of amendment should not make us accept a constitutional doctrine that treats artificial economic entities as if they had the same political claim as citizens. The First Amendment should protect persons, associations of persons, and the press. It should not require states and Congress to permit general-treasury corporate spending in candidate elections.

The amendment should be drafted carefully. It should not authorize incumbents to silence critics. It should not allow censorship of newspapers, books, films, or nonprofit advocacy. It should protect the right of individuals to spend reasonable amounts and to associate for political purposes. But it should restore legislative power to distinguish between citizens pooling political contributions and corporations spending wealth accumulated for commercial purposes under state-conferred privileges. It should also permit limits on independent expenditures, because the distinction between contributions and expenditures has become a constitutional fiction. In modern campaigns, massive outside spending can be as valuable to a candidate as direct contributions and more difficult to regulate.

Any reform agenda must also strengthen countervailing organizations. In the mid-twentieth century, labor unions, civic associations, local parties, and mass-membership organizations gave many citizens a route into politics. Those institutions have weakened. Business lobbies and professional advocacy groups have filled the space. If democracy is to be more than periodic voting amid privately financed propaganda, citizens need organizations that can aggregate their interests and sustain participation. Reviving labor law, protecting organizing rights, supporting public media, and rebuilding local party structures are therefore campaign-finance reforms in a broader sense. They alter who has durable political capacity.

Lessig’s frame is attractive because it does not demonize politicians. That is salutary. The fantasy that Washington is populated by uniquely wicked people distracts from institutional incentives. But we should be equally careful not to sentimentalize the institution. Members of Congress may be hardworking and decent, but Congress is not merely distracted from the public good by fundraising. It is embedded in a political economy that systematically privileges those who already possess wealth. The sugar tariff, the milk subsidy, the bank loophole, the oil preference, the defense contract, and the weak labor-law remedy are not anomalies. They are signs of an order in which concentrated benefits defeat diffuse publics.

Citizens United matters because it intensifies this order and gives it constitutional blessing. It says, in effect, that the unequal capacity to convert economic power into electoral power is a First Amendment value. The decision is especially perverse after the financial crisis. We have just seen how lightly regulated private power can endanger the whole economy and then rely on public rescue. To respond by expanding the political rights of corporate treasuries is to deepen the imbalance that helped produce the disaster.

Still, the path forward should not be despair. The American constitutional tradition contains more than judicial doctrine. It contains popular movements that have redefined what democracy requires: abolition, Reconstruction, women’s suffrage, direct election of senators, civil rights, labor rights. Each movement confronted entrenched interests that claimed constitutional shelter. Each expanded the meaning of self-government. The response to Citizens United should be similarly broad: public financing, vouchers, disclosure, shareholder controls, stronger lobbying rules, union renewal, media reform, and ultimately a constitutional amendment restoring democratic authority over corporate political spending.

Lessig has given us a useful lens: dependency corruption. We should use it, but we should also look through it to the deeper landscape. The problem is not only that Congress depends on funders. It is that funders are so unequal in the first place, and that law has helped make them so. Democracy after Citizens United will require more than cleaning up Congress. It will require democratizing power.

  3. 03Proposed remedies like small-dollar vouchers and disclosure are insufficient because massive independent expenditures can still exert coercive power over candidates and obscure the true sources of influence.

  5. 05Meaningful reform must go beyond campaign finance to include the revitalization of countervailing organizations, such as labor unions and civic associations, that aggregate the interests of non-wealthy citizens.
  6. 06Citizens United perversely constitutionalizes the conversion of economic power into political power, deepening a system that privileges concentrated financial interests over the diffuse public good.

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies 'dependency corruption' as a systemic institutional failure, his proposed legislative reforms are insufficient because only a constitutional amendment can effectively overcome the Supreme Court’s restrictive First Amendment jurisprudence."

The Dependency Trap and the Constitutional Amendment We Need

Lawrence Lessig has done a great service by reframing the corrupting influence of money in politics not as a series of isolated bad acts, but as an institutional sickness — what he calls “dependency corruption.” His distinction between personal venality and the systemic distortion that arises when lawmakers become dependent upon funders rather than voters is a genuine conceptual advance. Yet Lessig’s own remedies — voluntary small-dollar matching, democracy vouchers — rest on a premise that his analysis undermines. So long as the Supreme Court’s First Amendment jurisprudence remains unchanged, any legislative fix is built on sand. The only durable solution is a constitutional amendment that restores the people’s power to regulate the political economy of influence.

Lessig rightly identifies Citizens United as an expression of a deeper judicial pathology: First Amendment Lochnerism. Just as the Lochner Court substituted its own economic theories for legislative fact-finding, the Roberts Court now substitutes its own intuitions about corruption and democracy for the considered judgments of Congress. In Citizens United, Justice Kennedy breezily asserts that “the appearance of influence or access” will not cause the electorate to lose faith in democracy, despite overwhelming survey evidence that it already has. He reduces the only constitutionally cognizable corruption to quid pro quo bribery, a move that, as Lessig shows, has no grounding in the text, history, or structure of the First Amendment. And he dismisses the entire tradition of Austin, which recognized that corporate wealth can have a “corrosive and distorting” effect on the political process, with barely a shrug.

Lessig’s proposed legislative fixes — voluntary public financing and voucher systems — are ingenious and, if enacted, would meaningfully reduce the dependency on large private donors. The Fair Elections Now Act, which would provide public matching funds for candidates who accept small contributions, is an excellent example. Yet under the current doctrinal regime, such voluntary systems are vulnerable. The Court has already signaled hostility to the “coercive” aspects of public financing: in Davis v. FEC (2008), it struck down the “millionaire’s amendment” as an unconstitutional burden on the speech of wealthy candidates. A broad voucher system that displaces private funding could be challenged as an impermissible effort to “level the playing field,” a rationale the Court has repeatedly rejected. And even if a voluntary system survived, it would not directly address the vast independent expenditure regime that Citizens United has unleashed. As Lessig himself notes, the iceberg theory of campaign influence means that the mere threat of spending can distort legislative behavior, and transparency alone cannot neutralize that threat.

The real obstacle, then, is the Court’s cramped definition of corruption. Lessig tries to expand it by arguing that institutional corruption is no different in principle from the kind of corruption that justifies banning bribery. If the state can prevent the appearance of venal influence on individual legislators, why can’t it prevent the systemic dependency on funders that weakens the institution as a whole? The argument is elegant, but it asks the current Court to repudiate the central holding of Citizens United. That is unlikely. Five justices have already declared that only quid pro quo corruption justifies speech restrictions, and they did so with the apparent conviction that they were vindicating the First Amendment’s core meaning. They will not be persuaded by a law review article, however brilliant.

A constitutional amendment, by contrast, would change the rules of the game entirely. It would not need to argue with the Court about the boundaries of “corruption”; it would simply reassert the people’s authority to structure their own democracy. An amendment could specify that Congress and the states have the power to regulate the raising and spending of money in elections, including independent expenditures, and that such regulations do not violate the freedom of speech or press. It could explicitly overturn Citizens United and Buckley, restoring the understanding that prevailed for most of American history: that the Constitution does not disable the people from protecting their representative institutions from the undue influence of concentrated wealth.

Lessig is right that we need a better lens to see institutional corruption. But the lens must be ground into the Constitution itself. Only an amendment can break out of the trap that First Amendment Lochnerism has set, freeing future Congresses to adopt the kind of structural reforms — public financing, spending limits, and yes, even restrictions on corporate political expenditures — that will restore a democracy dependent upon the People alone. Lessig’s diagnosis is brilliant; the cure must be constitutional.

  2. 02Legislative fixes like voluntary public financing and democracy vouchers are vulnerable to being struck down by a Court that is increasingly hostile to any regulations aimed at leveling the political playing field.
  3. 03The Court’s narrow definition of corruption as only 'quid pro quo' bribery is a doctrinal barrier that is unlikely to be changed through legal scholarship or incremental litigation.
  4. 04A constitutional amendment is necessary to explicitly grant Congress the power to regulate election spending and restore the principle that representatives should be dependent upon the people alone.
  5. 05Transparency and voluntary systems cannot neutralize the 'iceberg' effect of independent expenditures, where the mere threat of corporate spending distorts legislative behavior.
Diversified Gemini

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies the crisis of institutional corruption, his proposed campaign finance reforms are insufficient because the root cause of the problem is the judicial doctrine of corporate personhood, which must be abolished via constitutional amendment."

Lawrence Lessig is entirely correct to identify “institutional corruption” as the defining crisis of modern American democracy, and his critique of the Supreme Court’s willful blindness in *Citizens United v. FEC* is devastatingly accurate. The Court’s reduction of political corruption to mere *quid pro quo* bribery is an intellectual failure that ignores the lived reality of every citizen who watches Congress routinely prioritize corporate bottom lines over the public good. However, while Lessig accurately diagnoses the disease, his proposed remedies—small-dollar vouchers and public matching funds—are akin to treating stage-four cancer with an aspirin. The problem runs much deeper than campaign finance architecture. The root cause of the crisis we face after *Citizens United* is the absurd, judge-made doctrine of corporate personhood.

To understand why Lessig’s statutory fixes are insufficient, we must examine the scale of the power imbalance that *Citizens United* has unleashed. Lessig points to the Fair Elections Now Act and the concept of "democracy vouchers" as ways to re-establish the dependency of representatives on the people. These are noble ideas, and in a pre-*Citizens United* world, they might have been enough. But in the world we live in as of September 2010, the mathematics of political power have been fundamentally altered.

Imagine a scenario where a principled candidate opts into a public financing system. She collects her small-dollar donations, receives her government match, and builds a war chest of a few million dollars, proudly declaring her independence from special interests. In the past, this might have guaranteed her a fighting chance. But today, a single multinational corporation, or a shadowy 501(c)(4) front group funded by a handful of billionaires, can spend ten million dollars in the final month of the campaign to absolutely carpet-bomb her district with negative advertising. The candidate’s publicly funded war chest will be exhausted in days. Her message will be drowned out. More importantly, every other politician watching this spectacle will learn the lesson: you cannot survive on small-dollar public financing if a corporation decides it wants you gone.

The "iceberg theory" of unspoken threats that Lessig cites will ensure that public financing systems remain largely decorative. Why would a candidate rely on a fifty-dollar voucher from a voter when angering a major corporate player could trigger an avalanche of independent expenditures against them? Public financing does not stop the flow of private money; it merely provides an alternative track that will inevitably be overshadowed by the limitless corporate treasuries unleashed by the Court.

The core error of *Citizens United*, and the historical mistake that Lessig glosses over, is the premise that a corporation is entitled to First Amendment rights in the same manner as a living, breathing human being. A corporation is not a person. It does not have a conscience, it cannot vote, it does not bleed, and it does not die. A corporation is a legal fiction, a piece of paper filed with a state government, designed specifically to shield investors from personal liability and to amass capital. It is a wealth-generating machine, and it is legally obligated to maximize returns for its shareholders. To grant this artificial entity the right to spend unlimited funds to influence elections is to allow an unstoppable economic force to dictate the terms of human self-governance.

Lessig tiptoes around this issue, preferring to frame the debate in terms of "institutional design" and the Framers' intent regarding dependency. But the Framers did not design the Constitution with multinational, trillion-dollar corporations in mind. At the time of the founding, corporations were rare, strictly chartered by the state for specific public purposes (such as building a bridge or a canal), and their charters could be revoked if they exceeded their mandate. The idea that a corporation could possess an inherent, constitutionally protected "freedom of speech" that supersedes the right of the people to regulate elections would have been unrecognizable and appalling to Jefferson and Madison.

The disastrous trajectory we are on began long before *Citizens United*. It traces back to the late 19th century, specifically the 1886 case *Santa Clara County v. Southern Pacific Railroad*, where a court reporter’s headnote casually suggested that corporations were protected under the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment—an amendment passed to protect the rights of emancipated slaves. For over a century, corporate lawyers have ruthlessly exploited this judicial accident, steadily expanding corporate rights to include Fourth Amendment protections against search and seizure, and ultimately, First Amendment free speech rights.

If we accept Lessig’s premise that we must eliminate "dependency corruption," we cannot do so while leaving the weapon of unlimited corporate spending intact. A publicly funded politician in a world of unlimited corporate independent expenditures is simply a sitting duck. The only viable solution to the crisis of *Citizens United* is a constitutional amendment that explicitly abolishes corporate personhood and establishes, once and for all, that money is not speech.

Such an amendment would clarify that the rights enshrined in the Constitution belong exclusively to natural persons. It would explicitly grant Congress and the states the authority to regulate and limit the raising and spending of money for political campaigns, including independent expenditures. This is not a radical idea; it was the consensus view of the American political establishment for a century before the Roberts Court decided to tear up the rulebook.

Lessig worries about "Lochnerism," but the best way to stop the Supreme Court from acting as a super-legislature on behalf of capital is to strip them of the constitutional ambiguity they use to justify their rulings. We cannot out-legislate the Court’s interpretation of the First Amendment with matching funds or vouchers. As long as the Court holds that a corporate bank account has a constitutional right to speak, our democracy will remain an oligarchy in all but name. We must stop trying to design clever workarounds to institutional corruption and instead strike at its heart. We the People—not We the Corporations—must reclaim the Constitution.
