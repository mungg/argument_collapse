"The term 'act of God' is a constructive legal tool for distinguishing human fault from unavoidable misfortune, but it should never be used as a moral or political excuse to absolve humans of responsibility for preventable harm."

The term “act of God” makes more sense in a courtroom than in a sermon or a newspaper headline. In law, it has a limited function: it identifies extraordinary events outside the control of the parties to a contract or dispute. If a warehouse is destroyed by an unforeseeable tornado, or a ship cannot deliver goods because of an exceptional storm, it may be unjust to treat nonperformance as ordinary negligence. The phrase is archaic, but the concept is not foolish.

Every legal system needs some way to distinguish fault from misfortune. Human beings cannot insure against every contingency, and liability without limit can become its own injustice. If a farmer loses a crop to a freak hailstorm, we do not usually think he has wronged his buyers in the same way as a merchant who simply refused to deliver. “Force majeure,” “vis major,” “act of God”—whatever the term—recognizes that some events overwhelm reasonable precautions.

The problem arises when the legal shorthand migrates into moral explanation. A hurricane in the abstract may be beyond human control; the collapse of a poorly maintained floodwall may not be. An earthquake cannot be prevented; the decision to build unreinforced schools in an earthquake zone is a human decision. Courts themselves often recognize this distinction. An event must not merely be natural; it must be extraordinary and not reasonably preventable. If reasonable care could have reduced the harm, “act of God” should not be a shield.

So the phrase is constructive only when it is disciplined. It should not be a blanket absolution for governments, builders, insurers or corporations. Nor should it be used to suggest that victims are outside the realm of justice because “God did it.” After disasters, there are always questions of duty: Were warnings issued? Were evacuation orders clear? Were shelters adequate? Were roads and hospitals prepared? Were known risks ignored because the people at risk were poor?

Perhaps we should retire the theological wording in public law and use more precise terms: “unavoidable natural event,” “extraordinary natural occurrence,” or simply “force majeure.” These phrases avoid implying divine authorship. But the underlying principle should remain. Not all loss is blameworthy. Not all tragedy is tort.

A mature society must hold two thoughts together. First, nature can exceed human control, and justice requires recognizing genuine limits. Second, many so-called natural disasters reveal very human failures before, during and after the event. The phrase “act of God” is acceptable as a narrow legal tool. It is harmful as a moral conclusion. The courtroom may need a doctrine of unavoidable events; the public square needs a relentless inquiry into avoidable deaths.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Legal systems require a concept like 'act of God' to ensure that individuals are not unjustly held liable for extraordinary events that overwhelm reasonable precautions.
  2. 02The term becomes problematic when it is used to mask human failures, such as poor infrastructure maintenance or inadequate disaster preparation.
  3. 03Courts must maintain a strict distinction between truly unforeseeable natural events and damages that could have been mitigated through reasonable care.
  4. 04Replacing the theological phrasing with more precise secular terms like 'force majeure' could help avoid the implication of divine authorship while preserving the necessary legal principle.
  5. 05A mature society must balance the recognition of nature's limits with a relentless inquiry into whether human negligence contributed to the severity of a disaster's impact.
Diversified MiniMax

"The concept of natural disasters as 'acts of God' should be viewed as a practical legal and policy tool rather than a theological claim, provided it is not used to excuse human negligence or lack of preparation."

I want to push back against the assumption embedded in the debate question: that whether natural disasters are "acts of God" is primarily a theological matter. It is, at least equally, a legal and policy question, and the stakes there are concrete and immediate.

The legal doctrine of "act of God" exists for a defensible reason. Contract law and tort law typically require parties to exercise reasonable care. If a business fails to deliver goods because a bridge washed out, the doctrine prevents liability that would effectively hold the business responsible for forces no human foresight could have anticipated or resisted. This is not the same as saying God willed the flood. It is saying: some events exceed the threshold of what preparedness can reasonably address, and it would be unjust to assign blame or damages on that basis.

The controversy arises when the doctrine is stretched beyond that legitimate core. Insurance contracts routinely invoke "acts of God" language to deny coverage in ways that are far more expansive than the original legal concept intended. A tornado that destroys a mobile home park is "an act of God"; a flood that follows because a developer filled in a wetland is not, yet the language sometimes gets applied that way anyway. The risk is that the theological framing gives a legal escape hatch a kind of moral authority it has not earned.

There is also a practical problem. If communities treat disasters as divine interventions that cannot be anticipated, they will not invest in the mitigation measures that most reduce harm. The Philippines' experience is instructive here: the nation does face extraordinary physical hazard exposure, but the depth of suffering in any given storm correlates strongly with governance quality, building enforcement, and infrastructure investment. Those are choices, not acts of God. Recognizing that distinction is not impiety—it is responsible stewardship. God gave humans minds capable of engineering and foresight; using them is, if anything, an act of faith in the value of human agency.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The legal doctrine of 'acts of God' serves a defensible purpose by preventing parties from being held liable for events that exceed the threshold of reasonable human foresight.
  2. 02The 'act of God' framing is often inappropriately expanded by insurance companies and developers to avoid responsibility for damages that were actually exacerbated by human choices.
  3. 03Treating disasters as divine interventions discourages communities from investing in the infrastructure and mitigation measures that are proven to reduce harm.
  4. 04Distinguishing between unavoidable natural forces and human governance is a form of responsible stewardship that honors human agency and foresight.
