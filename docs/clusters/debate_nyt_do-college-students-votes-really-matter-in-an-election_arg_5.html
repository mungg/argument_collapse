Diversified Gemini

"The significance of college students' votes is determined by the geographic location of their university, with those in swing states wielding enough power to decide national elections."

The question of whether college students’ votes matter cannot be answered with a simple yes or no, because the American electoral system does not treat all votes equally. Thanks to the Electoral College and the localized nature of congressional races, the importance of a college student's vote depends almost entirely on their zip code.

For a student attending university in deep-blue Massachusetts or deep-red Alabama, their individual vote for president is largely symbolic. The state’s electoral votes are already decided. However, for a student attending Ohio State, the University of Pennsylvania, or the University of Florida, their vote is incredibly consequential. These massive campus populations are situated in the very swing states that decide presidential elections. A highly mobilized campus in Columbus or Philadelphia can yield thousands of votes—more than enough to tip a close statewide race, and by extension, the presidency.

We know that college votes in these states matter because we can see how hard certain political actors work to suppress them. Over the last several years, Republican-led state legislatures in battleground states have passed a slew of restrictive voting laws clearly designed to dilute the power of the college vote. North Carolina’s strict voter ID law, which explicitly excluded student identification cards from state universities as valid forms of voter ID, is a prime example. Other states have attempted to ban early voting sites on college campuses or heavily restrict out-of-state students from establishing residency for voting purposes.

Politicians are highly rational actors when it comes to self-preservation. State legislators would not expend the political capital required to pass highly controversial voting restrictions targeting college students if those students were an irrelevant constituency. The aggressive efforts to make it harder for students to vote are the ultimate proof of their political power.

So, do college students matter? In our current system of geographic battlegrounds, a mobilized campus in a swing state is one of the most potent political forces in the country. They matter enough that one party is banking its future on them, and the other is actively trying to keep them away from the ballot box.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01In the American electoral system, students in battleground states like Ohio or Pennsylvania represent a massive, concentrated voting bloc capable of tipping statewide and presidential results.
  2. 02The systematic efforts by political actors to pass restrictive voting laws targeting students, such as banning student IDs for voting, demonstrate that politicians view the college vote as a genuine threat to their power.
  3. 03The high level of political capital expended to suppress campus voting proves that students are a highly consequential and feared constituency in the current political landscape.
