Vanilla DeepSeek

"While Akbar and Theoharis correctly identify the systemic and bipartisan nature of Islamophobia in U.S. policy, their critique risks being counterproductive by collapsing the distinction between illegitimate profiling and the legitimate necessity of state counterterrorism."

The essay by Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis is a searing and necessary indictment of the interlocking legal, surveillance, and military apparatus that has constrained and distorted Muslim life in the United States for more than fifteen years. Their insistence that Islamophobia is not a Trumpian aberration but a deeply embedded bipartisan project, nourished by a public willing to sort “good” Muslims from “bad,” is thoroughly convincing. They rightly demand that we examine the casual brutality of the No Fly List, the pretzel logic of material support prosecutions, the everyday humiliation of borders, and the fiction that our domestic criminal process is fundamentally just. Yet as compelling as their diagnosis is, the essay’s analytical frame sometimes overreaches, collapsing the distinction between illegitimate targeting and the legitimate, if deeply flawed, effort to prevent violence. In doing so, it risks weakening the very moral and political ground on which a new, rights-respecting security order could be built.

Nowhere is this more apparent than in the treatment of the Khizr Khan speech. The authors are correct that the public’s effusive embrace of the Khan family was drenched in a provisional logic: a Muslim had to offer a son’s military sacrifice to be fully heard. But reading that moment solely as an iteration of the “good Muslim/bad Muslim” dichotomy overlooks what else was happening. When Khan brandished his pocket Constitution and invited Trump to search it for the words “liberty” and “equal protection,” he was not merely performing a romanticized Americana for a liberal audience. He was invoking a long tradition of marginalized groups demanding that the nation live up to its professed ideals. From Frederick Douglass’s searing Fourth of July oration to the Black civil rights marchers who recited the Declaration of Independence, appeals to national creed have been subversive, not simply complicit. To dismiss Khan’s act as “showing America through a romantic filter” nearly strips Muslim Americans of the agency to define their own belonging on their own terms. The more devastating problem is not that the public celebrated the Khans, but that so many other Muslim claims to equal protection—by people who have not served in the military, or who criticize U.S. foreign policy, or who wear a hijab and say nothing at all—are dismissed. A critique that can honor the Khan family’s witness while demanding the same dignity for all Muslims is more powerful than one that risks treating any heartwarming national moment as a trap.

A similar slippage marks the discussion of counterterrorism tools like the material support ban and the paradigm of radicalization. Akbar and Theoharis rightly show that these instruments have been grotesquely overbroad, criminalizing association, charity, speech, and even dissent. They have overwhelmingly been deployed against Muslims and have enabled sting operations that look more like entrapment than protection. But the essay’s framing often seems to suggest that the very concept of cutting off material support for terrorism, or of paying attention to ideological pathways toward violence, is intrinsically Islamophobic. That is a difficult argument to sustain in early 2017, when the country was still processing a series of attacks in which perpetrators claimed Islamist inspirations—Orlando only eight months earlier, San Bernardino the year before—and in a world where civilians in Baghdad, Jakarta, and Istanbul were slaughtered by groups like ISIS. The challenge is not to abandon the state’s responsibility to prevent mass murder, but to demand a counterterrorism that follows the Constitution, that does not profile, and that is based on real evidence of criminal conduct rather than on the kinds of piety or politics the FBI finds unsettling. By conflating the existing, rights-abusing apparatus with the very idea of counterterrorism itself, the essay may inadvertently cede the most crucial terrain: the construction of a security policy that Muslim communities themselves can help shape and that protects them from the extremists who threaten their lives and their religion.

This concern extends to the essay’s treatment of surveillance. The authors are correct that Edward Snowden’s revelations laid bare a surveillance state with a disproportionately heavy footprint on American Muslims, and that the widespread public anger focused largely on the government spying on “all Americans” rather than on the communities most affected. But the picture they paint of unrelenting, broadly unchallenged advancement is not quite complete. The USA Freedom Act of 2015, while modest, ended the bulk collection of telephone metadata and imposed some new transparency requirements. It passed because a coalition of civil libertarians and some Muslim advocacy groups worked with skeptical legislators on both the right and the left. That coalition arose precisely because people began to grasp the kind of structural harm the essay describes. To imply that the No Fly List is simply “celebrated” by Democrats, citing John Lewis’s sit-in for gun control, is an uncharacteristically sloppy generalization that elides a more productive story: many actors across the political spectrum do object to the list’s secret, unaccountable nature, and that dissent is a resource to be mobilized, not dismissed. Recognizing that small but genuine contested spaces exist does not minimize the crisis; it points toward the possibility of actually dismantling the machinery.

Something similar must be said about the essay’s international framing. The authors note that the United States bombed six of the seven countries in Trump’s ban, a devastating fact. But the argument that these wars are simply a manifestation of the same domestic anti-Muslim logic runs the risk of flattening the genuinely complex, tragic nature of conflicts in Syria, Iraq, and beyond. The violence there has many engines—authoritarian regimes, regional proxy wars, and the very sort of extremist groups whose victims are overwhelmingly Muslim. The proper progressive response is not to imply that all U.S. military action is reducible to Islamophobia, but to demand a foreign policy that stops fueling the fires and that centers human security over geopolitical competition. A critique that fails to distinguish between the bombing of a hospital and the narrow, multilateral defense of a Yazidi community under siege (had such ever occurred) may find itself without the moral grammar to oppose the worst while leaving open a door for the genuinely protective.

The great strength of Akbar and Theoharis’s essay is that it insists we see the whole sprawling system: the connection between domestic surveillance and foreign war, between the war on drugs and the war on terror, between the conditional hospitality offered to some Muslims and the permanent suspicion cast on most. My concern is that in its rush to denounce the entire edifice as a seamless project of anti-Muslim prejudice, it sometimes leaves the impression that the very act of trying to prevent terrorism is a mask for bigotry. That will not serve the broad, durable coalition needed to undo the damage. The Americans who packed airports to protest the ban included people who still believe that al-Qaeda and ISIS pose a real threat. Many of them want security policies that are both effective and just. If we tell them that the tools they instinctively support are beyond redemption, we risk losing them entirely. The more difficult but necessary task is to show why the current approach does not actually make us safer—why blowing up mosques with informants and draining civil liberties fuels the very narratives extremists exploit—and then to build out the alternative. Akbar and Theoharis have done us an immense service by illuminating the horrors of the status quo. The next step is to avoid foreclosing the very possibility of a counterterrorism that is neither Islamophobic nor incompetent, because without that vision, we will be left forever protesting the latest ban without ever wrenching the law away from the logic of the trial.
