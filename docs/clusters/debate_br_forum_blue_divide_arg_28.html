"The Democratic Party's shift toward an ambitious economic agenda is not a calculated strategy by elites to manage a diverse coalition, but rather the direct result of an ideological revolution triggered by the 2008 financial crisis and the perceived failure of the neoliberal response to it."

Hacker and Pierson’s rich and provocative essay rewrites the recent history of the Democratic Party, replacing a story of cultural capture with one of strategic economic ambition. The details they assemble are compelling, and their pushback against the caricature of the “woke” Democrat is a valuable contribution. But in their zeal to foreground the party’s elite “policy demanders” and the internal logic of coalition management, they fundamentally misidentify the engine of change. The Democratic Party’s U-turn on economics is not the result of a smart plan by leaders to bridge the Blue Divide. It is the unmistakable, if top-heavy, reflection of an ideological revolution ignited by a single, catastrophic event: the 2008 financial crisis and the political response to it. The authors mention the Great Recession as a catalyst, but their analysis flattens it into just another pressure point. It was, in fact, the big bang that reshaped the entire political universe the party now operates in.

The neoliberal order that Hacker and Pierson describe was not just a set of policies from the Democratic Leadership Council. It was an all-encompassing belief system, a form of market fundamentalism so confident in its efficiency that it was taken for not just the best way to run an economy, but the scientific way. The 2008 crisis shattered that belief system in a way that poor economic performance alone could never achieve. It was not merely a recession; it was a systematic failure of the core institutions—banks, regulators, economists—that the technocratic neoliberal state had anointed as infallible. Yet what radicalized the Democratic base was not the crash itself, but the response. The Obama administration, staffed by many of the same figures who had built the deregulated order, executed a rescue that was widely perceived, across the left and right, as a massive transfer of wealth from the public to the very financial elites who caused the disaster. This was not a policy debate. It was a moral education. Millions of young people, including those who would become the suburban base of today’s Democratic Party, graduated into this wreckage with no job prospects and heavy debt, while watching bankers get bailed out and bonuses restored.

This was the true and final collapse of the center-left’s moral and intellectual authority on the economy. It broke the technocratic compact between the party and its emerging coalition. The authors note that Obama’s anemic recovery package was “advocated by Summers.” This is a tremendous understatement of its political legacy. The decision to prioritize speed and political expediency over a recovery large enough to genuinely heal the labor market was not a tactical mistake. It was proof of the bankruptcy of the entire Rubin-Summers worldview, a demonstration that these elites were psychologically and ideologically incapable of responding to the true scale of mass immiseration. The resulting discontent was the soil in which the Sanders and Warren campaigns grew, not the other way around. The “growing strength of the party’s progressive wing” is not an independent variable. It is the organizational consequence of the ideological discrediting of the Third Way by the people who lived through its final, catastrophic failure.

COVID-19, which Hacker and Pierson rightly credit with political momentum, served more as an accelerant for beliefs that were already dominant. The pandemic did not teach the cohort radicalized by 2008 that the market was fragile and the safety net full of holes; it confirmed an already deeply held conviction. The speed with which the American Rescue Plan expanded the safety net, including the child tax credit, was possible only because an entire generation of staffers, activists, and rising politicians had spent a decade waiting for the chance to implement what the 2008 response should have been. The Biden administration’s economic team, while still full of experienced hands, was fundamentally shaped by this generational experience. They were not going to be the ones to botch the response this time. They were going to go big, because going small was the original sin.

Hacker and Pierson want to give agency back to political elites, and they are right that strategy matters. But the strategy was not invented in a think tank. It was born in the traumatic realization that the old elite consensus was not just politically unpopular but objectively catastrophic. The suburban voters now in the Democratic coalition are not a mysterious group that needs to be coddled with selective benefits. They are, in their middle age, the very people who had their economic worldview forged in the crucible of 2008. Their support for a more active government is not a puzzle for elites to solve; it is a rational conclusion about the nature of modern capitalism drawn from searing personal and collective experience. The new Democratic economics is not a clever coalition-management project. It is the institutional memory of a betrayal, finally translated into a governing agenda.

  4. 04The COVID-19 pandemic acted as an accelerant for a pre-existing generational conviction that the market is fragile and requires aggressive government intervention.
  5. 05Affluent suburban voters support active government because their own economic worldviews were forged by the collective trauma and perceived betrayals of the 2008 crisis.
