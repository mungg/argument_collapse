"Mandatory public disclosure of corporate cyberattacks should not be required because it compromises the ability to gather intelligence and effectively eliminate advanced threats."

The impulse to demand immediate, mandatory public disclosure of every corporate cyberattack is understandable, but it is dangerously shortsighted. While transparency is generally a virtue, in the realm of cybersecurity and counterintelligence, a rigid disclosure mandate would do far more harm than good, ultimately crippling our ability to track and stop advanced adversaries.

Consider the recent, highly publicized breach of The New York Times. When the Times discovered that Chinese hackers had infiltrated its network, the company did not immediately blast out a press release. Instead, they brought in the security firm Mandiant and spent four months quietly monitoring the intruders. They watched how the hackers moved through the network, identified what passwords they used, and studied their digital signatures. By remaining silent, the Times gathered invaluable intelligence on state-sponsored cyber espionage and was able to comprehensively eradicate the threat from their systems.

If a federal law had forced The New York Times to publicly disclose the breach within days of discovery, that vital investigation would have been destroyed. The hackers would have known they were spotted, covered their tracks, and burrowed deeper into the system, or simply vanished only to attack another American institution using new, unknown methods.

Cybersecurity is not like a physical robbery where the crime is over in minutes. It is a persistent game of cat and mouse. When networks are breached by advanced persistent threats (APTs), incident response teams need time to quarantine the malware, patch the vulnerabilities, and understand the scope of the exfiltration. Forcing premature public disclosure tips off the attackers. It tells them exactly what we know and when we knew it, giving them the upper hand. Rather than a blanket public mandate, we need a system where companies can confidentially report breaches to federal law enforcement and cybersecurity agencies, allowing for coordinated defense without giving our adversaries a strategic advantage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Immediate disclosure alerts hackers that they have been detected, allowing them to cover their tracks or move deeper into a network.
  2. 02Maintaining silence during a breach enables security teams to monitor intruder behavior and gather vital intelligence on state-sponsored espionage.
  3. 03Prematurely announcing a hack prevents incident response teams from properly quarantining malware and understanding the full scope of data exfiltration.
  4. 04A confidential reporting system to law enforcement is a more effective alternative to public mandates for coordinating national defense without tipping off adversaries.
