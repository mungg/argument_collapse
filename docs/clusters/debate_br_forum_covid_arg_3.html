"While agreeing that pandemic institutions failed, the focus of the postmortem should be on the democratic failure of authorities who refused to treat citizens as adults and laundered moral and political judgments through the language of expertise."

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee ask whether our truth-seeking institutions performed well during COVID-19, whether we weighed tradeoffs honestly, and whether we have conducted the postmortem a crisis of this magnitude deserves. Their answer is no. Mine is substantially the same, though I would put the emphasis less on proving that every intervention failed than on the more basic democratic failure: institutions with enormous authority too often refused to speak to citizens as adults.

That failure matters even if one thinks some emergency restrictions were justified in March 2020. I did think so. When a novel respiratory virus was spreading, when hospitals in northern Italy and then New York were under visible strain, when the infection fatality rate was uncertain, when no vaccine or effective treatment existed, and when the medical system lacked masks, testing capacity, and therapeutics, temporary emergency measures were defensible. The early slogan “flatten the curve” was not irrational. It reflected a concrete fear: that an uncontrolled first wave would overwhelm hospitals and produce avoidable deaths not only from COVID-19 but from every other condition requiring urgent care.

But the justification for temporary emergency measures was never a blank check for a new style of governance. Flattening the curve became suppressing the virus; suppressing the virus became waiting for vaccines; waiting for vaccines became keeping schools closed in some jurisdictions well after evidence had accumulated that children faced low risks and schools were not major drivers of severe outcomes. A policy originally defended as a short bridge hardened into a moral identity. That is where Macedo and Lee’s indictment is strongest.

The most damning case is schools. It is now impossible to deny the scale of harm. Learning loss was real, large, and unequal. The burdens fell least on children whose parents could supervise Zoom school from a quiet house, hire tutors, organize pods, or move to private schools. They fell most on poor children, children with disabilities, English learners, children in unstable homes, and adolescents already at risk of disengagement. School is not merely a delivery system for algebra and reading. It is food, safety, friendship, speech therapy, counseling, routines, mandated reporting, and a path into adulthood. Treating it as just another congregate setting was a grave mistake.

The United States compounded that mistake because school decisions were not consistently tied to local hospital strain, teacher vaccination, ventilation, or demonstrated in-school transmission. They were tied to politics and institutional power. Some public schools remained remote while bars, restaurants, gyms, and private schools reopened. That inversion was morally indefensible. A society that puts children last and then calls the policy compassionate has lost the plot.

Macedo and Lee are also right that “the science” became a slogan that obscured value judgments. Science can estimate transmission risks, compare mortality rates, test vaccine efficacy, and model hospital demand. It cannot tell us, by itself, how to weigh a five-year-old’s developmental loss against a marginal reduction in community spread, or a restaurant worker’s unemployment against a nursing home resident’s risk, or the protection of hospital capacity against restrictions on worship and assembly. Those are political and moral judgments. They require democratic deliberation.

Instead, officials frequently laundered contested moral choices through the language of expertise. The public was told that “science” required policies that were, in truth, mixtures of evidence, prudence, fear, institutional incentives, and political calculation. That habit damaged public trust. It also damaged science, because it encouraged citizens to interpret later revisions not as normal learning under uncertainty but as proof that experts had been manipulating them all along.

The mask episode is illustrative. Early in 2020, officials discouraged public mask use. Some did so because evidence for community masking was uncertain, especially for cloth masks. But some also did so because they feared shortages for health workers. That concern was understandable; the communication strategy was not. A truthful message would have been: “Medical-grade masks are urgently needed in hospitals. Please do not hoard them. We are still evaluating whether ordinary masks reduce spread, and we will update guidance as evidence develops.” Instead, many citizens heard confident claims that masks were unnecessary, followed by confident claims that masks were essential, followed by a cultural insistence that only cranks remembered the earlier certainty. That is not how trust is built.

The same pattern appeared in the lab-leak debate. The point is not that a laboratory origin has been established; it has not. The point is that the hypothesis was treated for too long as disreputable, even racist, despite the obvious relevance of a major coronavirus research center in the city where the outbreak was first detected and despite China’s opacity. Responsible people could worry about xenophobia while still permitting open inquiry. Collapsing those questions into one moralized taboo was a mistake.

The censorship question is more complicated than some critics allow. Platforms are private entities; misinformation during a pandemic can kill; false claims about vaccines, miracle cures, and hospital conspiracies were rampant. No serious society has an obligation to algorithmically amplify every reckless claim. Yet it is equally true that the line between suppressing dangerous falsehoods and suppressing legitimate disagreement was crossed too often. When platforms defined misinformation as deviation from public health authorities, they transformed agencies under pressure, with imperfect information and political incentives, into arbiters of scientific truth. That was especially dangerous because some official positions changed. A claim can be premature, speculative, or wrong without being illegitimate. Public health authorities should have rebutted more and suppressed less.

Where I would slightly qualify Macedo and Lee’s argument is on the empirical claim that NPIs broadly “did not work.” It is hard to infer too much from state comparisons alone. States differ in age structure, density, climate, household composition, nursing home quality, prior infection, behavior independent of mandates, and timing of viral seeding. A formal stay-at-home order may matter less than whether people voluntarily reduce contacts; a mask mandate may matter less than whether masks are high quality and worn in crowded indoor spaces; a school closure in rural Montana is not the same intervention as one in Queens. The absence of a clean correlation between stringency and cumulative mortality does not prove that all restrictions were futile.

Still, that qualification does not rescue the sweeping policy regime that developed. If evidence for many measures was weak, officials should have said so. If the benefit of outdoor masking, beach closures, playground closures, curfews, and prolonged school closures was speculative or minimal, those measures should not have been imposed with moral fervor. The burden of proof belongs especially on policies that restrict basic liberties and impose unequal costs. In too many cases, that burden was not met.

Nor should we forget what did work. Vaccines sharply reduced severe disease and death, especially before immune escape and waning complicated the picture. Operation Warp Speed was one of the great public achievements of the period, combining public funding, private capacity, regulatory urgency, and scientific brilliance. Better therapeutics, clinical learning, and acquired immunity also changed the risk landscape. The tragedy is that trust in vaccination was undermined not only by anti-vaccine activists and partisan opportunists, but also by the earlier overreach and evasiveness of institutions that later needed credibility most.

A real postmortem should therefore avoid two temptations. The first is institutional self-exoneration: “We did the best we could; hindsight is 20/20; only extremists still care.” That will not do. Many critics warned in real time about school closures, collateral health harms, unemployment, social isolation, and the danger of suppressing debate. The second temptation is populist simplification: “All restrictions were tyranny; experts were useless; public health is the enemy.” That is also false. Hospitals really were strained. Many health workers behaved heroically. Vaccines saved lives. Public health expertise is indispensable.

The needed lesson is not anti-expertise. It is anti-paternalism. Experts should inform democratic judgment, not replace it. They should distinguish what is known, what is uncertain, what is guessed, and what is valued. They should disclose tradeoffs. They should welcome dissent from qualified critics. They should resist becoming political mascots.

What would accountability look like? Not show trials. Not ritual humiliation. Not another partisan committee designed to confirm what its members already believe. We need independent national and state-level reviews with access to internal communications, modeling assumptions, school reopening debates, nursing home policies, platform-government communications, and cost-benefit analyses—or the absence of them. These inquiries should include epidemiologists, pediatricians, economists, civil libertarians, educators, sociologists, mental health experts, local officials, parents, and representatives of essential workers. They should ask not only “How many infections were prevented?” but also “Who paid the price?” and “Who had a voice?”

Future emergency powers should sunset quickly unless legislatures renew them. School closures should be last-resort measures, geographically targeted, and accompanied by clear reopening criteria. Outdoor restrictions should require extraordinary evidence. Public agencies should maintain public uncertainty logs: what they believe, how confident they are, what evidence would change their minds. Government should not pressure platforms to remove contested scientific claims unless they are demonstrably fraudulent and imminently dangerous. And pandemic planning should treat children, the poor, the disabled, and essential workers not as afterthoughts but as central constituencies.

The deepest wound from COVID-19 is not merely that officials made mistakes. Mistakes were inevitable. The wound is that too many institutions reacted to uncertainty by projecting certainty, to dissent by stigmatizing dissenters, and to tradeoffs by denying that tradeoffs existed. Liberal democracy cannot function that way. It depends on citizens being trusted with hard truths.

Macedo and Lee are right to insist that the reckoning is overdue. We cannot restore trust by demanding that people forget what they saw: closed schools, open casinos; masked toddlers, unmasked politicians; censored debates later deemed plausible; confident pronouncements quietly abandoned. Trust will return, if it returns, when institutions become trustworthy enough to say: here is what we got right, here is what we got wrong, here is what we did not know, here is who was harmed, and here is how we will do better next time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The transition from temporary emergency measures to a permanent style of governance based on virus suppression was a moral and political overreach rather than a scientific necessity.
  2. 02The prolonged closure of schools was a catastrophic and indefensible policy that prioritized institutional power over the profound developmental and social needs of vulnerable children.
  3. 03Public health officials damaged trust by using 'the science' as a slogan to obscure value judgments and by providing shifting, paternalistic messaging on topics like masking and the lab-leak theory.
  4. 04While some content moderation was necessary, social media platforms and government agencies crossed the line into suppressing legitimate scientific disagreement and dissent.
  5. 05The lack of a clear correlation between policy stringency and mortality suggests that the burden of proof for restrictive measures was often not met, even if some interventions may have had localized utility.
  6. 06A meaningful reckoning requires institutional accountability that rejects both self-exoneration and populist anti-intellectualism in favor of transparent, anti-paternalistic expertise.
