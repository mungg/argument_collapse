**What Gourevitch Doesn't See About the Jewish Students**

Alex Gourevitch's essay is careful, learned, and in many ways admirable. It is also, as a Jewish reader, exhausting in a familiar way. The exhaustion comes from watching a thoughtful person describe my community's experience as essentially a category error—a confusion of "hurt" with "harm," a failure to "distinguish subjective feelings from objective circumstances"—while never quite confronting what the protests actually said and did.

Let me stipulate Gourevitch's core point. The hostile-environment standard is being weaponized by the Trump administration to gut universities, and the result is not the protection of Jewish students but the destruction of academic freedom along with whatever pluralism American higher education still possessed. Mahmoud Khalil's disappearance is a moral horror. The $400 million Columbia shakedown is a transparent power grab dressed in the language of civil rights. Many of the disciplinary actions taken by universities have been disproportionate and selectively enforced. On all of this, Gourevitch and I agree.

What I do not accept is the move he makes in the middle of his argument, where the testimonies of Jewish students fearful of walking through encampments become exhibits in a case study about therapeutic culture and "the politics of fear." The student who said "It's scary to walk through the protest" is not Gourevitch's straw figure of a fragile undergraduate who needs to read more Foucault. She is, very often, the granddaughter of survivors who has just spent six months listening to her classmates chant "globalize the intifada" in a language that her family knows from experience is not metaphorical. The intifadas killed Jewish civilians at bus stops and pizza shops. To pretend that the chant is purely a slogan of decolonization, with no reference to historical violence against Jews, is to perform exactly the kind of motivated obtuseness that Gourevitch elsewhere accuses universities of.

Gourevitch concedes that he heard "horrific celebrations of violence against Israelis." He should have stayed with that sentence longer. There is a difference between a protest that says "Israel is committing genocide and the university is complicit" and a protest that says "October 7 was a beautiful day of resistance." The first is the kind of hostile political speech a democracy must protect. The second is closer to harassment, particularly when delivered, day after day, in the common spaces that Jewish students cannot avoid if they want to attend class. The hostile-environment standard, properly understood, was designed for exactly this kind of pattern: not isolated offensive statements, but the cumulative experience of being told, by people who share your campus, that your existence as a member of a particular people is unwelcome here.

Gourevitch's response to this is to cite the Harvard study showing that Muslim students reported feeling unsafe at higher rates than Jewish students. This is rhetorically deft and substantively misleading. Of course Muslim students felt unsafe—they were watching their friends and relatives being killed in Gaza, they faced doxxing trucks parked outside campus, and many of them experienced real Islamophobia in the post-October 7 environment. The right response is to take both groups' fears seriously, not to use one as a club against the other.

What Gourevitch needs to grapple with—and does not—is that the standard he proposes (objective threat, individualized harassment, imminent harm) would not, in fact, capture much of what made the campus environment intolerable for many Jewish students. The harm was diffuse, atmospheric, cumulative. A single chant is not harassment. Five months of chants outside the Hillel building, combined with social ostracism in seminars, combined with professors signing letters that describe Zionism (a movement most Jews identify with in some form) as inherently genocidal—this is a pattern. The patternedness is what the law calls hostile environment, and it is not a category error invented by therapeutic liberals. It is what discrimination law has meant since the 1980s.

I do not want the federal government policing university discourse. I do not want Mahmoud Khalil deported. I do not want speech codes. But I also do not want a "right to be hostile" that is articulated entirely from the perspective of the protester and that treats the targets of hostility as inconvenient witnesses to their own experience. Gourevitch's essay would be more honest if it admitted that an expansive right to protest will, in practice, mean that Jewish students on some campuses will have to spend the next several years being told, loudly and publicly, that their identity is bound up with mass atrocity. That may be the price of free speech. But it is a real price, paid by real people, and the people paying it are not wrong to mind.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The chants and slogans used in the protests, such as 'globalize the intifada,' carry historical references to violence that make Jewish students' fears an objective reaction to hostility rather than a 'category error' of therapeutic fragility.
  2. 02There is a critical distinction between protected political speech criticizing a state and speech that celebrates violence against a specific group, the latter of which borders on harassment when delivered persistently in shared campus spaces.
  3. 03The legal definition of a 'hostile environment' is intended to address the cumulative pattern of exclusion and social ostracism that Jewish students experienced, which cannot be captured by Gourevitch’s narrow standard of imminent physical harm.
  4. 04Gourevitch’s use of data regarding Muslim students' safety is a rhetorical distraction that avoids taking the specific, documented fears of Jewish students seriously on their own merits.
  5. 05An honest defense of absolute protest rights must acknowledge that such a policy imposes a real, discriminatory burden on Jewish students' ability to participate in campus life, rather than dismissing their experiences as inconvenient witnesses.
