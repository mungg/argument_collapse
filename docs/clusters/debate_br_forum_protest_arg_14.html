**The Property Question Is the Whole Question**

Of the three "bad justifications" for the campus crackdowns that Gourevitch identifies—disruption, trespass, and hostile environment—he gives the second the least attention. This is a mistake, because the trespass argument is the one with the most legal teeth, the deepest historical roots, and the most consequential implications for how we should think about universities going forward.

Gourevitch acknowledges that universities formally own their campuses and that this ownership underwrites academic freedom (universities must be able to decide what happens on their grounds, free of state interference). But he then argues that university commons function as quasi-public fora and that administrators "deeply betray" the university's nonprofit mission when they treat such spaces as ordinary private property. This is an attractive argument, but it papers over a tension that the rest of his essay never resolves.

If university commons are public fora, then the First Amendment applies and the state, not the university, sets the rules. The university cannot ban a speaker because it disagrees with the speaker; cannot impose viewpoint-discriminatory time-place-manner restrictions; cannot expel protesters for content. This is roughly the regime governing public universities, and it has worked reasonably well, although not without controversy.

But if university commons are public fora, the university also forfeits much of its claim to institutional autonomy in governing campus life. It cannot, for instance, ban a Klan rally or a Hamas-celebration teach-in or a Christian nationalist street preacher from setting up shop on the quad. The same doctrine that protects the Gaza encampments also protects the white supremacists. Gourevitch seems to accept this implication, citing Henry Louis Gates on the perils of turning bigots into First Amendment martyrs, and noting that "whatever right protesters claim for themselves is a right they are also claiming for their enemies." Good. Then he should also accept that universities, in this model, are essentially small cities—open infrastructure rather than mission-driven institutions.

This is, I think, where the deepest disagreement lies. The traditional case for university autonomy is that universities are mission-specific institutions devoted to research and teaching, and that the conditions of serious inquiry sometimes require restrictions that would be intolerable in a town square. A library is not a public forum. A seminar room is not a public forum. A classroom is not a public forum. Universities have always reserved the right to say: you are not welcome here unless you are doing the thing we exist to do. That is what makes them universities rather than convention centers.

The encampments tested this principle at its weakest point, the quad—a space that is neither classroom nor town square but ambiguous between them. Gourevitch's solution is to treat it as a town square, governed by something like First Amendment principles. The administrators' (panicked, often hypocritical) response was to treat it as private property to be managed at administrative discretion. There is a third position, which Gourevitch never quite articulates: that the quad is part of the educational mission, and that protest on the quad is legitimate insofar as it contributes to that mission—to the collective work of inquiry, debate, and learning. This is a higher standard than mere viewpoint neutrality, and it might rule out some protests (those that primarily aim to intimidate, or that hijack the space for non-educational purposes) while protecting others.

The mission-based view has costs, of course. It requires someone to judge what counts as educational, and that someone will inevitably be biased. But the alternative Gourevitch proposes—a "less governed" commons where almost anything goes provided it does not pose imminent physical harm—has costs too. It transforms universities into shouting matches and concedes the field of inquiry to whoever can shout loudest. The libraries and laboratories will continue to function, but the cultural center of campus life will be the quad-as-rally, which is no one's idea of a good education.

What makes the trespass question central, then, is that it forces us to ask what universities are for. Gourevitch's answer—communities where people "work, play, study, sleep, argue, fall in love, pray, compete, credentialize, and develop themselves"—is sociologically accurate but normatively thin. It describes a residential college as a kind of small town with classrooms attached. If that is what universities are, then yes, they should be governed like small towns, with broad speech rights and modest restrictions. But if universities are something more specific than that—institutions dedicated to a particular kind of intellectual work—then their property rules should reflect that specificity, and protest will sometimes be in tension with mission in ways that property rights help adjudicate.

I lean toward the second view, which is why I find Gourevitch's argument incomplete. The right to protest is real, but it is not the only thing universities owe their members or themselves.
