"While supporting the goal of full employment, the responding essay argues that policy must move beyond mere job creation to address the distribution of work-time and ensure that employment is compatible with a decent life."

Robert Pollin’s essay revives a question that should never have disappeared: what would it take to make full employment the organizing purpose of economic policy? His answer is broadly Keynesian, social democratic, and green. I share much of his ambition. But I worry that the phrase “full employment” can hide an equally urgent question: employment doing what, for how many hours, under what distribution of income and time?

The American debate often assumes that the ideal economy is one in which every adult works full-time, year-round, for wages, with household life fitted around the job. That model no longer describes many families, and perhaps it should not be our unquestioned goal. We need enough paid work for everyone who wants it, and we need jobs that provide dignity, income, and security. But we should also ask whether productivity gains can be translated into more free time, shorter workweeks, paid leave, and more equal sharing of care responsibilities. Full employment should not mean universal exhaustion.

Pollin notes that average labor productivity has more than doubled since the early 1970s while nonsupervisory wages have fallen in real terms. That fact is damning. But there are two ways workers might claim productivity gains: higher pay and shorter hours. The United States has emphasized neither very successfully for ordinary workers. Instead, many professionals work extreme hours, many low-wage workers juggle unstable schedules, and many unemployed workers have no hours at all. This is an irrational distribution of work. Some are overworked; others are excluded.

A full-employment policy should therefore include work-sharing. During downturns, rather than laying off 10 percent of a workforce, firms could reduce hours modestly for many workers, with unemployment insurance partially replacing lost wages. Several European countries have used variants of this approach. The United States has short-time compensation programs in some states, but they remain underused. Work-sharing preserves firm-specific skills, reduces the trauma of job loss, and spreads the burden of recession more fairly. It is not a substitute for stimulus in a deep slump, but it is a valuable tool.

Shorter hours also matter beyond recessions. If productivity rises, society can choose to consume more, rest more, care more, or some combination. For decades, the gains have flowed disproportionately to the top, while many workers have lost bargaining power. Reclaiming those gains solely as wages is important, especially for low-income households. But reclaiming some as time would improve health, family life, civic participation, and gender equality. Paid family leave, paid sick days, predictable scheduling, and vacation are employment policies too. They shape the quality of work and the distribution of opportunity.

Pollin’s focus on education and clean energy is compatible with this broader agenda. Retrofitting buildings, teaching children, and building transit are socially useful. But job creation should not be measured only by head counts. Suppose one policy creates more jobs by paying low wages and requiring long hours, while another creates fewer jobs but allows shorter hours, training, and stable schedules. Which is better? The answer depends on our social goals. “Jobs, jobs, jobs” is a powerful slogan in a crisis, but it can be used to defend any employment, including dangerous, insecure, or environmentally destructive work. Pollin avoids that trap by insisting on decent jobs. We should extend the point to decent lives.

There is also a gender issue. The standard full-time employment norm was built around a worker with someone else at home handling children, meals, illness, and elders. As women entered paid employment in large numbers, the norm changed too little. Families compensated through stress, marketized care, delayed childbearing, and the heroic efforts of grandparents and low-paid caregivers. A full-employment society that ignores time will intensify these pressures. Paid work must be organized around the realities of human dependency. Everyone needs care at the beginning of life, many need it at the end, and illness or disability can arrive at any time.

This perspective changes how we view unemployment. Joblessness is destructive not only because it reduces income but because it strips people of structure and recognition. Yet overwork can also damage families and communities. The goal should be a fair distribution of both paid labor and free time. That means macroeconomic policy to ensure demand, labor standards to prevent exploitation, and social insurance to allow people to move in and out of paid work when caregiving or illness requires it.

Inflation concerns, which Pollin treats seriously, look somewhat different through a work-time lens. Tight labor markets can raise wages; employers may pass costs into prices. But shorter hours with stable hourly pay could distribute available work without necessarily producing the same wage-price dynamic as a bidding war for scarce labor in particular sectors. Training and mobility policies remain necessary, as do energy policies that reduce vulnerability to oil shocks. But work-sharing gives policymakers another instrument besides unemployment or austerity.

The politics may be difficult. Americans often equate long hours with virtue and public support with dependency. Employers like maximum flexibility for themselves, which often means insecurity for workers. Professionals may accept overwork as the price of status; low-wage workers may want more hours, not fewer, because wages are inadequate. Any shorter-hours agenda must therefore be class-conscious. A four-day week for salaried professionals means little if retail and care workers cannot get enough paid hours to survive. The first priority remains decent hourly wages and access to full-time work for those who want it. But once that floor is established, time should be part of the bargain.

We might imagine a full-employment program with several pillars. First, aggressive public investment in useful work, including Pollin’s education and clean-energy priorities. Second, a strengthened right to organize so workers can bargain over wages, hours, and conditions. Third, work-sharing in recessions to prevent layoffs. Fourth, paid leave and predictable scheduling as national labor standards. Fifth, expansion of care services so employment is not built on invisible unpaid labor. Sixth, tax and benefit rules that do not punish part-time work or caregiving transitions. Seventh, a Federal Reserve that treats unemployment as a grave harm, not merely an inflation-control device.

Pollin asks whether we have the political will to fight for the right to a decent job. We should answer yes, but add that a decent job is one compatible with a decent life. The old labor slogan asked for “eight hours for work, eight hours for rest, eight hours for what we will.” That aspiration remains radical. In an age of doubled productivity, smartphones, long commutes, involuntary part-time work for some and sixty-hour weeks for others, we have not solved the time question. We have merely privatized it.

Full employment should not be a return to the pre-crisis economy with more people plugged into it. It should be a step toward an economy in which prosperity is shared as income, security, public goods, and time. Pollin has reopened the door. We should walk through it with a broader imagination of work and freedom.

  3. 03Employment norms must be restructured to accommodate the realities of human dependency and caregiving responsibilities, rather than assuming a worker with no domestic obligations.
