Vanilla Claude

"The federal government should reform the holiday calendar to prioritize a lean core of civic observances while replacing specific religious holidays with floating days off to ensure equal treatment for all faiths."

Federal holidays do two things at once: they coordinate time off across the economy, and they declare what the republic considers worth honoring. The first function argues for restraint — every additional day off costs roughly $1 billion in federal payroll plus billions more in lost output — while the second function invites endless contention. The honest answer is that the current list should be pruned more than expanded, and the criteria for inclusion should be civic rather than sectarian.

Christmas is the awkward case. The Supreme Court upheld its federal status in *Ganulin v. United States* (1999) on the theory that it has become a secular cultural holiday, but this reasoning is strained. If Christmas stays, the argument for recognizing Eid, Diwali, Yom Kippur, or the Lunar New Year becomes very strong on equal-treatment grounds. A country whose Muslim, Hindu, Jewish, and East Asian populations together number in the tens of millions cannot indefinitely justify singling out one tradition. Either we acknowledge that Christmas has effectively become a national winter holiday and add comparable accommodations, or we should be candid that the federal calendar privileges Christian observance.

Civic holidays are easier. Washington's Birthday (informally Presidents' Day) already absorbs Lincoln by custom; formalizing that would cost nothing. More defensible would be replacing some thinly observed dates with ones tied to constitutional self-government: Constitution Day (September 17), already a federal observance, deserves full holiday status more than Columbus Day, whose honoree is increasingly contested and whose civic content is thin.

My preferred reform: keep a lean core of unambiguously civic holidays — Independence Day, Thanksgiving, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, MLK Day, Washington-Lincoln Day, Constitution Day, New Year's Day, Labor Day — and convert religious holidays into a "floating" day or two that employees may take for the observance of their choice. That preserves accommodation, ends the establishment problem, and treats Americans of every faith on equal terms.

  3. 03Singling out Christmas for federal recognition creates an unfair double standard that cannot be justified in a religiously diverse society without offering comparable status to other major faiths.
  4. 04Replacing contested or thinly observed dates like Columbus Day with significant civic milestones like Constitution Day would better honor the republic's values.
  5. 05Converting religious holidays into floating days off would resolve the constitutional establishment problem while providing flexible accommodations for Americans of every faith.

"The federal holiday calendar should prioritize civic purposes and democratic participation over religious or identity-based recognition, utilizing flexible leave policies to accommodate America's pluralism."

The federal holiday calendar should not try to be a census of American identities. If it did, it would fail immediately: every addition would raise the question of why another faith, ethnicity, region or historical cause was left out. A country as religiously and culturally varied as the United States cannot have a public calendar that gives everyone’s sacred days equal official status by making them all days off. Nor should the government pretend that Christmas, Mawlid, Diwali, Yom Kippur, the lunar new year and Orthodox Christmas are interchangeable civic occasions. They are not.

The better principle is that federal holidays should be reserved mostly for broadly civic purposes: constitutional self-government, national memory, sacrifice, labor, gratitude and democratic participation. On that basis, Martin Luther King Jr. Day is easy to defend. It is not simply the birthday of a minister or civil rights leader; it is a national day about citizenship, equality and the unfinished promises of the Declaration and the Constitution. Memorial Day, Independence Day, Labor Day, Veterans Day and Thanksgiving also have plausible civic meanings, even if their observance is often more commercial or recreational than solemn.

Christmas is the difficult case. As a legal matter, the Supreme Court has permitted public recognition of holidays that have both religious and secular dimensions. As a practical matter, Christmas is deeply embedded in American life; eliminating it as a federal holiday would create more resentment than neutrality. But its existence should not become the argument for adding every other religious holiday. The lesson should instead be humility: the government inherited a calendar shaped by the country’s Protestant past, and in a pluralistic society it should be cautious about multiplying official religious recognitions.

That points toward two reforms. First, the federal government should add Election Day as a holiday, or move national elections to a weekend. If any act deserves public time, it is voting. We close government offices for presidential birthdays, but not for the choosing of presidents, members of Congress and local officials. A democracy should not make voting depend on an employer’s generosity, a long lunch break or a citizen’s ability to stand in line after work. A voting holiday would not solve every problem of turnout or access, but it would state the right priority.

Second, federal employees—and ideally workers more broadly—should have more flexible personal holidays rather than a longer list of official ones. A Muslim should be able to take Eid or Mawlid, a Jew Yom Kippur or Passover, a Christian Good Friday or Orthodox Christmas, a Chinese-American the lunar new year, without needing the whole nation to stop. Many employers already handle vacation and floating holidays this way; public policy should encourage that model. Neutrality is not achieved by pretending differences do not exist. It is achieved by allowing citizens to honor them without forcing everyone into one calendar.

There is also room to simplify symbolism. Washington’s Birthday could honestly be renamed Presidents’ Day if that is how the public already understands it, or it could remain Washington’s Birthday and Lincoln can be honored in schools and civic life without another paid federal day. Not every worthy figure needs a holiday.

So yes, the United States should have a somewhat different calendar, but not a much larger one. Add a holiday for democratic participation. Preserve a modest set of civic commemorations. Treat religious and cultural diversity mainly through flexible leave, not official endorsement. A plural nation needs public rituals, but it also needs room for private conscience.

"The federal government should prioritize holidays that commemorate shared civic milestones and national history rather than expanding the calendar to include specific religious observances."

The current federal holiday calendar is a patchwork that reflects historical accretion rather than a coherent principle. The prompt rightly notes the lopsidedness: Christmas but not Mawlid, the Gregorian New Year but not the lunar or Orthodox new year. Yet the solution is not to simply add more holidays until every tradition is represented. The government’s role in recognizing holidays should be guided by a clear distinction between civic commemoration and religious observance, and by a recognition that federal holidays serve a practical, unifying purpose.

Federal holidays exist primarily to mark events of national significance and to give federal workers a day off. Washington’s Birthday, Independence Day, and Martin Luther King Jr. Day honor foundational figures and struggles that define the American experiment. Christmas and New Year’s Day, despite their religious roots, have long been so thoroughly secularized in public life that they function as cultural, not sectarian, holidays. The Supreme Court has effectively acknowledged this in cases like *Lynch v. Donnelly*, noting that “Christmas is a national holiday because it has secular purposes.” Adding Mawlid, the Prophet’s Birthday, would be an explicitly religious holiday for one faith community, which would almost certainly violate the Establishment Clause’s prohibition on government endorsement of religion. The same logic applies to Orthodox Christmas or Purim. The government cannot pick and choose among holy days without entangling itself in theological judgments.

This does not mean the status quo is perfect. The absence of Lincoln’s Birthday as a separate federal holiday, after all, is a historical quirk—it was never adopted nationally, though many states observe it. A more compelling case for expansion lies not in religious inclusion but in strengthening democratic participation. A federal holiday on Election Day, for example, would remove a barrier to voting and send a clear message about civic duty. Similarly, a day commemorating the end of slavery, such as Juneteenth, could recognize a profound national transformation without favoring any faith.

The federal government should not be in the business of sanctifying religious calendars. Its limited number of holidays should reflect shared civic milestones, not a cafeteria plan of sacred days. Rather than adding more holidays based on religious identity, we should refine the ones we have to better serve our republic’s secular ideals.

"The federal government should limit official holidays to a few secular occasions of broad national significance rather than expanding the calendar to include diverse religious or cultural observances."

The current federal holiday calendar reflects a hodgepodge of historical accidents rather than any coherent philosophy. We have holidays for Washington and Lincoln not because we've thought carefully about which figures deserve national commemoration, but because Congress was responsive to lobbying by interested parties at particular moments. That doesn't mean the system is broken beyond repair, but it does suggest we should think harder before adding more.

If the question is whether the federal government should recognize more holidays, the answer is probably no—not because diversity is unwelcome, but because federal recognition carries real costs and should be reserved for occasions with broad, secular significance. Christmas and New Year's Day make sense as federal holidays because they mark the year's boundaries and have become cultural constants even for Americans who don't observe them Christianly. Washington's Birthday and Memorial Day serve civic functions. Labor Day and Veterans Day honor categories of people central to the national story.

But adding religious holidays like Mawlid would raise serious Establishment Clause concerns. The federal government cannot simply recognize one faith's observances without violating the constitutional separation that protects all religions from governmental preference. That protection cuts both ways: it means the government doesn't interfere with religious practice, but also that it doesn't endorse particular traditions. Mawlid, Eid, Diwali, and other observances important to millions of Americans are appropriately left to families, communities, and private employers to accommodate.

This is where the private sector and state governments should do more of the work. Many private employers now offer "floating holidays" that workers can use for religious or cultural observances of their choosing—a sensible accommodation that doesn't require federal action. State governments, which set school calendars, have more flexibility to reflect local demographics.

The strongest case for a new federal holiday might be Indigenous Peoples' Day, which would replace or coexist with Columbus Day. It would acknowledge an important aspect of American history that the current calendar ignores, and it would replace a figure whose legacy is at best contested. Several cities have already made this change. But even here, there's something to be said for letting such transformations happen organically through state and local adoption rather than federal mandate.

In short, federal holidays should be few, secular, and broadly significant—not a comprehensive map of everyone's cultural calendar.

"The federal government should replace culturally specific holidays with a two-tiered system consisting of universal civic holidays and individual floating holidays to better reflect America's pluralistic society."

The current roster of federal holidays is less a deliberate reflection of American values than an artifact of historical accretion. Created piecemeal over the past century and a half, our official calendar is heavily weighted toward a Christian, Eurocentric tradition alongside a few established civic milestones. This is why the government shuts down for Christmas, yet operates normally during the lunar new year, Yom Kippur, or Eid al-Fitr.

Singling out one religion’s major festival while ignoring the sacred days of millions of other Americans creates an implicit state preference. While this is historically understandable given the demographics of the 19th and 20th centuries, it increasingly clashes with the pluralistic reality of 2013 America. Yet, the solution cannot simply be additive. We cannot make every cultural and religious milestone a federal holiday. Shutting down the federal government, the postal service, and the financial sector costs billions of dollars in lost productivity and disrupts essential commerce.

Instead of expanding the roster of fixed closures, the federal government should adopt a modernized, two-tiered system. First, we should maintain a streamlined schedule of strictly secular, civic holidays that unite us as a nation. Days like Independence Day, Thanksgiving, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, and Martin Luther King Jr. Day belong to all Americans. They commemorate our shared national narrative, our struggles for equality, and our collective sacrifices.

Second, the government should replace the remaining culturally specific holidays—including Christmas and Columbus Day—with a set number of paid "floating holidays" for federal workers. Many private-sector companies have already embraced this model to accommodate a diverse workforce. A floating holiday system allows Christian employees to take off for Christmas, Jewish employees to observe Rosh Hashanah, Muslim employees to celebrate Eid, and secular employees to take a day for the lunar new year or a personal milestone, all without the government privileging one group's rhythms over another's.

As the demographics of the United States continue to shift, our official calendar must evolve to match. We no longer live in a monoculture, and our government’s schedule should not pretend that we do. Moving toward a strictly civic public calendar, supplemented by individual floating holidays, respects both the economic necessity of a functioning nation and the rich diversity of the American public.

The federal holiday calendar should not try to be a demographic mirror. It should be a civic syllabus. The question is not whether Muslims, Orthodox Christians, Chinese-Americans, Jews, Hindus, secular humanists and everyone else have important days. Of course they do. The question is which occasions the national government should ask the whole country to stop and mark together.

By that standard, our calendar is oddly mixed. Some holidays have clear civic meaning: Independence Day, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, Martin Luther King Jr. Day, Thanksgiving and Labor Day. Others are more ambiguous. Christmas is a religious holiday that has also become a national cultural holiday. Columbus Day is a poor fit for a modern civic calendar, both because Columbus is a complicated symbol and because the holiday no longer performs much unifying work. Washington’s Birthday is defensible, but only if we use it to teach constitutional republicanism rather than sell mattresses.

So I would not add Mawlid, Orthodox Christmas, Lunar New Year, Purim, Diwali or other religious and ethnic holidays to the federal list. That route leads either to endless bargaining or to official exclusion. If Mawlid, why not Eid al-Fitr? If Lunar New Year, why not Rosh Hashanah? If Orthodox Christmas, why not Good Friday? Government should avoid becoming an arbiter of whose sacred calendar deserves recognition.

But that does not mean the calendar should remain unchanged. The United States should have Election Day as a federal holiday. It is astonishing that we close offices to honor presidents but not to choose them. A democracy should make voting easier and more solemn. A holiday would not solve every problem of access — many private workers would still need legal protection, early voting and flexible schedules — but it would send the right message: voting is not an errand squeezed between work and dinner; it is a public act.

I would also replace Columbus Day with a holiday that better reflects the country we are. One possibility is a day of civic reconciliation and national memory, focused on Native peoples and the long history of settlement, conquest, slavery, immigration and constitutional expansion. That may sound unwieldy, but Martin Luther King Jr. Day shows that a holiday can be both celebratory and morally demanding. It can ask citizens to remember unfinished work.

The purpose of federal holidays should be modest. They cannot include every identity, and they should not pretend to settle theological or cultural rank. But they can tell us what the republic values: self-government, sacrifice, equality, labor, gratitude and honest memory. On that measure, the answer is not simply “more holidays.” It is a better civic calendar.

Strip the Federal Calendar Down to Civic Essentials

The premise of this debate gets the question backwards. We shouldn't be asking which additional holidays the federal government ought to recognize. We should be asking why the government recognizes religious or sectarian holidays at all.

Consider the constitutional awkwardness of Christmas as a federal holiday. The Supreme Court has waved this away by treating it as a "secular" cultural celebration, but that's a polite fiction. Christmas is the commemoration of the nativity of Jesus Christ. When the federal government closes its offices, pays its workers, and stops mail delivery for a Christian feast day but not for Yom Kippur, Eid al-Fitr, or Diwali, it is endorsing a particular religious tradition in a way the Establishment Clause was meant to forbid. The fact that most Americans happen to be Christian is precisely the wrong reason to enshrine the preference in law.

The right move is not to add Mawlid, Purim, the lunar new year, and the Orthodox calendar to the federal roster. That path leads either to absurdity (a 40-holiday year) or to invidious line-drawing about which faiths are "big enough" to merit recognition. The right move is to confine federal holidays to genuinely civic occasions: Independence Day, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, Thanksgiving (in its harvest-festival, non-sectarian form), Martin Luther King Jr. Day, and a presidential holiday. Drop Christmas as a federal observance, while allowing federal employees who celebrate it — or any other religious holiday — to use floating personal days.

This is not hostility to religion. It is neutrality, which is what a government serving 320 million people of every faith and none owes its citizens. A Muslim postal worker, a Hindu IRS analyst, and a secular Jewish biologist at NIH all pay taxes that fund a calendar built around the liturgical year of a single tradition. That is not fair, and the unfairness is becoming more visible as the country grows more religiously diverse. By 2050, non-Christian and unaffiliated Americans will likely make up over a third of the population.

The floating-holiday model already works in much of the private sector and in many universities. It lets a Coptic Christian observe Orthodox Christmas, a Sikh observe Vaisakhi, and an atheist take an extra day in August, without forcing the government to pick favorites. Combined with a leaner roster of truly civic holidays — the ones that commemorate the nation itself rather than any single community within it — it produces a calendar that is both more honest and more inclusive.

Lincoln's birthday is a charming idea, and Mawlid matters deeply to millions of Americans. But the question isn't whether these days are meaningful. It's whether the federal government should be in the business of declaring whose meanings count. The answer, in a pluralist republic, should be: as rarely as possible.

Leave the Federal Calendar Alone

There is something to be said, in an era of constant institutional tinkering, for leaving well enough alone. The federal holiday calendar is not perfect. It is, however, the product of a long, organic process of accretion — Washington's birthday added in 1879, Labor Day in 1894, Veterans Day in 1938, MLK Day in 1983 — and the omissions people complain about are mostly reasonable.

Take Lincoln's Birthday. The case for adding it is sentimental: he was a great president, perhaps our greatest. The case against it is structural. In 1968, Congress passed the Uniform Monday Holiday Act precisely to consolidate scattered observances into predictable three-day weekends. Washington's Birthday was nudged to a Monday between the two presidents' actual birthdays, and most states now treat it as "Presidents' Day," honoring both. Adding a separate Lincoln holiday would either crowd the February calendar or require demoting Washington's. Neither serves any obvious purpose beyond symbolic tidying.

The religious-holiday question is more delicate but resolves similarly. Yes, Christmas is on the calendar and Mawlid, Diwali, and Yom Kippur are not. This is not because the federal government has carefully weighed the theological merits of each tradition; it's because the country was overwhelmingly Christian when the holiday roster was being built, and Christmas had already drifted into a hybrid religious-cultural occasion observed by tens of millions of nominal non-believers. The constitutional doctrine that treats it as a secular winter holiday is awkward, but the alternatives are worse. Either we add every major religion's holidays, ballooning the calendar and forcing the government into the absurd position of deciding which faiths are "big enough" to qualify, or we strip Christmas out, picking a fight with the cultural habits of roughly 90 percent of Americans for the sake of a tidiness no one was asking for.

The federal holiday calendar should be modest, stable, and civic-leaning. The current ten holidays already accomplish that. They include three founding-and-sacrifice observances (Independence Day, Memorial Day, Veterans Day), two great-American days (Washington, King), two labor and harvest celebrations (Labor Day, Thanksgiving), and three calendar-rooted observances (New Year's, Columbus Day, Christmas). Columbus Day is the weakest of these, and a fair argument exists for replacing or renaming it. Otherwise the structure works.

What employers and schools do with their calendars is another matter, and properly so. A Brooklyn school district closing for the lunar new year, a tech company giving floating holidays for Eid and Diwali, a university recognizing Yom Kippur — these decisions can and should reflect local communities. The federal calendar, by contrast, should reflect the nation in broad strokes, slowly and infrequently revised. Reformers reaching for new holidays are usually reaching for recognition. Recognition is a fine thing. Federal recognition is a clumsy way to deliver it.

  3. 03The current roster of ten holidays already provides a balanced representation of American civic life, including founding, labor, and calendar-rooted observances.
  4. 04Decisions regarding the recognition of specific cultural or religious holidays are better handled by local institutions and private employers rather than through the clumsy mechanism of federal law.

"The federal government should maintain its current holiday calendar as a pragmatic baseline for national rest rather than expanding it to include every religious or historical milestone."

When we look at the federal holiday calendar, it is tempting to see it as an exclusionary list that privileges certain historical figures and one specific religion over all others. However, the current slate of official government holidays should be understood not as a perfect mirror of our diverse demographics, but as a pragmatic compromise that facilitates national rhythm and collective rest.

The primary purpose of a federal holiday is practical: it is a designated pause in the machinery of government and commerce. For such a pause to be effective, it requires critical mass. Christmas and New Year’s Day are federal holidays not because the government is establishing Christianity or the Gregorian calendar as state dogma, but because a vast majority of Americans already treat these days as times of gathering, rest, and celebration. Over the decades, Christmas has evolved in the public sphere into a largely secularized cultural touchstone—a season of commerce, family, and winter rest that transcends its theological origins.

Similarly, combining the celebrations of Washington and Lincoln into a single Presidents’ Day weekend, or establishing a single day to honor the civil rights movement through Martin Luther King Jr., are acts of civic efficiency. We cannot possibly grant federal workers—and by extension, the banking and corporate sectors—a day off for every notable president, historical milestone, or religious festival. The economic cost of halting interstate commerce and public services is simply too high.

If the government were to attempt to recognize Mawlid, Purim, Lunar New Year, and Diwali, it would face an impossible dilemma: either we paralyze the nation’s productivity by adding weeks of paid leave to the calendar, or we are forced to pick and choose among minority faiths, creating even deeper resentments.

The current system is not broken. It provides a shared cadence to the American year.
