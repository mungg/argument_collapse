"The federal government should reform the holiday calendar to prioritize a lean core of civic observances while replacing specific religious holidays with floating days off to ensure equal treatment for all faiths."

Federal holidays do two things at once: they coordinate time off across the economy, and they declare what the republic considers worth honoring. The first function argues for restraint — every additional day off costs roughly $1 billion in federal payroll plus billions more in lost output — while the second function invites endless contention. The honest answer is that the current list should be pruned more than expanded, and the criteria for inclusion should be civic rather than sectarian.

Christmas is the awkward case. The Supreme Court upheld its federal status in *Ganulin v. United States* (1999) on the theory that it has become a secular cultural holiday, but this reasoning is strained. If Christmas stays, the argument for recognizing Eid, Diwali, Yom Kippur, or the Lunar New Year becomes very strong on equal-treatment grounds. A country whose Muslim, Hindu, Jewish, and East Asian populations together number in the tens of millions cannot indefinitely justify singling out one tradition. Either we acknowledge that Christmas has effectively become a national winter holiday and add comparable accommodations, or we should be candid that the federal calendar privileges Christian observance.

Civic holidays are easier. Washington's Birthday (informally Presidents' Day) already absorbs Lincoln by custom; formalizing that would cost nothing. More defensible would be replacing some thinly observed dates with ones tied to constitutional self-government: Constitution Day (September 17), already a federal observance, deserves full holiday status more than Columbus Day, whose honoree is increasingly contested and whose civic content is thin.

My preferred reform: keep a lean core of unambiguously civic holidays — Independence Day, Thanksgiving, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, MLK Day, Washington-Lincoln Day, Constitution Day, New Year's Day, Labor Day — and convert religious holidays into a "floating" day or two that employees may take for the observance of their choice. That preserves accommodation, ends the establishment problem, and treats Americans of every faith on equal terms.

The federal holiday calendar should not try to be a census of American identities. If it did, it would fail immediately: every addition would raise the question of why another faith, ethnicity, region or historical cause was left out. A country as religiously and culturally varied as the United States cannot have a public calendar that gives everyone’s sacred days equal official status by making them all days off. Nor should the government pretend that Christmas, Mawlid, Diwali, Yom Kippur, the lunar new year and Orthodox Christmas are interchangeable civic occasions. They are not.

The better principle is that federal holidays should be reserved mostly for broadly civic purposes: constitutional self-government, national memory, sacrifice, labor, gratitude and democratic participation. On that basis, Martin Luther King Jr. Day is easy to defend. It is not simply the birthday of a minister or civil rights leader; it is a national day about citizenship, equality and the unfinished promises of the Declaration and the Constitution. Memorial Day, Independence Day, Labor Day, Veterans Day and Thanksgiving also have plausible civic meanings, even if their observance is often more commercial or recreational than solemn.

Christmas is the difficult case. As a legal matter, the Supreme Court has permitted public recognition of holidays that have both religious and secular dimensions. As a practical matter, Christmas is deeply embedded in American life; eliminating it as a federal holiday would create more resentment than neutrality. But its existence should not become the argument for adding every other religious holiday. The lesson should instead be humility: the government inherited a calendar shaped by the country’s Protestant past, and in a pluralistic society it should be cautious about multiplying official religious recognitions.

That points toward two reforms. First, the federal government should add Election Day as a holiday, or move national elections to a weekend. If any act deserves public time, it is voting. We close government offices for presidential birthdays, but not for the choosing of presidents, members of Congress and local officials. A democracy should not make voting depend on an employer’s generosity, a long lunch break or a citizen’s ability to stand in line after work. A voting holiday would not solve every problem of turnout or access, but it would state the right priority.

Second, federal employees—and ideally workers more broadly—should have more flexible personal holidays rather than a longer list of official ones. A Muslim should be able to take Eid or Mawlid, a Jew Yom Kippur or Passover, a Christian Good Friday or Orthodox Christmas, a Chinese-American the lunar new year, without needing the whole nation to stop. Many employers already handle vacation and floating holidays this way; public policy should encourage that model. Neutrality is not achieved by pretending differences do not exist. It is achieved by allowing citizens to honor them without forcing everyone into one calendar.

There is also room to simplify symbolism. Washington’s Birthday could honestly be renamed Presidents’ Day if that is how the public already understands it, or it could remain Washington’s Birthday and Lincoln can be honored in schools and civic life without another paid federal day. Not every worthy figure needs a holiday.

So yes, the United States should have a somewhat different calendar, but not a much larger one. Add a holiday for democratic participation. Preserve a modest set of civic commemorations. Treat religious and cultural diversity mainly through flexible leave, not official endorsement. A plural nation needs public rituals, but it also needs room for private conscience.

A more compelling case for expansion lies not in religious inclusion but in strengthening democratic participation. A federal holiday on Election Day, for example, would remove a barrier to voting and send a clear message about civic duty. Similarly, a day commemorating the end of slavery, such as Juneteenth, could recognize a profound national transformation without favoring any faith.

The federal government should not be in the business of sanctifying religious calendars. Its limited number of holidays should reflect shared civic milestones, not a cafeteria plan of sacred days. Rather than adding more holidays based on religious identity, we should refine the ones we have to better serve our republic’s secular ideals.

The current federal holiday calendar reflects a hodgepodge of historical accidents rather than any coherent philosophy. We have holidays for Washington and Lincoln not because we've thought carefully about which figures deserve national commemoration, but because Congress was responsive to lobbying by interested parties at particular moments. That doesn't mean the system is broken beyond repair, but it does suggest we should think harder before adding more.

If the question is whether the federal government should recognize more holidays, the answer is probably no—not because diversity is unwelcome, but because federal recognition carries real costs and should be reserved for occasions with broad, secular significance. Christmas and New Year's Day make sense as federal holidays because they mark the year's boundaries and have become cultural constants even for Americans who don't observe them Christianly. Washington's Birthday and Memorial Day serve civic functions. Labor Day and Veterans Day honor categories of people central to the national story.

But adding religious holidays like Mawlid would raise serious Establishment Clause concerns. The federal government cannot simply recognize one faith's observances without violating the constitutional separation that protects all religions from governmental preference. That protection cuts both ways: it means the government doesn't interfere with religious practice, but also that it doesn't endorse particular traditions. Mawlid, Eid, Diwali, and other observances important to millions of Americans are appropriately left to families, communities, and private employers to accommodate.

This is where the private sector and state governments should do more of the work. Many private employers now offer "floating holidays" that workers can use for religious or cultural observances of their choosing—a sensible accommodation that doesn't require federal action. State governments, which set school calendars, have more flexibility to reflect local demographics.

The strongest case for a new federal holiday might be Indigenous Peoples' Day, which would replace or coexist with Columbus Day. It would acknowledge an important aspect of American history that the current calendar ignores, and it would replace a figure whose legacy is at best contested. Several cities have already made this change. But even here, there's something to be said for letting such transformations happen organically through state and local adoption rather than federal mandate.

In short, federal holidays should be few, secular, and broadly significant—not a comprehensive map of everyone's cultural calendar.

"The federal government should replace culturally specific holidays with a two-tiered system consisting of universal civic holidays and individual floating holidays to better reflect America's pluralistic society."

The current roster of federal holidays is less a deliberate reflection of American values than an artifact of historical accretion. Created piecemeal over the past century and a half, our official calendar is heavily weighted toward a Christian, Eurocentric tradition alongside a few established civic milestones. This is why the government shuts down for Christmas, yet operates normally during the lunar new year, Yom Kippur, or Eid al-Fitr.

Singling out one religion’s major festival while ignoring the sacred days of millions of other Americans creates an implicit state preference. While this is historically understandable given the demographics of the 19th and 20th centuries, it increasingly clashes with the pluralistic reality of 2013 America. Yet, the solution cannot simply be additive. We cannot make every cultural and religious milestone a federal holiday. Shutting down the federal government, the postal service, and the financial sector costs billions of dollars in lost productivity and disrupts essential commerce.

Instead of expanding the roster of fixed closures, the federal government should adopt a modernized, two-tiered system. First, we should maintain a streamlined schedule of strictly secular, civic holidays that unite us as a nation. Days like Independence Day, Thanksgiving, Memorial Day, Veterans Day, and Martin Luther King Jr. Day belong to all Americans. They commemorate our shared national narrative, our struggles for equality, and our collective sacrifices.

Second, the government should replace the remaining culturally specific holidays—including Christmas and Columbus Day—with a set number of paid "floating holidays" for federal workers. Many private-sector companies have already embraced this model to accommodate a diverse workforce. A floating holiday system allows Christian employees to take off for Christmas, Jewish employees to observe Rosh Hashanah, Muslim employees to celebrate Eid, and secular employees to take a day for the lunar new year or a personal milestone, all without the government privileging one group's rhythms over another's.

As the demographics of the United States continue to shift, our official calendar must evolve to match. We no longer live in a monoculture, and our government’s schedule should not pretend that we do. Moving toward a strictly civic public calendar, supplemented by individual floating holidays, respects both the economic necessity of a functioning nation and the rich diversity of the American public.

The Calendar Is a Mirror, and Ours Needs Updating

National holidays are not just days off. They are a society telling itself who it is. The Fourth of July says we are a republic born in revolution. Memorial Day says we honor those who died for the country. Martin Luther King Jr. Day, added only in 1983 over considerable resistance, says — eventually, haltingly — that the struggle for civil rights belongs at the center of the American story, not its margins.

So when we ask whether the federal calendar should change, we are really asking: has the country changed enough that its self-portrait is out of date? The answer, plainly, is yes.

Three additions deserve serious consideration.

First, Juneteenth. The end of slavery is arguably the most significant event in American history after independence itself. That we commemorate the Confederacy's defeat only obliquely — and celebrate Washington and Lincoln but not the day enslaved Texans learned they were free — is a strange omission. Juneteenth is already a state holiday in over 40 states. Federal recognition would acknowledge that emancipation belongs to all of us, not just to Black Americans.

Second, Election Day. A country that calls itself the world's leading democracy should not make voting harder than buying groceries. A federal Election Day holiday would say, with the force of law, that self-government is sacred work.

Third — and this is the more contested suggestion — the federal government should establish a mechanism for recognizing major religious holidays of large American communities, even if those days don't close federal offices. Eid al-Fitr, Diwali, the lunar new year, and Yom Kippur could be designated "observed federal holidays," carrying official acknowledgment and the right of federal workers to take the day with pay, without shutting down the government. The White House already holds Eid and Diwali receptions; codifying that recognition would matter symbolically to communities that have spent generations feeling invisible on the official calendar.

Critics will say this is identity-politics holiday inflation. I'd say it's the opposite: it is taking seriously the proposition that American identity is plural, and that the calendar should reflect the country we actually are rather than the country we were in 1870.

What I would not do is drop Christmas, despite its religious origins. Christmas in America has become something stranger and more capacious than a Christian feast — a midwinter civic ritual observed by Jews who go to the movies, by Muslims who exchange gifts, by atheists who decorate trees. Removing it would feel less like neutrality and more like vandalism. The better path is addition, not subtraction: keep the inherited holidays, examine which omissions look indefensible from the standpoint of 2013, and update the mirror.
