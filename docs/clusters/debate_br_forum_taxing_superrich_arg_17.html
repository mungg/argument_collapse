"While a wealth tax is a reasonable tool for fiscal justice, it is an insufficient remedy for the threat extreme inequality poses to democracy because it fails to address the specific institutional channels through which wealth is converted into political power."

Wealth, Power, and the Limits of Fiscal Policy

Saez and Zucman build their case for an aggressive wealth tax on a fundamentally political premise: that extreme wealth concentration threatens democracy by translating economic power into political power. I want to take this premise seriously—more seriously, in fact, than they do—and ask whether their proposed remedy actually responds to the disease they have diagnosed.

The Madisonian framing with which the essay opens is striking. Madison did not worry about inequality primarily because of its consumption implications. He worried because he believed concentrated wealth would buy concentrated political influence, distort the operation of the laws, and turn the republic into something that wore republican clothing while operating on oligarchic principles. This is, I think, a correct diagnosis of contemporary America. The question is whether a wealth tax actually addresses it.

Consider what we know about how money translates into political power in the current United States. It does so through several distinct channels. There is direct campaign contribution, now largely unconstrained at the high end through Super PACs and dark-money vehicles. There is media ownership: Bezos owns the Washington Post, Bloomberg his eponymous empire, Murdoch the largest cable news operation in the country. There is foundation funding, which shapes the agendas of universities, think tanks, and advocacy groups. There is the revolving door, in which former officials are paid handsomely to ease the way for current donors. There is the soft power of social access—the dinners, the boards, the Davos invitations—which shapes what counts as a "serious" idea in elite discourse. And there is the threat of capital flight or layoffs, which gives concentrated owners a kind of structural veto over policies that might displease them.

A wealth tax addresses none of these channels directly. It reduces the stock of wealth at the top, which over time reduces the resources available for political deployment. But it does so slowly, even at the radical rates Saez and Zucman propose; their own counterfactual has Zuckerberg still worth $21 billion in 2018 had a 10 percent annual wealth tax been in place all along. Twenty-one billion dollars is more than enough to buy a great deal of political influence. It is more than the GDP of dozens of countries. The wealth tax, even in its aggressive form, leaves the underlying political pathology largely intact.

Compare this to the impact of, say, a constitutional amendment overturning Citizens United and authorizing real limits on independent political expenditure. Or a serious public financing system for federal elections, of the sort proposed by Lawrence Lessig and others. Or strict separations between media ownership and political activity. Or robust antitrust enforcement that breaks up the platform monopolies whose owners have become the new political barons. Each of these would, I think, do more to address the Madisonian concern than any plausible tax instrument.

This is not to say a wealth tax is undesirable. It is to say that defenders should be clear about what it accomplishes and what it does not. As an instrument of tax justice—ensuring that ability to pay is matched by actual tax liability—it is a reasonable proposal, though one I would still prefer to see implemented through mark-to-market income taxation and a restored estate tax. As an instrument for the protection of democracy, it is much weaker than its advocates suggest.

There is a deeper issue here that I want to surface. Saez and Zucman's approach exemplifies what might be called the fiscalization of politics. Political problems—concentration of power, corruption of public discourse, capture of regulatory agencies—are recast as tax problems. This is appealing because tax policy is technical and the authors are technicians of unusual skill. It is also appealing because it allows progressive politics to be conducted through the relatively low-stakes terrain of revenue and rate-setting rather than through the high-stakes terrain of constitutional reform and institutional design.

But politics cannot be reduced to taxation. The reason the United States has the politics it has is not primarily that the rich pay too little; it is that the rules of the political game have been written to amplify the voice of capital and to mute the voice of labor. The right response is to rewrite those rules. Tax policy can support that project; it cannot substitute for it.

There is also a strategic question. Suppose, for the sake of argument, that a wealth tax is enacted. The class of people targeted by it—numerically tiny, organizationally formidable—will respond. They will fund litigation aimed at having the tax struck down under the direct tax clause; they will fund think tank work documenting its administrative failures; they will fund electoral campaigns aimed at electing officials who will repeal it. Some of this will succeed. The history of comparable reforms in the United States—the post-2009 financial reforms, the Affordable Care Act—is one of immediate counter-mobilization, partial rollback, and decade-long trench warfare over implementation. The wealth tax would be no different, and the implementing capacity of the IRS would be precisely the part of the system most vulnerable to defunding.

The Madisonian project of preventing concentrated wealth from concentrating power requires institutions strong enough to withstand the counter-attack. A tax instrument administered by a single, politically vulnerable agency, defended by a single statute, is not such an institution. Constitutional rules, broad-based political mobilization, and structural reforms to the political system itself are sturdier.

Let me put my own view as plainly as I can. I think Saez and Zucman are correct that the postwar consensus on progressive taxation was a great achievement, that its erosion is a great loss, and that restoring high rates at the top is a worthy goal. I think the specific tax design they propose is innovative and probably workable in principle, though I have technical concerns about valuation, constitutional concerns about Article I, and behavioral concerns about avoidance. But none of these are my main concern. My main concern is that we are channeling enormous political energy into a fiscal instrument that, even in its most ambitious form, will not by itself rescue American democracy from the plutocratic drift Saez and Zucman correctly diagnose.

The wealth tax has, in the current political moment, become a kind of synecdoche for a broader anti-oligarchy program. That is understandable; it is a concrete proposal in a sea of vague aspiration. But synecdoches mislead. If we treat the wealth tax as the whole program, we will discover, after enacting it (if we enact it), that the plutocracy is still here, that the politics still favors capital, and that the public has been told the problem is solved when it has barely been touched.

Madison's "silent operation of laws" was not, in his thinking, a fiscal program. It was a broader project of institutional design. Saez and Zucman gesture at this but their proposal does not deliver on it. A serious anti-oligarchy agenda for 2020 must include the wealth tax, or something like it, as one component. It must also include constitutional reform of campaign finance, structural separation in media and finance, vigorous antitrust enforcement, labor law reform that restores worker bargaining power, and a public investment program large enough to demonstrate that the public sector can deliver outcomes the private oligarchy cannot. Without these, the wealth tax will at best moderate the symptoms of our political pathology while leaving its causes untouched.

I close where the authors began: with Madison. Madison's worry was about power. We should not let ourselves be talked into believing that the way to combat the power of concentrated wealth is to send it a slightly larger annual bill. The way to combat power is with power—the power of organized citizens, structured by institutions, defending rules that constrain what private wealth can buy. The wealth tax is a small piece of that. It is not the whole piece, and it is not even the largest piece.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A wealth tax acts too slowly to dismantle the political influence of the ultra-wealthy, as even radical tax rates leave individuals with enough capital to exert significant control over the republic.
  2. 02The proposal suffers from the 'fiscalization of politics,' incorrectly treating deep-seated structural issues like regulatory capture and media concentration as mere technical revenue problems.
  3. 03Direct political reforms, such as campaign finance limits, antitrust enforcement, and labor law reform, are more effective than taxation at neutralizing the Madisonian concern of oligarchic influence.
  4. 04A wealth tax is strategically vulnerable to immediate counter-mobilization and legal challenges from the formidable class it targets, making it a fragile instrument compared to broader constitutional or institutional changes.
  5. 05Treating the wealth tax as a total solution for oligarchy creates a false sense of progress that leaves the underlying rules favoring capital over labor untouched.
