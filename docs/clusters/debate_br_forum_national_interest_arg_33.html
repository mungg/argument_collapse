"To restore its global legitimacy and ensure durable primacy, the United States must move beyond strategic restraint and offshore balancing to embrace a genuinely multilateral foreign policy that binds American power to international law and collective decision-making."

Stephen Walt’s essay is a powerful diagnosis of the self-inflicted wounds that have diminished American standing since 2001. His call for strategic restraint, for abandoning the doctrine of preventive war, and for reinvesting in the legitimacy of American power is exactly right. Yet his own preferred remedy—offshore balancing—does not go nearly far enough in addressing the deeper cause of America’s global crisis of legitimacy. The problem is not simply that the United States has been too forward-leaning or too unilateral; it is that even a selectively engaged or offshore-balancing America continues to see international institutions and the rule of law not as ends in themselves but as instruments to be wielded or discarded as convenience dictates. To rebuild lasting global support and to make American primacy both durable and acceptable, the United States must commit itself to a genuinely multilateral foreign policy embedded within a thick web of international law, treaties, and collective decision-making, even when those commitments constrain American freedom of action. Selective engagement or offshore balancing will not suffice; what is needed is liberal internationalism rebuilt for a post-Iraq age.

Walt’s essay is at its weakest when it treats international organizations as little more than a “buddy system” to reassure allies that Washington will not act recklessly. This view mistakes a tactical tool for a strategic foundation. The United Nations, the World Trade Organization, the emerging International Criminal Court, and the non-proliferation treaty regime are not merely stages on which the United States performs its good intentions; they are the bricks and mortar of a rules-based order that has, for all its imperfections, delivered unprecedented peace and prosperity among the great powers since 1945. When the world’s strongest power treats these institutions as optional accessories—signing treaties while exempting itself from their obligations, or demanding Security Council resolutions when convenient and ignoring them when not—it corrodes the very principles that give that order its legitimacy. America’s image as a “rogue superpower” was forged not merely in Iraq but in a pattern of unilateralism that predates George W. Bush: the Senate’s rejection of the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty, the refusal to join the International Criminal Court, the abandonment of the Kyoto Protocol, and the undermining of the biological-weapons verification protocol. These were not the excesses of offshore balancing or global hegemony; they were the expressions of a broader American exceptionalism that insists the rules apply to everyone else. Walt nods toward these issues but does not give them the centrality they deserve.

A genuinely reconstructive grand strategy must place the strengthening of international law and global institutions at its core. This means not only ratifying the Rome Statute of the ICC and accepting its jurisdiction, but also reversing the so-called “unsigning” of the treaty that the Bush administration performed, and embracing the principle that no nation—including U.S. soldiers and officials—is above accountability for war crimes. It means leading a new round of global climate negotiations with binding emissions targets that the United States itself honors, rather than proposing voluntary schemes that ask nothing of the world’s largest historical emitter. It means fully funding the United Nations and paying arrears, while also supporting long-overdue reforms of the Security Council to give permanent seats to major democratic powers like India, Japan, Brazil, and a representative of the African Union, so that the Council is not the staging ground for great-power vetoes but a credible global legislature. Walt’s “buddy system” imagines the United States using NATO or the UN as a fig leaf. But the goal should be the opposite: the United States should bind itself to these institutions, accepting that its own power will be exercised through collective authorization, and that its most important decisions will be subject to the scrutiny and consent of allies and partners who have a genuine stake in the outcome.

Nor is this merely a matter of image. Embedding American power within a multilateral framework yields concrete strategic benefits that offshore balancing cannot capture. It reduces the fear among secondary states that the United States will act arbitrarily, which in turn lowers the incentives for those states to balance against American predominance. It creates a dense fabric of routine cooperation—intelligence sharing, joint policing of proliferation, coordinated sanctions—that makes it easier to confront hard cases like Iran and North Korea when they arise, because the default is collective action rather than ad hoc American pressure. Most importantly, it makes American leadership sustainable over decades, because the costs of maintaining the order are distributed and the decisions about its direction are shared. Walt’s offshore balancing, by contrast, would leave the United States permanently on the outside, intervening as a deus ex machina when local balances fail, but never building the institutional muscle memory that makes multilateral action instinctive. That episodic engagement is precisely what breeds the resentment and unpredictability that Walt wants to avoid.

Some will object that this vision surrenders American sovereignty to unaccountable international bodies. This objection misunderstands sovereignty itself. In an interdependent world, sovereignty is not the power to act without constraint; it is the power to participate in shaping the constraints under which all states, including the United States, operate. By leading the construction of a more legitimate international order, the United States can ensure that the rules reflect its own values of democracy, human rights, and rule of law, rather than ceding that role to adversaries who would write a very different set of rules. The alternative—a world where the United States insists on its own unfettered right to act while demanding that others follow norms—is a recipe for endless contestation. The offshore balancer might calculate that the United States can manage those contests from a safe distance, but the real costs will be paid in the steady erosion of the global norms that are America’s most underappreciated strategic asset.

Walt is right that force should be a last resort, that preventive war is a dead end, and that America must reassure the world of its wisdom and restraint. But the best reassurance is not a periodic gesture of self-restraint by an offshore power; it is the permanent commitment of that power to a system of laws and institutions that no single nation can override. The United States would not be diminished by such a commitment; it would be anchored. And in a world where American power is already immense, making that power accountable to something larger than itself is the only path to a primacy that others will welcome rather than resist.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The United States must demonstrate its commitment to global norms by ratifying major international treaties, such as the Rome Statute, and accepting the same legal accountabilities it expects of other nations.
  3. 03Reforming and fully funding global bodies like the United Nations Security Council is essential to transforming them into credible legislatures for collective action rather than stages for great-power vetoes.
  4. 04Embedding American power within a multilateral framework provides concrete strategic benefits by distributing the costs of global leadership and reducing the incentives for other states to balance against the U.S.
  5. 05True sovereignty in an interdependent world is best exercised by participating in and shaping the international constraints that reflect democratic values rather than pursuing unfettered freedom of action.
  6. 06Permanent institutional commitment provides more reliable global reassurance than the episodic and unpredictable interventions characteristic of an offshore balancing strategy.
