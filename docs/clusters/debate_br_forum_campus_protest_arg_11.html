"Robin D. G. Kelley’s critique of contemporary campus activism unfairly dismisses the radical potential of student demands for curricular and cultural change by framing them as mere neoliberal reform rather than essential challenges to the epistemological foundations of white supremacy."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s reflections on the 2015 campus protests are characteristically rich and provocative, but they are marred by a surprising and frustrating tendency to flatten the very movement he seeks to advise. Kelley sets up a dichotomy between two camps of student activists: a larger, reformist group demanding inclusion and diversity, and a smaller, radical contingent demanding economic transformation. In doing so, he not only oversimplifies the texture of the movement but subtly dismisses the political intelligence of the students organizing around anti-racism and cultural change on their campuses. The demand for a curriculum that reflects black lives is not a neoliberal plea for belonging; it is a direct assault on the epistemological foundations of white supremacy.

Kelley worries that students who push for diverse reading lists and cultural-competency training are granting “the university so much authority over our reading choices” and merely seeking a “more hospitable” campus. This framing ignores over forty years of black feminist and postcolonial scholarship that demonstrates precisely why the battle over the canon is a battle over power, not just personal comfort. When a black student at Yale smashes a stained-glass window depicting enslaved people carrying cotton, or when students at Amherst demand that “American Masters” not begin and end with a procession of white men, they are not asking the university to love them. They are insisting that the university’s claim to produce universal knowledge is a lie, a lie that has historically rendered entire peoples as objects of study rather than producers of theory. This is a profoundly radical epistemological challenge.

Kelley himself supplies the evidence for this. He celebrates the creation of Black Studies as a field born “in opposition to a Eurocentric university culture.” But how does he imagine that opposition was institutionalized? It happened through precisely the kinds of fights students today are waging: battles over hiring, over curriculum, over what counts as a legitimate intellectual tradition. To dismiss contemporary curriculum struggles as merely multicultural management of difference is to erase the lineage of struggle that connects the 1968 strike at San Francisco State, which birthed the first College of Ethnic Studies, to the students at UNC Chapel Hill today. The canon is not a trivial thing to be left to the university’s magisterium while we retreat to the undercommons to study Frantz Fanon on our own. The canon is a weapon of epistemic violence, and dislodging it—forcing it to make room for Toni Morrison, Saidiya Hartman, or Kelley himself—is a material victory that reshapes the intellectual terrain for thousands of students, many of whom will never join a radical study group.

Furthermore, Kelley’s sweeping critique of the language of trauma, while sophisticated, runs the risk of another form of erasure. He relays Naomi Wallace’s concern that trauma theory takes the focus “out of the realm of justice and into psychology.” But for black feminists and queer of color activists, the psychological has never been separate from the structural. The insistence that microaggressions, triggers, and trauma are political categories arises from a deep understanding that structural oppression is metabolized in the body and the psyche. It is not a retreat from justice to demand that a university acknowledge how its everyday operations wear down the souls of its black students; it is a recognition that this wearing-down is a primary mechanism of racial exclusion, as effective as any admissions test. The brilliant organizer and scholar Mariame Kaba often speaks of practicing “everyday abolition”—the work of building non-carceral, healing-centered responses to harm in the here and now. The campus demands for mental health support are a crude but understandable part of this impulse to construct a world where we treat each other’s pain as a collective concern.

There is a danger, as Kelley points out, of getting stuck in trauma. But there is an equal danger, which his essay inadvertently embodies, of a left that is so allergic to the appearance of “victimhood” that it fails to take seriously the embodied, affective dimensions of oppression. Revolutionary change requires whole people. The students demanding that their campuses see them, hear them, and teach them are not just seeking a more comfortable assimilation. They are preparing the ground—in the curriculum, in the dorm, in their own minds—for the longer and more grueling struggles to come. The project of building a new house, to borrow Kelley’s metaphor from the Freedom Schools, requires a rigorous and loving critique of the architecture of the old one, and that includes its ugly intellectual foundations.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The battle over the academic canon is not a plea for inclusion but a radical assault on the university’s claim to universal knowledge and its history of treating Black people as objects rather than producers of theory.
  2. 02Kelley’s dismissal of current curriculum struggles ignores the fact that the very Black Studies programs he celebrates were established through the same types of institutional battles over hiring and course content.
  3. 03The student focus on trauma and psychological well-being is not a retreat from justice into psychology, but a recognition that structural oppression is metabolized in the body and must be addressed to sustain revolutionary agents.
  4. 04Demands for mental health support and 'safe spaces' represent an impulse toward 'everyday abolition' by attempting to build collective, healing-centered responses to the harms of racial exclusion.
  5. 05By flattening the movement into a dichotomy of reformists versus radicals, Kelley overlooks how students are using affective and intellectual critiques to prepare the ground for broader social transformation.
