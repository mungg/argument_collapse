"Peter Singer’s effective altruism is built on a flawed consequentialist foundation that fails to account for the complexity of human values and the legitimacy of personal moral commitments."

Peter Singer's effective altruism is a form of consequentialism—the ethical theory that right actions are those that produce the best outcomes. Singer does not argue for this premise explicitly; he treats it as obvious that we should "do the most good we can." But this assumption deserves scrutiny, because consequentialism faces deep philosophical difficulties that undermine Singer's framework.

The core problem is what philosophers call the "demands of morality." If we should maximize good outcomes, then presumably we should do so without limit—until we have sacrificed everything for others. Singer tries to soften this conclusion by distinguishing between a "minimally acceptable ethical life" and a "fully ethical life." Most effective altruists, he says, aim for somewhere in between. But this distinction is ad hoc. If we should do the most good, why is it acceptable to stop short of doing the most good? The only answer is that some consideration other than consequences—perhaps fairness to oneself, or respect for personal projects—trumps the consequentialist imperative. But once we admit such considerations, we have abandoned pure consequentialism.

This point matters because Singer's framework depends on our willingness to subordinate other values to the imperative of doing good. But most people, upon reflection, believe that their lives have intrinsic meaning beyond their utility to others. They want to raise children, create art, pursue knowledge, or simply enjoy leisure—not because these activities maximize good outcomes but because they are constitutive of a flourishing human life. Singer's suggestion that effective altruists can set aside time for these pursuits is reasonable, but it requires qualification of the foundational principle.

Consider Singer's treatment of giving to universities. He argues that donations to Princeton or Yale are not effective altruism because these institutions already have enormous endowments. Perhaps this is correct. But suppose the donor cares deeply about classical music and wants to support a university orchestra that brings joy to thousands of listeners each year. Singer would say this is not doing the most good—but why should "the most good" be the only criterion of ethical giving? Perhaps the donor's act is justified by the value of beauty, the importance of cultural transmission, or the meaning that comes from supporting something one cares about.

Singer might respond that these values are legitimate insofar as they contribute to human flourishing, which is itself a kind of good. But this move makes the framework circular: we judge actions by their contribution to flourishing, but we also count certain forms of flourishing as valuable in themselves. The theory becomes a list of approved goods rather than a coherent principle.

The Batkid example raises another difficulty. Singer calculates that $7,500 could save three lives if given to malaria prevention, implying that Make-A-Wish is a less effective cause. But this calculation assumes that saving lives is the highest good. What if the joy of a dying child's wish, the relief it brings to suffering parents, and the community solidarity it creates have value that cannot be captured by counting lives saved? Singer's framework has no way to incorporate such considerations except as instrumental contributions to some quantifiable outcome.

These difficulties do not mean that Singer's practical recommendations are wrong. Donating to highly effective health interventions is good, and thinking carefully about the impact of our giving is better than not thinking at all. But the philosophical foundations of effective altruism are shakier than Singer acknowledges. We need a more robust ethical theory that can account for the full range of human values—the demands of love and friendship, the intrinsic worth of beauty and knowledge, the importance of self-respect and personal integrity—without reducing everything to a single metric of good outcomes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The distinction between a 'minimally acceptable' and 'fully ethical' life is logically inconsistent with the core imperative to maximize good outcomes.
  2. 02Singer’s framework unfairly subordinates intrinsic values like art, beauty, and cultural transmission to a narrow, quantifiable metric of utility.
  3. 03The theory ignores the fact that personal projects, family, and self-respect are constitutive of a flourishing life rather than just instrumental tools for producing good.
  4. 04The focus on quantifiable outcomes, such as lives saved, cannot capture the qualitative value of community solidarity or the emotional relief provided by causes like Make-A-Wish.
  5. 05By treating all values as instrumental to a single goal, the framework becomes either circular or an arbitrary list of approved goods rather than a coherent ethical principle.
