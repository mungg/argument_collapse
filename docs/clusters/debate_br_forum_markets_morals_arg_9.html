"Michael Sandel’s critique of market overreach is flawed because it prioritizes the philosophical corruption of social goods over the material inequalities and political-economic structures that force people into market relationships."

Michael Sandel has written an eloquent lament for the moral corrosion wrought by markets, but his argument suffers from a fundamental blind spot: it focuses almost exclusively on the qualitative corruption of goods while downplaying the material conditions that give markets their power. In doing so, he offers a cultural critique that risks becoming an apology for the very inequalities that fuel the commodification he rightly decries. A more honest reckoning with the problem would start not with the meaning of wedding toasts, but with the political economy of desperation.

Sandel’s corruption argument holds that certain practices are “degraded” when bought and sold, regardless of whether the background conditions are fair. This is a clever philosophical move, but it abstracts too cleanly from real-world power relations. Consider his own example of kidney sales. The central objection to organ markets is not that a purchased kidney functions less well—it doesn’t—or even primarily that the transaction “corrupts” the meaning of bodily integrity. The horror is that poor people will be compelled by economic necessity to sell parts of their bodies while the rich profit from their suffering. The fairness objection and the corruption objection are not separate in practice; the corruption of the good arises precisely from the inequality that makes some people desperate enough to sell.

When Sandel writes that the fairness argument cannot explain why “it would be wrong to buy a kidney even if we could create a perfect market, one in which informed, freely consenting sellers were protected from exploitation,” he is tilting at windmills. No such market exists or could exist in a world of vast wealth disparities. The very act of “free consent” under conditions of hunger or medical bankruptcy is a fiction that liberal egalitarians have long recognized. By treating the corruption objection as a distinct and sometimes superior moral register, Sandel implicitly grants that we might, in theory, have morally permissible markets in kidneys—an idea that many on the left would regard as abhorrent regardless of market design.

The deeper issue is that Sandel’s framework gravitates toward a politics of recognition rather than redistribution. He worries about the “crowding out” of civic virtue and generosity, but says little about the political and economic forces that have systematically dismantled the social protections that once made those virtues viable. When public goods are starved of funding, when wages stagnate, and when healthcare and education become commodities, people are forced into market relationships not because they have been seduced by market ideology, but because they have no alternative. The single mother who accepts payment for her blood plasma is not expressing a preference for commodified bodily fluids; she is trying to keep the lights on. Blaming market norms for her predicament is a category mistake; the problem is poverty, not the conceptual corruption of blood as a gift.

This point has important implications for political strategy. If the primary problem is that markets corrupt our moral sensibilities, then the solution is a kind of cultural reclamation: we must deliberate about the proper ends of goods and guard them from market encroachment. But if the primary problem is that capital has broken the back of labor and eviscerated public provision, then the solution is to rebuild worker power, decommodify essential goods through universal social programs, and use the state to redistribute resources. The two are not entirely incompatible, but Sandel’s emphasis tilts heavily toward the first, which risks offering a moralized critique without the structural analysis needed to make change.

This isn’t to deny that there is something genuinely troubling about the spread of market logic into intimate life. The commercialization of care work, for example, does seem to erode relationships when it is driven by profit-maximization and low-paid, precarious labor. But the fix is not to insulate care from markets by sentimentalizing unpaid family labor (which usually means women’s unpaid labor). The fix is to fund care work generously, to unionize caregivers, and to ensure that care is accessible and valued. In other words, we need to change the political economy of care, not just the moral vocabulary.

Sandel’s essay is at its most insightful when it punctures the smug assumption that markets merely reflect pre-existing preferences. But he stops short of drawing the logical conclusion: that the preferences and norms of a society are shaped by the distribution of property and power. A democratic response to market overreach must therefore be a fight for economic democracy, not just a philosophical argument about the meaning of goods. Until we address the material conditions that make selling a kidney seem like a rational choice, the moral purity of our norms will be a luxury reserved for those who can afford them.
