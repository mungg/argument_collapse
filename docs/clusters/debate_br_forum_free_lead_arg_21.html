Vanilla Gemini

"While Governor Roemer correctly identifies campaign finance as the root of political corruption, his proposed solutions are insufficient because they fail to address the overwhelming power of independent expenditures authorized by Citizens United."

Governor Roemer’s diagnosis of our political dysfunction is refreshingly blunt and undeniably accurate. By tracing the straight line from campaign fundraising to the 2008 financial collapse, he lays bare the systemic corruption that dictates policy in Washington. Congress does not govern; it collects. The toothless nature of the Dodd-Frank reforms—which left the "too big to fail" banks larger than ever—serves as the ultimate testament to the financial sector's return on its campaign investments.

However, while Roemer’s diagnosis is flawless, his prescribed cure—a voluntary $100 contribution limit coupled with taxpayer-funded democracy vouchers—underestimates the structural hurdles of our current electoral landscape. We have already seen the limitations of the Internet-driven, small-donor revolution that Roemer praises. In 2008, Barack Obama shattered fundraising records largely by tapping into a vast network of small, online contributors. Millions of Americans had "skin in the game." Yet, upon taking office, the administration turned to Wall Street veterans to manage the economic recovery, favoring the very institutions that caused the crisis. A broad base of small donors does not automatically immunize a politician from the institutional gravity of the financial sector.

Furthermore, Roemer’s proposal to operate strictly within the boundaries set by *Citizens United* is a dangerous concession. He suggests that we can cure the system by expanding the pool of givers, thereby drowning out special-interest money. But how does a candidate subsisting on $100 vouchers compete with the limitless coffers of independent expenditures? The 2010 midterms offered a grim preview of our new reality: outside groups, operating free from coordination limits, unleashed unprecedented floods of negative advertising. The newly minted "Super PACs" can accept unlimited checks from billionaires and corporations. A voucher system might raise the financial floor for grassroots candidates, but *Citizens United* has entirely removed the ceiling for the wealthiest 1 percent. In this environment, asking a candidate to voluntarily cap their direct contributions at $100 is akin to sending them into a modern war zone armed only with a pocketknife.

Even if a candidate is pure, the threat of an independent, multimillion-dollar smear campaign is often enough to keep them in line. The implicit threat of corporate spending can dictate a legislative agenda just as effectively as a direct campaign contribution. If we accept the Supreme Court’s premise that spending money is equivalent to protected speech, then trying to out-fundraise corporate America through micro-donations is mathematically doomed. To truly free leadership to lead, we must pursue more aggressive structural reforms. At a minimum, Congress must pass robust disclosure requirements—such as the DISCLOSE Act, which died in the Senate last year—so that voters know exactly which corporate entities are funding the attack ads. Additionally, we should explore corporate governance reforms that require shareholder approval before a company’s treasury can be used for political expenditures. Ultimately, if the Court refuses to distinguish between democratic participation and corporate electioneering, the only permanent remedy may be a constitutional amendment.

Governor Roemer is entirely correct that money in politics is the foundational issue of our time. It is the bottleneck through which all other crises—from our ballooning national debt to our reliance on foreign oil—must pass. His willingness to eschew PAC money and rely solely on small donors is a noble, necessary demonstration of principled leadership. But we must be clear-eyed about the sheer scale of the opposition. Fixing the system requires more than adding new, small voices to the choir; it requires turning down the megaphone that the Supreme Court has handed to the highest bidders. Until we find a way to rein in independent expenditures, the boomtown of Washington will continue to thrive at the expense of Main Street.
