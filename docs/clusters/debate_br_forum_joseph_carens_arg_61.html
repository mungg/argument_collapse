"The moral status of irregular migrants is determined by the community's collective social judgment rather than the individual's personal attachments or abstract philosophical principles."

There is a tradition of communitarian critique that would reject Carens's entire framework, and I think this tradition has something important to say, even if I ultimately find Carens's liberal individualism more persuasive.

Communitariains argue that persons are not atomistic individuals who form attachments to communities as a kind of optional enhancement. Rather, persons are constituted by their communities: their identities, their values, their very capacity for moral reasoning is shaped by the cultural and social contexts in which they are raised. From this perspective, the question of whether irregular migrants have become members of society is not one that can be answered by examining their individual attachments. Membership is not a psychological fact about the migrant but a social fact about the community.

This matters because it suggests that the community's judgment about whether someone belongs may be as important as the individual's feeling of belonging. If the citizens of a political community do not view irregular migrants as members—if they continue to see them as outsiders despite years of residence—then the migrants have not fully become members, regardless of their individual integration.

This is a troubling conclusion, because it seems to make moral status depend on the prejudices of the majority. But it also captures something important: that political communities are not merely collections of individuals but organic entities with their own self-understandings. The generous amnesty Carens advocates requires a prior act of political will—a collective decision that irregular migrants are indeed one of us—and this decision cannot be generated by philosophical argument alone.

If this is right, then the path to amnesty runs through political mobilization and democratic persuasion, not through appeals to abstract principles of justice. Carens's essay is valuable as a contribution to moral philosophy, but its political implications are limited by its philosophical method.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Membership is a social fact defined by the community's self-understanding rather than a psychological fact about an individual's feelings of belonging.
  2. 02If a political community continues to view irregular migrants as outsiders, those migrants have not truly become members regardless of their length of residence or level of integration.
  3. 03Amnesty requires a collective political decision to recognize migrants as 'one of us' which cannot be achieved through philosophical argument alone.
  4. 04The path to achieving legal status for migrants must rely on democratic persuasion and political mobilization rather than appeals to abstract principles of justice.
