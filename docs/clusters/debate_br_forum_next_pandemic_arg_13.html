The Perils of Humility as a Top-Down Project

Sheila Jasanoff’s searching critique of pandemic preparedness strikes many of the right chords. Her call for “technologies of humility” is a welcome rebuke to the hubris that imagined we could simply check boxes, stockpile ventilators, and crush the curve with military precision. Her insistence that preparedness must confront its own moral blind spots—the unequal distribution of risk, the histories of marginalization that fuel vaccine hesitancy, the sheer cussedness of democratic politics—is overdue. And yet, for all its diagnostic brilliance, the essay leaves me uneasy. It diagnoses the disease but offers a remedy that may replicate the very syndrome it seeks to cure. Humility can itself become a technocratic project, another checklist assembled by the privileged, unless we understand it as an ethic that must be lived from the ground up, not handed down from on high.

Jasanoff rightly skewers the Global Health Security Index, the pandemic playbook, and Harvey Fineberg’s D-Day editorial for their unshakeable faith that the central state, armed with the right algorithms and a unified command, can tame a microbe. She points to the “extreme discrepancy between expected and actual performance,” the tragic comedy of a top-ranked United States leading the world in deaths. But her own “technologies of humility”—institutional mechanisms for incorporating memory, experience, and citizen participation—risk becoming a newer, gentler set of procedural requirements that sit atop the same ossified institutions. There is a whiff of the committee room here: expand the stakeholder consultations, map the power asymmetries, ask who gets hurt, and so on. All worthy, yet all too easily co-opted.

Consider the practical difficulties. Jasanoff asks that we “adopt the perspectives of those who are acted upon or are themselves not able to intervene.” Whose job will it be to adopt those perspectives? If we task the same agencies and expert panels that crafted the now-discredited playbooks with this new form of listening, we are likely to get the same results dressed in more inclusive language. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention and the National Security Council, for example, have a long history of “community engagement” initiatives that end up as box-ticking exercises, town halls that produce reports nobody reads. Humility as an institutional imperative quickly becomes a performance: a deputy assistant secretary is assigned to “incorporate equity concerns,” a new dashboard is developed to track “vulnerability indices,” and the fundamental power dynamics remain untouched. The unspoken assumption is that humility can be operationalized by the same class of managers who failed so spectacularly in 2020—and that this class will voluntarily cede its own authority. That is a heroic assumption of a different sort.

Real humility, the kind that might actually reshape public policy, requires a much deeper devolution of decision-making power. It means that communities themselves, not their officially designated representatives in a stakeholder process, must be given real authority over the allocation of resources and the design of interventions. The pandemic offered glimpses of what this might look like: mutual aid networks that delivered groceries and medicines to homebound neighbors, immigrant-run community health clinics that became trusted sources of information in the face of official bungling, and workers who organized their own safety protocols when employers and governments failed them. These were not “technologies of humility” in Jasanoff’s sense—they were neither designed by policy scholars nor embedded in institutional frameworks. They were spontaneous eruptions of collective care and practical reason, born of the very vulnerability that official preparedness had ignored. If there is a lesson to be drawn from them, it is not that governments should add a “citizen participation” module to their crisis plans, but that they should create the legal and fiscal conditions that enable such autonomous, bottom-up efforts to flourish—and then get out of the way.

This is not a plea for anarchism. Governments have indispensable roles in funding basic research, maintaining supply chains, and coordinating across borders. But humility requires that those central functions be exercised with a radically different sensibility: one that begins from the premise that the people closest to a problem possess a form of expertise that no amount of modeling can replace. Instead of asking, “How can we make our preparedness plans more humble?,” we should be asking, “How can we distribute the resources and authority that would allow communities to define their own version of preparedness?” The former keeps the state at the center; the latter pushes it to the periphery.

Jasanoff’s Lear-like call to “expose ourselves to feel what wretches feel” is noble, but it presupposes a sovereign who, having learned his lesson, will now rule more wisely. A humbler posture would recognize that the wretches themselves have long known what it is they need and that the greatest failure of the past two years was not a lack of predictive models but the systematic refusal to listen to—and cede power to—those who were already practicing a kind of grounded humility born of necessity. For a genuine public policy after pandemic, we need less a reformed king and more a genuine distribution of the keys to the kingdom.
