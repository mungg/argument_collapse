Diversified MiniMax

"To protect marine life while meeting consumer demand, we must move away from rigid sustainability targets and instead adopt a diversified, adaptive management strategy that can respond to the dynamic impacts of climate change."

Here's what I find missing from most sustainable seafood discussions: the temporal dimension. We talk as though sustainability is a destination—when we achieve it, we'll have solved the problem. But marine ecosystems are dynamic, constantly changing, responding to conditions we don't fully understand.

Ocean acidification from climate change is already affecting shellfish and will increasingly impact fish populations. Warming waters are shifting species ranges, disrupting food webs, and creating conditions for new parasites and diseases. The targets we set for sustainable fishing based on current conditions may become irrelevant as the underlying system changes.

This suggests we need adaptive management approaches that can respond to new information and changing conditions—not fixed quotas determined once and applied rigidly. Fisheries science should be treated as provisional, with regular reassessment and willingness to reverse course.

It also suggests we should be cautious about betting everything on aquaculture. Farmed fish depend on feed, much of which comes from wild-caught sources. They depend on water temperature and quality. Climate change affects aquaculture systems too. monoculture operations that are efficient under current conditions may become vulnerable.

Perhaps the most resilient approach is diversified portfolios of both wild and farmed production, spread across many species and many locations, with active management and genuine transparency. Not a silver bullet but a diversified strategy that can absorb shocks.

The Mediterranean diet that launched this discussion was never monolithic—it varied by region, season, and household. That flexibility and diversity was itself adaptive, a feature that served it well over time. A sustainable seafood future probably needs similar characteristics: variety, local adaptation, and humility about our ability to engineer permanent solutions to constantly changing problems.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fixed fishing quotas are insufficient because climate change and ocean acidification are constantly shifting species ranges and disrupting marine food webs.
  2. 02Fisheries science should be treated as provisional, requiring regular reassessment and a willingness to adjust management approaches as new information emerges.
  3. 03Over-reliance on aquaculture is risky because farmed fish remain vulnerable to the same environmental shocks and feed-supply disruptions as wild populations.
  4. 04Resilience is best achieved through a diversified portfolio of both wild and farmed production spread across multiple species and locations to absorb ecological shocks.
  5. 05A sustainable seafood future requires a shift toward local adaptation and flexibility rather than attempting to engineer permanent, monolithic solutions.
