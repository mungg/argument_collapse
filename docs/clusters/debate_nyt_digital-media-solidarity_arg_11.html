"While the Gawker union vote is a positive step, the future of labor in the disruption economy depends on expanding collective action beyond digital media to include more vulnerable, non-traditional workers."

It is worth celebrating Gawker’s union vote, but also worth keeping it in proportion. Journalists are very good at noticing when journalists do something. The disruption economy is much larger and harsher than a New York digital newsroom.

The workers most exposed to the new model are often not salaried writers with public platforms. They are drivers, warehouse workers, home-care aides, retail employees, food-service workers, cleaners, delivery workers and freelancers bidding for piecework. Many are classified as independent contractors. Many cannot identify a single workplace where an election could be held. Some are managed by software, customer ratings or scheduling systems rather than by a boss who sits across the room. For them, the classic union election is often unavailable or inadequate.

That is why the lesson of Gawker should not be that the disruption economy can be fixed one enlightened office at a time. It should be that workers everywhere are searching for leverage. The Fight for $15, worker centers, campaigns against wage theft, efforts to organize adjunct professors and challenges to misclassification are all part of the same landscape. They may matter more than what happens in digital media, even if they receive less affectionate coverage from digital media.

Traditional unions can have a role, but only if they stop thinking solely in terms of permanent employees bargaining with one employer. The new economy requires portable benefits, sector-wide standards, legal protections for contractors who organize, and public policy that prevents companies from evading responsibility by calling themselves platforms. It also requires alliances between relatively privileged workers and those with far less margin for risk.

Gawker employees did something important: they rejected the notion that being young, creative and Internet-native means accepting whatever terms management offers. But the future of unions will be decided where the power imbalance is greatest. If digital-media solidarity becomes solidarity only among people who write and edit websites, it will be a boutique movement. If it helps make collective action seem normal again, it could be a small but useful spark.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The classic union election model is often unavailable or inadequate for the majority of workers in the disruption economy, such as independent contractors and those managed by software.
  2. 02The lesson of the Gawker vote should be viewed as part of a broader, diverse landscape of labor movements seeking leverage against systemic power imbalances.
  3. 03Traditional unions must evolve to support portable benefits and sector-wide standards rather than focusing solely on permanent employees bargaining with single employers.
  4. 04The long-term success of digital media solidarity depends on its ability to normalize collective action for all workers rather than remaining a boutique movement for the relatively privileged.
