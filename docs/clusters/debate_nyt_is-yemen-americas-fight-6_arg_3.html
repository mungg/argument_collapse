Diversified Claude

"The United States should maintain its targeted counterterrorism operations against Al Qaeda in Yemen while strictly limiting and conditioning its support for the Saudi-led campaign against the Houthis."

The Wrong Question

"Is Yemen America's fight?" is the wrong frame. Yemen is already several fights, and the United States is already involved in some of them. The honest question is which of those fights we should be in, and on what terms.

We have been at war with Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula in Yemen since at least 2009. That campaign — drones, special operations, intelligence sharing with whoever holds Sanaa — has been bipartisan, low-profile, and broadly defensible on self-defense grounds. AQAP has tried to bring down American airliners. There is a direct line from Yemen's ungoverned spaces to attempted mass-casualty attacks in the United States. Nobody serious is proposing that we abandon that fight, and we should not.

The new question is whether to enlist in a second, very different war: a Sunni Arab coalition campaign against a Zaydi Shia insurgency that happens to be Iran-friendly. Here the answer should be much more cautious. The Houthis did not attack the United States. They are, in fact, fighting AQAP on the ground. Their rise was enabled less by Iranian machinations than by the breathtaking incompetence and corruption of the Hadi government and the cynicism of Ali Abdullah Saleh. Treating them as an Iranian fifth column conflates a local power struggle with a regional cold war, and risks turning a manageable Yemeni civil conflict into a proxy bloodbath.

The Obama administration's current posture — providing logistics and intelligence to the Saudis while not flying strike missions ourselves — tries to split the difference. It buys goodwill with Riyadh at a moment when the Gulf is rattled by the Iran talks, without committing American forces to a campaign whose objectives are unclear. As tactical diplomacy, that is understandable. As strategy, it is incoherent. We are simultaneously helping degrade the most effective ground force fighting AQAP and signing on to a Saudi war plan that has no obvious endgame.

What should we do? Three things. First, make our support conditional and finite: explicit limits on duration, on targets, and on the humanitarian impact, with real consequences if those limits are violated. Second, push relentlessly for a negotiated outcome that includes the Houthis rather than seeking to expel them from Sanaa by force, which is almost certainly impossible. Third, keep our own counterterrorism mission against AQAP separable from the Saudi campaign, even if that means awkward operational compartmentalization.

Yemen is partly America's fight. Pretending it is wholly ours, or wholly not, are both forms of evasion.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The campaign against Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula is a necessary act of self-defense because the group has directly attempted mass-casualty attacks against the United States.
  2. 02The Houthis are a local insurgency rather than an Iranian proxy, and they are actually an effective ground force against Al Qaeda.
  3. 03Current U.S. support for the Saudi coalition is strategically incoherent because it undermines the fight against Al Qaeda and lacks a clear endgame.
  4. 04U.S. involvement in the regional conflict should be contingent on explicit humanitarian limits and a commitment to a negotiated political settlement that includes the Houthis.
  5. 05The United States must keep its counterterrorism mission operationally distinct from the Saudi-led military intervention to avoid being drawn into a proxy bloodbath.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States should disengage from the Saudi-led military campaign against the Houthis to focus exclusively on the counterterrorism fight against Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula."

There is a simpler way to answer the question, one that requires stripping away the rhetoric about alliances and regional order and looking at the actual threat to the United States. The only reason U.S. military assets operate in Yemen is to pursue Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, the group that has repeatedly tried to bomb American airliners and remains the most capable external arm of the global jihadi network. That fight is indeed America’s. The fight between the Houthis and the Saudi-backed government is not. The mistake is confusing the two.

The current approach — providing intelligence and logistics to a Saudi-led campaign while simultaneously attacking AQAP with drones — creates a strategic contradiction. The Houthi movement, whatever its ties to Iran, has been fighting Al Qaeda on the ground for years. In fact, Houthi forces and AQAP are mortal enemies, engaged in fierce battles in central Yemen. When the United States is seen as the air force for a Sunni coalition that is bombing Houthi positions, we are effectively taking a side in a local war that divides the very forces that fight our actual enemy. We risk turning the Houthis — who have no ambition to attack the American homeland — into a new front of anti-American militancy, while simultaneously creating the kind of chaos from which Al Qaeda thrives.

The smarter play would be to firewall America’s counterterrorism operations from the Saudi-Houthi conflict. This means quietly disengaging from the coalition’s military campaign while making clear, through back channels and indirect contacts, that U.S. drone operations against AQAP will continue with or without a functioning government in Sanaa. Such a posture would not require a public denunciation of Saudi Arabia, but it would require a private, firm message: we will not be enlisted in your regional proxy war, and we will protect our own interests regardless of who controls the capital. The model should be something closer to the long-running campaign in Pakistan’s tribal areas, where U.S. strikes took place even as American officials publicly criticized some of Islamabad’s policies.

This is not a call for total withdrawal or isolation. The United States should still provide humanitarian aid, support UN mediation, and maintain its naval presence to secure shipping lanes. But when it comes to lethal force, the rule must be simple: target only those who plotted against us. AQAP is America’s fight. A Yemeni insurgency, born in a remote northern province with deep historical grievances, most certainly is not. Let the regional powers manage their own feud, and let us focus on the people who actually want to destroy us. Our resources and our moral credibility are too limited to do otherwise.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Houthi movement is a local insurgency with no ambition to attack the American homeland and is a mortal enemy of Al Qaeda.
  2. 02Supporting the Saudi coalition creates a strategic contradiction by weakening the Houthi forces that are actively fighting Al Qaeda on the ground.
  3. 03U.S. involvement in the regional proxy war risks turning the Houthis into a new anti-American militant front and creates the chaos necessary for Al Qaeda to thrive.
  4. 04The United States can effectively maintain its own counterterrorism operations and protect its interests without being enlisted in a local civil war or relying on a functioning central government.
  5. 05Limiting lethal force to those who directly plot against the United States preserves limited military resources and moral credibility.
Diversified Gemini

"The United States should immediately cease its support for the Saudi-led military campaign against the Houthis because it undermines the primary U.S. national security goal of defeating Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP)."

By throwing our weight behind the Saudi-led campaign against the Houthis, the United States is actively undermining its own paramount national security interest in Yemen: the defeat of Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula (AQAP).

For years, the U.S. has relied on a controversial but highly focused drone campaign to keep AQAP—historically the most dangerous and outward-looking Al Qaeda franchise—off balance. Now, we are helping Saudi Arabia bomb the one indigenous force in Yemen that has shown the willingness and capability to fight AQAP on the ground.

The Houthis, whatever their flaws and despite their anti-American rhetoric, despise Al Qaeda. In their sweep southward over the past several months, Houthi militias have vigorously engaged AQAP fighters in central Yemen. Yet, under pressure from Riyadh, Washington has decided to subordinate our counterterrorism strategy to Saudi regional paranoia. By degrading the Houthis, the Saudi coalition is functionally acting as the air force for Al Qaeda.

We are already seeing the disastrous results on the ground. As Houthi forces are pinned down by Saudi airstrikes, AQAP is exploiting the power vacuum. Just days ago, Al Qaeda fighters stormed the coastal city of Mukalla, freeing hundreds of inmates, including senior militants, from the local prison and looting the central bank. This is a direct consequence of the chaos created by the Saudi intervention.

The United States does not have a dog in the fight between the Saudi royal family and Iranian-backed rebels. Our fight is, and always has been, against the terrorists who actually seek to strike the U.S. homeland. We should immediately halt all logistical and intelligence support for strikes against the Houthis, refuse to be dragged into a Sunni-Shia proxy war, and return our exclusive focus to neutralizing AQAP before this manufactured chaos makes them stronger than ever.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Houthis are the only local force in Yemen with the proven capability and willingness to engage AQAP in ground combat.
  2. 02Saudi-led airstrikes are degrading Houthi capabilities, which effectively provides air cover for Al Qaeda to expand its territory and influence.
  3. 03The current intervention has created a power vacuum that AQAP is already exploiting to seize cities and free imprisoned militants.
  4. 04The United States should prioritize its own counterterrorism objectives over Saudi Arabia's regional interests in a sectarian proxy war.
