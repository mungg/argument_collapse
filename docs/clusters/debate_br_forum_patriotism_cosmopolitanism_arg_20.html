"While Martha Nussbaum correctly identifies the moral failures of patriotism, her solution of cosmopolitan education is insufficient without the creation of robust global political institutions and a restructuring of the international order."

Martha Nussbaum is entirely correct in her diagnosis: the traditional boundaries of the nation-state are insufficient for the moral and practical demands of our time. Patriotism, even in its most sanitized, progressive forms, inevitably stumbles into the moral trap of valuing one human life over another merely due to an accident of geography. But while Nussbaum’s diagnosis is flawless, her prescription—a shift in educational focus and a return to Stoic moral philosophy—is tragically inadequate to the scale of the crisis we face in 1994. We do not just need cosmopolitan education; we need cosmopolitan institutions. We need a fundamental restructuring of the global political order.

Consider the events of just this past year. Over the summer, we witnessed the systematic slaughter of hundreds of thousands of Tutsis in Rwanda. For years, we have watched ethnic cleansing tear apart the former Yugoslavia. In both cases, the "international community" stood paralyzed. Why? Because the reigning political paradigm of our era is state sovereignty, a doctrine deeply intertwined with the very nationalism Nussbaum critiques. The belief that a government has supreme authority within its borders, and that other nations must respect that boundary, has provided a shield for atrocities.

Nussbaum asks us to recognize our "moral obligations to the rest of the world." But moral recognition without political mechanisms is merely sentimentality. If we truly believe that a child in Kigali or Sarajevo has the same inalienable right to life as a child in Chicago, we cannot rely on the goodwill of sovereign nation-states to protect them. The failure to intervene in Rwanda was not primarily a failure of education or imagination; it was a structural failure of international law, which lacks the mandate and the independent enforcement mechanisms to override national sovereignty in the name of human rights.

Furthermore, as Nussbaum rightly points out, the ecological crises we face—air pollution, ozone depletion, global warming—do not respect national borders. The 1992 Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro was a step forward, but it also highlighted the fatal flaw of the current system: international agreements are entirely voluntary, subject to the veto of any nation prioritizing its short-term economic interests over the long-term survival of the planet. We cannot solve global problems with a patchwork of competing nationalisms.

If we are to take cosmopolitanism seriously, we must move beyond the metaphor of Diogenes declaring himself a citizen of the world. Diogenes had the luxury of being a cynic in a relatively stable empire. We live in a fractured, heavily armed world. Therefore, a genuine commitment to world citizenship requires a commitment to building a world state, or at the very least, a drastically empowered United Nations with its own standing military force, independent revenue streams, and a binding global judiciary.

Many will dismiss this as utopian. But what is truly utopian is the belief that we can survive the 21st century clinging to the tribal affiliations of the 19th. Nationalism has given us two world wars and pushed us to the brink of nuclear annihilation. Patriotism, as Tagore understood, is a curse when it elevates the part above the whole.

Nussbaum is right to attack the inward-looking nature of Richard Rorty’s project. Rorty’s desire to rebuild the American left through a "sense of shared national identity" is a dangerous regression. We cannot build a just society at home while remaining indifferent to the slaughter and starvation of our fellow humans abroad.

But we must demand more than just a change of heart. Cosmopolitan education is a necessary first step, but it must be an education that prepares students not just to appreciate other cultures, but to actively build the global political structures that will render the nation-state obsolete. We must forge a legal and political order that reflects the moral truth Nussbaum so eloquently defends: that we are, first and foremost, members of a single human community.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moral recognition of global obligations is merely sentimental unless it is backed by political mechanisms and international laws that can override national sovereignty to prevent atrocities.
  2. 02The doctrine of state sovereignty acts as a shield for human rights abuses, as evidenced by the international community's failure to intervene in the Rwandan genocide and ethnic cleansing in Yugoslavia.
  3. 03Global ecological crises cannot be solved by voluntary agreements between competing nations; they require a unified global authority to manage the planet's long-term survival.
  4. 04True cosmopolitanism requires moving beyond philosophical metaphors toward the practical establishment of a world state or a drastically empowered United Nations with its own military and judiciary.
  5. 05Education must go beyond cultural appreciation to actively prepare students to build the global structures necessary to render the obsolete and dangerous nation-state system irrelevant.
