"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the failures of liberal democracy regarding migration, a genuine alternative must be built through concrete institutional reforms that decouple political voice and labor rights from legal citizenship status."

I want to build on Lea Ypi's analysis by focusing on a dimension she treats as secondary: the institutional structures that mediate between global forces and individual lives. Her argument about the failures of liberal democracy is compelling, but she tends to describe these failures at a high level of abstraction. The specific ways in which institutions have been captured, distorted, and disconnected from popular control deserves more attention.

Consider the visa system Ypi mentions. She notes that visas depend on citizenship, income, connections, skills, and the assessment of visa officials. But what she does not fully explore is how these bureaucratic categories themselves shape the political field. Visa systems do not simply respond to migration pressures; they create them. The distinction between regular and irregular migration is not natural but institutional—produced by the rules that govern entry, stay, and work authorization.

This means that institutional reform is not simply a means to the ends Ypi identifies. It is itself a form of political struggle. When we demand that visa categories be eliminated, or that work permits not depend on employer sponsorship, or that appeals processes be transparent and fair, we are not just seeking pragmatic improvements. We are challenging the architecture of exclusion that makes certain forms of control possible.

The same analysis applies to citizenship. Ypi correctly identifies the sale of citizenship to wealthy investors as a scandal that reveals the class character of state policies. But the more fundamental problem is the link between legal status and political voice. In most democratic systems, non-citizens cannot vote, cannot run for office, cannot participate in the deliberative processes that shape collective decisions. This exclusion is not inevitable—it is the product of institutional choices that could be made differently.

Some countries have moved in this direction. In New Zealand, non-citizen residents can vote after one year of residence. In Portugal, some municipalities allow foreign residents to participate in local elections. These are small steps, but they represent the kind of institutional innovation that Ypi's argument demands.

The challenge is that institutional reform requires winning control of institutions that are currently structured to exclude. This creates a chicken-and-egg problem: we need control of the state to change the rules, but the rules determine who counts as a legitimate political actor. The solution, I suggest, is to focus on institutions where the citizen/non-citizen boundary matters least.

Labor relations is the clearest example. Workers do not need to be citizens to participate in strikes, to organize collectively, or to demand better conditions. Union organizing can proceed without regard to immigration status. Workplace democracy—works councils, co-determination, collective bargaining—can be structured to include all workers regardless of where they were born.

Similarly, in cities and municipalities, many decisions that affect daily life are made by local authorities whose jurisdiction does not depend on citizenship. Education, housing, policing, and public space are all domains where non-citizens have stakes and can organize to influence outcomes. Sanctuary city policies and similar measures represent institutional experiments in extending political voice beyond the citizenry.

Ypi's argument for linking migration to broader struggles for social justice is correct. But I would emphasize that this linking must happen through concrete institutional interventions rather than abstract political appeals. The alternative to the current order will be built piece by piece, institution by institution, not proclaimed in manifesto documents.
