Vanilla Gemini

"While the university must protect the expression of offensive ideas, it must reject an absolutist right to disruptive protest that allows physical occupation and the monopolization of shared academic spaces."

In his provocative and impassioned essay, Alex Gourevitch articulates a defense of campus protest that is both historically resonant and deeply timely. Writing against the backdrop of the Trump administration’s aggressive, heavy-handed financial threats against universities like Columbia, Gourevitch correctly identifies a crisis in American higher education. He warns that a confluence of donor pressure, administrative cowardice, and the weaponization of civil rights law is actively dismantling the right to free expression on campus. On this foundational premise—that the university must remain a place where intensely unpopular and even offensive ideas are freely expressed—Gourevitch and I are in complete agreement.

However, in his zeal to defend the pro-Palestinian encampments and protests that have roiled campuses over the last two years, Gourevitch makes a series of categorical errors about the nature of the university, the physical reality of the encampments, and the legal and moral difference between expressing a hostile *idea* and engaging in hostile *conduct*. By romanticizing disruption and minimizing the profound difference between a public square and an academic institution, Gourevitch proposes a framework that would not save the university, but fundamentally dissolve its primary educational mission.

Gourevitch’s argument rests on a crucial redefinition of campus space. He acknowledges that campus commons are legally the private property of the university, but insists that they must be treated as "public fora" where community members can congregate to protest, even if that entails the extended occupation of space. "A commons is not ungoverned," he writes, "but it is less governed—precisely so that members of the community may give voluntary expression to their relationship to the community."

This is a fundamental misunderstanding of what a university is. A university is not a municipality. It is not a city park, a public street, or a purely civic square. A university is a purpose-built, telos-driven institution whose primary mission is the pursuit of truth through research, teaching, and structured pedagogical discourse. The physical spaces of a campus—including its quads, plazas, and lawns—are entirely subordinate to this academic mission. They exist to facilitate the life of the mind, providing spaces for students to study, converse, and transit between classes and libraries.

When Gourevitch argues for an "absolutist defense of the right to protest," including "public, disruptive acts," he collapses the distinction between the civic sphere and the academic sphere. The First Amendment appropriately protects highly disruptive protest in a public city square because the public square belongs to no single enterprise. But a campus is a curated environment. To suggest that students have an inherent right to occupy these spaces indefinitely is to argue that the political desires of a mobilized faction should supersede the foundational purpose of the institution itself.

This brings us to Gourevitch’s treatment of the encampments themselves. He writes that the protests "sometimes interfered with ordinary foot traffic," characterizing the encampments as "genuinely inconvenient, but only that." He asserts that students could still access their dorms, faculty could still go to their classrooms, and that obstacles were erected primarily "to defend and protect themselves."

This is a staggering sanitization of the spatial and physical reality of the 2024 and 2025 encampments. The encampments were not merely noisy picket lines or temporary rallies; they were physical occupations that effectively privatized shared university space. By erecting barricades, pitching tents, and in many documented cases, establishing physical checkpoints where protestors demanded to see the IDs or ascertain the ideological commitments of those trying to pass, protestors did not just use the commons—they monopolized it.

When a group of students physically cordons off a central quadrangle, declaring it a "liberated zone," they are not engaging in speech; they are asserting physical dominion over shared resources. Gourevitch defends this by pointing out that universities have historically tolerated encampments for causes ranging from the Occupy movement to anti-apartheid protests. That universities have shown leniency in the past does not establish an unfettered right to occupy space in perpetuity. The fact that an administration tolerates a rule violation out of prudence or sympathy does not mean the rule itself is illegitimate.

Furthermore, Gourevitch’s claim that disruption must be tolerated if we are to have any right to protest at all creates a false binary. He insists that if noise and disruption are punishable, "there is effectively no right to protest." But academic freedom and free speech have never precluded content-neutral time, place, and manner restrictions. A university can absolutely guarantee the right of students to hold a loud, hostile, and deeply offensive rally on the quad at noon, while simultaneously maintaining a strict prohibition on pitching tents overnight, blocking walkways, or using amplified sound outside of a library during finals week. Enforcing these basic rules of shared physical space does not persecute individuals "for protesting the actions of Israel," as Gourevitch alleges; it simply enforces the baseline conditions required for a community of thousands of diverse individuals to live and work together.

Where Gourevitch's essay is most compelling, and most tragic, is in his dissection of the "hostile environment" justification. He rightly observes that university administrators, pro-Israel advocacy groups, and now the Trump administration have weaponized Title VI of the Civil Rights Act by equating subjective feelings of unsafety with an objectively hostile environment. He notes with alarm that under current pressures—including the suspension of $400 million to Columbia—administrators are treating the mere claim that a student feels harmed as incontrovertible evidence of actionable harassment.

Gourevitch is entirely correct that democracy and intellectual life demand that we distinguish between subjective discomfort and objective harassment. The testimonies he quotes from the Harvard Jewish Alumni Alliance—students feeling a "fight or flight reaction" from chanting, or being unable to focus in a library because protesters in keffiyehs are nearby—are indeed descriptions of deep psychological discomfort. But Gourevitch is right that in a free society, hearing political chants that distress you, or seeing symbols of political movements you find abhorrent, does not constitute a denial of your educational access. The right to be secure in a university does not include the right to be insulated from viewpoints or political expressions that cause you emotional pain.

If Gourevitch had stopped here, his essay would be a waterproof defense of free speech. But he sidesteps the instances where the protests moved far beyond offensive chanting and into the realm of targeted, objective harassment. While many Jewish students merely felt uncomfortable by the presence of keffiyehs or the chanting of "From the river to the sea," there were numerous documented instances across the country where Jewish students were physically blocked from pathways, spat upon, or subjected to targeted vitriol based on their presumed national origin or religion.

Title VI requires universities to prevent discrimination that is "severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive" such that it denies a student equal access to education. Gourevitch assumes that administrators used subjective feelings as the *only* metric for their crackdowns. In reality, the crackdowns—however clumsy and heavy-handed—often occurred because universities realized they had lost physical control of their campuses, creating environments where objectively discriminatory conduct (like physical blockades or the vandalism of Jewish centers) was becoming indistinguishable from the surrounding political expression. By failing to rigorously police the boundary between speech (which must be protected) and conduct (which must be regulated), the protesters provided the exact ammunition the Trump administration needed to launch its draconian intervention.

Which brings us to the profound irony of the current moment, an irony Gourevitch touches upon but does not fully metabolize. He admits that the current crackdowns are "the coercive endpoint of a way of thinking about harm and harassment that has been developing on and off campus for a long time, coming from the left as well as the right." He mentions the progressive history of demanding speech codes, trigger warnings, and the deplatforming of speakers who made marginalized students feel "unsafe."

This is the crux of the institutional failure. For the better part of a decade, elite universities actively cultivated and institutionalized the very "safetyist" rationale that Christopher Rufo and the Trump Department of Education are now ruthlessly deploying against them. Administrators expanded massive Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion bureaucracies predicated on the idea that speech is indistinguishable from violence, that impact matters more than intent, and that marginalized groups have an absolute right to dictate what constitutes harm. Universities routinely launched lengthy investigations into professors for microaggressions or controversial pedagogical choices because a student reported feeling "traumatized."

You cannot spend ten years teaching an entire generation of students and administrators that subjective emotional injury requires a coercive institutional response, only to suddenly pivot to First Amendment absolutism the moment the speech in question is directed against Israel. The reason universities capitulated so quickly to the "anti-antisemitism" advocates is because they had completely surrendered the philosophical vocabulary needed to defend free expression. When pro-Israel advocates adopted the precise language of progressive intersectionality—claiming trauma, demanding safe spaces, and pointing to the disparate impact of campus discourse on their mental health—administrators were paralyzed. They were hoisted by their own ideological petard.

Gourevitch recognizes this hypocrisy, quoting Henry Louis Gates Jr. to warn that limiting speech always backfires. Yet, his solution is to demand a "more specific and absolutist defense of the right to protest." But you cannot demand an absolutist defense of protest while ignoring the institutional architecture that makes such a defense impossible. Universities cannot successfully resist the Trump administration’s cynical weaponization of Title VI unless they are willing to dismantle the entire internal apparatus of safetyism they have built. They must return to strict institutional neutrality, as outlined in the Kalven Report, refusing to take official stances on geopolitical conflicts, and they must uniformly dismantle speech codes for *all* groups, not just when it protects pro-Palestinian activists.

Gourevitch correctly identifies the terrifying overreach of the state in the summer of 2025. The Trump administration’s decision to withhold hundreds of millions of dollars in federal research funding from Columbia and to threaten sixty other universities is an unprecedented escalation in the culture war. It is a blatant attempt to use the power of the federal purse to force private and public institutions to adopt the administration's preferred geopolitical orthodoxies. The disappearance of Mahmoud Khalil and the imposition of extreme disciplinary procedures are chilling markers of democratic backsliding.

Faced with this "hostile government takeover," Gourevitch argues that the way to fight back is to legitimize and elevate the very disruptive tactics that provoked the crisis. He writes, "rescuing the rights now being rolled back will require a range of tactics, including vigorous protest itself."

This is a disastrous miscalculation of both strategy and principle. The state’s assault on the university is predicated on the narrative that universities are chaotic, ungoverned spaces captured by radical ideologies that are incapable of protecting their own students or fulfilling their educational mandates. By insisting that universities must tolerate the indefinite occupation of their quads and the disruption of their libraries, Gourevitch is demanding that universities validate the exact caricature the right wing is using to destroy them.

To save the university from political purges and authoritarian defunding, the university must prove that it is actually a university. It must demonstrate that it is capable of self-governance. It must prove that it can foster the most vigorous, hostile, and uncomfortable debates imaginable, while simultaneously maintaining the physical and procedural order necessary for learning to take place.

If we adopt Gourevitch's standard—where the right to protest "must accommodate fervent dissent" to the point where noise makes it "harder to study and teach," and where occupying administration buildings warrants "only modest punishment"—we surrender the university's distinct character. A student studying for a medical exam, or a first-generation student trying to attend a seminar they have gone into debt to afford, has a fundamental right to access the educational resources of the institution without navigating physical blockades or enduring the deafening sound of bullhorns outside their classroom window. To dismiss these disruptions as "genuinely inconvenient, but only that" is to treat the core function of the university with contempt.

Gourevitch asks us to tolerate the intolerable. He writes, "The less we can tolerate each other’s freedom, the more we become subject to the authorities to whom we end up appealing for protection." He is right that a culture of fragility invites authoritarianism. We must demand that students tolerate hearing ideas they find abhorrent. We must demand that Jewish students tolerate the sight of Palestinian flags and the sound of anti-Zionist rhetoric, just as we must demand that Palestinian students tolerate the sight of Israeli flags and the arguments of staunch Zionists. We must definitively sever the link between "feeling unsafe" because of a political argument and "being unsafe" due to targeted harassment.

But tolerating another person's freedom of speech does not require tolerating another person's physical dominion over shared space. We can, and must, draw a bright, unyielding line between expression and occupation.

If a student group wants to stand on the library steps at noon and shout that the university is complicit in genocide, the university must protect their right to do so with every resource at its disposal, pushing back aggressively against any donor or politician who demands their expulsion. The university must tell the uncomfortable students that part of their education is learning to exist in a world of fierce disagreement.

But if those same students attempt to pitch tents on the quad, barricade the doors to the administration building, or physically harass individuals walking to class, the university must intervene immediately, decisively, and neutrally. Punishment for trespassing, vandalism, or the unauthorized occupation of space is not a violation of free speech; it is the enforcement of the spatial contract that makes free speech possible in a shared community.

Gourevitch views the administrative enforcement of time, place, and manner restrictions as "persecuting individuals for protesting." But neutral enforcement is the only shield the university has. The moment a university allows an encampment to stand because it implicitly sympathizes with the cause, or because it fears the PR backlash of clearing it, it loses all moral and legal standing to enforce its rules against anyone else. And as we have seen in 2024 and 2025, when universities selectively abandon their own rules, they open the door for bad-faith actors in the federal government to step in and enforce their own draconian agendas.

The crisis we face in July 2025 is dire. The right to dissent is under organized, well-funded assault by a political movement that has explicitly outlined a "counterrevolution blueprint" to purge higher education. Gourevitch is to be commended for sounding the alarm with such clarity and moral force. But his remedy—a romantic embrace of disruption and a dismissal of the necessary boundaries of academic space—will only accelerate the university's demise.

To defend the right to be hostile, we do not need to turn the campus into a battlefield. We need to restore the university as a sanctuary for the mind, a place where ideas can clash with absolute ferocity precisely because the physical safety and academic access of every student is unconditionally secured. We must reject the safetyism that views words as violence, but we must equally reject the radicalism that views physical disruption as an untouchable right. Only by reclaiming its unique identity as a governed space for rigorous, unobstructed inquiry can the American university survive the storm that is currently battering its gates.
