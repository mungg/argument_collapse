"While offshore balancing has general merit, Stephen Walt’s specific proposal to distance the United States from Israel is a strategic error based on a flawed understanding of Middle Eastern realities and the drivers of terrorism."

In his sweeping essay "In the National Interest," Stephen M. Walt identifies offshore balancing as the ideal grand strategy for the United States. Much of his analysis regarding the failures of the Bush administration’s preventive war doctrine and the tragic quagmire in Iraq is painfully accurate. However, when Walt turns his lens to the Middle East, his geopolitical realism degenerates into a disturbing scapegoating of America’s most vital regional ally: Israel. By arguing that the United States should treat Israel merely as it treats "a Norwegian state" or "a Thai state," Walt demonstrates a profound misunderstanding of both the strategic realities of the Middle East and the true drivers of radical Islamic terrorism.

Walt’s central thesis on this front is that American support for Israel is a primary cause of anti-Americanism in the Arab world and a major recruiting tool for al Qaeda. He asserts that to win the war on terror, the U.S. must force Israel to withdraw to its pre-1967 borders and threaten to end all economic and military support if it refuses. This perspective is dangerously naive. It operates on the flawed premise that the Israeli-Palestinian conflict is the root cause of Middle Eastern pathology.

One only needs to listen to the actual rhetoric of Osama bin Laden and radical Islamists to see the fallacy here. While bin Laden opportunistically uses the Palestinian cause to inflame the "Arab street," his primary grievances have always been the presence of U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia (the land of the two holy mosques), the existence of apostate regimes in the Arab world, and the historical loss of the Islamic Caliphate. Al Qaeda’s ideology is rooted in a fundamentalist rejection of Western civilization, secularism, and modernity. Does Walt truly believe that if Israel withdrew from the West Bank tomorrow, al Qaeda would lay down its arms and embrace the United States? The terrorism we face is existential, not territorial.

By tying America’s success in the war on terror to the resolution of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, Walt inadvertently grants terrorists a veto over American foreign policy. He suggests that we should distance ourselves from a democratic ally to appease the sensibilities of extremists who hate us for our values, not just our policies. This is the very definition of appeasement, and it is a strategy guaranteed to project weakness.

Furthermore, Walt completely mischaracterizes the nature of the U.S.-Israel relationship. He frames it as a liability, ignoring the massive strategic dividends it pays. In a region dominated by authoritarian regimes, unstable monarchies, and state sponsors of terrorism like Iran and Syria, Israel is the only stable, pro-Western democracy. It possesses the most capable military and intelligence apparatus in the region. Israeli intelligence has repeatedly thwarted attacks against American interests and provided critical intelligence on Soviet weapons systems during the Cold War and on terrorist networks today.

Walt advocates for a "balance of power" approach in the Middle East, yet he wishes to artificially weaken the most reliable anchor of American power in that very region. If the U.S. were to implement Walt’s threat to cut off aid to Israel, the result would not be a suddenly compliant Arab world ready to make peace. Instead, it would signal to regimes in Damascus and Tehran, and to terror groups like Hezbollah and Hamas, that violence and intransigence yield results. It would destabilize the region further, likely leading to the very regional hegemony (by Iran) that offshore balancing seeks to prevent.

Walt is also remarkably cavalier about the peace process itself. He writes that the U.S. should "expect" Israel to withdraw from "virtually all" the territories occupied in 1967, citing the Clinton parameters. He conveniently glosses over the fact that in 2000 at Camp David, and again at Taba, Israel offered precisely what Walt is demanding—a Palestinian state on virtually all of the West Bank and Gaza, with a capital in East Jerusalem. Yasser Arafat walked away and launched the Second Intifada, a brutal campaign of suicide bombings targeting Israeli civilians. To blame the lack of peace primarily on Israeli "expansionism" while ignoring the structural refusal of Palestinian leadership to accept a Jewish state within any borders is historical revisionism.

Treating Israel like Norway, as Walt suggests, ignores the fact that Norway does not have neighbors actively seeking its annihilation. Norway is not surrounded by regimes funding suicide bombers to detonate in Oslo cafes. The unique level of U.S. support for Israel is not the result of a hijacked foreign policy; it is a reflection of shared democratic values and a shared strategic imperative to survive in a hostile region.

Walt’s broader strategic vision of offshore balancing has merit. The U.S. should indeed be wary of nation-building and unnecessary wars. But realism should not mean abandoning our closest allies to appease our worst enemies. A truly mature great power stands by its friends and recognizes that shared values are a component of national interest. Abandoning Israel will not solve America’s problems in the Middle East; it will only embolden the radical forces that seek the destruction of both nations.
