"The decline in homeownership is a significant concern caused by a massive policy failure to address housing supply shortages rather than a shift in consumer preference."

The narrative that Americans are simply choosing to rent instead of buy is a convenient myth that masks a massive policy failure. The homeownership rate has not hit a 50-year low because people no longer desire to own homes; it has hit a low because we have completely failed to build enough housing to meet demand, driving prices far beyond the reach of the average first-time buyer.

Across the country, we are facing an acute shortage of housing supply, particularly in the "starter home" category. The culprits are well known but politically difficult to confront: restrictive zoning laws, aggressive NIMBYism (Not In My Back Yard), and arduous environmental review processes that drag out construction timelines for years. In major metropolitan areas—not just coastal hubs like San Francisco and New York, but increasingly in cities like Denver, Austin, and Seattle—it is practically illegal to build high-density, affordable housing in areas where the jobs are.

Faced with high land costs, expensive regulatory compliance, and a shortage of skilled construction labor, developers have made a rational economic choice: they only build luxury apartments or high-end single-family homes, because those are the only projects with high enough margins to justify the risk. The middle of the market has been entirely hollowed out.

The consequences are stark. Aspiring homebuyers are faced with historically low inventory, sparking bidding wars that push prices up at a rate far outpacing wage growth. Renters, meanwhile, are trapped paying exorbitant rents because of the same supply shortage, leaving them unable to save for the increasingly large down payments required to break into the ownership market.

To reverse this trend, we do not need more federal programs subsidizing demand, which only pushes prices higher. We need a massive deregulation of the housing supply. Cities must reform their zoning codes to allow for by-right development, accessory dwelling units, and missing-middle housing like duplexes and townhomes. Until we address the artificial scarcity of housing, the dream of homeownership will remain an exclusive privilege of the wealthy.
