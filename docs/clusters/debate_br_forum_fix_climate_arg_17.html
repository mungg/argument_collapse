Diversified Claude

"While experimentalist governance is the correct model for sectoral decarbonization, it must be paired with a rising, border-adjusted carbon price to provide the necessary economic signal and scaling mechanism that sectoral committees alone cannot achieve."

Sabel and Victor have written the most clear-eyed assessment of climate diplomacy that has appeared in a long time, and their experimentalist alternative deserves to be taken seriously by anyone who has watched the pageant of UNFCCC summits with mounting despair. They are right that the architects of Kyoto and Paris misread the lessons of Montreal, right that "global, binding, comprehensive" is a fantasy of stable hegemony rather than a workable design principle, and right that sectoral, multi-scale, iterative problem-solving has a far better track record than top-down targets. Their reading of John Dewey as the patron saint of climate governance is inspired. And yet I want to push back on one significant omission. For all their care in describing how Montreal actually worked, they oddly underplay the most replicable and scalable instrument we have for destabilizing fossil-fuel incumbency across sectors at once: a price on carbon.

The essay invokes "penalty defaults" repeatedly, and rightly so. The threat of trade sanctions on Russia, the looming loss of "license to operate" for DuPont, the prospect of being designed out of the metered-dose-inhaler market — these are the pressures that turned obstruction from the safe bet into the risky one. But what is a serious, escalating, border-adjusted carbon price if not a penalty default operating across the economy simultaneously? It tells every producer of steel, cement, ammonia, kerosene, and electricity that the cost of doing nothing is rising on a schedule, and that the cost of doing something — anything plausible — will fall relative to that baseline. It is the universal solvent of incumbent advantage.

Sabel and Victor will object, fairly, that carbon prices have been politically toxic, that the Yellow Vests in France and the failure of Washington State's ballot initiatives demonstrate the limits of pricing as a political project, and that emissions trading systems like the EU ETS have a long history of being gamed, overallocated, and rendered toothless. All true. But this is an argument for designing prices better, not for treating them as peripheral. The EU ETS, after a decade of disappointment, is now sustaining a carbon price above €25/tonne and is the reason German lignite is being shuttered ahead of schedule. The forthcoming Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, which the European Commission has signaled it will propose in 2021, is precisely the kind of trade-linked penalty default that Sabel and Victor extol in the Montreal context — except instead of operating on a single class of chemicals, it threatens to operate on every emissions-intensive traded good.

This matters because the experimentalist story, taken on its own, has a scaling problem the authors do not quite confront. Montreal worked because the number of substances was small, the number of producers smaller still, the substitutes were chemically tractable, and a handful of sectoral committees could plausibly track the state of play across the entire global industry. Climate change is qualitatively different: every productive activity emits, every consumer is implicated, and the number of relevant "sectors" runs into the hundreds depending on how you cut them. The IMO process for shipping that the authors celebrate is genuinely encouraging — and it has taken roughly fifteen years to produce a 0.5% sulfur cap and a not-yet-binding ambition to halve GHG emissions by 2050. ICAO's CORSIA scheme for aviation is similarly slow and similarly weak. If we need a Maersk-and-IMO process for cement, another for ammonia, another for petrochemicals, another for long-haul trucking, another for residential heating, and so on, we will run out of decade before we run out of sectors.

A carbon price addresses this scaling problem because it does not require the regulator to know in advance which sector should move first, which substitute will work, or which firm has the best technology. It puts the question to the entire economy at once and lets the experimentalist machinery the authors describe go to work inside each sector, with the price providing the constant background pressure that turns local pilots into business cases. This is not in tension with Sabel and Victor's vision; it is the connective tissue their vision needs.

Consider how this would actually work in the steel sector they mention. HYBRIT in Sweden and SALCOS in Germany are exactly the kind of frontier experiments they describe, with governments co-funding hydrogen-based direct reduction trials. But these projects only pencil out commercially if hydrogen-reduced steel can charge a premium, or if conventional steel pays a penalty. Without a rising carbon price (and a border adjustment to prevent the premium from being undercut by Chinese or Indian imports), HYBRIT remains a demonstration project; with it, HYBRIT becomes a template that other producers race to copy or be designed out of the market. The "penalty default" becomes concrete, quantified, and predictable rather than vaguely threatened.

The same point applies to the place-based, locally-elaborated sectors Sabel and Victor describe — buildings, grids, distribution. A carbon price flowing through electricity and fuel markets gives every utility planner, building code official, and appliance manufacturer a continuously updated signal about what is worth doing. Local experimentation tells you how to retrofit a Berlin tenement versus a Bangalore apartment block, but the price tells you why the retrofit is worth doing in the first place.

There is a deeper philosophical point here too. Sabel and Victor draw on Dewey to argue against the false choice between technocracy and democracy. I agree. But their essay tilts more heavily toward expert sectoral committees than its rhetoric admits. The Montreal committees of "industry, academia, and government regulators" reviewing MDIs are, whatever their virtues, not particularly democratic bodies. They work because the stakes are narrow and the public is largely indifferent. Climate policy reaches into every household's thermostat, commute, and grocery bill, and it will not be accepted as legitimate if it is governed by hundreds of analogous sectoral committees operating largely out of public view. A carbon price, by contrast — particularly one returned as a dividend, as advocated by the Climate Leadership Council and many on both left and right — is legible to citizens in a way that sectoral standard-setting is not. People can see it on their utility bill and see the dividend in their bank account. Whatever its flaws, it is a democratic instrument in a way that the IMO's MEPC committee is not.

None of this is to deny what Sabel and Victor have accomplished. Their critique of the Paris architecture is devastating and overdue: NDCs aggregated by country produce information organized along exactly the wrong axis, and the "rulebook" forecloses the kind of intrusive peer review that experimentalism requires. Their insistence that thin initial consensus, devolved problem-solving, and periodic recalibration must replace the fantasy of binding global agreement is correct. Their reframing of Montreal as a process of penalty-default-driven sectoral learning rather than a triumph of comprehensive treaty design is the most useful pedagogical move in the climate policy literature this year.

What I want to add — or perhaps to insist on — is that the experimentalist machinery they describe will run faster, cover more sectors, and produce more durable political coalitions if it is paired with a rising, predictable, border-adjusted carbon price as the connective signal across the economy. Montreal had something analogous, even if it is rarely described this way: the implicit "price" on chlorofluorocarbons was infinite, because they were being phased out entirely on a known schedule. Every producer knew the CFC business was ending and acted accordingly. We cannot phase carbon entirely on a fixed schedule across all uses, but we can put it on a rising trajectory that has the same psychological effect on capital allocation.

A final, more political observation. Sabel and Victor close with a sober appraisal of what the incoming Biden administration can do given Senate polarization: aggressive administrative action, place-based grid investment, and a struggle to mobilize resources. Fair enough. But the policy instrument with the most plausible bipartisan path — and the only one that does not depend entirely on regulatory rulemakings that the next Republican administration can reverse — is a carbon fee with dividend return. Some version of this is supported by figures as varied as James Baker, Janet Yellen, and Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez's allies in the climate caucus. It is the rare climate instrument that builds a constituency for its own continuation, because the dividend creates a class of beneficiaries who will resist its repeal.

Experimentalist governance is the right model for how decarbonization gets done sector by sector. A serious carbon price is the right instrument for telling every sector, at the same time, that the doing must begin. The two are complements, not substitutes. Sabel and Victor have given us the better half of the argument; we should not stop there.
