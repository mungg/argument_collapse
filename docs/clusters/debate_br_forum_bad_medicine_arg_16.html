"The Bayh-Dole Act is a highly successful piece of legislation that facilitates the commercialization of life-saving medical discoveries, and its reform—rather than its abolition—is the key to maintaining both innovation and integrity."

The Bayh-Dole Act, which Marcia Angell portrays as a Faustian bargain that corrupted academic medicine, is in fact one of the most successful pieces of science policy legislation in American history. Before its passage, the situation was grim: the government held tens of thousands of patents on discoveries arising from federally funded research, yet fewer than five percent had ever been commercialized. The reason was simple. Without the ability to secure an exclusive license, no private company would risk the hundreds of millions of dollars required to take a promising laboratory finding through clinical trials and into the hands of patients. Bayh-Dole changed that calculus, and the results have been spectacular.

Consider just a few of the therapies that trace their lineage to university patents licensed under the Act. Gleevec, the breakthrough drug for chronic myeloid leukemia, emerged from research at the University of California and the Dana-Farber Cancer Institute, and was developed by Novartis under a patent license. Herceptin, which has saved countless women from aggressive breast cancer, originated in the laboratory of Dr. Dennis Slamon at UCLA and was commercialized by Genentech in a partnership that simply would not have happened without the expectation of patent exclusivity. The recombinant DNA technology invented by Stanley Cohen and Herbert Boyer, which Angell holds up as a model of non-exclusive licensing, itself generated over $250 million in royalties for Stanford University—money that was ploughed back into research. That technology was an unusually broad platform invention; for most targeted therapeutics, investors demand at least a head start on competitors, and that is what a patent provides.

Angell’s nostalgia for a pre-1980 golden age of disinterested science is historically romantic. Yes, Jonas Salk famously declined to patent the polio vaccine, but that vaccine was developed with massive private philanthropic support from the March of Dimes, a funding model that is not replicable for most diseases. Even then, large-scale production and distribution required government procurement. Angell’s implication that the current era of patenting has not accelerated medical progress is contradicted by the simple fact that the pipeline of new drugs, particularly biologics and targeted therapies, has never been richer. The notion that “medical research was every bit as productive before Bayh-Dole” ignores the genomics revolution and the personalized medicines that patents helped make possible.

None of this is to deny that abuses exist. Some universities have indeed treated their technology transfer offices like lottery tickets, and a few have entered into exclusive licenses that led to exorbitant drug prices. But the Bayh-Dole Act itself contains a remedy: march-in rights. The government retains the authority to require a patent holder to grant additional licenses if the patented invention is not being made “available to the public on reasonable terms.” The fact that no administration has ever exercised those rights is a political failure, not a flaw in the statute. If we are serious about controlling drug costs, we should demand that the National Institutes of Health use its march-in authority, as patient groups have urged in the case of expensive HIV drugs. That would address the real problem without destroying the innovation ecosystem that has made the United States the world leader in biomedical research.

Angell’s call for academic medical centers to place all discoveries in the public domain or to license them non-exclusively is a recipe for stagnation. It would cut off the flow of private risk capital to early-stage biotech companies, leaving universities with brilliant ideas and no way to develop them. The federal government cannot shoulder that burden alone. The better path is to strengthen conflict-of-interest management, ensure that clinical trial data are transparent and independently analyzed, and use the leverage that Bayh-Dole already provides to protect the public interest. We can have both innovation and integrity; we do not need to burn down the house to fix a leaky roof.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Before the Bayh-Dole Act, the vast majority of government-held patents were never commercialized because private companies lacked the incentive of exclusive licensing to justify the high costs of clinical trials.
  2. 02Numerous breakthrough therapies, such as Gleevec and Herceptin, would likely not have reached patients without the patent exclusivity and university-industry partnerships enabled by the Act.
  3. 03The claim that medical research was just as productive before 1980 ignores how patents have specifically accelerated the modern genomics revolution and the development of personalized medicine.
  4. 04The high cost of drugs is a result of the government's failure to exercise existing 'march-in rights' to ensure public access, rather than an inherent flaw in the statute itself.
  5. 05Placing all academic discoveries in the public domain would stifle innovation by cutting off the private risk capital necessary to transform laboratory findings into usable medical treatments.
