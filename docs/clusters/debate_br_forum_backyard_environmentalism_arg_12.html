"While the shift toward a performance-based 'rolling-rule' regulatory regime is a positive development, its success is driven by managerial discovery and market competition rather than the democratic deliberation and civic participation emphasized by the lead essay."

Two Cheers for the Rolling-Rule Regime, From the Inside

I have spent twenty years in environmental compliance at a large manufacturing firm, and I read the Fung-Sabel-Karkkainen essay with a mixture of recognition and irritation. Recognition, because the architecture they describe resembles, in important respects, what people in my line of work have been quietly building since the late 1980s. Irritation, because the authors persist in framing this evolution as if it were primarily a democratic awakening rather than a managerial discovery, and because they cannot bring themselves to acknowledge that some of the smartest reform proposals in this area have come from people they would call "industry."

Let me start with the recognition. The authors are right that command-and-control regulation as it developed under the original Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, and RCRA produces a particular kind of pathology, which I would describe less politely than they do. The pathology is this: a regulation aimed at the wrong thing produces enormous compliance expenditure that delivers little environmental gain, and once written it cannot be unwritten because every party with a stake in the existing rule—including environmental NGOs that fundraised against it—has an interest in keeping it in place. I have personally watched my firm spend tens of millions of dollars retrofitting equipment to meet a technology-based standard that, by the time we installed it, had already been superseded by cheaper and cleaner alternatives we were not allowed to use. The "Best Available Control Technology" framework is the regulatory equivalent of buying a 1992 computer in 1999 because that's what the catalog says.

The authors are also right that the Toxics Release Inventory has been transformative. I was in the room when our senior leadership saw the first published TRI rankings in 1989. I have rarely seen anything concentrate executive attention so quickly. Within eighteen months we had a corporate-wide reduction program that produced cuts no rule-based system would have extracted from us, because we found efficiencies our engineers had never been incentivized to find. The Massachusetts TURA experience the authors describe is consistent with what we saw: once you require a plan, the planning itself reveals waste, and the waste, once revealed, gets reduced because reducing it is profitable.

So far, so good. Where I part company with the authors is in their insistence on framing this as a participatory and democratic phenomenon. The reason TRI worked is not that it activated deep deliberative citizenship; it is that it created a competitive comparison among firms that imposed reputational costs on laggards. The work was done not by community deliberation but by Wall Street analysts and trade press journalists. The authors note in passing that TRI disclosures move share prices, and then proceed to talk as if the engine of the reform were town-hall democracy. The actual engine is more prosaic: in a world where firms compete for capital, customers, and skilled workers, making environmental performance visible makes it expensive to be dirty. This is closer to a Hayekian story about information than a Deweyan story about deliberation.

Responsible Care, the chemical industry's program, gets a glancing dismissal in the essay—"so far inconclusive," "risks degenerating into a public-relations maneuver." I think this is unfair. Responsible Care has produced real improvements in process safety across CMA member firms, and it has done so without the prodding of an external monitor. It struggles where the authors say it struggles—with members who refuse to discipline themselves—but the answer to that problem is not necessarily the importation of "public institutions and authority" along the lines of INPO. The INPO model worked in nuclear power because there are about a hundred commercial reactors in the United States, all owned by utilities with deep regulatory entanglements, and because a single Three Mile Island can destroy the industry. The chemical sector is more diffuse, more competitive, and less existentially exposed. Different sectors will need different governance mixes.

This brings me to what I want to add to the authors' analysis. They have built an account that is mostly about how regulators should change. They have surprisingly little to say about how firms should change. In my experience, the rolling-rule architecture they describe works only when firms have built genuine internal environmental management systems—real measurement, real continuous improvement, real integration of environmental criteria into operational decision-making. The ISO 14001 standard, which has spread rapidly through global manufacturing since its publication in 1996, provides a generic template for this. Firms that have implemented 14001 seriously can engage productively with the kind of performance-based regulation the authors describe; firms that have not, cannot. Any reform agenda that does not address the management capacity of regulated entities is incomplete.

There is also a problem the authors notice but do not solve, which is the asymmetry between large and small firms. Big chemical companies have environmental staffs, lawyers, planners, and the bandwidth to engage with rolling-rule processes. A two-hundred-employee metal finishing shop does not. The TURA experience confirms that small firms find the planning requirement burdensome, even when they ultimately benefit from it. If we want a performance-based regime to apply broadly, we will need a delivery infrastructure—technical assistance providers like the Office of Technical Assistance in Massachusetts, but national, well-funded, and integrated with industry trade associations rather than positioned as adversaries.

Finally, a word about cost-benefit analysis, which the authors dispatch in a paragraph and which I think deserves better treatment. The authors are correct that formal cost-benefit analysis as practiced by OIRA does not deliver the orderly decentralization its promoters promised. But cost-benefit thinking, applied at the level of individual regulatory decisions, has prevented enormous quantities of wasted investment. The discipline of asking "what does this rule actually cost, and what does it actually deliver" is not the enemy of the rolling-rule regime; it is one of its essential intellectual tools. Without something like cost-benefit reasoning, the "minimal performance standards" and "plausible targets for improvement" the authors talk about cannot be calibrated. The choice is not between cost-benefit analysis and deliberation; it is between cost-benefit analysis done well and cost-benefit analysis done badly.

I will close where I began: the authors have correctly identified something real and important happening in environmental regulation. They have, in my view, slightly mistaken its character by giving it a participatory-democratic gloss that the underlying mechanisms do not quite support. The new architecture works because it harnesses competitive pressure, measurement, and continuous improvement—tools that come as readily from operations research and quality management as from democratic theory. Those of us who have been working on environmental performance from inside firms welcome the shift. We would welcome it more warmly if its academic chroniclers were willing to see us as collaborators rather than as cases to be transcended.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The effectiveness of the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) stems from reputational costs and capital market pressures on firms rather than town-hall style democratic engagement.
  2. 02The lead essay unfairly dismisses industry-led initiatives like 'Responsible Care,' which have achieved real safety improvements through internal discipline.
  3. 03A successful performance-based regime requires firms to develop internal environmental management systems, such as ISO 14001, to integrate environmental criteria into operational decision-making.
  4. 04The rolling-rule model creates a significant administrative burden for small firms that lacks the resources of large corporations, necessitating a national technical assistance infrastructure.
  5. 05Cost-benefit analysis is an essential intellectual tool for calibrating performance standards and should be viewed as a complement to, rather than an alternative to, the new regulatory architecture.
  6. 06The evolution of environmental regulation is rooted in tools from operations research and quality management rather than democratic theory.
