"The Supreme Court’s decision in Citizens United is based on a sociologically naïve understanding of corruption that ignores how the legal dependency of representatives on wealthy contributors systematically distorts democratic representation."

Lawrence Lessig is right to insist that the constitutional question after Citizens United cannot be reduced to whether anyone has been caught with a paper bag of cash. The most important corruption in American politics is not the comic-opera villainy of a Blagojevich trying to sell a Senate seat. It is the quieter corruption of dependence: the daily, normalized, professionally managed, entirely legal orientation of representatives toward the people and institutions that can finance their political survival.

That point is worth defending because it is precisely what the majority in Citizens United refuses to see. Justice Kennedy’s opinion treats corruption as a transaction: dollars exchanged for official acts. If the expenditure is independent, there is no bargain; if there is no bargain, there is no corruption; if there is no corruption, the First Amendment forbids regulation. This chain of reasoning is tidy, but it is sociologically naïve. It imagines politics as if influence operated only through explicit contracts. But political power is often exercised by anticipation. A member of Congress need not be told what a large industry wants if she knows that industry can flood her district with advertisements. A senator need not receive instructions from a wealthy donor class if he knows what kinds of votes will make future fundraising harder.

Lessig’s language of “institutional corruption” captures this anticipatory structure. It does not accuse every member of Congress of bad faith. In fact, its force depends on the opposite premise: that ordinary people, placed in institutions with perverse dependencies, will adapt to those dependencies while continuing to understand themselves as public servants. That is why the analogy to alcoholism, though harsh, is illuminating. The alcoholic parent may love her children. The problem is not the absence of love but the presence of a competing dependency that predictably distorts conduct. Likewise, the privately financed campaign system does not require members of Congress to stop caring about constituents. It requires them to care, constantly and professionally, about contributors too.

The framers’ phrase, “dependent upon the People alone,” does real work here. Of course the founding generation did not create a fully democratic republic by our standards. The electorate was restricted; slavery disfigured the constitutional order; women were excluded. Still, the structural idea that representatives should answer to the represented rather than to some separate patron was central to republican government. The constitutional design obsessively guarded against certain forms of improper dependence: foreign titles, royal patronage, offices of profit, emoluments. The modern campaign-finance system has created a dependency far larger than many of those the framers feared, yet we treat it as an inevitable feature of political life.

Citizens United worsens that dependency not simply because corporations can now spend money, but because the decision constitutionalizes blindness. It announces that the only corruption worth noticing is quid pro quo corruption and that independent expenditures cannot generate it. But independence in campaign law is a formal category, not a social reality. Anyone who has watched modern campaigns knows that candidates, parties, interest groups, consultants, and donors operate in a common ecology. They read one another’s signals. They hire from the same pool of professionals. They understand which messages help and which hurt. The law may forbid coordination, but it cannot forbid mutual awareness.

Nor can disclosure alone solve the problem. Disclosure is necessary, and the Court was correct to uphold it. But transparency is not the same as equality of dependence. If a corporation or union spends $5 million in a race, voters may learn that fact and discount the message. Yet the candidate will learn it too, and so will every other candidate. The lesson will not merely be “this speaker has a view.” It will be “this speaker has the capacity to reward friends and punish enemies.” That capacity changes the political environment even before it is used.

Lessig’s “iceberg” point is especially important. The credible threat to spend can matter more than the spending itself. A well-financed group need not defeat every opponent. It need only make examples of a few. After that, legislators internalize the danger. This is why industries can exert power even when public opinion runs strongly against them. A member of Congress may know that voters favor reform in the abstract, but also know that the affected industry can make the issue salient, confusing, and expensive at precisely the wrong moment. The dependency is not always ideological; often it is logistical. Campaigns need money to survive.

The best response, then, is not merely to lament Citizens United or to wait for the Court to repent. Congress should build an alternative system of campaign finance that changes the daily incentives of candidates. Public financing through small-dollar matching funds, as proposed in the Fair Elections Now Act, would not silence anyone. It would give candidates a different route to viability. A democracy-voucher system would be still better. If every citizen had a voucher to allocate to candidates, the act of fundraising would become more closely aligned with the act of representation. Candidates would still seek money, but they would seek it from the many rather than the few.

Critics object that public financing forces taxpayers to support candidates they dislike. But the current system already forces citizens to live under laws shaped by fundraising pressures they neither chose nor approved. A voucher system is less coercive than the status quo because each citizen directs the money. Critics also object that such systems will not prevent independent expenditures. True. But the goal is not to purify politics of all outside speech. It is to ensure that candidates can run competitive campaigns without becoming dependent on concentrated wealth. If outside groups speak, candidates should at least have a democratic funding base from which to answer.

A broader constitutional understanding of corruption would help, but reform cannot depend entirely on judicial conversion. The Court’s First Amendment doctrine is now deeply skeptical of expenditure limits. But the First Amendment does not require Congress to preserve a campaign-finance system in which members spend hours each day dialing for dollars. It does not forbid public subsidies, matching funds, vouchers, stronger disclosure, or rules against coordination with teeth. The reform agenda should shift from suppressing speech to multiplying democratic speech.

Lessig’s central contribution is moral as much as legal. He gives a name to a condition that Americans already recognize. People may not know the details of Buckley, Austin, McConnell, or Citizens United, but they know that Congress often seems attentive to organized money in a way it is not attentive to ordinary citizens. They see exemptions, subsidies, loopholes, and delays that line up suspiciously well with the interests of those able to finance campaigns. They infer corruption, and they are not irrational to do so.

The danger is not only bad policy. It is democratic despair. When citizens believe that government answers to funders first, they withdraw, or they rage, or they become susceptible to demagogues who promise to smash the whole system. A republic cannot function indefinitely on cynicism. It requires some plausible belief that participation matters.

Citizens United did not create the problem of institutional corruption. Buckley’s protection of independent expenditures and the escalating cost of campaigns had already done much of the damage. But Citizens United removes one restraint and, more troublingly, narrows the constitutional imagination. Against that narrowing, Lessig’s essay performs an essential task. It reminds us that corruption is not only a crime. It is also a deformation of dependence. A Congress dependent upon contributors as well as citizens is not the Congress a democracy deserves. The cure is not less speech, but a system in which the speech of candidates and representatives is made possible by dependence on the people, not on the funders who hover behind them.
