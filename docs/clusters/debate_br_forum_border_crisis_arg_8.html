"The response to migration must shift from moralizing discourse to institutional reforms that dismantle the legal and economic structures used to exploit migrant labor and divide the working class."

Lea Ypi’s central move is the right one: she refuses to let migration be treated as a self-contained “problem,” as if people arriving at borders were the original cause of political crisis rather than one of its clearest symptoms. The great strength of her essay is to restore causal order. People do not wake up in Dakar, San Salvador, Kabul, Tirana, Khartoum, or Port-au-Prince and decide, in a vacuum, to become objects of suspicion in countries whose languages they may not speak and whose police may deport them. They move because labor markets, wars, debt, climate shocks, family obligations, state collapse, and sheer bad luck have narrowed the field of viable choices.

This does not mean migration is never voluntary, never hopeful, never an expression of agency. It often is. But Ypi is right that liberal societies have built a moral vocabulary that recognizes freedom when the mobile person is rich and criminality when the mobile person is poor. A hedge fund manager with a second passport is “global.” A nurse recruited from the Philippines is “essential.” A farmworker without papers is “illegal.” A Syrian family in a rubber boat is a “flow.” These categories are not neutral descriptions. They are political judgments about which human beings are permitted to convert movement into opportunity.

Ypi’s example of golden visas is especially revealing because it strips away the pretense that contemporary border regimes are primarily about culture, security, or civic attachment. If the worry were really that newcomers might not share democratic values, we would not assume that a sufficiently large bank transfer proves devotion to constitutional government. If the worry were pressure on housing, schools, or hospitals, we would not welcome real estate investors whose arrival may worsen housing affordability. If the worry were national cohesion, we would not sell access to people whose lives may remain socially detached from the communities whose passport they purchase. Investor citizenship shows that the border is often a class filter masquerading as a moral boundary.

Yet the argument should be pushed further. The alternative to exclusionary nationalism cannot simply be a better discourse about migrants, though that matters. Nor can it be only a demand for compassion, which Ypi rightly treats as insufficient. The necessary response is institutional: build forms of democratic power that make workers less vulnerable to the border as a weapon.

The fundamental fact about “illegal” migration is that illegality is politically produced. A worker becomes deportable because the state has constructed a legal status that denies her ordinary rights. Employers then profit from that status. A worker who fears removal is less likely to report wage theft, unsafe conditions, sexual harassment, landlord abuse, or retaliation. Deportability is not an accidental cruelty; it is a labor discipline. It divides the workplace into citizens who have formal rights but shrinking bargaining power, documented migrants whose status may depend on an employer, and undocumented migrants who can be threatened into silence.

That is why the slogan “migrants undercut wages” gets the mechanism backward. Workers with fewer rights are used to undercut wages. The remedy is not to make those workers more precarious or to expel them. It is to remove the legal vulnerability that allows employers to use them as a wedge. Regularization, portable work permits, equal labor-law enforcement, sectoral bargaining, and union access are not sentimental pro-migrant gestures. They are pro-worker policies.

This is also where some versions of left restrictionism fail. They notice that employers benefit from vulnerable migrant labor and conclude that migration should be restricted in the name of workers. But employers benefit most from a system in which migrant labor is present yet rightless. The most exploitative arrangement is not open movement with equal rights; it is selective admission, temporary status, criminalized irregularity, and constant fear. Guest worker systems tied to a single employer, “seasonal” labor programs that isolate workers, and asylum backlogs that push people into informal work all create conditions in which bosses, not migrants, gain leverage.

A genuinely democratic migration politics would therefore begin with the workplace. It would make labor rights attach to the person, not to citizenship. It would prohibit employer-tied visas except under strict conditions. It would fund labor inspectorates at levels commensurate with the scale of abuse. It would allow migrants to change jobs without risking removal. It would guarantee firewalls between labor enforcement and immigration enforcement so that reporting exploitation does not become self-incrimination. It would impose serious penalties on employers who use immigration status as a threat. And it would support unions that organize across status lines.

The second institutional front is local democracy. Ypi argues for expanding political representation beyond inherited citizenship. This is not utopian. Many democracies have, at different times, allowed noncitizen residents to vote in local elections or participate in municipal institutions. The principle is straightforward: if you live in a city, pay taxes, send your children to its schools, ride its buses, rent its apartments, and are policed by its authorities, you should have a voice in its government. Local voting rights for long-term residents would not abolish citizenship, but it would weaken the sharp distinction between those who are governed and those who govern.

The third front is international. Here Ypi’s essay is most ambitious and, inevitably, least programmatic. She calls for transnational labor alliances and a global order in which no one is forced to leave home. That aspiration is morally sound, but it needs intermediate steps. Rich democracies should treat labor standards, tax justice, climate finance, and debt restructuring as migration policy. They should not pretend that border enforcement can compensate for trade rules that destroy livelihoods, climate emissions that intensify displacement, or wars that create refugees. A world that produces displacement and then militarizes its borders is not managing migration; it is laundering responsibility.

Consider climate. The existing refugee system was built around persecution, not the slow violence of drought, flooding, heat, crop failure, and disaster. Yet climate stress increasingly interacts with conflict, debt, food insecurity, and weak governance. The people displaced by these forces often do not fit neat legal categories. If the future of mobility is shaped by climate, then rich countries cannot respond only with walls and temporary humanitarian parole. They need planned mobility pathways, regional protection agreements, and serious funding for adaptation so that movement is a choice rather than a last resort.

Still, one must concede a difficulty that Ypi may understate. Democratic politics remains territorially organized. Voters judge governments by the conditions they experience: rents, wages, school places, hospital waiting times, neighborhood change, public order. If progressives tell citizens that their anxieties are merely false consciousness, they will lose both trust and elections. The answer is not to mimic the right, but neither is it to deny that migration can interact with scarcity in visible ways. A sudden arrival of people in a town with no available housing and underfunded services can create real strain. The key is to identify the cause correctly. The problem is not the newcomers as such; it is the austerity state, the landlord, the employer, the absence of planning, and the political decision to let infrastructure decay.

This matters strategically. A class-based migration politics must be able to say, without embarrassment: yes, people need homes; yes, schools need funding; yes, wages must rise; yes, public services must be expanded; yes, employers must not be allowed to recruit abroad in order to avoid training workers at home; yes, democratic communities deserve planning and voice. But it must add: none of these goals is advanced by making migrants into scapegoats. Indeed, scapegoating migrants is how governing elites avoid responsibility for failing to build, tax, invest, regulate, and redistribute.

The most urgent task is to break the emotional chain that connects insecurity to exclusion. When people feel that life is becoming more precarious, they look for an explanation. The right offers one: strangers are taking what is yours. The center too often offers managerial evasions. The left must offer a truer story: what has been taken was taken by organized wealth, not by the family down the street waiting for an asylum decision.

Ypi’s invocation of Nathan the Wise is therefore more than literary ornament. It reminds us that universality is not a mood of benevolence; it is a discipline of political imagination. To ask whether the migrant is deserving is already to accept the wrong tribunal. The deeper question is why the world is organized so that some people must beg for recognition while others buy it. The border crisis is real. But it is not, at its root, a crisis of too many people moving. It is a crisis of unequal freedom: the freedom of capital without corresponding freedom for labor, the freedom of the wealthy without security for the poor, the freedom of states to produce disorder without responsibility for those displaced by it.

If progressives cannot name that crisis, they will spend the next decade choosing between cruelty and cowardice. Ypi asks them to choose courage instead.
