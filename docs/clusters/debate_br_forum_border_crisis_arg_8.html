"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the moral and class-based failures of current migration policies, a truly humane response requires moving beyond theory to build a functional, rights-respecting administrative system that replaces chaos with legal order."

The most persuasive part of Lea Ypi’s essay is her insistence that the migration debate has become morally inverted. We scrutinize the desperation of people at the border while ignoring the systems that made desperation predictable. We ask whether migrants are sufficiently grateful, skilled, law-abiding, secular, employable, or “integrated,” but rarely whether the receiving state is sufficiently just. We categorize the poor with a severity we never apply to the rich. A passport bought through investment is treated as strategy; a border crossed without authorization is treated as stain.

Yet a response to Ypi must also begin from a practical truth: borders are where moral ideals encounter administrative systems. If those systems fail, cruelty fills the vacuum. The alternative to today’s punitive border politics is not simply a better theory of solidarity. It is a functioning protection regime: fast, fair, rights-respecting, sufficiently funded, and honest about the different reasons people move.

That regime is currently broken in many democracies. Asylum systems are overwhelmed not only because too many people apply, but because governments have underinvested in adjudication, narrowed legal pathways, externalized responsibility, and allowed backlogs to become politically useful. A backlog is not just bureaucratic delay. It is a machine that produces misery and resentment at the same time. Applicants wait months or years in limbo, often unable to work legally or settle. Local communities see hotels, camps, emergency shelters, and improvised arrangements. Politicians then point to the disorder they helped create as evidence that asylum itself is unmanageable.

The first obligation is to restore the distinction between asylum and migration without turning that distinction into a moral hierarchy. Refugees and asylum seekers have specific legal claims grounded in persecution, war, torture, and serious harm. Economic migrants may have compelling human reasons to move, but they do not all have the same legal claim to protection. If states collapse these categories, the asylum system loses legitimacy; if they weaponize the distinction, they deny reality. Many people are fleeing mixed causes: gang violence and hunger, climate stress and political corruption, domestic abuse and state indifference, ethnic discrimination and unemployment. The legal categories are cleaner than human lives.

A humane system would acknowledge this complexity. It would preserve asylum for those who need protection, expand complementary pathways for people displaced by climate and generalized violence, create more labor routes for people whose primary motive is work, and allow family reunion without impossible hurdles. The goal should be to reduce the incentive to make every migration claim pass through the narrow and overloaded gate of asylum.

Ypi is especially right about smugglers. Her portrait of Ben the Lame resists the cartoon of the smuggler as pure predator. Some smugglers are brutal criminals who extort, abandon, assault, and kill. Others are local facilitators in a world where legal movement has been blocked. The smuggling market is created by enforcement without access. When people have strong reasons to move and no lawful means to do so, someone will sell the journey. The more militarized the route, the more dangerous and profitable the market becomes.

This is why “smash the gangs” is such an incomplete slogan. States can and should prosecute traffickers and violent smugglers. But enforcement alone often changes routes rather than reducing movement. People take longer sea crossings, more remote deserts, more expensive clandestine paths. Deaths rise. The business model adapts. If governments want to weaken smugglers, they must compete with them: issue humanitarian visas, build resettlement programs, process claims closer to regions of origin, and create labor mobility agreements that are safer than irregular routes.

At the same time, Ypi’s essay underplays one problem that cannot be dismissed as right-wing propaganda: state capacity. A border regime can be too harsh, but it can also be too chaotic. Chaos harms migrants first. People sleep outside. Children miss school. Work authorization is delayed. Claims are decided inconsistently. Unscrupulous lawyers and employers exploit uncertainty. Public anger grows. The far right does not need migration numbers to be historically unprecedented; it needs visible disorder. A progressive politics must therefore care about order—not authoritarian order, but legal order.

That begins with speed. Asylum decisions should be made in months, not years, with high-quality legal assistance, trained adjudicators, interpreters, trauma-informed procedures, and meaningful appeals. Speed without fairness is a deportation pipeline. Fairness without speed becomes indefinite limbo. Democracies need both.

Second, the right to work should be granted early. Preventing asylum seekers from working is economically irrational and socially corrosive. It forces people into dependency or informal labor, fuels resentment among citizens who see public money spent on enforced idleness, and deprives communities of contribution. If someone is likely to remain for any significant period, allowing lawful work is better for everyone.

Third, reception must be funded nationally and managed locally. Too often central governments make border decisions while municipalities absorb the consequences. Cities and towns need predictable financing for schools, clinics, housing, language instruction, and legal support. They also need transparent information. Secrecy and improvisation breed rumor. If residents believe arrivals are being hidden from them or imposed without consultation, extremists will supply the narrative.

Fourth, enforcement must focus on rights, not spectacle. Publicly humiliating deportees, separating families unnecessarily, or sending people to unsafe third countries is not strength. It is theater. But a system with no return mechanism for those who have received due process and have no right to remain will not command consent. Returns should be lawful, monitored, appealable, and as humane as possible. Voluntary return assistance should be expanded. Detention should be rare, time-limited, and subject to judicial oversight. Children should not be detained.

Fifth, legal status should not be designed to maximize dependency. Temporary protection that is renewed indefinitely but never becomes secure creates a population that belongs sociologically but not politically. Employer-tied visas create vulnerability. Student-to-work pathways that depend on opaque salary thresholds invite unfairness. Family migration rules that require high income punish the poor for loving across borders. If people are building their lives in a country, law should provide a route to permanence.

Ypi’s class analysis helps explain why these reforms are so often resisted. Precarious status is useful. It supplies workers who harvest crops, clean hotels, care for the elderly, deliver food, build homes, and staff hospitals while remaining politically weak. The same societies that denounce “illegal migration” rely on the labor of migrants kept at the edge of legality. This contradiction is not accidental. It is a labor market strategy.

Still, one should be cautious about describing all migration conflict as class conflict. Class is central, but not exhaustive. Religion, gender norms, race, language, historical memory, and geopolitical fear also shape how people respond to migration. Some anxieties are manipulated; some are ugly; some are rooted in genuine experiences of rapid change. A society that receives newcomers must negotiate common rules about schools, gender equality, secular law, public space, and civic obligation. These questions cannot be solved by redistribution alone.

But they are easier to solve in conditions of equality. Integration fails when migrants are warehoused, underpaid, segregated, and politically voiceless. It also fails when citizens are told that their communities may change rapidly but their concerns are illegitimate. A democratic integration policy must be reciprocal. Newcomers should receive language instruction, rights, protection from discrimination, and a path to citizenship. They should also be expected to obey the law, respect equal rights, and participate in civic life. Host societies should abandon fantasies of cultural purity, but they need not abandon public norms.

The deepest point in Ypi’s essay is that border politics tests whether liberal democracies believe their own principles. A state that proclaims human rights while outsourcing cruelty to camps, deserts, seas, or third countries is not defending legality; it is avoiding witnesses. A state that welcomes capital and cages workers has revealed the hierarchy beneath its rhetoric. A state that treats citizenship as a commodity for the rich and a fortress against the poor has confused democracy with property.

The answer, however, is not to imagine that administrative distinctions can disappear. The answer is to make them morally defensible. Who needs protection? Who can come to work? Who can reunite with family? Who has lived here long enough to belong? Who must leave, and under what safeguards? These questions require rules. The challenge is to write rules that do not depend on cruelty for their enforcement or hypocrisy for their justification.

A real border policy for liberal democracies would begin from three commitments: no one should be forced to move by injustice we can prevent; no one who moves should be stripped of basic rights; and no democratic community should be asked to manage migration through chaos. Ypi gives us the first two commitments with eloquence. The third is not a concession to nationalism. It is the condition under which the first two can survive politically.
