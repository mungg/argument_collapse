"The primary path to achieving full employment in the current political climate lies with the Federal Reserve's monetary policy rather than the legislative reallocation of federal spending."

Robert Pollin correctly identifies the tragedy of our current economic moment: the acceptance of 9.5 percent unemployment as a tragic but unalterable fact of nature. His call for a revival of full employment as the central goal of public policy is morally urgent. Yet, as we sit here at the dawn of 2011, his nearly exclusive focus on fiscal policy—specifically, a $330 billion reallocation of federal spending—ignores the political and institutional realities of Washington. The true path to full employment now lies not in the halls of Congress, but at the Federal Reserve.

Following the 2010 midterms, the Republican Party has taken sweeping control of the House of Representatives. The Tea Party wave was fueled by intense, if misguided, anger over the deficit and the stimulus. The political reality is that there will be no new major spending bills passed during the remainder of President Obama’s current term. Pollin’s proposal to cut the defense budget by 25 percent and invest it in clean energy is a political dead letter. If we wait for fiscal policy to save the unemployed, we will be waiting for a generation.

This brings us to the elephant in the room that Pollin barely mentions: monetary policy. Pollin rightly notes Keynes’s insight that mass unemployment is caused by a shortfall in aggregate demand. But it is the Federal Reserve, not Congress, that has the ultimate power to dictate nominal demand.

Since the crisis, the Fed has lowered interest rates to zero, but they have been far too timid in their unconventional policies. To counteract the collapse in spending, the Federal Reserve needs to commit to a massive, open-ended program of Quantitative Easing. More importantly, the Fed should abandon its implicit, singular focus on keeping inflation at 2 percent and adopt Nominal GDP (NGDP) level targeting. By committing to return the economy to its pre-crisis trend line of nominal growth, the Fed would instantly change the expectations of investors and employers.

Pollin spends a great deal of time worrying about inflation, citing the Swedish model of wage restraint. But right now, inflation is the least of our problems; in fact, the threat of deflation is far more pressing. The Fed has effectively allowed the economy to run cold, terrified of the very "wage-price spirals" that Pollin discusses. If Chairman Bernanke were to publicly state that the Fed will tolerate temporarily higher inflation to ensure a rapid return to full employment, "animal spirits" would return to the private sector. Businesses would hire now to get ahead of future demand.

We do not need to radically restructure the American economy or reinvent the Swedish model to put people back to work. We need the central bank to do its job. Monetary dominance is the reality of modern macroeconomics, and until the Fed treats 9.5 percent unemployment as an existential emergency, no amount of proposed green-energy spending will close the gap.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The shift in congressional power following the 2010 midterms makes large-scale fiscal stimulus or budget reallocations politically impossible.
  2. 02The Federal Reserve has the ultimate power to dictate nominal demand and should adopt Nominal GDP (NGDP) level targeting to restore the economy to its pre-crisis trend.
  3. 03Concerns about inflation are misplaced in the current economic context, as the threat of deflation is more pressing and a commitment to higher inflation would stimulate private sector investment.
  4. 04The Federal Reserve must engage in massive, open-ended Quantitative Easing to counteract the collapse in spending and signal a commitment to full employment.
