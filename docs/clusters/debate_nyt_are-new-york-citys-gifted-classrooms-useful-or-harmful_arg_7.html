"New York City’s gifted classrooms are more harmful than useful because they institutionalize inequality by sorting children into a rigid hierarchy based on flawed early childhood assessments."

New York City’s gifted classrooms are more harmful than useful because they ask the public school system to ratify inequality at the very beginning of childhood. The label sounds benign, even celebratory. But in practice it often means that one group of 5-year-olds is declared academically special while another group, down the hall, is implicitly told it is ordinary.

The admissions process is the giveaway. A child’s access can depend on a high score on a test taken before kindergarten, after months of parental anxiety and, for many affluent families, paid preparation. Four-year-olds differ in maturity, exposure to books, comfort with adults, English-language experience and familiarity with test formats. Those differences matter greatly on test day, but they are not the same as innate intellectual promise. By building separate classrooms around these results, the city turns unequal early childhood experiences into an official school hierarchy.

The racial and economic patterns are impossible to ignore. In a school system made up largely of black and Latino children, the most coveted gifted programs have been disproportionately white and Asian and disproportionately middle class. That does not mean the children in those rooms are undeserving. It means the selection system is reflecting access to information, enrichment and preparation outside school. Public education should counterbalance those advantages, not magnify them.

The harm is not only statistical. Separate gifted classrooms change the culture of a school. Parents fight to get their children into the “good” class. Teachers and principals face pressure to treat one track as the place where serious academics happen. General education classrooms can become stigmatized, even when they contain bright, curious and capable children. Once that social meaning attaches to a classroom, no amount of polite language about “different learners” can erase it.

Of course children learn at different paces. Some need harder books, advanced math, independent projects or subject acceleration. But those needs do not require a full-time segregated track beginning in kindergarten. Schools can use flexible groups by subject, compact the curriculum for children who have mastered it, provide pull-out seminars, and train teachers to use richer tasks for everyone. They can identify strengths over time rather than pretending a preschool test has settled the matter.

Fariña’s phrase “gifted practices for all students” points in the right direction. The city should not reserve inquiry, debate, science experiments, arts integration and challenging texts for children who won a seat in a selective program. If gifted education means excellent teaching, then every child deserves it. If it means sorting small children into winners and losers, New York should leave it behind.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The admissions process relies on tests taken at an age where results reflect external advantages like parental preparation and socioeconomic background rather than innate intellectual promise.
  2. 02The programs create racial and economic segregation within a diverse school system by disproportionately favoring white, Asian, and middle-class students.
  3. 03Separate gifted tracks damage school culture by stigmatizing general education classrooms and creating a perception that serious academics only occur in the selective track.
  4. 04Academic needs such as subject acceleration and enrichment can be met through flexible grouping and differentiated instruction without the need for full-time segregated classrooms.
  5. 05High-quality educational practices like inquiry-based learning and challenging texts should be provided to all students rather than being reserved for a selected few.
