"The Occupy movement is not merely a call to align existing institutions with fixed ideals, but a vital laboratory for redefining democratic principles and rebuilding democratic culture from the ground up."

The Boston Review’s opening gambit—that the nation is seized by a tension between its ideals and its practices, and that Occupy Wall Street is a clarion call for alignment—is an elegant one. But it is also a profoundly undemocratic one. It presumes that “our country’s principles” are a fixed constellation, known in advance and simply awaiting fuller implementation. In fact, principles are contested ground. The very meaning of equal opportunity, political voice, and meaningful participation is what we are fighting about, and the Occupy movement’s greatest contribution is its insistence on expanding that fight beyond the sterile confines of Washington policy debates. The movement is not a cry of pain; it is a laboratory for a new kind of democratic practice.

The standard critique of Occupy—that it lacks a specific list of demands—profoundly misunderstands both the nature of the crisis and the ambition of the response. Our formal political institutions are not merely misaligned with our ideals; they have been hollowed out. Congress’s approval rating hovers near single digits. Policy is written by lobbyists. The public’s trust in banks, media, and even the courts has eroded. In this context, presenting a ten-point plan for legislative action would be an act of self-delusion, as if the broken machinery could be fixed by a fresh set of instructions. What is needed first is a renewal of democratic culture itself, a reconstruction of the social fabric that allows citizens to recognize one another as agents of common destiny. This is precisely what Occupy has done: in parks and squares, tens of thousands of people who had never participated in any civic activity beyond voting—if that—have discovered the skills of public deliberation, of listening, of collective problem-solving.

This is not a retreat from politics; it is a pre-political condition for any meaningful reform. The historian Michael Denning, writing about the Popular Front era, called such moments “the cultural front.” Before the New Deal’s legislative victories, there was a profound cultural shift—in music, literature, labor halls, and street-corner speeches—that made the idea of social security, collective bargaining, and financial regulation seem not just possible but necessary. Occupy has already achieved a comparable discursive shift. “We are the 99 percent” is now part of the national lexicon. The conversation about inequality has moved from obscure academic journals to dinner tables and editorial boards. The movement has changed the agenda, not by lobbying, but by creating a compelling moral narrative.

The crackdowns on encampments that began in earnest this month only underscore the movement’s importance. When a city’s response to a peaceful assembly of citizens using their public space to discuss public grievances is to send in riot police in the dead of night, the gap between our professed commitment to free speech and the reality of power is laid bare. It is a teachable moment. The challenge now is to generalize this democratic energy. Not every participant will join a political party or a union, but the habits of self-organization, the networks of trust, and the shared analysis of a rigged system will not simply evaporate. They migrate to neighborhoods, workplaces, and online forums. The principles that Occupy is trying to realize—radical inclusion, horizontal deliberation, direct action—are themselves the substance of a more authentic democracy. The question is no longer just whether our institutions live up to our principles; the protesters are asking a deeper question: whose principles, and what kind of democracy, are we even talking about?
