Diversified DeepSeek

"The left should move beyond the framework of populism toward a politics of abolition, because populism is historically and structurally rooted in whiteness and cannot adequately address the specificities of anti-Blackness and racial capitalism."

Reading Adom Getachew’s lucid and generous invitation to reclaim populism, I am struck by a persistent tension that runs through the entire discourse: the assumption that a “multiracial working class alignment” can be built on a populist foundation at all. The lead essay acknowledges the “persistence of white supremacy” and the horrific racial disparities of the COVID-19 pandemic. It gestures toward the need to center race in the populist project. But these acknowledgements remain at the level of a problem to be overcome, rather than a constitutive challenge that may render the very category of populism—even a “left-led, multiracial” one—untenable. The deeper history of populism in the United States, and indeed across the Americas, is not that it has occasionally been contaminated by racism. It is that populism as a political form has, from its inception, been structured by whiteness. Reclaiming populism for a multiracial future may be like reclaiming the master’s tools to dismantle the master’s house. The left’s task is not to rehabilitate populism but to build a politics that starts from the fact of anti-Blackness and settler colonialism, and that seeks not merely a rainbow coalition of the oppressed but a transformation of the very terms of political community.

Let me be blunt: the history of populism in the United States is a history of the white “people.” The People’s Party of the 1890s, often romanticized as the righteous predecessor of today’s left, was an overwhelmingly white movement rooted in agrarian grievances. While some Populist leaders in the South attempted to forge interracial alliances of poor farmers—a genuinely courageous effort—the movement as a whole was ultimately captured by the Democratic Party’s white supremacist faction, and many former Populists became the architects of Jim Crow. The language of “the people versus the interests” turned out to be perfectly compatible with the violent exclusion of Black people from the polity. This was not an accident. The “producer” identity that Populists celebrated was already racialized: the small farmer who worked the land was imagined as white, while Black agricultural workers—largely sharecroppers and tenant farmers—were cast as a dependent class, not fully part of the producing people. The Populist critique of “monopoly” and “speculation” occasionally took on anti-Semitic tones, targeting Jewish financiers as alien parasites. In other words, the rhetorical structure of populism—the appeal to a virtuous, hard-working people against a corrupt, rootless elite—has always been available for racial and ethnic scapegoating.

The progressive populism that Getachew and the Working Families Party champion is, of course, consciously anti-racist. The CTU, with its multiracial leadership and its demands that directly benefit Black and brown students, is a model of what is possible. But even here, I want to sound a note of caution. The “multiracial working class alignment” can function as a euphemism that avoids naming the specific experience of Black people in America. The working class is multiracial, yes, but it is not evenly racialized. Black workers face a distinct set of conditions: higher unemployment, lower wages, disproportionate incarceration, police violence, predatory housing policies, and a legacy of dispossession that no amount of class solidarity can simply erase. When left populists speak of “working people,” they often slide too quickly over these differences, imagining that a rising tide of social democracy will lift all boats. But as the historian Cedric Johnson has argued, the New Deal itself—often treated as the high-water mark of progressive populism—excluded the vast majority of Black workers from its most important programs, creating a welfare state that was, in Ira Katznelson’s phrase, “white by design.” Agricultural and domestic workers, overwhelmingly Black, were deliberately left out of Social Security and labor protections. The populist coalition that made the New Deal possible was built on a compromise with Southern segregationists, and it delivered material benefits to white workers that actually widened the racial wealth gap in many respects.

Today’s left populists are acutely aware of this history and would never countenance such explicit exclusions. But the structural problem persists: the racial hierarchy of American capitalism is not an overlay on an otherwise neutral class system; it is constitutive of that system. The contemporary “multiracial” working class is produced through a labor market that remains segmented by race, with Black workers concentrated in the lowest-paid, most precarious sectors—home health care, food service, warehousing—while white workers are disproportionately represented in higher-paid, unionized sectors like construction and manufacturing. The COVID-19 crisis has made this segmentation lethally visible: in Chicago and Milwaukee, as Getachew notes, Black residents account for the vast majority of deaths, not because of some genetic predisposition but because of pre-existing conditions caused by decades of environmental racism, housing segregation, and unequal access to health care, combined with overrepresentation in “essential” work that cannot be done from home. Any populism that does not foreground this specific racialized death-dealing as the central issue is engaging in a dangerous abstraction. The call for Medicare for All, for instance, is vital, but it will not, by itself, cure a Black community that has been systematically poisoned by lead pipes, confined to food deserts, and denied adequate hospital facilities. A populism that speaks of “universal” programs without addressing the particularity of anti-Black racism will reproduce racial inequality even as it reduces overall poverty.

This is why I believe the left must move beyond populism toward a politics of abolition. Abolition is not just about dismantling prisons and police, though that is a crucial part of it. Abolition is a comprehensive political orientation that seeks to dismantle the structures of racial capitalism in their entirety—structures that are, as Ruth Wilson Gilmore has argued, fundamentally organized around the production and management of premature death among Black and brown populations. The populist framework, with its focus on the “people” as the subject of politics, tends to obscure the fact that in a settler-colonial, slaveholding nation, the people were constituted precisely through the exclusion and domination of Black and Indigenous peoples. The very notion of a democratic people is haunted by this original sin. To reclaim populism without a radical re-founding that reckons with this history is to paper over a chasm that will eventually swallow any progressive coalition.

What would an abolitionist politics look like in the context of the pandemic? It would start not with the universal citizen but with the most vulnerable: the incarcerated, the undocumented, the unhoused, the immunocompromised. It would demand the immediate release of people from prisons, jails, and detention centers, where COVID-19 spreads like wildfire. It would demand a moratorium on evictions and foreclosures, and the cancelation of all rents, mortgages, and debts for those who cannot pay. It would demand reparations for Black communities devastated by the virus—not just a check but a full-scale redistribution of land, housing, and resources controlled by the community itself. And it would demand a transformation of the care economy, shifting control from for-profit nursing homes and hospitals to worker- and community-owned health cooperatives rooted in historically redlined neighborhoods. These are not demands that can be easily fit into a populist narrative about “the people.” They are particular, targeted, and unapologetically racial. They name the enemy not as “the elite” in the abstract but as the carceral state, the medical-industrial complex, and the ongoing system of racial extraction.

Getachew might respond that the Working Families Party’s multiracial alignment is precisely about building the coalitions necessary to make such demands politically viable. And she would be right that no single racial group can achieve these transformations alone. But the history of multiracial coalitions in America suggests that they are most durable and effective not when they are built around an abstract class identity but when each component group is allowed to articulate its specific grievances and when the coalition is bound by a shared commitment to dismantling racial hierarchy. This requires a political language that is far more attuned to difference than populism typically allows. It requires a practice of coalition that is not about merging into a single “people” but about working across irreducible differences, with the understanding that justice for Black people will sometimes require measures—reparations, for example—that are not universal, and that white members of the coalition must support those measures not because they are part of “the working class” but because they are committed to racial justice as a moral and political principle.

The international dimension is equally important. Populism is nation-bound; it imagines a people within a territory. But Blackness is a diasporic condition. The premature deaths of Black people in Chicago are linked to the deaths of Black people in Brazil, the highest toll of the pandemic in any democracy. Both are legacies of the transatlantic slave trade, plantation capitalism, and ongoing imperialism. An internationalist, abolitionist politics would link the struggles against police violence in Minneapolis to the struggles against extractivism in Latin America and the fight for reparations in the Caribbean. It would see the Trump administration’s sanctions on more than twenty-five countries as a form of collective punishment that amounts to slow-motion genocide, most acutely affecting Black and brown populations abroad. And it would demand not just an end to those sanctions but a positive program of reparative internationalism: the transfer of wealth, technology, and medical capacity from the Global North to the Global South, overseen by democratic institutions accountable to the people whose lives are at stake.

This is, I admit, a more radical vision than the one Getachew lays out. But I do not think the left has the luxury of moderation. The pandemic has exposed the rot at the core of American democracy, a rot that is fundamentally racial. The populist frame, in its search for a unifying “we,” risks pushing that rot to the margins again, hoping that a universal program will take care of the problem without ever having to name it directly. But we cannot heal what we will not name. The name is white supremacy. Until the left builds a politics that starts from that name and fights it as the central axis of oppression—not a secondary contradiction alongside class—we will continue to lose, no matter how often we reclaim the word “populism.”

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical foundation of American populism is inextricably linked to white supremacy and the exclusion of Black people from the category of 'the people.'
  2. 02Universalist populist programs often fail to address the unique material conditions of Black workers and risk reproducing existing racial hierarchies.
  3. 03Abolitionist politics provides a more effective framework than populism by centering the most vulnerable populations and targeting the specific structures of the carceral state and racial extraction.
  4. 04Effective multiracial coalitions must be built on a commitment to dismantling racial hierarchy and addressing specific grievances rather than merging into an abstract, unified class identity.
  5. 05Populism is limited by its nation-bound focus, whereas an abolitionist approach better addresses the international and diasporic nature of Black struggle and global racial capitalism.
