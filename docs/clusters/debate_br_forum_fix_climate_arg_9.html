"The proposed shift toward experimentalist governance and sectoral committees is a dangerously anti-democratic retreat into technocracy that risks regulatory capture by powerful industries."

David Victor and Charles Sabel have written a manifesto for a climate politics that dares not speak its name. In their sweeping critique of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the Paris Agreement, they argue that the global, consensus-driven model of climate diplomacy has been a charade, producing binding commitments that bind no one. Their alternative, “experimentalist governance,” promises to break the logjam by shifting the locus of action from global negotiating rooms to sectoral working groups, where firms and front-line regulators can learn their way to a post-carbon economy under the disciplining threat of penalty defaults. The argument is seductive because it flatters our technocratic sensibilities. But it rests on a profoundly anti-democratic impulse and a dangerously naive understanding of what makes rules legitimate. If we abandon the global democratic forum of the UNFCCC, even in its paralyzed state, for a world of insular, industry-facing committees, we will have traded a flawed but inclusive politics for a system of unaccountable governance. The cure may be worse than the disease.

The central gap in Victor and Sabel's analysis is a conceptual hole where democratic legitimacy should be. They praise the committees of the Montreal Protocol, which brought together technical experts, industry representatives, and government regulators to assess substitutes for ozone-depleting substances. They invoke John Dewey to argue that this kind of collaboration between “shoemakers and shod” is a form of democratic accountability. But this is a strikingly thin vision of democracy. The sectoral committees under Montreal did not set the ultimate goals; they managed a transition within a political mandate that had already been established by governments through a treaty. And the stakeholders in the room were a narrow slice of the public: scientists with a research interest in atmospheric chemistry, engineers from a handful of chemical companies, and bureaucrats from environmental agencies. Civil society, in any meaningful sense, was not present. Indigenous communities, workers who would lose jobs in the transition, public health advocates concerned with the toxicity of replacement chemicals, and the vast majority of the world’s citizens who would live with the consequences of the decisions made in those rooms were nowhere to be seen. Victor and Sabel call this a glimpse of democratic accountability. I call it the normalization of an expert cartel.

The problem becomes acute when we scale the model to climate change. Decarbonising the global economy is not a chemist’s puzzle of finding the right refrigerant. It is a project of profound social reconstruction. It will determine where people live, how they work, what they eat, and how they move. The choices we make—whether to pursue an energy system built around massive nuclear power plants or distributed solar microgrids; whether to rely on carbon capture and storage or impose strict limits on consumption; whether to use biofuels that compete with food crops or mandate deep efficiency gains—are not technical calibrations. They are political choices that will create winners and losers along lines of class, geography, and race. To delegate these choices to sectoral bodies organized around the industries being regulated is to invite regulatory capture. The oil majors who have funded climate denial will not vanish into the thin air of post-Paris experimentalism. They will send their most talented engineers and most charming lobbyists to sit on the committees that set emission standards for oil and gas, just as they do today in the International Maritime Organization and the International Civil Aviation Organization, where sectoral governance has been a byword for glacial progress and the protection of incumbent interests. Experimentalist governance, for all its talk of destabilizing the status quo, can easily become a mechanism by which powerful industries destabilize the regulators.

The Paris Agreement, for all its manifest weaknesses, has one supreme virtue that Victor and Sabel dismiss too readily: it is the creature of a universal, intergovernmental process. Its decision-making authority flows, however indirectly, from the governments that represent the world’s people. The national pledges, the “nationally determined contributions” (NDCs) that Victor and Sabel see as a recipe for churning and inaction, are in fact the first fragile threads of democratic accountability in the global climate regime. Every country that submits an NDC must, in principle, defend that plan to its own domestic constituency. The global stocktake, the Paris mechanism for periodic review, is designed to create moments of political pressure when governments must stand before the international community and explain why their domestic efforts are falling short. This process is messy, slow, and often disappointing. But it is the kind of politics we should want to build upon, not an embarrassing detour from the real work of technocratic problem-solving. To dismiss the UNFCCC as hopelessly gridlocked and retreat to the committee rooms of Maersk and the IMO is to abandon the project of building a global demos capable of collective self-government on the most consequential issue of our time.

Victor and Sabel argue that Paris cannot guide or participate in sectoral experimentation. This is true in a narrow sense. The UNFCCC secretariat does not have the expertise or the mandate to tell steelmakers which low-carbon technologies to test. But the political authority of Paris is exactly what makes sectoral coordination politically possible. The shipping industry, as the authors note, is moving towards new standards within the IMO. But it is doing so under the long shadow of Paris, knowing that if it does not act, governments—prodded by the Paris framework—may eventually impose more stringent and less industry-friendly regulations unilaterally. The Paris Agreement creates the political context, the generalized penalty default, that makes sectoral cooperation rational for first-movers. Strip away that context, and the incentive for Maersk to coordinate a technology demonstration program evaporates. Why would a first-mover invite the extra costs of decarbonization if there is no credible political pathway to ensuring its competitors will be forced to follow? The experimentalist institutions Victor and Sabel celebrate are, in truth, parasites on the legitimacy of the intergovernmental process they deride. The branches will not survive if the trunk is cut down.

What then is to be done? The path forward is not to jettison Paris but to democratize it further and link it more tightly to the domestic politics of member states. The European Green Deal, launched in late 2019, offers a more instructive model than the Montreal Protocol. It sets ambitious, binding targets at the level of the continent, but recognizes that the pathway to meeting those targets will vary dramatically by sector and by member state. It creates a framework of supranational law that can impose penalties for non-compliance, while leaving substantial room for national governments, regions, and industries to experiment with their own plans for a just transition. And crucially, it was the product of democratic electoral politics, driven into being by the surge of Green parties in the European Parliament and the pressure of youth-led climate strikes in the streets. It is a project of political will, forged in democratic contestation, deploying the coercive power of the state and the regulatory capacity of the bureaucracy in tandem. It is not a retreat into the quiet rooms of apolitical sectoral governance.

The lesson of the last three decades is not that we have been too democratic in our approach to climate change and must now turn the work over to the experts and the industries. It is that we have not been democratic enough. The power of the fossil fuel lobby has been exercised not just in the technical committees but in the ballot box, in the campaign finance system, in the editorial pages of major newspapers. Defeating that power requires a counter-mobilization of citizens on a scale that makes the experimentalist project seem like a parochial hobby. We do not need a new institutional design to bypass the public. We need a politics that trusts the public to understand the stakes and act accordingly. If we cannot build that, no committee structure, however elegantly designed, will save us.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Experimentalist governance lacks democratic legitimacy because it delegates profound social and political choices to narrow circles of technical experts and industry representatives while excluding civil society.
  2. 02Relying on industry-led sectoral bodies invites regulatory capture, allowing powerful incumbents like the oil and gas industry to stall progress and protect their own interests.
  3. 03The Paris Agreement’s universal, intergovernmental process provides a necessary foundation of global democratic accountability that sectoral committees cannot replicate.
  4. 04Sectoral cooperation is actually dependent on the political pressure and legitimacy created by the Paris Agreement, rather than being a viable alternative to it.
  5. 05The solution to climate change requires more robust democratic mobilization and political will, as seen in the European Green Deal, rather than a retreat into apolitical, expert-led governance.
