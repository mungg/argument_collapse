"The crisis in academic medicine is fundamentally a cultural failure of institutional self-understanding rather than a mere lack of regulatory oversight."

What the Academy Owes Itself

I want to set aside the policy debate for a moment and focus on what I take to be Marcia Angell's deepest point: that academic medicine has, over thirty years, lost a clear sense of what it is for. The conflicts of interest she describes are symptoms. The underlying disease is the erosion of an institutional self-understanding.

A university medical school is a strange institution. It teaches, it heals, it discovers, and it does so under a charter that gives it remarkable privileges: tax exemption, public funding, monopoly credentialing power, a presumption of public trust. These privileges are not granted because medical schools are particularly nice places. They are granted because the work they do — generating reliable knowledge, training competent practitioners, caring for the sick without regard to ability to pay — is understood to be of such public importance that society subsidizes it and protects it from the ordinary pressures of commerce.

The bargain implicit in this arrangement is that medical schools will conduct themselves in ways the market cannot reliably produce. They will fund research the market would not fund, ask questions a profit-seeker would not ask, teach students for the long-term benefit of the profession rather than the short-term needs of any employer, and care for patients whose care does not pay. In exchange, they receive the privileges of academic status.

What Angell documents is the unraveling of this bargain. When a department chair earns half a million dollars a year as a consultant; when a medical school sets up an office whose mission is to meet "industry's needs"; when faculty serve as listed authors on papers ghostwritten by drug companies; when the dean sits on a corporate board — these are not, primarily, problems of bias in any particular study. They are violations of the implicit contract under which the institution exists. The institution has begun to conduct itself as a business while continuing to claim the privileges of a non-business.

This is a problem of self-understanding before it is a problem of regulation. No external reform regime will work if the people inside the institution do not believe that they have a distinctive vocation. Conflict-of-interest rules can be drafted, but they will be evaded if those subject to them regard them as nuisances rather than as expressions of what their profession owes. The painful truth is that much of contemporary academic medicine no longer believes that it owes anything beyond what an honest business owes — produce good products, follow the rules, maximize your returns.

Angell knows this. Her closing line — that researchers are not entitled to whatever they want just because they are very smart — is aimed at exactly this attitude. But she frames the necessary reforms primarily in regulatory terms (rules about payments, rules about patents, rules about CME), when the deeper reform she is calling for is cultural. The question is whether the medical profession can recover the sense of itself as a profession — a body with internal standards, public obligations, and a willingness to forgo income that other comparable groups take for granted.

The historical precedents for this kind of cultural recovery are mixed. Law, accounting, finance — each profession has gone through periods of moral self-examination, and the results have been uneven. The accounting profession after Enron and Sarbanes-Oxley is arguably more rigorous than before, but no one would describe it as having recovered a pre-modern sense of vocation. Medicine's challenge is harder because the financial pressures are larger and the entanglements deeper.

What might recovery look like? I would offer three suggestions, modest in scope but, I hope, oriented toward the right target.

First, medical schools should re-articulate, in their own institutional documents, what they are for. This sounds like the kind of empty exercise that produces glossy strategic plans no one reads. But there is a real question whether faculties of medicine could currently write down, in language they would all sign, what distinguishes them from a research division of Pfizer. Forcing the question would clarify thinking.

Second, the profession should treat conflict-of-interest enforcement as a matter of professional honor rather than regulatory compliance. The relevant analogy is the bar association rather than the SEC. Egregious cases should result not just in fines or institutional embarrassment but in professional sanction — exclusion from journal editorial boards, ineligibility for academic honors, loss of standing. The profession has these tools. It does not use them.

Third, senior faculty should accept that part of their role is to model behavior they wish to see. The current generation of medical-school leaders includes many who built their careers in an era of expanding industry relationships and personally benefited from it. They cannot credibly preach to junior faculty about restraint while continuing the practices they accumulated. There is no painless way to handle this, but acknowledgment is a start.

I have spent this essay not arguing about policy because the policy arguments, important as they are, presuppose an answer to a prior question: what kind of institution does academic medicine want to be? Angell's essay is, at its best, an attempt to make her colleagues face that question. They have spent twenty years not facing it. The hour is later than they realize. The public's patience with corrupted expertise is not unlimited, and once it runs out, the privileges of academic medicine — the trust, the funding, the autonomy — will be much harder to recover than they have been to lose.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Academic medical centers have violated their implicit social contract by adopting the behaviors of for-profit businesses while continuing to claim the public privileges of non-profit institutions.
  2. 02External conflict-of-interest regulations will remain ineffective as long as medical professionals view them as nuisances rather than as essential expressions of their distinctive vocation.
  3. 03Medical schools must explicitly re-articulate their unique mission to distinguish their purpose from that of corporate research divisions.
  4. 04The medical profession should enforce ethical standards through professional honor and social sanctions, such as loss of standing, rather than treating them as simple compliance issues.
  5. 05Senior faculty and institutional leaders must model professional restraint by personally forgoing the lucrative industry entanglements they benefited from in the past.
