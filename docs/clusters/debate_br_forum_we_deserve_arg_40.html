Diversified MiniMax

"While Evgeny Morozov correctly identifies the need for non-instrumental AI, his critique fails to recognize that technology cannot substitute for the social and pedagogical infrastructure required to make such alternatives meaningful."

Morozov's discussion of Seymour Papert and the "one laptop per child" initiative deserves deeper scrutiny than he provides. Papert's work on constructionism—the theory that people learn best by constructing meaningful artifacts—is genuinely important and has been implemented in ways that do approximate eolithic principles. The problem was not with Papert's ideas but with the transformation of those ideas into a market product that stripped away their social and pedagogical context.

Constructionist education in its ideal form is highly contextual and interactive. Children learn programming not by following standardized curricula but by creating projects that matter to them, with peers and mentors providing guidance and feedback. This is not efficient by corporate metrics, but it is highly effective by learning metrics. The failure of one laptop per child was not that it provided laptops to children but that it provided a standardized technological solution to what is fundamentally a social and pedagogical problem. Technology alone cannot create the conditions for learning; it must be embedded in relationships and practices that give it meaning.

This points to a broader problem with Morozov's approach: the tendency to treat technology as if it could substitute for political and social transformation. He argues that we need a radical political project to direct technology toward human flourishing, but he also sometimes suggests that the right technology design could enable such flourishing even without the political transformation. These claims pull in opposite directions. If technology design alone were sufficient, the many attempts to develop humane computing technologies would have succeeded by now. If political transformation is necessary, then technology is at best a tool for movements that must do the harder work of changing power relations.

The educational context Morozov discusses is instructive. Teachers using AI tools face pressures from standardized testing regimes, accountability mandates, and resource constraints that make genuinely eolithic learning difficult to implement. The promise of AI to personalize learning sounds appealing until we recognize that personalization often means optimizing for measurable outcomes rather than enabling genuine exploration. A technology that tailors content to student gaps as identified by algorithmic assessment is still operating within an instrumental framework; it has just made that framework more efficient.

What would genuinely eolithic AI in education look like? Perhaps it would involve AI systems that help students and teachers discover possibilities they had not anticipated, rather than filling gaps identified by diagnostic algorithms. Perhaps it would involve AI that preserves ambiguity rather than resolving it, helping students tolerate uncertainty rather than rushing to conclusions. Perhaps it would involve AI that connects learning to community contexts, enabling students to pursue questions that matter to them and their communities rather than predetermined curriculum standards.

All of this is possible in principle, but it requires infrastructure and support that the current education system does not provide. Morozov is right that we need to imagine alternatives, but he underestimates how much infrastructure is necessary to make those alternatives real. The eolithic flâneur he describes did not just happen upon the right stone; he existed in a social context that valued craftsmanship, provided leisure for exploration, and transmitted traditions of making that gave raw materials meaning. We cannot recreate that context with an app.

The practical implication is that those of us who care about AI and education must do more than design better tools. We must build the social and institutional conditions that would make those tools meaningful. This means engaging in the unglamorous work of education reform: supporting teachers, reducing class sizes, providing resources, and resisting the accountability regimes that reduce education to measurement. Morozov's essay is a valuable contribution to the imagination of alternatives, but imagination is only the first step.

  2. 02Morozov’s argument contains a contradiction by suggesting that better technology design can enable human flourishing while simultaneously claiming that a radical political transformation is necessary.
