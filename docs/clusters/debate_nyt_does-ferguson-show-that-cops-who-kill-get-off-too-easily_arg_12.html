"The issue with police accountability is not the legal standard of 'objective reasonableness' itself, but rather the biased and inconsistent application of that standard."

There are two distinct questions tangled together in this debate, and conflating them produces confusion. The first is whether the legal standards governing police use of force are appropriate. The second is whether those standards are being applied fairly. Reasonable people can answer yes to one and no to the other.

On the legal standard, I think the current rule is roughly right. Under Graham v. Connor, an officer's use of force is judged by "objective reasonableness" from the perspective of a reasonable officer at the scene. This rule recognizes that policing involves rapid decisions in dangerous environments. We don't want a standard so strict that officers hesitate to defend themselves; that costs officer lives. We don't want a standard so loose that officers can shoot anyone they claim to fear; that costs civilian lives. "Objective reasonableness" attempts to balance these, and I think it does so adequately.

On application, however, I have serious concerns. The same standard, applied to the same facts, often produces different outcomes depending on who the officer is, who the victim is, who the prosecutor is, and what jury or grand jury sits in judgment. This is not a defect of the standard; it's a defect of implementation. And the disparate outcomes in cases involving black victims suggest that implementation is systematically biased.

The remedy, then, is not to change the substantive law of self-defense but to change the procedures by which it is applied. Three reforms strike me as essential. First, independent investigation and prosecution of police shootings by entities not embedded in the same jurisdiction as the officer. Second, mandatory data collection. The fact that we don't actually know how many people police kill each year—the FBI's voluntary figures are widely acknowledged to be incomplete—is itself scandalous. Third, body cameras, which constrain both bad-faith officers and bad-faith claimants.

These are modest, technocratic reforms. They will not satisfy those who believe the entire system is rotten, nor those who believe nothing needs to change. But they would substantially improve a process that currently strains public confidence to the breaking point.

Ferguson did not show that the law is wrong. It showed that the public has lost faith in how the law is administered. That loss of faith is corrosive—to policing, to communities, to the rule of law itself. Restoring it requires acknowledging that something is broken, identifying what specifically is broken, and fixing those specific things, without burning down the whole edifice.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current legal standard for use of force correctly balances officer safety with civilian protection by accounting for the rapid nature of police decision-making.
  2. 02Systemic bias in the justice system leads to disparate outcomes for victims of different races even when the same legal standards are applied.
  3. 03Public confidence in the law is being eroded by the perceived unfairness of how police shootings are investigated and prosecuted.
  4. 04Improving accountability requires procedural reforms such as independent prosecutions, mandatory data collection, and the use of body cameras rather than changing substantive self-defense laws.
