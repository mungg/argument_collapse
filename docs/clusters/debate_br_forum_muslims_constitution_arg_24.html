"While the lead essay correctly identifies systemic injustices, its focus on state oppression inadvertently reduces American Muslims to passive victims and fails to account for the spiritual, diverse, and affirmative dimensions of Muslim life."

I read “Islam on Trial” with the deep discomfort that comes from seeing one’s own community described almost entirely as an object—of suspicion, of surveillance, of state violence. Akbar and Theoharis have catalogued the gross injustices wrought by the post-9/11 security state, and their outrage is warranted. But in their relentless focus on the apparatus of oppression, they have inadvertently produced a portrait of American Muslims as a people defined by their subjugation, their contingency, their need to be “saved” by non-Muslim allies. I want to push back, not from the right, but from within: as a Muslim who has lived, worked, and prayed in this country my entire life. Our story is not just one of victimhood. And a politics built solely on grievance risks disempowering the very people it claims to defend.

The lead essay’s critique of the “good Muslim” trope is sharp and necessary. The celebration of Khizr Khan as a constitutional patriot was indeed a double-edged sword. But the essay’s own framework reproduces a different version of the same problem. By enumerating the six ways we have “gone wrong,” it implicitly constructs the “proper” Muslim subject once again—this time as one who resists the state, who connects her struggle to Black Lives Matter, who adopts a rigorously anti-imperialist posture. What of the Muslim who is not an activist, who feels genuinely proud to serve in the U.S. military, or who simply wants to run a halal grocery store and send her kids to a good school without being forever defined by the war on terror? Are those Muslims still trapped in false consciousness? The essay’s critical vocabulary leaves little room for the messy, ordinary, often apolitical lives that most people lead.

I am not arguing that the state’s excesses are acceptable. The FBI’s surveillance of mosques is reprehensible. Material support prosecutions have ensnared people who were guilty of nothing more than sending money to an orphanage in a conflict zone. The No Fly List is a bureaucratic monstrosity. But the essay’s insistence that we are “deeply afflicted with anti-Muslim prejudice” and that the whole architecture of counterterrorism is an expression of Islamophobia risks making the fight for justice a zero-sum war against the United States itself. That stance alienates many Muslims, myself included, who love this country not in a romanticized, flag-waving sense, but in the concrete way that you love the only home you have ever known. We can condemn drone strikes without concluding that the government’s entire counterterrorism mission is a racist enterprise. We can demand reform of the CVE program without denying that some form of community engagement to prevent radicalization might be valuable—especially when it is Muslim leaders, not FBI agents, designing the curriculum.

The most troubling elision in the lead essay is its treatment of religion. The authors mention that “conservative forms of Islam” are marked as extremist, and they rightly criticize the FBI for treating anti-American political speech as a proxy for terrorism. But they never actually engage the substance of Islamic practice. It is as though piety itself is relevant only insofar as it becomes a target of state surveillance. Yet for millions of American Muslims, faith is not a political identity; it is a daily rhythm of prayer, fasting, charity, and study. The mosque is a sanctuary, not just a site of FBI infiltration. By framing Muslim life entirely through the lens of counterterrorism, the essay mirrors the state’s own reductive gaze. It cannot tell you why a young woman chooses to wear hijab beyond noting that it might make her a target. It cannot capture the joy of an iftar dinner or the intellectual richness of an imam’s khutbah. This matters politically, because a movement that cannot affirm the spiritual fullness of Muslim life will struggle to mobilize the very people it claims to represent.

I also worry about the essay’s heavy emphasis on the domestic security apparatus while it only “briefly touches on the interconnected global dimension.” Any serious analysis of Islamophobia must grapple with the role of American Muslims in the global ummah. Many of us have family, friends, and co-religionists in the countries the U.S. bombs. Our perspective is not simply that of an aggrieved minority; it is that of a transnational community that sees the devastation firsthand. Yet the essay’s domestic focus risks reducing that global solidarity to yet another instance of “anti-American sentiment.” A more empowering approach would center the voices of Muslims globally—not as victims of American policy, but as agents of change within their own societies. The rise of democratically elected Islamist movements in the Arab Spring, the grassroots humanitarian work of Islamic Relief, the intellectual vibrancy of Muslim-majority universities: all of these are part of our story too, and they resist easy categorization into “good” and “bad” Muslims.

What would a politics of Muslim empowerment look like? It would begin by asserting, not begging for, our inherent dignity. It would reject the premise that we must prove our Americanness through military service or unthinking loyalty to foreign policy—but it would also reject the premise that our Americanness is a lie requiring constant debunking. It would fight the surveillance state not as a Muslim issue, but as an issue of human rights that we bring to the table along with our allies, not as supplicants. It would invest in building strong, independent Muslim institutions—schools, civic organizations, media outlets—that can tell our own stories on our own terms, rather than letting the dominant culture define us. And yes, it would hold the U.S. government accountable for its wars, but it would do so from a position of moral clarity rooted in Islamic ethics, not a derivative leftism that sometimes treats Muslims as interchangeable with other “communities of color” without acknowledging the specificities of our history and faith.

I do not wish to be misunderstood as advocating a quietist retreat. The protests at the airports were righteous, and they demonstrated a broad coalition that Muslims helped lead. But we must be careful not to let our political energy be entirely consumed by reacting to the latest outrage from the White House. The long game requires nurturing a generation of Muslims who are confident in their dual identity, who know the Constitution and their Quran equally well, and who do not see themselves as walking on eggshells, forever at the mercy of some amorphous “contingent belonging.” The lead essay’s call to contest oppressive policies is urgent, but it will only succeed if it is grounded in an affirmative vision of who we are, not just a litany of what has been done to us.
