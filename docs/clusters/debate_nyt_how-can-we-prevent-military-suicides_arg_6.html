"To effectively prevent military and veteran suicides, the U.S. must move beyond broad narratives and build a data-driven, integrated support system that tracks individuals from enlistment through civilian life."

The question asks why “so many” service members and veterans kill themselves, but one answer is that we do not yet know enough. The figures are alarming — one active-duty suicide roughly every 36 hours in recent years, and a much higher toll among veterans — but the categories can conceal as much as they reveal. “Veteran” includes a 90-year-old World War II veteran, a 62-year-old Vietnam veteran, a 35-year-old Iraq veteran and someone who served briefly decades ago. Active-duty suicides include people who deployed repeatedly and people who never deployed. If prevention is to work, it has to be guided by better data rather than by one dramatic narrative.

That is not an argument for inaction. It is an argument for precision. Suicide is usually not caused by a single factor. It is associated with mental illness, addiction, relationship breakdown, financial stress, chronic pain, legal trouble, humiliation, isolation and access to lethal means. Military service can intensify some of these risks, but it does not abolish the ordinary risks of young adulthood, masculinity, alcohol abuse or economic insecurity.

The U.S. should build a serious suicide surveillance and prevention system that follows service members from enlistment through separation and into V.A. or civilian care. Right now the handoff from the Department of Defense to the V.A. is too often a gap. Records, risk flags, medication histories and treatment plans should move with the person. A soldier who was hospitalized for suicidal thinking in uniform should not have to start from scratch as a veteran.

We also need to evaluate programs honestly. The military has introduced resilience training, screening questionnaires and awareness campaigns. Some may help; some may simply create the appearance of action. Programs should be tested for outcomes, not slogans. Are suicide attempts reduced? Do people seek help earlier? Are high-risk individuals reached after discharge? Are commanders rewarded for accurate reporting or punished for bad numbers?

Finally, prevention should not be left only to psychiatrists. Primary-care doctors, chaplains, peers, family members, police and employers all encounter people at risk. Many veterans will never walk into a V.A. mental health clinic. Training these front-line contacts to recognize danger and respond quickly is essential.

The suicide crisis deserves urgency, but urgency is not the same as certainty. The most respectful thing we can do for service members and veterans is to stop guessing, gather better evidence, and build a system that finds people before the last hour.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Prevention efforts must be guided by precise data that distinguishes between the diverse demographics and service histories within the military and veteran populations.
  2. 02The Department of Defense and the Department of Veterans Affairs must create a seamless handoff of medical records and risk histories to ensure continuity of care.
  3. 03Existing resilience and prevention programs must be rigorously evaluated based on measurable outcomes rather than their perceived intent.
  4. 04Suicide prevention training should be extended to non-psychiatric front-line contacts, such as primary-care doctors, chaplains, and family members, who are more likely to encounter at-risk individuals.

"To effectively prevent military suicides, the U.S. must move beyond simplistic solutions and treat the issue as a complex, data-driven systemic challenge that requires integrated tracking, accountability, and evidence-based interventions."

We should begin by admitting how much we still do not know. The estimate that a veteran dies by suicide every 80 minutes is horrifying, but veteran suicide data remain imperfect. Death certificates do not always record military service. The Defense Department and the Department of Veterans Affairs have separate systems. Reservists may fall through both. If we are serious, we need a national surveillance system that links service history, deployment history, medical care, prescriptions and cause of death while protecting privacy.

That may sound bureaucratic, but bad data lead to bad prevention. If we assume suicide is mainly caused by combat, we will miss nondeployed service members. If we assume it is simply depression, we will miss legal trouble, debt, domestic violence, alcohol abuse, sleep deprivation, chronic pain and access to firearms. If we focus only on active-duty troops, we will miss the high-risk period after discharge, when identity, income, health insurance and social support can all change at once.

Military suicide is not one problem. It is several overlapping problems. A 20-year-old recruit with a history of childhood trauma, a National Guardsman returning to unemployment, a platoon sergeant on his fourth deployment, and a Vietnam veteran facing illness and isolation may all show up in the same statistics. They will not be saved by the same PowerPoint briefing.

The services should borrow from fields where safety is taken seriously. Every suicide should receive a careful, nonpunitive “psychological autopsy” asking what warning signs were missed and what system failures occurred. Did the person recently lose rank, relationship or liberty? Was there a medication change? Was there a handoff from military medicine to the VA? Did anyone follow up after a missed appointment? Were peers afraid to report concerns because it would be seen as betrayal?

The VA and Defense Department also need shared accountability for transitions. The day a service member leaves uniform should not be the day the system loses track. Enrollment in VA care should be automatic unless declined. High-risk veterans should receive proactive calls, not merely instructions to call a hotline. Mental health care should be integrated with primary care, because many people will report insomnia, headaches or back pain long before they say, “I want to die.”

Finally, we should fund what works and stop what does not. Mandatory briefings may raise awareness, but awareness is not an outcome. Reduced deaths, fewer attempts, faster appointments, safer prescribing, less binge drinking and better follow-up are outcomes. The military prides itself on learning under pressure. Suicide prevention should be treated with the same seriousness as body armor or battlefield medicine: test, measure, adapt and hold leaders responsible.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A national surveillance system is necessary to link service history and medical data to overcome current gaps in veteran suicide statistics.
  2. 02Prevention efforts must address a broad range of stressors beyond combat, including financial, legal, and social factors that affect both deployed and non-deployed personnel.
  3. 03The military should conduct non-punitive psychological autopsies for every suicide to identify missed warning signs and systemic failures.
  4. 04The Department of Defense and the VA must establish shared accountability and automatic enrollment systems to ensure service members do not fall through the cracks during the high-risk transition to civilian life.
  5. 05Suicide prevention programs should be evaluated based on measurable outcomes like reduced death rates rather than just mandatory awareness briefings.
  6. 06Mental health care should be integrated into primary care to reach individuals who present with physical symptoms rather than explicit suicidal ideation.
