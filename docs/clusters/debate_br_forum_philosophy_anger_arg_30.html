Diversified Gemini

"Agnes Callard’s thesis that anger is inherently corrupting and morally damages the oppressed is a dangerous abstraction that ignores anger's vital role as a tool for survival, self-affirmation, and systemic transformation."

In her provocative essay, Agnes Callard asserts that anger is inherently corrupting and that victims of injustice are morally compromised by the very act of being wronged. By framing anger as fundamentally vengeful and grudge-bearing, she concludes that long-term oppression inevitably results in long-term moral damage to the oppressed. This thesis, while mathematically elegant in its cynical logic, is profoundly dangerous. It rests on a privileged, abstract conception of morality that entirely misses the role of anger as a vital instrument of survival, solidarity, and self-affirmation for marginalized groups. By pathologizing the anger of the oppressed as a "moral corruption," Callard inadvertently reinscribes the very power dynamics she claims to analyze, blaming the victim under the guise of philosophical rigor.

Callard builds her case on the assertion that anger cannot be separated from vengeance. The "Argument for Revenge," she claims, proves that to hold someone accountable is to make their "bad" your "good," to force them to suffer as you have suffered. Because vengeance is immoral, and anger necessitates vengeance, the angry victim is thereby morally corrupted. But this formulation fundamentally misunderstands the political and structural realities of oppression. When we talk about racism, sexism, homophobia, and other systemic injustices, the anger of the oppressed is not primarily directed at extracting interpersonal vengeance. It is directed at survival and systemic transformation.

Consider the work of Black feminist thinkers like Audre Lorde, whom Callard notably omits from her genealogy of anger. In "The Uses of Anger," Lorde argues eloquently that anger in the face of racism is not a corrupting vice, but a rational, clarifying, and energizing force. It is an act of supreme self-love in a world that insists upon the victim's worthlessness. When a marginalized person feels anger at their oppression, they are not necessarily plotting to enslave their oppressors or inflict disproportionate suffering (Callard’s "uncanny intimacy" of revenge). Rather, they are asserting their own humanity. The "good" they are pursuing is not the oppressor's "bad"; the "good" is their own liberation, dignity, and right to exist.

Callard’s framework assumes a zero-sum interpersonal game: I steal from you, you take revenge on me. But systemic oppression is not a simple theft between equals. It is a structural denial of personhood. Therefore, the anger that arises from it does not follow the petty logic of the vendetta. It follows the logic of radical self-assertion. To label this self-assertion "moral corruption" is to demand that the oppressed suffer their dehumanization with saintly, passionless grace—a demand that has historically been used to silence dissent and maintain the status quo.

Furthermore, Callard’s assertion that "oppressors have made the oppressed morally worse people" is a breathtakingly paternalistic claim. She uses the tragic story of her grandmother, a Holocaust survivor who forbade Callard from playing with a German girl, as evidence that victims lose their innocence and become morally compromised. While the grandmother's prejudice against a child is certainly a moral error, to attribute this to the "logic of moral responsiveness" is to mistake a trauma response for a philosophical principle.

More importantly, Callard treats the oppressed as passive receptacles of moral damage. But history is replete with examples of marginalized groups who have channeled their righteous anger into magnificent movements for human rights, creating communities of care, fierce solidarity, and profound moral vision. The Civil Rights Movement, the LGBTQ+ rights movement, anti-colonial struggles—these were fueled by deep, abiding anger at injustice. Were the participants in these movements "morally worse people" because they harbored anger at their oppressors? Did their anger at systemic violence constitute a "vengeful and grudge-bearing" corruption? On the contrary, their anger was the very engine of moral progress. It was an expression of deep moral clarity.

Callard attempts to preempt this critique by arguing that any attempt to sanitize anger—to call it "indignation" or "transition anger"—is a philosopher’s fiction. She insists that all affective responses to injustice "cling to the taste of blood." But this is a psychological assertion presented without sufficient evidence, relying instead on the historical generalizations of Nietzsche, Foucault, and Girard. While it may be true that human beings struggle with vindictiveness, it is empirically false that all anger at injustice demands the suffering of the oppressor. Much of it demands the dismantling of the oppressive *system*. The anger of the oppressed often seeks to destroy the master's house, not to move into the master's bedroom.

By flattening all anger into a universal urge for revenge, Callard erases the vast, qualitative difference between the anger of the powerful (which seeks domination) and the anger of the powerless (which seeks emancipation). We cannot talk about the morality of anger without talking about power. When Callard concludes that "we can't be good in a bad world," she surrenders the very concept of goodness to the oppressors. But goodness in a bad world is not found in sterile purity or the absence of anger. Goodness in a bad world looks like the fierce, angry, and uncompromising demand for a better one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Callard’s 'Argument for Revenge' incorrectly reduces the anger of the oppressed to a desire for interpersonal vengeance rather than a pursuit of liberation and dignity.
  2. 02Anger in the face of systemic injustice functions as a rational and clarifying force that asserts the humanity of the marginalized against their dehumanization.
  3. 03Labeling the anger of the oppressed as 'moral corruption' reinforces existing power dynamics by demanding that victims respond to their own suffering with a passive, saintly grace.
  4. 04The claim that oppression makes victims 'morally worse' is paternalistic and ignores how marginalized groups have historically channeled righteous anger into movements for moral progress and human rights.
  5. 05Callard fails to distinguish between the anger of the powerful, which seeks domination, and the anger of the powerless, which seeks to dismantle oppressive systems rather than inflict suffering on individuals.
