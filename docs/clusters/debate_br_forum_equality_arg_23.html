"While Sypnowich’s philosophical critique of opportunity-focused egalitarianism is compelling, her vision of an outcome-focused society fails to account for the significant practical and political hurdles of implementation."

Sypnowich's philosophical critique of opportunity-focused egalitarianism is valuable, but it does not adequately address the practical challenges of implementing an outcome-focused approach. Let me offer some considerations from the perspective of policy implementation that complicate her optimistic conclusion.

The core insight of Sypnowich's argument is compelling: we should care about what people actually achieve, not merely what opportunities they possess. A society where everyone has access to excellent education but where few children from poor backgrounds actually excel is not an egalitarian society, regardless of formal equality of opportunity. This is right. But translating this insight into practical policy is enormously difficult.

The first challenge is measurement. How do we know whether people are flourishing? Sypnowich invokesSen's capabilities approach, but capabilities are difficult to observe and quantify. We can measure income, educational attainment, health status, and other proximate indicators of well-being, but these are imperfect proxies for the flourishing she seeks. Income inequality has been extensively documented; capability inequality is far harder to track. Any outcome-focused policy will therefore rely on imperfect metrics that may not capture genuine flourishing and could create perverse incentives. If we measure flourishing by educational attainment, schools will be incentivized to credentialize rather than educate. If we measure it by health outcomes, providers will focus on treatable conditions at the expense of others. The choice of metric shapes the policy, and the choice of any metric is contestable.

The second challenge is intervention. Sypnowich argues that we should do more than provide opportunities; we should actively enable people to take them up. This might involve compulsory education in the arts, as she suggests, or other interventions to shape preferences and capabilities. But such interventions require detailed knowledge of what will enable flourishing—knowledge we do not possess. The evidence on what interventions actually improve long-term outcomes is mixed and often contradictory. Early childhood programs show promise; many subsequent interventions do not replicate their success. We know that poverty depresses outcomes, but we do not know precisely which features of poverty are most damaging or which interventions most effectively address them.

This uncertainty does not mean we should do nothing. But it does mean that outcome-focused policies must be experimental, adaptive, and humble—qualities that sit uneasily with the sweeping claims Sypnowich makes. She writes as though we know what flourishing looks like and how to promote it; in practice, we often do not.

The third challenge is political sustainability. Outcome-focused policies are inherently more intrusive than opportunity-focused ones, and they generate political resistance. Policies that assess whether people are flourishing and intervene when they are not—policies that, in effect, evaluate how well people are living their lives—will face opposition from those who value liberty and those who resent state interference. The history of the welfare state suggests that redistribution is more politically feasible when it is targeted and temporary—when it helps people get back on their feet—than when it is comprehensive and permanent. The flourishing approach seems to require the latter, which may explain why it has rarely been implemented successfully.

Sypnowich might respond that political obstacles should not determine our normative commitments. This is true, but it is also true that normative commitments must be implementable to be meaningful. An egalitarian theory that cannot guide practical politics is of limited value.

Perhaps the most important practical lesson from attempts to implement outcome equality is that structural transformation matters more than individual intervention. Countries that have achieved relative equality of outcome—Iceland, Norway, Denmark—have done so primarily through universal social programs, strong unions, and extensive social insurance, not through intensive efforts to shape individual choices. The emphasis on social structure rather than individual behavior is consistent with Sypnowich's critique of luck egalitarianism, but it suggests a different policy agenda than the one her essay implies. Rather than focusing on whether individuals are flourishing, we should focus on whether social institutions are structured in ways that enable flourishing. This is more tractable and perhaps more sustainable.

None of this refutes Sypnowich's philosophical argument. But it suggests that implementing her vision will require more attention to practical challenges than her essay provides.
