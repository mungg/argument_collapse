"The proposal to decentralize domestic intelligence by integrating local police into a national network is fundamentally flawed because it misinterprets the nature of transnational terrorism and risks compromising national security through fragmentation and parochialism."

Daniel Richman’s essay, “The Right Fight,” frames the debate over domestic intelligence in the post-9/11 era as a complex negotiation between federal imperatives and local community relations. By portraying state and local law enforcement as indispensable partners in the prevention of terrorism, Richman elevates the role of the municipal beat cop to the level of national security asset. He argues that this decentralization is not only operationally necessary but also politically beneficial, as it introduces a level of democratic accountability that federal agencies alone cannot provide. While Richman’s sociological analysis of local policing is astute, his central prescription fundamentally misunderstands the nature of the terrorist threat we face today and vastly overestimates the capacity of 18,000 fractured, under-resourced local police departments to contribute meaningfully to a coherent national intelligence picture.

The fundamental error in Richman’s analysis is his conflation of ordinary street crime with transnational terrorism. He draws heavily on the history of federal-local cooperation in the War on Drugs and the fight against violent crime during the 1990s. In those contexts, local police were indeed the primary repositories of knowledge. Drug cartels and street gangs operate on specific geographical turf; they interact constantly with the local illicit economy; their members are known to local informants, vice cops, and beat officers. In this paradigm, the federal government acts as a force multiplier, bringing money, advanced surveillance, and stiff federal sentencing guidelines to bear on targets identified from the ground up by local police.

Al Qaeda and its affiliated networks do not operate like the Bloods, the Crips, or local narcotics syndicates. They are transnational, highly disciplined, and explicitly designed to evade local detection. The "sleeper cells" that executed the September 11 attacks did not embed themselves in the local criminal underworld. They did not sell drugs on the corner or engage in the kind of disorderly conduct that attracts the attention of a patrol officer. They were educated, well-funded, and lived quietly in middle-class neighborhoods. They exploited vulnerabilities in federal systems—immigration, aviation security, and international finance.

Expecting a beat cop in Detroit or a county sheriff in Florida to uncover the next Mohamed Atta by leveraging routine community contacts is a fantasy. The intelligence required to preempt a terrorist attack is highly specialized, often derived from classified signals intelligence (SIGINT), foreign intelligence intercepts, and complex financial tracking. When Richman talks about "connecting dots," he assumes that the dots are evenly distributed across the jurisdictions of local police departments. But the most critical dots are almost always federal or international in origin.

The National Commission on Terrorist Acts Upon the United States (the 9/11 Commission) correctly identified that the primary failure leading up to the attacks was not a lack of local police involvement, but a profound failure of the federal government to share information within its own ranks. The CIA, the FBI, and the NSA were operating in isolated "stovepipes." The solution to a fragmented federal bureaucracy is not to further fragment the intelligence picture by dispersing it among thousands of independent local agencies.

Richman argues that creating a stand-alone federal domestic-intelligence agency, akin to Britain’s MI-5, would be ineffective because it would lack the local informational networks that police possess. This severely underestimates the capability of a dedicated, highly trained federal cadre. A domestic intelligence agency, stripped of the FBI’s traditional law enforcement and prosecutorial mandates, could focus entirely on analyzing patterns, tracking suspected terrorists across state lines, and coordinating with the CIA and NSA. By contrast, trying to train hundreds of thousands of local police officers in the nuances of international terror networks is an exercise in futility and a massive misallocation of resources.

Furthermore, integrating local police deeply into the national intelligence network presents severe operational and security risks. Counterterrorism investigations rely on tightly controlled, highly classified information. If federal agencies are forced to constantly share sensitive intelligence with local police departments—many of which lack secure facilities and whose officers do not hold high-level security clearances—the risk of leaks increases exponentially. A local police chief, responding to the "electoral accountability" Richman prizes, might feel compelled to assure his constituents that a specific threat is being handled, inadvertently compromising a years-long federal surveillance operation.

Richman also glosses over the profound parochialism of local law enforcement. He acknowledges that disputes over "credit-grabbing" plague Joint Terrorism Task Forces (JTTFs), but dismisses this as a minor byproduct of overlapping jurisdictions. In reality, local political imperatives often directly conflict with national security objectives. A mayor facing a tough reelection campaign may pressure his police department to prioritize high-visibility, low-level arrests to show the public they are "tough on terror," rather than cooperating with a quiet, long-term federal investigation that yields no immediate press conferences. National security cannot be held hostage to municipal election cycles.

This is not to say that local police have no role whatsoever. As first responders, their role in crisis management and target hardening is undeniable. And in the rare instance that a local officer stumbles upon evidence of terrorism during a routine traffic stop or domestic disturbance call, there must be a seamless mechanism for passing that information up to federal authorities. The JTTF model, despite its flaws, is the appropriate venue for this limited interaction. It allows a select number of vetted, cleared local officers to work under federal direction, bridging the gap without compromising the integrity of the intelligence.

But Richman envisions something much broader: a systemic, nationwide integration where local police demand "more of a voice in federal domestic-intelligence policy" as the price for their cooperation. This is a recipe for strategic paralysis. The federal government has the constitutional responsibility and the institutional capacity to protect the nation from foreign attacks. Delegating a significant portion of that responsibility to local entities that are ultimately accountable only to their immediate geographic constituents undermines the coherence of the national defense.

In our haste to fix the intelligence failures of the past, we must not swing the pendulum too far toward radical decentralization. The fight against global terrorism requires a unified, federal response, guided by national priorities and executed by professionals trained in the specific arts of counterintelligence. Relying on the community relations of local police departments to save us from international terrorism is placing a burden on local law enforcement that they are neither equipped nor constitutionally designed to bear. The right fight requires the right tools, and for domestic intelligence, those tools must remain firmly in federal hands.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Transnational terrorist cells, unlike street criminals, operate with high discipline and evade the local detection methods that beat cops typically rely on.
  2. 02The specialized intelligence required to preempt attacks, such as signals intelligence and international financial tracking, is beyond the institutional capacity and expertise of local law enforcement.
  3. 03Dispersing sensitive intelligence among thousands of under-resourced local agencies increases the risk of security leaks and compromises classified federal operations.
  4. 04Local political imperatives and electoral accountability often conflict with the long-term, quiet surveillance necessary for effective counterterrorism.
  5. 05The primary failure of 9/11 was a lack of coordination within the federal government, which is best solved by a unified federal domestic intelligence agency rather than further decentralization.
  6. 06While local police are essential as first responders, their role in intelligence should be limited to the existing Joint Terrorism Task Force model rather than a systemic policy-making voice.
