"While progressives should challenge conservative judicial supremacy, they should avoid responding with a 'constitutional maximalism' that frames every policy goal as a constitutional necessity, as this risks undermining democratic flexibility and institutional stability."

The lead essay offers a bracing diagnosis of conservative constitutional power and a tempting prescription: progressives should reclaim constitutional argument, revive an older democracy-of-opportunity tradition, and insist that egalitarian legislation is not merely allowed but constitutionally necessary. I share much of the diagnosis. The Supreme Court has indeed become an aggressive political actor. It has used the First Amendment, federalism, religious liberty, and separation-of-powers doctrines to disable legislation that elected branches could reasonably enact. It is poised, in the wake of the leaked Dobbs draft, to unsettle still more of the constitutional settlement on which many Americans have relied.

But I am less convinced by the prescription. The problem with contemporary constitutional politics is not only that conservatives have monopolized it. The problem is that constitutionalizing policy disputes often makes them less democratic, less revisable, and more existential. Progressives should certainly reject judicial supremacy. They should defend Congress’s power to legislate. They should expose the ideological character of conservative judging. But they should be wary of responding to conservative constitutional maximalism with progressive constitutional maximalism of their own.

A politics in which every major policy goal becomes a constitutional imperative is a politics that risks turning ordinary democratic disagreement into constitutional betrayal. That may be emotionally satisfying, especially when the opposing side is acting in bad faith. Yet constitutional inflation has costs. If public financing of elections, the PRO Act, wealth taxation, aggressive antitrust, Medicaid expansion, codetermination, and reparative investment are all constitutional necessities, what remains in the domain of political judgment? What room is left for disagreement among citizens who accept democracy but differ over means, sequencing, institutional design, or tradeoffs?

The authors will answer that constitutional necessity need not mean judicial enforceability. They repeatedly emphasize that progressive constitutional claims should function mostly as a shield against courts, not as a sword for judges. That distinction is important. But in practice, the boundary between constitutional politics and constitutional law is porous, as the essay itself insists. If progressives teach citizens that particular social and economic arrangements are constitutionally required, they should not be surprised when future judges are asked to enforce those requirements. Nor should they assume that judges appointed by progressives will show more restraint than conservative judges once given a vocabulary of constitutional political economy.

The New Deal settlement, at its best, rested on a different insight: courts should stop constitutionalizing economic theory. Legislatures should have wide latitude to experiment. The people, through elections and institutions, should decide whether to pursue social insurance, labor rights, public investment, or market regulation. That settlement was never perfect. It coexisted with racial exclusion, anti-communist repression, and uneven protection for civil liberties. But its core lesson remains sound. When courts constitutionalize political economy, democracy suffers.

The lead essay rightly criticizes the Court’s Lochner-era hostility to labor and regulation. But the great achievement of the anti-Lochner tradition was not that it replaced laissez-faire constitutionalism with a judicially enforceable social-democratic constitutionalism. It was that it removed courts from the business of treating one contested economic theory as constitutional command. The lesson of West Coast Hotel, Carolene Products, and the post-1937 cases was not that the Constitution compels the welfare state. It was that the Constitution permits democratic majorities to build one.

That permissive principle may sound weak compared with the authors’ call for constitutional duty. Yet it is more powerful than it appears. In a country as large, diverse, and institutionally fragmented as the United States, durable reform usually depends not on declaring one’s goals constitutionally mandatory but on building coalitions, winning elections, administering programs competently, and making benefits visible. Social Security and Medicare are not secure because Americans believe Article I requires them. They are secure because they work, because constituencies defend them, and because politicians threaten them at their peril. The Affordable Care Act became more politically resilient as people gained coverage and Republicans failed to produce a workable alternative. The constitutional defense mattered in NFIB, but the program’s long-term survival depends more on politics than on constitutional rhetoric.

The authors are right that silence about the Constitution cedes ground to courts. But there is a middle path between silence and constitutional compulsion. Progressives can say: the Constitution gives Congress broad authority to solve national problems; the Reconstruction amendments empower Congress to protect equal citizenship; the Guarantee Clause embodies a republican commitment inconsistent with minority entrenchment; the First Amendment should not be distorted into a deregulatory weapon; federalism must not become a license for subordination. These are constitutional arguments, but they are arguments for democratic space, not for one comprehensive constitutional political economy.

This distinction matters especially on the First Amendment. The lead essay criticizes Citizens United, Arizona Free Enterprise, and Janus for treating egalitarian regulation as censorship. Much of that criticism is justified. The Court’s campaign finance doctrine is too hostile to anti-corruption legislation, too naïve about corporate power, and too dismissive of the ways money distorts representation. Janus was a serious blow to public-sector unions and rests on an implausibly broad account of compelled speech. Still, progressives should hesitate before recasting the First Amendment chiefly as a tool for balancing political power.

The First Amendment’s anti-egalitarian uses are real. But so are its anti-authoritarian uses. Dissenters, protesters, whistleblowers, journalists, religious minorities, artists, and unpopular movements all rely on courts willing to distrust government regulation of speech. A progressive movement angry at corporate speech today may one day face state governments eager to classify protest, boycott, or classroom discussion as harmful conduct rather than protected expression. Indeed, many Republican-led states are already using laws against critical race theory, protest, and boycotts to chill political activity. Progressives need a robust First Amendment, not only a democratic one.

The better approach is not to make equality the master principle of speech law but to distinguish more carefully among forms of regulation. Public financing that expands speech should be upheld. Disclosure requirements should generally be upheld. Reasonable contribution limits should be upheld. Corporate treasury spending can be regulated because corporations are state-created economic entities with special privileges, not because government may equalize all political influence. Union agency fees should be permissible when tied to representational duties. But courts should remain skeptical of laws that directly suppress advocacy, punish viewpoints, or empower officials to decide which speakers have too much influence.

A similar caution applies to race and political economy. The lead essay powerfully reminds us that racial justice cannot be separated from material conditions. Reconstruction failed in part because emancipation without land, education, labor rights, and federal enforcement left freed people vulnerable to new forms of domination. Modern racial inequality is inseparable from housing, health care, schools, labor markets, policing, and wealth. Yet the constitutional law of race is already dangerously polarized. If every redistributive program aimed at reducing racial inequality is framed primarily as a racial constitutional requirement, it may become more vulnerable before a Court eager to expand colorblind doctrine.

That does not mean abandoning racial justice. It means designing programs that are both morally honest and legally durable. Universal programs can be structured to address racially unequal need. Place-based investment can target communities harmed by segregation and disinvestment. Baby bonds, child allowances, health coverage, housing vouchers, and school funding can dramatically reduce racial disparities without relying exclusively on racial classifications. Congress should also use its Reconstruction powers more boldly where there is a clear enforcement record. But constitutional rhetoric should not outrun institutional capacity or legal strategy.

On antitrust and corporate power, the authors are on especially strong ground in criticizing technocratic depoliticization. The consumer-welfare framework narrowed antitrust too much. It ignored labor markets, supplier dependence, innovation, political power, and the civic dangers of concentrated private authority. Reviving antimonopoly politics is essential. But again, the best argument is that democratic legislatures and expert agencies should have authority to revise antitrust policy, not that the Constitution itself settles the matter. A neo-Brandeisian antitrust policy may be wise; it should not become immune from democratic revision by being wrapped too tightly in constitutional necessity.

The deeper issue is institutional trust. The authors distrust courts, for good reason. But constitutional politics has a way of returning power to lawyers and judges even when it begins as popular mobilization. If progressives want to democratize constitutional meaning, they should invest less in grand constitutional theory and more in institutions that make democracy possible: voting rights, fair districting, labor organization, local journalism, civic education, campaign finance reform, state capacity, and party reform. They should make Congress work better. They should reform the Senate filibuster. They should consider Supreme Court term limits and ethics rules. They should build administrative agencies capable of delivering benefits competently and fairly. These are constitutional projects in a broad sense, but their success depends on power, design, and performance.

The conservative legal movement did not win merely because it had constitutional arguments. It won because it built a pipeline, captured courts, developed donor networks, disciplined politicians, and linked elite legal theory to mass politics. Progressives cannot answer that with rhetoric alone. Nor should they assume that invoking the Constitution will make popular majorities appear. Many Americans distrust concentrated wealth; many also distrust federal power. Many support unions in general; fewer understand the details of secondary boycotts or sectoral bargaining. Many dislike money in politics; they also value free speech. Constitutional argument can help organize these intuitions, but it cannot substitute for persuasion about policy.

The lead essay is right that progressives should stop speaking as though their agenda is constitutionally suspect. They should recover the confidence to say that democracy requires material foundations, that Congress has broad constitutional authority, and that courts have no warrant to impose oligarchic constitutionalism. But they should resist the urge to mirror the right’s habit of converting policy preferences into supreme law.

A progressive constitutionalism worth defending should be modest in judicial ambition and bold in democratic aspiration. It should say not “the Constitution mandates our entire program,” but “the Constitution belongs to the people, and it leaves us free to build a more equal republic.” In the current emergency, preserving that freedom may be the most important constitutional task of all.

"The proposal to 'constitutionalize' progressive politics by claiming the Constitution requires a specific egalitarian economic order is a dangerous invitation to weaken the very limits and structural restraints that make constitutional democracy possible."

The lead essay is candid in a way many progressive constitutional arguments are not. It does not merely say that the Supreme Court has misread a clause here or misapplied a precedent there. It argues that the Constitution should be understood to require a broad progressive political economy: aggressive campaign finance regulation, stronger unions, racialized economic redistribution, expanded federal authority, antitrust reorientation, wealth taxation, and perhaps codetermination in corporate governance. The authors call this the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition. I would call it an invitation to constitutionalize progressive politics and thereby weaken the very limits that make constitutional democracy possible.

The essay begins from a real problem: the country is polarized, economically unequal, and governed through institutions that frustrate majoritarian impulses. But its diagnosis of the Court as an “archconservative” or “juristocratic” threat is overdrawn. In many of the areas discussed, the Court has done what courts are supposed to do: enforce constitutional limits against transient political coalitions. Progressives may dislike those limits because they interfere with preferred policies. Conservatives sometimes dislike limits too. But a Constitution that never obstructs one’s political agenda is not a constitution. It is a platform.

The authors repeatedly describe conservative jurisprudence as “weaponized.” This language obscures more than it reveals. The First Amendment is not “weaponized” when it protects speakers whom progressives dislike or when it prevents government from manipulating the political playing field. Federalism is not “weaponized” when it preserves the constitutional role of states. Religious liberty is not “weaponized” when it protects people from being forced to violate conscience. The Takings Clause is not “weaponized” when it prevents government from redistributing private property without constitutional scrutiny. Rights and structural limits are not illegitimate simply because they complicate egalitarian policymaking.

Consider campaign finance. The lead essay faults the Court for rejecting laws designed to “level the playing field” or reduce the conversion of economic power into political power. But that is precisely why such laws are constitutionally dangerous. A government empowered to decide that some speakers have too much influence and others too little is a government empowered to regulate political debate according to its preferred distribution of power. Today progressives may wish to restrain corporations and wealthy candidates. Tomorrow a conservative legislature may decide that universities, unions, newspapers, nonprofit advocacy groups, or technology platforms have too much cultural power and need to be “balanced” by legal restrictions.

The First Amendment rests on distrust of such official engineering. It protects speech not because every speaker is equally powerful but because government cannot be trusted to ration political influence. Citizens United is unpopular in many circles, but its core proposition is sound: the government may not ban independent political advocacy because the speaker is a corporation. Corporations are not merely piles of capital. They include media corporations, nonprofit corporations, advocacy organizations, small businesses, and associations of citizens using legal forms to act collectively. The corporate form does not erase First Amendment interests.

The authors note that PACs remained available before Citizens United. But constitutional rights are not usually satisfied by telling speakers to use a more burdensome government-approved vehicle. Nor is it clear why a union’s political spending should be celebrated as collective democratic action while a corporation’s spending is treated as oligarchic corruption. Both are associations. Both aggregate resources. Both can be powerful. A principled First Amendment cannot protect only the associations favored by one political movement.

Public financing and small-donor matching are different. Properly designed, they can expand participation without suppressing speech. But the lead essay’s deeper ambition—to treat equalization as a constitutional duty—would endanger free expression. Political liberty includes the liberty to persuade others, spend resources on advocacy, and join with others for common political purposes. Inequality may be a social problem, but it does not follow that government should police the influence of speakers.

The discussion of labor law suffers from a similar imbalance. The authors describe unions as necessary countervailing power and condemn Janus for allowing public employees to “free ride.” But Janus involved compelled payments to public-sector unions, whose bargaining demands directly affect government budgets, taxation, education policy, policing, and public administration. Public-sector collective bargaining is inherently political. Forcing dissenting employees to subsidize that speech and bargaining violates a basic principle: citizens should not be compelled to support political advocacy with which they disagree.

Abood’s attempted distinction between chargeable bargaining expenses and nonchargeable political expenses was unstable because in the public sector the distinction is artificial. Bargaining over teacher tenure, class size, pension obligations, police discipline, or agency staffing is political. If a state wishes to bargain exclusively with a union, it may do so. But it should not force nonmembers to fund that union. The “free rider” problem is created by exclusive representation itself; it is not a justification for compelled speech.

Nor is Taft-Hartley the constitutional villain the essay suggests. Secondary boycotts and closed shops raise serious concerns about coercion, economic disruption, and the rights of workers who do not wish to join unions. A constitutional order committed to freedom of association must protect the right not to associate as well as the right to organize. The authors speak often of domination by employers. That domination can be real. But unions can also dominate dissenting workers, small businesses, consumers, and the public. The law must balance these interests, not constitutionalize one side’s power.

On race and political economy, the lead essay makes a morally serious point: racial injustice has material dimensions. No account of equal citizenship can ignore education, health, housing, employment, and wealth. But the authors too quickly transform that observation into a defense of expansive federal power and race-conscious economic policy. The Constitution’s federal structure is not an obstacle to justice by definition. States are not mere administrative units awaiting federal correction. They are constitutional communities with their own electorates, institutions, and responsibilities.

NFIB v. Sebelius is a useful example. The Court upheld most of the Affordable Care Act, including the individual mandate as a tax. Its Medicaid holding was limited: Congress could not threaten states with the loss of existing Medicaid funds unless they accepted a dramatic expansion of the program. That is not an exotic neo-Confederate doctrine. It reflects the common-sense idea that federal grants to states can become coercive when Congress uses financial dependence to force state compliance. If Congress wants to create a national health program, it can tax and spend directly. What it cannot do is transform a cooperative federal program into a compulsory one while pretending states retain a choice.

Shelby County is harder, because voting discrimination is a grave constitutional evil and Congress has special enforcement power under the Fifteenth Amendment. But even there, the question was whether a decades-old coverage formula could continue to subject some states and localities to extraordinary federal supervision based on outdated data. One may think the Court should have deferred more to Congress. Yet the principle that extraordinary remedies require current justification is not absurd. Federalism does not become illegitimate because Southern states have ugly histories. Constitutional enforcement must respond to present conditions with appropriate means.

The essay’s treatment of religious liberty is especially revealing. Religious exemptions are depicted largely as carve-outs from civil rights law. Sometimes they can be. But religious freedom is not a minor inconvenience to the progressive state. It is a foundational constitutional commitment. In a pluralistic society, conflicts between antidiscrimination norms and religious conscience are unavoidable. The law must protect gay and lesbian citizens from unjust exclusion; it must also avoid crushing religious institutions and individuals whose beliefs differ from the majority’s moral consensus. A constitutional politics that treats religious liberty as a disguise for hierarchy will deepen cultural conflict and invite retaliation when power changes hands.

The authors’ antitrust argument has more merit. Conservatives should not be indifferent to concentrated private power. The American tradition has long worried about monopoly, privilege, and crony capitalism. Markets require law, and law should prevent fraud, collusion, predation, and barriers to entry. There is a respectable conservative case for stronger antitrust enforcement, particularly when dominant firms use market power to suppress competition or collude with government.

But again, the lead essay reaches too far. The consumer-welfare standard may be incomplete, but replacing it with an open-ended mandate to fight “oligarchy” risks politicizing antitrust beyond recognition. Agencies could punish firms for size, political influence, labor practices, speech policies, or cultural role without clear legal standards. Antitrust would become a general-purpose weapon against disfavored companies. That may feel satisfying when aimed at Big Tech or agribusiness. It will feel less satisfying when wielded by a future administration against media organizations, renewable energy firms, universities, or companies associated with progressive causes.

Corporate governance raises the same concern. The authors admire proposals requiring large corporations to consider stakeholders or include workers in governance. These may be debated as policy. But a constitutional defense of codetermination should not dismiss property and associational concerns. Investors risk capital under legal expectations. Entrepreneurs build firms. Shareholders have legitimate claims. Government can regulate corporate charters, but forcing governance structures to pursue broad political goals risks turning private enterprise into an arm of the state. A free society needs space for civil society and markets outside direct political control.

The most troubling feature of the lead essay is its impatience with constitutional restraint. The authors say progressives should stop merely arguing that their policies are permitted and start arguing that they are required. But constitutional humility is a virtue, not a surrender. In a diverse republic, many important questions should remain open to democratic contestation. How much redistribution? What kind of health system? Which labor rules? What tax structure? How to regulate corporations? These are not easy questions with single constitutional answers. They involve tradeoffs among liberty, equality, efficiency, pluralism, and local self-government.

The conservative legal movement has its own excesses. Some libertarian constitutional arguments against regulation are too aggressive. Some originalist rhetoric masks political preference. Courts should not invent doctrines casually. But the answer to overreach is not progressive overreach. It is a renewed commitment to constitutional limits, judicial modesty, and democratic politics within those limits.

Progressives are free to campaign for public financing, union expansion, health insurance, reparations, wealth taxes, and antitrust reform. They should make moral, economic, and political arguments for those policies. They should win elections and pass laws within constitutional bounds. But when they say the Constitution requires their agenda, they imply that opponents are not merely wrong but unconstitutional. That is a dangerous claim in a country already strained by mutual delegitimation.

The Constitution establishes a framework for ordered liberty, not a mandate for any comprehensive political economy. It protects speech even when speech is unequal. It protects federalism even when states make bad choices. It protects religious exercise even when conscience frustrates regulation. It protects property and association even when redistribution is popular. These protections are not defects. They are part of what prevents democratic politics from becoming simple majoritarian command.

The lead essay wants a republic, not an oligarchy. So do many of us who reject its conclusions. But a republic is not preserved by empowering government to equalize political influence, conscript dissenters into unions, override states whenever national majorities disapprove, or subordinate private institutions to a single egalitarian vision. It is preserved by constitutional limits, plural centers of power, free association, open debate, and the discipline of persuading fellow citizens rather than constitutionalizing one’s program.
