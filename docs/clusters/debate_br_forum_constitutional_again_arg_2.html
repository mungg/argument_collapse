Diversified DeepSeek

"While reclaiming constitutional rhetoric is a necessary corrective to liberal passivity, it is an insufficient strategy that must be secondary to aggressive institutional reforms and mass political mobilization aimed at curbing the Supreme Court's power."

The lead essay in this forum mounts an ambitious case for progressives to reclaim the Constitution as a tool in the fight against oligarchy. Drawing on the rich “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, Fishkin, Forbath, and their colleagues are right about one central point: for too long liberals have ceded the terrain of constitutional interpretation to conservatives, retreating into a defensive posture that treats the Supreme Court as the sole oracle of constitutional meaning. Their call to make progressive politics constitutional again is a necessary corrective to decades of self-imposed silence. But as a strategy for the present crisis, it is deeply insufficient. The danger is not that progressives will make constitutional claims; it is that they will overinvest in those claims at the expense of the hard political and institutional work that must be done outside the courtroom.

The authors’ historical narrative is compelling. From the Jacksonians to the New Deal, reformers argued that the Constitution compelled, not merely permitted, a broad distribution of power and opportunity. They understood that economic structures are themselves constitutional questions—that the concentration of wealth and the subjugation of labor are not merely policy problems but threats to republican government. The lead essay is at its strongest when it shows how conservatives never abandoned this insight. Their judicial project, from the Lochner era through Citizens United and Janus, has consistently weaponized the Constitution to dismantle egalitarian legislation. The right understands that constitutional law is political economy by other means, and it has spent generations embedding that vision in the judiciary. Liberals, by contrast, persuaded themselves that law was an autonomous domain best left to technocrats and judges.

Given this asymmetry, the call to re-politicize constitutional argument is sound. Progressives should indeed argue, as the authors suggest, that there is a constitutional duty to build countervailing power against oligarchy—that the First Amendment protects workers’ collective action, that campaign finance reforms are a democratic imperative, and that racial justice cannot be severed from economic transformation. These are not merely permissible arguments; they are historically rooted and normatively powerful. They can shift the terms of public debate, signal to legislators what is at stake, and, over time, influence a future generation of judges.

Yet the lead essay underestimates how thoroughly the current Court is insulated from such rhetorical shifts. The conservative majority does not lack for progressive counterarguments; it systematically disregards them. In Janus v. AFSCME, the Court knew exactly what was at stake: the dissent laid out in excruciating detail how destroying agency fees would cripple public-sector unions. The majority did not care. In Citizens United, Justice Stevens’s dissent invoked a long history of democratic duty. The majority shrugged. The Roberts Court is not engaged in a good-faith debate about constitutional political economy; it is advancing a substantive vision that it is willing to impose regardless of what Congress, the president, or the people think. Making better arguments is unlikely to sway justices who have already committed to a rigid, outcome-driven methodology.

This is not a counsel of despair; it is a recognition of the real balance of institutional power. The lead essay gestures toward legislative responses—the PRO Act, small-donor matching funds, a wealth tax—and rightly predicts that these will face constitutional challenges. But the authors are vague about what happens when the Court predictably strikes them down. They speak of protracted fights and the need for “a strategy for contesting hostile judges’ visions.” Yet what does such contestation look like in practice? The Court has shown no compunction about dismantling the Voting Rights Act, hobbling the Affordable Care Act, and inventing new doctrines to block federal action. If Congress enacts a wealth tax, the Court will almost certainly find that it violates the Direct Tax Clause or some novel federalism principle. If Congress passes the PRO Act, the Justices will discover that mandatory collective bargaining infringes employers’ First Amendment rights. The pattern is already set. Constitutional arguments outside the courts are vital, but they will not, by themselves, stop a majority bent on protecting capital and constraining democracy.

The real work must therefore be focused on altering the institutional landscape. That means, first and foremost, addressing the Senate filibuster, which the lead essay acknowledges only in passing. Without abolishing or substantially reforming the filibuster, none of the legislative proposals the authors champion—campaign finance reform, labor law reform, voting rights protections, a wealth tax—can pass. The filibuster is itself a constitutional artifact, though not in the written text: it is a Senate rule that has become a de facto supermajority requirement for most legislation, and it overwhelmingly benefits the conservative minority. Progressives should argue, as part of their constitutional vision, that the filibuster is incompatible with democratic self-government and must be eliminated. But that is a political fight, not a constitutional one in the legal sense.

Even more important, progressives must grapple seriously with court reform. The lead essay briefly acknowledges that some liberals have proposed “judicial reforms aimed at restoring an imagined past of judicial nonpartisanship,” but dismisses this as a golden-age myth. That is a fair critique of the notion that the Court can be made “apolitical.” But the authors do not fully engage with the more muscular reforms that are now on the table: expanding the number of justices, imposing term limits, stripping jurisdiction over certain types of cases, or requiring a supermajority for judicial review. These are not attempts to depoliticize the judiciary; they are efforts to democratize it. If the Court is going to make political economy decisions—as it always has—then the composition of the Court and the scope of its power must be subject to democratic contestation. Constitutional rhetoric can help here: the democracy-of-opportunity tradition implies that the Court itself must not be allowed to become an instrument of oligarchy. But that argument will be made in Congress and in the streets, not in a legal brief.

There is a risk that the lead essay, for all its historical depth, will lead progressives to double down on the very court-centric thinking they are trying to escape. By framing so much of the analysis around the First Amendment, the Takings Clause, and federalism doctrines, the authors inadvertently reinforce the idea that the ultimate battlefield is the courtroom. Yet the most successful progressive constitutional moments—the Reconstruction Amendments, the New Deal—were achieved not primarily through legal argument but through mass mobilization, economic crisis, and raw political power. FDR did not persuade the Court to change; he threatened to pack it, and the Court retreated. Lincoln did not win the constitutional argument about secession in a court of law; he won it on the battlefield. The point is not that violence is the answer, but that constitutional change is often a byproduct of broader power struggles, not their cause.

Progressives should absolutely reclaim the language of the Constitution. Abandoning it entirely, as some on the left suggest, cedes too much ground to those who wrap themselves in the flag and the Founding. But reclaiming it must be part of a larger project of building democratic power outside elite institutions. That means organizing unions in defiance of hostile laws, even when that invites court injunctions. It means enacting state-level policies that push the boundaries of what is permissible, daring the Court to strike them down and create a political backlash. It means treating court decisions as provisional, as obstacles to be overcome rather than final pronouncements. And it means being willing, when necessary, to restructure the judiciary itself so that it no longer serves as a bulwark against democratic self-rule.

In the end, the lead essay’s call to make progressive politics constitutional again is a valuable provocation. It reminds us that the Constitution is too important to be left to conservative judges. But the real challenge is not to persuade courts; it is to build a political movement powerful enough to make courts irrelevant—or to remake them entirely. That is a project that will require more than historical retrieval and better legal briefs. It will require a sustained, multi-generational struggle for democracy, in which constitutional arguments are a tool, not a destination.

  3. 03Progressives must prioritize muscular court reforms, such as expansion or term limits, to democratize the judiciary and prevent it from functioning as an instrument of oligarchy.
  4. 04Historical constitutional transformations, such as the New Deal, were the result of raw political power and mass mobilization rather than the success of legal arguments within the courtroom.
  5. 05Constitutional claims should be used as a tool to build a movement powerful enough to restructure or bypass the judiciary rather than as a primary means to persuade hostile judges.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While reclaiming the democracy-of-opportunity tradition is a valuable rhetorical strategy, it will fail unless progressives prioritize structural court reform to strip the current conservative supermajority of its power to veto egalitarian legislation."

The lead essay is a learned and impassioned call for progressives to stop ceding constitutional ground to the right. Its authors, an eminent group of legal scholars, argue that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition shows that the Constitution not only permits but demands a broad distribution of economic and political power. They urge progressives to reclaim the First Amendment for collective action, reforge the link between racial justice and political economy, and recognize that antitrust and corporate law are constitutional questions. This is a valuable intervention. But for all its richness, the essay suffers from a critical blind spot: it has almost nothing to say about the structure of the judiciary itself. The strategy of constitutional reclamation presupposes that courts are open to persuasion—or at least susceptible to long-term shifts in legal culture. Yet the current Supreme Court is a 6–3 conservative supermajority that is not merely unsympathetic to progressive arguments but actively hostile to the very notion of democratic regulation of the economy. Before progressives can make their politics constitutional again, they must first make the Constitution’s interpreters accountable to democracy. That requires structural court reform, and it requires it now.

The lead essay acknowledges the challenge. It notes that the Court is “archconservative” and that it is overturning precedents at an accelerating pace. It references the leaked draft opinion in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, which signals the end of Roe. Yet the solutions it offers are almost entirely discursive: make better constitutional arguments in public, pass legislation with constitutional findings, and prepare to contest hostile rulings “both inside and outside the courts.” This is like advising a patient with a broken leg to walk it off. The conservative majority has shown that it is willing to ignore not only progressive arguments but also the basic constraints of precedent, text, and institutional legitimacy. In the 2021 term alone, the Court issued radical decisions on abortion, gun rights, the administrative state, and environmental regulation. In each case, the progressive justices filed powerful dissents; in each case, the conservative majority was unmoved. The idea that a revived constitutional rhetoric will somehow alter this dynamic is wishful thinking.

What then is to be done? The lead essay flirts with the idea when it mentions that “some liberals and progressives have proposed judicial reforms,” but it dismisses these as attempts to “restore an imagined past of judicial nonpartisanship.” That is a straw man. The most serious reform proposals are not about making the Court apolitical; they are about democratizing it. Court expansion—adding seats to the Supreme Court—is a blunt but constitutionally permissible tool, used several times in the nineteenth century and seriously threatened by Franklin Roosevelt. Term limits for justices, which would require a constitutional amendment or a creative statutory workaround, would end the lottery of life tenure that now gives small political minorities outsized influence over the judiciary for decades. Jurisdiction stripping, authorized by Article III, would allow Congress to remove certain subjects from the Court’s review entirely, restoring to the elected branches the final say on matters like campaign finance or health care. A supermajority requirement for judicial review, adopted in some other democracies, would prevent a bare majority of justices from overturning legislation that has passed both houses of Congress and been signed by the president.

These reforms are not about restoring a mythical golden age. They are about confronting the reality that the Supreme Court, as currently constituted, is a veto point for oligarchic interests. The lead essay itself demonstrates this: it shows how the Court has systematically dismantled the legislative tools that might counter concentrated wealth. If that is the case, then the first order of business is not to persuade the Court to adopt a different constitutional vision, but to limit the Court’s ability to enforce its current one. Progressive constitutional rhetoric has an important role to play here. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition implies that the Court itself must be constrained when it becomes an instrument of oligarchy. In the 1930s, FDR argued that the “nine old men” were blocking the will of the people and threatened to expand the Court. He lost the legislative battle but won the political war: the Court retreated, and a new constitutional settlement emerged. That lesson should not be forgotten. The current moment is analogous, but this time progressives must be prepared to follow through.

The lead essay’s reticence on court reform is symptomatic of a deeper ambivalence about the role of judicial power. The authors want progressives to argue that the Constitution compels a more egalitarian political economy, but they also seem to want courts to remain as the ultimate arbiters of those arguments. This is a contradiction. If the Court is truly captured by a conservative ideology that treats economic inequality as a natural right, then no amount of constitutional argument will win the day. The only viable path is to change the composition or the power of the court itself. That will require a political battle that goes far beyond law reviews and op-ed pages. It will require abolishing or reforming the filibuster, winning elections with clear mandates for court reform, and organizing mass movements that demand judicial accountability. The lead essay’s proposals—public financing, labor law reform, wealth taxes—all depend on a Congress that can pass them and a Court that will uphold them. But as the authors acknowledge, the Court will “inevitably” challenge these laws. So the question is not whether to challenge the Court, but how.

One might object that court expansion is too radical, that it would provoke a tit-for-tat escalation and destroy whatever legitimacy the Court retains. But the Court’s legitimacy is already in freefall, not because progressives threaten it, but because the conservative majority openly acts as a partisan actor. The leaked Dobbs draft, the rushed confirmations, the appearance of justices at Federalist Society galas—all of this has shattered the myth of judicial neutrality. In such a context, inaction risks more than action. If progressives do nothing, the Court will continue to entrench minority rule for a generation. The lead essay’s strategy, for all its elegance, amounts to hoping that a better historical narrative can counteract the brute force of five or six votes. It cannot.

This is not to say that constitutional reclamation has no value. It absolutely does. When progressives argue, as the PRO Act’s proponents do, that the First Amendment protects the right to strike and boycott, they shift the terms of debate. They create a vocabulary for legislators and citizens to understand what is at stake. They lay the groundwork for a future, more sympathetic judiciary. But these are long-term investments. In the short term, they are no match for Samuel Alito and Clarence Thomas. The immediate task is to prevent the Court from doing irreparable harm—to voting rights, to labor law, to the planet itself. That requires structural reform, not just rhetorical contestation.

The lead essay’s authors are among the most insightful constitutional thinkers of our time. Their book, The Anti-Oligarchy Constitution, is a major work. But in this forum, they have underplayed the institutional preconditions of their own project. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition will not be revived in the courts until the courts themselves are made responsive to democracy. That means progressives must be willing to wield power, not just arguments, against a judiciary that has abandoned all pretense of restraint. The Constitution is not just a text to be interpreted; it is a system of power. And until progressives are willing to contest power with power, all the constitutional arguments in the world will remain elegant briefs for a case that has already been lost.
