"While conflict can be transformative for solidarity, it is often destructive in practice, necessitating that organizations supplement conflict tolerance with emotional literacy and robust relational structures."

I want to push back against a certain romanticism in Mie Inouye's treatment of conflict and conflict tolerance. The essay tends to present conflict as potentially transformative, as a site of learning and growth. But conflict can also be destructive, and the line between productive and destructive conflict is not as clear as Inouye suggests.

My concern emerges from experience in organizations that embraced conflict as a value. In my twenties, I was active in a left organization that prided itself on democratic deliberation and the open expression of disagreement. The theoretical framework was compelling: internal conflict would check domination, transform participants, and make the organization stronger. In practice, the organization was paralyzed by factionalism and consumed by internal warfare. People burned out, left, and developed lasting grievances against former comrades.

This experience taught me that conflict is not inherently productive. Whether conflict is transformative or destructive depends on the organizational context in which it occurs: the relationships that exist before conflict arises, the institutional structures that channel conflict toward resolution, the norms that govern how disagreement is expressed, and the stakes that participants attach to outcomes. Without these conditions, conflict can destroy organizations as surely as any external attack.

Inouye acknowledges this concern when she writes that "even if conflict can be productively transformative, it doesn't always have that effect." She draws on DuFord's distinction between "realistic" and "unrealistic" conflict. But she notes that "this distinction may be plausible in principle, but it is less plausible in practice." This is a significant concession. If the distinction between productive and destructive conflict cannot reliably be made in practice, then the argument for cultivating conflict tolerance becomes much more complicated.

What are organizers to do when they cannot know in advance whether a particular conflict will be productive or destructive? Inouye's answer is that we should "cultivate the capacity to show up" and "embrace organizing as a mechanism of political education." But this answer sidesteps the question of what to do when showing up means participating in conflicts that feel petty, draining, or even abusive.

I want to suggest that what is missing from Inouye's account is an account of emotional literacy in organizing. Most conflict in organizations is not purely strategic; it is also emotional. Participants bring histories of trauma, patterns of relating, and interpersonal needs that shape how they engage with disagreement. Organizations that ignore these dimensions of conflict often find that strategic disagreements quickly become personal conflicts, and personal conflicts quickly become organizational collapse.

This suggests that conflict tolerance must be supplemented by practices of emotional support and skill-building. Organizations need to develop the capacity to hold conflict without reproducing abuse, to express disagreement without destroying relationships, to recognize when conflict is becoming unproductive and intervene appropriately. These capacities cannot be assumed; they must be cultivated through explicit training and ongoing attention.

Inouye's essay is valuable for challenging the tendency to avoid conflict in the name of unity. But we should not replace one extreme with another. The goal is not to eliminate conflict or to embrace all conflict as potentially transformative. The goal is to develop organizational cultures that can hold conflict productively, which requires attention to the emotional and relational dimensions of disagreement that purely strategic analyses neglect.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The distinction between productive 'realistic' conflict and destructive 'unrealistic' conflict is difficult to maintain in practice, making the romanticization of conflict risky for organizational stability.
  2. 02Conflict is not inherently productive and can lead to factionalism, burnout, and permanent grievances if the proper institutional norms and pre-existing relationships are not in place.
  3. 03Inouye’s focus on 'showing up' and 'social endurance' fails to address how organizers should respond to conflicts that are petty, draining, or abusive.
  4. 04Organizational conflict is deeply emotional and rooted in personal histories of trauma, meaning strategic success requires explicit training in emotional support and interpersonal skill-building.
  5. 05Effective solidarity requires developing organizational cultures that can distinguish between healthy disagreement and relational collapse through ongoing attention to the emotional dimensions of organizing.
