Diversified MiniMax

"The events of September 11 demonstrate that the United States should revitalize the founders' vision of a citizen-based militia to ensure national defense is both effective against distributed threats and compatible with republican freedom."

Elaine Scarry's invocation of the Second Amendment and the militia tradition points toward a dimension of the defense debate that has been largely neglected in mainstream discussions. The founders' commitment to an armed citizenry as a check on standing armies reflected deep concerns about the relationship between military power and political freedom. Scarry is right to suggest that this tradition deserves reconsideration in light of September 11.

The original case for the militia was not simply that citizens could defend themselves against external enemies, but that a dispersed military force was less likely to be turned against the population itself. Standing armies under executive control had been instruments of tyranny throughout European history; citizen militias, accountable to the communities they served, were thought to be inherently more compatible with republican government. The Second Amendment was designed to preserve this capacity, not merely to protect hunting rights or individual self-defense.

This tradition has been largely forgotten in the professionalization of the American military. The transformation of the militia into the National Guard, and the separation of the Guard from meaningful civilian participation in its command, represents a drift away from the founders' vision. What Scarry's analysis suggests is that this drift has costs beyond the constitutional ones she identifies: it has created a defense establishment that is structurally incapable of responding to threats that require distributed rather than centralized action.

The return to a genuinely citizen-based defense would require not just structural changes but a transformation in how Americans understand their relationship to national security. It would mean abandoning the assumption that defense is something done by professionals on behalf of a passive population, and embracing the idea that citizenship entails responsibilities that extend to the most consequential decisions a society can make. This is a daunting prospect, but September 11 revealed the costs of the current arrangement in a way that demands serious consideration of alternatives.

The implementation of this vision would require careful attention to the relationship between citizen participation and military effectiveness. Not all citizens can be soldiers, and military operations require coordination that is difficult to achieve through purely democratic processes. But the founders understood that some friction between democratic deliberation and military efficiency was not a bug but a feature—the protection against the slide toward tyranny that efficient military organization inevitably produces.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The original intent of the Second Amendment was to prevent tyranny by ensuring military power remained dispersed among the citizenry rather than concentrated in a standing army under executive control.
  2. 02The professionalization of the military and the centralization of the National Guard have created a defense establishment that is structurally incapable of responding to threats requiring decentralized action.
  3. 03True national security requires Americans to abandon the role of a passive population and reclaim the responsibilities of citizenship regarding the most consequential decisions of the state.
  4. 04While a citizen-based defense may be less efficient than a professional one, the founders viewed this friction as a necessary safeguard against the inevitable slide toward tyranny produced by centralized military organization.
Diversified MiniMax

"The success of the Flight 93 passengers demonstrates that national security policy should be restructured to empower and involve citizens as active participants in a distributed defense model rather than treating them as passive beneficiaries of centralized military protection."

Scarry's essay raises the question of what practical lessons should be drawn from the events of September 11. Her argument that the failure of the Pentagon defense and the success of Flight 93 demonstrate the superiority of distributed over centralized defense is compelling, but it requires translation into specific policy recommendations if it is to have any effect on the choices facing policymakers.

The most immediate lesson concerns airline security. If the defense of aircraft depends on the passengers themselves, then the system of security screening must be designed with this in mind. Current measures focus on preventing weapons from boarding aircraft, but they do little to equip passengers for the role they may need to play. Better communication systems on aircraft, clearer protocols for passenger response to security threats, and training for flight crews in managing hostile situations would all strengthen the distributed defense capacity that proved its worth on Flight 93.

Beyond airline security, the question of how to involve citizens more directly in defense planning deserves attention. Scarry suggests that the views of Pentagon analysts and Joint Chiefs of Staff should be shared more widely with the public, and that public deliberation about defense matters should be genuine rather than formal. This would require changes in how classified information is handled, how defense budgets are discussed, and how military options are presented to the public. It would also require citizens to accept greater responsibilities for understanding security issues, not just as voters but as participants in the ongoing work of national defense.

The longer-term agenda Scarry outlines—return to conventional forces, abandonment of nuclear weapons, revival of citizen militia tradition—is more controversial and will be harder to achieve. But the events of September 11 have created a window for rethinking assumptions that have governed defense policy for fifty years. Whether we take advantage of this opportunity depends on whether we can move beyond the immediate security concerns of the moment to engage in the kind of fundamental reflection that Scarry's essay invites.

The passengers on Flight 93 succeeded not because they had special capabilities but because they exercised the judgment and took the responsibility that citizenship entails. If their example is to mean more than memorial, it must become the basis for a politics that treats all citizens as potential defenders of the republic, not merely as beneficiaries of the protection provided by professionals. This is a difficult but necessary step toward a defense policy that is worthy of a democratic society.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Airline security protocols should be redesigned to equip passengers with better communication systems and clearer response protocols to strengthen their inherent defensive capacity.
  2. 02National defense planning must become more transparent and deliberative, requiring changes in how classified information and military budgets are shared with the public.
  3. 03Citizens must accept a greater responsibility for understanding and participating in security issues to move beyond a reliance on professional military managers.
  4. 04The events of September 11 provide a necessary opportunity to challenge long-standing defense assumptions and shift toward a policy that treats all citizens as potential defenders of the republic.
Diversified MiniMax

"The United States must transition from a centralized nuclear-based defense to a democratic, conventional military model because nuclear deterrence is structurally incompatible with constitutional governance and ineffective against modern threats."

Elaine Scarry's argument about nuclear weapons deserves particular scrutiny, for it gets at something that has been largely absent from American political discourse: the incompatibility between nuclear deterrence and democratic governance. Her observation that Europeans refer to nuclear weapons as "monarchic" captures a fundamental truth that American discussions have been reluctant to acknowledge.

The logic of nuclear deterrence requires that decision-making authority be concentrated in a single executive, capable of responding within minutes to incoming threats. This concentration is not incidental but structural: any system that requires human judgment at the speed of nuclear weapons delivery must simplify chain-of-command and restrict consultation to the minimum necessary. The result is a form of executive power that stands outside the normal constitutional order, answerable to no one in the critical minutes when decisions must be made.

This arrangement was tolerable during the Cold War when the adversary was a state with similarly concentrated nuclear authority, and when the existential stakes made the tradeoff between democratic accountability and survival seem acceptable. But it becomes indefensible in the post-Cold War environment where the primary threats are non-state actors and where the temptation to use nuclear weapons in situations of less than existential threat grows. Scarry's reference to advice given to Tony Blair about threatening nuclear wipeout of states that harbor terrorists points to a mode of thinking that treats nuclear weapons as just another instrument of state policy, rather than as weapons of existential consequence.

The solution Scarry proposes—a return to conventional military forces, supported by citizen conscription—is ambitious but necessary. The connection she draws between nuclear disarmament and the spread of nuclear weapons to other nations deserves more attention than it has received. America cannot credibly demand that other states forgo nuclear weapons while maintaining the largest nuclear arsenal in the world and a doctrine that includes first-use options. If we believe that nuclear weapons are uniquely dangerous, we must be willing to give them up ourselves, not just to restrict their proliferation to others.

This is a long-term project, but September 11 has demonstrated that the current arrangements are both dangerous and ineffective. A military based on conventional forces and citizen participation would be more accountable, more sustainable, and ultimately more secure than one built on the foundation of nuclear terror.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Nuclear weapons function as 'monarchic' tools that necessitate an unconstitutional concentration of executive power by bypassing democratic consultation in favor of rapid, unilateral decision-making.
  2. 02The Cold War justification for centralized nuclear authority is no longer valid in a post-Cold War era where threats come from non-state actors and nuclear use is increasingly treated as a standard policy tool.
  3. 03The U.S. cannot effectively lead global non-proliferation efforts while maintaining a massive arsenal and a doctrine of nuclear first-use.
  4. 04A return to conventional forces and citizen conscription is necessary to restore democratic accountability and ensure that the decision to go to war is tested by the citizenry.
