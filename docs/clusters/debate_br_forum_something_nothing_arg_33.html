Diversified Gemini

"Nir Rosen’s dismissal of the new counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy in Afghanistan is premature and based on a misunderstanding of the strategy's long-term mechanics and strategic necessity."

Nir Rosen’s bleak assessment of the war in Afghanistan captures the raw frustrations of American soldiers and the severe dysfunction of Afghan security forces. His dispatch from Highway 601 is a visceral reminder of the friction of war. However, in his rush to declare the new counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy dead on arrival, Rosen substitutes battlefield anecdotes for strategic analysis and fundamentally misunderstands the purpose, mechanics, and timeline of population-centric operations.

To read Rosen, one would think that General Stanley McChrystal and his advisory team are blind to the realities of Helmand Province, unaware of the corruption in the Afghan National Police (ANP), and stubbornly trying to force an Iraq template onto a completely different country. This is a profound mischaracterization. The very reason President Obama recently authorized the deployment of 30,000 additional troops—and why McChrystal fought so hard for them—is precisely because the status quo Rosen vividly describes was failing. COIN is not a magic wand that instantly transforms corrupt police into elite forces; it is a grueling, long-term framework designed to reverse the exact downward spiral Rosen documents.

Rosen’s central error is his impatience. He evaluates a COIN strategy that has barely begun as if it were already in its mature phase. He mocks the Afghan security forces for their incompetence and highlights the frustrations of Team Prowler as evidence that state-building is a fool’s errand. But creating a capable indigenous force is the hardest part of any counterinsurgency. It takes years, not months. The U.S. military’s experience in El Salvador, the Philippines, and indeed Iraq, demonstrates that early iterations of local forces are almost always heavily compromised by corruption, poor leadership, and tactical ineptitude.

The fact that the ANP in Helmand are currently struggling is not an argument against training them; it is an argument for resourcing that training properly. For the first six years of the Afghan war, the United States attempted to fight a counterterrorism campaign on the cheap. We relied on warlords, ignored the rampant corruption in Kabul, and underfunded the Afghan National Army and Police. What Rosen is witnessing is the toxic legacy of Donald Rumsfeld’s "light footprint" approach, not the failure of McChrystal’s COIN doctrine. You cannot starve an institution of resources and mentorship for a decade and then point to its weakness as proof that the new rescue plan is flawed.

Furthermore, Rosen’s dismissal of the Iraq analogy is highly selective. It is entirely true that Afghanistan in 2010 is not Iraq in 2007. The demographic distribution is different, the urban-rural divide is different, and the educational baseline of the population is vastly lower. McChrystal’s team knows this. But the foundational principle of COIN—that the population is the center of gravity—remains universally applicable. Rosen claims that population protection is impossible in Afghanistan because the people are spread out across thousands of remote villages. But COIN does not require building a wall around every hamlet. It requires securing the key population centers, the vital economic corridors (like Highway 601), and the agricultural hubs. By protecting the major districts in Helmand and Kandahar, the coalition can break the Taliban’s momentum and disrupt their shadow governance.

Rosen also drastically misrepresents the nature of the Taliban and their relationship with the Afghan people. He suggests that the Taliban only attack those who collaborate with the Americans, implying a sort of live-and-let-live dynamic for everyone else. This is a romanticized view of a brutal insurgency. The Taliban systematically assassinate tribal elders, burn down girls' schools, and terrorize communities into submission. They do not enjoy broad popular support; polling consistently shows that the overwhelming majority of Afghans loathe the Taliban. They rule through intimidation and the exploitation of the justice vacuum left by the corrupt Kabul government. Providing an alternative to that vacuum is the core of McChrystal’s strategy.

Perhaps most dangerously, Rosen attempts to minimize the threat of al-Qaeda, arguing that their presence in Pakistan makes Afghanistan irrelevant, and questioning whether a "few hundred angry, unsophisticated Muslim extremists" really pose a danger to the United States. This cavalier dismissal of the global jihadist network is stunning. Al-Qaeda is not merely a collection of angry men; it is a resilient, adaptive organization that thrives in ungoverned spaces. If the United States and its allies abandon Afghanistan to a Taliban resurgence, the psychological and operational victory for al-Qaeda would be immense. The Taliban have shown no willingness to sever ties with al-Qaeda, and the porous border with Pakistan means that a Taliban-controlled Afghanistan would instantly become strategic depth for the architects of 9/11.

Rosen rightly points out that the American military struggles with the cultural nuances required for effective COIN. It is true that soldiers who just want to survive their deployments resent being asked to be "Wyatt Earp and Mother Theresa." But this is a failure of institutional training, not a flaw in the doctrine itself. The U.S. military has made massive strides since 2006 in adapting its language, cultural training, and operational design to the realities of counterinsurgency. The revised Rules of Engagement, which Rosen’s embedded soldiers complain about, are absolutely essential. Yes, they increase the short-term tactical risk to conventional forces, but they mitigate the devastating strategic risk of civilian casualties. Every civilian killed by an errant airstrike is a recruiting poster for the insurgency. Restricting firepower is not political correctness; it is tactical necessity.

Ultimately, Rosen’s essay offers a counsel of despair. He provides a granular, compelling picture of the localized failures of the Afghan state, but his only implicit solution is withdrawal. He argues that addressing the "root causes" of anti-Americanism—such as U.S. foreign policy in the Middle East—would do more than military intervention. While resolving global grievances is a noble goal, it does nothing to stop the Taliban from executing a schoolteacher in Kandahar tomorrow.

We are in Afghanistan because the consequences of failure are unacceptable. The COIN strategy adopted by President Obama is the first time in eight years that resources are being aligned with objectives. It will be bloody, it will be frustrating, and there will be many more days on Highway 601 where progress seems impossible. But dismissing the entire endeavor because of the profound difficulties of the transition phase is historically shortsighted. Counterinsurgency is the slow, grinding work of building something functional out of the ashes of a broken state. It is not "something from nothing"—it is the painstaking assembly of security and governance, piece by piece.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The dysfunction Rosen observes in the Afghan security forces is a legacy of past underfunding and the 'light footprint' approach rather than a failure of the current COIN doctrine.
  2. 02Counterinsurgency is a grueling, multi-year process, and it is logically flawed to judge its ultimate success based on the incompetence of local forces during the early transition phase.
  3. 03While Afghanistan differs from Iraq, the foundational COIN principle of prioritizing population protection remains the only viable way to break the Taliban's momentum and provide an alternative to their rule by intimidation.
  4. 04The restrictive Rules of Engagement are a tactical necessity to mitigate the strategic damage caused by civilian casualties, which serve as a primary recruiting tool for the insurgency.
  5. 05Rosen dangerously underestimates the threat of al-Qaeda and the strategic depth they would gain if the United States abandoned Afghanistan to a Taliban resurgence.
  6. 06Addressing the root causes of global anti-Americanism is a long-term diplomatic goal that does not solve the immediate, practical need to provide security and governance to the Afghan people.
