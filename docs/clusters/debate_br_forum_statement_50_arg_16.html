"Participatory democracy should be understood not as a romanticized mood of uprising or a theatrical manifesto, but as a disciplined, durable set of habits and organizational practices learned through patient local work."

I share Tom Hayden’s affection for participatory democracy, but I want to defend it in a less exalted key. Participatory democracy is not primarily a manifesto, a slogan, or a mood of uprising. It is a set of habits. It is learned in rooms that are too hot, meetings that run too long, arguments that seem to go in circles, and relationships that survive disagreement. If the idea is to matter fifty years after Port Huron, it must be rescued from both nostalgia and theatricality.

Movements often begin with moral shock. A student is pepper-sprayed. A family is deported. A bank is bailed out while a homeowner is evicted. A tuition bill arrives. A young black man is killed and the authorities seem indifferent. Shock matters because it breaks the spell of normality. But movements do not grow through shock alone. They grow when people find one another, tell the truth about their lives, identify targets, develop leaders, and build enough trust to act together.

The Port Huron Statement’s language of alienation remains powerful because it describes a real loneliness in modern public life. Many people believe politics is something done to them or for them by professionals. They are consumers of news, recipients of policy, objects of polling, names on mailing lists. Participatory democracy says: no, you can sit at the table. But sitting at the table is only the beginning. The harder work is learning what to do there.

Occupy has been a great experiment in this regard. Its assemblies, working groups, hand signals, facilitation practices, and insistence on horizontalism have introduced thousands to the experience of direct political speech. That is no small achievement. People accustomed to being ignored have stood up and found an audience. People who thought of politics as voting every four years have learned to deliberate, cook, clean, marshal, livestream, write, march, and defend one another. The encampments created schools of citizenship.

They also revealed the limits of process as identity. A meeting can be formally open and practically inaccessible. Consensus can protect minority voices, but it can also produce paralysis. Facilitation can distribute power, but it can become its own expertise. Hand signals can make discussion more orderly, but they can also alienate newcomers who feel they have entered a subculture. A movement that spends too much time discussing how to discuss risks mistaking procedure for politics.

Good organizing starts not with the meeting but with the relationship. The civil rights movement had mass meetings, but it also had churches, porches, beauty shops, student networks, local leaders, and years of patient work. The farm workers’ movement had marches and fasts, but also house meetings, boycotts, organizers, and disciplined membership. The best community organizing traditions begin by listening: What keeps you up at night? Who has the power to change it? What are you willing to do? Who else should be here? This is participatory democracy in its plainest and most durable form.

Hayden’s inside-outside strategy is important because it prevents two common errors. The first is insider fatalism: the belief that change comes only through elections, legislation, litigation, and expert negotiation. This leaves ordinary people as spectators and produces weak reforms easily reversed. The second is outsider romanticism: the belief that moral witness alone transforms power. This produces beautiful defeats. The art is in the combination. A protest should open a door. An organizer should know who can walk through it, what demand they will make, and how they will bring others along.

The Americans with Disabilities Act, which Hayden mentions, is a useful example. The public remembers, if it remembers at all, the law and perhaps a few dramatic images of activists crawling up the Capitol steps. But behind such moments lay years of organizing by disabled people who challenged not only inaccessible buildings but paternalistic assumptions. They developed policy knowledge, built coalitions, pressured officials, and changed public expectations. Direct action dramatized the issue; organization carried it.

The same lesson applies to immigrant youth. The courage of undocumented students coming out publicly cannot be overstated. But their power comes not only from courage. It comes from networks that train, protect, message, and strategize. “Undocumented and unafraid” is a moral declaration, but it is also an organizing achievement. People become brave together.

Participatory democracy must also include accountability. Movements are often good at challenging the accountability of officials and bad at creating their own. Who controls money? Who speaks to the press? Who negotiates with allies? Who handles accusations of abuse within the movement? Who decides when a tactic is too risky for those most vulnerable? If the answer is “everyone,” the real answer may be “whoever already has influence.” Democratic groups need clear roles, rotation, transparency, and ways to remove people from responsibility when necessary. Structure is not the enemy of participation; bad structure is.

Leadership should be treated the same way. Fear of leaders is understandable, especially given the betrayals and egos that litter movement history. But leaderless movements often hide leadership rather than abolish it. Some people have more experience, charisma, free time, access to media, or control over communication channels. The question is whether leadership is accountable and whether it multiplies the leadership of others. A good leader asks, “Who is not in the room? Who can take this on next time? What do you think we should do?” A bad leader says “we” and means “I.”

Hayden’s broad coalition is attractive, but coalitions require craft. They are not built by assuming everyone’s issue fits naturally under one umbrella. Unions, environmentalists, immigrant groups, racial justice organizers, civil libertarians, faith communities, students, and neighborhood associations often have different cultures, timelines, and definitions of victory. Coalition work requires translation. It requires knowing when to compromise and when not to. It requires showing up for others before asking them to show up for you. Participatory democracy is less a sea with many currents than a set of bridges that must be maintained.

One of the most promising areas today is local democratic experimentation. Participatory budgeting, neighborhood assemblies, community benefits agreements, school organizing, tenant unions, and local media projects may seem modest compared with national mobilizations. But they teach people that democracy can change material conditions. A parent who helps win a safer school crossing, a tenant who joins neighbors to force repairs, a worker who helps recover stolen wages—these experiences build confidence. They are the seedbed of larger politics.

This does not mean abandoning national struggles. Climate change, immigration, war, student debt, health care, and financial regulation require national and even global action. But national action is strongest when rooted in local organization. Otherwise movements become brands. They surge, trend, and disappear.

The Port Huron Statement at fifty should remind us that democracy is not only a demand we make on the state. It is a practice we undertake with one another. It requires patience, humility, humor, memory, and a tolerance for imperfection. It asks us to become the kind of people who can govern ourselves, not just denounce those who govern badly.

Hayden’s ghosts are real. But ghosts cannot organize. The living must do that, one conversation, one meeting, one campaign, one institution at a time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Movements require more than the moral shock of injustice; they must build trust and identify specific targets through long-term relationships rather than just meetings.
  2. 02While horizontalism and consensus-based processes can empower the ignored, they risk becoming exclusionary subcultures that prioritize procedural purity over political effectiveness.
  3. 03An effective 'inside-outside' strategy requires combining the moral witness of protest with the organizational capacity to negotiate policy and navigate institutional power.
  4. 04True democratic participation requires clear structures of accountability and leadership to prevent informal power dynamics from replacing transparent decision-making.
  5. 05Building broad coalitions is a craft that requires active maintenance and translation between different group cultures rather than an assumption of natural unity.
  6. 06Local democratic experiments, such as participatory budgeting and tenant unions, serve as essential training grounds where citizens gain the confidence and skills necessary for national political struggles.
  7. 07Democracy is a practice of self-governance that demands the patience and humility to move beyond denouncing bad leaders toward the collective responsibility of governing ourselves.
