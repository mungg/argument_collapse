"The Ferguson case demonstrates that the criminal justice system is poorly designed to judge police killings with the independence and skepticism required to hold officers accountable."

Ferguson does not prove that every police shooting is a crime. It does show that the criminal justice system is poorly designed to judge police killings with the independence and skepticism such cases require.

Police officers face real dangers, and the law properly gives them latitude to make split-second decisions. Under Supreme Court doctrine, the question is not whether an officer was right in hindsight but whether his fear and use of force were objectively reasonable in the moment. That is a necessary protection. We should not want officers hesitating when a life is genuinely at risk.

But that standard has too often become a nearly insurmountable shield. When an unarmed person is dead, the officer’s account may be the most important surviving narrative. Prosecutors, who work daily with police, are then asked to investigate and indict their professional partners. Grand juries almost always follow prosecutors’ lead, yet in police cases prosecutors may present evidence in a way that resembles a trial for acquittal rather than a request for charges. Even if no misconduct occurs, the conflict of interest is obvious and corrosive.

The broader numbers deepen that concern. Hundreds of people are killed by police each year, with young black men disproportionately represented. It strains belief to think that virtually every questionable shooting is legally blameless. The low rate of charges reflects not only the difficulty of proving criminal intent, but also institutional deference: to the badge, to claims of fear, and to a culture that often treats police violence as regrettable but presumptively justified.

The answer is not to criminalize honest mistakes or pretend policing is easy. It is to create procedures the public can trust: independent prosecutors for police killings, mandatory public reporting of all police uses of deadly force, body and dashboard cameras with clear rules, and tighter training and policy on when officers may shoot. Civil remedies and departmental discipline also need to be real, not symbolic.

Police are not criticized too much when they kill; they are scrutinized too little by institutions with power. Ferguson’s anger is not simply about one grand jury. It is about a system that asks communities to accept death, secrecy and deference as justice.

"The Ferguson grand jury decision highlights a structural system designed to defer to police officers at every stage, creating a pattern of impunity rather than simple leniency."

The Ferguson grand jury's refusal to indict Darren Wilson does point to a real structural problem, but the problem is less that officers are "let off easily" and more that the system used to evaluate police shootings is designed to defer to officers at almost every stage. That deference, taken cumulatively, produces outcomes that look like impunity even when individual decisions are defensible.

Start with the legal standard. Under Tennessee v. Garner and Graham v. Connor, an officer's use of deadly force is judged by what a reasonable officer would have perceived in the moment, not by what we know after the fact. That standard is appropriate in principle — split-second decisions shouldn't be second-guessed with 20/20 hindsight — but in practice it gives officers a doctrinal cushion no civilian defendant enjoys.

Layer onto that the institutional incumbencies. Local prosecutors depend on police cooperation for every other case they try, and they know it. When prosecutor Bob McCulloch presented essentially all the evidence to the grand jury and let Wilson testify uncrossed for four hours, he was doing something he would never do for an ordinary suspect. Grand juries indict, the saying goes, ham sandwiches; they apparently do not indict officers. The asymmetry is the story.

Then add the data gap. The FBI's 461 "justifiable homicides" is a voluntary count from a fraction of departments; the real number is unknown, as is the racial breakdown. ProPublica's 21-to-1 disparity is a striking estimate precisely because the government itself doesn't track these killings reliably. A society serious about either exonerating or charging officers would at minimum require uniform reporting.

So the answer isn't that cops face too little criticism or too much. It is that we have built a system in which the same office that works with police decides whether to prosecute them, under a legal standard tilted toward their account, with data too thin to evaluate the pattern. Ferguson didn't create that system; it exposed it.

The Ferguson grand jury decision reveals something more fundamental than whether Darren Wilson personally deserved indictment: it exposes a structural problem in how American prosecutors handle police shootings. Grand juries indict in well over 99 percent of federal cases brought before them. The old adage that a prosecutor can "indict a ham sandwich" exists for a reason. Yet when the defendant is a police officer, that near-automatic process suddenly becomes an elaborate, months-long presentation of every conceivable piece of exculpatory evidence, with the prosecutor functioning less like an advocate for charges and more like a defense attorney.

This isn't accidental. Local prosecutors depend on local police every single day to build cases, testify at trials, and provide investigative manpower. Asking a county prosecutor to vigorously pursue charges against an officer in his own jurisdiction is asking him to attack the institution he relies on to do his job. Robert McCulloch's handling of the Wilson case—dumping a mountain of evidence on grand jurors without recommending charges, allowing the target to testify for four hours unchallenged—was unusual in form but predictable in motive.

The fix is not to lower the legal standard for charging officers, which would create its own injustices. The fix is structural: independent special prosecutors, drawn from outside the jurisdiction, should handle every case involving a police killing. Several states have considered such reforms, and Wisconsin recently passed a law requiring outside investigation of officer-involved deaths. Some jurisdictions have proposed standing civilian review boards with prosecutorial authority.

We should also be honest that the substantive law gives officers tremendous latitude. Under Tennessee v. Garner and Graham v. Connor, the question isn't whether the officer was right but whether his perception of threat was "objectively reasonable" in the moment. That's a forgiving standard, and probably should be somewhat forgiving—we ask officers to make split-second decisions in dangerous situations. But "forgiving" has shaded into "essentially unaccountable," especially when the dead man is black and the witnesses are poor.

So yes: Ferguson shows that cops who kill get off too easily, but not because individual prosecutors are corrupt or individual juries are racist. It shows that the entire institutional apparatus for evaluating these killings is built to produce non-indictments. Until we separate the investigation of police violence from the police themselves and from the prosecutors who work alongside them daily, the pattern will continue, and communities will keep losing faith in a system that appears to grade officers on a different curve.
