"Doping should not be allowed in sports because the imperfection of current testing is not a reason to surrender the aspiration of fair competition."

Do not mistake imperfection for futility. The fact that some athletes still use banned substances is not a reason to stop testing; it is the main reason testing must continue. Every rule in sport is violated sometimes. Runners false-start, linemen hold, boxers hit low, and cyclists have doped. We do not therefore abolish starting guns, referees, weight classes or finish lines.

The strongest case for anti-doping rules is not romantic purity. Elite sport is already unnatural in many ways: athletes use altitude tents, sports science, legal supplements, costly coaching and biomechanical analysis. The issue is whether the contest remains one in which talent, training, judgment and courage are the central variables, rather than access to a pharmacological arms race. EPO is a clear example. It can improve endurance by increasing red blood cell production, but it can also thicken blood and raise serious health risks. If it is permitted, the clean athlete is no longer simply choosing “natural” competition; he is choosing to compete at a medically engineered disadvantage.

That choice would not remain confined to Olympians. The habits of professional sport flow downward. If doping becomes accepted at the top, the pressure reaches college, high school and even younger athletes long before they are capable of weighing the risks. The question is not whether a 28-year-old champion may take a calculated risk, but whether an entire athletic culture should make drug use a condition of realistic ambition.

None of this means every current rule is wise. Marijuana in judo is not the same problem as EPO in race walking, and anti-doping authorities damage their credibility when they blur performance enhancement, recreational use and genuine safety concerns. Nor should an “unbelievable” swim be treated as evidence of guilt. Suspicion is not proof.

But those are arguments for better enforcement, not surrender. Testing should be smarter, more targeted, more transparent and more proportionate. Biological passports, out-of-competition testing and improved laboratory methods are imperfect, but they deter some cheating and catch some cheaters. Fair competition is always an aspiration, never a completed fact. The right response to doping is not to abandon the aspiration. It is to defend it more intelligently.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The violation of rules by some athletes does not justify the abolition of those rules, just as the existence of fouls does not justify removing referees.
  2. 02Anti-doping rules ensure that athletic contests are decided by talent and training rather than by participation in a pharmacological arms race.
  3. 03Permitting performance-enhancing drugs forces clean athletes to choose between competing at a medically engineered disadvantage or taking serious health risks.
  4. 04Allowing doping at the professional level creates a dangerous culture where drug use becomes a prerequisite for success for younger athletes in college and high school.
  5. 05The flaws in current anti-doping enforcement should be addressed through smarter and more proportionate testing rather than by abandoning the system entirely.
