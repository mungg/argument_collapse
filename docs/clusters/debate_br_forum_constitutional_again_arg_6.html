"While constitutional argument is a necessary tool for legal defense, progressives should not make the U.S. Constitution the primary source of legitimacy for their movement because the document is historically and structurally entangled with systems of domination like white supremacy, empire, and capitalism."

The lead essay asks progressives to make politics constitutional again. It is an intelligent and historically rich argument, and it is right about one thing above all: liberal legalism has been a dead end. The fantasy that constitutional law is an apolitical craft practiced by neutral judges has left progressives defenseless before a conservative legal movement that never believed it. The Court is a political institution. Constitutional meaning is made through struggle. Economic power and racial domination are constitutional questions whether or not judges acknowledge them.

Yet I am uneasy with the proposed recovery. The essay invites progressives to return to an American “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, to say that the Constitution is the constitution of a republic rather than an oligarchy. But for many people in this country—and for many more subjected to U.S. power beyond its borders—the Constitution has always been both. It has contained emancipatory possibilities, yes. It has also organized conquest, slavery, settler colonialism, racial capitalism, empire, patriarchy, and exclusion. A progressive politics that seeks legitimacy mainly by mining constitutional tradition risks domesticating demands that should be more radical than the tradition can bear.

The authors know this. They do not present the Constitution as innocent. Aziz Rana’s work, in particular, has insisted that American democracy has often expanded for some through domination of others, and the lead essay briefly acknowledges that the “high tides” of democracy of opportunity coincided with territorial and global expansion. But the implications of that acknowledgment are not fully absorbed. If the historical moments from which progressives are supposed to draw inspiration were also moments of Indigenous dispossession, imperial expansion, anti-Black compromise, and exclusionary nationalism, then retrieval cannot be enough. We need not only an anti-oligarchy Constitution. We need a politics capable of confronting the Constitution’s own entanglement with oligarchy, empire, and racial rule.

Take the Jacksonian strand of the democracy-of-opportunity tradition. It spoke powerfully against concentrated financial privilege. It also helped build a white man’s democracy on Native removal and Black subordination. The Reconstruction strand is more compelling because it came closest to refounding the republic on equal citizenship. But Reconstruction was crushed not only by hostile courts and Southern violence; it was also abandoned by Northern capital and white political fatigue. The New Deal strand built social citizenship and labor power, but it did so through compromises that excluded many Black, Mexican American, Asian American, Indigenous, domestic, and agricultural workers. It expanded the administrative state while preserving racialized local control. It offered security to many while leaving the carceral and imperial state intact.

This history does not mean constitutional argument is useless. It means the Constitution is a dangerous home for emancipatory politics. Movements enter it seeking legitimacy and often emerge narrowed. Demands for abolition become demands for better procedure. Demands for economic democracy become calls for fair competition. Demands for reparations become race-neutral investment. Demands for decolonization become consultation. Demands for labor freedom become collective bargaining within employer-dominated capitalism. Constitutional translation can win reforms, but it can also sand off the edges of transformation.

The lead essay’s discussion of race and political economy illustrates both the promise and the danger. It is absolutely right that racial justice cannot be confined to antidiscrimination and voting rights. The wealth gap, health inequality, environmental racism, housing segregation, school funding, labor precarity, debt, policing, incarceration, and exposure to premature death are all part of the constitutional order in the broadest sense. The Movement for Black Lives and the Poor People’s Campaign understand this far better than the courts do. Their programs demand public investment, democratic control, demilitarization, labor rights, health care, housing, and repair.

But why must these demands be made primarily as constitutional claims? The moral force of reparations does not come from the fact that a clever lawyer can locate it in the Reconstruction amendments, though perhaps one can. It comes from centuries of stolen labor, land theft, state violence, exclusion from wealth-building, and ongoing extraction. The demand to defund police and invest in communities does not depend on whether current doctrine recognizes policing as political economy. It arises from the lived reality that policing and prisons manage inequality through organized violence. The demand for health care does not become more legitimate when redescribed as a requirement of republican citizenship. People need care because they are human beings.

There is a strategic answer: courts will attack reforms, so progressives need constitutional defenses. Fair enough. If Congress enacts voting rights legislation, labor reform, wealth taxes, or reparative programs, lawyers must defend them in constitutional terms. But legal defense is not the same as movement horizon. A movement that begins by asking what the Constitution can authorize may ask too little.

This is especially true of abolitionist politics. The lead essay mentions mass incarceration but folds it into a broader account of race and political economy. Yet prisons, policing, surveillance, borders, and punishment are not peripheral. They are central institutions of American political economy. They discipline labor, contain surplus populations, extract fines and fees, separate families, disenfranchise millions, and mark entire communities as governable through force. The Constitution has not merely failed to prevent this. It has often enabled it: the Thirteenth Amendment’s punishment clause, Fourth Amendment doctrine that permits vast police discretion, qualified immunity, cramped habeas review, deference to prison administrators, and immigration exceptionalism all help sustain the carceral state.

Can a democracy-of-opportunity constitutionalism confront this? Perhaps partially. It can say that republican freedom is incompatible with caste policing and mass civic death. It can support voting rights for incarcerated and formerly incarcerated people. It can defend public investment as an alternative to punishment. But abolition asks a deeper question: why should institutions built for domination be reformed rather than dismantled? That question does not fit easily within a tradition that often imagines inclusion into the republic as the answer. For people whom the republic has governed through cages, borders, reservations, and occupied neighborhoods, inclusion may not be enough.

Labor presents a similar tension. The lead essay rightly argues that unions are essential countervailing power and that the Court’s First Amendment has been turned against workers. The PRO Act, repeal of Taft-Hartley restrictions, protection for secondary boycotts, and recognition of gig workers are important reforms. But the older constitutional language of “industrial democracy” often stopped at collective bargaining within firms still owned and controlled by capital. Workers could bargain over wages and conditions, but investment decisions, automation, plant closures, supply chains, and ecological destruction remained largely private prerogatives.

A more radical labor politics would ask not only how workers can gain bargaining power but how production itself can be democratized. That includes unions, but also worker cooperatives, public ownership, sectoral bargaining, community control, just transition, and planning in the face of climate crisis. The Constitution may be made to tolerate some of this. But the scale of ecological emergency and capitalist instability may require institutional imagination beyond inherited constitutional categories.

The same is true of antitrust. The neo-Brandeisian revival is welcome insofar as it challenges corporate concentration and the poverty of consumer-welfare economics. But antimonopoly can become a small-business republicanism that romanticizes competition while leaving exploitation intact. Breaking giant firms into smaller firms does not necessarily give workers power, decarbonize production, decommodify care, or repair racial wealth extraction. A thousand petty tyrannies are not democracy. Sometimes public ownership, social provision, and democratic planning are better answers than competition. The anti-oligarchy tradition should not be allowed to shrink socialism into antitrust.

The lead essay’s constitutional frame also has little to say about empire. This is not a minor omission. U.S. political economy is global: supply chains, military bases, sanctions, debt regimes, migration controls, resource extraction, and climate emissions connect domestic prosperity to international hierarchy. The constitutional tradition the essay invokes was often built alongside expansion abroad and domination at the border. A politics of democracy of opportunity for U.S. citizens can coexist with exploitation of noncitizen workers, militarized borders, and sacrifice zones overseas. If progressives revive constitutional nationalism without internationalism, they may reproduce the old bargain: democracy at home for some, domination elsewhere.

What, then, should progressives do? Not abandon constitutional struggle. That would be foolish. Courts are real. Constitutional language moves people. The Reconstruction amendments remain powerful resources. The First Amendment should be reclaimed for protest, organizing, journalism, and dissent rather than surrendered to corporations. Congress should assert its enforcement powers. State constitutions can be sites of social rights. Popular constitutionalism can delegitimate reactionary courts.

But constitutional argument should be subordinate to movement politics, not the other way around. Movements should define freedom in terms of human need, democratic power, repair, ecological survival, and liberation from domination. Then lawyers can translate when translation is useful. The danger is when translation becomes authorship—when lawyers and scholars tell movements that their demands matter because they fit within a usable past. Sometimes the past is not usable enough. Sometimes the point is rupture.

This also means being honest about the Supreme Court. The current Court is not simply mistaken. It is an institution of minority rule, insulated from democratic accountability, shaped by elite legal networks and partisan hardball, and likely to obstruct much of what egalitarian movements seek. Persuading it with better history will rarely work. The answer must include court reform, jurisdiction stripping where appropriate, expansion if necessary, term limits if achievable, and open defiance of judicial supremacy by political branches willing to act. But even court reform is insufficient if the Senate, Electoral College, policing systems, corporate lobbying, and campaign finance remain engines of minority and oligarchic power.

The lead essay ends by saying the Constitution is the constitution of a republic, not an oligarchy. That is a noble aspiration, but as description it is at best incomplete. The Constitution has been the constitution of slaveholders, settlers, creditors, industrialists, segregationists, and imperial administrators, as well as abolitionists, freedpeople, workers, suffragists, and civil rights activists. Its meaning has never been pure. The task is not simply to recover the good Constitution from beneath conservative distortion. The task is to build forms of collective power capable of forcing the Constitution—when possible—to serve liberation, and capable of going beyond it when necessary.

Progressives should make constitutional arguments, but they should not make constitutional belonging the measure of justice. The people most harmed by American political economy have always had to speak in registers the Constitution did not recognize. Their claims were true before they were constitutional. They remain true even when courts reject them. A radical democratic politics must remember that.
