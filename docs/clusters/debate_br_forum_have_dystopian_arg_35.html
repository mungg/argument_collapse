"Daron Acemoglu’s proposal to centrally plan the direction of AI research through government intervention and tax policy is a misguided approach that would stifle economic progress and ignore the historical reality that automation is the primary driver of new job creation."

Daron Acemoglu’s warning about the dystopian trajectory of artificial intelligence is built upon a fundamental misdiagnosis of the relationship between technology, labor, and economic growth. In his framework, technology is a dial that policymakers and researchers can simply tune either toward "automation" (bad for workers) or "new tasks" (good for workers). This mechanical view of innovation leads him to propose top-down interventions—altering tax codes and using government funding to steer AI research—that are far more likely to stifle economic progress than to save democracy. The notion that we can or should centrally plan the direction of artificial intelligence ignores the organic, unpredictable nature of creative destruction and the profound benefits that unapologetic automation has historically delivered to human societies.

The core of Acemoglu’s argument rests on a nostalgic view of the post-World War II era, the *Trente Glorieuses*, where he claims shared prosperity was the result of technologies deliberately generating "new tasks." But he puts the cart before the horse. The creation of new tasks is not an alternative to automation; it is the direct, downstream consequence of it. When agricultural mechanization freed millions from the fields, nobody explicitly designed the manufacturing jobs that followed to "create new tasks." The massive productivity gains in agriculture lowered the cost of food, which left consumers with more disposable income. That surplus capital and demand, combined with newly freed human capital, organically created the entirely new industries of the twentieth century. The same process is happening today, even if our current economic metrics struggle to capture it.

Acemoglu points to the decline in Total Factor Productivity (TFP) growth since the 1970s as evidence that current AI and automation are somehow defective or socially suboptimal. Yet, the sluggish TFP growth we see today is arguably the result of exactly the kind of government intervention and heavy-handed regulation he prescribes. Over the last four decades, Western economies have erected massive barriers to innovation in the physical world—from restrictive zoning laws that halt construction, to the FDA’s labyrinthine approval processes, to endless environmental reviews. Consequently, technological innovation has been pushed almost entirely into the digital realm, the one area where permissionless innovation was still somewhat possible. To now invite government bureaucrats to establish a "measurement framework" to determine which AI applications are socially beneficial and which are merely "profit-boosting automation" is to introduce the sclerosis of the physical economy into the digital one.

Furthermore, the distinction between "good" task-creating AI and "bad" automating AI is intellectually fragile. Acemoglu uses education as an example, suggesting AI should adapt teaching material rather than grade papers. But automating grading *is* what allows teachers to focus on adaptive, student-centered learning. You cannot have the latter without the former. By eliminating the rote, repetitive aspects of jobs, automation inherently forces labor up the value chain toward more complex, empathetic, or creative tasks. We do not need AI researchers to artificially invent tasks for humans; we need them to ruthlessly automate the drudgery so that humans are forced to apply themselves to higher-order problems.

The fear that this time is different—that AI will automate everything and leave humans permanently redundant—relies on the lump of labor fallacy, the mistaken belief that there is a fixed amount of work to be done in the world. Human desires are infinite. As AI dramatically lowers the cost of goods and services, from medical diagnostics to legal research, it will unleash demand for entirely new categories of consumption and employment that we cannot currently imagine. In 2021, we are already seeing the explosion of the creator economy, specialized consulting, and bespoke human-centric services—all facilitated by the very digital platforms Acemoglu eyes with suspicion.

If we wish to support workers through this transition, the answer is not to hobble our most promising technology by artificially subsidizing inefficient human labor. Modifying the tax code to penalize capital investment in software and machinery, as Acemoglu suggests, would be economically disastrous, particularly as the United States tries to recover from the COVID-19 pandemic and build more resilient domestic supply chains. Instead of trying to steer AI away from automation, we should embrace it fully. The wealth generated by unprecedented AI productivity will be immense. How we distribute that wealth—through a broader social safety net, wage subsidies, or direct cash transfers—is a separate political question. But we cannot redistribute wealth we fail to create because we were too frightened to let the market automate the economy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The creation of new tasks for workers is not an alternative to automation, but rather a direct downstream consequence of the productivity gains and surplus capital that automation generates.
  2. 02The decline in Total Factor Productivity is likely caused by the same type of heavy-handed government regulation Acemoglu advocates for, rather than a failure of modern technology itself.
  3. 03The distinction between 'good' task-creating AI and 'bad' automating AI is a false dichotomy, as automating rote tasks is often the prerequisite for enabling humans to perform higher-order, creative work.
  4. 04Concerns about permanent human redundancy rely on the 'lump of labor' fallacy, failing to recognize that human desires are infinite and will always generate demand for new types of employment as costs fall.
  5. 05Artificially subsidizing inefficient human labor through the tax code would be economically disastrous and undermine the creation of the very wealth needed to fund a robust social safety net.
