"The expansion of markets into all spheres of life must be resisted because commodification corrupts the intrinsic moral and civic norms of social practices and erodes the democratic character necessary for self-government."

Michael Sandel is right to insist that the debate about markets cannot be settled by asking only whether exchanges are voluntary or efficient. The harder question is what market exchange does to the meaning of the goods exchanged, and to the habits of character a democratic society needs. In an age when public schools sell advertising space, prisons are run for profit, soldiers are supplemented by private contractors, and wealthy travelers can buy their way to the front of airport security lines, the question is not merely “Who gains and who loses?” It is “What kind of people are we becoming?”

The most important part of Sandel’s argument is his distinction between fairness and corruption. Much liberal argument about markets stops with fairness: Were the parties coerced? Did they have adequate information? Were background conditions sufficiently equal to make the bargain meaningful? These are essential questions. A “choice” made under hunger, debt, unemployment, or lack of medical care may be formally voluntary and substantively desperate. But Sandel is right that fairness does not exhaust the moral terrain. Some exchanges may be troubling even under fair conditions because they transform the norms appropriate to the activity.

This is clearest in cases involving civic obligation. Paying people to vote, serve on juries, or accept burdens that belong to all citizens does not simply add an incentive to an unchanged practice. It redefines the practice. The Swiss nuclear-waste case is powerful because it shows that compensation can be understood not as gratitude or burden-sharing, but as a bribe. Residents who were willing to accept a repository as a matter of public duty became less willing when the proposal was reframed as a cash transaction. The offer told them, in effect, that their civic judgment was for sale. The insult was not imaginary. Political communities depend on reasons that citizens can share: safety, necessity, fairness, reciprocity. Money can sometimes accompany such reasons, but when it replaces them, it hollows out public consent.

The same is true of education. Universities need money; no serious person denies it. But when admission comes to be seen as purchasable—through legacy preference, donor influence, or other discreet channels—the injury is not only to the rejected applicant. It is to the university’s claim to be a community of learning rather than a credentialing club for the already powerful. Defenders often argue that donations fund scholarships. Perhaps they do. But if the institution funds opportunity by selling exceptions to its own standards, it teaches a lesson more corrosive than any lecture can repair: that merit is negotiable and ideals are ornamental.

One might object that markets are not always corrupting. That is true, and Sandel’s argument does not require denying it. Paying a doctor does not necessarily corrupt healing; paying a teacher need not corrupt education; paying a musician need not corrupt art. The question is not whether money is present, but what role it plays and what norms govern the practice. A salary can support a vocation. A bonus can distort it. A fee can make a service available. A market can also turn a relationship into a transaction and a calling into a revenue stream. The moral task is to discriminate.

This point matters especially because economic reasoning has acquired the authority of common sense. We have become accustomed to describing social life in the language of incentives, preferences, utility, and choice. That vocabulary is illuminating in many settings, but it is disabling when treated as complete. To call a gift a “signal” of private information about affection is not merely a harmless metaphor. It misses the way gifts can express attention, memory, vulnerability, and shared history. A gift is not always a failed cash transfer. Sometimes its value lies precisely in the giver’s imperfect but loving attempt to see the recipient.

The deeper issue is democratic character. Markets are excellent at organizing production and coordinating dispersed information. But a society cannot be held together by market relations alone. Citizenship requires capacities that markets do not reliably cultivate: trust, patience, sacrifice, argument, mutual recognition, and a willingness to act for reasons other than personal gain. These capacities are not natural resources we should hoard. Sandel’s muscle metaphor is apt. Civic virtue grows through use or decays through neglect.

This is why the expansion of markets into every corner of life should worry conservatives as well as liberals. A conservative who values family, religion, local attachment, and inherited practices should resist the reduction of all value to price. A liberal who values equality and democratic citizenship should resist arrangements that let wealth colonize every social good. A republican who values self-government should resist a politics in which public purposes are replaced by private incentives.

Of course we need markets. We also need moral limits on markets, and we need public argument about those limits. That argument will be contentious because it requires judgments about the purposes of institutions and the meanings of social practices. But avoiding such judgments does not make us neutral. It simply leaves market logic to decide by default. Sandel’s great service is to remind us that “efficiency” is not a substitute for moral and civic reasoning. The question is not whether markets are good or bad. It is whether we still possess the confidence to say that some things should not be for sale.

"While Michael Sandel correctly identifies how market reasoning corrupts social goods, his philosophical focus on the nature of goods must be joined with a political analysis of power and inequality to effectively limit the reach of markets."

Michael Sandel gives us a needed vocabulary for describing something many people feel but cannot easily name: the sense that more and more of life is being reorganized around buying, selling, branding, ranking, and bidding. Yet his essay is most convincing when it shows that market reasoning is morally incomplete, and less convincing when it implies that our main problem is a philosophical mistake about the nature of goods. The spread of markets is not only a confusion of values. It is also a distribution of power.

Sandel distinguishes fairness objections from corruption objections, and then chooses to emphasize corruption. This is understandable. American public argument is already fluent in fairness talk, at least in principle, while corruption talk sounds more elusive and moralistic. He is right that some goods are damaged when bought and sold even if the parties are equal. Friendship, civic honor, votes, and certain forms of recognition cannot be fully understood through consent. Their meanings depend on norms that markets may displace.

But in practice, fairness and corruption are harder to separate than Sandel suggests. Markets often corrupt because inequality gives them their social force. A market in kidneys would not be populated by hedge-fund managers selling spare organs to schoolteachers. It would draw disproportionately on the poor, the indebted, the uninsured, and those with few alternatives. The corruption of the body into a revenue source would be inseparable from the injustice that makes such “choice” attractive. To say that the market degrades the meaning of the body is true; to say only that risks making exploitation look like a secondary concern.

The same is true of university admissions. Sandel’s account of “selling out” captures the injury to institutional integrity. But the more concrete scandal is that affluent families already buy educational advantage long before the admissions office opens a file. They buy houses in districts with better public schools, private tutors, SAT preparation, unpaid internships, music lessons, summer enrichment, and the kind of social confidence that admissions committees mistake for merit. Legacy preferences and donor influence are not aberrations from an otherwise pure meritocracy. They are visible tips of a much larger structure in which money quietly shapes opportunity while institutions preserve the language of talent.

This matters because the rhetoric of “markets crowding out morals” can sometimes make the remedy sound like a change in attitude. We should deliberate better, honor civic goods, and resist economistic thinking. Yes. But we also need institutions that prevent wealth from dominating social life. Without campaign-finance reform, progressive taxation, strong public schools, labor protections, universal access to health care, and serious limits on conflicts of interest, moral exhortation will not keep markets in their place. Citizens asked to practice civic virtue while watching the affluent purchase exemption from common burdens will eventually conclude that virtue is for suckers.

Sandel’s examples of gifts and wedding toasts are charming, but they also point to a limitation. The commercialization of intimate life is not always driven by a philosophical belief in efficiency. Sometimes it is driven by time scarcity, work pressure, geographic mobility, and the thinning of communal supports. People buy services once performed by relatives, neighbors, congregations, or friends because those networks are weaker, because adults work longer hours, because families live farther apart, and because care responsibilities are unevenly distributed. A purchased wedding toast may be tacky; paid child care, elder care, therapy, housekeeping, and food preparation are often necessities. The moral question is not simply whether money belongs in intimate life, but how to prevent paid care from being degraded because it is associated with dependence, women’s work, immigrant labor, or low status.

Indeed, one risk in romanticizing non-market norms is that those norms have often concealed domination. The family is governed by love, but also by unequal power. Communities cultivate solidarity, but also conformity and exclusion. Professional vocations inspire service, but also paternalism. Markets may corrode, but they may also give exit to people trapped in oppressive relationships. A woman able to earn her own income, a patient able to choose another doctor, a worker able to leave a feudal dependence for wages—these are not trivial moral achievements. The line between commodification and emancipation is historically variable.

Still, Sandel’s central warning stands. The past several decades have seen not only the use of markets, but the prestige of market metrics. Hospitals, universities, charities, and public agencies increasingly borrow the language of customers, incentives, performance targets, and revenue streams. Some of this improves accountability. But it also narrows imagination. A school becomes a producer of test scores; a student becomes an investor in human capital; a patient becomes a consumer; a citizen becomes a taxpayer shopping for services. These metaphors do not merely describe reality. They train us to notice some obligations and forget others.

The challenge, then, is not to purge money from morally significant domains. That is impossible and not always desirable. The challenge is to democratize the terms on which money enters. Compensation for hosting a waste facility, for example, need not be a bribe if it emerges from a fair public process, reflects real burdens, and accompanies rather than replaces deliberation about safety and necessity. Paying teachers need not corrupt education; tying every educational judgment to test-based bonuses might. Paying caregivers need not corrupt love; treating care as low-wage servant work does.

We need Sandel’s language of moral limits, but joined to a political economy of power. Markets do not float above society; they are made by law, custom, bargaining power, and public policy. The question is not markets or morals. It is whose morals are built into which markets, who benefits, who bears the costs, and what forms of common life remain protected from the rule of money.
