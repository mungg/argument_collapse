"While a wealth tax is a necessary tool for achieving tax justice and funding public goods, it is insufficient on its own to democratize the economy and dismantle the underlying structures that produce extreme inequality."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman make the strongest possible case for a wealth tax on the superrich. I support such a tax. But I worry that the very elegance of their proposal narrows our imagination. The United States does not merely have a problem of undertaxed billionaires. It has a problem of ownership. A wealth tax can reduce the scale of private fortunes and fund public goods, but it cannot by itself democratize the economy that produces those fortunes in the first place.

This distinction matters because the lead essay oscillates between two goals. One is fiscal: get the ultrarich to contribute according to ability to pay. The other is structural: reduce the concentration of wealth and power. The first goal can be advanced by a well-designed wealth tax. The second requires a broader reconstruction of economic power, including labor law, corporate governance, public ownership, antitrust, campaign finance, and social rights.

Saez and Zucman are right that the existing tax system is indefensible. A worker’s wages are visible and taxed immediately. A billionaire’s economic gains can be hidden inside corporations, deferred as unrealized appreciation, borrowed against, passed through trusts, and transformed into philanthropic influence. The idea that this is a “free market” outcome is mythology. It is a legal achievement. The state creates corporations, enforces contracts, grants patents, protects property, supplies infrastructure, educates workers, stabilizes finance, and then lets owners treat the resulting gains as private desert.

A wealth tax punctures that mythology. It says that ownership of vast assets is not a purely private matter. It says that if a person commands $50 billion in claims on society’s productive resources, society may make recurring claims in return. It also targets the peculiar injustice of our current arrangement: a middle-class family can pay property tax every year on a modest home, while the owner of billions in stock may owe little personal tax unless he chooses to sell. That contrast is politically explosive because it is morally obvious.

Yet even a radical wealth tax leaves the basic structure intact. The billionaire remains the central figure; the public merely takes a share. The corporation remains governed primarily in the interests of shareholders; workers remain subordinate within the firm; investment remains dependent on private profitability; communities remain vulnerable to capital flight; philanthropy remains a way for the rich to convert private wealth into public authority. A tax can reduce these problems, but it does not eliminate them.

The New Deal order that Saez and Zucman admire was not built by tax rates alone. The mid-twentieth-century compression of inequality owed much to unions, wage norms, financial regulation, public investment, wartime mobilization, and the political power of working-class organizations. High marginal tax rates mattered partly because they operated in an environment where labor had leverage. Executives could not as easily take all productivity gains for themselves; corporations were more constrained by regulation and custom; capital was less globally mobile; unions could bargain for a share. To revive only the tax component without reviving worker power would be insufficient.

Indeed, one reason wealth has concentrated so dramatically since the late 1970s is that workers lost bargaining power. Deindustrialization, anti-union law and employer tactics, fissured workplaces, shareholder primacy, deregulation, and global capital mobility changed the distribution of income before taxes. Tax policy then reinforced the shift. Saez and Zucman emphasize that high tax rates can discourage rent extraction at the top, such as excessive executive pay. That is true. But workers also need institutions that let them claim value directly: sectoral bargaining, easier union formation, works councils, wage boards, and protections against misclassification and subcontracting abuses.

The same point applies to corporate power. If Amazon, Google, Facebook, Apple, and other giants possess too much market and political power, taxing their founders’ wealth is only one response. We also need antitrust enforcement, data rules, interoperability requirements, labor standards, and perhaps public alternatives in key digital infrastructures. Otherwise the tax system skims gains from concentrated corporate power while leaving that power in place.

A democratic program should therefore treat the wealth tax as a floor, not a ceiling. First, enact an annual tax on extreme fortunes, with robust enforcement and an exit tax. The rates should be high enough to matter. A 2 or 3 percent tax above very high thresholds is a reasonable beginning, but if we agree that extreme wealth threatens democracy, then we should not be afraid of rates that actually reduce the largest fortunes over time. No one has a democratic right to be a centibillionaire.

Second, restore steeply progressive income taxation, including on capital gains. Saez and Zucman rightly note that the income tax cannot reach all billionaire wealth, but that does not mean it is secondary. High salaries, bonuses, carried interest, financial windfalls, and monopoly rents should be taxed at rates that discourage extraction. The old top rates above 70 or 90 percent were not socialism; they were republican self-defense against oligarchy.

Third, rebuild the estate and gift tax. A wealth tax addresses fortunes while their owners live, but inheritance remains the mechanism by which class power hardens into caste. Large bequests should be taxed heavily, trusts should be limited, and dynasty planning should be curtailed. If meritocracy means anything, it cannot mean allowing children to inherit permanent command over resources they did nothing to create.

Fourth, democratize the firm. Workers should have rights to organize, bargain, and participate in governance. Germany’s codetermination model is not perfect, but it reminds Americans that shareholder dictatorship is not the only possible capitalism. Employee representation on boards, profit-sharing, and sectoral bargaining would change the pre-tax distribution of income rather than merely correcting it afterward.

Fifth, create social wealth. Instead of relying only on taxing private wealth after it accumulates, government should build public assets. A national investment authority, green infrastructure bank, or social wealth fund could hold diversified assets on behalf of the public. Alaska’s Permanent Fund is a limited example of the principle: collective ownership can generate universal dividends or finance public goods. If technological progress and network effects create enormous returns, why should those returns accrue only to private shareholders?

Sixth, remove essential goods from dependence on private fortune. Universal health care, tuition-free or debt-free public college, child care, housing investment, and paid leave would reduce the power that employers and creditors hold over ordinary people. Economic democracy is not only about the distribution of wealth at the top; it is about freedom from domination in daily life.

The emerging coronavirus crisis underscores this point. The pandemic is revealing the fragility of a society in which health insurance is tied to employment, millions lack paid sick leave, hospitals operate under financial pressure, and workers are told to absorb risks they did not create. A wealth tax could help finance emergency and long-term public capacity. But the crisis also shows that what we need is not merely redistribution after markets operate. We need institutions organized around care, resilience, and solidarity rather than maximum private return.

There is one more reason not to stop at taxation: billionaires are not only rich taxpayers; they are political actors. Even after paying a wealth tax, they can fund think tanks, media ventures, advocacy groups, university centers, ballot initiatives, and philanthropic projects that shape public priorities. Philanthropy can do good, but it is not democracy. When public schools, global health priorities, museums, journalism, or scientific agendas depend on billionaires’ preferences, private wealth governs public life. A wealth tax reduces the scale of that influence; campaign finance reform, public funding, lobbying restrictions, and democratic public institutions are needed to challenge it directly.

Saez and Zucman’s technical confidence is valuable. For too long, opponents of progressive taxation have treated administrative difficulty as impossibility. The IRS can value assets. It can require reporting. It can tax in kind. It can enforce rules if Congress gives it resources and authority. But we should be wary of a politics that becomes too technocratic. The problem of inequality is not just that economists have not yet designed the perfect tax instrument. It is that organized wealth defeated organized labor and captured much of the state. Reversing that defeat requires mass politics, not only better spreadsheets.

This is also a lesson from the historical period the authors invoke. The high tax rates of the 1930s through 1960s were born of upheaval: depression, labor militancy, war, fear of radicalism, and broad democratic mobilization. Elites did not simply read the optimal tax literature and agree to be taxed. They were forced to compromise. A wealth tax today will likewise require political conflict. Its durability will depend on whether it is embedded in a larger coalition that tangibly improves life for the majority.

So yes: tax the superrich. Tax them annually, progressively, and seriously. Reject the absurd claim that a democracy capable of taxing wages and homes cannot tax portfolios and private businesses. Reject the idea that billionaires’ paper wealth is too delicate for public claims while workers’ paychecks are not. Reject the mystique of philanthropy as a substitute for taxation.

But do not mistake the tax for the transformation. The goal should not be a society in which billionaires remain the protagonists and the public gets a somewhat larger cut. The goal should be a society in which no one’s life chances depend so heavily on the decisions of billionaires at all. A wealth tax is one instrument for getting there. It is necessary, but it is not enough.

Vanilla GPT

"While taxing the superrich is essential for democracy, a wealth tax should be viewed as one tool among many rather than the sole solution, requiring a sober design that prioritizes moderate rates and administrative feasibility over radical redistribution."

Saez and Zucman are right about the central fact: the American tax system has lost much of its ability to reach the very top. A person whose income comes as wages encounters withholding, payroll taxes, and regular reporting. A person whose economic income appears as the rising value of a company can often choose when, whether, and how much to realize. That asymmetry is not a loophole at the margins; it is a basic architectural feature of the modern tax state. It helps explain why many Americans find the tax code both unfair and mystifying. The nurse’s paycheck is transparent to the IRS; the billionaire’s appreciation is often invisible until sale, borrowing, gift, or death.

They are also right that the issue is not merely revenue. Extreme wealth is not only purchasing power. It is agenda-setting power, veto power, philanthropic power, media power, and political power. The United States need not believe that every large fortune is ill-gotten to worry that a society of hundred-billion-dollar fortunes will become less democratic over time. Campaign finance law, antitrust law, labor law, corporate governance, and public financing of elections all matter here. But taxation is inevitably part of the answer, because taxation is one of the few instruments that can operate at the scale of the fortunes themselves.

Still, the case for taxing the superrich is stronger than the case for any particular wealth tax design. Saez and Zucman sometimes write as though the choice is between an annual wealth tax and resignation to plutocracy. That is too narrow. The United States has several ways to tax wealth more effectively: taxing capital gains at death, ending stepped-up basis, strengthening the estate and gift tax, taxing unrealized gains of the very rich on a mark-to-market basis where valuation is straightforward, raising corporate taxes while limiting avoidance, and enforcing existing law with a rebuilt IRS. A wealth tax may belong in that toolkit, but it should not be treated as the only serious instrument.

The most compelling argument for a wealth tax is that income is too easily manipulated. Buffett can avoid dividends; founders can borrow against appreciated shares; private equity managers can convert labor income into capital gains; multinational firms can shift profits. But annual net worth is also manipulable. The superrich do not simply hold piles of Berkshire Hathaway stock. They hold family partnerships, trusts, carried interests, private funds, art, real estate entities, derivatives, intellectual property, and claims on future income streams. Some of these assets can be valued; others can be valued only with wide bands of uncertainty. A tax system can tolerate some uncertainty, but annual recurrence magnifies every dispute. If a valuation question must be fought every year, the administrative burden is not incidental.

Saez and Zucman’s proposed solution—allowing payment in kind—is ingenious but not a cure-all. If a family that owns a private company disputes the IRS valuation, it could hand over shares and let the government sell them. In theory this creates the missing market. In practice, the sale price of a small, illiquid, minority stake in a controlled family business may reflect lack of control, lack of information, restrictions on transfer, and strategic behavior by potential buyers. A buyer may demand a steep discount precisely because the shares do not confer governance rights. The family may then argue that the sale proves the IRS overvalued the firm. The government could try to auction a larger or more powerful stake, but then the tax begins to alter corporate control, not merely collect public revenue. Perhaps that is acceptable in some cases, but it should be acknowledged as a choice rather than a technical fix.

The European experience does not prove that wealth taxes are impossible, but it does counsel modesty. Several European countries repealed or narrowed wealth taxes because they raised less revenue than hoped, encouraged avoidance or emigration, and created administrative headaches. France’s wealth tax became politically notorious even though its revenue was limited; Sweden and Germany abandoned their versions under different pressures. The United States is larger, has citizenship-based taxation, and could design a better system with better third-party reporting. It also has a uniquely sophisticated financial sector that can help value assets—but that same sector can help transform assets into harder-to-tax forms. The lesson is not “don’t try.” It is “design for adversaries.”

A moderate wealth tax with a very high exemption threshold is therefore much more plausible than a radical one. A tax of 2 or 3 percent on fortunes above $50 million or $1 billion would still be ambitious, but it would leave room for normal returns, valuation error, and compliance costs. A 10 percent annual tax on billionaires is a different creature. Saez and Zucman embrace that difference: the point is not simply to raise money but to reduce the number and size of large fortunes. That goal is defensible, but it raises harder questions than their carbon analogy suggests.

Carbon emissions are a social harm independent of who emits them. Wealth is more complicated. Some fortunes reflect monopoly, rent extraction, inherited privilege, or political favoritism. Others reflect genuine innovation, scale, and consumer surplus, even if our tax code then allows the resulting gains to compound too lightly taxed. A tax that deliberately makes it difficult to remain a multibillionaire may be justified as a democratic safeguard, but it will also tax patient ownership, company-building, and concentrated founder stakes. If the concern is monopoly power, antitrust law may be better targeted. If the concern is dynastic inheritance, estate and gift taxation are better targeted. If the concern is political influence, campaign finance and lobbying rules are better targeted. Wealth taxation can supplement these, but it cannot substitute for them.

There is also the question of constitutional risk. Many scholars believe a federal wealth tax could survive as an indirect tax or as a tax distinguishable from the unapportioned direct taxes prohibited by the Constitution. Others disagree, pointing to the constitutional treatment of direct taxes and the history that led to the Sixteenth Amendment. A wealth tax would almost certainly face immediate litigation. That does not mean policymakers should avoid it, but it does mean that progressives should not put all their eggs in a basket the Supreme Court may throw out. Repealing stepped-up basis, taxing capital gains at death, raising estate tax rates, tightening grantor trusts, improving information reporting, and taxing publicly traded assets of billionaires on an accrual basis would face fewer constitutional obstacles.

The income tax can also be made to reach more wealth than it currently does. For publicly traded securities, annual mark-to-market taxation for the very richest households is administratively easier than a comprehensive wealth tax. If Jeff Bezos’s Amazon shares rise by $10 billion in a year, the gain can be measured. Taxing that gain annually, with loss offsets and rules for liquidity, would attack the “buy, borrow, die” strategy directly. For private assets, a retrospective tax upon sale or transfer—with an interest charge for deferral—could reduce the advantage of waiting without requiring yearly appraisals of every asset. This approach is imperfect, but it aims at the core problem: untaxed appreciation.

None of this diminishes the moral force of Saez and Zucman’s argument. The United States has allowed the estate tax to wither, the corporate tax to erode, capital gains preferences to persist, and enforcement against sophisticated taxpayers to decline. Meanwhile, payroll taxes fall heavily on work, and state and local taxes often burden lower- and middle-income families. A democratic society can reasonably ask why someone with $50 billion should be permitted to decide almost entirely for himself when his economic income becomes taxable.

The best path is a layered one. Restore the IRS’s capacity to audit complex wealth. End stepped-up basis and tax large gains at death. Strengthen the estate and gift tax, including rules on trusts and valuation discounts. Tax capital gains more like labor income. Adopt mark-to-market rules for the ultrawealthy where market prices exist, and deferral charges where they do not. Raise corporate taxation while coordinating internationally against profit shifting. Then consider a moderate wealth tax as a backstop for the largest fortunes, with strong reporting rules, an exit tax, and careful treatment of illiquid assets.

Saez and Zucman have done a great service by shifting the debate from whether extreme wealth should be taxed to how. But “how” matters. A tax system that overpromises and underdelivers could discredit the very egalitarian project they seek to advance. The goal should be radical in ambition and sober in design: not symbolic punishment of billionaires, but a durable fiscal constitution for a democracy in which private fortunes no longer tower over public power.

Diversified Gemini

"While taxing the superrich is necessary, it is an incomplete solution that treats the symptoms of inequality rather than the underlying structural mechanisms of wealth accumulation."

In "Taxing the Superrich," Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman provide an unassailable empirical indictment of American oligarchy. They definitively show that the tax system has become an engine of upward redistribution, functioning as a regressive flat tax that shields the multi-billion-dollar fortunes of a tiny elite. Their insight that "wealth is power" and that extreme wealth functions as a negative externality on our democracy is profound and urgently needed in mainstream economic discourse. However, their reliance on taxation as the primary mechanism to solve the crisis of inequality is fundamentally incomplete. By focusing almost entirely on redistributing wealth after it has been hoarded, they fail to address the structural mechanisms that allow such grotesque fortunes to be amassed in the first place.

If we accept Saez and Zucman's premise that a billion dollars in the hands of one person is a democratic failure, we must ask: how did that person acquire a billion dollars? In the modern American economy, extreme wealth is rarely the result of mere thrift and brilliant innovation. It is almost always the result of market power, monopoly rents, and the systematic suppression of labor.

Take Jeff Bezos, whom the authors frequently cite. His centibillion-dollar fortune is not just a reflection of Amazon's convenience. It is the direct result of predatory pricing to crush competitors, massive leverage over third-party sellers, relentless union-busting, and the grim exploitation of warehouse workers who endure brutal quotas and unsafe conditions. Mark Zuckerberg's wealth stems from a monopolistic surveillance advertising empire that has bought up every viable competitor and commodified human attention at the expense of privacy and democratic stability. The Waltons built their fortune on poverty wages, forcing taxpayers to subsidize their labor force through food stamps and Medicaid.

A wealth tax, even a radical one of 10 percent, treats the symptoms of this disease while leaving the underlying pathology intact. If we implement a wealth tax but allow Amazon to maintain its monopoly power and continue exploiting its workers, we are effectively entering into a corrupt bargain. We are saying that we will tolerate the ruthless extraction of value from labor and the destruction of local economies, as long as the federal government gets its 10 percent cut of the spoils. This turns the state into a junior partner in the exploitation, dependent on the continued extreme profitability of monopolies to fund public services.

To truly "disrupt privilege, impede rent extraction, and protect democracy," as Saez and Zucman desire, we must focus on pre-distribution. We must stop the accumulation of unmerited wealth at the source. This means reviving aggressive antitrust enforcement. Companies like Amazon, Facebook, and Google should not simply be taxed; they should be broken up. We must separate their marketplace functions from their proprietary retail arms and break their chokeholds on digital infrastructure. When markets are competitive, the ability of a single individual to extract tens of billions of dollars in monopoly rents evaporates.

Furthermore, we must fundamentally alter the balance of power between labor and capital. The decline of the American middle class perfectly mirrors the decline in union density. For four decades, federal policy has actively undermined workers' ability to organize, allowing corporate executives and shareholders to capture nearly all the gains of productivity growth. Passing sweeping labor law reform, such as the PRO Act, enacting sectoral bargaining, and instituting a living federal minimum wage would ensure that the wealth created by American corporations is distributed equitably to the workers who produce it, rather than flowing upward to inflate the stock portfolios of the 0.1 percent.

Corporate governance reform is equally critical. The current model of "shareholder primacy" legally mandates that corporations extract maximum value for investors, leading to massive stock buybacks, outsourcing, and wage stagnation. We should mandate worker representation on corporate boards (co-determination), following the successful models of Northern Europe. If workers had a say in how corporate profits were allocated, we would see far fewer billionaire executives and far more equitable wage structures.

Saez and Zucman's vision of taxation as a weapon to de-concentrate wealth echoes the policies of the New Deal era. But they forget that Franklin Roosevelt's highest tax rates were just one pillar of a much broader structural transformation. The New Deal also brought us the Wagner Act, which empowered labor, and fierce anti-monopoly enforcement under figures like Thurman Arnold. The mid-century middle class was not created solely by taxing the rich; it was created by empowering workers to demand a larger share of the pie before the tax collector ever arrived.

None of this is to say we should abandon the idea of taxing the wealthy. A wealth tax, alongside higher top marginal income rates and a restored corporate tax, is a necessary component of a just society. But we must be clear-eyed about its limitations. If we rely on taxation alone, we will always be playing a reactionary game of catch-up. The lawyers and accountants of the superrich will always find new loopholes, and the immense political power of billionaires will constantly threaten to roll back our redistributive victories.

True economic democracy requires that we dismantle the machinery of extreme wealth creation. We must build an economy where the exploitation required to become a centibillionaire is legally impossible and structurally unfeasible. Taxing the robber barons is a noble goal, but it is far better to stop them from becoming robber barons in the first place.
