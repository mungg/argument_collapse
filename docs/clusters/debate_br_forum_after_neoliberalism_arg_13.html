"A post-neoliberal economic framework for inclusive prosperity must move beyond market-centric models to center the household and the essential, often undervalued labor of care."

The essay by Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman is powerful because it recognizes that markets are embedded in institutions. But one institution remains too hidden in their account: the household. Any economics after neoliberalism must take seriously the unpaid and underpaid labor of care, and the gendered structure of economic life.

The crises named in the lead essay—inequality, stagnant wages, declining mobility, health insecurity, automation, climate change—are real. Yet they are experienced through families and care networks. When wages stagnate, households compensate with longer hours, second earners, debt, delayed childbirth, or unpaid labor by women. When public services are inadequate, families fill the gaps. When health care is unaffordable, relatives become caregivers. When schools close early, work schedules become impossible. When elder care is unavailable, daughters reduce paid work. The economy relies on care, but economic policy often treats care as an afterthought.

Neoliberalism intensified this contradiction. It celebrated labor market participation and individual responsibility while cutting or constraining public support. Women entered paid work in large numbers, which was a major social transformation and a gain in independence. But the organization of work did not adjust enough. The typical job still assumed a worker with someone else at home to handle children, elders, meals, appointments, and emergencies. The result has been a time squeeze, especially for mothers and low-wage workers with little schedule control.

Economics has struggled with this because much care work happens outside markets or in low-wage sectors where productivity is hard to measure. Unpaid child care, elder care, and household labor do not show up in GDP, though society would collapse without them. Paid care work—child care workers, home health aides, nursing assistants, domestic workers—is often treated as low-skill because it is associated with women’s traditional labor, even though it requires patience, judgment, emotional intelligence, and responsibility. Many care workers are women of color and immigrants, which compounds the devaluation.

The lead essay’s institutional approach can help. If property rights and labor market rules are constructed, so are the boundaries between production and reproduction, paid and unpaid labor, public and private responsibility. We should ask: what institutions would allow people both to give and receive care without poverty, exhaustion, or dependence?

Start with child care. High-quality early childhood care and education produce benefits for children, parents, and society. Yet in the United States child care is often unaffordable for families while wages for providers remain extremely low. This is a classic sign that private markets alone cannot solve the problem. Parents need care early in life when their earnings are often modest; children cannot borrow against future gains; quality is difficult for parents to observe; and social benefits exceed private benefits. A post-neoliberal agenda should include public child care or large public subsidies tied to quality standards and decent wages.

Paid family and medical leave is equally central. The absence of a national paid leave system forces workers to choose between income and care at moments of birth, illness, disability, or death. Higher-income professionals may have employer benefits; low-wage workers often do not. A universal social insurance model would pool risk and normalize care as part of life, not an individual inconvenience.

Elder care and long-term care deserve the same attention. The aging population will make this unavoidable. Families already struggle to care for elderly relatives while maintaining employment. Medicaid supports much long-term care, but often only after people have spent down assets, and quality varies. Home care workers are underpaid and overworked. A humane system would expand home- and community-based services, improve wages and training, and support family caregivers through respite care and income supports.

Labor policy must address scheduling as well as wages. Unpredictable schedules are devastating for caregivers. A mother cannot arrange child care if her hours change at the last minute. A worker cannot take an elderly parent to a medical appointment if refusing a shift risks retaliation. Fair scheduling laws, paid sick days, and rights to request flexibility are not luxuries; they are infrastructure for care.

The authors discuss wage boards as a tool to reduce inequality. Care sectors should be a priority. Because care is labor-intensive, there is a danger that wage increases will raise costs for families unless accompanied by public funding. That is not an argument against raising wages; it is an argument for socializing the cost of care. We already accept that public education should be publicly financed. Early childhood and elder care deserve similar treatment.

Health care also belongs in this framework. The United States wastes resources while leaving people insecure. Employer-based insurance ties families to jobs and makes caregiving transitions riskier. A universal health system—whether through Medicare for All or another robust universal model—would support labor mobility, entrepreneurship, caregiving, and dignity. It would especially benefit women, who often manage family health needs and are more likely to work part-time or in jobs without benefits.

A feminist economics after neoliberalism must also rethink taxation and social insurance around individuals, not only households. Policies that assume income pooling within families can hide dependence and vulnerability. A woman in a household with adequate income may still lack control over resources. Domestic violence, financial control, and unequal bargaining within households are economic issues. Benefits paid directly to caregivers or individuals can increase autonomy.

The lead essay’s focus on power should extend inside the household. Bargaining power in families is shaped by wages, assets, legal rights, public benefits, and social norms. When women have independent income, child care, health care, and housing options, they have more freedom in intimate life. Conversely, austerity and weak labor protections can trap people in dependency.

Technology policy should also include care. Much discussion of automation focuses on manufacturing or artificial intelligence replacing cognitive labor. But many growing jobs are in care. Some technologies may assist care workers and patients; others may intensify surveillance or reduce human contact in damaging ways. Public innovation policy should not only chase labor-saving devices. In care, the goal is often better relationships, not fewer workers. A society that treats all labor-saving as progress may misunderstand what care is.

Climate policy also has gender and care dimensions. Disasters increase care burdens. Heat waves endanger the elderly and children. Pollution affects pregnancy and child health. A Green New Deal, now entering public debate, should be a care agenda as well as an energy agenda: resilient schools, health clinics, public transit, safe housing, and good care jobs.

The central point is that “inclusive prosperity” cannot mean merely adding women to an economy designed around the male breadwinner and the unencumbered worker. It must redesign work, welfare, and public investment around human dependency. Everyone is dependent at some point: as children, in illness, in disability, in old age. Neoliberalism denied this by imagining self-sufficient individuals transacting in markets. A better economics must begin from interdependence.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right that economists can help build a fairer society. But they should expand their map of the economy. The household is not outside economics. Care is not sentimental background. It is the foundation on which markets rest. If economics after neoliberalism fails to value care, it will remain trapped in one of neoliberalism’s deepest errors: mistaking dependence for private trouble rather than public responsibility.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Economic policy must recognize that unpaid and underpaid care work is the foundational infrastructure upon which the rest of the economy relies.
  2. 02The boundaries between public and private responsibility should be redrawn through universal social insurance for child care, elder care, and paid family leave.
  3. 03Labor market reforms, such as wage boards and fair scheduling laws, must prioritize care sectors and the needs of workers with caregiving responsibilities.
  4. 04Inclusive prosperity requires socializing the costs of care to ensure high-quality services and decent wages without placing an undue burden on individual families.
  5. 05Economic analysis must address power imbalances and dependency within the household by focusing on individual autonomy rather than assuming equitable resource pooling.
  6. 06Technology and climate policies should be evaluated based on their ability to support human relationships and resilient care networks rather than just labor-saving efficiency.
  7. 07A truly inclusive economics must reject the neoliberal myth of the self-sufficient individual in favor of a model that acknowledges universal human interdependence.
