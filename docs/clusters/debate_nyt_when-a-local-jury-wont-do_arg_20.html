"Courts should be more willing to move trials to a different venue when a crime has become a defining part of a community's identity, as local jurors may feel subconscious pressure to act as representatives of a traumatized collective rather than impartial evaluators of evidence."

The hardest venue cases are not simply famous; they are identity cases. A local jury may fail when the crime becomes part of how the community defines itself. That is the danger in the Tsarnaev trial. The Marathon bombing was followed by a citywide shutdown, an armed search, public memorials, benefit concerts, athletic tributes and an understandable language of unity. “Boston Strong” was a humane response to violence, but it also created a civic narrative in which the defendant may appear less as an accused individual than as the enemy of the city.

Courts tend to trust jurors who say they can be fair. That trust is often excessive. Bias is not always conscious. A juror may sincerely believe she can weigh the evidence while also feeling pressure not to disappoint neighbors, victims, police officers or the memory of those killed. In terrorism cases, there is an added risk that fear of Muslims, immigrants or foreigners will mix with local grief, even when jurors reject prejudice in principle.

The standard for transfer should therefore focus on social role as much as media exposure. Did the event make a large share of the jury pool feel like participants or victims? Were ordinary residents ordered indoors, asked to assist police, or drawn into public rituals of mourning? Has the defendant been described in local discourse as the symbol of an attack on “us”? If the answer is yes, courts should be far more willing to move the trial.

This does not mean every notorious case leaves town. A scandal widely reported on television may still be fairly tried where it happened. But a mass-casualty attack that becomes a community’s defining trauma presents a different problem. The issue is not information; it is identification.

A transfer should be to a district with comparable demographic diversity and no special connection to the event, not to a place chosen for harsher attitudes. The goal is distance, not advantage. In the Tsarnaev case, that distance would strengthen the verdict. A society confronting terrorism should be able to say that it punished only after proof, and before jurors who did not have to decide whether mercy would make them bad Bostonians.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'Boston Strong' narrative risks transforming the defendant from an accused individual into a symbolic enemy of the entire city.
  2. 02Judicial trust in jurors' self-reported fairness is often excessive because bias and the pressure to not disappoint one's community are frequently unconscious.
  3. 03In terrorism cases, local grief can dangerously merge with latent prejudices against Muslims, immigrants, or foreigners.
  4. 04The standard for transferring a trial should focus on whether the event forced the local jury pool into the role of participants or victims through lockdowns and public rituals.
  5. 05Moving a trial to a neutral district ensures that the verdict is based on proof rather than a juror's fear that showing mercy would betray their civic identity.
