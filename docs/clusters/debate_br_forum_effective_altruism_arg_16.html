"While Peter Singer’s effective altruism provides a necessary critique of philanthropic complacency, its narrow focus on measurable 'emergency moral arithmetic' risks devaluing the pluralistic cultural and civic institutions essential for long-term human flourishing."

Peter Singer’s essay is a bracing attack on philanthropic complacency, but it also expresses a characteristically modern temptation: the wish to reduce the plurality of human goods to a single scale. “Doing the most good” sounds unassailable until one asks what kind of good, for whom, over what time horizon, and within what form of life. Singer’s examples are carefully chosen, and in many of them his conclusion is plainly right. But a society guided too narrowly by his reasoning would risk starving institutions and practices whose value is real precisely because it cannot be captured by emergency moral arithmetic.

Begin where Singer is strongest. Gifts to wealthy universities and elite cultural institutions deserve scrutiny. When an alumnus gives millions to a university with a multibillion-dollar endowment, one may reasonably suspect that gratitude, status, nostalgia, or naming rights are doing more work than justice. Likewise, a $100 million gift to renovate a concert hall in New York is hard to defend as the best use of money in a world of preventable disease. Singer’s question—what else could this money have done?—should haunt every donor wall.

But the conclusion should not be that art, universities, libraries, parks, local theaters, and museums are luxuries to be considered only after extreme poverty has vanished. Human beings do not live by survival alone. We live in languages, histories, disciplines, neighborhoods, rituals, and shared spaces. The arts enlarge imagination; universities preserve and generate knowledge; local institutions knit citizens together; monuments and museums help societies remember and argue about their past. These goods can be abused by elites, but they are not mere ornaments.

Singer says that in a world that had overcome major problems, promoting the arts would be worthy. This sets the bar too high. The world will always contain urgent suffering. If every cultural expenditure must wait until no child is dying from preventable disease, culture will always lose. The same logic could apply to almost anything not directly lifesaving: basic research in mathematics, preservation of endangered languages, public broadcasting, historical archives, botanical gardens, even some forms of education. Yet many such activities contribute to human flourishing in ways that are diffuse and intergenerational.

The issue is not whether saving a life is more important than improving an opera hall. In a direct choice, it usually is. The issue is whether all moral decisions should be modeled as direct choices between marginal dollars. Societies require portfolios of value. We need emergency relief and public health, but also courts, schools, art, science, journalism, democratic forums, and spaces of beauty. Some of these will never rank well in a cost-per-life-saved comparison, because that is not what they are for.

Effective altruism may respond that individual donors in affluent societies should prioritize the most urgent needs precisely because other institutions already fund culture and education. Sometimes this is true. But donor behavior also shapes public culture. If the language of “ineffectiveness” spreads too broadly, fragile local institutions may suffer while only the wealthiest museums and universities survive through endowments and elite patronage. A community arts center serving immigrants, a historically black college, a rural library, or a prison education program is not equivalent to a vanity gift to an Ivy League university. Singer’s categories need finer distinctions.

There is also a democratic concern. Effective altruism often places moral authority in the hands of those who can compare outcomes across the world: evaluators, philosophers, economists, and unusually conscientious donors. Their work is valuable. But democracy depends on citizens deliberating together about common goods, not only on donors optimizing private transfers. When public goods are funded by philanthropy, even effective philanthropy, priorities may reflect the judgments of the wealthy rather than the community. A billionaire who funds malaria prevention may do great good. A billionaire who decides the future of education reform, urban policy, or scientific research may exercise power that ought to be publicly accountable.

Singer’s framework also underplays uncertainty about long-run cultural effects. The university he criticizes may produce the scientist who develops a vaccine, the judge who defends constitutional rights, or the writer who changes public imagination. Of course, Princeton and Yale do not need marginal gifts as urgently as poor children need health interventions. But the general point matters: institutions that cultivate knowledge and creativity may have vast, unpredictable benefits. Their value cannot be dismissed because it resists measurement.

A better approach would distinguish between moral emergencies and sustaining goods. Extreme poverty, preventable disease, and mass suffering create urgent claims on affluent people. These claims should command a significant share of our resources, far more than they currently do. Singer is right that giving almost nothing to the world’s poorest while funding luxuries is indefensible. But once we recognize those urgent claims, we should not conclude that every other good is morally suspect. We should ask how to sustain a rich ecology of institutions while correcting grotesque imbalances.

For an individual donor, this may mean giving a large portion—perhaps the largest portion—to highly effective poverty or health interventions, while also supporting local and cultural institutions that serve genuine public purposes. It may mean refusing prestige philanthropy while defending public funding for the arts and universities. It may mean evaluating cultural gifts not by lives saved, but by access, inclusion, civic value, and intellectual contribution.

Singer’s essay is a needed rebuke to self-flattering generosity. But moral seriousness should not become moral monism. The good is plural. Lives must be saved; suffering must be reduced; distant strangers must count. Yet we must also build and preserve the forms of common life that make a world worth living in. Effective altruism can help us see urgent need. It should not be allowed to define the whole horizon of value.
