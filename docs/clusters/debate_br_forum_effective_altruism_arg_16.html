"While Peter Singer’s goal of maximizing charitable impact is noble, his reliance on pure rationalism ignores the psychological realities of human motivation and the emotional infrastructure necessary to build a sustainable mass movement."

**What the Psychologists Know That Singer Doesn't**

Peter Singer's essay leans, at a crucial moment, on a piece of psychological research: the distinction between donors who give substantially to one or two evaluated charities and "warm glow givers" who scatter small amounts widely. He cites this approvingly, as evidence that the effective altruist style of giving is the rational one and that ordinary charitable behavior is irrational sentiment. As someone who works on the psychology of prosocial behavior, I want to push back. The empirical literature does not quite say what Singer wants it to say, and the lessons it offers for movement-building are more interesting than his essay suggests.

Start with warm glow itself. The term comes from James Andreoni's economic models of giving, and it does pick out a real phenomenon: people derive utility from the act of giving, somewhat independently of the impact of the gift. But Andreoni's point was not that warm glow is a moral failing to be overcome. It was that giving is a multi-motive activity, in which impact, identity, social signaling, and direct hedonic reward all play roles. Designing institutions and norms that ignore this multiplicity will fail. And Singer's framework, in its purer forms, does ignore it. He treats giving as if it were a problem of cool optimization that has been distorted by emotional contaminants. The psychology suggests the opposite: emotional engagement is the engine that makes any sustained giving possible at all, and severing it in favor of pure spreadsheet rationalism is a recipe for burnout and disengagement.

Second, on the identifiable victim effect. Singer invokes the Batkid story to show that we are biased toward visible, identifiable beneficiaries over statistical ones. This is true and well documented — Paul Slovic's work on "psychic numbing" shows that our compassion can actually decrease as the number of victims grows. But Singer draws the wrong lesson. He concludes that effective altruists should override the identifiable-victim bias by relying on numbers. The more nuanced lesson, which the same body of research supports, is that effective altruism needs identifiable victims of its own. The reason GiveDirectly has been so successful at attracting donors is that it tells specific stories about specific recipients. The reason AMF's website works is that it shows photographs of children under nets. Effective communication of effective causes requires the same emotional infrastructure that Singer wants to discount.

Third, there is an underappreciated body of research on how moral demandingness affects behavior. When ethical norms are set very high — when we are told that anything short of "doing the most good we can" is a failure — the predictable psychological response is not greater effort but moral licensing, disengagement, or guilt-driven withdrawal. People who are told they should give 50 percent of their income and feel they cannot, often give less, not more, than people who are told a modest target like 10 percent and reach it. There is some evidence that the existential-stakes rhetoric Singer favors actually decreases sustained giving compared to softer framings. Whether or not "a fully ethical life" requires what Singer says, telling ordinary people that it does is not the most effective way to get them to give more.

Fourth, and most importantly: who actually becomes a sustained, generous giver? The longitudinal research suggests that the strongest predictors are not exposure to philosophical arguments about marginal utility. They are early experiences of giving within communities of giving, identity formation around generosity, religious or civic embeddedness, and direct relationships with beneficiaries or organizations. The communitarians and religious traditionalists, in other words, have been doing the empirical work of producing generous people for a long time, with techniques very different from Singer's. The effective altruist movement has been remarkably successful at attracting a particular demographic — analytically minded, often male, often atheist, often in tech and finance — but it has been less successful at the mass project of moving a society toward greater generosity. Understanding why requires taking the psychology of giving seriously, on its own terms, rather than dismissing most of it as warm-glow noise.

None of this is an argument against effective altruism. Evidence-based giving is better than evidence-free giving, and Singer is right to push donors toward the question of impact. But a movement that wants to grow, and to grow durably, needs to do more than rationally outclass the alternatives. It needs to build emotional, relational, and institutional architectures of giving — and those will look more like the religious communities and civic associations Singer dismisses than like the optimization seminars he champions. The psychology, on this, is fairly clear.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Emotional rewards like 'warm glow' are not moral failings but essential drivers of sustained giving that cannot be replaced by spreadsheet-style optimization.
  2. 02The identifiable victim effect should be harnessed through storytelling rather than dismissed, as human compassion naturally responds to individuals rather than statistics.
  3. 03Setting extreme moral demands, such as the requirement to do the 'most good' possible, often leads to psychological disengagement or withdrawal rather than increased generosity.
  4. 04Long-term generosity is more effectively cultivated through community, identity, and civic embeddedness than through exposure to philosophical arguments about marginal utility.
  5. 05To grow beyond a niche demographic, the effective altruism movement must adopt the relational and institutional architectures of traditional religious and civic organizations.
