The Trouble with Reclaiming a Concept

Adom Getachew opens her essay by quoting Michael Denning's pleasantly disarming line: "Every politician is a populist." It is the kind of formulation that has the ring of common sense and the function of an evasion. Yes, every politician courts voters; yes, every democratic appeal invokes some figure of "the people." But to slide from this banality to the conclusion that populism is a defensible — even necessary — political form for the left is to perform a sleight of hand. The critics of populism have not been worrying about the trivial fact that candidates campaign in voting districts. They have been worrying about something much more specific: a political style that claims an exclusive moral monopoly on representing "the real people," that treats opposition as illegitimate by definition, and that erodes the institutional checks that allow political losers to remain political actors. These are not pedantic concerns invented by anxious centrists. They are descriptions of what populism, in its actually existing twenty-first-century forms, has tended to do.

I want to make a case that seems unfashionable on the contemporary American left: the project of "reclaiming populism" is more likely to corrupt the left than to revitalize it. The case rests on three claims. First, populism as a concept does meaningful analytic work only when it names something more specific than democratic mass politics — and what it names, when properly specified, is incompatible with the multiracial, pluralist future Getachew and her co-thinkers want to build. Second, the historical record of left populism, including some of the cases Getachew gestures toward, is not as encouraging as the rhetoric suggests. Third, the actual political weaknesses of the left in 2020 — the collapse of unions, the fragmentation of working-class identity, the geographic sorting of the electorate — are not problems populism is well-suited to solve. They demand patient institutional work, not rhetorical reanimation.

Begin with the concept. Jan-Werner Müller's now-familiar definition of populism is not a smear but a tool: populism is an anti-pluralist politics that claims to represent the singular, authentic people against a corrupt elite, and that treats any electoral or institutional loss as evidence of betrayal by that elite rather than as a legitimate outcome. This is what unites Erdoğan, Orbán, Modi, Trump, and Bolsonaro across very different ideological terrains. Notice what this definition does not include: redistributive economics, mobilization of the poor, criticism of oligarchy, anti-imperialism, or any number of substantive left commitments. One can be a passionate egalitarian without being a populist. Eugene Debs was a socialist who built mass support without claiming that he and he alone embodied the will of "the real America." Many of the most successful European social democracies of the twentieth century were built not by populists but by patient coalition-builders who took pluralism — including the pluralism of the working class itself — as a starting condition.

The temptation to "reclaim" populism rests on the assumption that the term's anti-pluralist core can be drained off and only its mobilizational energy retained. But concepts are sticky. To call your politics populist is to invite the friend/enemy logic, the moralized division between authentic people and corrupt elite, that has tended to overwhelm whatever substantive program populists begin with. Hugo Chávez began with a real redistributive agenda; the regime he bequeathed to Maduro is a cautionary tale about what happens when you build institutions around the premise that opposition is illegitimate. One can argue this was a deviation, an accident of personality, the work of external sabotage — but the structural vulnerability was there from the start, and it was a vulnerability populism as a form invites.

Getachew points to the recent setbacks of three left projects — Morales in Bolivia, Corbyn in the UK, Sanders in the United States — and asks us to think about them in a global frame. Fair enough. But the comparative frame is not flattering to the populist hypothesis. Morales, whose ouster Getachew rightly calls a coup, had also pushed through a constitutional reinterpretation allowing himself a fourth term in violation of the result of a referendum he himself called. Whatever one thinks of the coup that followed — and I think it was indefensible — the prior erosion of constitutional self-limitation was real, and it was the kind of erosion populist logic specifically encourages. Corbyn's Labour was defeated in part because it could not assemble a pluralist coalition: it lost working-class Leave voters in the north and midlands while failing to consolidate Remain voters elsewhere, and the populist insistence on Corbyn as a singular embodiment of "the many against the few" made it difficult to compromise on the very issue — Brexit — that was tearing his coalition apart. Sanders, similarly, ran a campaign that often relied on the rhetorical structure of populist appeal — a corrupt establishment, an authentic movement, a singular leader — and discovered in primary after primary that large parts of the Democratic electorate, including substantial blocs of older Black voters, did not recognize themselves in that "people." The failure was not just tactical. It was a failure of pluralism within the left's own constituency.

This brings me to the racial dimension, which Getachew rightly insists must be central. American populism's foundational historical episode — the People's Party of the 1890s — is genuinely instructive, but not in the way the reclaimers usually suggest. Yes, there were moments of interracial cooperation in the Southern Farmers' Alliance. Tom Watson, in his early career, made remarkable speeches insisting that Black and white farmers shared a common economic enemy. But the trajectory of that movement — and of Watson himself, who ended his career as a virulent racist — is a warning, not a model. The producerist logic of populism, which divides the world into honest workers and parasitic elites, has a chronic tendency to ethnicize the categories: the "real people" become the racially or culturally authentic people, and the "elites" come to include not just bankers and bosses but immigrants, Jews, cosmopolitans, "rootless" intellectuals. This is not an aberration that occasionally afflicts populism. It is a recurring structural temptation. The American left in 2020, confronting a Trumpist movement that has fused economic grievance with racial revanchism, should be especially wary of building its own politics on a form whose history is so closely entwined with this fusion.

What about the alternative? If not populism, what? Here the answer is unglamorous: institutions. The Chicago Teachers' Union story that Getachew tells so movingly is a story about institutions — about a union that rebuilt itself from within, formed alliances with parents and community organizations, ran candidates patiently for offices large and small, and accumulated power slowly over a decade. The rhetoric of that movement has not, on the whole, been populist in the Müllerian sense. It has been the rhetoric of solidarity, of public goods, of shared interests across difference. When Stacy Davis Gates speaks about the schools, she does not claim to represent the authentic people against an inauthentic elite; she names specific policies, specific actors, specific harms, and builds coalitions that are explicitly pluralist. That is what mass democratic politics looks like when it is working. To call it populist is to obscure what makes it work.

The COVID-19 crisis, which Getachew rightly identifies as a hinge moment, does not, in my view, vindicate the populist turn. It vindicates the welfare-state tradition: the unromantic, technocratically-administered, broadly-supported provision of public goods, from unemployment insurance to public health infrastructure to universal medical coverage. The countries that are weathering the pandemic best are not the ones with the most charismatic mobilizers but the ones with the most competent institutions — Germany, South Korea, Taiwan, New Zealand. Some of those are governed by the center-left, some by the center-right, none by populists of any stripe. (The populists, from Bolsonaro to Trump to Lopez Obrador to Modi, are mostly failing their citizens spectacularly.) If there is a political lesson in this crisis for the left, it is not "be more populist." It is "build the state, defend the institutions, and persuade your fellow citizens — including those who do not already agree with you — that public capacity is worth funding."

I do not want to dismiss what Getachew and her colleagues are reaching for. The impulse behind "reclaiming populism" is the recognition that the technocratic, market-friendly liberalism that dominated the Democratic Party from Clinton through Obama has failed to produce either substantive equality or political stability, and that the absence of a confident, redistributive, mass-based left in the United States is a catastrophe. On all of that I agree. But the diagnosis does not entail the cure. The left can be confident without being populist. It can be redistributive without being Manichean. It can mobilize the working class without claiming that the working class has a single, authentic voice that the leader alone can channel.

The dangers of populism are not abstract. We are living, right now, through what its dangers look like when realized: a president who insists that any electoral or institutional check on his power is a fraud perpetrated by the corrupt elite against the real people, who treats public health expertise as enemy propaganda, who governs by grievance and spectacle. To respond to this politics with a populism of our own — even one that aspires to be multiracial and progressive — is to accept the terms of his battle and to relinquish the only ground from which he can be defeated: the patient, pluralist, institutional ground of democratic constitutionalism. That ground is not glamorous. It is not insurgent. It is not "movement." But it is, I think, where the left's future actually lies.

  3. 03The failures of recent left-wing campaigns like those of Jeremy Corbyn and Bernie Sanders reveal that populist rhetoric struggles to build the broad, pluralist coalitions necessary for electoral success.

  5. 05The success of organizations like the Chicago Teachers' Union and the effective global responses to COVID-19 suggest that the left should prioritize building durable, technocratic institutions and broad solidarity over charismatic populist mobilization.
  6. 06Adopting populist forms to fight right-wing populism cedes the essential democratic ground of institutionalism and pluralism to the opposition.
