"While Sypnowich correctly identifies the failures of liberal egalitarianism, her 'flourishing' approach remains insufficient because it focuses on individual well-being rather than the necessary structural transformation of capitalist relations of production."

Sypnowich's essay is a powerful indictment of liberal egalitarianism, but her argument remains trapped within the conceptual framework she seeks to transcend. To truly advance egalitarian thought, we need not merely to critique opportunity-based approaches but to reconnect egalitarian ambition to its historical roots in socialist political economy.

The problem with liberal egalitarianism is not simply that it focuses on opportunity rather than outcomes; it is that it accepts the basic structure of capitalist society and seeks to make it more equitable through redistributive mechanisms while leaving underlying relations of production unexamined. Rawls's difference principle, for instance, permits inequalities so long as they benefit the least advantaged. But this means that inequalities generated by the market—wage differentials, returns to capital, profit accumulation—are permissible provided they "trickle down" sufficiently. The emphasis on opportunity amplifies this tendency: as long as the formal rules of competition are fair, the substantive outcomes can be wildly unequal.

This is not an accident but a feature of liberal theory. Liberal egalitarianism emerged in the 1970s as a response to the perceived failures of welfare state capitalism and the political defeats of the left. Its theoretical sophistication—elaborate principles of justice, sophisticated accounts of procedural fairness—served to make peace with a fundamentally unjust economic order. Cohen's late turn against luck egalitarianism was recognition of this accommodation, but even he did not fully escape it.

The flourishing approach that Sypnowich proposes is superior to liberal egalitarianism, but it remains too focused on individual well-being rather than social transformation. When she writes that "the egalitarian ideal requires socialism," she gestures at the right conclusion, but she does not explain what socialism means or why it is necessary. Let me be direct: socialism means the abolition of private property in the means of production and its replacement by social ownership and democratic control. Anything less—the extensive welfare states of Scandinavia, the nationalization of key industries—does not fundamentally alter the capitalist dynamic of accumulation, exploitation, and crisis.

Capitalism generates inequality not because of bad policies or unjust distributions but because of its structural logic. The pursuit of profit requires the extraction of surplus value from labor; investment decisions are made by private owners seeking returns rather than by communities seeking flourishing; the market allocates resources according to effective demand rather than social need. These are not features that can be reformed away while preserving capitalism's essential character. The equality that Sypnowich rightly seeks requires the democratic control of investment, production, and distribution—social ownership of the commanding heights of the economy.

Moreover, the flourishing approach as Sypnowich develops it remains individualistic in problematic ways. She writes about people flourishing, about individuals taking up opportunities, about personal responsibility. But flourishing is not primarily an individual achievement; it is a social condition. People flourish when they live in communities characterized by solidarity, cooperation, and democratic participation—not when they are given opportunities to compete in markets. The socialist tradition understood this: Marx's vision of communism was of a society where "the free development of each is the condition for the free development of all." This is not about maximizing individual utility or expanding individual capabilities; it is about transforming the social conditions under which people live and relate.

Sypnowich's discussion of solidarity is valuable, but it is underdeveloped relative to what socialist theory offers. The solidarity she invokes is not merely a desirable feature of an egalitarian community; it is the foundation of a genuinely transformed society. Under capitalism, solidarity is necessarily partial and compromised: workers are pitted against each other in competition for jobs and wages; communities are fractured by the logic of market competition; even ostensibly cooperative relationships are mediated by commercial transactions. Only the abolition of capitalism creates the material conditions for genuine solidarity—the association of free and equal producers who collectively determine the use of social surplus.

I recognize that this analysis will seem extreme to many readers. The socialist tradition has been discredited by its catastrophic failures in the twentieth century, and those failures cannot be dismissed. But the alternative—a liberal egalitarianism that accepts capitalist social relations while seeking to mitigate their worst effects—has proven equally incapable of delivering equality. The past forty years have seen the steady erosion of the welfare state, the proliferation of precarious labor, and the concentration of wealth at the top of society. Liberal egalitarianism provided no defense against this regression because it never challenged the underlying structure of power.

Sypnowich is right that we need a radical alternative. But that alternative must be socialist in a substantive sense, not merely in the rhetorical invocation of solidarity. The flourishing approach provides philosophical foundations for this project, but it requires a theory of capitalist social relations to guide practical transformation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Liberal egalitarianism is fundamentally flawed because it attempts to make capitalism more equitable through redistribution without challenging the underlying market structures that generate inequality.
  2. 02True equality and human flourishing require the substantive socialist abolition of private property in favor of social ownership and democratic control of the means of production.
  3. 03Capitalism’s structural logic of profit and exploitation inherently precludes the realization of equality, regardless of how many opportunities or outcomes are reformed.
  4. 04Flourishing should be understood as a collective social condition achieved through communal transformation rather than an individual achievement or a set of expanded personal capabilities.
  5. 05Genuine solidarity can only exist once the material conditions of capitalist competition are replaced by an association of free producers who collectively determine the use of social surplus.
