"While equal opportunity is an insufficient ideal for a just society, a framework of equal flourishing must be democratized to ensure it does not become a tool for cultural paternalism or forced assimilation."

Christine Sypnowich’s impatience with opportunity talk is justified. In settler, immigrant, and multicultural democracies, equal opportunity has too often meant an invitation to assimilate into institutions whose standards were set by dominant groups. The gates open, but the road leads in one direction. Women may enter workplaces designed around male life patterns. Racial minorities may enter schools that devalue their histories. Indigenous peoples may be offered opportunities within economies built on dispossession. Immigrants may be told that success means shedding language, religion, and communal obligation. A thin opportunity ideal cannot answer these problems.

Yet Sypnowich’s alternative—equal flourishing—raises a different worry. Who defines flourishing in culturally diverse societies marked by unequal power? If the answer is “democratic debate,” we must ask what democracy looks like when some voices are louder, richer, more credentialed, and more at home in public institutions than others. A state that promotes “valuable pursuits” may correct market domination, but it may also reproduce cultural domination. The history of public efforts to improve people’s lives is not only a history of emancipation; it is also a history of assimilation, moral regulation, and paternalism.

This concern does not refute Sypnowich’s argument. It complicates it. She is right that neutrality can be a myth. Liberal states already promote values: monogamy or its legal alternatives, paid work, formal schooling, national language, private property, punctuality, credentialed expertise, and particular forms of family responsibility. Markets promote values as well: consumption, individual branding, mobility, competition, convenience. When public authorities claim neutrality, they often mask the dominance of majority norms. The question is not whether society shapes people, but whose values shape whom and through what institutions.

Equal opportunity is especially weak where injustice concerns recognition and collective self-determination. Suppose an Indigenous child has the opportunity to attend a well-funded school, compete for scholarships, and enter a profession. If that school erases her people’s history or treats her language as irrelevant, opportunity coexists with cultural injury. Suppose Muslim, Sikh, Jewish, or other religious minorities may compete equally for jobs but face workplace norms that make observance costly. Suppose Black students may access elite universities but must navigate stereotypes, isolation, and curricula that marginalize Black thought. The issue is not merely whether individuals can rise. It is whether institutions can be transformed so that different communities can belong without surrendering themselves.

Sypnowich’s flourishing framework can help here because flourishing is not reducible to income or position. It includes language, memory, ritual, land, family, art, faith, and communal recognition. People flourish not only as choosers but as members of histories and relationships they did not invent. The liberal picture of the individual selecting among options can miss the way some goods are inherited and shared. A language cannot flourish if everyone merely has the individual opportunity to learn it but no institutions sustain it. A religious community cannot flourish if formal freedom of worship exists but work schedules, zoning, prison rules, and school calendars make practice difficult. A people cannot flourish if land and ecological relations central to its identity are destroyed while its members receive equal opportunities in the labor market.

But precisely because flourishing is culturally embedded, the state should be cautious about defining it from above. Sypnowich’s music example is benign at first glance. Of course children should encounter music. But which music? Classical European traditions? Jazz, hip-hop, Indigenous drumming, devotional song, folk forms, electronic production? Who teaches it, in what setting, with what assumptions about excellence? Public arts education can democratize beauty, but it can also rank cultures. Many students have been taught, explicitly or implicitly, that their home culture is noise while elite culture is music.

The answer is not to abandon public cultural education. That would leave culture to market inequality and private inheritance. The answer is cultural democracy. Public institutions should expose students to many traditions, including those of marginalized communities, and should treat students as bearers of culture rather than empty vessels needing refinement. Flourishing cannot mean bringing everyone to the same canon. It must mean creating conditions in which many canons can be encountered, criticized, preserved, and transformed.

This principle applies beyond the arts. Education should not simply equalize access to dominant credentials; it should share authority over curriculum and pedagogy. Health care should not simply provide equal services; it should respect different understandings of body, family, mental distress, and end-of-life care while protecting individuals from coercion within communities. Urban planning should not simply offer equal housing units; it should sustain social networks, religious life, local commerce, and attachment to place. Environmental policy should not simply distribute green amenities; it should recognize that land may have spiritual and collective meaning.

A multicultural egalitarianism must therefore hold together three commitments. First, material equality matters. Cultural recognition without housing, income, health care, and safe work is hollow. Communities cannot flourish when their members are poor, overpoliced, or excluded from wealth. Second, cultural membership matters. Redistribution that requires assimilation is not equality. Third, individual dissent matters. Communities are not harmonious wholes. Women, queer people, converts, secular members, youth, and internal minorities may challenge communal norms. A politics of flourishing must avoid empowering community leaders to define flourishing for everyone within the group.

This third point is crucial. Liberalism’s emphasis on individual choice arose partly to protect people against imposed roles. Sypnowich is right that choice is socially shaped, but socially shaped choices can still be acts of resistance. A young woman leaving a restrictive family, a queer teenager rejecting religious condemnation, an Indigenous artist transforming tradition, a child of immigrants choosing a different vocation from parental expectation—all need rights and opportunities as shields. If egalitarians move too quickly from individual choice to communal flourishing, they may overlook internal domination.

Thus equal opportunity remains indispensable, though insufficient. Anti-discrimination law, freedom of conscience, exit rights, language access, voting rights, fair employment, and education for girls are not neoliberal distractions. They are tools by which people navigate and sometimes contest the communities that formed them. The task is to embed these rights in social conditions that make them real, not to replace them with a collective ideal of flourishing.

Sypnowich’s critique of responsibility also has multicultural dimensions. Dominant societies often interpret minority outcomes as evidence of bad choices: parents who do not value education, cultures that resist integration, neighborhoods that lack work ethic. Such judgments ignore discrimination, segregation, wealth gaps, immigration status, trauma, language barriers, and the social meaning of institutions. They also ignore the possibility that people may define success differently. A family that prioritizes elder care, religious study, or small business over elite mobility is not necessarily failing to flourish. Egalitarians should distinguish between adaptive preferences formed under oppression and legitimate plural values. That distinction can only be made through dialogue, not assumption.

What would a culturally democratic version of Sypnowich’s view require? It would invest in universal material goods: health care, housing, child care, income security, education, and transit. It would support minority languages, community media, cultural centers, and curricula shaped by affected communities. It would protect religious and cultural practice while enforcing individual rights. It would democratize workplaces and schools so marginalized groups have voice, not merely access. It would treat land, environment, and public space as conditions of collective flourishing. It would collect data on unequal outcomes but interpret them with communities rather than over them. It would regulate markets that commodify culture or displace neighborhoods. It would ensure that public institutions can be challenged by those they claim to serve.

This approach accepts Sypnowich’s central insight: we cannot stop at chances. If an immigrant child has an equal chance to become a lawyer but loses her language, sees her parents humiliated, and grows up in a city where her community is priced out, equality has failed. If an Indigenous person has equal access to employment while her nation lacks control over land and education, equality has failed. Outcomes matter, including collective and cultural outcomes.

But the language of equal flourishing must be democratized from the bottom up. It should not authorize the state to promote a settled list of valuable lives. It should require institutions to ask, continuously and publicly, what different people and communities need in order to live without domination, deprivation, or erasure. Flourishing is not a single destination beyond opportunity. It is a contested field of material, cultural, and political conditions.

Equal opportunity is not enough because people do not enter society as abstract competitors. They enter as members of families, cultures, neighborhoods, nations, religions, and histories. Equal flourishing is a promising corrective only if it honors that plurality and protects dissent within it. The egalitarian ideal should be neither assimilation into a fairer race nor state-defined human fulfillment, but a democracy in which many ways of living can endure, change, and meet one another on equal terms.
