"While behavioral economics correctly identifies that small incentives can rapidly reduce gender inequality, development policy must look beyond simple household 'nudges' to address the internal power dynamics, labor market structures, and social burdens that specifically affect women and girls."

One of the most encouraging findings in Glennerster and Kremer’s essay is that gender inequality can shift quickly when incentives and opportunities change. The examples are striking: scholarships for girls in Kenya improved effort not only among potential winners but among boys and teachers; uniforms reduced dropout and teen pregnancy; call-center recruiters near Delhi changed families’ investments in girls’ schooling and nutrition. These results challenge a fatalistic view of “culture” as immovable. Families are not bound forever to underinvest in daughters. When the perceived returns to girls’ education rise, behavior can change.

That is an important corrective. Too much development commentary treats gender norms in poor countries as ancient, mysterious, and uniquely resistant to policy. The evidence here suggests that norms are responsive to markets, schools, peers, and public programs. Parents who once saw little reason to educate daughters may reconsider when girls can earn wages in respectable employment. Girls who see classmates staying in school may imagine a different future. Teachers who see girls rewarded may take them more seriously. Incentives can open doors.

But a gender lens also reveals some limits in the behavioral framing. The decision to send a girl to school is rarely made by a single rational—or irrational—household mind. It is negotiated within families marked by unequal power. Fathers, mothers, grandparents, brothers, and girls themselves may have different preferences. A mother may value education for her daughter but lack control over cash. A girl may want to attend but fear harassment on the road or punishment for neglecting domestic chores. A father may support schooling until adolescence, when concerns about marriageability and sexuality intensify. To speak of “the family” responding to incentives can hide these conflicts.

Conditional cash transfers often recognize women as the channel through which child welfare improves. Payments are commonly made to mothers, on the sensible theory that women are more likely to spend on children. This can increase women’s bargaining power. But it can also add to their burdens. Mothers become responsible for ensuring school attendance, clinic visits, paperwork, and compliance. If a child misses school because the teacher is absent, because the family migrates for work, or because a girl is needed to care for siblings, the mother may be blamed. Programs designed to empower women can become systems for monitoring them.

The school uniform example is similarly double-edged. Providing uniforms reduces a direct cost and can help girls stay in school. That is good policy. Yet uniforms also symbolize the way girls’ education is entangled with respectability. In many communities a uniform may make schooling socially legible and protect a girl’s reputation; in others it may expose her to attention or impose standards families struggle to meet. If uniforms reduce teen pregnancy, is it because girls spend more time in school, because schooling changes aspirations, because relationships with older men decline, or because uniforms mark girls as students under institutional supervision? The mechanism matters, because each implies a different approach to girls’ safety and autonomy.

The HIV information study is one of the essay’s most powerful examples. Telling Kenyan teenagers that older men are more likely to be infected reduced pregnancies by older men without increasing pregnancies by younger men. This suggests that girls were not simply ignorant of morality lessons; they lacked specific risk information. It also suggests that information can increase agency when it gives girls grounds to revise choices. But here too, agency is constrained. Some relationships between adolescent girls and older men are shaped by economic need, coercion, or social pressure. Information helps most when a girl has the power to act on it. Reducing risky age-disparate relationships may require school subsidies, safe transport, enforcement against abuse, and economic options for young women as well as information.

The call-center recruitment finding deserves special attention because it points beyond nudges to labor markets. Families invested more in girls when recruiters signaled that educated young women could obtain paid work. This is not merely behavioral economics; it is political economy. The value of schooling depends on the structure of opportunity. If girls complete school but face discrimination, unsafe workplaces, or a lack of jobs, returns will remain uncertain. Programs that encourage girls’ schooling should be linked to efforts to expand employment, enforce labor protections, and make cities safe for women. Otherwise education may raise aspirations that societies refuse to honor.

Peer effects also have gender implications. When many girls attend school, the social meaning of schooling changes. A lone adolescent girl in school may be seen as deviant; a cohort of girls may be seen as normal. This is why modest incentives can have broader effects. But peer dynamics can also reinforce exclusion. If menstruation is stigmatized and schools lack latrines, adolescent girls may miss class together. If early marriage spreads through a cohort, dropout can become contagious. Behavioral insights should be used not only to exploit positive peer effects but to identify and disrupt harmful ones.

The essay is right that poor people are “more alike than different,” but gender reminds us that likeness is not sameness. Women and girls experience the costs of poverty through specific responsibilities and risks: fetching water, caring for siblings, managing illness, preserving sexual reputation, navigating violence, and absorbing shocks when household resources are scarce. A chlorine dispenser at a water source may save time for the women and girls who collect water. An immunization incentive may compensate mothers for a trip to the clinic. A school subsidy may postpone marriage. These interventions have gendered consequences even when not framed as gender policy.

For that reason, randomized evaluations should measure more than enrollment, attendance, and take-up. They should ask who controls transfers, who bears time costs, whether girls learn, whether they feel safe, whether domestic labor shifts, whether marriage timing changes, whether boys’ attitudes change, and whether women gain bargaining power. A program can increase a girl’s school attendance while leaving her exhausted from chores. It can reduce pregnancy while increasing surveillance of girls’ sexuality. It can pay mothers while leaving fathers’ responsibilities untouched. These are not secondary outcomes; they are central to development.

The most hopeful implication of the evidence is that gender inequality is not destiny. Small changes can indeed produce large shifts when they alter the perceived returns, costs, and norms surrounding girls’ lives. But we should be ambitious about what counts as success. Getting girls into school is not enough if schools fail to teach them. Keeping girls unmarried longer is not enough if they lack work and voice. Informing girls about HIV risk is not enough if they cannot refuse dangerous relationships.

A feminist reading of Glennerster and Kremer would therefore embrace their empiricism while widening the question. Do incentives work? Often, yes. Do small costs matter? Clearly. But for whom, within the household? At whose expense? With what effect on power? The poor are human beings with behavioral biases, but they are also women and men situated unequally in families, markets, and states. Development policy will be better when it designs for that reality.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of incentives in changing behavior proves that gender norms are not fixed cultural traits but are responsive to shifts in perceived economic returns.
  2. 02Treating 'the family' as a single rational unit ignores the unequal power dynamics and conflicting preferences between genders that dictate how resources like education are allocated.
  3. 03Conditional cash transfers and health programs can inadvertently increase the labor and monitoring burdens placed on mothers, who are typically made responsible for program compliance.
  4. 04The effectiveness of information and incentives is limited by the broader political economy, such as whether labor markets actually provide safe and equitable employment for educated women.
  5. 05Peer effects and behavioral interventions must be carefully designed to account for gender-specific risks, such as the social stigma of menstruation or the physical safety of girls.
  6. 06Randomized evaluations should expand their metrics to measure shifts in bargaining power, domestic labor, and female autonomy rather than just focusing on enrollment or take-up rates.
