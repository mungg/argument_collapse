"While the behavioral economics approach to development offers pragmatic solutions for poverty, it risks being paternalistic and ethically problematic by prioritizing expert-designed interventions over the agency and perspectives of the poor."

The most striking feature of the Glennerster and Kremer essay is its cheerful pragmatism. Faced with evidence that the poor often fail to make choices that would improve their lives, the authors respond by designing better nudges, incentives, and programs. This is admirable as far as it goes, but it raises questions that deserve more attention: questions about human agency, paternalism, and what we owe to the people whose behavior we are trying to change.

The authors describe a program in Kenya that provided information to teenagers about the relative HIV infection rates of older versus younger men, resulting in a 65 percent reduction in teen pregnancies by older men. This is presented as a straightforward success. But the intervention worked by encouraging teenage girls to avoid older men. Did anyone ask these girls whether they wanted to be the targets of a behavioral intervention designed to change their mating patterns? Did anyone consider the power dynamics that might make such "choices" unfree even if they were individually made?

More broadly, the behavioral economics approach assumes that it is appropriate for experts—academics, NGO officials, government technocrats—to design interventions that alter the choices available to the poor. The assumption is that the poor are making errors that enlightened policy can correct. But this framing raises uncomfortable questions about who has the authority to define what counts as an error.

Consider the decision not to purchase a mosquito net. The rational calculation for a poor family facing many competing demands might be that the expected health benefit does not justify the cash outlay and the hassle of hanging and maintaining the net. This may not be irrational procrastination; it may be a reasonable response to genuine uncertainty and genuine trade-offs. A behavioral economist who designs a free net distribution program may be improving welfare—or may be overriding the family's own judgment about its priorities in ways that are paternalistic and disrespectful.

The authors note that "we are finding certain patterns that seem consistent across contexts and cultures." But this consistency could be interpreted in two ways. One interpretation is that humans share common cognitive biases that interventions can address. Another interpretation is that the conditions of poverty are similar across contexts, creating similar constraints on choice, and that what appears as behavioral bias may be rational adaptation to constrained circumstances.

A genuinely respectful approach to development would begin with listening to the people it aims to help, understanding their own theories of their situation, and designing interventions in partnership with them rather than upon them. The RCT methodology, despite its strengths, tends to treat beneficiaries as subjects to be experimented on rather than partners in designing solutions.

None of this means that the programs the authors describe are harmful or that the evidence they present is invalid. Conditional cash transfers have helped millions of families keep children in school. Chlorine dispensers have prevented diarrheal disease. These are real benefits that deserve to be scaled up.

But the development community should also grapple with the ethical dimensions of its work. Using behavioral science to change the choices available to the poor is a form of power, and that power should be exercised with humility, transparency, and genuine respect for the agency of the people it aims to help. The essay describes an impressive body of research; what it does not fully address is the question of what we owe to the people we are trying to assist beyond efficient delivery of interventions they did not design.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The behavioral economics framework assumes experts have the authority to define what constitutes a 'decision error' for the poor, potentially overriding their legitimate priorities and judgments.
  2. 02What behavioral economists categorize as cognitive biases may actually be rational adaptations to the extreme constraints and uncertainties of living in poverty.
  3. 03The randomized controlled trial methodology often treats beneficiaries as experimental subjects rather than active partners in the design of development solutions.
  4. 04Interventions that manipulate behavior, such as those influencing mating patterns or household spending, raise significant questions about power dynamics and informed consent.
  5. 05A more respectful approach to development would prioritize listening to the poor and designing interventions in genuine partnership with them to ensure their agency is respected.
