Money as Speech: Why Roemer’s Crusade Threatens Democracy

Several times in his speech, Buddy Roemer insists that he does not want to “silence anyone or any view.” Yet the central feature of his reform agenda—a $100 limit on individual contributions and an outright ban on PACs—would do exactly that. The Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that political contributions and expenditures are forms of speech protected by the First Amendment. Citizens United, for all the outrage it provokes, simply applied that logic to corporations and unions: the identity of the speaker does not strip speech of its constitutional value. Roemer’s plan, however well intentioned, would replace the messy cacophony of a free political marketplace with a government-constricted system in which only those with existing celebrity, personal wealth, or a pre-organized mass following could be heard.

Roemer asks us to believe that if he convinced one in a hundred Americans to give $100, he would raise more money than McCain or Romney did in the 2008 primaries. The mathematics may be correct, but the assumption that a middle-of-the-road former governor can activate a grassroots donor base of that scale without the infrastructure of a major party is fanciful. Digital fundraising works best for candidates who can tap into intense, ideologically charged networks—think Ron Paul’s money bombs or the Tea Party–aligned candidates who flooded into the 2010 midterms. Roemer, whose message is essentially that both parties are equally corrupt, would struggle to generate that kind of excitement. His $100 limit would, in practice, starve his campaign of resources while doing nothing to limit the speech of his opponents’ independent supporters. The first amendment he would inadvertently write is one that protects the speech of the well-funded while rationing the speech of those who dare to challenge the establishment.

Banning PACs is an even more troubling proposition. PACs are not inherently corrupt; they are simply a mechanism through which individuals with shared interests pool their resources to speak collectively. The National Rifle Association’s PAC, for example, amplifies the voices of millions of gun owners who could never individually fund a political ad. Environmental PACs allow people concerned about climate change to compete with ExxonMobil. Roemer might respond that such groups can still speak through independent expenditures, but that concedes the point: restricting contributions directly to candidates only forces money into outside groups that are less accountable and less transparent. The result is a system in which candidates themselves lose control of their own message, and voters are left to untangle a web of unattributed attack ads.

Worse, contribution limits of the kind Roemer proposes have a demonstrated tendency to entrench incumbents. A challenger needs resources to build name recognition and convey a message against a sitting member of Congress who already enjoys the benefits of free media coverage and franking privileges. By capping what any individual can give, you handicap the insurgent while the incumbent can coast on institutional advantages. This is precisely what happened after McCain-Feingold’s “soft money” ban, as political scientists have documented: the law effectively shifted power to independent groups and wealthy self-funders, while doing little to increase the competitiveness of elections.

The deeper flaw in Roemer’s diagnosis is his assumption that money is the root of the problem rather than a symptom of a government that has arrogated to itself vast powers to regulate, subsidize, and pick winners. If Congress did not have the ability to dispense tax breaks, bailouts, and regulatory shelter, lobbyists would have far fewer reasons to spend money in Washington. As the late Milton Friedman often noted, the most effective campaign finance reform is a smaller state. Roemer’s own party history—a Reagan Democrat who crossed the aisle to cut taxes and deregulate—once understood this. Today, his focus on the dollar amounts rather than the power of government to dispense favors leads him to propose restrictions on speech that would only deepen public cynicism. Free political speech is an indispensable check on concentrated government power. We should not sacrifice it in the name of cleaning up a mess that a more humble government would never have created.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Strict contribution limits favor wealthy, famous, or ideologically extreme candidates while starving moderate challengers of the resources needed to build a viable campaign infrastructure.
  2. 02Banning PACs eliminates a vital mechanism for citizens to pool resources for collective advocacy, ultimately pushing political spending toward less accountable and less transparent outside groups.
  3. 03Low contribution caps entrench incumbents by making it harder for challengers to overcome the institutional advantages of name recognition and media coverage.
  4. 04The focus on campaign spending misidentifies the problem, as the true root of political corruption is the expansive power of the state to dispense favors rather than the money itself.
  5. 05Restricting the ability of individuals and groups to fund political messages constitutes a form of government-mandated speech rationing that contradicts the constitutional protection of a free political marketplace.
