Diversified MiniMax

"The lead essay’s proposal to 're-constitutionalize' progressive politics is counterproductive because it reinforces the very judicial supremacy and constitutional framework that conservatives have successfully weaponized to obstruct reform."

The lead essay offers a sophisticated account of how conservatives achieved constitutional supremacy and proposes that progressives counter by re-constitutionalizing politics. But the essay's core prescription—making progressive politics constitutional again—reproduces the very problem it diagnoses. By insisting that progressive goals be understood as constitutional requirements rather than ordinary political commitments, the essay strengthens the constitutional framework that has enabled conservative judicial dominance. The path forward is not more constitutional argument but less.

The lead essay's central move is to retrieve a tradition of constitutional argument linking democracy to economic distribution. Progressives should make this tradition live again, the essay argues, because silence cedes constitutional ground to conservatives. But this analysis underestimates how much conservative success flows from constitutionalization itself. When the ACA is "constitutionally questionable," when campaign finance reform violates the First Amendment, when labor law reform raises constitutional concerns, conservatives have achieved their goals regardless of whether specific provisions are struck down. The *threat* of constitutional invalidation disciplines legislative imagination. By treating every progressive policy as a potential constitutional question, the essay ratifies this discipline.

The deeper problem is that constitutional argument is not a neutral medium that either side can use equally well. The current Court has shaped doctrine over decades to serve conservative ends. When the essay proposes that progressives meet conservative First Amendment arguments "head on," it implies a level playing field that does not exist. The current First Amendment is not a neutral text; it is a conservative legal construction that progressives have been losing in for decades. Meeting these arguments "head on" means playing on a field conservatives designed, with rules conservatives have shaped, before judges conservatives appointed.

The essay's treatment of the three arenas illustrates this dynamic. On the First Amendment, the essay proposes alternatives that "are consistent with even the current Court's judge-made doctrinal Constitution." If this is true, why do we need constitutional arguments at all? The essay acknowledges that these laws "will face constitutional challenges anyway." So the constitutional consistency is not enough to protect them. The essay then pivots to arguing that "there is not only constitutional power, but a constitutional duty" to preserve democratic political economy. But this is precisely the kind of argument that a hostile Court can simply reject, rendering the duty unenforceable and the "power" illusory.

The antitrust arena shows more promise, because the essay correctly notes that antitrust is not primarily a constitutional question but a matter of regulatory policy. But then why should antitrust be part of an essay about making politics constitutional? The essay says antitrust is "constitutional in nature" because excessive corporate size "tends to hurt democratic self-government." This is true but does not make antitrust a constitutional question. The essay itself acknowledges that "constitutional arguments will...take place both inside and outside the courts." But arguments outside courts do not constitutionalize politics; they are ordinary political advocacy. The essay conflates making constitutional arguments with constitutionalizing politics.

On race and political economy, the essay makes its strongest points but again conflates constitutional argument with political mobilization. The Medicaid expansion was struck down not for lack of constitutional argument but because Chief Justice Roberts had the votes. The Voting Rights Act provision was gutted not because Congress lacked constitutional authority but because a majority of justices disagreed with Congress's judgment. These outcomes cannot be reversed by better constitutional arguments; they require different Court composition or different constitutional rules.

The essay's vision of a restored democracy-of-opportunity tradition is compelling as intellectual history. But as a political strategy, it faces a basic problem: constitutional arguments work when made by people with institutional power, not when made by people seeking such power. FDR could threaten Court-packing because he had won three elections and commanded a congressional majority. Progressives today lack that leverage. Offering constitutional arguments to a hostile Court does not build political power; it provides intellectual content for judicial rejection.

The alternative is to de-constitutionalize politics—to treat progressive policies as ordinary legislative choices rather than constitutional requirements, and to build the political power needed to defend them against judicial invalidation. This means structural reforms to check judicial power (court-packing, jurisdiction-stripping, term limits), but more fundamentally, it means shifting the terrain from constitutional argument to democratic contestation. The Constitution is not going away, and courts will always play some role in constitutional interpretation. But progressives should not make constitutional argument the centerpiece of their politics, because that ceded the field to conservatives for four decades and will not produce different results simply by reversing the direction of the argument.
