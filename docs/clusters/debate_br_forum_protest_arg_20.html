"Universities must maintain a strict distinction between subjective feelings of discomfort and objective threats of harm to preserve the right to protest and the integrity of academic freedom."

Alex Gourevitch is right to insist on a distinction that American institutions have spent years blurring: feeling unsafe is not the same thing as being threatened. A university that cannot maintain that distinction cannot sustain protest, academic freedom, or even ordinary intellectual disagreement.

The most persuasive part of Gourevitch’s essay is not his defense of any particular campus encampment, slogan, or political demand. It is his insistence that a right to protest is not meaningful if it survives only when protest is quiet, brief, tasteful, and emotionally agreeable to its audience. Protest is not a seminar paper. It is not a panel discussion. It is not an op-ed with footnotes and a civil tone. Protest is often accusatory, repetitive, loud, morally charged, and inconvenient. Its purpose is not merely to communicate a proposition but to dramatize a conflict.

That is why universities’ recent behavior has been so alarming. Many administrators have spoken as if they support free expression in principle while treating the ordinary features of protest as evidence that protest has become impermissible. Encampments were disruptive. Chants were upsetting. Students had to walk around tents. Some people saw symbols or heard slogans they found frightening. But if those facts are enough to justify police raids, suspensions, expulsions, degree denials, and criminal prosecution, then “the right to protest” has been reduced to a managed expressive privilege granted at administrative convenience.

Gourevitch is especially strong on collective punishment. Where a protester threatens someone, assaults someone, vandalizes property, or blocks access to a building, universities should act. But they should act against the person who did the thing, with evidence, due process, and proportionate sanctions. Instead, many institutions treated whole encampments as presumptively guilty. They used the existence of some ugly rhetoric, some alleged threats, and some rule violations to justify suppressing an entire political mobilization.

That is not safety. It is governance by panic.

The panic has been intensified by the instrumentalization of antisemitism. Antisemitism is real. It has surged in frightening ways. Jewish students have faced slurs, conspiracy theories, vandalism, and sometimes intimidation. No serious defense of protest can dismiss that. But the moral and legal seriousness of antisemitism is precisely why it should not be diluted into a general claim that anti-Zionist protest, criticism of Israel, or the presence of keffiyehs creates a hostile environment. When universities collapse the difference between antisemitic harassment and political protest against Israel’s war in Gaza, they do two things at once: they chill legitimate speech, and they weaken the credibility of efforts to identify actual antisemitism.

The Title VI frame has proved particularly dangerous. Properly understood, civil rights law is meant to secure equal access to education. It should protect students from targeted harassment, exclusion, threats, and discriminatory treatment. It should not be converted into a tool for suppressing political movements that make some members of a protected group feel distressed. A Jewish student’s fear may be sincere; so may a Muslim, Arab, or Palestinian student’s fear in the face of pro-Israel advocacy, donor pressure, doxxing campaigns, or official statements treating Palestinian grief as suspect. The university cannot adjudicate political expression by asking whose fear has more institutional backing.

A regime based on subjective injury will not protect the vulnerable. It will protect those whose vulnerability is legible to power.

This is the hard lesson of the last year. For decades, many progressives became too comfortable with administrative management of speech. They often assumed that speech regulation would be used against racists, sexists, homophobes, and reactionaries. Conservatives, meanwhile, spent years denouncing “cancel culture” only to embrace state coercion when the speech at issue was anti-Israel. The result is a bipartisan safety bureaucracy: the left helped legitimate the language of harm, and the right seized the machinery.

The Trump administration’s threats against universities show where this leads. Federal funding can be made conditional on ideological compliance. Departments can be targeted. Faculty can be purged. Immigrants can be made examples. Students can be disciplined not because they pose a threat but because they embarrass institutions and offend powerful constituencies. Once universities accept that the government may dictate campus rules in the name of protecting students from political discomfort, academic freedom becomes a hollow phrase.

Gourevitch’s critics will say that he romanticizes disruption. But his argument does not require universities to allow anything and everything. A serious protest regime can include rules against blocking entrances, occupying laboratories, preventing classes from meeting, targeted harassment, property destruction, and violence. It can regulate amplified sound during exams or late at night. It can require fire lanes and emergency access. It can impose modest discipline for civil disobedience. But such rules must be clear, viewpoint-neutral, and proportionate. They must not be rewritten under donor pressure or enforced selectively against one movement.

The university commons matters here. A campus is not merely a collection of classrooms and offices. It is a community where students learn democratic habits by exercising them. If the central quad is treated exactly like a corporate lobby, then the university has already conceded that its educational mission is subordinate to property management. Students should not have unlimited control over campus space, but they should have meaningful access to it for collective dissent.

The most important word in Gourevitch’s essay is “hostile.” He does not defend cruelty for its own sake. He defends the right to express hostility toward political positions, institutions, states, wars, ideologies, and movements. That right is indispensable because politics often concerns matters of life and death. To demand that protest avoid hostility is to demand that the powerless address power in the language power prefers.

A democratic university should teach students to argue, organize, endure disagreement, answer speech with speech, and distinguish danger from discomfort. It should not teach them that the first response to moral shock is administrative suppression. The current crackdown teaches exactly that lesson. It tells students that rights are conditional, that property beats conscience, that donors outrank faculty, that police are the final arbiters of campus conflict, and that the state may decide which forms of grief count as discrimination.

That lesson is far more dangerous than a noisy encampment.
