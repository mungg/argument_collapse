"The best way to oppose the Pakistani Taliban is through a sustained, disciplined military and political campaign that treats the group as an armed insurgency to be broken by force rather than a political entity to be negotiated with."

The best way to oppose the Pakistani Taliban is to treat them first as an armed insurgency, not as a debating society with grievances waiting to be understood. The attack on the Army Public School in Peshawar was not a tragic misunderstanding; it was a deliberate military-political message. The TTP wanted to punish the army for operations in North Waziristan and to show that even soldiers’ children could be reached. Such a movement must be broken by force.

Pakistan has sometimes wavered between negotiation and coercion. The 2009 Swat experience should have ended illusions. When the state ceded space to militants in the name of peace, they used it to flog women, kill opponents and expand their reach. Talks can be useful when an adversary is exhausted and willing to disarm. They are disastrous when they give militants time, legitimacy and territory.

A serious campaign requires more than dramatic retaliation. Operation Zarb-e-Azb has disrupted sanctuaries in North Waziristan, but counterinsurgency cannot be measured only in bodies counted or compounds destroyed. Militants survive by dispersing into cities, exploiting refugee flows, intimidating locals and using sectarian networks. Pakistan needs integrated intelligence, better coordination among the army, police and provincial governments, and a relentless effort to cut financing, communications and recruitment. Karachi, Quetta, Peshawar and southern Punjab matter as much as the tribal areas.

The courts are also part of the battlefield. It is understandable that many Pakistanis want swift executions after Peshawar, especially when convicted terrorists have lingered for years on death row. But speed without competence will not solve the problem. The state needs protected witnesses, forensic capacity, secure prisons and prosecutors who can win cases without relying on coerced confessions. Otherwise the guilty escape, the innocent suffer, and militants claim martyrdom.

Nor should Pakistan pretend it can fight the TTP while ignoring the Afghan border. Militants cross both ways. Islamabad and Kabul must share intelligence and deny sanctuary to each other’s enemies. President Ashraf Ghani’s outreach offers a chance that should not be squandered.

There is hope if Pakistan sustains pressure after the funerals are over. The Taliban count on outrage fading and politics returning to old habits. The answer is a long, disciplined campaign: clear militant strongholds, hold territory, protect communities, prosecute captured fighters and prevent regrouping. Mercy for schoolchildren requires severity toward those who slaughter them.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02A successful counterinsurgency requires integrated intelligence and coordination across all levels of government to dismantle militant networks within urban centers and sectarian groups.
  3. 03The judicial system must be strengthened with better forensic capacity and witness protection to ensure that militants are effectively prosecuted and held in secure prisons.
  4. 04Pakistan must cooperate with the Afghan government to share intelligence and deny cross-border sanctuaries to militant groups.
  5. 05The state must maintain long-term pressure by clearing strongholds and protecting communities to prevent the Taliban from regrouping once public outrage fades.
Diversified Claude

"The only viable response to the Pakistani Taliban's depravity is a decisive, uncompromising military offensive that treats the group as an existential enemy of the state."

Let us be honest about what happened in Peshawar and what it requires. Seven men walked into a school and methodically executed children, some by shooting them in the face in front of their classmates, some by burning a teacher alive. The Tehrik-i-Taliban Pakistan claimed the attack openly and proudly. There is no political settlement available with such an enemy. The only response that fits the crime is military.

For years, well-meaning analysts have urged Islamabad to "address root causes" — poverty, drone strikes, American foreign policy, the Kashmir dispute. This analysis has the cart before the horse. Pakistan has plenty of poor regions that do not produce mass murderers of schoolchildren. The TTP's ideology is supremacist and millenarian; it does not negotiate, it imposes. Imran Khan's PTI and the religious parties spent 2013 and most of 2014 insisting that talks with the Taliban were the answer. The militants used those talks to regroup. Every ceasefire became a recruitment drive.

What needs to happen now is straightforward, if difficult. First, Operation Zarb-e-Azb must be carried to completion in North Waziristan and extended to the Khyber agency, where the Peshawar attackers were reportedly directed from. Second, the Afghan border must be sealed in cooperation with Kabul; Mullah Fazlullah, the TTP emir, operates from Kunar and Nuristan provinces, and President Ashraf Ghani's stated willingness to work with Pakistan is an opportunity that did not exist under Karzai. Third, military courts should be empowered to try terrorism cases, because civilian courts have demonstrably failed — over 90 percent of terrorism defendants are acquitted, not because they are innocent but because judges and witnesses fear assassination. Fourth, the moratorium on capital punishment for convicted terrorists should end, as Sharif has now announced.

This is not a recipe for a liberal paradise. It is a recipe for survival. Pakistan has tolerated, fed, and made excuses for jihadism for forty years, and the bill has finally come due in the bodies of its own children. Hope against the Taliban exists, but it depends on Pakistan finally choosing — without ambiguity, without "strategic depth," without sympathetic clerics on television — to treat these men as the enemies of the state they declared themselves to be. The time for nuance ended on Tuesday morning.
