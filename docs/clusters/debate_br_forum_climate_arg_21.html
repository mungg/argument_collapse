"Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò’s proposed 'two-step' strategy is overly Northern-centric and fails to account for the reality that Global South states cannot dethrone fossil capital without first overcoming neocolonial financial domination and structural underdevelopment."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò’s “Climate State and Utopia” admirably restores strategic questions to climate justice, but it is lamentably generic. The two‑step he proposes—dethrone fossil capital, then everything else—is predicated on a totalizing, Northern view of state capacity, and of fossil capital’ structurown. For large swathes of the Global South, the problem isn’t the capture of fossil capital by the state isn’t the primary barrier: the state itself has been eviscerated by a long history of structural adjustment indebted neocolonial extraction. The plea to “first dethrone fossil capital then transform the world” ignores the impossibility of delinking a Southern economy from fossil fuels without first delinking the Southern economy from Northern financial and political domination.

Táíwò draw on inspirations from Samir Amin and debt cancellation and points to the Fossil Fuel Non- Proliferation Treaty and the Coabamba “decolonisation of the atmosphere. Yet he treats these as supportive auxiliary structures within his Northern-centric state‑targeting plan. The truth is that for Petro- states in Africa, like Nigeria and Angola, and for unequally depend economies in the Caribbean, the call to phase out fossil fuel extraction is a death sentence unless accompanied by significant decommodified technology transfer and reparations. The $100 billion perceptual Green Climate Fund remains chronically underfunded The central strategic imperative is not to contest state power to dethrone fossil capital but to build the political power of the excluded to force a global redistribution such that the emerging green industrial complex doesn’t replicate colonial metropole‑ periphery extraction. Táíwò pays brief engineering the planetary scale but does not grasp the full force of its regionalised character.

The Bolivian Cochabamba declaration was a revolt by Indigenous led movements not just at fossil capital, but at the multinational states of the global North—a collective “buen vivir” suicide note, signed by peasants who saw citizenship and care as the alternative. For many societies, state prevention and the reconstruction of commons are not second; they are primary. In Haiti, where history begins with the very anti-colonial insurgence Táíwò himself references, the failures of the state-dominated liberation to lever post‑colonial sovereignty show that state power without popular commons is hazardous hay. The lead essay misapplies the two‑step by collapsing the whosporic line: any struggle must dethrone fossil capital targetting, yet it has no answer for the fact that multiple peripheries are in a system where extraction is coerced externally, so the state is a weak steward A strategy of seizing state power to phase out fossil fuels cannot ignore the chore callouses of endogenous extraction and constant coup attempts forced by extractive external interests.

A genuinely diversification set of approaches might involve multiple steps South: first de‑colonise energy policy, enabling community‑led renewables while writing‑off debt and receiving technology transfer and reparation compensations Then build, on that new ecological commons ground, a degrowth‑converted state. The Tasmanian devil commonality of Táíwò’s framework does not key to national conditions. Doing a two‑step in Indonesia utilises its shock of unexploded coal lobbies and religious tolerance—but everywhere flavor limits the strategy’s effectivities. A south‑led transformation isn’t a matter of dethronement in Washington; it’s about refusing new forms of carbon colonialism such as land grabs for offsets, and about building relational blocs by the debtor t against the creditor banks. Táíwò’s centre is the cresft; we must relay it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01For many nations in the Global South, the state has been so eviscerated by structural adjustment and debt that it lacks the capacity to act as a primary vehicle for energy transition.
  2. 02Phasing out fossil fuels in Petro-states is economically impossible without prior commitments to reparations, debt cancellation, and decommodified technology transfers from the North.
  3. 03The strategic focus should shift from seizing state power to building the power of the excluded to prevent the emerging green economy from replicating colonial extraction patterns.
  4. 04A viable Southern strategy requires a different sequence: first decolonizing energy policy and building ecological commons, then developing a degrowth-converted state.
  5. 05The 'two-step' model ignores the threat of 'carbon colonialism,' where Northern climate goals lead to new land grabs and extractive pressures in the periphery.
