"While genetic engineering is a valid tool, it is often promoted as a technological fix for agricultural problems that are fundamentally rooted in political economy and could be more effectively addressed through agroecological methods."

**Right Diagnosis, Wrong Prescription: Why Agroecology Is Still the Answer**

Pamela Ronald makes a sophisticated case for genetic engineering, and I want to grant her many of her empirical claims. Bt cotton has reduced insecticide use. Glyphosate is less acutely toxic than atrazine. There is no documented evidence that the GE crops on the market cause harm to those who eat them. Where she goes wrong is not in defending the technology, but in implying that the technology can solve problems whose roots are not technological.

The crisis of world agriculture is not a crisis of insufficient yield. Global food production already produces enough calories to feed nine or ten billion people. The problem is distribution, waste, poverty, and the displacement of subsistence agriculture by export monocultures. Roughly a third of food produced is wasted between field and plate. Roughly a third of global cropland produces feed for livestock that converts grain to meat at terrible efficiency. The problem of hunger is, overwhelmingly, a problem of political economy, not of agronomy. Adding a few percentage points of yield to corn or soybeans does not change this. It may even worsen it, by encouraging further concentration of production in the hands of large commercial operators and further displacement of smallholders.

Ronald rightly notes that subsistence farmers face real agricultural problems — banana wilt, cassava virus, drought. But it is worth asking what the most efficient interventions for these problems actually look like, and the answer is rarely "a transgene." For cassava virus, clean-seed systems and farmer-to-farmer breeding have made significant progress. For drought, the breakthroughs at CIMMYT have come primarily from conventional marker-assisted selection, not from transgenes. For banana wilt, cultural practices — disinfecting tools, removing male buds — can achieve substantial control where they are taught and supported. The reason these approaches are underfunded relative to GE is not that they are less effective. It is that they do not generate patentable products and so attract less private investment.

This is where Ronald's framing becomes problematic. By centering the discussion on "the technology" rather than on the comparative effectiveness of different interventions, she stacks the deck. A serious cost-benefit analysis of, say, the funds invested in golden rice over twenty years would have to ask: would those same dollars, spent on home gardens, on community supplementation, on the dark leafy vegetables that are already part of many traditional diets, have prevented more vitamin A deficiency? The honest answer is probably yes. Golden rice is a fascinating technology and a humanitarian gesture by its developers, but it is also a vehicle for legitimizing GE in the public mind, and that role distorts how its actual impact should be assessed.

Consider the contrast with the System of Rice Intensification, developed in Madagascar in the 1980s and now used by millions of smallholders across Asia and Africa. SRI uses fewer seeds, less water, no transgenes, and routinely doubles or triples yields. It received almost no funding from the seed industry, was attacked by some agronomists when it first appeared, and has made its way through farmer-to-farmer networks. Or consider push-pull pest management for maize in East Africa, a polyculture system developed by ICIPE that uses companion plants to repel stem borers and trap them. Effective, cheap, regenerative, scalable, and a poor match for the patent system. Where are these in Ronald's essay? Nowhere. Because the framing is "GE versus its critics," not "what works."

I do not want to be misunderstood. I am not opposed to genetic engineering as such. If a transgenic resistance gene saves Ugandan banana farms, deploy it. If virus-resistant cassava works, plant it. I am opposed to the elevation of one tool over others on grounds that have more to do with the political and economic interests organized around that tool than with its agronomic merit. A serious sustainability agenda would be tool-agnostic, would let multiple approaches compete on the basis of actual outcomes, and would recognize that the dominance of one approach often reflects the dominance of its sponsors rather than its merit.

Ronald closes with the three pillars of sustainability — environmental, economic, and social — and asks that we judge technologies by their contribution to all three. I agree. But by that standard, the herbicide-tolerant monocultures that account for the great majority of GE acreage worldwide score poorly. They are environmentally mediocre, economically extractive, and socially corrosive of rural communities. We should be honest about that, and we should not let the genuinely promising humanitarian applications of GE — the bananas, the cassava, the papaya — serve as ideological cover for the rest. Ronald asks for a more sophisticated debate. So do I. We may end up in different places.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Global hunger is primarily a crisis of food distribution, waste, and poverty rather than a lack of crop yield that genetic engineering can solve.
  2. 02Non-transgenic interventions, such as conventional marker-assisted selection and cultural farming practices, are often more efficient than genetic engineering but receive less funding because they do not produce patentable products.
  3. 03The focus on genetic engineering ignores successful, low-cost alternatives like the System of Rice Intensification and push-pull pest management that improve yields without corporate seed dependency.
  4. 04Humanitarian GE projects like Golden Rice often serve as ideological cover to legitimize a broader industry of herbicide-tolerant monocultures that are socially and environmentally extractive.
  5. 05A truly sustainable agricultural agenda should be tool-agnostic and prioritize the comparative effectiveness of different interventions over the promotion of a specific technology.
