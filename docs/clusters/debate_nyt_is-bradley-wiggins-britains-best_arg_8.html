Diversified Claude

"Bradley Wiggins should not be considered Britain’s greatest athlete because his Tour de France victory is undermined by the sport's systemic doping history and the team-dependent nature of cycling."

Let me make the heretical case: Bradley Wiggins's Tour de France win, glorious as it was, is not quite the towering achievement the British press is making of it, and crowning him our greatest athlete on the basis of it would be a category error.

Cycling at the elite level is, to put it gently, a sport with a credibility problem. Every Tour winner since 1996 has either tested positive, confessed, been stripped of titles, or had serious accusations made against them. Lance Armstrong's USADA case is grinding on as we speak. Floyd Landis, Alberto Contador, Jan Ullrich — the roll call is dispiriting. This is not to accuse Wiggins of anything; he has been outspokenly anti-doping and submitted to extensive testing. But it is to say that any victory in the modern Tour comes with an asterisk that the sport itself has stamped onto every yellow jersey.

Compare that with the achievements of British athletes in sports whose integrity is less compromised. Steve Redgrave's five Olympic golds in rowing. Daley Thompson's two decathlon golds and four world records. Sebastian Coe's middle-distance dominance. Nick Faldo's six majors. Jackie Stewart's three F1 titles, won in an era when drivers regularly died. None of these come with the same shadow.

There is also the question of what cycling actually rewards. The Grand Tours are team events disguised as individual contests. Wiggins won because Team Sky was the strongest squad in the race, with the deepest pockets, the best preparation, and a teammate (Froome) clearly capable of winning himself but riding in support. The yellow jersey is awarded to one rider; the victory belongs to a system. That is not a knock on Wiggins — he rode brilliantly and managed his team superbly — but it complicates any claim that he singularly outclasses athletes in genuinely individual sports.

So by all means, celebrate him. He has done something no Briton has done before, and he did it with style and grace. But before we engrave his name above all others, let's see how the next decade of cycling history looks, and let's remember that "first" and "greatest" are not synonyms.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The pervasive history of doping scandals in elite cycling casts an unavoidable shadow of doubt over any modern Tour de France victory.
  2. 02Other British sporting legends, such as Steve Redgrave and Daley Thompson, achieved dominance in sports that possess greater integrity and fewer credibility issues.
  3. 03Wiggins's victory was a collective achievement of the well-funded Team Sky system rather than a purely individual feat of athletic superiority.
  4. 04Being the first Briton to achieve a specific milestone does not automatically equate to being the nation's greatest overall athlete.
