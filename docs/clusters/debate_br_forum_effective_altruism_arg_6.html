"While effective altruism provides a valuable framework for maximizing impact, its narrow focus on rational efficiency overlooks the essential role that emotional connection, community solidarity, and diverse social institutions play in a healthy moral life."

Peter Singer’s vision of effective altruism is seductively clear: use evidence and reason to identify the charities that produce the most good per dollar, and give there, often in sizable amounts. On its own terms, the logic is airtight. Yet many people, myself included, find that something vital is lost in this translation of moral generosity into a quasi-investment calculus. Singer dismisses the small, scattered donations of the “warm glow giver” as irrational, perhaps even wasteful. But warm glow giving, far from being a moral failure, reflects deep truths about human psychology and the social function of charity. An ethical life, and a healthy philanthropic culture, depends not only on efficiency but on the cultivation of empathetic connection and community.

Singer’s critique of warm glow giving relies on a sharp contrast between emotional impulse and rational effectiveness. He notes that some donors spread small amounts across many charities without investigating impact, and that the cost of processing a $10 donation may exceed its benefit. This is a fair practical point. But his analysis flattens the diverse motivations behind giving. People often donate to a local homeless shelter, a friend’s fundraising walk, or an arts program not because they are ignorant of global poverty but because these acts express solidarity, build relationships, and strengthen the social fabric. When I give to my neighbor’s cancer research fundraiser, I am doing more than transferring money; I am participating in a ritual of mutual support that affirms our shared vulnerability and care. The value of that act cannot be reduced to a numerical comparison with malaria bed nets.

Singer would likely reply that a life saved is objectively more important than social bonding. But this framing presupposes that the two are in direct competition for the same moral resources. In practice, many warm glow donors also support effective causes when they are properly motivated. The emotional energy generated by small, personal giving can spill over into broader altruistic commitments. Psychological research suggests that empathy is not a fixed pie; it can be cultivated and expanded. Asking people to suppress their natural sympathies in favor of a cold calculus may actually reduce overall generosity by alienating them from the affective roots of benevolence. A movement that treats ordinary donors as irrational may inadvertently shrink the donor pool rather than redirect it.

Moreover, the warm glow critique ignores the value of sustaining a diverse civil society. A world in which all philanthropy were optimized for QALYs per dollar would be one in which local cultural institutions, religious congregations, and neighborhood mutual-aid groups might wither. Singer acknowledges that art and education might deserve support in a world without extreme poverty, but he underestimates the importance of these institutions in maintaining the very social capital that makes altruism possible. Human beings are not self-contained utility maximizers; we are embedded in networks of meaning and tradition. The local church that takes up a collection for a family in need does not have the cost-effectiveness of the Against Malaria Foundation, but it binds a community together in ways that a direct debit to a Geneva-based NGO cannot.

Singer’s most potent example is the Batkid story. He contrasts the cost of Make-A-Wish’s fulfillment with the lives that could have been saved by an anti-malaria charity. On paper, the choice is clear. Yet even here, the narrow focus on saving lives misses something essential. Batkid was not only a gift to Miles Scott; it was a collective imaginative act that brought an entire city together, inspired thousands, and reminded us all of the power of shared magic. These intangible goods are not easily plugged into a utilitarian calculus, but they are not nothing. A society that can no longer see the worth in such gestures is a poorer one, not just culturally but morally.

To be clear, I am not arguing that effectiveness doesn’t matter. The growth of GiveWell and other charity evaluators is a welcome development, and donors should absolutely be encouraged to think about where their money can do the most good. But Singer’s dichotomy between the rational effective altruist and the foolish warm glow donor is a false one. A mature philanthropic culture integrates head and heart, blending the rigor of evidence with the warmth of human connection. Effective altruism can be a valuable corrective to unreflective giving, but if it cannot make room for the messy, emotional, and relationship-based impulses that drive so much of our best moral behavior, it will remain a niche movement for the hyper-rational few, while the rest of us continue to give not only with our wallets but with our hearts.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small-scale or local giving serves a vital social function by building relationships and strengthening the communal fabric through rituals of mutual support.
  2. 02Suppressing natural empathetic impulses in favor of cold calculus may actually decrease overall generosity by alienating donors from the affective roots of benevolence.
  3. 03A philanthropic culture solely optimized for cost-effectiveness would neglect the local cultural and religious institutions that maintain the social capital necessary for altruism to exist.
  4. 04Intangible collective experiences, such as the Batkid event, provide moral and cultural value through shared inspiration that cannot be captured by a utilitarian investment calculus.
  5. 05The dichotomy between rational effectiveness and emotional giving is false; a mature ethical life should integrate the rigor of evidence with the warmth of human connection.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Effective altruism’s reliance on impartial, utilitarian calculation fails to account for the moral importance of care, embodied relationships, and the specific needs of communities."

Peter Singer’s exposition of effective altruism is, in many ways, a triumph of Enlightenment reason. It asks us to disregard arbitrary distinctions of distance, nationality, and species, and to allocate our resources where they will do the most good, as measured by the reduction of suffering and the promotion of well-being. Yet for all its intellectual power, the movement Singer describes rests on a conception of the moral agent that is deeply partial: a detached, disembodied reasoner who surveys the world’s needs and calculates the optimal distribution of his surplus. What this picture misses is the centrality of care—of concrete, embodied relationships of responsibility and responsiveness—to the moral life. From a feminist perspective, effective altruism’s universalizing logic threatens to erode the very relational bonds that sustain us and that give morality its motivational force.

Consider Singer’s central example, Matt Wage. Wage chooses a lucrative finance job in order to give large sums to charity, forgoing a path in academic philosophy that would have been less remunerative but arguably more aligned with his talents and passions. In Singer’s framing, this is a heroic sacrifice in service of global good. But notice what is sacrificed: not merely personal fulfillment, but a life embedded in a particular community of scholars, students, and collaborators. Effective altruism treats careers primarily as income-generating vehicles, abstracted from the thick web of relationships and responsibilities they entail. Yet for many people, a career is not just a means to a donor’s budget; it is a site of moral agency where one can care for others directly—as a teacher, a nurse, a social worker. To rank such vocations below finance, simply because the latter yields more dollars to donate, is to adopt a curiously bloodless view of what it means to do good.

This neglect of care relationships becomes even more pronounced when we think about gender. The work of caring for children, the elderly, and the sick has historically been undervalued and overwhelmingly performed by women. It is not easily captured by the cost-effectiveness metrics that effective altruism prizes precisely because the goods it produces are relational and particular. When a daughter cares for an aging parent, the good she does is not fungible with a donation to a malaria charity; it is expressive of a specific bond of love and duty. Singer acknowledges that parents may permissibly favor their own children, but he defends this allowance on the instrumental grounds that close families benefit children and that strict impartiality would be unrealistic. This misses the point that the partiality of care is not a concession to human weakness but a basic moral fact. We are not, and should not try to be, equally responsible for all the world’s strangers. We find ourselves already situated in networks of obligation, and morality requires us to attend to those near us, not simply because doing so maximizes utility but because caring is a fundamental mode of ethical being.

Effective altruism’s ideal of impartial maximizing also risks reinforcing a kind of moral colonialism. The movement’s emphasis on evidence and reason often privileges the judgment of Western-trained analysts at GiveWell over the voices of those they seek to help. The Against Malaria Foundation may look highly effective on paper, but what do the recipients think they need? Do they want only bed nets, or would they prioritize clean water, education, or political empowerment? By focusing so heavily on measurable health outcomes, effective altruism can override the expressed preferences of communities in the name of a superior rationality. An ethic of care, by contrast, emphasizes attentiveness, responsiveness, and dialogue. Doing good, from this perspective, is not a matter of applying a universal formula but of listening to the specific needs of concrete others and responding in ways that honor their dignity as agents, not merely as units of welfare.

To be fair, Singer notes that effective altruists are real people, not saints, and that they leave room for relationships and personal projects. But this concession sits uneasily with the rest of his argument. The “minimally acceptable ethical life” he describes still demands that we use a “substantial part” of our spare resources to maximize global welfare. The question of how to balance that demand with the claims of those we love is treated as a matter of permissible self-indulgence rather than a deep moral tension. A more adequate ethics would recognize that the tension between care and impartiality is not a bug but a feature of our moral condition. We cannot resolve it by relegating care to the category of licensed partiality while making utilitarian calculation the gold standard. Instead, we need a moral framework that honors both the universal and the particular, the demand of justice and the call of care. Effective altruism, for all its rigor, has not yet shown that it can meet that challenge.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement adopts a bloodless view of work by valuing careers based on income-generating potential for donations rather than the direct moral agency and care provided within specific professional communities.
  2. 02Effective altruism treats the partiality of care for family and friends as a concession to human weakness rather than a fundamental moral fact and a primary mode of ethical being.
  3. 03The movement’s focus on measurable health outcomes risks moral colonialism by prioritizing the metrics of Western analysts over the expressed preferences and agency of the people being helped.
  4. 04The tension between universal utility and particular relationships is a central feature of morality that cannot be resolved by relegating care to a secondary category of 'permissible self-indulgence.'
  5. 05An ethic of care, which emphasizes attentiveness and dialogue, provides a necessary alternative to the movement's universalizing logic.
