"While the lead essay correctly identifies the limits of popularism, it overcorrects by dismissing polling and data-driven strategy as essential tools for effective political leadership."

I want to offer a defense of polling and data-driven strategy that addresses the critique in the lead essay.

Bonica and Grumbach argue that popularism—tailoring positions to polls—confuses measurement with meaning. They are right that polling alone cannot substitute for leadership. But they overcorrect in dismissing polling as a tool.

Polling serves multiple functions. It can identify which issues are salient to voters. It can reveal which messages resonate and which fall flat. It can track the effectiveness of communications over time. These are valuable functions that campaigns ignore at their peril.

The problem with popularism is not polling itself but the inference that what polls well should be adopted as policy. That inference is indeed mistaken. But the answer is better inference, not less data.

Consider climate change. Polling consistently shows that climate change is not a top priority for most voters. Does this mean Democrats should abandon climate policy? No. It means they should invest in communications that make climate salient, or accept that some priorities are values-based rather than vote-based.

The authors argue that leader-follower dynamics are more important than follower-leader dynamics. This is true in some contexts but not all. Leadership can shift preferences, but only within bounds. A leader who promises policies that voters find abhorrent will not succeed by sheer force of personality.

The empirical reality is that both leadership effects and preference effects exist. A sophisticated strategy incorporates both: it offers direction and it listens to feedback. The authors undervalue the listening component.

The anti-corruption frame the authors recommend is itself a product of strategic thinking that includes polling data. The fact that corruption registers as a concern is relevant information that should inform strategy. The authors are using data even while criticizing data-driven strategy.

A more defensible position is that data should inform strategy but not determine it. The authors' argument would be stronger if it acknowledged this more fully.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Polling provides vital functions beyond policy-setting, such as identifying issue salience, testing message resonance, and tracking communication effectiveness.
  2. 02The failure of popularism lies in the flawed inference that polling should dictate policy, rather than in the data itself.
  3. 03Leadership and voter preferences exist in a reciprocal relationship, meaning a sophisticated strategy must balance providing direction with listening to voter feedback.
  4. 04The authors' own recommendation of an anti-corruption frame relies on the very type of polling data they criticize.
  5. 05Data should be viewed as a necessary component that informs political strategy rather than a rigid force that determines it.
