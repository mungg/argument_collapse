"While Miller correctly identifies the historical abuses of federalism, her critique is too sweeping because it overlooks how the dispersion of power enables progressive policy experimentation and provides a vital infrastructure for the very mass democratic mobilization she advocates."

I want to push back on Miller's account of federalism specifically, because her critique, while accurate about its historical uses, proves too sweeping. Yes, Southern states used federalism to protect segregation. But the same federalism that enables states to discriminate also enables states to experiment with progressive policies that would be impossible at the federal level. California's climate policies, Massachusetts's health care reforms, and Oregon's pioneering work on climate action all represent democratic governance at the state level that would have been impossible to achieve nationally. The problem with American federalism is not that it creates veto points but that it creates asymmetric veto points—states with small populations have disproportionate power in the Senate and in the Electoral College, while states with large populations have fewer resources to implement the policies their residents support. Reforming federalism to reduce these asymmetries would enhance democratic accountability without eliminating the laboratory of democracy that Miller seems ready to discard. Similarly, Miller's treatment of state resistance to Trump as mere obstruction misses how federalism has enabled democratic participation that would be impossible in a more centralized system. Grassroots organizing in blue states has created political infrastructure and mobilization capacity that will matter for future national elections. The resistance to Trump has not been primarily about blocking the national government but about demonstrating that democratic alternatives exist and can be built. This is exactly the kind of mass politics Miller advocates, and it has been enabled rather than hindered by federalism's dispersion of power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Federalism functions as a 'laboratory of democracy' by allowing states to implement progressive policies, such as climate action and healthcare reform, that are currently impossible to achieve at the national level.
  2. 02The fundamental flaw in the system is not the existence of state-level power itself, but the asymmetric nature of veto points that grant disproportionate influence to small-population states.
  3. 03State-level resistance to authoritarianism serves as a constructive form of democratic participation that builds political infrastructure and demonstrates viable policy alternatives to the national public.
  4. 04The dispersion of power under federalism facilitates rather than hinders mass politics by providing local and regional arenas for grassroots organizing and mobilization.
