"The primary obstacle to American democracy is not the constitutional system of checks and balances, but rather the underlying imbalance of class power and the dominance of capital over labor in a globalized economy."

Lisa L. Miller’s lament over American veto points is an eloquent entry in a long tradition of institutional critique. Her documentation of the ways in which the fragmentation of power serves entrenched elites will resonate with anyone who has watched promising legislation die in the Senate or the courts. Yet for all its native insight, the argument remains trapped in a particularly American cage—the assumption that institutional design is the primary variable determining democratic outcomes. A broader comparative view, however, reveals that many of the pathologies Miller attributes to checks and balances are, in fact, features of contemporary capitalism in general, manifesting in very different institutional arrangements. The problem is not fundamentally about the number of veto points; it is about the balance of class power in a globalized economy where capital is mobile and organized, while labor is fragmented and disciplined by market forces. Until we address that underlying imbalance, institutional tinkering of any kind will only rearrange the deck chairs.

Consider Western Europe, which Miller invokes mainly through the lens of health care policy. Most European democracies lack the extreme veto point architecture of the United States. They have parliamentary systems, proportional representation, unicameral or weak bicameral legislatures, and limited judicial review. Yet they too have experienced a pronounced hollowing-out of social democracy, a capture of policy by financial and corporate interests, and a rise of right-wing populism fueled by popular disenchantment with unresponsive elites. The European Union’s own complex structure of checks—the Commission, the Council, the European Parliament—functionally serves as a neoliberal lock-in, enforcing austerity and market liberalization even against the expressed preferences of national electorates. The point is not that institutions don’t matter; it is that even the most responsive institutional framework will be circumvented or captured when the balance of social forces is overwhelmingly tilted toward capital.

Miller’s own evidence unintentionally demonstrates this. She notes that the United States stands alone among wealthy countries in lacking universal health care, and she attributes this to the exceptional number of veto points. But the United States is also exceptional in its degree of income inequality, its racial hierarchy, its legacy of slavery and dispossession, and its uniquely aggressive business culture. These are not incidental features; they are constitutive of a political economy in which the very idea of a universal public good is systematically deconstructed by a corporate-funded ideological apparatus. In such a context, reducing veto points might actually accelerate the downward pressure on wages, labor protections, and social provision, because a more streamlined legislative process could be captured just as easily by the Right as by the Left. The Trump administration’s ability to pass a massive regressive tax cut in 2017 through a unified Republican government that bypassed many of the usual deliberative hurdles is a cautionary tale: majoritarianism without a progressive social base produces Trumponomics, not Social Democracy.

The comparative lesson, then, is that democratic responsiveness cannot be assessed in the abstract; it depends on the organization of the working class and its allies across civil society. The heyday of social democracy in the mid-twentieth century was not primarily a consequence of parliamentary rules, but of powerful trade unions, mass parties with deep roots in working-class communities, and a Cold War context that made capital somewhat amenable to compromise. As those unions declined, as parties became professionalized and detached from their social bases, and as capital broke free of national constraints, the policy output shifted rightward in nearly every advanced democracy, regardless of constitutional design. The United States, with its enduring racial fissures and weak labor laws, was an early and extreme case, but the trend is general.

Miller gestures toward this reality when she calls for “mobilizing mass majorities,” but she imagines that mass narrowly, in terms of electoral majorities and citizen assemblies. What is missing is any sustained attention to the workplace, the site where most adults spend most of their waking hours and where the experience of powerlessness is most immediate. A genuine democratic revitalization would require a massive expansion of sectoral collective bargaining, worker representation on corporate boards, and public ownership of essential services—reforms that directly challenge the prerogatives of capital. It would also require rebuilding the institutional infrastructure of working-class power, such as unions, cooperative enterprises, and community organizing networks, that can counteract the atomizing pressures of neoliberalism. Constitutional amendments for citizen assemblies, by contrast, are a pallid substitute; they offer a venue for deliberation without any leverage over the decisions of those who control investment and production.

None of this is to say that institutional reform is irrelevant. The abolition of the Senate filibuster, as I and others have argued elsewhere, is a necessary step to unlock legislative capacity. But it is not sufficient. If a Democratic trifecta were to end the filibuster tomorrow, it would still face a party apparatus that is deeply dependent on corporate donors and that has repeatedly disappointed its progressive base. The leadership that wrote off the white working class as unredeemable, embraced Wall Street-led globalization, and treated mass incarceration as a political necessity is not likely to become the vehicle of a working-class resurgence simply because the rules change. That will require an internal transformation of the party, driven by sustained social movement pressure from below—pressure that, itself, depends on rebuilding the very organs of collective action that decades of neoliberal policy have eviscerated.

Miller ends with a call to recognize that Americans have long fought against elite power. She is right, but that fight has always been about more than institutional architecture; it has been a fight over the distribution of wealth, the organization of work, and the meaning of freedom in a capitalist society. To reframe the current crisis as one of veto points is to mistake a symptom for the disease. The dead end we face is not the design of the Constitution alone. It is the broader crisis of a global economic order that concentrates power in boardrooms and financial markets, hollows out public institutions, and leaves ordinary people feeling helpless. The way forward is not to perfect the machinery of majority rule, but to build the countervailing power that can fill that machinery with progressive content. That is a longer, more difficult struggle than Miller allows, but it is the only one that has ever truly worked.
