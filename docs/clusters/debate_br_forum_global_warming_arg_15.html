"Climate change should be addressed primarily as a matter of global justice and historical responsibility rather than as a calculation of net global economic effects or atmospheric chemistry."

The most revealing sentence in Kerry Emanuel’s essay is not about hurricanes, ice sheets, or carbon dioxide concentrations. It is this: “Sure, there will be winners and losers, but will the world really suffer in the net?” Emanuel asks the question in order to move beyond it, and rightly so. But it deserves to be confronted directly, because much of the climate debate in rich countries quietly depends on that phrase: “in the net.”

The language of net effects is indispensable for certain kinds of analysis. Economists and policy makers must aggregate; they must compare; they must estimate. But climate change is not experienced “in the net.” It is experienced by particular people in particular places with particular degrees of power. A Canadian farmer who gains a longer growing season and a Bangladeshi family who loses its home to a cyclone-amplified storm surge do not meet on a ledger where one cancels the other. A reduction in winter heating bills in Boston does not compensate a pastoralist whose grazing lands fail. If there is a moral weakness in the way climate change is often discussed, it is the temptation to let global averages hide human inequality.

Emanuel’s essay is admirably clear about the science: greenhouse gases are increasing because of human activity; carbon dioxide is far above its preindustrial concentration; the planet has warmed; sea level has risen; arctic sea ice has declined; further warming is likely; extreme precipitation, drought, and storm intensity are serious concerns. He is also appropriately cautious about uncertainty. But the next step is to ask who is most exposed, who is most responsible, and who has the capacity to respond. Once those questions are asked, climate change appears not only as an environmental problem but as an issue of justice.

The injustice begins with causation. The industrialized countries built their wealth by burning coal, oil, and gas without paying the atmospheric cost. The United States, Western Europe, Japan, and other wealthy economies have enjoyed the benefits of energy-intensive development for generations. They have filled much of the atmospheric space now being recognized as dangerous. Meanwhile, many of the countries most vulnerable to climate change contributed little to the problem. A child born today in a low-lying delta, a dryland village, or a small island state has not chosen a carbon-intensive life. Yet that child may face the consequences of choices made in London, Pittsburgh, Houston, Essen, and Tokyo over the last century.

This does not mean that developing countries are irrelevant to the climate future. China’s emissions are rising rapidly; India’s energy demand will grow; deforestation and land-use change matter; and no global solution can ignore the large emerging economies. But any fair agreement must begin with historical responsibility and present capacity. It is neither politically realistic nor morally defensible to demand that poor countries accept permanent energy constraints while rich countries merely promise gradual reform. The principle should be simple: those who emitted most and benefited most must act first, cut deepest, and help pay for a cleaner path elsewhere.

This matters because the word “adaptation” can be used in two very different ways. In wealthy countries, adaptation often means better zoning, stronger levees, improved air conditioning, revised insurance rules, drought planning, or coastal engineering. These are difficult and expensive but possible. In poor countries, adaptation may mean whether a government can build cyclone shelters, whether farmers can access drought-resistant seeds, whether girls receive education, whether public-health systems can detect disease, whether there are roads to evacuate before a flood, whether people have legal rights to relocate, and whether international assistance arrives before catastrophe rather than after. To tell the poor simply to “adapt” without transferring resources is to rename abandonment as resilience.

Emanuel’s discussion of hurricanes illustrates the point. The same physical hazard produces radically different human consequences depending on wealth, infrastructure, and power. Katrina devastated a city in the richest country on earth, and the suffering in New Orleans exposed racial and class inequalities that had long been ignored. Yet even Katrina, horrific as it was, occurred in a country with enormous resources for rescue, reconstruction, and compensation. Compare that with the 1970 Bangladesh cyclone, which killed hundreds of thousands. The lesson is not only that storms can be deadly. It is that poverty is a magnifier of climate risk.

The hydrological changes Emanuel describes may be even more politically explosive than temperature itself. More intense rainfall in wet regions, longer droughts in dry ones, and greater variability in water availability could destabilize food production and settlement patterns. Water scarcity rarely causes conflict by itself, but it can intensify existing tensions, weaken states, and deepen migration pressures. Farmers living close to subsistence cannot easily absorb a sequence of failed rains. Urban slum dwellers cannot easily escape heat waves or floods. Indigenous peoples in arctic regions cannot simply replace a melting ecological world with a new income stream. Climate change threatens not an abstract humanity, but unequal societies already marked by vulnerability.

For that reason, the common claim that global warming will produce both winners and losers should make us more alarmed, not less. Winners are likely to be those with capital, mobility, insurance, political representation, and diversified economies. Losers are likely to be those with the least responsibility and the fewest options. In a world already divided by wealth, climate change acts like a regressive tax imposed by the rich on the poor, payable in crop failures, disease risk, displacement, and disaster recovery.

What follows politically? First, the United States must stop hiding behind the inaction of others. American refusal to ratify Kyoto has often been defended on the ground that the treaty did not impose binding limits on China and India. Kyoto has flaws, certainly. But the argument has too often functioned as an excuse for doing nothing. A country with roughly one-twentieth of the world’s population and a vastly disproportionate share of cumulative emissions cannot credibly demand equal sacrifice from countries where hundreds of millions still lack reliable electricity. American leadership is not a sentimental slogan; it is a prerequisite for any legitimate global bargain.

Second, mitigation must be ambitious, but it must be designed to protect low-income households. A carbon price is necessary because the atmosphere cannot remain a free dumping ground. But energy costs are a larger share of poor families’ budgets than of rich families’ budgets. If carbon taxes or cap-and-trade systems simply raise prices without compensation, they will be regressive and politically fragile. Revenues should be returned progressively, invested in public transit, weatherization, efficient housing, and clean-energy access. Climate policy must be visibly linked to social policy, or it will be attacked, with some justice, as elite environmentalism.

Third, rich countries must finance adaptation abroad. This is not charity. It is partial repayment of a debt. Funding should support coastal protection, water systems, agricultural research, disaster preparedness, disease surveillance, and relocation planning where necessary. It should be predictable, not dependent on television images after disaster. The sums required are large but not unimaginable when compared with military budgets, fossil-fuel subsidies, or the economic losses from major storms. If we can mobilize vast resources to protect oil supply routes, we can mobilize resources to protect people placed at risk by the carbon economy.

Fourth, technology transfer must be treated as central rather than decorative. Clean energy cannot remain a luxury good. Solar, wind, efficient appliances, advanced cookstoves, small-scale hydro, cleaner coal technologies if they can be made to work, and perhaps nuclear power under strict safeguards all need institutional and financial support. Intellectual property rules should reward innovation but not become barriers to planetary necessity. If wealthy countries want developing countries to leapfrog dirty stages of industrialization, they must help build the bridge.

Fifth, forests and land use must be part of the agreement. Many poor countries are pressured to convert forests into short-term income because the global economy rewards timber, cattle, soy, and plantation crops more readily than carbon storage, biodiversity, or watershed protection. A fair climate regime should pay countries and communities to preserve forests, but it must do so in ways that respect land rights. Conservation imposed from above can dispossess indigenous and rural people. Climate justice is not served by protecting carbon in trees while violating the people who live among them.

Finally, we need democratic accountability in climate policy. The people most affected by climate change often have the least voice in negotiations. Small island states, least-developed countries, indigenous communities, and poor urban residents should not be props in speeches by powerful governments. Their knowledge of vulnerability and adaptation is practical and urgent. Institutions that exclude them will make worse decisions.

Some will say this justice framework makes an already difficult problem impossible. On the contrary, it is the only way to make a solution politically durable. A climate agreement perceived as unfair will fail. Developing countries will not accept constraints that freeze inequality. Poor citizens in rich countries will resist policies that raise their bills while leaving affluent consumption untouched. Communities asked to host wind farms, transmission lines, nuclear plants, or carbon storage sites will demand benefits and protections. Justice is not an optional moral ornament. It is a condition of consent.

Emanuel’s insurance metaphor is helpful, but again the justice question must be added: who pays the premium, and who receives the payout? If the premium is paid by workers through higher fuel costs while industries receive exemptions, the policy will be unjust. If the payout protects wealthy coastlines while poor deltas are left to drown, it will be unjust. If the insurance policy is written by countries that caused the problem and imposed on countries that did not, it will lack legitimacy. A real climate insurance policy must be progressive, global, and accountable.

We should also be honest that stopping global warming in the full sense is no longer possible. Some warming is already built into the system. The question is whether we will limit further warming and whether we will protect those who are already exposed. This requires mitigation and adaptation together. Mitigation without adaptation says to the vulnerable: wait for long-term atmospheric benefits while you suffer now. Adaptation without mitigation says: cope with a problem we intend to keep making worse. Both halves are necessary.

The moral imagination demanded by climate change is difficult because the victims are dispersed across distance and time. The people most harmed may not speak our language, vote in our elections, buy our products, or be born yet. Our political institutions are poorly designed for such obligations. But that does not make the obligations less real. If anything, climate change is a test of whether democratic and market societies can recognize duties beyond immediate transaction.

Can we stop global warming? We can stop treating it as a problem of atmospheric chemistry alone. We can stop pretending that a ton of carbon emitted for luxury consumption and a ton emitted to provide basic electricity raise the same moral question. We can stop measuring success only in global averages while ignoring who lives in the floodplain, who owns insurance, who has a passport, and who can rebuild.

Emanuel gives us a sober account of risk. The next step is to give that risk a human face and a political structure. The climate crisis will not be solved by science alone, though science is indispensable. It will not be solved by markets alone, though markets must be redirected. It will not be solved by national action alone, though nations must act. It will be solved, if it is solved, by a bargain rooted in responsibility, capacity, and solidarity.

The atmosphere has recorded the history of unequal development. Our response should not reproduce that inequality. It should begin to repair it.
