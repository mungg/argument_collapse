"While behavioral economics correctly identifies that small fees can deter the adoption of beneficial health and education products, development policy must balance free provision with long-term fiscal sustainability, political accountability, and respect for the autonomy of the poor."

Glennerster and Kremer present a powerful brief against a certain kind of market fundamentalism in development: the belief that if a poor household does not buy a beneficial product at a small price, it must not value it enough. Their evidence shows that this inference is often wrong. Small fees deter use of preventive health products; those who buy are not necessarily those who need them most; free distribution does not necessarily destroy future willingness to pay. These findings should make any honest defender of prices more cautious.

Yet the essay may swing too quickly from “prices can deter valuable behavior” to “free provision plus behavioral design is usually the answer.” Prices do several things, not all of them captured in short-run take-up studies. They ration scarce goods, signal demand, support supply chains, reduce political dependence on donors, and sometimes encourage accountability between users and providers. In poor settings, each of these functions is imperfect, but abolishing prices does not abolish scarcity. It shifts the rationing mechanism elsewhere: queues, stockouts, favoritism, corruption, or donor priorities.

The strongest case for free provision is in infectious disease prevention, where external benefits are large and the product is cheap. Mosquito nets, deworming pills, vaccination, and chlorine can plausibly belong in this category. If my use protects you, then my private willingness to pay understates social value. Here Glennerster and Kremer are convincing: small charges can be penny-wise and pound-foolish. But not every good thing in development is a public health externality. Secondary schooling, fertilizer, improved seeds, latrines, and financial services all have different mixes of private and social benefits, and the case for subsidy varies accordingly.

Even with health goods, the long-run political economy of “free” deserves scrutiny. A donor may fund bed nets today because malaria is high on the global agenda. What happens when priorities shift? A small user fee may exclude some households, but a donor-financed free system may collapse if external funding ends. Sustainability is not a slogan invented by stingy aid agencies; it is a real constraint in countries with limited tax bases. The question is not whether poor people should pay at the point of use for essential prevention. Often they should not. The question is who will pay reliably instead.

That question leads to an uncomfortable truth: many states in poor countries are weak not only because they lack money, but because the relationship between taxpayers and government is thin. User fees are a bad way to build citizenship in health care, but complete reliance on donors can also weaken accountability. Citizens may demand less from governments when services arrive as projects branded by NGOs. Governments may prefer donor-funded commodities to the harder work of raising revenue and managing systems. A development strategy built around free distribution should be paired with domestic financing and public accountability, not endless philanthropic substitution.

There is also a risk that behavioral economics underestimates knowledge embedded in local choices. The fact that households do not buy chlorine does not prove irrationality; it may reflect taste, distrust, unpleasant smell, uncertainty about water quality, experience with failed products, or competing urgent needs. Researchers can test some of these explanations, and Glennerster and Kremer do so more carefully than many. Still, a policy culture that begins from “people are biased” can become condescending. In wealthy countries, we call our own reluctance to exercise a self-control problem. In poor countries, similar reluctance may be treated as a justification for outside experts to redesign daily life.

Libertarian anxieties about paternalism should not be dismissed merely because the beneficiaries are poor. Nudges are usually defended as preserving choice. A chlorine dispenser at a water source does preserve choice; no one is forced to chlorinate. Information about HIV risk expands choice. But conditional cash transfers are more complicated. They pay poor families to behave in ways policymakers approve: send children to school, visit clinics, comply with schedules. Those behaviors may be beneficial, but conditions can also imply distrust. Wealthier citizens receive many public benefits without proving their virtue at regular intervals. Why should the poor be subject to behavioral supervision?

One response is that conditions make transfers politically acceptable. Taxpayers, whether in Mexico or donor countries, may support cash for the poor if it is tied to education and health. Another response is that conditions can solve intra-household conflicts: a parent who wants to keep a child in school gains a reason to resist pressure for child labor. These are serious arguments. But policymakers should compare conditional transfers with unconditional ones, and with broader child allowances or public services. If the objective is poverty relief, unconditional cash may respect autonomy more. If the objective is school attendance, conditions may be justified. Clarity about objectives matters.

The essay’s examples of timing are especially useful because they suggest a less paternalistic path: help people do what they already want to do. If families value schooling but cannot assemble fees when due, then savings mechanisms, commitment accounts, or timed transfers may be better than lectures. If farmers want fertilizer but spend cash before planting season, offer ways to prepay after harvest. Such tools respect the fact that people may understand their own self-control problems and welcome commitment devices. The Kenyan farmers who choose fewer options are not irrational dupes; they are sophisticated about their own limitations.

A market-oriented lesson from the evidence would therefore not be “charge fees” but “design markets around real constraints.” Products should be available in small units, at convenient locations, at times when households have cash. Sellers should build trust through trials and guarantees. Subsidies should be transparent and targeted where social benefits justify them. Information should be credible and specific. Where free distribution is warranted, it should be financed predictably and monitored for leakage. Behavioral economics can improve markets as well as justify public provision.

The authors are right to reject the claim that payment automatically creates value. That idea has caused real harm when applied mechanically to essential health goods. But there is an opposite mistake: assuming that high take-up at a zero price demonstrates the best policy. Of course people take more when something is free. The real question is whether the social benefits exceed the full costs, including distribution, administration, distortion, and sustainability. For many preventive health interventions, the answer is yes. For others, it may not be.

Development policy should treat poor people neither as perfect consumers nor as patients to be nudged into compliance. They are decision-makers operating under severe constraints, often with better knowledge of their circumstances than outsiders possess, but also with the same human frailties as everyone else. The challenge is to build institutions—public, private, and civic—that expand their options without infantilizing them.

“Small Changes, Big Results” is most persuasive when it asks us to remove needless barriers. It is less persuasive if read as an invitation to replace markets with clever subsidies wherever take-up disappoints. Prices can be cruel, but they can also carry information. Free goods can save lives, but they can also create dependence on weak delivery systems. The hard task is not choosing ideology. It is deciding, case by case, where public health, autonomy, fiscal realism, and human behavior point in the same direction.
