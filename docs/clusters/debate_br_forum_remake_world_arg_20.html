Plantation, Anthropocene, Earth

The sixth of Walter Johnson's "principal virtues" is the one I want to take up: the suggestion that an account of justice rooted in racial capitalism can connect a critique of the instrumentalization of human beings to a critique of the instrumentalization of nature, "over and against many recent efforts which assert that a forthright treatment of global environmental history requires the elevation of the categories of the 'human' and the 'Anthropocene' over and against other historical categories." This is an important intervention, and I want to develop it further than Johnson's essay has space to do.

The debate Johnson is alluding to is the debate over the Anthropocene — the proposed geological epoch defined by human impact on the Earth system. Scholars working in environmental humanities have, over the past decade, raised the question of whether "the human" is the right unit of analysis for the planetary crisis we are confronting. The argument that has emerged from critical quarters — Donna Haraway, Jason Moore, Kathryn Yusoff, T. J. Demos, and others — is that the Anthropocene framing obscures more than it illuminates. The "anthropos" that has driven planetary transformation is not humanity in general but particular human populations, organized in particular economic systems, pursuing particular forms of accumulation. To say "humans" caused climate change is to occlude the historical fact that the carbon emissions in question were generated overwhelmingly by particular polities (the industrialized North Atlantic), particular classes (the owners of fossil capital), and particular projects (industrial capitalism, colonial extraction, the postwar consumer society).

Alternatives have been proposed. Moore has argued for "Capitalocene," locating the geological signature of human impact in the rise of capitalism specifically rather than in human activity generally. Haraway has proposed "Chthulucene" and, more pointedly for our purposes, "Plantationocene," arguing that the plantation — as a specific form of biological and labor organization — is the deeper origin of the planetary patterns we are now living with. Yusoff, in work that is just beginning to circulate, has argued that geology itself, as a science and as a mode of relating to the Earth, is implicated in the racial and colonial structures that the Anthropocene framing tends to obscure.

Johnson's essay sits comfortably alongside these alternatives without quite endorsing any of them by name. What he offers is a framework — racial capitalism — that can do similar work to "Capitalocene" or "Plantationocene" but with more historical specificity than either. The strength of Johnson's framework, for environmental purposes, is that it locates the origin of the planetary transformation we are now living through in a specific set of historical processes: the integration of African enslavement, Indigenous dispossession, and fossil-powered industrial production into a single global system of accumulation. The plantation, the mill, and the railroad were not three separate developments that happened to coincide; they were components of a single system that, taken together, set the planet on its current trajectory.

Let me develop this with some specifics. The cotton plantation of the antebellum South was, by the standards of its time, one of the most ecologically transformative institutions on Earth. The clearing of forest for cotton cultivation, the exhaustion of soil that drove the constant westward expansion of the plantation frontier, the displacement of native ecologies by monocultural production, the introduction of new species (cotton itself, but also the various plants and animals that traveled with the plantation system) — these were not side effects of plantation production but constitutive features of it. The plantation was, among other things, a machine for converting ecological diversity into commodity uniformity, with consequences for soil, water, biodiversity, and climate that we are still living with.

Crucially, the plantation also pioneered modes of biological and labor organization that have been generalized far beyond their original site. The disciplined, time-managed, output-optimized organization of labor that we associate with industrial capitalism was, as historians like Caitlin Rosenthal have argued, developed first on the plantation. The use of detailed accounting techniques to measure and increase labor output; the systematic breeding of plants and animals (and, monstrously, of human beings) for productive characteristics; the management of large populations in confined spaces — these technologies of biopower were refined on the plantation before they were generalized to the factory, the feedlot, and the modern industrial farm. Haraway's term "Plantationocene" tries to capture this lineage: the planetary patterns of biological management we now live within have a specific origin in the plantation form, and that form was racial from its inception.

If we follow this line of thought, the planetary environmental crisis is not detachable from the racial-capitalist history Johnson narrates. The carbon emissions that are now altering the Earth's climate were generated by an industrial economy whose original capitalization came from plantation slavery. The species extinctions we are now witnessing trace back, in significant part, to land-use patterns established by plantation agriculture and colonial extraction. The vulnerabilities to climate disruption are distributed along racial and colonial lines: the populations most exposed to rising seas, intensifying storms, agricultural disruption, and heat stress are predominantly the populations whose ancestors bore the brunt of the racial-capitalist accumulation that generated the crisis in the first place. This is not coincidence. It is the continuing operation of the system Johnson describes.

What follows for the politics of climate? Several things, I think.

First, climate justice cannot be reduced to a question of emissions reduction abstractly considered. The "we" of humanity-in-general that the Anthropocene framing posits is not the relevant political subject. The relevant subjects are differentiated: those most responsible for emissions, those most vulnerable to consequences, those whose forms of life have been most destroyed by the system that generated the crisis. A climate politics adequate to the moment must hold these differentiations at its center.

Second, climate redress has a reparative dimension that is continuous with the reparative dimension of the racial-capitalist accumulation that produced the crisis. The Global North owes the Global South not just emissions reductions but transfers — of resources, of technology, of accumulated knowledge — that respond to the historical fact of asymmetric extraction. The "loss and damage" framework that has been struggling for traction in international climate negotiations is one institutional expression of this. So is the broader argument that climate finance must be additional to, not substitutive for, development aid that was itself inadequate compensation for colonial extraction.

Third, the response to the climate crisis cannot be technocratic in a way that leaves the underlying system intact. Carbon capture, geoengineering, market-based emissions trading — these can be parts of a response, but they cannot be the whole response, because they leave intact the patterns of differential extraction and vulnerability that constitute the deeper problem. A genuinely transformative climate politics has to be a politics of system change, in something close to the sense the climate-justice movement has been arguing.

Fourth, the relationship between ecological domination and racial domination is not, as Johnson rightly insists, analogical. It is historical and structural. The same processes that produced the racialization of human populations also produced the instrumentalization of nature, and the categories that organized one organized the other. Sylvia Wynter has argued that the Western conception of "Man" — bourgeois, rational, propertied, white — was constructed in opposition not just to non-Western humans but to nature, and that the same conceptual operation that produced racial hierarchy produced the domination of the natural world. The undoing of one is bound up with the undoing of the other.

This is where I think Johnson's framework can do more than he claims. It can serve not just as a framework for understanding the history of racial capitalism but as a framework for understanding the planetary crisis we are now confronting. The "world-wasting race to the end of time" Johnson invokes is not a metaphor; it is a description of the trajectory the system is on. The plantation, the slave ship, the cotton mill, the coal pit, the oil well, the suburban subdivision, the data center — these are episodes in a single history, and that history is now bringing us up against planetary limits.

The political implication is not despair. It is that the work of dismantling racial capitalism and the work of addressing the planetary crisis are, properly understood, the same work. The frameworks that treat them as separate — environment over here, justice over there — are inadequate to both. The frameworks that treat them as continuous — racial capitalism, Plantationocene, climate justice — are doing the deeper diagnostic work the moment requires. Johnson's essay points in this direction. The next step is to make the connection explicit and to develop the politics that follows from it.
