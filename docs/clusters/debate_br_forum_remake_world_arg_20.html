"While Walter Johnson’s theoretical reframing of slavery and racial capitalism is intellectually irrefutable, its academic abstraction and specialized language limit its effectiveness as a tool for actual political struggle and social change."

There is a moment in Walter Johnson’s essay that made me catch my breath. It comes near the beginning, when he describes, in a single, devastating sentence, the violence of the slaveholder: “swing a baby by the feet into a post to silence its cries, or jam the broken handle of a hoe down the throat of a field hand.” These images are not new to anyone who has studied the history of slavery, but Johnson’s prose gives them a terrible freshness. He wants us to see what slavery looked like—not the abstract institution, not the statistics on cotton exports and collateralized debt, but the hands-on, intimate, bodily violation that was the daily reality of life for millions of human beings. And he wants us to understand that this violence was not the product of a failure to recognize the humanity of the enslaved, but rather the product of that recognition. The slaveholder who jammed a hoe handle down a throat knew exactly what he was doing: he was torturing a person, because the person’s capacity to suffer was the point.

This reframing is the core of Johnson’s essay, and it is, I think, irrefutable. But having established it, Johnson does something unexpected, and to my mind unfortunate. He moves, with hardly a pause, from the visceral horror of the hoe handle to an extended discussion of Marxist theory, human rights, and the historiography of capitalism. By the time we reach the end of the essay, the baby and the field hand have receded into the background, replaced by Du Bois and Robinson and the six virtues of a justice rooted in the history of slavery. The abstraction that Johnson criticizes in other historians—the tendency to treat enslaved people as exemplars of a theory rather than as specific, suffering individuals—has crept back into his own argument.

I do not mean this as a cheap shot. Johnson is writing a theoretical essay, not a narrative history, and he has every right to operate at the level of abstraction that the genre requires. But his choice of genre matters, because it reflects a set of assumptions about what kind of argument is most likely to produce change. Johnson seems to believe that the way to advance the cause of justice is to provide a better theory—a more adequate account of racial capitalism, a more historically grounded notion of rights, a more comprehensive framework for reparations. The essay is, in this sense, an intervention in an academic debate, addressed primarily to other scholars and intellectuals. The hope, presumably, is that better ideas will trickle out into the wider culture and eventually shape the terms of political action.

I am not convinced. The history of social movements suggests that ideas matter, but they matter less than solidarity, organization, and the willingness to take risks. The abolition of the slave trade, the end of Jim Crow, the expansion of labor rights—none of these things happened because the right theoretical framework finally emerged. They happened because people organized, protested, struck, boycotted, and sometimes fought. They happened because the moral claims of the oppressed became impossible to ignore, not because those claims were grounded in a sophisticated account of racial capitalism. Frederick Douglass did not need to read Marx to understand that slavery was wrong, and he did not need to read Du Bois to understand that the struggle for freedom was inseparable from the struggle for political power. He needed courage, and he needed comrades, and he needed a language that could reach people whose minds were not yet made up.

Johnson’s language, for all its power, is not that kind of language. It is the language of the seminar room, not the street corner. The sentences are long and intricate, full of qualifiers and subordinate clauses. The argument unfolds through a series of engagements with other scholars, most of whom will be unknown to readers outside the academy. The key terms—“dehumanization,” “human rights,” “racial capitalism”—are defined with scrupulous care, but they are not terms that resonate in the lives of the people on whose behalf Johnson claims to speak. I am not saying that Johnson should write differently; the essay is what it is, and it does what it does very well. I am saying that we should not mistake a contribution to an academic debate for a contribution to a political struggle.

This distinction between the academic and the political is not neat, and I do not want to overstate it. Ideas developed in the academy can and do shape political movements; the work of legal scholars on critical race theory, for example, has had a significant impact on the way activists talk about systemic racism. And political movements can and do generate ideas that transform the academy; the black studies movement of the 1960s is a case in point. There is traffic in both directions, and Johnson’s essay is part of that traffic. But I worry that the traffic is increasingly one-way: that the academy absorbs the energy of political movements and transmutes it into a specialized discourse that is then circulated among a narrow audience of the like-minded. The radical critique of racial capitalism becomes a genre of academic writing, with its own conventions, its own canon, its own career incentives. The urgency of the street is lost in the quiet of the library.

Johnson, to his credit, seems aware of this danger. His essay is an explicit attempt to bridge the gap between academic historiography and political activism, between the “new history of capitalism” and the Movement for Black Lives. He wants to show that the history of slavery is not just a subject for scholarly inquiry but a standpoint from which to demand a different kind of justice. The question is whether the essay succeeds in this ambition, and I am not sure it does. The theory is elegant, but the practice is missing. We learn a great deal about what Johnson thinks is wrong with the world, but very little about what he thinks we should do about it.

If I were to offer a constructive suggestion, it would be this: that the next step in Johnson’s project should be a reckoning with the problem of persuasion. Who needs to be persuaded in order for the movement for racial justice to succeed, and what arguments are most likely to persuade them? The readers of the *Boston Review* are, by and large, already sympathetic to Johnson’s general outlook. We are not the ones who need to be convinced that slavery was a capitalist enterprise or that reparations are a legitimate demand. The people who need to be convinced are the ones who do not read the *Boston Review*: the white working-class voters in the Midwest who swung the 2016 election, the small-business owners who fear that racial justice will come at their expense, the suburban parents who support diversity in principle but balk at concrete measures like school integration or affordable housing. Reaching those people requires a different kind of argument—not necessarily a less radical one, but one that speaks to their concerns and experiences in a language they can understand.

This is not, I want to emphasize, an argument for moderation or respectability politics. It is an argument for strategic communication. The history of successful social movements is, in part, a history of framing: of finding ways to present radical demands in terms that resonate with widely held values. The civil rights movement did not simply denounce white supremacy; it appealed to the American creed of liberty and justice for all. The labor movement did not simply denounce capitalism; it appealed to the dignity of work and the rights of working people. The Movement for Black Lives has, at its best moments, done something similar: linking the specific experience of anti-Black racism to broader concerns about police militarization, economic inequality, and the corruption of American democracy.

Johnson’s essay is, in its way, a contribution to this project of framing. It is an attempt to shift the terms of debate, to establish new intellectual foundations for the movement. And on that level, it succeeds. But I hope that Johnson, and others who share his commitments, will also attend to the unglamorous work of persuasion: the door-knocking, the town halls, the op-eds in local newspapers, the conversations with skeptical relatives over holiday dinners. Theory is necessary, but it is not sufficient. The world will not be remade in the seminar room.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Johnson’s shift from the visceral, bodily reality of slavery to high-level Marxist and historiographical theory inadvertently replicates the very abstraction he criticizes in other historians.
  2. 02Social progress is historically driven by solidarity, organization, and strategic communication rather than the emergence of superior academic frameworks.
  3. 03The essay’s dense, scholarly language is suited for the seminar room but fails to resonate with or persuade the broader public necessary for a successful political movement.
  4. 04The academy risks absorbing the energy of radical political movements and transmuting it into a specialized discourse that circulates only among a narrow, like-minded audience.
  5. 05A truly effective pursuit of justice requires a reckoning with the problem of persuasion and the development of arguments that speak to the concerns of skeptical outsiders.
