"Debates over candidate ideology and messaging are a distraction because the primary threat to American democracy is a structural electoral system that is fundamentally rigged against the Democratic coalition."

If there is one thing on which both the authors of “How Not to Defeat Authoritarianism” and the popularists they critique agree, it is that elections are won and lost on the basis of mass public opinion. They just disagree about what that opinion is and how to respond to it. But both camps miss something more fundamental: the structural deck is so stacked against the Democratic coalition that tactical debates over candidate ideology are a dangerous distraction from the only reforms that can save American democracy—reforms to the electoral system itself.

Bonica and Grumbach are persuasive that in the Trump era, the electoral payoff to moderation has dwindled to near-zero. They are also right that the old median-voter logic has been scrambled by nationalization, polarization, and differential turnout. But their prescription—reframe the battlefield, build anti-establishment credibility, boost turnout—is yet another in a long line of strategies that treat the existing rules of the game as fixed. What the country needs instead is a sustained movement to change those rules: abolish the Electoral College, adopt proportional representation for the House, expand the Senate or move toward a more proportional upper chamber, and enact automatic voter registration with a robust nonpartisan election administration.

The 2024 election underscored the futility of trying to win within a rigged system. Democrats won the national popular vote by several percentage points—exact numbers are still debated given the chaos around certification in some states, but it was clearly a popular majority—yet lost the presidency, the Senate, and the House. This is not a fluke; it’s a feature. The Senate’s malapportionment gives a Wyoming voter roughly 70 times the power of a California voter. The Electoral College similarly tilts the playing field, while gerrymandering and the House’s cap of 435 members further distort representation. Under these conditions, talk of a “turnout crisis” among Democrats is wildly incomplete: it is indeed a crisis that Democrats can’t seem to turn out enough of their supporters in key states, but the deeper problem is that even when they do, the system negates those votes.

Focusing on candidate ideology or message frames implicitly assumes that if Democrats just run the right kind of campaign, they can overcome these structural biases. The authors’ own data suggests otherwise: for example, they note that progressive candidates slightly boost turnout among registered Democrats, yet this effect is overwhelmed by national tides and the electoral map. In a truly representative democracy, such a turnout advantage would translate into governing power. Instead, it likely produces little more than an extra percentage point in the popular vote while the Senate remains solidly Republican.

Some might object that structural reform is politically impossible because Republicans would never agree to it. This is true in the short run, but every major expansion of democracy in American history—from the abolition of property requirements to women’s suffrage to the Voting Rights Act—was achieved by movements that refused to accept the existing rules as permanent. The authors are right that the moment calls for a reframing of the battlefield. But the most powerful reframe is not corruption; it is democracy itself. A democracy frame can unite fragmented opposition: it speaks directly to the authoritarian threat, it gives a reason for young and disengaged voters to mobilize (they know the system is broken, but they need to see a path to fixing it), and it can even appeal to traditional conservatives who worry about minority rule and arbitrary government.

An anti-corruption message, as the authors propose, is a subset of this broader democracy frame. Corruption is a symptom of a system in which concentrated power and unrepresentative institutions allow elites to capture government. But putting corruption at the center risks narrowing the focus to individual bad actors—the Clarence Thomases and insider traders—rather than the institutional design that enables them. A democracy agenda, by contrast, runs the gamut from fighting voter suppression and gerrymandering to publicly funding elections, ending the filibuster, and ultimately constitutional change. It’s an expansive, hopeful vision that can be paired with the economic populism many progressives rightly demand, because economic power and political power are inseparable.

The authors briefly nod to institutional reform when they mention “genuine, costly reform” like banning congressional stock trading. But that’s a far cry from a full-throated call for proportional representation. Here, the international experience is instructive. Countries that have weathered authoritarian threats—such as Germany after the war or, more recently, small-d democratic reformers in places like Taiwan and the Czech Republic—did not merely tweak their messaging. They rewrote electoral rules to ensure that majoritarian backlash against democracy could not easily entrench itself. Many established proportional systems after periods of crisis specifically to prevent the “winner-take-all” dynamic that can hand an authoritarian faction total control with a minority of the vote.

What would a democracy-focused Democratic Party look like? It would make fighting for the For the People Act (or its successor) the central legislative priority, even if it failed, to educate the public. It would champion a constitutional amendment to abolish the Electoral College, force repeated votes, and campaign on it in every election cycle until it became a defining national issue. It would recruit and support state-level candidates committed to ending partisan gerrymandering and adopting ranked-choice voting. And it would talk about democracy not as a dry civics lesson but as a moral fight against oligarchs who want to rig the game. This message could energize exactly the sporadic voters the authors worry about—the young, the poor, people of color—who have the most to gain from a system that actually counts their votes.

Skeptics will say that most voters don’t care about process issues. That’s a half-truth. Voters may not walk around thinking about the Electoral College, but they do feel that the system is broken and that their voice doesn’t matter. The authors cite polling that 73 percent of Americans think a member of Congress would accept a bribe. That’s not just about bribery; it’s about the sense that the whole shooting match is rigged. A democracy frame doesn’t ask voters to get excited about legislative procedure; it channels their frustration into a tangible solution: make this a real democracy where the majority governs. That’s a far more resonant call than “government corruption,” which too often sounds like yet another Washington ethics scandal that changes nothing.

Moreover, structural reform offers a strategic benefit that candidate-centered strategies do not: it works even in a heavily polarized, nationalized environment. The authors concede that in the Trump era, candidate ideology barely moves the needle because voters are locked into partisan identities. But electoral system design shapes the very nature of the partisan contest. By building a multi-party system through proportional representation, the rigid binary would soften, reducing the zero-sum, existential tenor of American politics and making it harder for authoritarian demagogues to capture an entire party. The path to that world is long, but the first step is recognizing that no single candidate, no matter how authentic or ideologically positioned, can overcome the deep structural incentives toward minority rule and democratic decay.

The authors close with a stark warning: failure means the end of democratic self-governance. I agree. But the most profound failure would be to keep trying to win a game that is designed for one side to lose, when the only genuine victory is to change the game entirely.

  3. 03A 'democracy frame' is more politically potent than an anti-corruption frame because it addresses the institutional design that enables elite capture rather than just focusing on individual bad actors.
  4. 04Structural reform is the only effective strategy in a hyper-polarized environment because it changes the incentives of the partisan contest rather than relying on individual candidate quality which currently has little impact.
  5. 05International precedents show that countries successfully resisting authoritarianism do so by rewriting electoral rules to prevent minority rule and winner-take-all dynamics.

"The debate over political moderation is a distraction from the real issue: the American constitutional order is fundamentally broken and requires structural revolution rather than strategic messaging to defeat authoritarianism."

I come not to praise Bonica and Grumbach, nor to bury them, but to suggest that their energy is misdirected. The academic pissing match over whether moderation “works” is a colossal waste of time for anyone actually trying to stop the authoritarian turn. The problem isn’t that Democrats are following the wrong strategy; it’s that the American constitutional order is fundamentally broken in ways that strategic voting cannot fix. The only way out is constitutional revolution.

The essay does an admirable job of dismantling the popularist illusion that you can poll your way to victory. It rightly notes that elections are national referendums, that candidate ideology has minimal effect, and that the median voter theorem is a historical artifact. But then it pivots to a new strategic hope: anti-corruption as a mobilizing frame. This is like rearranging deck chairs on the Titanic while ignoring the iceberg that just tore a gash in the hull. The iceberg is our constitutional system: the Electoral College, the Senate, the Supreme Court’s life tenure, the filibuster, gerrymandering, and the pervasive role of money in politics. All of these are designed, whether intentionally or by accretion, to empower a minority to govern over a majority, and they are now being weaponized by an authoritarian movement that has no interest in fair play.

The authors ask, “What should Democrats do?” as if the party’s strategy were the independent variable. But the Democratic Party is trapped within the same decaying architecture as everyone else. Even when it wins the House and the presidency, it cannot govern in a way that builds durable support because the Senate and the Court block transformative legislation. Even when it passes popular laws—like the Inflation Reduction Act—those laws are too modest, too means-tested, too delayed to be felt by ordinary people in time for the next election. As a result, the party perpetually fails to deliver on its promises, reinforcing the cynicism that the authors correctly identify as driving voter withdrawal. The problem isn’t message; it’s capacity. And capacity is constitutional.

No amount of anti-corruption rhetoric will fix the fact that a voter in Wyoming has the Senate power of 70 Californians, or that the Electoral College can hand the White House to the loser of the popular vote, or that five unelected justices can strike down entire regulatory regimes. Until those structures are changed, any democratic victory is provisional and any popular mandate can be reversed by a counter-majoritarian institution. So the first and most urgent task of any anti-authoritarian movement is to make constitutional reform its central demand. Not as a long-term aspiration, but as the immediate, non-negotiable condition for democracy’s survival.

The authors are hesitant to go there. They mention “genuine, costly reform” but only in the context of stock trading and campaign finance—important but marginal. They don’t call for abolishing the Electoral College, reforming the Supreme Court, ending the filibuster, or admitting new states to partially correct the Senate’s malapportionment. This is a failure of nerve. The public is ready for structural reform. Polls have shown consistent support for ending the Electoral College, for imposing term limits on Supreme Court justices, and for curtailing the filibuster. And the experience of living under a second Trump presidency, with its lawless actions and capture of the Justice Department, has only increased the public’s appetite for constraints on executive power and for more responsive institutions.

The anti-corruption frame could be powerful precisely if it is embedded in a constitutional reform agenda. Corruption isn’t just individual bad actors; it’s the systemic legalized bribery of campaign finance, the revolving door between government and industry, the gerrymandered districts that make incumbents unaccountable. By linking the fight against corruption to the fight for democratic institutional redesign, you can channel diffuse anger into a concrete set of changes that would actually alter the rules of the game. But that requires Democrats to be willing to wield power aggressively when they have it, which they have consistently failed to do. The Biden administration had a unique opportunity in 2021-22 to nuke the filibuster and pass voting rights and democracy reform. It balked. That failure neutered the party’s credibility on democracy and paved the way for the 2024 disaster. No new frame can make up for a party leadership that is unwilling to break norms to save the system.

History is instructive here. The last time the United States faced a comparable crisis of democratic legitimacy was the period leading up to the Civil War, which was fundamentally a constitutional crisis over the expansion of slavery. It was not resolved by better messaging but by constitutional transformation—the Reconstruction Amendments. And the earlier struggle against the oligarchic First Gilded Age resulted in the Progressive Era amendments: direct election of senators, the income tax, women’s suffrage. In each case, structural change was the prize that allowed democratic forces to rebalance power.

So what would a constitutional revolution strategy look like? The Democratic Party would make statehood for Washington, D.C., and Puerto Rico a central campaign promise and priority. It would commit to adding seats to the Supreme Court as a check on its radical right-wing majority. It would propose a constitutional amendment making the popular vote decisive for presidential elections, and it would push for a federal law mandating independent redistricting commissions. It would, in other words, make democracy itself the content of its politics, not just the container. This is not a distraction from kitchen-table issues; it’s the prerequisite for being able to address them. No healthcare plan, no climate policy, no labor law reform is safe as long as a minority faction can veto it or an authoritarian president can undo it by executive fiat.

The authors’ call for experimentation is well-taken. But the most important experiment is to attempt to build a cross-partisan, popular coalition for constitutional retooling. That coalition would include progressives, good-government conservatives, business leaders tired of instability, and the broad mass of Americans who simply want a government that can solve problems. It would be messy and difficult, but it’s the only project commensurate with the scale of the threat. The essay’s anti-corruption frame is a piece of that puzzle, but only if it leads inexorably to structural change. Otherwise, it’s just one more poll-tested message in a graveyard of failed campaigns.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current constitutional architecture, including the Electoral College and the Senate, empowers a minority to govern over the majority and prevents Democrats from delivering on transformative promises.
  2. 02Anti-corruption efforts are only effective if they are used as a vehicle for deep structural redesign rather than just a rhetorical frame for campaigns.
  3. 03The Democratic Party's failure to protect democracy stems from a lack of capacity to govern within decaying institutions rather than a failure of ideological positioning.
  4. 04Historical precedents, such as the Reconstruction and Progressive Eras, demonstrate that crises of democratic legitimacy are only resolved through constitutional transformation.
  5. 05Democrats must prioritize aggressive institutional reforms, such as Supreme Court expansion and ending the filibuster, to ensure that popular mandates cannot be vetoed by counter-majoritarian bodies.
