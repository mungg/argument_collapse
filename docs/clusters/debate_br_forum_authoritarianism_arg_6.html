"The Democratic Party must abandon its reliance on ideological moderation and technocratic caution in favor of a bold, anti-establishment strategy centered on anti-corruption and genuine mobilization."

Bonica and Grumbach are right about the central strategic mistake: Democrats have treated moderation as if it were a machine that converts caution into votes. The machine is broken. It may never have worked quite as well as its admirers claimed, but even if it did in the age of split-ticket voting, local newspapers, and weaker party identities, that is not the world Democrats now inhabit. The party cannot spreadsheet its way out of authoritarianism by shaving down every sentence until it offends no one and inspires almost no one.

The most important contribution of the lead essay is not the claim that progressives always win. Bonica and Grumbach explicitly avoid that claim. Their point is more modest and more devastating: the confident case for moderation rests on an outdated model of voters and elections. In that model, citizens possess stable preferences, compare candidate platforms, and reward the candidate nearest to them ideologically. In today’s politics, many citizens are instead sorting through identity, distrust, resentment, media narratives, leader attachments, and a pervasive sense that the system is rigged. A candidate who says, in effect, “I am exactly 7 percent less alarming than my coalition’s activists,” is not obviously solving any of those problems.

The old moderation story also mistakes absence for appeal. When a Democrat avoids talking about climate, immigration, policing, or trans rights, commentators often describe that as a move toward the center. But voters do not experience politics as a candidate’s internal memo. They experience it as a noisy field of signals. If Republicans spend hundreds of millions defining Democrats as dangerous radicals, and Democrats respond by whispering about bipartisan tax credits, the electorate does not necessarily conclude that Democrats are moderate. Many conclude that Democrats are evasive, weak, or guilty as charged.

That is why the essay’s emphasis on authenticity matters. A candidate’s position is not merely a dot on an ideological line. It is a claim about who they are, whom they fight for, and what kind of risks they are willing to take. Voters may not know the details of a health care plan, but they often have a strong sense of whether a politician sounds like a real person or a consultant-generated product. Strategic retreat can easily read as contempt: “We know what we believe, but we have been advised not to say it around you.”

The Harris campaign’s moderation cannot bear all the explanatory weight some left critics place on it. Incumbent-party fatigue, inflation, international turmoil, media fragmentation, sexism, racism, and the strange dynamics of a late nomination all mattered. But Bonica and Grumbach are not saying moderation caused the defeat. They are saying it failed to prevent it, and that matters because moderation had been sold as the safe harbor. If democracy was on the ballot, and if Democrats adopted the safer, more popular, less ideologically threatening style recommended by the popularists, then the result should at least make the theory’s proponents less certain.

The anti-corruption frame they propose is powerful because it cuts beneath the left-center argument. Americans may disagree sharply about taxes, guns, immigration, and schools, but they overwhelmingly suspect that the powerful play by different rules. This is not a fringe intuition. It is reinforced every time a Supreme Court justice accepts lavish gifts from billionaires, every time members of Congress trade stocks while receiving sensitive briefings, every time the wealthy seem to buy access and then call the resulting system “freedom.” Anti-corruption politics can connect the material and the moral: high prices, corporate concentration, tax avoidance, regulatory capture, and democratic decay are not separate problems but symptoms of government serving insiders.

Yet anti-corruption cannot be only a slogan. Here Bonica and Grumbach are especially persuasive. Democrats cannot credibly attack a rigged system while relying on the same lobbyists, consultants, dark money, and donor courtship that make politics feel rigged. The party does not need purity in the adolescent sense; politics is coalition-building, not monastic life. But it does need visible sacrifices. Ban congressional stock trading. Refuse the most corrupting forms of fundraising. End manipulative recurring-donation schemes. Make donor access less central to advancement. Recruit candidates who have lived the consequences of policy rather than merely studied them at elite institutions. If Democrats want to be believed as an anti-establishment party, they must behave in ways that cost the establishment something.

The turnout argument also deserves emphasis. Democrats have spent years speaking as though the marginal voter is always a white suburban moderate who reads both parties’ websites and chooses the less extreme plan. That voter exists, but the fixation on this archetype has warped the party’s imagination. The missing voter is often younger, poorer, less trusting, less likely to see politics as responsive, and more likely to think both parties are compromised. Such citizens are not mobilized by being told that Democrats have adopted the least controversial available position. They are mobilized, if at all, by a sense of stakes, solidarity, and possibility.

This is where the democracy message needs repair. “Democracy is on the ballot” was true, but it was too abstract when separated from everyday corruption and domination. For many citizens, democracy does not mean a civics-textbook procedure; it means whether anyone in power can be forced to listen. A democracy message that sounds like “protect institutions” will fail if people experience those institutions as indifferent or predatory. A democracy message that says “the billionaires, judges, lobbyists, and authoritarian politicians are trying to take away your power, and we will stop them” has a better chance.

Still, I would add one caution to Bonica and Grumbach’s argument. Mobilization without persuasion is not enough. The electorate Democrats need includes irregular voters, but it also includes citizens who voted Republican, citizens who distrust both parties, and citizens whose cultural instincts are not progressive. The anti-corruption frame works precisely because it can speak across those lines. But it will fail if it becomes merely progressive politics with a new label. To redraw the battlefield, Democrats must make room for people who are angry at concentrated power but not fluent in liberal cultural language. That means a politics less controlled by professional-class norms and more grounded in workplaces, churches, unions, local associations, veterans’ groups, tenant organizations, and community institutions.

The point is not to replace moderation with maximalism. It is to replace defensiveness with purpose. A party facing authoritarianism cannot afford to be reckless, but neither can it afford to be bloodless. The question should not be, “How do we move to the center?” It should be, “What conflict can we make clear enough that people know which side they are on?” Authoritarians thrive by turning politics into spectacle, grievance, and loyalty. They are not defeated by technocratic caution. They are defeated when democratic forces build a broader, deeper loyalty: to one another, to equal citizenship, and to the belief that public power should serve the many rather than the protected few.

Bonica and Grumbach’s essay is valuable because it asks Democrats to stop confusing risk avoidance with strategy. The risk of saying too much is real. But the risk of saying too little has already been tested. It produced a politics unable to match the emotional force of the authoritarian right. If Democrats want to defend democracy, they must offer more than competent management of a distrusted status quo. They must become the vehicle for unrigging it.
