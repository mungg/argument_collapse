"While markets can sometimes crowd out moral norms, they can also support, protect, and extend them, meaning that the moral limits of markets should be determined by specific institutional harms rather than a general suspicion of commodification."

Michael Sandel’s essay is eloquent, but it proves less than it suggests. Markets can sometimes crowd out moral norms. But they can also support, extend, and protect them. The danger in Sandel’s argument is that vivid examples of tasteless commodification—bought wedding toasts, fake Facebook friends, purchased honors—are made to cast suspicion on market exchange as such. That suspicion may satisfy our moral intuitions, but it can easily become a license for paternalism, scarcity, and hypocrisy.

Begin with Sandel’s best examples. A hired friend is not a friend. A ghostwritten toast may disappoint the bride and groom if it purports to be the best man’s own words. A Nobel Prize sold at auction would not be a Nobel Prize in the morally relevant sense. These cases show that some goods are constituted by intentions, histories, or judgments incompatible with purchase. But that point is not an indictment of markets. It is a point about fraud, misdescription, and category mistake. If I pay an actor to play Hamlet, I have not bought a prince of Denmark. If I pay a therapist, I have not bought a friend. We are usually capable of distinguishing the good purchased from the good imagined.

Sandel’s more controversial cases require a different argument. Consider blood, kidneys, college admissions, and compensation for accepting public burdens. Here money does not dissolve the good in the same obvious way. A kidney works whether donated or sold. Blood can save a life whether the donor was paid or unpaid. A community may bear real costs from hosting a nuclear-waste facility, and compensation may be a matter of justice rather than bribery. The question is not simply whether money corrupts, but whether the relevant practice can be designed so that money serves appropriate ends.

The kidney example is especially revealing. Thousands of people wait for transplants; many die waiting. A blanket prohibition on kidney sales preserves our sense that body parts should not be commodities, but it does so at a cost borne by the sick. It also coexists with morally awkward practices we already tolerate. Surgeons, hospitals, pharmaceutical companies, and insurers are paid. The donor alone must be moved by altruism. Why is it ennobling to forbid compensation to the person who assumes the physical risk, while everyone else in the chain receives payment? A regulated system—perhaps with fixed compensation, long waiting periods, medical screening, and lifetime health coverage for donors—might reduce exploitation and increase supply. Sandel may still reject it on corruption grounds, but he needs to explain why preserving a symbolic norm outweighs avoidable deaths.

The same applies to blood. Richard Titmuss’s comparison of British and American blood systems is suggestive, but it does not establish that payment necessarily corrupts donation. The American system’s problems may have reflected poor screening, fragmented institutions, and reliance on high-risk sellers. Modern testing and regulation can change the equation. It is entirely possible for voluntary donation to remain central while compensation is used in limited contexts, such as plasma collection or rare blood types. The moral meaning of payment depends on institutional setting.

Sandel is also too quick to treat economic analysis as wooden. The idea of “signaling” may sound bloodless when applied to lovers, but social life is full of costly signals. Engagement rings, handwritten letters, long journeys, remembered anniversaries, even the time spent choosing a gift all communicate seriousness partly because they require effort. To describe this is not to reduce love to information. It is to notice one dimension of expression. Economists sometimes overreach, but moral philosophers sometimes caricature.

The claim that altruism is a muscle rather than a scarce resource is attractive, but it is not always true. Anyone who has cared for an elderly parent, volunteered in a struggling school, or served in a demanding nonprofit knows that moral energy can be exhausted. “Compassion fatigue” is not an economist’s fantasy. Institutions that rely entirely on unpaid virtue may become exploitative, especially toward women, family members, and the poor. Payment can recognize burdens that sentimentality conceals. A society that refuses to price care may not honor care; it may simply expect someone to provide it for free.

This does not mean everything should be for sale. Some market exchanges should be prohibited because they are coercive, fraudulent, dangerous, or incompatible with the institution in question. Selling votes undermines democracy because political equality requires that each citizen’s vote count as an expression of judgment, not purchasing power. Selling judicial verdicts destroys the rule of law. But the reasons must be specific. “Commodification” cannot be a trump card.

The language of corruption also invites a troubling question: who decides the true meaning of a good? Sandel wants democratic deliberation, and that is better than technocratic rule. But democratic majorities have often enforced moral meanings in oppressive ways. They have decided what marriage means, what sexuality means, what family means, and who may participate in honorable institutions. Markets, for all their faults, sometimes allow pluralism. They let people pursue goods without first winning a public argument about the highest purpose of life.

We should resist both market imperialism and moral imperialism. The first says that if a transaction is voluntary and efficient, nothing more need be asked. The second says that if money makes us uneasy, prohibition follows. A better approach is experimental and institutional: identify the harm, design safeguards, compare alternatives, and remain alert to unintended consequences. Sometimes money corrupts. Sometimes it compensates. Sometimes it liberates. The moral limits of markets are real, but they are not found by treating markets as a solvent that predictably dissolves virtue wherever it appears.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Cases where money seems to dissolve a good, such as hired friendship or bought honors, are actually instances of fraud or misdescription rather than an indictment of market exchange itself.
  2. 02In controversial cases like organ or blood donation, the focus should be on whether a regulated market can be designed to serve appropriate ends and save lives rather than whether money inherently corrupts the good.
  3. 03The claim that altruism is an inexhaustible muscle ignores the reality of compassion fatigue and the fact that refusing to price care can lead to the exploitation of those expected to provide it for free.
  4. 04Economic concepts like 'signaling' are not necessarily reductive but provide a useful way to understand the expressive dimensions of social effort and gift-giving.
  5. 05Relying on a 'corruption' argument to prohibit markets risks moral imperialism, whereas markets can provide a space for pluralism by allowing people to pursue goods without requiring a democratic consensus on their 'true' meaning.
  6. 06The moral limits of markets should be addressed through an experimental approach that identifies specific harms and designs safeguards rather than treating market exchange as a predictable solvent of virtue.
