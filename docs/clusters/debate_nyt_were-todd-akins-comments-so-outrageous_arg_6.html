"While Todd Akin’s specific phrasing and scientific claims were indefensible, the political outrage surrounding them is hypocritical because his underlying opposition to abortion exceptions is a standard position within the Republican party."

Todd Akin’s statement was ignorant and deserved correction, but the political explosion around it has the feel of a ritual sacrifice. Akin did not hold a uniquely bizarre position on abortion. He voiced, in especially clumsy and medically false terms, a view shared by many abortion opponents: that the unborn child should not be aborted because of the crime of the father.

That view is unpopular with many voters, including many who otherwise favor restrictions on abortion. But it is not a secret. The Republican platform has long used broad anti-abortion language. Paul Ryan, now the vice-presidential nominee, co-sponsored legislation that at one stage used the term “forcible rape.” Democrats are happy to make Akin the face of the party; Republicans are desperate to make him disappear. Neither reaction should be confused with moral clarity.

The phrase “legitimate rape” was awful. A woman who has been assaulted should not hear a Senate candidate parsing whether her rape was real enough. His biological claim was also wrong: rape can and does result in pregnancy. A serious candidate should know better.

Still, the outrage risks substituting denunciation for debate. Every rape exception in an abortion statute requires some line-drawing. Does the woman’s statement suffice? Must she report to police? What about statutory rape, coercion, incapacitation, incest, threats, or abuse by a partner? These are not easy questions, and legislators have tried to answer them with terms like “forcible” precisely because they are trying to define the exception. Sometimes that effort is callous or foolish. But pretending Akin alone created the issue is dishonest.

The better response would be to press all candidates, not just Akin, to state their positions plainly. If they oppose rape exceptions, they should say so and defend it. If they support exceptions, they should explain how they would work without humiliating victims. If they believe abortion is murder, they should stop hiding behind reassuring claims that the hard cases almost never happen. If they believe women must be trusted, they should oppose statutory language that implies otherwise.

Akin’s comment was outrageous in its wording and science. But the greater outrage may be the political class’s sudden shock at a position that has been sitting in plain sight. The country would be better served by less theater and more honesty.
