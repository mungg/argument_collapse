"The Citizens United decision is a policy failure that ratifies the dominance of economic elites, necessitating structural reforms rather than mere disclosure to decouple economic power from political power."

There is a class dimension to Citizens United that Lessig's analysis does not fully capture. The decision was not merely a legal ruling about the scope of the First Amendment. It was a ruling that ratified the dominance of economic elites in American politics.

The corporate form itself is a creature of state law. Corporations exist because governments grant them charters and protect them with limited liability and other privileges. The question of whether these legal entities should have the same speech rights as natural persons is not a question the Constitution answers. It is a question of policy and values.

In the early twentieth century, Progressive Era reformers were deeply skeptical of corporate power. The Clayton Antitrust Act of 1914 was designed to break up concentrations of economic power. The Sixteenth Amendment's income tax was designed to ensure that the wealthy paid their fair share. These reformers understood that economic power and political power are connected, and they sought to disrupt the connection.

The modern corporate form is far more powerful than its predecessors. The largest corporations have revenues exceeding the GDP of many nations. They have sophisticated political operations. They can spend tens of millions of dollars on political campaigns without meaningfully affecting their bottom lines. This is a qualitative difference from individual political spending, no matter how wealthy the individual.

Lessig is right that we need to think about the "economy of influence" that has developed around privately funded campaigns. But this economy has a specific content: it is an economy in which the wealthy and powerful use their resources to protect and advance their interests at the expense of everyone else.

The Great Recession illustrates this dynamic. Financial industry lobbying shaped the regulatory environment in ways that contributed to the crisis. When the crisis came, the same industry secured massive government bailouts. The political economy of financial regulation was corrupted, but the corruption was not merely the corruption of individual legislators. It was the corruption of a system that gave financial interests privileged access to the regulatory process.

Global warming presents a similar pattern. The fossil fuel industry has invested heavily in political influence to block climate legislation. This investment has been effective: comprehensive climate legislation has repeatedly failed in Congress, despite overwhelming scientific consensus about the need for action. The people who will suffer most from climate change are not the people with the resources to influence the political process.

Citizens United has made this situation worse by removing whatever constraints existed on corporate political spending. The decision creates a legal regime in which economic power translates directly into political power without meaningful limit. This is not the democracy the framers envisioned, and it is not the democracy we should accept.

The solution is not disclosure. It is structural reform that limits the role of money in politics. Public financing of campaigns, strict contribution limits, and possibly constitutional amendment are all on the table. We should not be satisfied with half-measures that preserve the essentials of a corrupted system.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The corporate form is a state-created entity with legal privileges, and the Constitution does not mandate that these entities possess the same speech rights as natural persons.
  2. 02Modern corporations possess a qualitative advantage over individuals because their immense revenues allow for sophisticated political operations that do not meaningfully impact their bottom lines.
  3. 03The 'economy of influence' is specifically designed to protect the interests of the wealthy at the expense of the public, as evidenced by the financial industry's role in the Great Recession and the fossil fuel industry's obstruction of climate legislation.
  4. 04Citizens United exacerbates systemic inequality by allowing economic power to translate directly into political power without limit.
  5. 05Meaningful change requires aggressive structural interventions, such as public financing and strict contribution limits, rather than the 'half-measure' of transparency.
