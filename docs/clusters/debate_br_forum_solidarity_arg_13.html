"Asian American solidarity must move beyond shared identity or symbolic representation toward a materialist, internationalist politics that honestly locates Asian Americans within the contradictions of racial capitalism."

Mie Inouye’s essay is at its strongest when it refuses to treat Asian American experience as a simple bridge between Black and white politics. Her family history—Japanese incarceration, postwar assimilation through Mormonism, rural Utah conservatism, professional striving, and the unresolved anger of a family that survived by not speaking too much about what happened—captures something essential about Asian America: it is a location of both injury and complicity, exclusion and aspiration, memory and forgetting.

This matters for the broader argument about solidarity. Asian Americans are often invoked in U.S. racial politics as evidence for someone else’s theory. Conservatives use “Asian success” to deny structural racism. Liberals use anti-Asian violence to affirm a multiracial anti-racist coalition. Universities use Asian Americans awkwardly, sometimes as “overrepresented” minorities, sometimes as people of color, sometimes as inconvenient plaintiffs in debates about affirmative action. The category itself contains refugees and billionaires, adoptees and undocumented workers, caste privilege and caste oppression, Pacific Islander erasure, old Chinatowns and new suburbs, nail salon workers and tech executives. Any solidarity that begins by assuming Asian Americans know where they stand will fail.

Inouye’s encounter with Henry dramatizes this. Both are Asian American, but their class positions, migration histories, and motivations differ. Henry organizes because his family needs material changes to survive. Inouye, at first, says she organizes because she needs meetings. The moment is uncomfortable because it punctures the fantasy that shared racial identity produces shared politics. It also punctures the fantasy that “Asian American” is politically meaningful without organizing.

Asian American identity itself was forged through organizing, particularly in the late 1960s and 1970s, when activists connected opposition to the Vietnam War, ethnic studies, labor struggles, housing fights, and solidarity with Black, Chicano, Indigenous, and Third World liberation movements. That history is often flattened today into representation politics: first Asian American this, more Asian Americans in that institution, better media visibility, more recognition of hate crimes. Representation matters, especially amid violence and exclusion. But without a material and internationalist politics, Asian American identity easily becomes a vehicle for assimilation.

Inouye’s Mormon story is one version of that assimilation. Her great-grandparents and grandparents suffered one of the most blatant racial violations in American history: removal and incarceration by the U.S. government during World War II. Afterward, like many Japanese Americans, they rebuilt through silence, work, patriotism, and respectability. Sending children to the Mormon church helped them become legible as Americans. The church offered community and advancement, but at the price of attachment to a racial order that could accept some Asians as disciplined, family-oriented, anti-communist, and upwardly mobile.

This is the model minority bargain. It does not eliminate racism; it reorganizes it. It says: you may belong if you prove useful to the existing hierarchy. You may be praised as hardworking if your success can be weaponized against Black demands. You may enter elite institutions if you do not ask too many questions about empire, labor exploitation, or the people left behind. You may mourn anti-Asian violence, but preferably in a way that increases policing rather than challenges the conditions that produce violence.

The rise in anti-Asian harassment and attacks during the COVID pandemic exposed the fragility of conditional belonging. Elderly Asian people assaulted in public, workers spat on, businesses vandalized, children bullied—these incidents made clear that Asians could still be cast as foreign contagion. But the political response was divided. Some demanded more policing and hate crime prosecution. Others, especially abolitionist Asian American organizers, argued that police do not make vulnerable communities safe and that anti-Asian violence must be connected to housing insecurity, mental health crises, misogyny, imperial rivalry with China, and anti-Black criminalization. The split revealed the unresolved question: solidarity with whom, against what?

Inouye’s critique of deference politics is helpful here. Asian Americans often occupy ambiguous positions in deference hierarchies. In some spaces they are told to defer to Black and Indigenous leadership because their communities have benefited from anti-Blackness and settler colonialism. In other spaces their experiences of racism are minimized because they are perceived as privileged. In still others, Asian women, queer Asians, Southeast Asian refugees, Muslim Asians, caste-oppressed South Asians, and low-wage Asian immigrant workers are rendered invisible by the image of the affluent East or South Asian professional.

A better politics would begin, as Bob Lee did, by asking: what do you want? But it would ask that question concretely. What does the undocumented Korean worker want from a labor campaign? What does the Cambodian refugee family facing deportation want from immigrant justice movements? What does the Filipino nurse want from a health care union? What does the Chinese elder in public housing want from anti-violence work? What does the Dalit student want from a university that celebrates diversity while ignoring caste? What does the Japanese American whose family was incarcerated want from a state that now surveils Muslims and migrants?

The answers will not be the same. Some will conflict. That is why “Asian American solidarity” cannot mean unity around shared identity. It must mean building organizations where these different histories become politically legible to one another. The category should function less as a demographic label than as an invitation to connect immigration law, war, labor, policing, language access, caste, gendered violence, and memory.

Inouye’s discussion of Uncle Gerald is especially resonant in this regard. Many Asian American families contain figures like him: generous, hardworking, family-centered, politically conservative, allergic to victimhood, proud of having survived without complaint. Their conservatism is not simply ignorance. It is often a survival strategy calcified into ideology. If your family endured incarceration, war, displacement, or poverty by keeping your head down and working, then movements that name victimization can feel threatening. They seem to reopen wounds that assimilation promised to heal.

But the wounds do not disappear. They reemerge as anger, shame, perfectionism, anti-Blackness, suspicion of welfare, contempt for protest, and sometimes fierce loyalty to America precisely because America’s betrayal is too painful to face. Solidarity requires ways to speak about this without condescension. It must say to our elders: what happened to you was political, not personal failure. Your silence protected you, but it also cost us. Your work ethic is admirable, but it should not be used to judge those denied the same opportunities. Your desire for safety is real, but police and prisons will not save us all.

The connection Inouye draws between George Floyd and Uncle Gerald is provocative because it asks Asian Americans to see racial capitalism not only where we are excluded but where we are recruited. George Floyd’s murder and Japanese incarceration are not identical; neither is Uncle Gerald’s COVID death. But all belong to a country that distributes vulnerability through race, class, state power, and ideology. The model minority bargain encourages Asians to see ourselves as closer to safety than to danger, closer to whiteness than to Blackness, closer to property than to dispossession. The pandemic and anti-Asian violence reminded us that such safety is conditional. Black liberation struggles remind us that conditional safety is not freedom.

Yet solidarity cannot be built on guilt alone. Many Asian Americans have been told that their political role is to “show up for” others, especially Black communities, without articulating their own stakes. This can produce noble gestures but shallow commitment. Inouye is right: people need to know why they are there. Asian American solidarity with Black movements against policing should be rooted not only in moral debt but in our own histories of surveillance, exclusion, deportation, labor exploitation, and state violence—while still recognizing the specificity of anti-Blackness. Solidarity with Indigenous sovereignty should reckon with Asian settler participation in Hawai‘i and the continental United States. Solidarity with Latino immigrants should include Asian undocumented people and refugees facing deportation. The point is not to center Asians but to locate us honestly.

What would social endurance look like for Asian American politics? It would mean intergenerational spaces where elders can speak without being immediately shamed and youth can challenge without being dismissed as ungrateful. It would mean multilingual organizing, not just English-language panels about identity. It would mean labor organizing in restaurants, salons, home care, warehouses, hospitals, and tech. It would mean confronting anti-Blackness in our communities without pretending Asians are white. It would mean resisting U.S.–China militarism even when fear of China becomes a route to American belonging. It would mean remembering Japanese incarceration not as proof of eventual national redemption but as warning.

Inouye’s essay asks us to stay in difficult rooms. For Asian Americans, one of those rooms is the family gathering where politics is avoided until it erupts. Another is the multiracial coalition where we are uncertain whether we belong. Another is the workplace where Asian labor is exploited beneath a veneer of immigrant success. Another is the university where diversity rhetoric obscures hierarchy. We need the courage to remain in these rooms, but also to change what can be said in them.

Solidarity now requires Asian Americans to refuse both innocence and disappearance. We are not merely victims, not honorary whites, not model minorities, not permanent guests, and not a bridge by nature. We become useful to movements only through organizing that uncovers our own contradictory stakes and binds them to the freedom of others.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The 'model minority' bargain offers conditional belonging to Asian Americans at the cost of reinforcing racial hierarchies and weaponizing their success against Black liberation.
  3. 03Solidarity should be built on articulating specific, concrete stakes—such as histories of surveillance, labor exploitation, and deportation—rather than on guilt or a politics of deference.
  4. 04Social endurance in an Asian American context requires creating intergenerational and multilingual spaces where internal conflicts, such as community anti-Blackness or the trauma of assimilation, can be addressed without immediate dismissal.
  5. 05Asian American identity is not a static demographic label but a political project that must be continually forged through organizing across difference.
