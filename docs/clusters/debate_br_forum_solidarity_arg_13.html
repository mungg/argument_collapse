"While deference politics can be practiced poorly, a refined version of deference remains a necessary component of solidarity because it ensures that those most affected by domination have the authority to set the political agenda."

Mie Inouye’s criticism of deference politics captures something real in contemporary progressive spaces: the stiff ritual in which some people speak as representatives of injury while others perform self-erasure as proof of virtue. Anyone who has spent time in universities, nonprofits, activist meetings, or online political subcultures has seen this pattern. It can be manipulative, shallow, and exhausting. Yet I want to defend a version of deference from the essay’s critique—not because deference is a complete politics, but because the rush to transcend it can become its own evasion.

The term “deference politics” now does a great deal of work. It can refer to elite capture, as Olúfemi Táíwò argues: the laundering of elite interests through the language of marginalized identity. It can refer to conflict avoidance by privileged actors. It can refer to tokenization, where one Black person or one trans person or one working-class person is made to stand in for a collectivity. All of these are real problems. But deference can also mean something simpler and more necessary: those who are less affected by a form of domination should not presume to set the agenda for those who are more affected by it.

That principle is not a bourgeois etiquette. It is a hard-won lesson from social movements. Women in labor movements had to insist that male comrades stop treating sexism as secondary. Black activists in multiracial organizations had to insist that white liberals and white radicals stop universalizing their own experience. Disabled people had to insist that doctors, parents, and philanthropists stop speaking over them. Queer and trans people had to insist that respectability-minded allies stop bargaining away their lives for broader acceptability. Indigenous communities have repeatedly had to insist that environmentalists not treat land as an abstract wilderness while ignoring sovereignty.

In each case, “listen to the most affected” was not a retreat from politics. It was an intervention into domination inside movements. It did not solve every strategic question. But without it, the room was already rigged.

Inouye acknowledges the validity of standpoint theory, but she worries that its practical application has become a politics of self-silencing. That worry is justified in some settings. Still, the alternative language of “epistemic humility” may not be strong enough. Humility is a virtue of the knower. Deference, at its best, is a redistribution of authority. It is not only about holding my beliefs open to correction; it is about recognizing that in some struggles, I should not have equal agenda-setting power.

Consider police violence. A white professional who joined protests in 2020 may have sincere reasons for opposing policing: fear of state repression, concern about democracy, recognition that police protect property over people, even personal experiences at demonstrations. Those reasons matter. They may help sustain commitment. But when the question is what demands to make—defund, reform, abolish, prosecute, invest, withdraw—communities most subject to police violence deserve more than inclusion in a dialogue. They deserve leadership. That leadership will not be unanimous, and it will not be infallible. But the fact of internal disagreement among the oppressed does not restore equal authority to those historically empowered to ignore them.

This distinction matters because “we are all transformed through organizing” can slide, if we are not careful, into a flattening of asymmetry. Yes, everyone has something to learn. Yes, oppressed people can be wrong, cruel, confused, or strategically mistaken. Yes, privileged people can develop knowledge beyond their experience. But domination is not a seminar in which all partial perspectives meet on neutral ground. It is a structure that has already distributed credibility, confidence, money, institutional access, and the presumption of reasonableness unequally. In such a world, merely inviting everyone to speak from their stakes may reproduce the hierarchy.

Bob Lee’s organizing, which Inouye reads beautifully, is instructive here. Lee asks white working-class people, “What do you want?” He draws out their experiences of police violence and poverty. But he is not approaching them as an empty facilitator. He is a Black Panther organizer with an analysis, a discipline, and a political project. He is not deferring to white pain; neither is he dismissing it. He is exercising leadership. He brings the authority of a Black radical organization into a white working-class room and uses that authority to construct a coalition.

That example suggests a better formulation: solidarity requires both deference and contestation. Deference without contestation becomes passivity. Contestation without deference becomes domination by another name. The art is knowing when to yield, when to question, when to follow, when to lead, and when to insist that someone else discover their own stake.

Inouye is especially persuasive that privileged participants must know why they are there. A politics in which white people, men, professionals, or citizens participate only by borrowing the motivations of others will not last. It also imposes an unfair emotional burden on the marginalized, who must continually supply moral meaning for everyone else. But the demand that privileged participants articulate their own stakes should not become an excuse to recenter them. There is a difference between saying, “I need to know why I am committed enough to stay,” and saying, “My reasons must be equally determinative of the agenda.”

The language of “shared interests” can obscure this. Fred Moten’s provocation—recognize that the same system is killing you too, however much more softly—is powerful because it punctures charity. But not all harm is commensurable, and not all stakes should carry equal weight. The professional who feels alienated by capitalism and the worker who cannot afford insulin may both be harmed by the same order, but they are not harmed in the same way. The white protester tear-gassed in 2020 learned something real about state violence; the Black resident whose neighborhood has been occupied by police for decades did not need that lesson. Solidarity requires connection without equivalence.

The same applies to Inouye’s moving discussion of Uncle Gerald. It is politically meaningful to connect his COVID death to racial capitalism, state failure, libertarian ideology, and the broader system that also produces police violence. But the phrase “the same shit that killed George Floyd killed my Uncle Gerald” is both illuminating and risky. It illuminates because it identifies a common structure of abandonment, violence, and distorted freedom. It is risky because George Floyd was killed by an agent of the state kneeling on his neck, while Uncle Gerald’s death involved a more mediated chain of ideology, public health failure, and personal choice. “However more softly and however more self-inflicted” does important work in the sentence. Without such qualifications, solidarity can become appropriation.

A chastened defense of deference would insist on those qualifications. It would say: start by listening where domination has made you least likely to hear. Treat the testimony of the most affected not as sacred, but as presumptively important. Do not demand that oppressed people translate every claim into terms that flatter your experience. Do not confuse your discomfort with exclusion. Do not mistake the discovery of your own stake for the right to coauthor every demand.

At the same time, such deference must be organized democratically rather than performed individually. The worst versions of deference politics emerge when individuals are asked to embody entire groups. A Black student becomes “the Black perspective”; a low-wage worker becomes proof of a nonprofit’s authenticity; a trans staffer becomes the gender department. This is not deference to the oppressed. It is extraction. Real deference should be to organized constituencies, accountable leadership, and collective processes. A tenants’ union can speak in ways an isolated tenant cannot. A union caucus, disability justice collective, tribal government, prisoner organization, or neighborhood assembly can deliberate, revise, and authorize demands. The point is not that such bodies are pure. It is that they are more democratic than the charismatic elevation of individual pain.

This is where Inouye’s social endurance becomes indispensable. Deference worth having cannot mean avoiding conflict. If frontline leadership makes a strategic mistake, comrades must have ways to raise concerns without staging a coup or retreating into resentment. If a marginalized leader abuses power, identity cannot shield them from accountability. If privileged participants hide behind silence, they must be pressed to speak honestly about their interests. These are not departures from deference but conditions for its legitimacy.

The choice, then, is not between deference and epistemic humility. We need both, plus organization. Humility reminds us that no one sees the whole. Deference reminds us that society has not distributed blindness evenly. Organization gives us a way to transform partial perspectives into collective judgment.

Inouye’s essay asks us to stay in the room despite annoyance and heartbreak. I agree. But some people have been trapped in rooms built by others for a very long time. When they ask us to sit down, listen first, and not assume equal authority, that is not necessarily fragility or moralism. Sometimes it is the beginning of justice.
