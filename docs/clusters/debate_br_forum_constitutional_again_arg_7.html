The Risks of Constitutionalizing Everything

There is much to admire in the lead essay, and I want to begin by acknowledging it. The authors are correct that the Roberts Court has politicized constitutional law in ways that demand a response. They are correct that Citizens United, Janus, Shelby County, and NFIB v. Sebelius rest on constitutional theories that did not exist a generation ago and that systematically disadvantage egalitarian legislation. They are correct that liberals have ceded too much ground by treating the Constitution as the courts’ private property. And they are right to call attention to a forgotten tradition of constitutional argument that linked political democracy to economic structure. These are real contributions.

But I want to enter a dissent from the broader project. The authors are urging progressives to do what conservatives have done for fifty years: develop a substantive constitutional vision of political economy and press it in every domain of public life. I think this is a mistake, both strategically and as a matter of constitutional principle. Politicizing constitutional law symmetrically does not restore balance. It accelerates the very degradation the authors deplore.

Begin with the strategic point. The authors’ implicit theory is that constitutional argument is a tug-of-war: one side has been pulling hard, the other side hasn’t pulled at all, and the rope has consequently moved. Pull back, and equilibrium can be restored. But constitutional argument is not really a tug-of-war. It is more like a public norm whose stability depends on widespread agreement that there are some questions law cannot answer. When everyone treats every contested policy question as a constitutional question, the consequence is not that egalitarian policies become constitutionally entrenched. It is that the very idea of constitutional law as something other than naked policy preference collapses. And when constitutional law collapses into policy, the side with more institutional power—judicial nominations, Senate seats, state legislatures—wins. That is the side conservatives are on, and have been on for some time.

There is a reason postwar liberals tried to depoliticize constitutional law: they had just lived through the Lochner era and seen what happened when the Court treated its preferred political economy as constitutionally required. The response was not to develop a counter-Lochnerism. It was to insist on judicial humility—the idea, articulated in cases like Williamson v. Lee Optical and Carolene Products, that elected branches should generally be allowed to legislate as they saw fit on economic questions. The authors call this “depoliticization,” and treat it as a defeat. I would call it a hard-won settlement: the Court would protect democracy and discrete minorities, but stay out of the policy disputes that elected branches were better suited to resolve. This settlement broke down because the right walked away from it, not because it was incoherent.

The authors’ proposal is essentially to walk away from it from the other direction. They want progressives to argue, in court and out, that the Affordable Care Act is constitutionally required, that labor reform is constitutionally required, that aggressive antitrust enforcement is constitutionally required, that wealth taxes are constitutionally required. Take the long view here. Suppose this argumentative campaign succeeds in some future court. We would then have a constitutional regime in which judges enforce a left-wing political economy. The next time the political winds shift—and they always do—the right will inherit that machinery and use it for their own ends. Indeed, that is essentially what happened with substantive due process: a doctrine progressives developed to defend liberty has, in Dobbs and elsewhere, become a vehicle for conservative outcomes. We should learn this lesson, not repeat it.

There is also a democratic objection. The authors invoke democracy throughout the essay—democracy of opportunity, democratic self-rule, democratic political economy. But the practical effect of constitutionalizing political economy is to remove these questions from democratic contestation. If a wealth tax is constitutionally required, then voters who oppose it are constitutionally in the wrong. If aggressive antitrust is a constitutional necessity, then a future Congress that prefers a different approach is violating the Constitution. This is precisely the move conservatives have made with respect to property and contract, and the authors rightly criticize it. But the criticism cuts equally against the proposed progressive version. Either elected branches should be able to set economic policy in a wide range of ways, or they should not. The authors want it both ways—democratic discretion when progressives are in power, constitutional necessity to entrench their gains.

A more candid response to the Roberts Court is to fight it on the terrain where it is most vulnerable: not constitutional theory, but democratic legitimacy. The Court today is unpopular, and increasingly seen as a partisan institution. The right response to this is not to construct a competing constitutional ideology, but to make the Court behave more like a court—through structural reforms (term limits, ethics rules, expansion if necessary), through political pressure, and through a renewed commitment by the political branches to act under their own constitutional authority rather than waiting for permission. The authors note, correctly, that Congress can override Janus through legislation reshaping labor law, that Congress can rebuild the Voting Rights Act, that Congress can respond to Citizens United with public financing. Good. These are democratic responses to anti-democratic rulings. They do not require progressives to construct a parallel constitutional ideology; they require progressives to act as if elected branches have the powers the Constitution gives them.

I worry, too, about the historical narrative on which the essay leans. The “democracy-of-opportunity tradition” is presented as a coherent constitutional lineage running from Jackson through Reconstruction through the New Deal. But each of these movements had a very different relationship to the Constitution. Jacksonians were hostile to the Court and to federal power generally; Reconstruction Republicans wielded federal power against state oligarchy in ways Jacksonians would have abhorred; New Dealers built an administrative state that some Progressives had championed and others, including Brandeis, distrusted. Treating these as moments in a single tradition smooths over genuine conflicts and risks producing a usable past that is more retrospective construction than historical reality. There is nothing wrong with constructing a usable past—every political movement does it—but we should be honest about what we are doing.

Let me close by saying what I think a better response to the Roberts Court looks like. First, restore judicial humility as a constitutional ideal. Not the false neutrality the authors rightly criticize, but a genuine principle that on most contested questions of economic policy, the Court should defer to elected branches. This is a constitutional argument too—arguably the central one of the post-1937 era—and it deserves to be defended, not abandoned. Second, support structural court reform to address the asymmetry that has produced the current Court, including term limits and possibly expansion. Third, pursue an aggressive legislative agenda that takes seriously Congress’s own constitutional authority to interpret the Fourteenth Amendment and to legislate under the Commerce Clause and the Spending Clause. Fourth, defend democracy itself through federal voting rights legislation, anti-gerrymandering reforms, and an end to the filibuster. Fifth, build the political coalitions and movements that can actually win elections at every level.

None of this requires progressives to mirror the conservative legal movement’s ideological project. It requires progressives to do the unglamorous work of democratic politics. The authors are right that the courts are not nonpartisan and never were. But the answer is not to treat constitutional law as partisan all the way down. The answer is to limit the role of courts in the questions that democracy is supposed to settle—and to win those democratic battles when they come. Constitutionalizing progressive politics, in the end, would make progressives more dependent on courts, not less. That is a trade we should refuse.
