"While Robert Chapman’s critique of 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is valuable, a truly internationalist neurodiversity movement must move beyond Western-centric ideological debates to address the material realities of disabled people in the Global South, where basic survival and enforceable rights are more urgent than identity politics."

Robert Chapman’s essay is strongest when it refuses to let neurodiversity become a lifestyle brand for the already included. It is true that a language born from autistic self-advocacy can now be heard in corporate workshops, elite universities, and social media performances of identity while many cognitively disabled people remain poor, institutionalized, incarcerated, or untreated. It is also true that war, poverty, environmental destruction, policing, and borders produce disability on a vast scale. Any serious future for neurodiversity must look beyond the seminar room and the human resources department.

Yet Chapman’s framework, for all its anti-imperial ambition, remains shaped by debates in wealthy English-speaking countries. The terms are familiar: ADA-style rights, workplace accommodations, identity politics, university departments, corporate co-optation, Black Power analogies, abolition, and revolutionary parties. These are important, but they do not exhaust the global politics of cognitive difference. If neurodiversity is to become genuinely internationalist, it must listen more carefully to settings where diagnosis is scarce, welfare states are thin, schools are under-resourced, families are the main support system, and disability categories arrive entangled with donor agendas, missionary histories, humanitarian NGOs, and pharmaceutical markets.

In much of the world, the first problem is not that neurodiversity has been co-opted by corporations. It is that children and adults with cognitive and psychosocial disabilities have no reliable access to assessment, education, communication support, seizure medication, mental health care, assistive technology, or protection from violence. Autism may be unnamed or named only as shame. Epilepsy may be interpreted through spiritual stigma. Intellectual disability may mean lifelong confinement at home. Psychosis may lead to chaining, homelessness, prison, or abuse in religious or traditional healing settings. Schools may exclude children simply because there are no trained teachers or aides. Families may provide heroic care with no respite, no income support, and no future plan.

In such contexts, rights language can be both inadequate and urgently needed. Chapman criticizes liberal rights as abstract and easily captured by neoliberal standards of “reasonableness.” But for many disabled people, the absence of enforceable rights means the absence of any claim at all. The right to attend school, to be free from abuse, to access health care, to vote, to inherit property, to avoid arbitrary detention, or to receive public support is not a bourgeois distraction. It is a shield, however fragile. The United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities has given activists across the Global South a language to challenge exclusion. Implementation is often weak, but the answer is not to move beyond rights; it is to make rights material.

At the same time, the Western identity model does not always travel smoothly. “Neurodivergent” can be empowering where people have access to diagnosis and online communities. Elsewhere it may be unintelligible, elitist, or less salient than kinship, religion, caste, tribe, race, class, or local disability categories. A child may not need an identity so much as a school that will accept him. A woman with psychosocial disability may need protection from sexual violence. A family may need money for transport to a clinic. A non-speaking adult may need a communication device in a language the device does not support. International neurodiversity politics should not presume that liberation begins with self-identification. Sometimes it begins with food, safety, medicine, and someone willing to teach.

Chapman’s discussion of Palestine rightly insists that war is disabling. Bombs, displacement, hunger, grief, amputation, traumatic brain injury, psychological trauma, destroyed hospitals, and interrupted care create impairment and intensify existing disability. The same is true in Sudan, Ukraine, Yemen, Congo, Syria, Myanmar, and many other places marked by conflict and militarized abandonment. Antiwar politics belongs in disability politics because war attacks the infrastructures that disabled people need to live. But we should be careful with the phrase “neurodivergifying event.” Trauma and brain injury are not merely additions to a political category. They are injuries, often devastating ones, experienced by people who may not seek identity in them. To name mass disablement is essential; to fold all psychic suffering into neurodivergence may blur distinctions that matter for care, reparations, and accountability.

An international neurodiversity movement must also confront economic structures more specifically than “capitalism.” Global inequality determines who is diagnosed, who receives services, who becomes a research subject, who migrates to perform care work, and who is left behind. Wealthy countries recruit nurses, aides, and doctors from poorer countries, draining already fragile care systems. International donors fund short-term projects that collapse when grants end. Pharmaceutical companies may expand markets without building comprehensive support. Genetic research may collect data from diverse populations while benefits accrue elsewhere. Climate change, driven disproportionately by industrialized economies, produces malnutrition, displacement, infection, and trauma that affect neurological development. These are not abstract global systems; they are pipelines of vulnerability.

Chapman is right to connect neurodiversity to decolonial politics, but decoloniality should also make us suspicious of exporting a ready-made radical vocabulary. In some societies, the most emancipatory actors may not be revolutionary parties or identity-based movements but parent associations, disabled people’s organizations, women’s cooperatives, community health workers, inclusive educators, or religious groups willing to challenge stigma. Their politics may be pragmatic rather than ideologically pure. They may seek ramps, classrooms, medication, cash transfers, or respite care before cultural recognition. They may work with states, NGOs, clinics, and international agencies because those are the available routes. It would be a mistake to view such work as merely liberal or co-opted.

Family is another area where global perspectives complicate the argument. Western disability politics often emphasizes independence from parents, professionals, and institutions, for understandable reasons. Autistic self-advocates had to fight parent-dominated narratives that erased them. Psychiatric survivors had to fight coercive doctors. People with intellectual disabilities had to fight guardianship and institutionalization. But in many places, family is not only a site of control; it is the only welfare state available. A mother, grandmother, sibling, or cousin may provide lifelong care without pay. Neurodivergent liberation cannot mean simply freeing individuals from family authority unless there is a public system to support life beyond it. Nor can it romanticize family, because families can abuse, hide, or overrule disabled members. The political demand must be support and accountability: resources for caregivers, rights for disabled people, and community structures that reduce dependence on any one household.

The movement should also be multilingual and epistemically humble. Neurodiversity theory has been built largely in English, often by people with access to universities and digital networks. But understandings of mind, personhood, distress, and disability vary across cultures. Some local models may be stigmatizing and need challenge. Others may contain resources that Western psychiatry and disability studies lack: relational accounts of personhood, communal obligations, spiritual meanings, or nonindividualistic forms of care. The point is not to romanticize tradition. It is to avoid replacing medical imperialism with activist imperialism.

What would a globally serious neurodiversity agenda include? First, investment in community-based supports designed with disabled people and families: inclusive education, local-language communication tools, seizure care, mental health services, respite, and supported living. Second, protection from violence in homes, institutions, prisons, camps, and streets. Third, cash supports that recognize the cost of disability and caregiving. Fourth, training for primary health workers and teachers, not only specialists concentrated in cities. Fifth, ethical research governed by communities, with strong safeguards against eugenics and exploitation. Sixth, antiwar and climate justice commitments that treat disabled people as central, not afterthoughts. Seventh, global solidarity that moves money and power, not just vocabulary.

Chapman’s call for Neurodivergent Power can contribute to this agenda if power means the capacity of disabled people and their communities to shape the conditions of life. But if it becomes mainly a slogan for anticapitalist identity politics in the North Atlantic, it will miss many of those it claims to include. A farmer’s autistic child in rural India, a woman with schizophrenia in Lagos, a Palestinian child traumatized under bombardment, an Indigenous teenager with fetal alcohol spectrum disorder in Canada, a domestic worker with ADHD in the Gulf, and a non-speaking adult in a Brazilian favela do not inhabit the same political terrain. They may share vulnerability to systems that rank minds and abandon dependency, but their routes to freedom differ.

The future of neurodiversity should therefore be internationalist in method, not only in sentiment. It should ask before it names. It should fund before it theorizes. It should translate, literally and politically. It should join struggles against war, debt, austerity, racism, caste hierarchy, gender violence, and climate breakdown while remaining attentive to the everyday infrastructures of disability support. It should defend rights without worshiping law, use medicine without surrendering to medical domination, honor families without allowing them absolute power, and build identities without requiring everyone to adopt them.

Chapman is right that neurodiversity must not be reduced to dimmed supermarket lights and corporate talent pipelines. But neither should it be reduced to the ideological battles of rich countries. The global future of neurodiversity will be decided in classrooms without aides, clinics without specialists, households without respite, refugee camps without medication, and movements without translation. If Neurodivergent Power can meet people there, it will deserve the name.
