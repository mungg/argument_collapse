The Innovation Discourse Is Still Too Narrow

Dan Breznitz wants us to expand our conception of innovation beyond Silicon Valley's narrow vision of disruptive startups. He is right to want this, and his stages-of-production framework is a real contribution. But I want to argue that even his expanded framework is too narrow. By keeping the focus on what happens inside firms competing in tradable-goods markets, the essay reproduces a productivist conception of innovation that excludes much of what actually drives human welfare improvement. If we are serious about "inclusive prosperity," we need a much broader account.

Consider what is missing from the four stages. There is no stage for innovation in the delivery of public services. The transformation of New York City's traffic safety record through Vision Zero, the adoption of conditional cash transfers in Latin America, the Estonian e-government infrastructure, the Singapore public housing model—each represents enormous innovation in the production of human welfare, and none fits neatly into Breznitz's typology because the "product" is not a tradable good and the "firm" is a public agency. Yet for ordinary people's daily lives, these innovations matter at least as much as the latest carbon-fiber bicycle frame.

There is no stage, either, for social innovation in the broader sense: new organizational forms (worker cooperatives, B-corps, community land trusts), new institutional arrangements (participatory budgeting, mini-publics, deliberative polling), new ways of organizing care work and household reproduction. The history of welfare improvement in the twentieth century is not mainly a history of new products. It is a history of public health systems, universal education, social insurance, labor rights, women's emancipation, and the gradual democratization of voice. None of this shows up in conventional innovation discourse, including, I'm afraid, Breznitz's expanded version.

This matters because the inclusive prosperity Breznitz wants is mostly delivered through these channels, not through which stage of bicycle production a community specializes in. Compare the actual welfare delivered to a working-class household by, say, Medicare expansion versus a marginal increase in local manufacturing employment. The latter is real and valuable; the former is transformative. A serious agenda for inclusive prosperity has to put public goods and social institutions at the center, not at the periphery.

Breznitz gestures at this in his concluding paragraph when he mentions the need for education, shared assets, and collaborative public spaces. But he frames these as inputs to industrial competitiveness rather than as ends in themselves or as innovation domains in their own right. The implicit theory of value is still firm-centered: build the cluster, hire the workers, generate the prosperity. The trouble is that even the best clusters do not generate prosperity for everyone in their communities, and even communities without thriving clusters can be made decent places to live through serious public investment.

Take a concrete example. Cleveland, one of Breznitz's lost innovation hubs, has not failed because it picked the wrong innovation stage. It failed because its industrial base eroded, yes, but also because its tax base eroded, its schools were starved, its public transit was hollowed out, and its housing stock fell into disrepair. A Cleveland that became a stage three component innovation hub would be richer, but absent reforms in its public sector and metropolitan governance, the gains would not be widely shared. Conversely, a Cleveland that aggressively rebuilt its public services and metropolitan infrastructure could substantially raise welfare even without a dramatic industrial revival.

I'd push further. Some of the most important innovations of the next twenty years will likely happen in the public-services domain. The climate transition will require massive innovations in how we provide energy as a service, how we organize transit, how we retrofit buildings at scale. Healthcare reform—if we ever undertake it seriously—will require innovations in how care is delivered, financed, and integrated. Education will need to grapple with the implications of automation for what skills are worth teaching and how. Each of these is an innovation domain in its own right, with its own ecosystem of actors and its own potential for local employment generation.

The reason these don't appear in standard innovation discourse, I suspect, is that they don't generate the kinds of returns that animate venture capital or the kinds of measurable productivity gains that animate growth economics. Public-service innovation rarely produces unicorns. Social innovation rarely produces patents. But this is precisely the productivist bias I want to question. The metric that ultimately matters is whether ordinary people live longer, healthier, more dignified lives. Many of the highest-impact paths to that goal run through the public sector and civil society rather than through firms.

A second point: by focusing on stages of production within firms, Breznitz tends to treat workers as outputs of the innovation process—they are employed at various skill levels, paid various wages—rather than as agents of innovation themselves. But some of the most important forms of incremental, continuous improvement happen on shop floors, in offices, in hospitals, and in classrooms through the tacit knowledge and creativity of frontline workers. Toyota's famous production system worked because it took worker suggestions seriously. Many of the productivity differences across firms in the same industry come down to whether they tap their workers' knowledge or treat them as inputs. An innovation policy that takes this seriously would look quite different from one focused on firm-level R&D capacities. It would invest in workplace democracy, codetermination, union representation, and the institutional infrastructure that allows worker knowledge to surface and shape production.

Third: Breznitz's framework, like most innovation discourse, treats innovation as unambiguously good. But of course many innovations are harmful. Surveillance capitalism is innovation. Predatory financial products are innovation. Engineered addiction in social media platforms is innovation. The political economy of innovation requires us to evaluate not just whether it happens but whether it points in welfare-improving directions. The current American innovation ecosystem has, in important respects, been remarkably effective at producing innovations whose welfare consequences are dubious or negative. A discourse that focused only on how to do more innovation, faster, in more places, without asking what we want innovation for, would be misguided. Breznitz partially answers this by emphasizing inclusive prosperity as the goal, but the question of which kinds of innovation we want more of and which we want less of deserves more direct treatment.

So my response to Breznitz is: yes, expand the discourse beyond Silicon Valley, but expand it much further than the stages-of-production framework. The most consequential innovations of the next generation will happen in public services, social institutions, and the climate transition. Industrial innovation matters, but it is one channel among many for delivering the welfare improvements we ultimately care about.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The framework excludes non-tradable public sector innovations, such as e-government and public housing, which are essential for improving daily life.
  2. 02True inclusive prosperity is delivered through social innovations and institutional arrangements—like universal healthcare and labor rights—rather than just industrial specialization.
  3. 03Public services and social institutions should be viewed as primary domains of innovation and ends in themselves, rather than merely as inputs for firm-level competitiveness.
  4. 04The focus on production stages treats workers as passive outputs of innovation rather than active agents whose tacit knowledge and workplace democracy drive improvement.
  5. 05A narrow focus on production stages fails to account for the political economy of innovation, ignoring the fact that many technological and financial innovations can be socially harmful.
  6. 06The most critical innovations for the future will likely occur in the public domain, specifically regarding the climate transition, healthcare delivery, and education.
