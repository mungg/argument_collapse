"The international community should prioritize a moratorium on Arctic offshore oil drilling in favor of sustainable public investment and a binding international safety regime."

The starting point for Arctic policy should be humility. The region is warming faster than the rest of the planet, its ecosystems are poorly understood, and the practical ability to respond to a major oil spill in dark, ice-choked, storm-prone waters remains doubtful. In that setting, the Obama administration’s conditional approval for Shell to drill sends exactly the wrong signal. It says that the world can lament Arctic melting with one hand and exploit the newly accessible oil with the other.

A serious balance among environmental protection, economic development and social equity would begin with a moratorium on offshore oil drilling in the Arctic Ocean, at least until there is a binding international safety regime, demonstrated spill-response capacity and a climate policy consistent with avoiding dangerous warming. The Arctic is not merely another frontier oil province. Its remoteness makes ordinary industrial risks extraordinary. The 2010 Gulf of Mexico spill occurred in a region with extensive infrastructure, ports, roads, trained personnel and months of open-water access. Even there, the damage was enormous. In the Arctic, a comparable accident could be harder to contain and slower to clean up, with consequences for marine mammals, fisheries and subsistence communities that depend directly on them.

Economic development is a real need in the North. Many Arctic communities face high unemployment, high energy costs, inadequate housing and limited infrastructure. But it is lazy to equate development with oil extraction. Public investment in ports, broadband, health care, renewable energy, local fisheries, research stations and climate adaptation would create jobs without locking communities into boom-and-bust dependence on distant corporations. If the international community can mobilize capital to drill in the Arctic, it can mobilize capital to help Arctic residents live safely and prosperously in a changing climate.

Social equity also requires that indigenous peoples not be treated as symbols in environmental campaigns or obstacles in corporate permitting. They must have real power over decisions affecting their lands and waters. But consent is meaningful only if communities are offered more than a choice between poverty and risky extraction. Equity means revenue-sharing where development occurs, but also the right to say no.

The Arctic Council should use the American chairmanship to move beyond speeches about stewardship. It should press for protected marine areas, stricter rules on shipping and black carbon, coordinated scientific monitoring, and a binding offshore drilling code. The United States should also strengthen its credibility by aligning Arctic policy with climate policy. The oil beneath the Arctic Ocean is valuable only if burned. The world cannot protect the Arctic while planning to profit from its destruction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The extreme environmental conditions and remoteness of the Arctic make the risks of an oil spill far more dangerous and difficult to contain than in other regions.
  2. 02Economic development for Arctic communities should focus on public infrastructure and renewable energy rather than a boom-and-bust dependence on oil extraction.
  3. 03Social equity requires that indigenous peoples have genuine decision-making power, including the right to reject risky industrial projects.
  4. 04The United States and the Arctic Council must align their regional policies with global climate goals by ending the pursuit of new fossil fuel reserves.
  5. 05A binding international safety and environmental regime must be established before any industrial exploitation of the region is considered.
