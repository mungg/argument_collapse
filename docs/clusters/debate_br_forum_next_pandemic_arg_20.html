Humility Beyond the West

Sheila Jasanoff’s forum essay is a powerful dissection of the hubris embedded in Western pandemic preparedness. Her call to exchange the militaristic, predictive mindset for a “technology of humility” that foregrounds consequence, vulnerability, and plural perspectives deserves wide attention. Yet, for all its critical acuity, the essay remains trapped within a Euro-American frame. It dissects the failures of the Global Health Security Index, U.S. presidential playbooks, and the New England Journal of Medicine as if they constitute the sum total of pandemic governance worth engaging. The real technologies of humility, I want to suggest, were already being practiced in 2020 and 2021 across the Global South and in marginalized communities—often in spite of, not because of, the interventions of Geneva, Washington, or London. Any genuinely humble public policy after the pandemic must begin by decentering the West and learning from those who have long managed existential uncertainty with scarce resources and without the pretense of control.

Consider the response to COVID-19 in countries that the GHSI ranked near the bottom. Senegal, for instance, entered the pandemic with a health system that any index would dismiss. Yet through a combination of community health workers, mobile testing labs adapted from HIV and Ebola programs, and the trusted authority of local religious leaders, the country achieved remarkably low case counts in the first year without the sweeping lockdowns that characterized many wealthy nations. This was not a fluke born of good fortune or a hot climate. It was the expression of an epistemic orientation that Jasanoff’s framework could help us name: a practical humility that begins by acknowledging the limits of the central state and builds response capacity from existing social fabrics rather than from a distant command center. The government did not try to crush any curve with a D-Day invasion; it wove a net from threads that already existed.

Similarly, Kerala in India mounted a famous defense against the virus long before the national government’s catastrophic second wave. Its success drew on a deep infrastructure of local governance, with thousands of trained community volunteers, a robust public health system that had been nurtured by decades of political investment, and an ethos of mutual care that owed as much to the state’s history of land reform and democratic participation as to any technical playbook. When Keralan officials tracked contacts or set up community kitchens for quarantined families, they did not consult a dashboard of 140 indicators. They drew on a lived tradition of what one might call distributed humility: the assumption that effective action requires trusting and resourcing citizens at the very edge of the state.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” gesture toward such participatory mechanisms, but they remain conceptually nested in the institutional imagination of Harvard, the Nuclear Threat Initiative, and the Economist Intelligence Unit. They are things to be designed by policy scholars and inserted into the bloodstream of an existing nation-state apparatus. This overlooks the possibility that the most important knowledge might not be procedural but cultural, embedded in social movements, informal mutual aid networks, and Indigenous ways of knowing that do not translate easily into a federal advisory committee’s terms of reference. The Zapatista communities in Chiapas, to take another example, closed their borders early and survived the pandemic with minimal state support by activating centuries-old traditions of communal decision-making, rotating labor, and agroecological self-sufficiency. Their precautionary practice was not a policy choice deduced from a Rio Declaration text; it was a way of life that had long understood that survival depends on humility before the land and solidarity among neighbors.

None of this is to romanticize poverty or to suggest that the Global South possesses some innate wisdom that the North lacks. These communities made painful sacrifices, and many suffered grievously from economic collapse and the brutal inequities of international vaccine hoarding, a topic Jasanoff scarcely mentions. But they also offer living proof that the “middle way” she seeks between paralysis and hubristic action is not a theoretical puzzle to be solved by a Boston Review forum. It is an existing, tested, plural reality. A truly humble global public policy would therefore stop trying to export the playbook model—even a reformed, more participatory one—and would instead invert the flow of learning. It would ask how the informal solidarity economies of Latin America, the community health wisdom of West Africa, or the federated local governance of Kerala can inform a new architecture of pandemic response in which the global North is a student, not a teacher.

The essay’s concluding image, of King Lear on the heath discovering the suffering of wretches, is revealing. In Jasanoff’s telling, the sovereign must expose himself to feel what wretches feel. But millions of those so-called wretches have been feeling and acting for themselves all along, with a clarity that Lear never achieved. The project for the future is not to cultivate a kinder, more humble sovereign; it is to dismantle the global sovereign structure that still presumes to know what is best for everyone else.
