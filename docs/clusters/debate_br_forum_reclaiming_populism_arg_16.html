"Reclaiming populism for the left requires more than a rhetorical shift; it demands a direct confrontation with the historical and structural ways populism has been used to reinforce white supremacy and divide the working class."

I want to engage with a dimension of Adom Getachew's essay that the forum's essays otherwise address but that deserves more sustained attention: the relationship between populism and white supremacy. This is not a peripheral concern but a central tension that any effort to build a multiracial progressive coalition must confront. Getachew gestures toward this tension when she notes that the left must reckon with "the persistence of white supremacy," but the essays do not fully explore what this reckoning requires or how it might be accomplished. In this response, I want to suggest that the effort to reclaim populism is more complicated than it might appear precisely because populism and white supremacy have been so deeply intertwined in American history—and continue to be intertwined in ways that the left has not adequately confronted.

The history is familiar, if not always acknowledged. The original Populist movement of the late nineteenth century explicitly excluded Black Americans and used racial appeals to split the working class along color lines. The New Deal coalition, for all its achievements, was built on a deal with Southern segregationists that systematically subordinated Black interests to the interests of white workers. The George Wallace campaign of 1968 demonstrated the political efficacy of a populist appeal that explicitly combined anti-establishment rhetoric with white supremacist content. And Donald Trump's victory in 2016 showed that a racially coded populism could win presidential elections in the twenty-first century.

The pattern is clear: populism in America has frequently served as a vehicle for white identity politics, even when it has also contained genuine critiques of concentrated economic power. The reasons for this are structural. White supremacy has always been functional for elite interests, providing a divide-and-conquer mechanism that prevents the formation of the multiracial working-class coalitions that would threaten concentrated wealth and power. The elites who benefit from the existing order have consistently been willing to share racial privileges with working-class whites in order to prevent them from recognizing their common interests with workers of color.

Given this history, what does it mean to "reclaim" populism for the left? The essays in this forum offer a partial answer: a populism that is explicitly multiracial, that centers the experiences of the most marginalized, and that refuses the exclusionary logic of its reactionary counterparts. This is a necessary correction, but it is not sufficient. The problem is not only what populism says but what it has done—and what it has done is to provide a rhetorical and organizational framework that white supremacists have used more effectively than the left has.

Consider the contemporary moment. The COVID-19 pandemic has generated conditions that are potentially fertile for populist mobilization: economic devastation, institutional failure, a sense that the established order has abandoned ordinary people. The left wants to channel this potential in a progressive direction. But the right is already doing so—with appeals to Chinese blame, immigrant scapegoating, and the usual battery of racialized resentments. The question is not whether a progressive populism can be constructed but whether it can compete with a reactionary populism that has deep roots in American political culture.

My answer is: not easily, and not without fundamental changes in how the left approaches the challenge. The effort to build a "multiracial working class alignment" that the Working Families Party advocates is exactly right, but it requires more than a commitment to racial justice in the abstract. It requires a sustained engagement with the ways that white workers have been integrated into an identity that has historically been defined in opposition to Black and brown Americans. It requires an honest reckoning with the ways that progressive politics has sometimes failed to deliver for white workers—even as it has delivered far less for workers of color—and with the ways that racial resentments have been weaponized by conservative forces.

This is not a comfortable analysis, and it does not offer easy solutions. But the alternative—to proceed as if the reclamation of populism is primarily a matter of discourse and framing—is to repeat the mistakes of the past. The left cannot build a durable multiracial coalition by simply insisting that white workers should see their interests as identical to those of workers of color. It must earn that recognition through sustained organizing, through concrete victories that demonstrate progressive politics can deliver for all workers, and through an honest engagement with the history that has made racial division so central to American political life.

Getachew and her collaborators have done valuable work in calling our attention to these questions. The essays in this forum do not always pursue the implications as far as they might, but they have at least put the right issues on the table. The left that emerges from this crisis will be measured by its capacity to build the multiracial coalition that is essential for progressive governance—and by its willingness to confront the history of racial division that has prevented such a coalition from forming in the past. This is the task that "reclaiming populism," at its best, might help to accomplish. It is also a task that will require much more than the reclamation of a word.

  5. 05The left must earn the trust of a multiracial coalition through concrete material victories rather than assuming that shared economic interests will automatically overcome historical racial divisions.
