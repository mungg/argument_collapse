"To effectively reclaim the concept of freedom from the right, the left must radicalize it by integrating a climate politics that recognizes ecological stability and public decarbonization as essential preconditions for genuine self-rule."

Aziz Rana’s essay invites the left to reclaim freedom from the right’s individualism, militarism, and racial exclusion. A climate politics should accept that invitation and radicalize it. In the twenty-first century, no account of freedom is adequate if it ignores the atmosphere. A person is not free when heat makes outdoor labor deadly, when wildfire smoke closes schools, when floods destroy homes, when drought ruins farms, when insurance markets retreat, when fossil fuel companies decide the conditions of planetary life, or when future generations inherit a destabilized world they did not choose.

Climate change exposes the limits of the right’s freedom with unusual clarity. The freedom of fossil fuel firms to extract and burn carbon becomes unfreedom for everyone else. The freedom of wealthy consumers to emit without limit becomes heat stress for the poor. The freedom of developers to build in risky places becomes public disaster costs. The freedom to oppose regulation becomes asthma, displacement, crop failure, and mass death. Here the old question—freedom for whom?—cannot be avoided. Carbon pollution is domination stretched across space and time.

Rana’s history of settler freedom is deeply relevant. The United States was built on land treated as inexhaustible and on peoples treated as removable. That orientation did not disappear when the frontier closed. It became a broader extractive habit: forests, coal, oil, gas, minerals, soil, water, labor, and sacrifice zones. The fantasy of unencumbered individual freedom depends on an ecological illusion: that one can act without consequences for others. Climate change is the collapse of that illusion.

It is also the return of empire in atmospheric form. The countries and communities least responsible for cumulative emissions are often among the most vulnerable to climate harms. Small island states, drought-prone regions, floodplains, and poorer nations face losses shaped by industrialization elsewhere. Within the United States, low-income communities, Indigenous nations, Black neighborhoods, farmworkers, prisoners, elderly people, and outdoor workers face disproportionate risks. A climate freedom politics must therefore be anti-imperial and anti-racist not as an add-on but because the climate crisis is structured by unequal power.

The right has tried to frame climate policy as a threat to freedom: bureaucrats taking your gas stove, environmentalists taking your truck, globalists taking your sovereignty, regulators taking your job. This rhetoric works when climate policy is experienced as sacrifice imposed from above. If decarbonization means higher bills, lost work, unreliable transit, expensive electric cars for the affluent, and lectures from professionals who fly frequently, resentment is predictable. The left cannot answer with moral scolding. It must make green freedom material.

What would green freedom mean? First, freedom from fossil fuel dependency. Households should not be at the mercy of volatile oil and gas prices or utilities invested in old infrastructure. Public investment in renewable energy, transmission, storage, efficiency, and electrification can reduce bills and pollution. But ownership matters. If the energy transition simply replaces fossil monopolies with clean-energy oligopolies, democratic freedom will remain thin. Public power, rural electric cooperatives, community solar, and strong regulation can make energy a public good rather than a profit center.

Second, freedom for workers in the transition. Coal miners, refinery workers, pipeline workers, autoworkers, and utility workers cannot be treated as collateral damage. A just transition must include wage guarantees, pensions, retraining that leads to real jobs, union rights in new industries, and investment in affected communities. The phrase “green jobs” is not enough. Jobs must be good, stable, and democratically governed. Rana’s emphasis on workplace democracy is crucial here. Decarbonization will fail politically if it is done to workers rather than with them.

Third, freedom from climate precarity requires adaptation as a public project. Sea walls, cooling centers, urban trees, floodplain restoration, resilient housing, public health systems, wildfire management, and disaster response are not luxuries. They are freedom infrastructure. A family without air conditioning during a deadly heat wave is not free. A renter in a basement apartment during extreme rainfall is not free. A farmworker without heat protections is not free. A prisoner locked in an overheated cell is not free. Climate adaptation must be planned democratically, with priority for those at greatest risk.

Fourth, housing and transportation must be central. Many Americans are locked into car dependency not because they love oil companies but because zoning, sprawl, underfunded transit, and housing costs leave them little choice. A climate politics that only raises fuel prices will be experienced as punishment. A freedom-centered approach would build abundant housing near jobs and transit, protect tenants, invest in buses and trains, make walking and biking safe, and reduce the time people lose to commutes. Time is a climate issue; so is land use.

Fifth, Indigenous sovereignty must be more than symbolic. Indigenous communities have long resisted extraction and protected ecosystems, often while facing poverty, legal marginalization, and state violence. A different freedom would respect treaty rights, return land where possible, require consent for projects affecting Native nations, and support Indigenous-led conservation and renewable development. The climate movement cannot reproduce settler habits in green form by imposing mines, transmission lines, or conservation regimes without justice.

Sixth, global climate responsibility must be taken seriously. The United States owes more than domestic decarbonization. It owes finance for mitigation and adaptation abroad, technology sharing, support for loss and damage, and reform of debt burdens that force poorer countries to choose between development and resilience. This is not charity. It is a matter of historical responsibility and shared survival. Rana’s critique of American primacy points directly here: a country that helped create the crisis cannot claim freedom while externalizing the costs.

There are tensions. Rapid decarbonization requires permitting and building at a pace that can conflict with local control. Transmission lines, dense housing, wind farms, and transit projects will face opposition, sometimes from communities with legitimate concerns and sometimes from affluent veto players defending exclusion. A democratic climate politics cannot mean that every local objection prevails. Freedom as self-rule must include the people downwind, downstream, and in the future. Institutions must weigh local voice with broader obligations.

There is also the danger of technocracy. Climate policy can become a domain of models, targets, and acronyms inaccessible to ordinary citizens. But if people are to experience climate action as freedom, they need agency: participatory planning, public jobs programs, community benefits agreements, union bargaining, citizen assemblies, and local ownership. The energy transition should be a school of democracy.

The great merit of Rana’s essay is that it rejects the idea of freedom as escape from interdependence. Climate change makes that rejection unavoidable. We are in each other’s atmosphere. The question is whether interdependence will be governed by corporations, militarized borders, disaster capitalism, and unequal suffering—or by democratic planning, public goods, and solidarity.

The right’s freedom promises are especially hollow in a burning world. What good is the freedom to buy anything if the supply chains collapse? What good is property if it is uninsurable? What good is national sovereignty if smoke, heat, and storms cross borders? What good is individual choice when the basic conditions of choice are destabilized?

A different freedom must be livable—literally. It must mean clean air, safe water, public energy, dignified work, resilient communities, and a stable climate for those not yet born. Reclaiming freedom now means freeing ourselves from the fossil order and from the illusion that any of us can be secure alone.
