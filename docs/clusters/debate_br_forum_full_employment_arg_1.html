"Achieving full employment is a moral and economic necessity that requires a fundamental shift in public investment toward education and clean energy, supported by new political institutions and direct government action."

Robert Pollin is right to insist that the unemployment catastrophe is not merely a regrettable side effect of a financial crisis, but the central social failure of our time. The most important sentence in his essay is not a technical claim about multipliers or an estimate of clean-energy jobs; it is the moral claim that full employment is “basic as the right to a decent job.” That language is indispensable now because the debate in Washington has become strangely bloodless. We are invited to discuss “confidence,” “bond markets,” “structural adjustment,” and “fiscal consolidation” while millions of people lose not only paychecks but homes, health insurance, marriages, skills, and standing in their communities.

Pollin’s proposed reorientation—from military and fossil-fuel spending toward education and clean energy—has the great virtue of connecting the short-term jobs emergency to long-term public needs. It is not enough to “stimulate” the economy in the abstract. We should ask what kind of economy we are stimulating. If the answer is a revived version of the pre-crisis order—household debt, asset bubbles, stagnant wages, oil dependence, and a swollen financial sector—then recovery will be fragile even if the unemployment rate falls. A real recovery must rebuild the foundation on which broad prosperity rests.

Education is the obvious place to start. Few investments are more labor-intensive, less outsourceable, and more socially valuable. A child in a smaller class is not a make-work project. A repaired school building is not a statistical trick. A community college instructor retraining displaced workers is not a drain on the economy. These are the very activities that increase human capability and future productivity. If we can find public resources to rescue large financial institutions on the theory that their failure would damage the whole economy, we should be able to find resources to prevent the slow-motion destruction of human capital caused by prolonged unemployment and underfunded schools.

Clean energy is similarly attractive because it addresses several crises at once. Retrofitting buildings, modernizing the electrical grid, expanding public transit, producing wind and solar equipment, and improving energy efficiency create jobs that are both immediate and tied to a durable national project. They also reduce vulnerability to oil shocks, which, as Pollin notes, played a central role in the inflationary trauma of the 1970s. A full-employment strategy that ignores energy is incomplete; a climate strategy that ignores employment is politically vulnerable. Pollin’s approach is powerful because it refuses to choose between them.

Still, the strongest case for full employment should not rest only on sectoral job counts. Those numbers matter, but they are subject to dispute: How many jobs are truly additional? How quickly can workers move from one sector to another? What wages will new green jobs pay? How much will be imported? The deeper argument is macroeconomic and democratic. When labor markets are tight, ordinary workers gain bargaining power. Employers hire people they might otherwise overlook. Discrimination becomes more costly. Wages at the bottom rise. The late 1960s and late 1990s showed this clearly. Welfare reform’s more benign results in the late 1990s depended heavily on a labor market in which low-wage workers could actually find jobs. Without tight labor markets, lectures about responsibility become punitive moralizing.

Pollin is also correct that inflation cannot be dismissed. A serious full-employment politics must be more than a demand for endless deficit spending. It must include institutions capable of managing bottlenecks, training workers, coordinating wage expectations, and preventing finance from transforming every boom into a speculative bubble. Here the Swedish example is useful, not as a blueprint, but as a reminder that macroeconomic outcomes are shaped by institutions. Inflation is not a natural disaster. It is mediated by wage bargaining, pricing power, energy costs, productivity growth, imports, and the behavior of central banks. The United States lacks Sweden’s centralized labor movement, but that is an argument for institution-building, not resignation.

Where I would press Pollin further is on the political vehicle. He ends by asking whether there is political will. But political will is not an atmospheric condition; it is organized. Who will fight for full employment? Organized labor is weaker than at any point in modern memory. Many state and local governments are cutting payrolls because balanced-budget rules force them into austerity. The business community, or at least its most powerful voices, has discovered that it can enjoy profits and rising stock prices even with weak employment, especially if it operates globally. The financial sector has largely survived the crisis politically intact. Under these conditions, full employment will not be restored by better arguments alone.

A full-employment movement needs concrete demands that people can understand and defend. First, federal aid to states and municipalities should be expanded, not withdrawn, until employment recovers. It is self-defeating for Washington to support demand while states lay off teachers, transit workers, librarians, and health-care employees. Second, the federal government should directly fund public-service jobs in communities with severe unemployment. We should not be embarrassed by direct job creation. If private investment is paralyzed, public employment is not a distortion; it is a remedy. Third, infrastructure and energy investment should be planned over a decade, not improvised as temporary stimulus. Businesses and workers need to know that the commitment is real. Fourth, the Federal Reserve’s dual mandate should be treated as a genuine dual mandate. Price stability cannot be allowed to mean indifference to mass unemployment.

Pollin’s critics will say we cannot afford this. But the current path is expensive too. Unemployment increases deficits by reducing tax revenue and raising safety-net spending. It leaves factories idle, skills unused, and young people scarred at the beginning of their working lives. It shifts costs onto families, neighborhoods, and future budgets. The question is not whether we pay; it is whether we pay for decay or renewal.

The United States has tolerated mass unemployment partly because its burdens are unequal. If fifteen million unemployed people were drawn proportionally from the boardrooms, editorial pages, law firms, universities, and congressional offices that shape opinion, austerity would not sound so prudent. Pollin’s essay reminds us that full employment is not utopian. We have approached it before. Other countries have pursued it more deliberately. The obstacle is not knowledge but power.

A society that can mobilize trillions for war, tax cuts, and financial rescue can mobilize for work. The task now is to make unemployment politically intolerable.

"A viable full-employment program must move beyond education and clean energy to place the care economy—including child care, elder care, and disability support—at its foundational center."

Robert Pollin’s call for full employment is welcome, but his essay gives too little attention to the kinds of work that make employment possible in the first place. He identifies education and clean energy as high-employment sectors and contrasts them with the military and fossil fuels. That is a useful comparison. Yet any serious full-employment program must place care work—child care, elder care, home health care, disability support, early childhood education, and related social services—at its center. If full employment is about building a decent society, then the care economy is not peripheral. It is foundational.

The recession has exposed a long-standing contradiction in American economic life. We depend on care, but we undervalue it. Families cannot participate fully in paid work unless children are safe, elders are supported, and people with disabilities have assistance. Hospitals discharge patients into homes where family members—usually women—provide unpaid labor. Low-wage workers patch together child care arrangements that collapse when a shift changes. Middle-class families spend college-level sums on preschool or nursing-home care. Immigrant women, African American women, and Latinas perform much of the paid care labor, often for low wages and few benefits. This is not a side issue in employment policy. It is employment policy.

Pollin rightly says that full employment cannot mean the Bolivian version, where a person selling gum on the street is counted as employed. But the United States has its own version of disguised underemployment: unpaid or underpaid care work hidden inside households. A daughter who leaves a job to care for a parent with dementia disappears from the unemployment statistics. A mother who can work only part-time because child care is unaffordable is counted as employed, though not as fully as she would like. A home health aide working for poverty wages is “employed,” but not in the sense Pollin means when he speaks of decent jobs. If we want an abundance of decent work, we must transform the care sector.

The employment potential is enormous. Care work is labor-intensive, geographically rooted, and difficult to offshore. It creates jobs in every community, including rural areas and deindustrialized towns that may not attract high-tech firms. It also enables other employment by reducing the constraints that keep caregivers—again, disproportionately women—from taking or keeping jobs. A public investment in universal pre-kindergarten, affordable child care, long-term care services, and home health support would have a double employment effect: hiring care workers directly and expanding labor-force participation among family caregivers.

This is not merely a matter of compassion. It is a matter of economic efficiency. When parents cannot find reliable child care, employers face absenteeism and turnover. When elder care is unavailable, middle-aged workers reduce hours or exit the labor force. When early childhood development is neglected, schools later struggle with problems that could have been addressed earlier. When home care is underfunded, patients end up in more expensive institutional settings. Care investment produces social returns that conventional productivity statistics often miss.

Pollin’s emphasis on education points in this direction, but education should be understood broadly, beginning before kindergarten and extending through lifelong learning. The divide between “education” and “care” is artificial. A good preschool teacher is both educator and caregiver. A home health aide helping an elderly person remain independent is sustaining human capability. A disability support worker enabling someone to participate in community life is expanding freedom. If we reserve the word “investment” for buildings, machines, and energy systems, we reproduce the very blindness that has made care labor invisible.

A care-centered full-employment policy would also help correct gender inequality. The late 1960s and late 1990s showed that tight labor markets benefit marginalized workers. But tight labor markets alone do not solve the care problem. Women’s employment rose dramatically over recent decades, yet public policy did not build the infrastructure needed to support dual-earner and single-parent families. The result has been private stress: rushed mornings, unstable arrangements, part-time penalties, and a low-wage care workforce. A genuine full-employment economy must be compatible with family life. Otherwise “full employment” simply means everyone is expected to sell labor in the market while unpaid work is squeezed into the margins.

There is also a racial dimension. Pollin notes that full employment reduces discrimination because employers must recruit more broadly. True. But many care jobs are already filled by women of color and immigrants. If we expand these jobs without raising wages, benefits, training, and bargaining power, we will deepen occupational segregation. A care jobs program must therefore be a good jobs program: decent wages, health coverage, paid leave, career ladders, certification pathways, and the right to organize. The point is not to create a larger servant class. It is to professionalize and dignify socially necessary labor.

Some will object that care services are expensive and produce no tradable product. But this objection reveals a narrow view of wealth. A society is not richer because it neglects children cheaply or relies on exhausted relatives to care for elders. Moreover, many accepted public expenditures also do not produce tradable goods: policing, courts, much of health care, and the military. We fund them because they meet collective needs. Care meets collective needs as surely as roads and bridges do.

The financing question is real. Pollin proposes shifting money from military and fossil-fuel spending to education and clean energy. I would broaden the destination. Reduce unnecessary military commitments, end subsidies that encourage carbon dependence, and use progressive taxation to fund a national care infrastructure. Some benefits should be universal, both to avoid stigma and to build political support. Others can be income-related. States and localities should administer many programs, but federal funding is essential, especially in recessions when state budgets collapse.

A care economy strategy also complements clean energy. Retrofitting buildings and expanding transit are essential, but they will not employ everyone. Some workers will be better suited to human services than construction or manufacturing. A diversified full-employment program should recognize varied skills, ages, and aspirations. It should include jobs for people with college degrees and people without them, for young workers and older workers, for those in cities and those in small towns. Care work does that.

Pollin asks whether there is political will to fight for the right to a decent job. One way to build that will is to connect employment policy to the daily realities of families. “Green jobs” can sometimes sound abstract; “infrastructure” can sound like concrete and steel. But every voter understands the need for a safe place for a child after school, a trained aide for a frail parent, or a decent wage for the person who bathes, feeds, teaches, and comforts those we love.

Full employment is not only about producing more things. It is about organizing society so that human beings can flourish. That means clean energy, good schools, and repaired bridges. It also means care. If we leave care out of the full-employment agenda, we will reproduce an economy that counts what it can price and ignores what it cannot imagine living without.

"While Robert Pollin’s call for a full-employment economy is necessary, his framework must be expanded to prioritize the public sector, the specific economic needs of women, and the valuation of caregiving work."

Where Are the Women, the Caregivers, and the Public Sector?

Robert Pollin's essay covers a lot of ground, and I do not want to fault it for what it could not cover in 5,000 words. But I do want to register what is missing, because the absences shape the argument in important ways.

First, the essay barely mentions women. There is a passing reference to the late 1960s, when full employment brought "less job discrimination against women, African Americans, and other historically marginalized populations." That's it. And yet women's labor force participation is one of the largest economic transformations of the postwar period, and one of the most pertinent to a discussion of unemployment. Women's participation rose from about 33 percent in 1948 to 60 percent today. Most of this represents real liberation — women gaining economic autonomy, the ability to leave bad marriages, careers that match their talents. But some of it reflects economic necessity — the wage stagnation that Pollin documents for male workers from 1973 onward was masked, at the household level, by the addition of a second earner. The "American family income" did not collapse the way the male wage did, because women went to work.

This matters for the full-employment debate in several ways. First, the appropriate measure of "full employment" today must include women, not just men. The 1960s model that Pollin reaches back toward was implicitly a male-breadwinner model. We cannot and should not return to that. Second, the kinds of jobs that have been growing in the American economy — healthcare, education, retail, food service — are disproportionately filled by women and disproportionately undervalued in compensation. Full employment that left those jobs at their current low wages would not be a great victory. Third, the work of social reproduction — childcare, eldercare, household management — is overwhelmingly done by women, frequently unpaid, and is largely invisible in conventional labor statistics. A serious full-employment agenda has to engage this work, either by socializing it (publicly funded childcare, eldercare) or by valuing it (paid family leave, fair compensation for care workers, social security credits for caregiving years).

Pollin's reallocation proposal directs new money to "education and clean energy." Education is good, but the proposal as described — smaller class sizes, more financial aid, building improvements — is heavily K–12 and college-focused. What about early childhood education and childcare, where the United States lags far behind every other rich country? A full-funded universal pre-K and childcare system would do more for women's labor force participation, for child development, and yes, for employment (childcare workers, teachers) than reducing average class size from 23 to 19. It would also unlock the labor of women currently kept out of the workforce by the cost of childcare, which is now greater than college tuition in many states.

Second, the essay underplays the public sector. Pollin treats "education" as a sector to be invested in, but he frames the investment as flowing through ordinary fiscal channels — federal money to states, money to school districts, etc. The deeper structural question is that the United States runs an unusually small public sector by rich-country standards. Public employment in the U.S. is about 16 percent of total employment, compared with 25–30 percent in Scandinavian countries and around 20–22 percent in continental Europe. Much of the gap is in exactly the sectors Pollin highlights: healthcare, education, social services. If U.S. public employment as a share of the workforce were merely at the OECD average, we would have several million more public-sector jobs.

This points to a structural answer to the unemployment problem that Pollin gestures at but does not embrace. Rather than (or in addition to) shifting funding between sectors, simply expand the public sector. Hire more teachers, more public health nurses, more parks and recreation staff, more librarians, more social workers, more inspectors of all kinds. The work is needed; the workers are available. The reason it doesn't happen is not technical but ideological — the United States has, since the 1980s, treated public-sector employment as suspect, parasitic, and economically inferior to private-sector employment.

The current recession has actually been a public-sector contraction. State and local governments have shed several hundred thousand jobs since 2009, almost entirely because of revenue shortfalls and balanced-budget requirements. Federal aid to states would have prevented most of these layoffs. We laid off teachers, librarians, and bus drivers in the middle of a depression because of an artificial fiscal constraint. This is policy malpractice on an enormous scale. Pollin's essay, focused on the longer-term reallocation question, does not register the immediate public-sector emergency with the urgency it deserves.

Third, the essay treats globalization and immigration in a single brief paragraph each, in a way that, while correct on the headline conclusions, misses the gendered and racialized dimensions of both. The jobs most exposed to outsourcing are not evenly distributed across the workforce; the jobs most filled by immigrants are not the ones outsourcing pressures most directly affect. A real analysis of who gains and who loses from globalization and immigration policy needs to disaggregate by race, gender, sector, and region. The blunt aggregate claim "globalization is not the problem" is technically defensible but politically inert. The problem is which workers in which places are bearing the costs of how the U.S. has chosen to integrate with the world economy.

Fourth, and finally, Pollin's framing of full employment as "decent jobs" — the move he makes against the Bolivian Ministry of Planning economists who counted street hawkers as employed — is exactly right, but it deserves more development. What makes a job "decent"? Pay above some threshold, yes. Benefits, certainly. But also: hours that allow a life outside work, schedules that are predictable enough to permit caregiving, safety from harassment and physical danger, dignity at work. By any of these measures, large numbers of currently "employed" Americans hold "indecent" jobs. The fast-food worker on an unpredictable schedule, the retail worker without benefits, the Amazon warehouse worker on a brutal pace, the home healthcare aide making $9 an hour with no overtime protection — these are technically employed people, but they are not what most of us mean by the right to a decent job.

A full-employment agenda for the twenty-first century has to be not just about quantity of jobs but about the structural transformation of what work means in America. Pollin opens the door to this question and then walks past it. We should walk through.

"While Robert Pollin is correct to prioritize full employment over fiscal austerity, his specific policy prescriptions underestimate the structural, institutional, and global economic challenges required to achieve that goal."

Robert Pollin's call to make full employment the organizing principle of economic policy is bracing and overdue. After two years in which official unemployment has hovered above 9 percent and a broader U6 measure near 17 percent, the political conversation has nonetheless drifted toward deficit hawkery and a premature pivot to austerity — witness the recent Bowles-Simpson report and the ECB's tightening posture even as European peripheral economies bleed. Pollin is right that the choice is not between stimulus and a return to "normal," because the pre-crisis normal was a debt-fueled bubble economy that delivered stagnant median wages for three decades. The question is whether his prescription can carry the freight he assigns to it.

Start with the green-jobs arithmetic, which is the empirical centerpiece. The PERI input-output exercise is useful, but it elides a crucial distinction between gross and net job creation. A million dollars shifted from oil refining to weatherization generates more direct hires per dollar, but some of the displaced oil-sector workers will not be quickly absorbed, and the differential is partly a labor-intensity story that is another way of saying productivity is lower in the receiving sector. Over the long run, an economy whose marginal investments yield more jobs per dollar but less output per worker is one in which living standards rise more slowly. That tradeoff may well be worth making — especially when carbon externalities are priced in — but it should be named rather than absorbed into a headline figure of 4.8 million net jobs. A 25 percent cut to the Pentagon is also presented as if it were simply a fiscal switch; in practice it would generate dislocations concentrated in particular congressional districts, which is precisely why such cuts have proven politically unattainable since Eisenhower warned about them.

Second, the essay underweights what is probably the binding constraint right now: aggregate demand and the zero lower bound on nominal interest rates. The Fed's second round of quantitative easing, announced in November, is an admission that conventional monetary policy is exhausted. In that environment, the multiplier on fiscal expansion is unusually high, and the case for a much larger stimulus — not just a reallocation of existing spending — is overwhelming. Pollin nods at this but spends most of his pages on a longer-run structural agenda. The danger is that the structural agenda becomes a substitute for the cyclical fight rather than a complement to it. If we accept 7 percent unemployment as the baseline from which his green-jobs program subtracts 3 points, we have already conceded too much.

Third, the Swedish detour is illuminating but the conclusion drawn from it understates the institutional gap. The Rehn-Meidner model rested on centralized wage bargaining, solidaristic wage compression, and union density above 70 percent. American private-sector union density is now under 7 percent. Asking U.S. unions to "design a workable full-employment program" that internalizes inflation discipline is asking a movement without coordination capacity to do what only encompassing labor federations have managed. Without first rebuilding the institutional substrate — card check, sectoral bargaining, something — the Swedish lesson is more cautionary than instructive. Pollin's own evidence that Sweden itself abandoned the model under EU pressure should give pause.

Fourth, Pollin too quickly dismisses the trade-deficit channel. He is right that a competitive devaluation race is unappealing, but a persistent current-account deficit of 3–5 percent of GDP, sustained by Chinese reserve accumulation and an undervalued renminbi, is a steady drain on tradable-goods employment that no domestic reallocation can offset. A serious full-employment program needs an external strategy — coordinated rebalancing, perhaps via the kind of capital-flow management Keynes proposed at Bretton Woods — not just a domestic one.

These are friendly amendments. Pollin has the fundamental diagnosis right: the natural-rate framework has functioned as an ideological tranquilizer, persuading policymakers that 6 or 7 percent unemployment is a fact of nature rather than a policy choice. Recovering the moral and analytic standing of full employment as a goal is the necessary first step. The harder work — confronting the Fed's inflation orthodoxy, rebuilding labor power, taming finance, and coordinating internationally — still lies ahead.

"While Robert Pollin correctly identifies full employment as a moral and economic necessity, achieving it requires more than long-term spending shifts; it demands immediate direct public employment programs and a fundamental rebuilding of labor institutions to empower workers."

Robert Pollin is right to insist that “full employment” cannot mean merely the statistical disappearance of idleness. A society in which people survive by juggling temp work, involuntary part-time hours, unpaid family labor, and desperate self-employment has not solved unemployment; it has disguised it. The moral center of his argument is therefore powerful: a decent job is not only a paycheck but a source of bargaining power, social participation, and security. In the present crisis, with long-term unemployment at postwar highs and state and local governments cutting teachers, transit workers, and social services, treating joblessness as a regrettable side effect of deficit reduction is both economically foolish and politically corrosive.

But Pollin’s strongest claims also point to the hardest questions. He proposes a long-term redirection of spending from the military and fossil fuels toward education and clean energy. I agree that this is the right direction. Education is labor-intensive, locally rooted, and socially productive. Energy retrofits, transit, smart-grid investment, and renewable power would employ people while addressing climate change and reducing oil dependence. In principle, this is a better jobs program than another round of tax cuts tilted toward households likely to save rather than spend.

Still, the transition he describes cannot be the whole answer to the employment emergency of 2011. Millions of workers need jobs now, not after the political system has reorganized the Pentagon, rewritten energy policy, and built new financing channels for green investment. The immediate crisis calls for a more direct commitment: federal aid to states and cities to prevent layoffs; extension of unemployment insurance and food assistance; infrastructure repair with “use it or lose it” deadlines; and, if private hiring remains weak, public employment programs targeted at distressed communities. The Works Progress Administration is not a perfect model for a modern economy, but its premise remains sound: if the private sector will not hire idle workers and useful public work is waiting to be done, government should connect the two.

Pollin also underestimates the political difficulty of his proposed reallocation. Cutting military spending by 25 percent is economically defensible, especially as the Iraq war winds down and Afghanistan policy comes under scrutiny. But defense spending is not merely a budget category; it is a congressional jobs system, a foreign-policy ideology, and a network of contractors with deep local influence. Likewise, reducing fossil-fuel spending is not simply a matter of shifting dollars from dirty energy to clean energy. It means confronting consumers’ dependence on cheap gasoline, regional economies built around coal and oil, and companies whose profits are tied to existing reserves. A full-employment coalition will have to offer transition guarantees to workers in these sectors, not just promises about future green jobs.

The inflation question deserves similar realism. Pollin is right that the 1970s should not be reduced to a morality tale about excessive worker power; oil shocks mattered enormously. Climate change is not a problem about polar bears. Unemployment is not a problem about statistical aggregates. Both are problems about what kind of country, and what kind of species, we want to be. The climate-jobs nexus offers a story that connects the daily anxieties of ordinary people to the largest civilizational stakes imaginable. We should tell it everywhere, constantly, and back it with policy proposals that match the scale of what we say.

Pollin opens this door. Let us push through it.
