Pollin Is Right — and Doesn't Go Far Enough

Reading Robert Pollin's essay is bracing precisely because it is one of the few mainstream-adjacent pieces of policy writing in 2010 that takes mass unemployment seriously as a moral catastrophe. Thirty million people. Twice the combined populations of New York, Los Angeles, and Chicago. We are now nearly two years into the Obama administration, and the official conversation in Washington has moved on to deficit reduction. Pollin's reminder that 16.5 percent of American workers are unemployed or underemployed is exactly what is needed.

But I want to push the argument further, because Pollin, for all his radicalism on full employment, remains too cautious in his diagnosis of why we are not getting it. He treats the failure as ideological and political — the rise of neoliberalism, the weakness of American labor, Federal Reserve culture. All of this is true. But there is a deeper structural fact that the essay only glances at, and it is this: the American capitalist class has, for forty years, organized its political power specifically to prevent full employment, because full employment threatens its profits and its power. This is Kalecki's argument, which Pollin cites and then sets aside almost too politely.

Kalecki's 1943 essay "Political Aspects of Full Employment" is one of the most prescient documents of the twentieth century. He observed that businessmen oppose full employment not because they don't understand the Keynesian arithmetic — they understand it perfectly well — but because they understand the politics. Sustained full employment changes the balance of power on the shop floor. It changes the balance of power in collective bargaining. It changes the balance of power between renters and landlords, debtors and creditors, workers and managers. In a tight labor market, the boss who treats people badly loses them. In a slack labor market, the boss who treats people badly knows they have nowhere to go. Capital, as a class, prefers a slack market. Always. Everywhere.

This is not a left-wing fantasy. The minutes of Federal Reserve meetings over the past several decades, the editorial pages of the Wall Street Journal, the lobbying record of the Business Roundtable, the campaign-finance flows that punish politicians who push too hard on labor issues — all of it lines up. When unemployment falls below 5 percent, the financial press fills with warnings about "wage pressures" and "overheating." When unemployment is at 9 percent, the same press writes about the urgent need to cut social spending and restrain the deficit. The asymmetry is so consistent that it cannot be coincidence.

Pollin acknowledges this in his peroration: "Putting an end to neoliberalism will require nothing less than an epoch-defining reallocation of political power away from the interests of big business and Wall Street and toward the middle class, working people, and the poor." Yes. But he then offers as his concrete program a reallocation of $330 billion across four sectors, plus financial regulation, plus implied union revival. Will this reallocation by itself produce the necessary shift in political power? I doubt it. The reallocation can only be enacted by a political coalition that has already won the power struggle. Otherwise it won't get through Congress.

So what does the power struggle actually look like? Here are some directions Pollin's essay points toward but does not fully develop.

First, we need a massive rebuilding of American labor. Not just the existing AFL-CIO unions, but new forms of worker organization — sectoral bargaining, works councils, worker-cooperative networks, alt-labor organizations like the Restaurant Opportunities Centers and Domestic Workers United. The labor-law reform that died in 2009–10 with the failure of the Employee Free Choice Act needs to come back, in stronger form, and to extend to the millions of workers (independent contractors, gig workers, domestic workers, agricultural workers) currently outside the NLRA's protections entirely.

Second, we need to break up the banks. Pollin alludes to this — "financial-regulatory regime that channels private funding toward productive, employment-generating activities" — but he soft-pedals. The financial sector at its current size and political power is structurally incompatible with full employment, both because it diverts capital from productive uses and because it serves as the chief political enforcer of austerity discipline. The financial sector's share of corporate profits roughly tripled between 1980 and 2007, and this growth was directly correlated with the stagnation of wages. Either finance is brought back under firm regulatory control — Glass-Steagall, leverage limits, a financial transactions tax, public banking options — or it will continue to dominate macroeconomic policy.

Third, we need to think about workplace democracy as well as employment levels. Even if we achieved Pollin's 3.9 percent unemployment goal, an American worker would still spend most of their waking hours under conditions of authoritarian control. The boss decides everything: hours, breaks, what tasks you do, whether you can talk during work, often whether you can pee. Full employment in a context of workplace despotism is better than mass unemployment in the same context, but it is not full freedom. Worker representation on boards (the German model), expanded worker cooperatives, and stronger workplace rights deserve to be on the agenda alongside the employment level.

Fourth, and most fundamentally, we need to stop thinking of "the economy" as a neutral machine that policy adjusts and start thinking of it as a contested social institution. Pollin almost gets there — the Marx and Kalecki sections of his essay are excellent — but he keeps returning to a technocratic vocabulary of sectoral multipliers and labor-market interventions. The point of full employment is not that it is technically optimal; the point is that it is morally required, and politically possible only if working people organize themselves to demand it.

Pollin ends by asking whether Americans have "the political will to fight for something as basic as the right to a decent job." It is the right question. But political will is not a free-floating quantity. It is built by organizations, in struggles, over years. The right asks the same question constantly — "Do Americans have the will to cut taxes / restrict abortion / deregulate finance?" — and it builds, year after year, the institutions that answer "yes." The left needs to do the same for the right to a job, which means starting now, with the people we have, where they actually live and work.

Full employment is achievable. It will not arrive as a gift from a sympathetic technocracy. It will be taken.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Sustained full employment is actively resisted by capital because it shifts the balance of power toward workers, making it a political threat rather than just a technical challenge.
  2. 02Achieving a full-employment economy requires a massive rebuilding of labor power through new forms of worker organization and comprehensive labor-law reform.
  3. 03The financial sector must be aggressively downsized and regulated because its current size and power are structurally incompatible with full employment and serve as an enforcer of austerity.
  4. 04Policy goals should expand beyond mere employment levels to include workplace democracy and the dismantling of authoritarian control within the workplace.
  5. 05Full employment will not be granted by sympathetic technocrats through sectoral spending shifts but must be seized through a long-term reallocation of political power and institutional struggle.
