"While Nancy Hirschmann correctly identifies the pitfalls of state-subsidized care, her solution of pressuring women into the workforce to gain political leverage is a coercive new orthodoxy that undermines genuine pluralism and individual freedom."

Nancy Hirschmann has done an admirable job of tracing the unintended consequences of a particular strand of feminist advocacy. She shows convincingly that once care is framed as a public service, the door opens to public scrutiny and moralistic oversight. She also exposes the coercive power of a tax code that punishes two-earner families and the hollow arrogance of a “choice” discourse that ignores how choices are structured by law and custom. But where she goes badly wrong is in substituting one set of coercions for another. Her prescription—that women should reenter the workforce to gain bargaining power and effect a cultural revolution—amounts to a new orthodoxy that would treat the decision to stay home as a political sin. The better path is to stop trying to engineer family life from either the left or the right and instead allow a genuine, uncoerced pluralism.

Consider the tax penalty on dual-earner married couples that Hirschmann mentions. This is a real injustice, and it should be eliminated. A tax code that, through its rate structure or the treatment of Social Security, makes a second earner’s net contribution near zero is a tax code that distorts family decisions. But the right response is not to lobby for a system that “conducive to women’s pursuit of a wider variety of options”; that language still presumes that the state knows which options are preferable and should tilt the field accordingly. The right response is a flat, low-rate system that taxes individuals regardless of marital status, so that the state neither rewards nor punishes the choice of a single-earner household. Let families decide, without fiscal pressure, how to organize their private lives.

The same principle applies to the broader project of care feminism. Hirschmann is right to be skeptical of public compensation for care work, because once the state decides that raising children is a service for which it pays, the state will inevitably want to define and enforce standards. That is the logic of all public funding, and those who think they can avoid it with clever legal drafting are not being honest. The only safe way to prevent the state from sitting in judgment of mothers is to keep the state’s hand out of the family’s pocket in the first instance. If feminists want women to have economic security independent of husbands, the answer is not to put them on a government payroll but to eliminate the barriers—legal, regulatory, and cultural—that prevent women from accumulating their own wealth. Abolish occupational licensing that shuts women out of trades, lower the tax burden on savings and investment, remove the regulatory thicket that makes it hard to start a small business from home. Help women build capital, not dependency.

Hirschmann’s call for women to care less and work more is just another version of the old Puritan gospel of self-discipline, dressed up in feminist language. It assumes that the market is a sphere of freedom and the home a sphere of subjection, a binary that should have died with the first wave of industrialism. Plenty of women find the market alienating and the home a place of real creativity and satisfaction; plenty of men would rather spend more time with their children but feel bound by a breadwinner ethic that feminism has often fed rather than challenged. Telling women that their highest responsibility is to produce a political effect—to force men to change by wielding a paycheck—instrumentalizes personal life in the worst way. It takes the intimate decision of how to raise one’s children and makes it a weapon in a gender war.

A genuinely liberal feminism would defend the right of every woman to determine her own path, whether that means staying home, working full-time, or some mixture of both, and would fight to remove the structural obstacles that make any of those paths needlessly difficult. It would not assess a woman’s legitimacy by whether she chooses the path that most challenges the patriarchy. The mark of a free society is that women are not made to feel that their private arrangements are anyone else’s political business. If Hirschmann’s students want to be full-time mothers, and they do so with open eyes about the risks, that is their prerogative. Feminists should spend less time lecturing them and more time making sure that no woman is ever forced, by poverty or law or social stigma, to take a path she would not freely choose.
