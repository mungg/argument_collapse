A Modest Proposal: Treat Pension Trustees Like the Fiduciaries They Claim to Be

Lost in the heat of the transparency debate is a simple legal point. Trustees of public pension funds owe an undivided duty of loyalty to beneficiaries. That duty, articulated under ERISA-analog state laws and in centuries of trust jurisprudence, is not a vague aspiration. It requires trustees to act solely in the interest of plan participants, to avoid self-dealing, to monitor agents prudently, and — critically — to be able to document and defend every material decision they make.

A trustee who signs a nondisclosure agreement so restrictive that he or she cannot explain to beneficiaries what fees are being paid, what benchmark is being used, or who is profiting from a placement arrangement is arguably breaching that duty on its face. The "we can't tell you, it's confidential" defense would not fly in any private trust context. It should not fly here.

That suggests a different reform framing than the one we usually hear. Rather than legislate disclosure in granular detail — which invites endless lobbying and gamesmanship — strengthen fiduciary standards and enforce them. Specifically: (1) Require trustees to certify annually, under penalty of personal liability, that all material terms of every investment, including fees, side letters, and placement agent arrangements, have been reviewed and are prudent. (2) Create a private right of action for beneficiaries against trustees who execute confidentiality provisions that prevent meaningful oversight. (3) Empower state inspectors general or independent auditors — not the trustees themselves — to be the recipients of confidential commercial information, with statutory authority to report aggregate findings publicly.

This approach finesses the legitimate concerns about commercial sensitivity raised by alternative managers. The hedge fund that genuinely needs to keep its positions confidential can disclose to an independent auditor under a protective order, the way litigants do in court every day. What it cannot do is contract around the trustee's underlying duty of care and loyalty.

There is precedent for this. Several states have moved in this direction in fits and starts — New York after the Hevesi scandal, New Mexico after its placement agent debacle. The pattern is clear: scandal, brief reform, gradual backsliding as memories fade. Embedding the reforms in fiduciary law, rather than in transparency statutes that legislatures can quietly amend, makes the gains harder to reverse.

The pension system is a trust, in the deepest legal and moral sense. Trusts run on accountability, not on the goodwill of those who manage them. Restoring that accountability — not just publishing more documents — is the work that needs doing.
