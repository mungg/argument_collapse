Diversified DeepSeek

"Progressives should pursue a politics of constitutional abolition rather than reclamation, as the U.S. Constitution is an inherently undemocratic document designed to protect white supremacy and capital accumulation."

The lead essay offers a sweeping and erudite call to arms: progressives must reclaim the Constitution as a tool to fight oligarchy. In the democracy-of-opportunity tradition, they argue, lies a robust constitutional vision that demands the broad distribution of power and the dismantling of economic and racial hierarchy. This vision, they contend, is not only historically authentic but strategically necessary to counter the conservative judiciary’s weaponization of the First Amendment, federalism, and property rights. Yet for all its historical nuance, the essay remains trapped within the very liberal legalism it purports to transcend. It treats the Constitution as a fundamentally redeemable document, a vessel into which progressive content can be poured if only we recover the right interpretive traditions. This is a profound mistake. The Constitution is not a neutral container; it is a charter of white supremacy and capital accumulation, and the democracy-of-opportunity tradition itself is deeply implicated in the imperial and racial violence that made American “democracy” possible.

Let us take the authors’ historical narrative seriously, but on its own terms. They celebrate the Jacksonians’ anti-oligarchy fervor—their assault on the Second Bank of the United States and their championing of the “common man.” But whose common man? Jacksonian democracy was built on the ethnic cleansing of Native nations from the southeastern United States, an expansion of chattel slavery into new territories, and the consolidation of a white male polity that excluded women and Black people from the very “opportunity” the tradition promised. The Reconstruction Republicans, too, are hailed for their vision of political economy, yet their commitment to Black freedom quickly gave way to the restoration of planter oligarchy in the South once Northern industrial capital found it expedient to abandon Reconstruction. The New Deal’s labor laws, as the lead essay notes but does not dwell on, systematically excluded agricultural and domestic workers—overwhelmingly Black and Latino—from the protections of collective bargaining and social insurance. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition, in short, has always been a democracy of opportunity for some, built on the land, labor, and lives of others. To revive it without confronting this legacy is to invite a new iteration of the same bargain: a multiracial but still fundamentally capitalist polity that manages inequality rather than abolishing it.

The authors are right that conservatives have never abandoned the constitutional politics of political economy. They have used the courts to entrench property rights, dismantle labor power, and shield corporate speech. But this is not a perversion of the constitutional order; it is the constitutional order’s default setting. The original Constitution protected the international slave trade for twenty years, required the return of fugitive slaves, and counted enslaved people as three-fifths of a person for purposes of representation—not out of some accidental oversight, but because the slaveholding elite demanded these provisions as the price of union. The electoral college, the Senate, and the life-tenured judiciary were all designed to check democratic majorities, especially those that might threaten property. As legal historian Paul Finkelman has shown, the Constitution was “pro-slavery” in its fundamental structure. The Fourteenth Amendment transformed that baseline, but it was immediately neutered by a Supreme Court that invented corporate personhood and struck down civil rights laws while upholding segregation. The Constitution is not, and never has been, a friend to the poor, the working class, or racial minorities. It is a framework for managing elite conflict while containing popular insurgency.

The lead essay’s proposed strategy—to reclaim the First Amendment, reforge the link between race and political economy, and democratize antitrust and corporate law—accepts this framework as the terrain of struggle. But it vastly overestimates what can be achieved within it. The Supreme Court today is dominated by justices who are not merely conservative but reactionary, committed to an originalist methodology that will find no room for the democracy-of-opportunity tradition. As Andrea Scoseria Katz acknowledges in the forum, “so long as a conservative Supreme Court with textualist leanings remains the final arbiter of questions of constitutionality, this will be an uphill battle.” That is an understatement. The current majority is actively hostile to the very premises of social democracy. They will strike down campaign finance reforms, labor protections, and wealth taxes no matter how compelling the progressive constitutional narrative. And because the Court’s legitimacy rests on the myth of judicial neutrality, every progressive constitutional argument that loses in court reinforces the idea that the right’s vision is the true one.

A more honest progressive response would begin not with constitutional reclamation but with constitutional abolition. This is not a call for nihilism; it is a recognition that the existing constitutional order is incompatible with a truly egalitarian, multiracial democracy. Abolitionist thinkers like Ruth Wilson Gilmore and Angela Davis have taught us that institutions like the prison cannot be reformed; they must be dismantled and replaced with entirely different ways of organizing society. The same logic applies to the Constitution. It is a 1787 document, amended only sparingly and mostly in ways that expanded state power rather than popular power. Its core structures—federalism, separated powers, judicial review—systematically disperse authority in ways that make coordinated action against capital extraordinarily difficult. The amendment process is a dead letter, requiring supermajorities that are impossible to achieve in an era of minority rule. The Senate gives a structural veto to sparsely populated, predominantly white states. The electoral college has twice in recent memory installed a president who lost the popular vote. These are not bugs; they are features of a system designed to frustrate majoritarian democracy.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition, whatever its rhetorical appeal, cannot undo these facts. It offers no path to abolishing the Senate or the electoral college. It offers no mechanism for stripping the Court of its power to veto legislation. It offers, at best, a set of arguments that might occasionally influence a swing justice in a different era. This is a thin reed on which to rest the hopes of a generation facing climate collapse, mass incarceration, and staggering inequality.

What would a politics beyond the Constitution look like? It would start by treating the Constitution not as sacred text but as a political obstacle, to be ignored, circumvented, or overthrown as circumstances require. It would draw inspiration from the revolutionary movements that actually secured progressive gains—the abolitionists who defied the Fugitive Slave Act, the labor radicals who occupied factories, the civil rights activists who broke segregation laws and forced the federal government’s hand. In none of these cases did the Constitution provide the blueprint; it was the obstacle that had to be overcome through extralegal action. Even the New Deal, which the lead essay holds up as a model, was less a constitutional revolution than a political one: FDR threatened the Court, and it retreated. The Constitution didn’t compel the welfare state; mass strikes and the specter of socialist alternatives did.

Today’s movements—from the Fight for $15 to Black Lives Matter to the climate justice movement—are already operating largely outside the constitutional frame. They make demands on the state without worrying overly about the legal niceties. They build power in the streets and in workplaces, not in law reviews. This is where the energy lies, and it is where progressives should invest their intellectual and political capital. The lead essay’s call to make progressive politics constitutional again is a call to divert that energy into a dead-end fight over constitutional interpretation. It is a plea to play a rigged game, in the hope that this time the rules will be different.

The Constitution will not save us. It never has. The task is not to reclaim it but to build a new one—a democratic constitution forged in the struggles of the present, not the compromises of 1787. That requires a politics that is unashamedly revolutionary in its ambition, willing to break both laws and norms in pursuit of justice. It requires the abolition of the Senate, the electoral college, and the Supreme Court as we know them. It requires a constitutional convention that is not bound by Article V but driven by popular assemblies and social movements. This is not a fantasy; it is the only horizon worth fighting for. The lead essay, for all its erudition, offers a map to a past that never was. We need a compass that points toward a future that could be.

Diversified DeepSeek

"Progressives should stop trying to reclaim the Constitution through legal interpretation and instead focus on building the political power necessary to confront and transcend its inherently anti-democratic structures."

The lead essay’s proposal to “make progressive politics constitutional again” is seductive in its ambition. It promises a way to fight the conservative judicial juggernaut without abandoning the Constitution to the right. By reviving the democracy-of-opportunity tradition, progressives are urged to argue that egalitarian legislation is not just permitted but constitutionally required—that the First Amendment protects collective action by workers, that the Reconstruction Amendments demand economic justice, and that antitrust law is a constitutional necessity. This is a well-intentioned project, but it is also a dangerous trap. The Constitution, as both text and institutional practice, is fundamentally hostile to substantive equality. Trying to reclaim it for progressive ends is like trying to reclaim the master’s tools to dismantle the master’s house; it may yield temporary rhetorical victories, but it ultimately reinforces the very structures that block transformative change.

The authors are correct that the conservative legal movement never accepted the postwar liberal settlement, which separated law from politics and economics from democratic contestation. The right has always understood that the Constitution is a weapon for protecting property and hierarchy. But what the lead essay does not fully acknowledge is that this is not a perversion of the Constitution; it is the Constitution working as designed. The document ratified in 1787 was, from the start, a counter-majoritarian framework engineered to protect creditors, slaveholders, and propertied elites from the unruly passions of democratic assemblies. Its vaunted checks and balances were explicitly designed to filter and blunt popular will. The Senate, the Electoral College, the life-tenured judiciary, and the amendment process were all intended to insulate governance from the kind of broad, redistributive majorities that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition purports to celebrate. The Reconstruction Amendments expanded the franchise and promised equal protection, but they were quickly hollowed out by a judiciary that protected corporations as “persons” while abandoning Black citizens to Jim Crow. The New Deal’s constitutional revolution, far from fundamentally altering this architecture, was a political accommodation that left the underlying property regime intact, merely adding a modest welfare state on top.

In light of this history, the call to re-constitutionalize progressive politics risks legitimizing an illegitimate system. Every time progressives make a constitutional argument, they implicitly accept the authority of the courts to adjudicate the boundaries of the possible. They signal to the public that what matters is what the Constitution means, rather than what the people need. This is a gift to the right, which has spent decades populating the bench with true believers who will not be swayed by even the most eloquent progressive briefs. The Roberts Court is not interested in a dialogue. It is intent on imposing a radical vision of economic liberty and state sovereignty, and it is doing so with breathtaking speed. The leaked draft opinion in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, which would overturn Roe v. Wade, is only the most visible example. Behind it lies a whole docket of cases that will dismantle the administrative state, eviscerate labor protections, and lock in the political power of minority rule. Against this onslaught, the lead essay’s proposal is to argue back—to say that the Constitution really means the opposite of what the Court says it means. That is like bringing a historical monograph to a gunfight.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition itself is far from the unalloyed good that the authors paint. As Aziz Rana acknowledges in a passing quote, the “historical high tides” of that tradition occurred during periods of territorial expansion and empire. Jacksonian democracy was built on the genocide of Native Americans and the expansion of slavery. The New Deal’s labor protections were explicitly structured to exclude agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately Black—as a concession to Southern segregationists. And the postwar liberal consensus that the authors criticize was itself a product of the Cold War, in which any hint of economic radicalism was purged in the name of anti-communism. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition has always been deeply compromised by racial exclusion and imperialism. To resurrect it as a template for today is to whitewash its sins and to invite a politics that again subordinates the most marginalized to the interests of a relatively privileged labor elite.

The alternative to constitutional reclamation is not passivity. It is to treat the Constitution as what it has always been: a site of political struggle, not a source of binding authority. The right has already abandoned any pretense of constitutional fidelity when it suits them. They packed the courts through cynical procedural maneuvers, most famously by refusing to hold a hearing for Merrick Garland while rushing through Amy Coney Barrett. They use the filibuster to block majority-supported legislation while carving out exceptions for tax cuts and judicial appointments. They embrace “constitutional hardball” while lecturing liberals about norms. The proper response is not to play the constitutional argument game better; it is to change the rules of the game itself.

Progressives should be explicit that the current Supreme Court is a political entity with no legitimate claim to the final word on constitutional meaning. Congress has the power, under Article III, to strip the Court of jurisdiction over whole categories of cases. It can expand the lower federal courts and the Supreme Court itself. It can refuse to enforce judicial rulings, as President Andrew Jackson allegedly did in Worcester v. Georgia. These are not extra-constitutional measures; they are within the constitutional text, but they require the political courage to confront the judiciary directly. The lead essay flirts with this idea when it mentions that legislators must “contest hostile judges’ visions,” but it pulls back from the logical conclusion: that the judiciary must be broken as a veto point on democratic governance.

More radically, progressives should consider that the entire framework of constitutionalism may be an obstacle to the kind of transformation needed to forestall climate catastrophe, build racial justice, and end oligarchic rule. The Constitution’s amendment process is so onerous as to be a near-impossibility in an era of deep polarization. Its structural features—equal state representation in the Senate, the electoral college—systematically favor rural, white, conservative populations. No amount of clever constitutional interpretation can overcome the fact that Wyoming has the same number of senators as California. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition cannot fix this; it can only offer consoling fables about a Constitution that never truly existed.

A truly emancipatory politics would focus not on reinterpreting the Constitution but on building the extra-constitutional power to force change. That means mass strikes, civil disobedience, mutual aid networks, and the creation of alternative institutions that prefigure a more democratic society. It means treating election victories as opportunities to pass transformative legislation and daring the courts to strike it down, using noncompliance as a tool of political mobilization. It means, in the long run, constitutional reconstitution—a new founding that replaces the eighteenth-century framework with a genuinely democratic republic. The Movement for Black Lives has called for a “people’s constitution”; that is the right direction, not a retreat to a mythical anti-oligarchy past.

The lead essay is a serious and important contribution. Its authors know their history, and their frustration with liberal timidity is well placed. But their solution—making progressive politics constitutional again—is a strategy for a bygone era. We no longer live in a time when Supreme Court justices can be shamed into moderation or when eloquent briefs can sway the course of law. We live in an era of constitutional crisis, where the old rules no longer bind. In such moments, it is not the best arguments that win, but the side with the power and the will to impose its vision. Progressives should spend less time trying to reclaim the Constitution and more time building the power to transcend it.
