Diversified MiniMax

"Acemoglu’s proposal to redirect AI through government intervention is ideologically biased and relies on a flawed assumption that state planning can effectively and equitably correct market failures."

Acemoglu's essay exemplifies progressive thought on technology: sophisticated diagnosis, weak prescription, and an unexamined faith in state capacity. The essay deserves credit for engaging seriously with AI's societal implications. But its analysis ultimately serves a political project—expanding government intervention—that deserves scrutiny.

The essay's framing reveals its ideological commitments. It presents the postwar compact as a success story, achieved through technology and labor institutions working in tandem. But whose success? Women's unpaid domestic labor remained invisible in GDP statistics. Black Americans faced systematic exclusion from the postwar prosperity Acemoglu celebrates. Global South countries bore the costs of Bretton Woods institutions while Western nations industrialized.

The labor market institutions Acemoglu praised—"minimum wages, collective bargaining, and regulations introducing worker protection"—varied dramatically in their coverage and enforcement. The Nordic model he admires required specific preconditions: ethnic homogeneity, strong social trust, and export-oriented manufacturing. These conditions don't generalize.

When Acemoglu argues that "market forces" fail because they don't internalize externalities, he implicitly assumes that government planning would internalize them correctly. But governments have their own failures: regulatory capture, bounded rationality, and political constraints on technical judgment. The essay doesn't compare these failure modes; it simply assumes government correction is available.

The proposed measurement framework—using labor share to evaluate AI applications—would entrench existing power structures. Industries with high labor shares (like healthcare) already have strong political representation. Industries facing automation pressure (like logistics) have weaker voices. A labor-share-based policy would naturally favor the already-powerful.

The essay's treatment of surveillance focuses on authoritarian governments while understating how democratic nations conduct digital surveillance. The Snowden revelations documented mass collection of communications by democratic governments. The essay notes these revelations but understates their significance for domestic policy.

A critical perspective would start from different premises: that affected communities should control technological development, that we need robust institutions for contesting algorithmic decisions, and that we should be skeptical of proposals to expand state power over technology. This isn't a call for laissez-faire; it's a demand for democratic accountability grounded in existing rights rather than new government direction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s celebration of the postwar economic compact ignores the systemic exclusion of women, Black Americans, and the Global South from that era's prosperity.
  2. 02The Nordic labor model cited as a solution depends on specific social and ethnic preconditions that are not universally applicable to other nations.
  3. 03Acemoglu fails to account for inherent government failures such as regulatory capture and political constraints, assuming instead that the state is a neutral and rational corrector of externalities.
  4. 04Using labor share as a metric for AI policy would unfairly favor politically powerful industries over those with weaker representation.
  5. 05The essay understates the extent to which democratic governments, not just authoritarian ones, utilize surveillance technologies, undermining the claim that state oversight is a sufficient safeguard.
  6. 06True technological accountability should be rooted in community control and the contestation of algorithmic decisions rather than the expansion of centralized state power.
Diversified MiniMax

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the risks of AI, its proposed solution of state-directed technological development is a dangerous and impractical expansion of government power that risks stifling innovation and creating perverse economic incentives."

Acemoglu's proposed remedies read like a wish list for industrial policy enthusiasts, and I say this as someone sympathetic to the diagnosis. The essay correctly identifies market failures and concentration in the AI sector. But its proposed solutions—government direction, measurement frameworks, norm changes—collectively constitute a dangerous expansion of state power into technological development.

The track record of government "direction" in technology is checkered at best. DARPA created the internet, but also produced weapons systems that failed spectacularly. The Department of Energy subsidized solar panels for decades while the technology remained uneconomical. Private markets produced the smartphone revolution, the vaccine development that ended smallpox, and the green energy breakthroughs Acemoglu credits to government policy.

The essay's specific policy proposals compound the problem. Eliminating the capital-labor tax differential would reduce the tax preference for automation. But this ignores that automation often reduces emissions, improves workplace safety, and increases product quality. The appropriate policy isn't a blanket disincentive for automation but a carbon tax that internalizes externalities.

The proposed measurement framework—using labor share as the metric for beneficial AI—would create perverse incentives. Industries where automation genuinely raises productivity (manufacturing, logistics) would face pressure to adopt less efficient methods. Industries with naturally high labor shares would receive subsidies regardless of whether AI genuinely served human flourishing.

Consider the history of regulatory responses to new technologies. The FDA's premarket approval process protects patients but delays life-saving drugs. Banking regulation after 2008 constrained lending while failing to prevent the next crisis. Technology regulation risks similar unintended consequences: compliance costs that favor large incumbents, definitions that become obsolete as technology evolves, capture by the regulated industry.

Acemoglu's essay shares the progressive concern about AI with the techno-optimist dismissal. Both assume the state can effectively direct technological development. I'm less confident. The answer to bad AI isn't government-selected good AI; it's robust institutions that let affected parties contest harms and demand accountability.

This doesn't mean no role for policy. Antitrust enforcement against platform concentration deserves support. Data protection frameworks like GDPR establish useful constraints. Liability rules that make developers responsible for foreseeable harms create appropriate incentives. These interventions constrain bad outcomes without requiring the state to distinguish good technology from bad.

The essay's vision of democratic oversight raises additional concerns. Which democratic institutions? How do they acquire technical expertise? The EU's AI Act shows how democratic processes can produce incoherent regulation when political considerations distort technical judgments.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical track record of government-directed technology is inconsistent, and private markets have been more effective at driving major technological revolutions.
  2. 02Using labor share as a measurement framework for 'beneficial' AI would create perverse incentives that prioritize inefficient production methods over genuine productivity gains.
  3. 03Eliminating tax incentives for automation ignores the secondary benefits of machine labor, such as improved workplace safety and reduced environmental emissions.
  4. 04The state lacks the technical expertise to effectively distinguish 'good' AI from 'bad' AI, and such regulatory efforts are prone to industry capture and obsolescence.
  5. 05Instead of top-down government direction, society should rely on robust institutions like antitrust enforcement, data protection frameworks, and liability rules to hold developers accountable for harms.
