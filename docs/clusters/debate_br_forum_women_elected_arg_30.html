"The United States must shift from a negative to a positive conception of political rights by adopting structural reforms like gender quotas to achieve true legislative representation for women."

Jennifer Piscopo's essay arrives at exactly the right moment. The centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should prompt not just celebration but honest reckoning: the United States ranks alongside Afghanistan in women's legislative representation, a fact that should embarrass any citizen who believes their country leads the world in democratic values. Piscopo's central insight—that the United States has conceptualized political rights as negative rather than positive, focusing on what government cannot restrict rather than what it must actively guarantee—offers a framework for understanding why the nation has lagged so dramatically behind countries in Latin America and Europe.

The evidence from gender quota adoption worldwide is compelling. Of the fifty countries achieving thirty percent or more women in their lower or single legislative chamber by 2019, twenty-nine employed gender quota laws. This is not coincidence but causation. The voluntary, individualistic approach championed by organizations like EMILY's List and She Should Run has merit but cannot substitute for structural change. As Piscopo correctly notes, these organizations respond to "the individual ethos of U.S. elections, helping aspirant women pull themselves up by their electoral bootstraps." But when boots are chained to the ground, bootstrapping accomplishes nothing.

The philosophical arguments for gender parity are more powerful than opponents acknowledge. Critics invoke meritocracy as though politics operated as a pure merit-based competition, but no such competition exists. Incumbency advantages, name recognition, family connections, and party establishment backing all distort candidate emergence. The performance premium documented by Sarah Fulton—where women who win demonstrate higher quality across eleven metrics than male winners—reveals not meritocracy but selection bias. Women must be exceptional to run; men can be ordinary.

Piscopo's observation that the "right to be elected" language in Ibero-American constitutions ultimately served as "a trumpet for equality" rather than a door-slam should inspire American advocates. Constitutional language is not fixed in meaning. The Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of "equal protection" once excluded women and African Americans; it now protects both. Courts in Costa Rica, Spain, and Indonesia have upheld quota laws by recognizing that substantive equality sometimes requires unequal treatment of previously favored groups.

The argument that gender quotas face unique obstacles in the United States due to their association with racial affirmative action deserves attention, but Ruth Rubio-Marín's analysis points toward a solution rather than surrender. By focusing specifically on patriarchy rather than conflating gender and race, parity advocates can build coalitions with constituencies historically skeptical of positive action on racial grounds. The Democratic Party's own 50-50 gender balance requirements for convention delegates and committee positions demonstrate that American political institutions can adopt such measures voluntarily.

What practical steps might jumpstart this process? First, national legislation requiring parity for federal campaigns—perhaps by conditioning public financing on gender balance among major party candidates. Second, state-level reforms mandating that major parties provide gender-balanced slates for statewide offices. Third, judicial appointments specifically calibrated to ensure benches that understand substantive equality. Fourth, constitutional amendments in willing states establishing positive rights to political participation.

The resistance will be fierce. Opponents will invoke constitutional originalism, individual freedom, and the specter of government overreach. But the global evidence demonstrates these fears are overblown. Countries that adopted parity did not become totalitarian; they became more representative. Courts consistently upheld these measures when framed around substantive rather than formal equality. The women of Rwanda, who now occupy more than sixty percent of legislative seats, have not undermined their democracy but strengthened it.

Piscopo concludes that "the impossible only becomes possible if someone tries." This is correct, but we should note what happens when others try: they succeed. The question is not whether gender parity can work—evidence proves it can—but whether American institutions have the will to adopt it before the next centennial.
