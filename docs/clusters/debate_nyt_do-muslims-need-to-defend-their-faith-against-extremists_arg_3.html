"Muslims must respond to extremism by addressing the underlying political disasters and institutional collapses that allow radical ideologies to flourish, rather than relying solely on theological defense."

Focusing only on Islam as a set of beliefs risks misunderstanding the Islamic State as if it were a debating society with artillery. ISIS is also the product of state collapse, dictatorship, invasion, sectarian rule, prisons, money, weapons and war. Muslims should oppose its ideology, but no amount of theological clarification will by itself repair the political disasters that made ISIS possible.

Consider the terrain. The American invasion of Iraq shattered institutions and empowered sectarian politics. Nouri al-Maliki’s government alienated many Sunnis, creating openings for militants who presented themselves as protectors. In Syria, Bashar al-Assad’s savagery radicalized the conflict and helped turn peaceful protest into a regional war. Gulf money and sectarian preaching have fed Sunni militancy; Iran and its allies have fed sectarian militias on the other side. Young men who see no justice from states may become susceptible to movements promising revenge and order.

This does not excuse ISIS. Many people suffer without beheading journalists or massacring minorities. Ideas matter. But ideas become armies when institutions collapse and grievances are organized. If the response is reduced to asking Muslims to say Islam is peaceful, the world avoids harder questions: Who arms whom? Which allies export intolerance? Which prisons incubate radicals? Which dictators are tolerated because they promise stability? Which wars are launched without plans for the morning after?

Muslims should respond politically as well as religiously. Muslim-majority states must stop playing double games with sectarian militias and extremist preachers. They should protect equal citizenship, not treat minorities as bargaining chips. Sunni leaders in Iraq and Syria need real power and security so that ISIS cannot pose as their only defender. Regional powers should curb the funding networks and television pulpits that turn political conflicts into cosmic wars. Muslim civil society should support refugees, document atrocities and insist that opposing Western policy does not require romanticizing jihadists.

Non-Muslims have responsibilities too. Broad attacks on Islam may feel brave in a studio, but they do nothing to rebuild Mosul, protect Aleppo or govern Anbar province. Worse, they confirm the extremist narrative that Muslims are under civilizational assault.

The question is not whether Islam can pass an abstract test of peacefulness. The question is whether Muslims and non-Muslims together can build political orders in which violent absolutists no longer look like the only force with conviction. Theology is part of the answer. Justice is the rest.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rise of groups like ISIS is a product of state collapse, sectarian politics, and foreign invasions rather than just religious belief.
  2. 02Theological clarification alone is insufficient to repair the political grievances and power vacuums that turn ideas into militant movements.
  3. 03Muslim-majority states must end sectarian double games and prioritize equal citizenship to prevent extremists from posing as the sole protectors of marginalized groups.
  4. 04Regional powers and civil society must dismantle the funding networks and media platforms that transform political conflicts into religious wars.
  5. 05Broad condemnations of Islam by non-Muslims are counterproductive because they reinforce the extremist narrative of a civilizational assault against Muslims.
  6. 06Defeating extremism requires building just political orders where violent absolutists are no longer seen as the only alternative to instability.
