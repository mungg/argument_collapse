"The United States should expand and reform its official holiday calendar to better reflect the country's multicultural identity and to ensure workers have necessary time for civic and personal obligations."

The debate over official holidays is often framed as a problem of too many cultures and too few days. But that framing misses the most important point: Americans do not have too much time off. Many have too little. Compared with other wealthy democracies, the United States is unusually stingy about paid leave. Federal holidays matter not only because they symbolize recognition, but because they represent one of the few widely visible acknowledgments that workers are human beings with families, communities, memories and obligations beyond the job.

So yes, the United States should have more official holidays — and the discussion should not be limited to federal employees. A federal holiday is meaningful, but millions of retail, restaurant, hospital, transportation and service workers do not actually get the day off. Some receive premium pay; many do not. If the country is serious about holidays, it should connect them to labor standards, not just government office closures.

Which holidays should be added or changed? Start with Election Day. No democracy should make voting feel like a favor granted by an employer. Making Election Day a holiday would not by itself fix long lines, voter ID fights or unequal access, but it would establish the right norm. Participation in self-government deserves at least as much public respect as shopping on Presidents’ Day.

Second, we should rethink Columbus Day. For many Italian-Americans, it became a symbol of acceptance in a country that once treated them with contempt. That history should not be dismissed. But Columbus also represents conquest and catastrophe for Native peoples. A modern holiday should not force one community’s dignity to rest on another’s wound. Replace it with a broader holiday honoring the peoples of the Americas, immigration and encounter — a day honest enough to include both pride and mourning.

Third, the calendar should make room for a major holiday reflecting America’s multicultural reality. Lunar New Year is a strong candidate in many parts of the country, not because it is the only deserving holiday, but because it is observed by large and diverse Asian communities and already functions as a major public season worldwide. Others could make a case for Eid, Diwali or César Chávez Day. The point is not tokenism; it is that the national calendar should not look frozen in the ethnic and religious assumptions of the nineteenth century.

Opponents will say that too many holidays reduce productivity. But rest is not the enemy of productivity. Burned-out workers, strained families and citizens too busy to vote are not signs of a healthy economy. Holidays are civic infrastructure. They tell us when to pause, whom to honor and what kind of people we want to be.

A richer holiday calendar would not solve inequality or create social harmony. But it would be a small, concrete way to say that the country belongs to more of its people — and that work is not the only measure of national life.

  2. 02Federal holidays should be tied to broader labor standards so that service and retail workers actually receive time off or premium pay rather than just government employees.
  3. 03Election Day should be a federal holiday to establish voting as a fundamental right and a respected act of self-government.
  4. 04Columbus Day should be replaced with a holiday that honors both the history of immigration and the experiences of Native peoples without celebrating conquest.
  5. 05The national calendar should be updated to include major multicultural celebrations, such as Lunar New Year, to reflect modern ethnic and religious realities.
  6. 06Increasing the number of holidays acts as vital civic infrastructure that promotes long-term productivity by preventing worker burnout and strengthening community ties.

"The federal government should update its official calendar to include new holidays that accurately reflect the country's evolving history, democratic values, and religious diversity."

The Calendar Is a Mirror, and Ours Needs Updating

National holidays are not just days off. They are a society telling itself who it is. The Fourth of July says we are a republic born in revolution. Memorial Day says we honor those who died for the country. Martin Luther King Jr. Day, added only in 1983 over considerable resistance, says — eventually, haltingly — that the struggle for civil rights belongs at the center of the American story, not its margins.

So when we ask whether the federal calendar should change, we are really asking: has the country changed enough that its self-portrait is out of date? The answer, plainly, is yes.

Three additions deserve serious consideration.

First, Juneteenth. The end of slavery is arguably the most significant event in American history after independence itself. That we commemorate the Confederacy's defeat only obliquely — and celebrate Washington and Lincoln but not the day enslaved Texans learned they were free — is a strange omission. Juneteenth is already a state holiday in over 40 states. Federal recognition would acknowledge that emancipation belongs to all of us, not just to Black Americans.

Second, Election Day. A country that calls itself the world's leading democracy should not make voting harder than buying groceries. A federal Election Day holiday would say, with the force of law, that self-government is sacred work.

Third — and this is the more contested suggestion — the federal government should establish a mechanism for recognizing major religious holidays of large American communities, even if those days don't close federal offices. Eid al-Fitr, Diwali, the lunar new year, and Yom Kippur could be designated "observed federal holidays," carrying official acknowledgment and the right of federal workers to take the day with pay, without shutting down the government. The White House already holds Eid and Diwali receptions; codifying that recognition would matter symbolically to communities that have spent generations feeling invisible on the official calendar.

Critics will say this is identity-politics holiday inflation. I'd say it's the opposite: it is taking seriously the proposition that American identity is plural, and that the calendar should reflect the country we actually are rather than the country we were in 1870.

What I would not do is drop Christmas, despite its religious origins. Christmas in America has become something stranger and more capacious than a Christian feast — a midwinter civic ritual observed by Jews who go to the movies, by Muslims who exchange gifts, by atheists who decorate trees. Removing it would feel less like neutrality and more like vandalism. The better path is addition, not subtraction: keep the inherited holidays, examine which omissions look indefensible from the standpoint of 2013, and update the mirror.

A calendar tells a story. Ours is overdue for a new chapter.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Juneteenth should be a federal holiday to acknowledge that the end of slavery is a foundational event for all Americans.
  2. 02Establishing Election Day as a federal holiday would demonstrate that participating in self-government is a sacred civic duty.
  3. 03The government should create a formal mechanism to recognize major religious holidays like Eid, Diwali, and Yom Kippur to validate the pluralistic nature of American society.
  4. 04Existing traditional holidays like Christmas should be maintained because they have evolved into broad civic rituals that transcend their religious origins.
  5. 05Updating the federal calendar is an act of inclusion that ensures the nation's official self-portrait matches its current demographic and social reality.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The federal holiday calendar should be expanded and updated to reflect the nation’s actual demographic and cultural breadth, signaling that the state recognizes all its citizens rather than just those of Western European descent."

The winter months bring an almost comical density of celebrations: Christmas and New Year’s, Orthodox observances, the Prophet’s Birthday, the lunar new year, Lincoln’s and Washington’s birthdays, Mardi Gras, Valentine’s Day, and now Purim. Yet only a narrow slice from that rich feast appears on the federal calendar. The list of official holidays—Christmas but not Mawlid, New Year’s Day but not the lunar new year, Washington’s Birthday but not Lincoln’s—tells a clear story about which cultures and traditions have been deemed central to American identity and which have been politely ignored. It is a story that no longer matches the country we live in, and it is time to rewrite it.

The federal holiday calendar should be expanded to reflect the nation’s actual demographic and cultural breadth. This does not mean creating a day off for every tradition, an obviously unworkable proposition. It means thoughtfully adding a few key observances that signal that the state sees all its citizens, not just those who trace their roots to Western Europe. A lunar new year holiday would honor the millions of Asian Americans for whom it is the most important celebration of the year. Adding Eid al‑Fitr, at the end of Ramadan, would extend similar recognition to the country’s growing Muslim population. Diwali, the festival of lights celebrated by Hindus, Sikhs and Jains, is already a major event in many communities and deserves a place in the national calendar. And as a matter of justice, the government should replace Columbus Day with a day honoring Native peoples—Indigenous Peoples’ Day—acknowledging not conquest but resilience and the first nations whose land we inhabit.

Critics will object that holidays cost money in lost productivity, and that government has no business meddling in religious or cultural choices. But these objections miss the point. Official holidays are not fundamentally about granting days off; they are about what a nation chooses to remember and whom it chooses to honor. The federal government already picks winners when it sanctifies Christmas and Thanksgiving, both rooted in Christian narratives, while ignoring holidays of equal gravity for millions of its citizens. That asymmetry sends a powerful, exclusionary message. Recognizing additional holidays would not force anyone to observe them; it would simply say that the nation’s public life makes room for everyone. It would, over time, help weave a more genuinely pluralistic civic fabric, one in which a child of Korean immigrants can see the lunar new year displayed on the official calendar next to the Fourth of July. The stumbling block is not logistics or cost but a failure of imagination about what a truly inclusive American calendar might look like. We are long overdue for that leap.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current selection of federal holidays creates an exclusionary narrative by prioritizing Western European and Christian traditions over other significant cultural observances.
  2. 02Adding key holidays like the lunar new year, Eid al-Fitr, and Diwali would provide necessary official recognition to the country's diverse and growing populations.
  3. 03Replacing Columbus Day with Indigenous Peoples’ Day would shift the national focus from a history of conquest to one of justice and resilience for Native peoples.
  4. 04Official holidays serve as a symbolic tool for defining national identity and memory, making the inclusion of diverse traditions essential for building a pluralistic civic fabric.
  5. 05The economic costs and logistical challenges of adding holidays are secondary to the importance of creating a truly inclusive public life.
Diversified MiniMax

"The federal government should reform its holiday calendar to move away from a system that unfairly prioritizes specific religious and historical traditions over others."

The question of which holidays merit federal recognition is ultimately a question about what kind of nation we want to be. The current system reflects a particular historical settlement — one that prioritized Christianity and Western European traditions — and it is long past time we interrogated that settlement frankly.

The Constitution forbids religious tests for officeholding and prohibits laws respecting an establishment of religion, yet our federal holiday calendar implicitly elevates certain faith traditions over others. Christmas gets a statutory holiday; Mawlid does not. Orthodox Christmas — celebrated by millions of American Orthodox Christians — goes unrecognized. This isn't neutrality; it's preference baked into the calendar.

Some will argue that these traditions have simply been "American" longest, that they reflect the cultural mainstream, and that changing them would amount to political correctness run amok. But that's precisely backwards. The question isn't whether to impose new observances on an unwilling public; it's whether our government should continue treating some people's sacred days as worthy of national pause while others remain invisible. A government that recognizes some religious holidays but not others has made a theological judgment, even if it pretends otherwise.

The practical alternative isn't an endless expansion of holidays until every tradition gets its day off — that's the straw man in the prompt. The alternative is a principled approach. We could recognize a limited set of major secular holidays that cut across communities: Independence Day, Thanksgiving, Labor Day. We could recognize holidays from the three Abrahamic faiths that have significant American populations — Christian, Jewish, and Muslim. Or we could take a page from countries like India, where the government recognizes holidays from multiple traditions and lets states choose among them, reflecting federalism's promise that diversity need not mean uniformity.

What's genuinely strange isn't the idea of changing our holiday calendar — it's the idea that this particular arrangement, determined largely by 19th-century immigration patterns and political compromise, should be permanent by some logic other than its own merit.

  2. 02Treating certain sacred days as national holidays while ignoring others constitutes an unconstitutional theological judgment by the government.
  3. 03The existing holiday schedule is an outdated reflection of 19th-century demographics that does not represent the diversity of the modern American population.
  4. 04The government could adopt a more principled approach by focusing on secular holidays, recognizing major holidays from multiple faiths, or allowing for regional flexibility.
Diversified MiniMax

"The federal government should reform the official holiday calendar to move away from its exclusionary Protestant and industrial origins toward a system that reflects America's religious and cultural pluralism."

The current federal holiday calendar is a historical accident wearing the clothes of principle, and we should stop pretending otherwise. It tells a story about American identity that was always incomplete and has become increasingly implausible.

The original federal holiday calendar was essentially a gift to northeastern Protestant Christianity and the commercial calendar of the industrializing North: Christmas, New Year, Thanksgiving. Later additions reflected the political calculus of expansion — Washington's Birthday to honor the founding era, Memorial Day and Labor Day for the labor movement, MLK Day for civil rights. Each was a hard-won victory of social movements demanding recognition.

What's striking is how rarely the expansion has moved in the direction of religious pluralism. Orthodox Christians, Muslims, Jews, Hindus, Buddhists — all have significant American populations whose holy days go unacknowledged at the federal level. When the Lunar New Year rolls around, the federal government is open for business while millions of Asian Americans mark the most important holiday in their calendar. This isn't a neutral arrangement; it's an arrangement that treats some citizens as more default American than others.

The solution isn't to add every community's holidays — that's logistically impossible and would trivialize each observance. But we could establish a rotating system of optional holidays, or allow federal employees to swap one standard holiday for one from their own tradition, as some European countries do. We could formally recognize Lunar New Year alongside the fixed holidays.

The deeper point is that a democracy should be honest about whose identities it's legitimizing and whose it's marginalizing. Pretending the current calendar is religiously neutral or culturally representative doesn't make it so — it just means we're comfortable with invisible hierarchies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current federal holiday calendar is a historical accident that prioritizes northeastern Protestant Christianity and specific political movements over a diverse population.
  2. 02The lack of federal recognition for non-Protestant holy days, such as those in Islam, Judaism, or Buddhism, creates a hierarchy that treats some citizens as more 'default' Americans than others.
  3. 03Maintaining the current calendar under the guise of neutrality marginalizes minority identities and reinforces invisible social hierarchies.
  4. 04The government could address these inequities by implementing flexible solutions like a rotating holiday system or allowing employees to swap standard holidays for their own cultural observances.
Diversified Gemini

"The federal government should expand and reform its official holiday calendar to reflect the multicultural reality of modern America and ensure civic inclusion for all citizens."

As the United States continues to undergo profound demographic shifts, our federal holiday calendar remains stubbornly frozen in the past. The current list of recognized holidays reflects the America of the 19th and mid-20th centuries—predominantly white and Christian. If our public institutions are to genuinely reflect and respect the multicultural reality of 2013, we must expand and reform the holidays our government officially recognizes.

The current system creates a hierarchy of citizenship. When a Christian child has off from school for Christmas, their family traditions are validated by the state. When a Muslim child has to choose between taking a test and observing Eid, or a Chinese-American family must treat the Lunar New Year as just another Tuesday, they are sent a clear message that their culture is peripheral.

While it is practically impossible for the federal government to shut down for every religious and cultural festival, we can adopt a more inclusive tiered system. First, we should re-evaluate our current fixed holidays. It is long past time to replace Columbus Day—a holiday celebrating a figure associated with the devastation of indigenous populations—with something universally empowering, such as Election Day, making it easier for all citizens to participate in our democracy.

Second, the government should create a new category of "Officially Recognized Observances." While these would not trigger a mandatory shutdown of federal offices, they would come with powerful statutory protections. On days like Lunar New Year, Diwali, Eid al-Fitr, and Yom Kippur, federal and state institutions—including public schools—would be required to grant excused absences without penalty. Furthermore, no major public school examinations or significant government deadlines could be scheduled on these dates.

Several diverse school districts across the country have already begun adjusting their calendars to close on major minority holidays, proving that it is logistically possible to honor a wider array of traditions. The federal government should lead by example.

Updating our holiday calendar is not about political correctness; it is about civic inclusion. A calendar is a statement of values. By expanding recognition to the traditions of the communities that are actively shaping America's future, we declare that the American story is broad enough to encompass all of us.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current holiday system creates a hierarchy of citizenship by validating Christian traditions while treating minority cultures as peripheral.
  2. 02Columbus Day should be replaced with a more universally empowering holiday, such as Election Day, to better reflect modern values and facilitate democratic participation.
  3. 03The government should establish a category of 'Officially Recognized Observances' that provides statutory protections and excused absences for major religious and cultural festivals like Diwali and Eid.
  4. 04The success of diverse school districts in adjusting their calendars proves that it is logistically feasible to honor a wider array of traditions.
  5. 05A modernized holiday calendar serves as a vital statement of values that acknowledges the diverse communities shaping the nation's future.
