"The Democratic Party's recent economic shift was driven primarily by a bipartisan national security consensus regarding competition with China rather than purely domestic coalitional dynamics."

The Missing Country in the Story Is China

Hacker and Pierson have produced a careful and largely persuasive account of the Democratic Party's recent leftward turn on economics, anchored almost entirely in domestic coalitional dynamics: the rise of the progressive wing, the influence of think tanks like the Roosevelt Institute, the activism that followed George Floyd, the shock of COVID. These are real factors. But there is a startling omission at the heart of their analysis, one that becomes more conspicuous the longer one reads. The word "China" does not appear. The word "Russia" does not appear. The phrase "supply chain" does not appear. The international context within which the Biden administration's industrial policy was actually conceived and sold is simply absent.

This matters because the most distinctive features of Bidenomics — the CHIPS Act, the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic-content requirements, the export controls on advanced semiconductors, the willingness to subsidize manufacturing on a scale not seen in generations — were not primarily products of the Sanders-Warren wing of the party. They were products of a bipartisan elite consensus that the United States had become dangerously dependent on Chinese manufacturing and that great-power competition required reshoring strategic industries. Jake Sullivan's "foreign policy for the middle class" was the conceptual hinge: a recognition that the geopolitical and the distributional could be welded together, with industrial subsidies serving both ends.

Read the actual political speeches surrounding the passage of CHIPS and the IRA. Read the floor statements of the Republicans who voted for CHIPS. The framing is overwhelmingly about Beijing, not about inequality. Senators who would not vote a dime for child care found tens of billions for semiconductor fabs because Taiwan looks fragile and TSMC sits at the choke point of the global economy. This is industrial policy, yes, and it has redistributive consequences, yes. But its political logic does not run through the U-shaped coalition Hacker and Pierson describe. It runs through Lockheed Martin, Intel, Micron, and the Pentagon.

Once you see this, several puzzles in the Hacker-Pierson account begin to resolve. Why did Democrats manage to pass the most ambitious industrial legislation in decades while failing to pass paid family leave and the Child Tax Credit extension? Not because Manchin was capricious. Because industrial policy aligned with a bipartisan national-security consensus and a powerful set of corporate interests, while social policy did not. Why did the party's affluent suburban wing tolerate massive subsidies to manufacturing? Because those subsidies enrich firms in which the professional-managerial class holds stock and provide a respectable patriotic frame for spending that would otherwise look like industrial favoritism. Why did the bipartisan elements of the Biden agenda survive while the partisan ones did not? Because the bipartisan elements were the ones with a China rationale.

This is not a small point. It changes the political economy of what we are seeing. Hacker and Pierson present a story in which a left-leaning party, pressured from below by progressives and activists, embraced a redistributive agenda and ran into the wall of the Senate. The actual story is more layered: a national-security-driven shift in elite consensus opened space for a partial redistributive agenda that progressives then tried to ride. When the geopolitical justification ran out — when there was no Beijing-flavored reason to make the CTC permanent — the agenda collapsed. The progressive wing did not have the autonomous power to carry the rest. It had power only insofar as its preferences overlapped with the China hawks' priorities.

There is a parallel here to a much older story. The American welfare state was largely built under the impetus of two world wars and a cold war. The GI Bill, the interstate highway system, the National Defense Education Act, even substantial parts of the postwar housing finance system, were sold as national-security imperatives. Sweden and Britain did not need this framing; the United States, with its more individualist political culture and its powerful business class, did. The Biden agenda continues this pattern. Where it could be framed as competition with China — chips, batteries, electric vehicles, critical minerals, rare earths — it advanced. Where it could not — care work, child poverty, paid leave — it stalled.

This frame also illuminates the international dimension of the leftward turn that Hacker and Pierson treat as exceptional. They note that other center-left parties have lost working-class voters and gained the highly educated. They might also have noted that those same parties — the German SPD, the French Socialists insofar as they still exist, even British Labour under Starmer — are also moving toward industrial policy, energy security, and reshoring. The synchronization is not coincidence. It is the response of advanced-economy political systems to the breakdown of the post-Cold War globalization consensus and the recognition that strategic dependence on geopolitical rivals is a vulnerability. Bidenomics is the American instantiation of a wider pattern.

The omission of China also leads Hacker and Pierson to misjudge the durability of the shift. They suggest, hopefully, that with a slightly stronger congressional hand, the 2021 agenda could be revived. But the durable parts of that agenda were durable because they had a national-security backstop. The vulnerable parts were vulnerable because they did not. Unless the Democratic coalition can find a way to give care policy, child poverty, and housing the same geopolitical valence as semiconductors — which is genuinely difficult, though demographic decline and labor force participation arguments offer some purchase — the next window will see the same pattern: industrial spending advances, social spending stalls.

There is a critical political lesson here for the left. If you want to expand the welfare state in the United States, you almost certainly have to find a way to frame your preferred policies in terms that the national-security state can endorse. This is uncomfortable. It involves alliances with people whose other commitments — to defense spending, to confrontation with China, to military deployments — many progressives find odious. But the historical record is clear: in the American context, the welfare state grows when it can plausibly claim to be making the country stronger against rivals, and stalls when it cannot. The Hacker-Pierson account treats the international as a kind of stage backdrop. It is in fact the engine of much of what they describe.

I do not mean to dismiss the domestic story they tell. The Sanders campaigns mattered. The Roosevelt Institute mattered. Sabeel Rahman and Lina Khan mattered. The energy of post-Floyd organizing mattered. These were necessary conditions. But they were not sufficient, and treating them as the whole story produces an account that cannot explain why some parts of the 2021 agenda survived and others did not, why the synchronization with other rich democracies is so striking, or why the next administration — Democratic or Republican — will continue to do industrial policy regardless of which coalition wins. The new blue divide cannot be understood without the new Cold War.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The most successful elements of the Biden agenda, such as the CHIPS Act and industrial subsidies, passed because they were framed as geopolitical imperatives to reduce dependence on China.
  2. 02The failure of social policies like the Child Tax Credit and paid family leave demonstrates that the progressive wing lacks the autonomous power to pass legislation without a national security justification.
  3. 03The shift toward industrial policy and reshoring is a global phenomenon among center-left parties responding to the breakdown of the post-Cold War globalization consensus.
  4. 04Historically, the American welfare state has only expanded when social investments could be plausibly linked to making the nation stronger against international rivals.
  5. 05Ignoring the international context leads to a misjudgment of the durability of the Democratic agenda, as future progress depends on aligning social policy with national security goals.
