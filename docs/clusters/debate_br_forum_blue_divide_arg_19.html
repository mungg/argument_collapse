"The Democratic Party must shift its primary focus from developing ambitious economic policy agendas to pursuing fundamental institutional and constitutional reforms that address the U.S. system's anti-majoritarian biases."

Policy Is Not the Right Object of Analysis Anymore

Hacker and Pierson have written a careful and learned essay about the policy shift within the Democratic Party. I want to suggest, in a friendly but fundamental disagreement, that they are studying the wrong object. The most important political development of the past decade is not what Democrats believe about economics. It is what is happening to the American constitutional order — and Democrats, including the Democrats who wrote this essay, have not yet found a way to make institutional reform the center of their political project. Until they do, the policy debates Hacker and Pierson care about will continue to play out under conditions that systematically defeat democratic majorities.

The authors are honest about this in places. They acknowledge the density tax. They acknowledge that the Senate has not represented a Republican majority of voters since 1996 yet has been controlled by Republicans for half the period since. They acknowledge that Democrats have won seven of eight presidential popular votes while losing six of nine Supreme Court seats. They acknowledge gerrymandering, the filibuster, the Electoral College. All of this is in the essay. And yet the essay is structured around the policy story, with the institutional story functioning as a constraint on policy rather than as the primary terrain of struggle.

This is a misallocation of analytic attention. Consider what the institutional asymmetries mean for the policy story. Even if every contestable claim Hacker and Pierson make about the leftward shift of Democratic economics is correct, that shift operates within a system in which fifty-five percent of the voters routinely produce something less than fifty percent of the legislative power. A center-left agenda that commands majority support is structurally unable to enact itself. The 2021 near miss the authors describe was not an aberration. It is the modal outcome of an antimajoritarian system facing a center-left majority.

What follows? In my view, the most important strategic question for the Democratic Party is no longer "what should our policy agenda be" but "how can we change the rules of the game so that policy agendas, ours or anyone else's, can actually be enacted when they have majority support." This is a procedural question, and procedural questions are politically difficult because they are abstract and remote from voters' daily concerns. But the alternative — continuing to write detailed policy programs that the institutional system is designed to block — has reached the limits of what it can accomplish.

The procedural agenda is reasonably well understood. It includes filibuster reform; admission of D.C. as a state and serious consideration of Puerto Rican statehood; expansion or restructuring of the federal judiciary; a national popular vote interstate compact or constitutional amendment for the presidency; voting rights legislation along the lines of the Freedom to Vote Act; reforms to the redistricting process; reforms to campaign finance. None of these are policy in the conventional sense. All of them would change the structural environment within which policy is made. And none of them are politically easy, because the institutional Republican Party correctly understands that its survival as a competitive force depends on the maintenance of these antimajoritarian rules.

Hacker and Pierson are not unaware of this agenda. But they treat it as background. The essay's emphasis is on what Democrats want to do, not on what would have to change for them to be able to do it. This is the same imbalance one finds in much liberal policy commentary, and it is, I think, partly responsible for a kind of strategic exhaustion. We are encouraged to celebrate near-miss policy victories rather than to focus on the institutional architecture that produced the near miss.

There is also a deeper issue, which is that the antimajoritarian features of the system are interacting with the radicalization of the Republican Party to produce something genuinely dangerous. The January 6 reference in the essay's opening paragraph is real and grave. The authors note that "the ability of the United States to resist the threat of democratic backsliding seems to rest almost entirely on the electoral success of the Democratic Party." This is true. It is also a precarious foundation for democratic stability. If American democracy depends on a single party winning electoral majorities under rules that systematically disadvantage that party, the long-run probability of democratic failure is high. We are essentially asking the Democrats to hit a moving target that gets harder to hit each cycle. They will not succeed forever.

The institutional reform agenda is the only path out of this trap. A democracy that can routinely translate majorities into governing power is more stable than one that cannot. A judiciary not dominated by minority-appointed justices is more legitimate. A Senate that does not give Wyoming the same weight as California is more representative. These changes are not partisan in the simple sense, even though one party would lose power under them. They are structural preconditions for the functioning of constitutional democracy in a country that has become too unequal and too polarized for its eighteenth-century rules.

I would also press Hacker and Pierson on a related point. Their account of the Democratic Party's policy evolution emphasizes endogenous factors: the rise of the progressive wing, the influence of think tanks, the COVID shock. But these factors operate within an institutional environment that selects for certain kinds of politicians and certain kinds of policy entrepreneurship. A party that knows it will face filibusters and gerrymandered House maps and an unfriendly Court will produce a different set of leaders than a party that knows it can enact what it wins. The institutional environment shapes the policy environment, and not just at the margins.

There is one consolation in the Hacker-Pierson essay that I want to credit. They are right that the Democratic Party has been more ambitious in the Biden era than its critics on the left have acknowledged. They are right that the Brahmin-left framing misses something important about the current coalition. They are right that the work of preparing for the next opportunity has to happen now. I would simply add: the most important work is not policy design. It is institutional reform. The next Democratic majority, if it gets one, should spend its political capital on the filibuster, on D.C. statehood, on voting rights, on the courts, before it spends any of it on policy. Without those changes, the policy near miss will repeat itself indefinitely, and each near miss will be celebrated by essays like this one as a moral victory while the system continues to grind toward minority rule.

Hacker and Pierson have written the policy essay that the moment calls for. The essay the moment more urgently requires is the institutional one. I hope they write it next.
