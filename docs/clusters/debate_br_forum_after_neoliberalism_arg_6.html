Diversified DeepSeek

"While the 'Economics for Inclusive Prosperity' manifesto correctly identifies the need to decouple economics from market fundamentalism, its technocratic approach fails to address the deeper structural power of capital that underpins and often subverts market regulations."

The lead essay “Economics After Neoliberalism” is a welcome and long-overdue manifesto. It correctly diagnoses the toxic conflation of economics with market fundamentalism and rightly insists that modern economics, properly understood, is far from a cheerleader for laissez-faire. As a heterodox economist who has spent decades fighting against the tyranny of DSGE models and the smugness of the Chicago School, I find the authors’ call for inclusive prosperity both refreshing and strategically important. By marshaling an array of mainstream, empirically grounded policy proposals—from wage boards to rethinking trade agreements—they demonstrate that the center of gravity within the discipline is shifting. This is no small feat, and the EfIP network is precisely the kind of institutional counterweight we need to combat the well-funded machinery of conservative think tanks.

Yet, precisely because the essay is so important, I want to push it on a deeper structural issue it only grazes: power. The authors acknowledge asymmetries of power, noting that under perfect competition there is no scope for it, but that in reality it pervades markets. Still, their treatment of power remains oddly bloodless. Power appears as a series of discrete market failures—monopsony in labor, information asymmetries in finance, bargaining imbalances in trade—that can be corrected by clever policy design. This is the technocrat’s view of power: a glitch to be debugged, not a constitutive feature of capitalism.

The reality is starker. Power is not just something that distorts otherwise efficient markets; markets themselves are arenas for the creation and exercise of power. The very act of treating labor as a commodity, as Karl Polanyi argued, requires a violent social dislocation that is sustained by state power and legal coercion. The authors cite Polanyi approvingly but then retreat to a world where the state can benignly “embed” markets through smart regulation. This underestimates the degree to which capital employs its disproportionate influence to shape those regulations in the first place. The campaign-finance reforms Ethan Kaplan proposes are sensible, but they operate at the level of electoral politics while ignoring the deeper structural power of capital to exit, to disinvest, to threaten the stability of entire economies if its profit expectations are not met. No wage board can overcome that systemic blackmail.

Moreover, the essay’s faith in the ability of economics to transcend its own bad habits feels overly optimistic. The authors blame “bad habits” and “over-enamored” modelers, as if the problem were merely one of intellectual sloppiness or insularity. But what if the mathematical formalism and methodological individualism they acknowledge are not incidental but essential to the discipline’s social function? Economics, as a profession, provides the ideological scaffolding for a capitalist order by rendering distributional questions invisible or subsuming them under efficiency. The “free lunch” interventions they champion are attractive because they promise to improve justice without threatening prosperity, but this framing concedes that prosperity itself is an unalloyed good defined in terms of GDP growth. Even a more inclusive GDP growth, as measured by median incomes or broader well-being, still presumes that endless capital accumulation on a finite planet is possible and desirable. The climate proposals, like a carbon price, are the most market-friendly, least disruptive way to address the crisis—and they are manifestly insufficient.

I do not mean to dismiss the EfIP project. In a political landscape dominated by nativist demagogues and a complacent centrism, these proposals could improve millions of lives. But we must be clear-eyed about their limits. They are reforms that stabilize capitalism, not transform it. And as long as the fundamental power relations of our economy remain untouched, every victory will be precarious, always at risk of being clawed back by the next financial crisis or the next wave of capital-friendly technological change. The authors want to take economics back from the market fundamentalists; I want to take society back from the market itself. Those two projects are allied but not identical, and we should not mistake a truce for a shared vision.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors treat power as a series of discrete market failures to be corrected by policy rather than as a constitutive and inescapable feature of the capitalist system.
  2. 02The essay underestimates how capital uses its structural influence—such as the threat of disinvestment—to shape or bypass the very regulations intended to embed markets.
  3. 03The discipline's reliance on mathematical formalism and methodological individualism serves as an ideological scaffolding that prioritizes capital accumulation over fundamental social transformation.
  4. 04The proposed reforms are ultimately aimed at stabilizing capitalism rather than addressing the inherent conflict between endless economic growth and a finite planet.
  5. 05The authors' focus on reclaiming economics from fundamentalists is a limited project that stops short of the necessary goal of reclaiming society from the market itself.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network successfully demonstrates that economic tools can be used for social justice, its reliance on market-based fixes fails to address the fundamental power imbalances and commodification inherent in capitalism."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman open their lead essay with a catalogue of social ills so comprehensive and dire that it reads almost like a biblical lamentation. And then, remarkably, they assert that these are “solvable problems.” This statement is either profoundly naïve or a deliberate rhetorical gambit to shake us out of fatalism. I suspect it is the latter, and it largely works. The essay succeeds in its primary mission: it shows that the intellectual resources of economics, far from being exhausted by market worship, can be mobilized in the service of a more just society. The EfIP network is a welcome antidote to the relentless drumbeat of austerity and deregulation. But as the authors push their vision of “inclusive prosperity,” they inadvertently reveal the limits of the very intellectual tradition they champion.

The core tension lies in the essay’s treatment of “inclusion.” The authors frame their project as expanding prosperity’s definition to include the whole distribution and non-pecuniary well-being. Yet their policy proposals remain overwhelmingly focused on economic outcomes—wages, employment, taxation, financial stability—with only perfunctory nods to health, climate, or political rights. Inclusion is operationalized as a fairer distribution of market-generated goods, not as a reordering of social relations. Even the most ambitious proposals, such as wage boards or financial regulation, leave intact the fundamental structure of a capitalist economy in which the vast majority of people depend on wage labor for survival and have little say over the conditions of their work. Is that truly “inclusion,” or is it merely a less brutal form of subordination?

The essay cites Polanyi approvingly, but Polanyi’s argument was more radical than the authors acknowledge. He insisted that treating land, labor, and money as commodities is a dangerous fiction that, if left unchecked, will destroy society. The solution, he believed, was not just to embed markets in smarter regulations but to de-commodify these fundamental spheres of life. The authors’ proposals do not de-commodify anything; they merely seek to improve the terms on which people are forced to sell their labor and to insure them against the worst risks of that commodification. Universal social insurance, for instance, is a good policy, but it still presumes a world where people’s access to health care and retirement security is mediated by markets and states, not by communities or self-provisioning.

This matters because the essay’s political strategy depends on the idea that economics can deliver “free lunches”—policies that make everyone better off without painful trade-offs. But if the goal is genuine inclusion—a society where no one’s life chances are determined by accidents of birth, where work is not merely a means to consumption but an expression of human creativity, where political equality is not undermined by economic might—then there are no free lunches. Achieving that vision will require confronting entrenched interests and uprooting deeply held cultural norms about merit, property, and individualism. It will be a fight, not a tweak. By framing inclusive prosperity as a set of clever fixes to market failures, the authors may make their proposals more palatable to a centrist audience, but they also risk draining the movement for economic justice of its moral urgency.

The economists in EfIP have done a great service by assembling evidence and policy ideas. But evidence and policy ideas are not enough. What is needed is a political movement that can articulate a vision of the good society and build the power to realize it. In that larger project, economics is a helpful servant but a dangerous master. The authors would do well to remember that the true agents of inclusive prosperity are not policy designers in university offices but ordinary people organizing in their workplaces and communities.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' definition of inclusion is too narrow, focusing on the distribution of market goods rather than reordering social relations or the conditions of work.
  2. 02The proposals fail to embrace Karl Polanyi’s radical call for the de-commodification of labor, land, and money, opting instead for better-regulated commodification.
  3. 03The 'free lunch' framing of inclusive prosperity avoids the reality that genuine social transformation requires confronting entrenched interests and making painful trade-offs.
  4. 04Technical policy design by economists is insufficient for achieving justice without a broad political movement led by ordinary people rather than academics.
