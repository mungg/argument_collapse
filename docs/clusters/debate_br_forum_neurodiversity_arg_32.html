"While supporting the shift from liberal reform to radical neurodivergent power, a truly inclusive movement must integrate 'mad' perspectives by prioritizing practices of non-abandonment and community accompaniment over rigid identity consolidation."

Robert Chapman’s essay asks neurodiversity to become more than recognition, more than accommodation, and more than a corporate celebration of unusual talent. As someone sympathetic to mad politics and psychiatric survivor traditions, I welcome much of this challenge. The language of neurodiversity has often been safer and more respectable than the language of madness. It has traveled easily into schools and workplaces because it can sound tidy: brains are different, difference can be valuable, inclusion benefits everyone. But many forms of psychic difference are not tidy. Voices, paranoia, mania, dissociation, addiction, self-harm, terror, rage, catatonia, and despair do not fit comfortably into the cheerful imagery of diversity. If neurodiversity cannot speak with and for people marked by madness, it will remain partial.

Chapman knows this. Their example of the worker experiencing paranoia who wants webcams covered is important because it tests the boundaries of “reasonable accommodation.” Liberal inclusion welcomes the disabled person whose needs can be made administratively sensible. It struggles when a need appears irrational, frightening, or disruptive. A person with psychosis may not be denied dignity in theory, but in practice they are often treated as unreliable, dangerous, or incomprehensible. The same workplace that celebrates neurodivergent innovation may call security on someone speaking to voices.

The psychiatric survivor movement has long argued that diagnosis is not a neutral description of brain difference. It can be a tool of care, but also a tool of social control. Women, queer people, racialized people, poor people, political dissidents, and traumatized people have all been pathologized for responses to unbearable conditions. The history of psychiatry includes asylum abuse, forced sterilization, lobotomy, overmedication, conversion efforts, restraint, seclusion, and the medicalization of resistance. Today’s systems are less overtly brutal in some places, but coercion remains: involuntary hospitalization, forced drugging, community treatment orders, police transport, guardianship, risk assessment regimes, and benefit systems that demand diagnostic proof while punishing instability.

Chapman’s critique of neuronormativity can help connect neurodiversity and mad liberation. “Normal” affect and behavior are politically regulated. Grief must return to productivity on schedule. Anger at injustice must be calm to be credible. Suspicion of authority is pathological when held by the powerless and strategic when held by the powerful. Refusal to work can be depression, laziness, or deviance depending on who names it. The mad person often reveals the violence hidden in normal life: the impossibility of the job, the cruelty of the family, the terror of policing, the loneliness of cities, the spiritual emptiness of consumption, the unprocessed trauma of war and poverty.

Yet neurodiversity and mad politics do not fit together automatically. Neurodiversity often reclaims diagnosis as identity: autistic, ADHD, dyslexic, dyspraxic. Mad politics has been more ambivalent, sometimes rejecting diagnosis altogether as a language imposed by institutions. Some people find “bipolar” or “schizophrenic” meaningful; others prefer voice-hearer, mad, psychiatric survivor, traumatized, spiritually sensitive, or no label at all. Some want medication; some experience medication as life-saving; others experience it as chemical restraint. Some seek therapy; others have been harmed by therapists. Some want crisis care; others fear hospitals more than crisis. A future neurodiversity movement must make room for these contradictions without forcing them into a single identity.

This is where I both agree and disagree with Chapman’s proposal for Neurodivergent Power. I agree that rights-based reform is insufficient. The right to refuse treatment means little when refusal leads to homelessness. The right to accommodation means little when unemployment is built into psychiatric stigma. The right to community living means little when the community has no housing, no peer support, no respite, and no tolerance for distress. Liberal systems often offer a cruel choice: comply with treatment plans designed by others, or be abandoned until police arrive. Power must mean changing that structure.

But I worry about the umbrella term “neurodivergent” becoming too capacious and too confident. If everyone outside cognitive norms is gathered under one political identity, who decides the agenda? Articulate autistic academics? ADHD influencers? Parent advocates? Peer support workers? People with intellectual disabilities? Survivors of forced psychiatry? Clinicians who identify as neurodivergent? Each has knowledge; each can also dominate others. The history of disability politics shows that broad coalitions often reproduce hierarchies of speech, respectability, race, class, and capacity. Mad people who are currently homeless, incarcerated, sedated, or in crisis are not likely to be well represented at conferences on Neurodivergent Power.

A mad-inflected neurodiversity politics should therefore begin not with identity consolidation but with practices of non-abandonment. The central question is: what would make it possible for people in extreme states to remain members of the community without being caged, coerced, mocked, or left to die? The answer is not simply “abolish psychiatry,” though abolitionist critique is necessary. It is to build alternatives so robust that coercion becomes less thinkable: peer respite houses, Soteria-like homes, hearing voices groups, crisis cafes, mobile teams independent of police, advance directives, supported decision-making, trauma-informed care, open dialogue approaches, housing first, income support, safe drug use services, and communities trained to sit with distress without immediately calling armed responders.

These alternatives should not require a person to renounce medicine. One weakness of some antipsychiatric rhetoric has been its tendency to treat medication use as false consciousness. That is unfair. Many people take antipsychotics, mood stabilizers, stimulants, antidepressants, or anti-anxiety medication because the benefits are real. Others endure terrible side effects, withdrawal, emotional blunting, sexual dysfunction, metabolic damage, or cognitive fog. The political demand should be choice under noncoercive conditions: honest information, access to tapering support, medication when wanted, alternatives when not, and no punishment for disagreement. Consent is meaningful only when people have somewhere safe to live and someone to call besides police.

Chapman’s essay is right to link prisons and psychiatric institutions. The line between them is porous. Jails are filled with people diagnosed with mental illnesses, brain injuries, intellectual disabilities, and substance use disorders. Psychiatric hospitals often mimic prisons through locked doors, surveillance, forced compliance, and loss of ordinary rights. Police are the default mental health responders, which means crisis can become a death sentence, especially for Black, Indigenous, and other racialized people. Neurodiversity politics must be abolitionist at least in this sense: it must aim to remove cages and armed force from the center of our response to distress.

But abolition has to answer the frightened neighbor, the exhausted mother, the person being threatened by a manic spouse, and the voice-hearer terrified of commands to self-harm. If we do not answer them, the state will—with police, locked wards, and courts. Community safety cannot be dismissed as carceral ideology. Sometimes people in crisis hurt themselves or others. More often they are hurt by others, but the hard cases are real. A credible movement must develop accountable crisis systems that can intervene without domination. That means trained teams, places of refuge, de-escalation, voluntary medication, temporary separation when necessary, and due process when liberty is at stake. The goal is not purity; it is minimizing coercion while refusing abandonment.

Chapman’s criticism of “superpower” narratives is especially relevant to madness. Bipolar disorder is romanticized as creativity; schizophrenia as visionary insight; depression as depth; trauma as resilience. These stories may contain truth for some, but they become cruel when imposed. Madness can produce art, perception, solidarity, and rebellion. It can also destroy sleep, trust, memory, and hope. We do not need to prove that voices are gifts or mania is genius to defend mad people’s lives. The right to exist must include the person whose madness yields no marketable brilliance and no poetic wisdom.

The movement should also be careful with martyrdom. Chapman discusses Aaron Bushnell and the pathologizing of his self-immolation. I share the concern that psychiatric labels are often used to depoliticize protest. People have long called radical acts “crazy” in order to avoid confronting the conditions that produced them. Bushnell’s protest cannot be reduced to illness. But neither should neurodiversity politics romanticize self-destruction as the highest form of sanity in an insane world. Many mad and neurodivergent people struggle with suicidality. We can honor political anguish and still insist that movements need their people alive. A liberatory politics should make room for unbearable grief without turning death into proof of clarity.

What, then, is the future of neurodiversity from a mad perspective? It is a coalition, not a merger. Autistic self-advocacy, intellectual disability rights, ADHD organizing, dyslexia advocacy, psychiatric survivor movements, addiction harm reduction, dementia justice, and brain injury activism have overlapping enemies but not identical needs. The coalition should fight forced normalization, poverty, institutional violence, police response, eugenics, and productivity-based human worth. It should also defend access to care, including medical care, without allowing medicine to monopolize meaning.

Chapman is right that the movement must build power. But the deepest form of power for many mad and neurodivergent people is not the power to be celebrated, or even the power to disrupt, but the power to stay connected when our minds make us difficult to love, employ, house, or understand. Liberal neurodiversity has taught society to say “difference.” Neurodivergent and mad liberation must teach society to practice accompaniment: staying with people through terror, confusion, slowness, dependency, contradiction, and crisis.

If that is what Neurodivergent Power comes to mean, it will be worth building. Not a new respectability politics of brilliant atypical minds, and not a single ideology into which all distress must fit, but a material promise: no cages, no forced normality, no abandonment, no worth measured by productivity, and no one left alone with the unbearable.
