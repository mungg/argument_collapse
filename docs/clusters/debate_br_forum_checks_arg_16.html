"While the lead essay correctly identifies how checks and balances can protect wealthy elites, it fails to recognize that these same mechanisms are essential for restraining the unchecked power of modern administrative, corporate, and technocratic institutions."

Lisa Miller’s essay usefully punctures a lazy patriotism. Americans do often recite “checks and balances” as if the words themselves could save a republic. They cannot. But her account replaces one partial story with another. She argues that American checks and balances mainly empower wealthy minorities and obstruct popular reform. Sometimes they do. Yet the essay gives too little weight to another form of elite power: the administrative, cultural, legal, academic, philanthropic, and corporate-managerial power that often operates in the name of public good while remaining distant from democratic control. For those skeptical of both Trumpian strongman politics and progressive state-building, the problem is not simply that government is too checked. It is that the wrong institutions are checked and the wrong institutions are unchecked.

The lead essay treats democratic government as the main instrument by which ordinary Americans can discipline concentrated private power. That is a necessary function of government. A republic cannot tolerate monopolies, corruption, predatory finance, unsafe workplaces, or a health care sector that bankrupts the sick. But it is not enough to say “private power” and “public power” as if the former is always elite and the latter is always popular. Modern government itself is a vast concentration of power. Agencies write rules with enormous economic and social consequences. Prosecutors decide whom to charge. Police enforce order. Schools shape children. Public health authorities can restrict activity. Intelligence and law enforcement bodies can surveil. Universities and nonprofits influence policy through credentialed networks. Large corporations often do not oppose this state; they partner with it, lobby it, staff it, and learn to profit from it.

Trump’s second administration makes this problem vivid in one way: executive power can be abused by a president who personalizes government and treats law as an obstacle. But recent years have also made vivid another concern: many Americans believe major institutions ignored them, managed them, or treated them as backward. They saw trade policy promoted by bipartisan elites while towns hollowed out. They saw schools and universities speak in moralizing language while costs rose and trust fell. They saw pandemic rules that sometimes appeared arbitrary, class-inflected, or dismissive of small businesses and parents. They saw speech norms enforced by platforms and employers in ways that felt opaque. They saw immigration policy debated in elite humanitarian or economic terms while local communities absorbed costs. Whether one agrees with every grievance is beside the point. A constitutional system must be able to hear such concerns without translating them into either authoritarian rage or technocratic condescension.

Miller is right that Republican elites failed many of their own voters. Low-tax, anti-union, anti-public-investment politics left many communities weaker. But Democratic and liberal elites failed too, not only by writing off rural and working-class voters but by equating expertise with legitimacy. Expertise is valuable; contempt is poisonous. A politics that says, in effect, “give us more national power and we will solve your problems” will not persuade people who believe national institutions helped create those problems.

This is where checks and balances still matter. They are not only devices for plutocrats to stop health care reform. They are also ways of forcing government to justify itself, slowing moral panics, protecting dissenters, and preserving space for communities that do not share elite consensus. The First Amendment is a check. Due process is a check. Federalism is a check. Bicameralism is a check. Judicial review is a check. The appropriations power is a check. Civil juries and criminal juries are checks. So are local elections. These devices can be abused, but they also express a sober truth: no faction, class, party, profession, or movement should be trusted with unchecked power.

Miller’s treatment of minority protection is especially troubling. She asks, correctly, which minorities are protected. Economic elites should not be able to launder privilege through minority-rights rhetoric. But vulnerable minorities are not always the groups favored by current progressive majorities. In some places, the vulnerable minority may be religious parents opposed to state educational mandates. In others, gun owners fearful of arbitrary enforcement, small landlords facing sweeping regulation, small business owners crushed by compliance burdens, or dissident students and professors challenging campus orthodoxy. One need not equate these groups with historically oppressed racial minorities to see that liberty requires more than majoritarian economic legislation.

A constitutional system should be judged by how it treats people when they lose elections. Miller’s essay focuses on the frustration of majorities unable to enact policy. But politics is cyclical. Any institutional arrangement that empowers today’s majority will empower tomorrow’s. If progressives want a streamlined national state when they hold Congress and the presidency, they must imagine that same state in the hands of Trump or a successor more disciplined than Trump. The administrative capacity used to enforce labor rights can be used to punish political enemies. The national standards used to protect voting rights can be manipulated to centralize election control. The public health authority used for benevolent purposes can be abused. The answer is not paralysis. It is constitutional humility.

That humility should not mean defending every existing veto point. The filibuster is hard to justify as a routine requirement for ordinary legislation. Congressional committees should not be burial grounds where accountability disappears. The House should be larger. Congress should reclaim authority from the presidency. Emergency powers should be narrowed. War powers should be enforced. Spending should be transparent. The Electoral College deserves criticism, though abolishing it would be extraordinarily difficult and would require careful attention to national coalition-building. Judicial life tenure could be replaced by long staggered terms without destroying independence.

But these reforms should aim to restore republican government, not simply empower national majorities. Congress, not the president, should be central. Laws should be clear enough that agencies are not effectively making the largest political decisions on their own. Courts should protect enumerated and deeply rooted liberties while showing modesty about managing policy. States should retain meaningful authority, not because states are morally superior, but because a continental republic needs pluralism. Local communities should be able to govern many matters locally, subject to basic constitutional rights.

Health care illustrates both Miller’s point and its limits. The United States spends too much and leaves too many people insecure. Interest groups have blocked reform. But it does not follow that the only democratic answer is a national universal system designed in Washington. There are multiple ways to expand coverage, reduce prices, increase competition, subsidize the poor, regulate billing, encourage primary care, and protect people with preexisting conditions. Some require national action; some can be state-based; some involve markets; some involve public provision. A less veto-ridden system might enact reform more easily, but it might also lock in a bad design. Deliberation and decentralization can expose tradeoffs that slogans conceal.

The same is true of labor and economic policy. Workers need more power. But policy must also account for small firms, regional variation, automation, global competition, and the fact that not all workers want the same arrangements. A politics of ordinary people should not assume that unions, federal agencies, or national party leaders always speak for ordinary people. Sometimes they do. Sometimes they become institutions with interests of their own.

Miller’s best line of argument is anti-oligarchic. America does need to constrain concentrated wealth, especially when it buys politics, dominates markets, or converts public policy into private rent. Campaign finance reform, antitrust enforcement, lobbying restrictions, stock-trading limits for officials, procurement transparency, and anti-corruption rules should attract support across ideological lines. But anti-oligarchy should include skepticism of all oligarchies: tech billionaires, defense contractors, public-sector monopolies, university credential cartels, foundation networks, agency insiders, and party machines.

The danger of Trump is that he channels distrust of elite rule into personal loyalty and institutional vandalism. The danger of Miller’s alternative is that it may channel justified anger at private power into excessive faith in national political power. A healthier republicanism would reject both. It would say: make Congress more accountable, make presidents less imperial, make courts independent but not supreme, make agencies lawful and transparent, make markets competitive, make states responsible, make elections fair, and make civil society strong enough that neither government nor corporations can dominate it.

Checks and balances are not dead. But they need reorientation. They should not be excuses for inaction or shields for privilege. Nor should they be dismissed as obstacles to democracy. They are the architecture of a free people who know that power is dangerous even when exercised in their name.
