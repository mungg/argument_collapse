"Progressives must move beyond technocratic and proceduralist responses to the conservative judiciary by developing their own substantive constitutional politics that frames economic and social reforms as essential to democratic self-government."

The lead essay is right about the central asymmetry in American constitutional politics. Conservatives have never treated the Constitution as a lawyerly settlement to be administered by neutral experts. They have treated it as a charter of political economy. They have argued, organized, appointed judges, funded litigation, and educated voters around a substantive account of freedom, property, religion, federalism, and speech. Progressives, by contrast, have too often responded as if the problem were merely bad judging: if only courts would respect precedent, if only doctrine were more technically sound, if only nominees had better temperaments. That posture is not enough for the moment we are in.

The Supreme Court is now poised to exercise enormous power over voting rights, labor law, reproductive freedom, climate regulation, gun regulation, campaign finance, affirmative action, and the administrative state. The leaked draft in Dobbs has made especially vivid what many already knew: appeals to precedent and institutional modesty are weak reeds when a majority has a constitutional project. If progressives answer that project only with nostalgia for a less partisan Court, they will lose. They need a constitutional politics of their own.

But the strongest case for reviving progressive constitutional argument is not that it will quickly persuade this Court. It probably will not. The point is that constitutional meaning in the United States has never been made by courts alone. Abolitionists, labor organizers, suffragists, civil rights activists, New Dealers, and opponents of the Vietnam War all made constitutional claims outside the judiciary. Sometimes courts eventually ratified those claims; sometimes they resisted them; often the more important victories came through statutes, constitutional amendments, administrative action, and changed public common sense. The Constitution has always been a language through which Americans argue about who counts, who rules, and what kind of power is compatible with republican government.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition the authors describe is therefore worth recovering. It reminds us that economic inequality is not merely a regrettable social fact to be corrected after constitutional law has done its work. It is itself a constitutional problem when it enables domination: employers over workers, monopolists over markets, donors over elections, states over disenfranchised citizens, police and prisons over racially subordinated communities. A republic cannot be reduced to formal rights possessed by isolated individuals. It requires institutions that allow ordinary people to exercise power together.

That point is especially compelling in the First Amendment context. The current Court’s free speech jurisprudence often treats economic power as constitutionally innocent and democratic regulation as constitutionally suspect. In campaign finance cases, this means that efforts to prevent great wealth from drowning out ordinary citizens become illicit attempts to “level” speech. In labor cases, the same amendment that once protected picketing and association becomes a weapon against unions’ ability to sustain themselves. The result is not neutrality. It is a choice to protect some forms of power—corporate, managerial, plutocratic—while disabling others.

Progressives should say this plainly. A small-donor public financing system is not a technocratic tweak; it is an effort to vindicate political equality. Labor law reform is not simply industrial policy; it is a way to make freedom of association meaningful for people who otherwise face employers alone. Antitrust enforcement is not only about prices; it is about preventing private governments from emerging inside the economy. These claims may not fit neatly into the doctrinal boxes law students memorize, but they fit the deeper constitutional concern with domination and self-government.

Still, there is a danger in saying that progressive reforms are not only permitted by the Constitution but required by it. The danger is not that such claims are illegitimate. Conservatives make equivalent claims constantly. The danger is that progressives may reproduce the very judicial supremacy they oppose if every major policy preference is redescribed as a constitutional command. A constitutional politics of democracy should enlarge the space for democratic judgment, not replace one set of judicially enforced absolutes with another.

For that reason, the language of “constitutional necessity” should be used carefully. It is one thing to say that Congress has a constitutional responsibility to protect voting rights, counter oligarchy, and secure the material bases of citizenship. It is another to say that the Constitution dictates a particular health care system, wealth tax, campaign finance formula, or corporate governance model. The first claim mobilizes constitutional values in democratic politics. The second risks collapsing prudential disagreement into constitutional betrayal.

This distinction matters because the progressive coalition itself contains real disagreements. Some favor single-payer health care; others prefer expanded public options or Medicaid-like programs. Some want aggressive breakups of dominant firms; others emphasize regulation, public utility models, or worker power. Some support race-conscious remedies; others stress universal programs designed with racial justice in mind. A democracy-of-opportunity Constitution should be capacious enough to guide these debates without pretending to settle all of them.

The authors’ account also needs to confront more directly the exclusions embedded in the very traditions they wish to reclaim. Jacksonian anti-oligarchy was often bound up with white male democracy, settler expansion, and Indigenous dispossession. New Deal constitutionalism empowered industrial workers while leaving many agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately Black workers and women—outside key protections. Even some Progressive Era attacks on monopoly coexisted with racial hierarchy, immigration restriction, and imperial ambition. These facts do not make the tradition unusable, but they mean it cannot simply be restored. It has to be reconstructed.

That reconstruction should place race, gender, and empire at the center rather than treating them as later additions to a primarily class-based story. The lead essay does important work in connecting Medicaid, voting rights, and federalism to racial political economy. That insight should be extended. The constitutional meaning of labor law looks different when domestic workers, care workers, incarcerated workers, and gig workers are placed at the center. The constitutional meaning of public investment looks different when viewed from communities subjected to redlining, environmental racism, school segregation, and predatory policing. The constitutional meaning of anti-oligarchy looks different when one remembers that concentrated private power has often worked through racialized markets in land, credit, labor, and punishment.

The practical question is how such a constitutional politics can gain traction. It cannot be limited to law reviews, judicial opinions, or Supreme Court confirmation hearings. It must appear in legislative findings, presidential speeches, union campaigns, state constitutional litigation, ballot initiatives, agency rulemaking, party platforms, and movement demands. Congress should not merely pass voting rights legislation; it should explain that political equality is a constitutional duty. Labor reformers should not merely cite economic studies about wages; they should argue that democracy requires countervailing worker power. Antitrust officials should not merely invoke consumer welfare; they should name domination by concentrated capital as a republican danger.

At the same time, institutional reform remains indispensable. A revived progressive constitutional politics should not become an excuse to ignore the structure of the Court itself. Jurisdiction stripping, ethics rules, term limits, expansion, supermajority requirements for invalidating federal statutes, and other reforms all deserve serious debate. If the Court is exercising political power, then democratic actors are entitled to ask how that power should be checked. Constitutional argument and institutional reform are complements, not substitutes.

The lead essay’s most important contribution is to reject learned helplessness. For too long, liberals have spoken as though the Constitution belongs to judges and the economy belongs to experts. That division has left citizens with too little democratic authority over the basic structures that govern their lives. Recovering the Constitution as a language of democratic political economy will not by itself defeat an entrenched conservative judiciary or reverse decades of inequality. But without such a recovery, progressive politics will continue to sound merely managerial while conservative politics sounds foundational.

The Constitution should not be a museum piece guarded by courts, nor a weapon reserved for the powerful. It should be a site of democratic struggle over the conditions of freedom. Progressives should enter that struggle without apology—but also without imagining that constitutional rhetoric can spare them the hard work of organizing majorities, building institutions, and governing well. A republic is not saved by doctrine alone. It is saved, if at all, by people who understand that concentrated power is a constitutional problem and who build the democratic power needed to overcome it.

"Progressives must reclaim constitutional interpretation from the conservative judiciary by articulating a substantive vision of 'constitutional political economy' that treats egalitarian reforms as democratic necessities rather than mere policy options."

The lead essay’s most important claim is also its simplest: if progressives do not argue about the Constitution, the Constitution will not disappear from politics. It will simply be interpreted by others. In practice, that means it will be interpreted by a Supreme Court majority that has shown remarkable creativity in turning constitutional law into a shield for concentrated wealth, corporate power, state obstruction, and minority rule. The answer cannot be for progressives to retreat into policy talk and hope courts stay away. Courts will not stay away. They have already arrived.

The call to “make progressive politics constitutional again” is therefore not a matter of rhetorical ornament. It is a matter of democratic self-defense. The Court’s conservative majority does not merely enforce neutral limits. It advances a constitutional political economy—one in which property rights, corporate speech, state sovereignty, and religious exemption are treated as foundational, while social citizenship, labor power, multiracial democracy, and egalitarian participation are treated as optional, suspect, or invisible. Progressives need a constitutional politics equal to the struggle.

The essay is especially persuasive in recovering the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition. Too often, constitutional debate is presented as a contest between originalism and living constitutionalism, as though progressives must choose between historical memory and modern needs. But American constitutional history is not the property of the right. The Constitution has been invoked not only to restrain government but also to justify public action against domination: the Reconstruction amendments, the antimonopoly tradition, the New Deal defense of labor rights and social insurance, the civil rights movement’s demand for full citizenship. These were not merely policy movements that happened to operate in constitutional space. They were constitutional movements because they asked what kind of republic the Constitution was meant to sustain.

That question is unavoidable today. A republic cannot endure if economic power is so concentrated that it can buy political power, discipline workers, shape the information environment, and block collective action. Formal political equality means little when one citizen can write a check that alters the entire terrain of an election and another cannot afford a day off work to vote, attend a meeting, or care for a sick child. The Constitution does not specify every detail of campaign finance, labor law, health insurance, or corporate governance. But it does presuppose a political community capable of self-rule. The lead essay is right to insist that economic structure is not external to constitutional democracy. It is one of its conditions.

The First Amendment is the clearest example. In popular mythology, free speech is about dissidents, pamphleteers, journalists, union organizers, religious minorities, and unpopular speakers resisting censorship. That tradition remains vital. But the modern Court has increasingly transformed the First Amendment into a deregulatory tool. Campaign finance restrictions become censorship. Public-sector union fees become compelled speech. Disclosure and professional regulation become suspect burdens. Corporate expenditures are treated as the speech of “disfavored speakers,” while the democratic interest in preventing plutocratic domination is treated as illegitimate.

This is not neutrality. It is a substantive theory of power. It imagines the political arena as a marketplace in which unequal resources may be freely converted into unequal influence, and it treats democratic attempts to alter that conversion as constitutionally dangerous. The result is a First Amendment that protects the strong against collective regulation more reliably than it protects the weak against private domination. To call this “weaponization” is not hyperbole. It describes a doctrinal pattern.

A progressive constitutional response should not be anti-speech. On the contrary, it should be more genuinely pro-speech than the Court’s current doctrine. A campaign finance system that enables more candidates to run, gives small donors greater influence, and reduces dependence on wealthy patrons expands political speech in any meaningful democratic sense. A labor law regime that protects organizing, picketing, striking, and solidarity action expands the speech and association of working people. The right’s great success has been to identify “speech” with the power of already powerful actors to spend, resist regulation, or opt out. Progressives should reclaim speech as a practice of democratic participation.

The same is true of race and political economy. One of the lead essay’s strengths is its refusal to confine constitutional racial justice to antidiscrimination doctrine. That doctrine matters, but it is too narrow to capture the constitutional legacy of racial subordination. Segregated schools, racialized labor markets, exclusion from New Deal programs, predatory lending, environmental injustice, mass incarceration, and unequal health access are not separate from constitutional democracy. They shape who has real freedom, who can participate, who can build wealth, who is exposed to state violence, and whose communities flourish.

The Court’s decisions in NFIB v. Sebelius and Shelby County v. Holder illustrate the point. In both, the Court presented itself as defending constitutional structure: state sovereignty, federalism, equal sovereignty among states. But structure is never innocent. Allowing states to reject Medicaid expansion predictably left millions without coverage, heavily concentrated in the South and disproportionately affecting Black and poor people. Striking down the Voting Rights Act’s coverage formula predictably enabled new voting restrictions in jurisdictions with histories of racial exclusion. In both cases, abstract solicitude for states limited Congress’s ability to secure equal citizenship for people.

Progressives should be explicit about this inversion. Federalism has democratic value when it enables participation, experimentation, and local self-government. But when state power is used to entrench racial hierarchy, restrict voting, deny health care, suppress labor, or shield oligarchic interests, federal constitutional authority must be available to secure republican freedom. Reconstruction established that principle at terrible cost. The Court has often forgotten it. Political movements cannot afford to.

The lead essay’s third field—antitrust, corporate power, and political economy—may be the most promising because public opinion has begun to shift. Across ideological lines, many Americans sense that concentrated corporate power has become intolerable: in technology, agriculture, pharmaceuticals, finance, hospitals, meatpacking, and labor markets. The old consumer-welfare antitrust framework seems inadequate to a world in which low prices can coexist with surveillance, wage suppression, supplier coercion, political lobbying, and private governance by dominant firms.

Here the constitutional argument is not that the Sherman Act has a single mandated meaning written into Article I. It is that a constitutional republic needs laws preventing private concentrations of power from becoming rival sovereigns. This was obvious to earlier generations who feared trusts, monopolies, and chartered privileges. It should be obvious again in an age when large corporations can structure markets, employment, speech platforms, supply chains, and politics. Antimonopoly is not nostalgia. It is republicanism applied to modern economic life.

Still, the lead essay’s project faces two dangers. The first is abstraction. “Constitutional political economy” can sound remote from everyday politics unless attached to concrete institutions and campaigns. The authors gesture in the right direction: public financing, the PRO Act, voting rights legislation, wealth taxation, antitrust reform, corporate governance. But the constitutional argument must be translated into organizing language. Workers do not need to hear only that unions are consistent with the First Amendment; they need to hear that the Constitution belongs to them when they organize against arbitrary power. Patients do not need only a theory of federal spending; they need to hear that equal citizenship includes the material security necessary to live as free people.

The second danger is court-centrism in reverse. The essay rightly rejects the idea that constitutional meaning belongs only to judges. But progressive constitutional politics must not become a strategy aimed primarily at persuading courts. The current Court is not waiting to be convinced by better Brandeisian history. Constitutional arguments matter because they shape public legitimacy, legislative courage, administrative action, and movement identity. They can also prepare the ground for future judicial change. But if the measure of success is immediate Supreme Court acceptance, disappointment is guaranteed.

That is why constitutional politics must be paired with institutional reform. The filibuster, malapportioned Senate, Electoral College, partisan gerrymandering, voter suppression, and life-tenured Court all distort democratic authority. A democracy-of-opportunity tradition cannot flourish while antidemocratic veto points make legislative majorities nearly powerless. Progressives should not choose between substantive constitutional argument and procedural democracy reform. They need both. A constitutional movement that says Congress has a duty to protect voting rights must also be willing to change Senate rules that prevent Congress from fulfilling that duty. A movement that says courts have overreached must consider jurisdictional limits, ethics rules, term limits, or Court expansion.

The lead essay’s deepest contribution is to recover a lost confidence. For too long, many liberals have spoken as though the Constitution is mainly a constraint on progressive ambition. They defend programs as permissible, not as necessary; acceptable, not as expressions of democratic principle. Conservatives have rarely been so modest. They have built institutions, trained lawyers, mobilized voters, and told a constitutional story in which their political goals are linked to freedom, tradition, and limited government. Progressives need not imitate their substance, but they should learn from their seriousness.

The Constitution is not self-executing. It is a terrain of struggle. It has sanctioned slavery and enabled emancipation; protected property and authorized reconstruction; been invoked against labor and for labor; narrowed democracy and expanded it. To enter that struggle is not to worship the Constitution or deny its failures. It is to insist that democratic majorities, social movements, and elected representatives have as much authority to interpret our constitutional commitments as judges do.

A progressive constitutional politics should say plainly: oligarchy is incompatible with republican government; racial hierarchy is incompatible with equal citizenship; worker domination is incompatible with freedom of association; mass exclusion from health, housing, education, and political participation is incompatible with democracy. These claims will not win every case tomorrow. But without them, progressives enter the central constitutional conflicts of our time already disarmed.

"To effectively counter a hostile Supreme Court, progressives must move beyond recovering constitutional traditions and adopt an operational governing strategy centered on legislative redundancy, institutional diversification, and state capacity."

The lead essay is right to warn that the next great obstacle to progressive reform may not be winning public support or even passing legislation, but surviving judicial review. On voting rights, labor law, campaign finance, climate regulation, health care, racial justice, taxation, antitrust, and corporate governance, any ambitious Democratic Congress would face a Supreme Court more than willing to entertain constitutional theories that would have seemed implausible a generation ago. The lesson is not that progressives should ignore courts. It is that they should plan for constitutional conflict before it arrives.

But planning for conflict requires more than recovering a tradition. The lead essay’s account of the democracy-of-opportunity tradition is valuable, even inspiring. It reminds progressives that they are not constitutional trespassers when they seek to disperse power and secure equal citizenship. Yet the immediate challenge is operational. How should legislators, administrators, organizers, and lawyers design reforms in a world where a hostile Court may strike some of them down? What does constitutional politics look like as a governing strategy?

The first rule should be redundancy. Progressive legislation should be built with fallback provisions, severability clauses, alternative authorities, and multiple implementation pathways. The lead essay briefly suggests this in discussing wealth taxation: if Congress enacts a wealth tax, it should also prepare alternative taxes that take effect if the Court invalidates the first design. That principle should be generalized. A Congress serious about governing under judicial uncertainty should legislate the way engineers build bridges: assuming stress, anticipating failure points, and preventing collapse.

For example, if Congress wants to reduce concentrated wealth, it should not rely on a single constitutionally vulnerable instrument. It can combine estate tax reform, mark-to-market taxation where feasible, corporate minimum taxes, surtaxes on high incomes, stronger enforcement against avoidance, excise taxes on stock buybacks, and reforms to capital gains treatment. Some tools may be less elegant than a direct wealth tax. But a package with overlapping mechanisms is harder for courts to disable in one blow.

The same applies to voting rights. A new Voting Rights Act should be supported by extensive findings, updated coverage formulas, nationwide rules, private rights of action, federal enforcement authority, election administration funding, and constitutional grounding in multiple amendments. If preclearance is narrowed, other rules should remain. If one coverage formula falls, another should activate. If private enforcement is restricted, federal enforcement capacity should be strengthened. The goal is not only to win the ideal constitutional argument but to ensure that voters are protected even if judges reject part of it.

The second rule should be institutional diversification. The lead essay focuses heavily on Congress and the Supreme Court, understandably. But progressive constitutional politics must be federal, state, local, administrative, and organizational. State constitutions often contain affirmative education clauses, labor protections, environmental provisions, privacy guarantees, and democratic commitments that have no federal equivalent. State courts can be reactionary, but they can also be sites of innovation. City governments can experiment with public financing, labor standards, tenant protections, procurement rules, and public options. Agencies can enforce existing statutes with renewed attention to power disparities.

This matters because national reform is bottlenecked by the Senate, filibuster, and Court. A democracy-of-opportunity politics that waits for unified federal power will remain mostly aspirational. The right understood this long ago. It built power through state legislatures, school boards, attorneys general, legal networks, and administrative appointments. Progressives need a similarly distributed strategy. Constitutional argument should be heard not only in Supreme Court briefs but in state legislative hearings, agency rulemakings, union campaigns, ballot initiatives, city charters, and public utility commissions.

The third rule should be record-building. Courts often pretend to decide legal questions in abstraction, but factual records matter. Congress’s failure to assemble the right record can make legislation vulnerable; a strong record can make judicial invalidation look more openly political. Before passing major reforms, committees should document how concentrated economic power distorts democracy, how voter suppression operates, how employer coercion defeats worker choice, how racial inequality is reproduced through facially neutral systems, how monopoly power harms workers and communities, and how climate risk threatens national welfare. These records should be public-facing, not only lawyer-facing.

The original Voting Rights Act was powerful partly because Congress built an undeniable record of disenfranchisement. Modern legislation requires the same seriousness. If Congress seeks to regulate corporate political spending, it should document channels of influence, dependence, corruption risks, and the inadequacy of disclosure alone. If it seeks labor reform, it should document captive-audience meetings, retaliation, fissured employment, misclassification, and the decline of worker voice. If it seeks antitrust reform, it should document harms beyond consumer prices: wages, suppliers, innovation, privacy, local communities, and political influence.

The fourth rule should be administrative competence. Constitutional politics is not only about winning arguments; it is about making government worthy of trust. The pandemic showed both the capacity and the fragility of the American state. Direct relief payments, expanded unemployment benefits, the child tax credit, vaccine development, and emergency public health measures demonstrated that government can act at scale. But administrative failures, confusing eligibility rules, state-level unemployment breakdowns, and uneven public health messaging damaged confidence.

If progressives argue that the Constitution permits and even requires democratic government to secure material foundations, they must show that democratic government can deliver. A poorly administered benefit becomes an argument for austerity. A confusing regulatory scheme becomes an argument for deregulation. A public program that is hard to access empowers those who say government cannot solve problems. Social Security and Medicare endure not because they are constitutionally theorized but because people experience them as reliable. Any revived constitutional political economy must be paired with a revival of state capacity.

The fifth rule should be movement partnership. The lead essay rightly emphasizes labor, racial justice movements, antimonopoly advocates, and campaigns for democratic reform. But constitutional arguments cannot remain the property of professors and appellate lawyers. Workers organizing a warehouse, tenants resisting eviction, formerly incarcerated people fighting disenfranchisement, debtors challenging predatory lending, and communities opposing environmental racism all have constitutional stories to tell. They know what domination feels like before it is named in doctrine.

Yet partnership requires humility from legal elites. Movements often need lawyers, but they do not need every demand translated into the safest possible constitutional language at the outset. Sometimes disruption, moral clarity, and ambitious demands shift the terrain more effectively than carefully tailored arguments. The role of constitutional lawyers should be to expand options, not narrow imagination. They can help identify powers Congress already has, draft durable statutes, defend local experiments, and expose judicial overreach. They should not become gatekeepers of political possibility.

The sixth rule should be democratic reform as foundation. The lead essay discusses campaign finance and voting rights, but these should be understood not as separate issue areas but as prerequisites for everything else. A polity distorted by gerrymandering, voter suppression, malapportioned representation, dark money, partisan election administration, and the filibuster cannot reliably enact or sustain egalitarian reform. The Constitution’s republican aspiration is hollow if legislative majorities cannot govern.

Progressives should therefore prioritize a democracy agenda: voting rights protections, independent redistricting or national anti-gerrymandering standards, statehood for disenfranchised citizens where appropriate, campaign finance public matching, election administration funding, protection against election subversion, and reform of Senate rules. Some of these measures face constitutional challenge. All face political difficulty. But without them, the democracy-of-opportunity tradition becomes a sermon delivered to institutions designed to ignore majorities.

The seventh rule should be a realistic approach to the Supreme Court. The lead essay is right that progressives should not treat the Court as the sole interpreter of the Constitution. But they should be clear about what follows. If the Court continues to invalidate major legislation on aggressive and contestable grounds, political actors must consider structural responses: term limits, ethics rules, transparency requirements, jurisdictional regulation, expansion of the lower courts, and perhaps expansion of the Supreme Court itself. These proposals differ in feasibility and wisdom, but they belong on the table.

At the same time, court reform cannot be a substitute for popular legitimacy. Many Americans do not follow doctrine closely, but they do have intuitions about fairness, democracy, rights, and institutional abuse. If progressives want to challenge judicial supremacy, they must explain not only that the Court is wrong but why its decisions harm people’s ability to govern themselves. “The Court sided with corporations against workers.” “The Court made it easier for states to suppress votes.” “The Court blocked Congress from providing health care.” “The Court invented a rule that protects money as power.” These claims should be precise, repeated, and connected to everyday consequences.

The eighth rule should be constitutional pluralism. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is an important resource, but it should not become a single master narrative. Different communities draw on different constitutional memories: Reconstruction, abolitionism, Indigenous sovereignty, labor republicanism, feminist equality, disability justice, immigrant rights, religious dissent, civil libertarianism, environmental stewardship. A successful progressive constitutional politics will not flatten these into one story. It will create alliances among them.

This matters because there are real tensions inside the progressive coalition. Labor rights and environmental regulation can collide in fossil fuel regions unless joined by a credible just transition. Religious liberty and LGBTQ equality require careful institutional design. Race-conscious remedies and universal programs must be balanced strategically. Antitrust enforcement against technology platforms may intersect uneasily with concerns about speech moderation and disinformation. Public health authority must be defended without indifference to civil liberties. A constitutional politics that denies these tensions will sound propagandistic. One that acknowledges them can build trust.

Finally, progressives should avoid promising that constitutional argument alone can overcome political weakness. The conservative legal movement did not triumph simply because it had a better story. It built institutions for decades: law school networks, judicial pipelines, think tanks, donor alliances, litigation shops, media ecosystems, and party discipline. Progressives have begun to build counter-institutions, but they remain uneven. A democracy-of-opportunity constitutionalism will need candidate recruitment, judicial nominations, clerkship networks, state solicitor general offices, public-interest litigation, popular education, labor education, and sustained funding. Ideas matter when they are carried by institutions.

The lead essay’s core insight remains indispensable: progressive reformers should stop approaching the Constitution as hostile terrain. The Constitution has been invoked to defend slavery and property, but also to destroy slavery, reconstruct citizenship, protect labor, expand voting, and authorize national action against private domination. It does not belong to judges alone, and certainly not to the current Court majority. Citizens and legislators have constitutional responsibilities too.

But the next step is to turn that insight into craft. Draft laws with fallbacks. Build records. Use state and local power. Strengthen administration. Partner with movements. Reform democracy first. Prepare for judicial attack. Explain the stakes in ordinary language. Develop institutions capable of sustaining conflict over years, not news cycles.

The question is not merely whether progressive politics should be constitutional again. It must also become strategic again. A hostile Court can defeat isolated reforms. It will have a harder time defeating a movement that legislates redundantly, governs competently, organizes broadly, and speaks constitutionally in the name of democratic power.

The lead essay by Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet offers a bracing historical narrative and a provocative strategic recommendation: progressives must re-embrace constitutional argument about political economy, drawing on the anti-oligarchy tradition running from the Jacksonians through Reconstruction and the New Deal. As a diagnosis of our present predicament, the essay is incisive and historically rich. But as a strategy for confronting an archconservative Supreme Court, it warrants deeper scrutiny. In particular, the essay understates the institutional and cultural obstacles to the constitutional politics it envisions, overestimates the persuasive power of progressive constitutional narrative inside the courtroom, and underestimates the risk that this very approach might further entrench judicial supremacy rather than challenge it.

The authors’ central historical claim is that during periods of reform, elected officials and movements openly contested the Court’s interpretations and asserted their own constitutional visions, and that these public arguments helped shift legal doctrine and political possibility. The New Deal Court realignment occurred only after a protracted fight that included court-packing threats, massive electoral victories, and a judiciary already divided and, in part, open to change. Today’s conservative supermajority is younger, more ideologically cohesive, and structurally insulated, with lifetime tenure and a jurisprudence that treats many of the very areas the authors want to reclaim—campaign finance, labor rights, federal regulatory power—as settled against progressive intervention. The argument that lawmakers have a “constitutional duty” to preserve democratic political economy, even if passionately made in the public square, would almost certainly be treated by this Court as a mere policy preference, not a binding legal command. The justices would not need to be persuaded because they have already constructed an elaborate doctrinal apparatus that translates conservative economic premises into constitutional imperatives: that spending money is speech, that protecting corporate property is a takings concern, that states possess “equal sovereignty.” Mere invocation of an older tradition is unlikely to dislodge these edifices; it risks being dismissed as a flavor of living constitutionalism the majority explicitly rejects.

The “shield” metaphor, in which progressive constitutional arguments are wielded primarily to defend legislation, implicitly acknowledges that the courts will remain the ultimate arbiters. But the difficulty is that the current Court’s First Amendment jurisprudence is not simply a mistaken reading that can be corrected by better historical or theoretical arguments; it is an ideological commitment embedded in a larger vision of liberty that treats economic inequality as natural and voluntary associations as threats to individual autonomy only when they are collective. That may be a long-term project, yet the urgency of the problems the essay identifies—climate change, voting rights, economic inequality—suggests that time is short. One might ask whether the energy spent developing a constitutional counter-narrative would be better directed toward building political power to pass legislation despite the Court, or toward more aggressive structural reforms such as court expansion or term limits, which many progressives by 2022 were seriously debating.

Another concern is that the essay’s invocation of constitutional obligation might inadvertently constrain progressive policy ambition. The argument that the Constitution *compels* certain egalitarian measures could cut both ways: if the political branches fail to enact them, does that constitute a constitutional failure? And if the Court strikes down such measures, does that signal that the constitutional vision itself is legally untenable? The New Deal reformers succeeded not by simply asserting a constitutional duty but by creating new institutional realities—social security, labor rights, regulatory agencies—that eventually became woven into the fabric of expectations and legal acceptability. The essay’s examples, such as the PRO Act and the wealth tax, are precisely the kind of legislative initiatives that can reshape the baseline on their own terms. Wrapping them in constitutional rhetoric might be inspiring to some constituencies, but it also gives hostile courts an opening to define the constitutional limits more sharply. A more cautious path might be to pursue these policies as exercises of statutory authority under the Commerce Clause or the spending power, employing the full scope of existing doctrinal openings without prematurely inviting constitutional adjudication.

That said, the essay’s recovery of the anti-oligarchy tradition is a genuine intellectual contribution, and its call to reconnect racial justice with political economy is especially timely. The observation that the Medicaid-expansion ruling and the gutting of the Voting Rights Act both rest on newly invented principles of state sovereignty, with racially disparate impacts, illuminates how racial hierarchy is embedded in the architecture of constitutional federalism. Perhaps the answer lies less in persuading the current Court than in shaping the appointment process and the broader judicial ideology over decades. Its strength is that it treats political economy as a constitutional subject, refusing the long-standing liberal separation of law from economics. Its weakness is that it does not fully reckon with the practical reality that the current Court is prepared to treat any progressive constitutional vision as illegitimate by definition. To reclaim the Constitution, progressives may need not only to speak in constitutional terms but to build the political power necessary to alter the composition and jurisdiction of the courts themselves—a project that, in 2022, remains deeply uncertain. Without that, the democracy-of-opportunity tradition may remain a library of noble arguments, all of them overruled.

The Argument Is Right; The Subjects Are Missing

The lead essay performs a genuine service by recovering a constitutional tradition that mainstream liberalism has neglected for two generations. Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet make a compelling case that the postwar settlement—courts handle the Constitution, economists handle the economy, politics fills in the residual blanks—was always a bad deal for working people, and that today’s right-wing court has cashed in the bill. I want to push their argument in a direction they only partially explore: toward the question of who, exactly, is going to make these constitutional arguments and through what social formations.

The essay is rich with prescriptions for what Congress should do, what doctrine should look like, what legislative bills should anticipate. It is much thinner on the question of where the political force comes from. The authors note, almost in passing, that “workers will need to exercise their rights to organize, strike, and act in solidarity in contexts where this is now illegal.” This is the single most important sentence in the essay, and it deserves to be the center rather than an aside. Constitutional traditions do not assert themselves. They are asserted by organized people with the leverage to make their assertions stick.

Consider the historical episodes the authors invoke. The Jacksonian assault on the Bank of the United States was not a triumph of constitutional reasoning. It was an outcome of a mass party with deep roots in local political culture, capable of mobilizing voters around an explicit anti-oligarchy program. Reconstruction’s constitutional achievements—the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments—were imposed at gunpoint by an army on a defeated South, and they collapsed when the political will to enforce them collapsed. The New Deal’s constitutional revolution was made possible by industrial unions whose sitdown strikes in Flint, Akron, and elsewhere demonstrated that the existing legal order could not be maintained at acceptable cost. In each case, constitutional argument followed political force; it did not precede it.

What this means is that the authors’ project—and I do think it is a project worth pursuing—will succeed only insofar as it is connected to organizations capable of generating political force. The contemporary American left has very few such organizations. Unions represent about 10 percent of the workforce, and only 6 percent of the private sector. Civil rights organizations have professionalized to the point of becoming, in many cases, donor-driven nonprofits rather than mass-membership institutions. The Movement for Black Lives, which the authors cite, has produced remarkable policy platforms, but it is a network of local groups without the durable institutional infrastructure of, say, the NAACP in its mid-century prime. The Democratic Party, especially since the Clinton-era turn toward college-educated professionals as its core constituency, is in many ways structurally incapable of carrying the kind of program the authors describe.

So the constitutional argument the authors want to make has, at present, no organized subject. It has authors—several distinguished ones, who have written a book—and it has scattered legislative champions, and it has a sympathetic but inattentive base. What it lacks is the social muscle to make adverse court rulings unenforceable, to make legislative leaders fear the consequences of inaction, and to make constitutional arguments resonate in workplaces, neighborhoods, and churches as well as in seminar rooms.

I do not say this to belittle the authors’ contribution. The intellectual work is necessary. A movement without ideas is no better off than ideas without a movement. And there is a real chicken-and-egg problem here: it is hard to organize without a vision, and hard to articulate a vision without an organized base to test it against. The authors have done the harder of the two preliminary tasks. But the next steps belong to organizers, not professors.

What might those next steps look like? Let me sketch three.

First, the labor movement needs to recover the constitutional self-understanding the essay describes, and quickly. The PRO Act is a good bill, but it will not pass under current conditions. What might shift those conditions is a wave of strikes and organizing drives that frame themselves explicitly in constitutional terms—not just as wage disputes, but as exercises of fundamental rights to collective action that the current legal regime unjustly restricts. The recent organizing victories at Amazon and Starbucks suggest that something is stirring. If those campaigns can be linked to a broader narrative about democracy, oligarchy, and constitutional self-rule, they become more than economic actions; they become a politics. The authors’ framework is exactly what such campaigns need.

Second, civil rights organizations and racial justice movements need to make the kind of political-economy turn the essay describes, but as organizing strategy as well as theory. The brilliance of Reverend Barber’s Poor People’s Campaign is precisely that it links racial justice to economic transformation in a way that builds multiracial coalitions in places (the South, in particular) where the politics of racial division has long been used to defeat economic reform. This is not just a policy program; it is a theory of how to assemble a political majority capable of overcoming the structural barriers to reform. Constitutional argument can be a glue here, providing a shared vocabulary that crosses identity lines.

Third—and this is the hardest—progressives need new institutional forms to replace the mid-century mass institutions that have decayed. The labor movement’s decline is the most visible example, but parallel decay has affected churches, civic organizations, and political parties at the local level. Without thick institutions, constitutional arguments cannot travel from law reviews to ordinary politics. The right has its own infrastructure—Federalist Society, evangelical churches, talk radio, gun clubs, increasingly social media networks—and this infrastructure carries constitutional ideas about religious freedom, gun rights, federalism, and limited government into millions of households. The left needs analogous infrastructure, and the lack of it is the deepest reason its constitutional arguments fail to resonate.

I want to add one more observation that the essay underplays. The authors treat the constitutional argument as something to be conducted in elite venues—courts, Congress, op-eds, books like the one this essay condenses. But constitutional argument in the historical episodes they cite happened largely in popular venues: pamphlets, mass meetings, election campaigns, even church sermons. Frederick Douglass made constitutional arguments to crowds of thousands. Eugene Debs made them from a prison cell. The Knights of Labor and the Populists made them in farmhouse organizing meetings across the Midwest and South. There is something a little ironic about an essay calling for popular constitutionalism that is itself addressed largely to other law professors and policy elites. The work the authors describe needs to be vernacularized, and that is not something legal scholars can do alone.

I close on a hopeful note. The conditions for the kind of politics the authors envision are better than they have been in decades. Inequality is at levels not seen since the Gilded Age. Public skepticism of corporations and billionaires is widespread and bipartisan. The pandemic exposed the cruelty of the existing economic order in ways that briefly made universal social provision politically thinkable. Young people, in particular, hold views about capitalism, democracy, and the role of government that align closely with the democracy-of-opportunity tradition the authors describe. There is, in short, a constituency for these arguments. What remains is to organize it.

The authors have given us the script. Now we need a theater company, a stage, and an audience. None of these will assemble themselves. Constitutional politics, like all politics, is built by people in rooms, in workplaces, in neighborhoods, in the slow accumulation of institutional capacity. The book this essay distills is one piece of the larger project. The rest will be made not by writing but by organizing, and the gap between the elegance of the argument and the meagerness of our current organized base is the gap that must be closed.

The Court Won't Be Argued Out of Power

Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet have written a bracing essay, and one I largely admire. They are right that postwar liberalism took a wrong turn when it ceded constitutional political economy to the courts and economic policy to technocrats. They are right that conservatives never made that mistake, and right that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition offers progressives a usable past. But I worry that the essay rests on a hopeful premise about constitutional argument that the present moment will not bear: that if progressives speak in a more constitutional register, courts will eventually respond, the public will eventually rally, and the political branches will eventually feel empowered to push back. I am not so sure.

Consider what the authors are actually asking elected officials and movements to do. They want Congress to pass a wealth tax with a backup inheritance tax in case it gets struck down. They want a robust labor reform that anticipates First Amendment and federalism attacks. They want voting rights statutes that openly defy Shelby County’s logic. They want antitrust reform that confronts takings and contract challenges head-on. In each case, progressives are supposed to win the legislative fight, then win the litigation, or, failing that, mobilize public sentiment to delegitimize an adverse ruling. That is an enormous amount of work, and it presupposes a Congress capable of passing such laws in the first place. The same antidemocratic structures that the essay rightly identifies—the Senate, the filibuster, gerrymandered House districts, the Electoral College—stand between us and the legislative victories that would be needed to even tee up these constitutional confrontations.

The harder question the essay sidesteps is what to do when the legislative path is blocked not just by ideological opponents but by structural features of the system that themselves enjoy constitutional cover. The Senate gives Wyoming and California equal weight. The Electoral College has handed the presidency to popular-vote losers twice in two decades, and each of those presidents reshaped the Court. The federal judiciary is staffed for a generation by a movement that lost the popular vote in seven of the last eight presidential elections. You cannot argue your way out of that. You can only restructure it.

This is where I part company with the authors, not in their diagnosis but in their menu of responses. They are right that judicial reforms premised on restoring a nonpartisan past are based on a myth. But the alternative is not merely to recover a richer tradition of constitutional argument. It is to use the powers the elected branches still have to change the shape of the judiciary itself. Court expansion, jurisdiction-stripping, supermajority requirements for invalidating federal statutes, term limits enforced through circuit-riding rotation, even straightforward statutory overrides under Section 5 of the Fourteenth Amendment—all of these belong on the table. The authors gesture toward popular constitutionalism but seem reluctant to embrace the institutional weapons that would actually disarm a hostile Court. They want a battle of arguments. The right is fighting a battle for control of an institution.

There is a deeper theoretical problem too. The essay’s rhetoric implies a roughly symmetrical situation: conservatives have a constitutional political economy, progressives need one. But the asymmetry is enormous. Conservatives have spent fifty years building the Federalist Society, capturing law schools’ feeder pipelines, training clerks, grooming nominees, and constructing a doctrinal apparatus (originalism, textualism, the major questions doctrine, anti-commandeering, equal sovereignty of states) that operates as a coherent ideology. Progressives have... law professors writing books. I do not mean to be glib; this book and this essay are valuable contributions. But constitutional argument does not move judges who are confident in their power and their numbers. It moves judges who feel insecure—who fear public backlash, institutional delegitimation, or being overruled by political action. The lesson of 1937 is not that FDR persuaded the Court of a richer constitutional political economy. It is that he threatened to pack it, and they blinked.

I also want to register a more uncomfortable doubt about the “democracy-of-opportunity tradition.” The essay traces a noble lineage: Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, Progressives, New Dealers. But several of these movements were built on racial exclusion, and the New Deal famously bought Southern support by carving Black workers out of social insurance. Aziz Rana himself acknowledges in the passage they quote that the “high tides” of democracy of opportunity coincided with territorial expansion. That is not incidental. The American political economy has often expanded the franchise of citizenship by drawing tighter borders around who counts. If we are going to revive this tradition, we have to grapple seriously with how to extract its anti-oligarchic energy without inheriting its exclusionary scaffolding. The essay gestures toward this in its section on race and political economy, but the gesture is too brief given the weight the tradition is being asked to bear.

None of this is to say the authors are wrong about the importance of constitutional argument. They are right that the liberal silence about the Constitution outside of court is a strategic catastrophe. They are right that when Stevens spoke of a “democratic duty” to regulate corporate political spending, he was invoking a register that has gone almost entirely missing from mainstream Democratic rhetoric. The reclamation project the essay proposes is necessary. I just do not believe it is sufficient.

What might a more adequate strategy look like? It would combine the constitutional argument the authors propose with three additional elements. First, structural reform of the Court and of democracy itself: statehood for D.C., abolition of the filibuster, court expansion or jurisdiction-stripping, federal voting rights protections enforced through means that do not require the Court’s acquiescence. Second, building durable institutions—not just movements, which come and go, but the kinds of mass-membership organizations that once made labor a force the political system could not ignore. The PRO Act is good, but legislation alone will not rebuild unions that have been hollowed out for forty years. Third, a willingness to engage in confrontational popular politics around adverse rulings: not just disagreeing with Citizens United or Shelby County, but treating them as illegitimate exercises of power that the political branches have an affirmative obligation to work around and, where possible, override.

The authors come close to this last point when they write that “workers will need to exercise their rights to organize, strike, and act in solidarity in contexts where this is now illegal, in the face of judicial injunctions, fines, and jail time.” That is exactly right, and it is the most radical sentence in the essay. But it sits oddly with the surrounding emphasis on constitutional argumentation. Civil disobedience is not really an argument about constitutional meaning; it is a wager about political power. The 1930s labor movement won not because it persuaded the Hughes Court of a better reading of the Commerce Clause, but because sitdown strikes in Flint and elsewhere made the cost of resistance higher than the cost of accommodation. The constitutional revolution followed the political revolution, not the other way around.

So here is my friendly amendment to the authors’ project. Yes, retrieve the democracy-of-opportunity tradition. Yes, teach progressives to speak constitutionally about wages, monopoly, race, and political voice. Yes, make the case in legislation, in litigation, and in public argument that egalitarian reforms are constitutional necessities. But do not imagine that argument alone can constrain an unelected, life-tenured majority that views itself as the vanguard of a counter-revolution. The Court will be brought back in line, if at all, by a combination of political organization, institutional restructuring, and yes, the kind of confrontational lawmaking and movement politics that forces a choice between legitimacy and obstruction.

The essay’s subtitle could be “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again.” I would add a second clause: “And Be Prepared to Make the Constitution Political Again, Too.” The Court has acted politically for fifty years while being treated as an oracle. The remedy is not more sophisticated oracular interpretation. It is to puncture the mystique, demonstrate that the emperor is naked, and use the tools democracy still gives us to constrain him. The authors have written a book that helps with the first half. We need the second half too.

"While reclaiming the 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is valuable, prioritizing constitutional arguments over the development of organized political power risks providing intellectual cover for a hostile conservative judiciary and distracting from essential coalition-building."

The lead essay performs a valuable service by recovering the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition from relative neglect and arguing that progressives must reclaim constitutional discourse from conservatives. These are genuinely important interventions in an era when many on the left have ceded constitutional territory entirely. But the essay's strategy carries underappreciated risks that warrant more careful scrutiny before progressives bet heavily on "making our politics constitutional again."

The essay acknowledges the fear—that re-constitutionalizing politics cedes power to courts—but responds by asserting that *not* speaking about the Constitution cedes even more power to courts. This symmetry argument is rhetorically clever but analytically incomplete. The asymmetry between speaking and not speaking depends entirely on who is doing the speaking and in what institutional context. When progressives make constitutional arguments before a Court dominated by movement conservatives who have already committed to a particular vision of constitutional political economy, those arguments do not simply "show that all branches of government...have the authority and duty to debate what our constitutional principles require." They also provide intellectual cover for judicial striking down of progressive legislation. The Court does not simply adjudicate constitutional arguments; it selects which arguments to credit, and on the current Court, progressive arguments face an unsympathetic audience.

Consider the three arenas the essay identifies. On the First Amendment, the essay proposes public financing mechanisms, labor law reform, and campaign finance equalization that would face immediate constitutional challenge under doctrines that a conservative majority has already substantially shaped. The essay acknowledges that "novel extensions of First Amendment doctrine" will be offered against these proposals. But acknowledging this does not resolve it. If the Court is willing to strike down laws that "create more speech" (as Elena Kagan noted in dissent) because they indirectly disincentivize wealthy candidates from spending, what constitutional argument protects the essay's proposed alternatives? The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is invoked as a source of argument, but the current Court has demonstrated its ability to dismiss such arguments when they conflict with its preferred outcomes.

The antitrust arena is perhaps the most promising, because the essay correctly notes that the current antitrust consensus emerged through political contestation, not judicial determination. But this cuts both ways. If antitrust doctrine was shaped by Reagan-era ideologues and a cooperative Court, it can be reshaped through political mobilization—legislative mandates, agency appointments, and statutory reform—that does not require convincing a conservative Court to adopt a new constitutional vision. The essay itself acknowledges that neo-Brandeisians are "making a claim that Brandeis would have found obvious, but that today requires argument." But arguments in political discourse, not constitutional argument, are what will shift the regulatory agenda.

The essay's strongest section concerns the racial dimensions of economic policy— Medicaid expansion, voting rights, and the connection between social insurance and racial justice. Here the historical tradition is richest and the constitutional stakes most urgent. But even here, the path forward requires political coalition-building that the essay treats as secondary to constitutional argument. The Medicaid expansion was struck down not because progressive forces lacked constitutional arguments, but because Chief Justice Roberts had the votes. No amount of constitutional argument will recover those votes without broader changes in political alignment.

This is not to say that constitutional argument is useless. The essay is right that arguments made in politics shape arguments in courts, and that progressive forces should develop robust accounts of constitutional meaning. But these arguments are most powerful when deployed in service of, rather than as substitutes for, organized political power. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition was effective in the 1930s not because reformers made superior constitutional arguments, but because they built movements capable of threatening the political standing of opponents, making Court-packing credible, and sustaining reform across decades. The lead essay gives constitutional argument pride of place in a way that risks distracting from the harder, slower work of coalition-building and political transformation.

The essay concludes by asserting that "these are not merely constitutionally permissible goals; they are constitutional necessities." This is a powerful claim, but it proves too much. If everything progressives want is constitutionally required, then anything a Court strikes down is constitutionally forbidden—and the result is a form of judicial supremacy in which unelected judges determine what "constitutional necessities" exist. The alternative is to recognize that constitutional argument is one arena of contestation among others, valuable but not determinative. Progressives should mine the democracy-of-opportunity tradition for arguments, yes—but they should do so while building the political power that makes constitutional arguments effective.

Diversified Gemini

"To effectively operationalize an anti-oligarchy constitutionalism, progressives must move beyond public rhetoric and legislative battles to embed the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition within the administrative state's regulatory and enforcement actions."

In their compelling manifesto, “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again,” Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, and their co-authors identify a critical failure of the modern liberal project: the surrender of the Constitution to the conservative movement. The authors rightly argue that progressives must revive the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, insisting that the Constitution requires a political economy free from oligarchy. While their focus on public rhetoric, legislative battles, and eventual judicial transformation is essential, there is a missing strategic pillar in their account. If we are to operationalize this anti-oligarchy constitutionalism today, the immediate battleground is not just in the halls of Congress or the court of public opinion, but deep within the administrative state.

For decades, liberals have viewed the administrative state—agencies like the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Department of Justice (DOJ), the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)—through a purely technocratic lens. As the authors note, we left "economic questions to the economists." This technocratic retreat allowed conservative, neoliberal assumptions—such as the consumer welfare standard in antitrust or shareholder primacy in corporate law—to become the default operating systems of these powerful agencies.

But administrative agencies are not merely neutral executors of statutory text; they are sites of profound constitutional interpretation. What scholars call "administrative constitutionalism" recognizes that agencies routinely interpret their mandates in light of broader constitutional values. If progressives want to revive a constitutional political economy, they must empower agency leaders to explicitly reject neoliberal technocracy and embrace the democracy-of-opportunity tradition in their rulemakings and enforcement actions.

Take the realm of antitrust, which the authors correctly identify as crucial for preserving a democratic constitutional order. The transformation of antitrust in the 1980s under the influence of Robert Bork and the Chicago School was not achieved primarily by amending the Sherman Act. It was achieved by changing how the DOJ and the FTC interpreted their enforcement mandates, which courts eventually adopted. The neo-Brandeisian movement today, championed by figures like Lina Khan (currently chairing the FTC), understands that the agencies must take the lead in reversing this disaster.

When the FTC moves to block a mega-merger or regulate noncompete clauses, it should not merely issue a dry economic analysis predicting a marginal increase in consumer prices. It should explicitly ground its actions in the structural, constitutional imperatives of its founding statutes. The FTC was created in 1914 precisely to be an administrative bulwark against the political power of monopolies. By articulating an anti-oligarchy vision in its enforcement guidelines and administrative rulings, the FTC can force the courts to engage with a progressive theory of political economy. Even if conservative courts push back, the agency’s framing shapes the legal and public discourse, validating the idea that extreme market concentration is a threat to democratic governance.

The same logic applies to the NLRB. The authors brilliantly dissect how the conservative Supreme Court has weaponized the First Amendment against labor unions in cases like *Janus*. But before these issues reach the Supreme Court, the NLRB shapes the landscape of labor rights. A progressive NLRB can aggressively interpret the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) to expand the definition of "employee" in the gig economy, protect secondary boycotts where the statute allows, and aggressively sanction union-busting employers. In doing so, the NLRB should revive the rhetoric of its New Deal architects, explicitly arguing that its aggressive enforcement is necessary to secure the "freedom of association" and the countervailing power required by a democratic republic.

The SEC provides another vital arena. The authors note the shift in corporate law toward shareholder primacy and Elizabeth Warren’s proposal for federal corporate chartering. While awaiting such sweeping legislation, the SEC has immense power to shape corporate behavior through disclosure requirements. When the SEC mandates disclosures regarding climate risk, political spending, or the ratio of CEO-to-worker pay, it faces fierce opposition from corporate lobbies who argue the agency is exceeding its statutory authority. The SEC must defend these rules not just as technical protections for investors, but as essential mechanisms for ensuring that large, publicly traded corporations remain accountable to the public and compatible with a democratic society.

Embracing administrative constitutionalism is particularly urgent now because the conservative Supreme Court is actively mobilizing to dismantle the administrative state. The Court’s conservative majority is rapidly expanding the "Major Questions Doctrine" (MQD)—a judicially invented rule stating that agencies cannot issue regulations of vast "economic and political significance" without highly specific, explicit congressional authorization. The MQD is the conservative legal movement’s newest weapon of constitutional political economy; it is designed to paralyze the executive branch’s ability to respond to modern crises like climate change or gig-economy exploitation, thereby protecting corporate power.

If progressive agencies continue to defend their actions merely through narrow, technocratic interpretations of ambiguous statutes, they will be easily swatted down by the MQD. To fight back, agencies must meet constitutional arguments with constitutional arguments. They must argue that Congress purposefully delegated broad authority to these agencies to prevent the exact kinds of economic crises and concentrations of power that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition abhors. They must assert that paralyzing the administrative state in the face of oligarchic corporate power violates the foundational constitutional imperative to maintain a functional, responsive democratic government.

The authors warn that the progressive fear of "re-constitutionalizing" claims cedes power to the courts. They are right. But failing to constitutionalize the work of the administrative state cedes power to both the courts and the corporations. The executive branch has an independent duty to interpret and defend the Constitution. By embedding the anti-oligarchy vision into the day-to-day regulatory actions, guidelines, and enforcement priorities of the federal government, progressives can create facts on the ground.

When an agency protects workers or breaks up monopolies, it provides material benefits to citizens, demonstrating the tangible value of a progressive political economy. This material success, in turn, builds the popular political support necessary to sustain the broader legislative and rhetorical movement the authors advocate. Fishkin and his colleagues have provided the historical and theoretical map. Now, progressives must use the levers of the state they currently control to start marching the territory.

  2. 02Agencies like the FTC and NLRB must ground their enforcement actions in the structural constitutional imperative to prevent monopolies and protect labor as a countervailing power to oligarchy.
  3. 03The SEC should defend corporate disclosure requirements not merely as technical investor protections, but as essential mechanisms for ensuring large corporations remain accountable to a democratic society.
  4. 04Asserting a constitutional basis for agency authority is a necessary defense against the Supreme Court’s 'Major Questions Doctrine,' which seeks to paralyze the executive branch's ability to regulate corporate power.

Vanilla Gemini

"While reviving the 'democracy-of-opportunity' constitutional tradition is an essential intellectual corrective, it will fail to achieve progressive goals unless it is coupled with aggressive structural reforms to the judiciary to strip the conservative Supreme Court of its veto power."

Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, and their co-authors offer a compelling and desperately needed diagnosis of the left’s current ideological malaise. For too long, the post-war liberal consensus has artificially severed constitutional law from political economy. By outsourcing economic policy to technocratic experts and constitutional interpretation to a specialized legal elite, progressives unilaterally disarmed. They allowed the conservative legal movement to aggressively fill the void, weaponizing the Constitution to dismantle campaign finance regulations, weaken labor unions, and constrain the administrative state. The authors’ call to revive the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition—arguing that the Constitution actively requires the elected branches to prevent oligarchy—is a potent intellectual corrective.

However, while their proposed rhetorical shift is intellectually satisfying, relying on “re-constitutionalizing” progressive politics underestimates the immediate, intractable barrier of a 6-3 conservative Supreme Court. Furthermore, it overestimates the political utility of constitutional rhetoric for the modern progressive coalition. Shifting our narrative is necessary, but without coupling this new constitutional vision with aggressive, structural reforms to the judiciary itself, the anti-oligarchy agenda will remain trapped in academic thought experiments.

The authors propose using the Constitution as a “shield” for egalitarian legislation, hoping to demonstrate to judges and the public why such laws should survive constitutional attack. But in the reality of 2022, a shield is fundamentally useless if the referees are explicitly hostile to the very concept of the defense. The conservative legal movement did not just win the rhetorical battle of constitutional interpretation; it systematically seized the machinery of the judiciary. Decisions like *Citizens United* and *Janus* are not merely the result of superior conservative arguments that progressives failed to counter; they are the result of a decades-long, heavily funded project to install movement judges who view the protection of capital and corporate speech as their primary jurisprudential duty.

Making popular, anti-oligarchic arguments outside the Court is a noble endeavor, but it will not magically sway Justices Alito, Thomas, or Gorsuch, who hold life tenure and operate insulated from the very democratic pressures the authors wish to mobilize. The authors suggest that progressives must explain to the people why constitutional challenges to laws like the PRO Act or wealth taxes should fail. But public persuasion does not automatically translate into judicial restraint. When the Court strikes down a democratically enacted wealth tax, public outrage grounded in the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition will provide little immediate comfort if the progressives lack the tools or the will to override the Court’s decree.

This brings us to the historical precedents the authors cite. They note that Jacksonian Democrats, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Deal Democrats challenged hostile courts. But these historical actors did not merely make better constitutional arguments in the public square; they wielded raw political power against the judiciary. Abraham Lincoln openly defied Chief Justice Roger Taney. Franklin D. Roosevelt won his constitutional revolution not just through rhetorical persuasion, but by explicitly threatening to pack the Supreme Court, forcing a doctrinal retreat. Today, the doctrine of judicial supremacy—the belief that the Supreme Court alone dictates the final meaning of the Constitution—is so deeply internalized by the political establishment that even modest proposals for term limits or court expansion are met with institutionalist panic.

If progressives are to take the authors’ advice and assert that elected branches have a co-equal duty to interpret the Constitution, they must be willing to engage in the kind of institutional hardball that makes that assertion real. Reclaiming a progressive constitutional political economy requires stripping the courts of their outsized veto power. Without advocating for jurisdiction stripping, court expansion, or the willingness of the executive and legislative branches to aggressively check judicial overreach, asking progressives to fight a conservative juristocracy using only the power of a "shield" narrative is sending them into a gunfight armed with a history book.

Furthermore, asking today’s progressive coalition to invest heavily in constitutional veneration carries its own political risks. The authors want to wrap progressive policies in the mantle of the Constitution, particularly regarding racial justice. But for many in the modern progressive base—especially racial justice advocates and younger voters—the Constitution is viewed with deep, justified skepticism. It is a document rooted in compromises with enslavers, originally designed precisely to protect elite property rights against the redistributive impulses of democratic majorities. Demanding that activists revere this document as a fundamentally anti-oligarchic text might ring hollow. For many organizers, making straightforward moral and material arguments for universal healthcare, labor rights, and environmental protection is vastly more effective than attempting to reverse-engineer a constitutional mandate out of a document that historically codified their subjugation.

Where the essay shines brightest is in its granular application of political economy to modern legal battles. Reclaiming the First Amendment from its current status as a tool for corporate deregulation, reviving the Brandeisian vision of antitrust law to break up monopolies, and recognizing that racial justice cannot be achieved without massive public investment are the exact policy goals the left must pursue. The authors are absolutely right that we cannot allow the right to monopolize the language of fundamental rights and constitutional fidelity.

Ultimately, Fishkin, Forbath, and their co-authors have provided a vital blueprint for *what* progressives should argue. The Constitution is indeed the charter of a republic, and vast inequality threatens the foundations of that republic. But winning the battle for the nation's political economy will require more than just recovering lost historical arguments. It will require the political courage to confront, and ultimately restructure, the unaccountable judicial institutions that currently stand in democracy's way.
