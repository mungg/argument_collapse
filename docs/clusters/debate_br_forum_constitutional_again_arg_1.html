Diversified MiniMax

"While the 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is worth retrieving, progressives must recognize that constitutional arguments are only effective when they are secondary to mass political mobilization and the building of material power."

The lead essay offers a sophisticated recovery of the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition and a compelling account of how conservatives weaponized constitutional argument against progressive goals. These are genuine contributions to a neglected strand of American legal history. But the essay's prescription—retrieving constitutional argument for progressive purposes—risks repeating the critical error it identifies: treating constitutional law as an autonomous domain. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition was never simply a matter of constitutional argument; it was embedded in mass politics and material interest. Retrieving the tradition requires recovering that embedding, not just the legal rhetoric.

The essay's intellectual history is largely sound but selectively presented. The Jacksonians, Reconstruction Republicans, and New Deal Democrats did make arguments linking democratic self-government to economic distribution. But they also made these arguments in contexts where the constitutional order itself was contested and fluid. The post-Civil War amendments dramatically expanded federal power and constitutional rights in ways that would have been unthinkable in 1860. The New Deal fundamentally transformed the relationship between government and economy. These were not business-as-usual politics with constitutional argument sprinkled on top; they were moments of constitutional transformation driven by mass mobilization and political crisis.

The current moment is different in crucial respects. The constitutional framework is more settled, and the Court has constructed doctrine that makes certain progressive interventions constitutionally vulnerable. But the essay understates how much this settlement reflects conservative organizational success rather than inherent features of constitutional interpretation. The Roberts Court did not simply discover that the ACA was unconstitutional or that the Voting Rights Act exceeded congressional power; it constructed doctrines tailored to reach these outcomes. This construction can be contested, but not by argument alone.

The essay correctly identifies that "constitutional confrontations over rival visions of our political and economic future are inevitable; courts are always engaged in such contests." But this insight cuts against the essay's strategy. If courts are always engaged in these contests, then the question is not whether to make constitutional arguments but how to build the political power that makes those arguments effective. The essay addresses this partially by discussing legislative proposals (the PRO Act, H.R. 1, public financing) that would enact progressive policy independent of constitutional argument. But it insists that these proposals be accompanied by constitutional arguments, as if the arguments were essential rather than contingent.

The three arenas the essay identifies—First Amendment, racial political economy, and antitrust—are all important, and the essay's specific proposals are generally sound. But the essay treats them as separate domains for constitutional argument when they are better understood as interconnected dimensions of a single project: building a political economy that supports democratic self-government. The First Amendment arguments about campaign finance and labor law are political economy arguments; the racial political economy arguments are constitutional in the deepest sense of engaging fundamental questions about who counts as a citizen and what participation requires; the antitrust arguments are about the structural conditions for democracy.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition was never purely legal; it was always a fusion of legal argument, political mobilization, and economic program. The Jacksonians organized mass politics around anti-bank and anti-monopoly sentiment. The Reconstruction Republicans built a coalition linking freedpeople's political participation to land reform and economic security. The New Dealers constructed a welfare state through legislation, not just constitutional argument. The constitutional arguments came afterward, providing retrospective justification for political choices that had already been made.

This suggests a different strategy for contemporary progressives. Rather than leading with constitutional argument, lead with political mobilization around concrete grievances—health care costs, monopsony power in labor markets, racial wealth gaps, corporate concentration. Build coalitions that can sustain pressure on elected officials across multiple electoral cycles. Develop policy proposals that command popular support. And make constitutional arguments in service of this mobilization, not as a substitute for it.

The essay ends by asserting that certain progressive goals "are not merely constitutionally permissible goals; they are constitutional necessities." This framing is rhetorically powerful but analytically problematic. If progressive goals are constitutional necessities, then anything that prevents their achievement is constitutionally illegitimate—including current Senate rules, the electoral college, and judicial review itself. The essay gestures toward this conclusion by criticizing "antidemocratic veto points" but does not follow through. A genuinely democratic constitutional politics would not merely argue that progressive goals are constitutionally required; it would argue that constitutional structures that prevent democratic self-government are themselves illegitimate and should be reformed.

  4. 04Progressives should prioritize organizing around concrete grievances like healthcare and labor rights to build the coalitions necessary to make constitutional claims viable.

"While reclaiming the democracy-of-opportunity tradition is intellectually sound, progressive constitutional arguments are insufficient to counter a hostile judiciary without first building the massive institutional and political power necessary to enforce them."

The lead essay diagnoses correctly that conservatives achieved constitutional supremacy partly because they made constitutional argument central to their politics, while liberals imagined constitutional law as an autonomous domain best left to lawyers. The prescription—to retrieve a democracy-of-opportunity tradition and make progressive politics constitutional again—is intellectually compelling and historically grounded. But the essay underestimates how much this strategy depends on political conditions that currently do not exist and cannot be created by argument alone.

The essay's core insight is that constitutional argument is not a separate sphere from politics but part of the broader contestation over political economy. This is true and important. But the essay does not adequately reckon with the gap between argument and institutional power. When the essay argues that "there is not only constitutional power, but a constitutional duty" to preserve democratic political economy, it assumes that this argument will land with some audience capable of acting on it. The Court has demonstrated that it will not. The political branches are constrained by filibuster rules and the composition of the Senate. The people themselves are divided by polarization and unequal access to political participation. Making constitutional arguments does not fill these gaps.

The essay identifies three arenas where progressive constitutional argument is needed: First Amendment (campaign finance and labor law), racial political economy (Medicaid, voting rights, reparations), and antitrust/corporate law. These are the right arenas. But the essay does not adequately address what happens when constitutional arguments are made and rejected by a hostile Court. The ACA narrowly survived because Chief Justice Roberts chose to uphold it on a highly strained interpretation. The Medicaid expansion was gutted. The Voting Rights Act provision was invalidated. Public financing schemes face certain challenge. Labor law reform faces certain challenge. A wealth tax faces certain challenge. The antitrust revival faces certain challenge from corporate litigants who have the resources and incentives to contest it.

In each of these arenas, the essay proposes that progressives make constitutional arguments to defend their proposals against judicial invalidation. But the constitutional arguments are not what will save these proposals. What will save them is political power: sustained majorities committed to these goals, capable of withstanding judicial invalidation through override statutes, constitutional amendments, structural reforms to the courts, or simply the passage of time and accumulation of precedent. The constitutional arguments matter, but they matter as part of a broader political strategy, not as the central element.

The essay's treatment of the PRO Act illustrates the problem. The essay celebrates this legislation as embodying the democracy-of-opportunity tradition and argues that it should be understood as exercising constitutional rights rather than merely statutory authority. This is a sound rhetorical move. But the PRO Act will face judicial challenge regardless of its constitutional grounding. The essay acknowledges that conservatives "will find First Amendment arguments against efforts to restrain employers' anti-union activity." They will also find Takings Clause arguments, federalism arguments, and whatever other doctrines they can construct. These challenges will succeed or fail based on Court composition and doctrine, not on the quality of progressive constitutional argument.

The essay concludes by arguing that progressives should "mine this rich vein of U.S. constitutional history" to prepare for "confrontations with a far-right court." This framing is apt but suggests that the goal is to win constitutional arguments before the Court. This is not a realistic strategy. The goal should be to build the political power that makes constitutional argument effective—to expand the Court, to constrain its jurisdiction, to override its decisions, or to wait it out while building durable majorities. Constitutional argument is one tool among many, valuable but not sufficient.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition the essay retrieves was effective when it was embedded in mass politics. The Jacksonians organized millions of small farmers against Eastern moneyed interests. The Reconstruction Republicans built a coalition linking formerly enslaved people to white yeomen through promises of land and citizenship. The New Dealers constructed a coalition of labor, farmers, and urban immigrants that sustained Democratic dominance for decades. These coalitions did not simply make constitutional arguments; they built political power that constitutional argument served.

Contemporary progressives face a more fragmented landscape. Labor has been decimated. The South is no longer a potential site of biracial democracy but a region of concentrated conservative power. The regulatory state has been captured by the industries it was supposed to regulate. The electoral system is structured to favor rural, white, and wealthy interests. The constitutional framework is stacked against majoritarian governance. These conditions cannot be changed by constitutional argument alone. They require sustained organizing, coalition-building, and the patient accumulation of power.

The lead essay offers a valuable contribution by recovering the democracy-of-opportunity tradition and arguing that progressives must reclaim constitutional discourse. But the essay should be read as a complement to, not a substitute for, the harder work of building political power. Constitutional arguments become powerful when they are made by people with institutional standing, coalition support, and the capacity to impose costs on those who reject them. Progressives should develop the constitutional arguments the essay proposes—but they should do so in service of broader political projects, not as the central element of a strategy that cannot succeed without them.

"While reclaiming the 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is valuable, prioritizing constitutional arguments over the development of organized political power risks providing intellectual cover for a hostile conservative judiciary and distracting from essential coalition-building."

The lead essay performs a valuable service by recovering the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition from relative neglect and arguing that progressives must reclaim constitutional discourse from conservatives. These are genuinely important interventions in an era when many on the left have ceded constitutional territory entirely. But the essay's strategy carries underappreciated risks that warrant more careful scrutiny before progressives bet heavily on "making our politics constitutional again."

The essay acknowledges the fear—that re-constitutionalizing politics cedes power to courts—but responds by asserting that *not* speaking about the Constitution cedes even more power to courts. This symmetry argument is rhetorically clever but analytically incomplete. The asymmetry between speaking and not speaking depends entirely on who is doing the speaking and in what institutional context. When progressives make constitutional arguments before a Court dominated by movement conservatives who have already committed to a particular vision of constitutional political economy, those arguments do not simply "show that all branches of government...have the authority and duty to debate what our constitutional principles require." They also provide intellectual cover for judicial striking down of progressive legislation. The Court does not simply adjudicate constitutional arguments; it selects which arguments to credit, and on the current Court, progressive arguments face an unsympathetic audience.

Consider the three arenas the essay identifies. On the First Amendment, the essay proposes public financing mechanisms, labor law reform, and campaign finance equalization that would face immediate constitutional challenge under doctrines that a conservative majority has already substantially shaped. The essay acknowledges that "novel extensions of First Amendment doctrine" will be offered against these proposals. But acknowledging this does not resolve it. If the Court is willing to strike down laws that "create more speech" (as Elena Kagan noted in dissent) because they indirectly disincentivize wealthy candidates from spending, what constitutional argument protects the essay's proposed alternatives? The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is invoked as a source of argument, but the current Court has demonstrated its ability to dismiss such arguments when they conflict with its preferred outcomes.

The antitrust arena is perhaps the most promising, because the essay correctly notes that the current antitrust consensus emerged through political contestation, not judicial determination. But this cuts both ways. If antitrust doctrine was shaped by Reagan-era ideologues and a cooperative Court, it can be reshaped through political mobilization—legislative mandates, agency appointments, and statutory reform—that does not require convincing a conservative Court to adopt a new constitutional vision. The essay itself acknowledges that neo-Brandeisians are "making a claim that Brandeis would have found obvious, but that today requires argument." But arguments in political discourse, not constitutional argument, are what will shift the regulatory agenda.

The essay's strongest section concerns the racial dimensions of economic policy— Medicaid expansion, voting rights, and the connection between social insurance and racial justice. Here the historical tradition is richest and the constitutional stakes most urgent. But even here, the path forward requires political coalition-building that the essay treats as secondary to constitutional argument. The Medicaid expansion was struck down not because progressive forces lacked constitutional arguments, but because Chief Justice Roberts had the votes. No amount of constitutional argument will recover those votes without broader changes in political alignment.

This is not to say that constitutional argument is useless. The essay is right that arguments made in politics shape arguments in courts, and that progressive forces should develop robust accounts of constitutional meaning. But these arguments are most powerful when deployed in service of, rather than as substitutes for, organized political power. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition was effective in the 1930s not because reformers made superior constitutional arguments, but because they built movements capable of threatening the political standing of opponents, making Court-packing credible, and sustaining reform across decades. The lead essay gives constitutional argument pride of place in a way that risks distracting from the harder, slower work of coalition-building and political transformation.

The essay concludes by asserting that "these are not merely constitutionally permissible goals; they are constitutional necessities." This is a powerful claim, but it proves too much. If everything progressives want is constitutionally required, then anything a Court strikes down is constitutionally forbidden—and the result is a form of judicial supremacy in which unelected judges determine what "constitutional necessities" exist. The alternative is to recognize that constitutional argument is one arena of contestation among others, valuable but not determinative. Progressives should mine the democracy-of-opportunity tradition for arguments, yes—but they should do so while building the political power that makes constitutional arguments effective.
