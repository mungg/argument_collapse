**The Real Crisis Is Economic, Not Institutional**

Ira Katznelson's elegant tour of democratic anxiety treats the legislature as the patient and prescribes more respect for representation as the cure. But this gets the diagnosis backward. The reason American citizens have lost faith in Congress, the reason European publics flirt with Marine Le Pen and Syriza, the reason Tea Partiers and Occupiers alike are convinced that "the system is rigged," is not that we have forgotten the dignity of legislation. It is that for roughly four decades, the political economy of the advanced democracies has produced stagnant wages, ballooning wealth at the top, and a generation entering adulthood poorer than its parents. The institutional symptoms Katznelson catalogues—polarization, money's grip on politics, the retreat to executive action—are downstream of this.

Consider the evidence accumulating since Thomas Piketty's *Capital in the Twenty-First Century* landed in English last year. The top 1 percent's share of national income in the United States has returned to levels not seen since the 1920s. Median household income is lower in real terms than it was in 1999. Labor's share of GDP is falling across the OECD. Meanwhile, recent work by Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page has shown that, on issues where the preferences of affluent Americans diverge from those of the median voter, policy tracks the affluent almost perfectly and the median voter not at all. This is not Claus Offe's "non-convertibility" softening at the edges; it is its outright inversion.

Katznelson nods at all this when he mentions Citizens United and voter ID laws. But he frames it as one challenge among several, and then pivots quickly to the more abstract worry about whether deliberative or plebiscitary alternatives might erode representative institutions. The pivot is revealing. It treats the institutional form as primary and the distributive content as a complication. I think the relationship runs the other way. The New Deal—Katznelson's own example of democratic rescue—did not save American democracy by perfecting parliamentary procedure. It saved democracy by delivering Social Security, the Wagner Act, federal deposit insurance, rural electrification, and a tax code that placed real burdens on the wealthy. The institutions were vindicated because they produced *material results* for ordinary people.

By contrast, the great legislative achievements of the past quarter-century—NAFTA, financial deregulation, the bipartisan reconfiguration of welfare, the Bush tax cuts, even the Affordable Care Act in its insurance-company-friendly final form—have either widened inequality or, at best, been compromises so freighted with concessions to powerful interests that they failed to feel like victories. Why are we surprised that voters tune out, gravitate toward populist outsiders, or embrace plebiscitary shortcuts? They have watched representative institutions function smoothly for thirty years while their economic situation deteriorated.

This reframing has implications for what reform should look like. Katznelson worries about "seductive substitutes" for representation and warns against Peronism. Fair enough. But the surest antidote to Peronism is not a stern lecture about the dignity of legislation. It is a representative politics that *delivers*—that taxes capital, raises wages, regulates finance, and restores some real correspondence between work and reward. The Scandinavian democracies are not immune from populist temptation, but they are notably less convulsed by it, and that is not because their parliaments are procedurally more elegant than ours. It is because their welfare states still buffer ordinary people against market shocks.

There is also a more uncomfortable point lurking here. Katznelson invokes Juan Linz on the need for "policy neutrality" and "background agreement"—the willingness to live without a singular sense of the public interest. This worked tolerably well during the postwar boom, when growth was broad-based and a rising tide really did lift most boats. Procedural neutrality is much harder to sustain when the underlying economic game is producing systematically lopsided results. People then quite reasonably suspect that "neutrality" is a euphemism for accepting the existing distribution.

I do not want to dismiss the institutional concerns Katznelson raises. Filibuster abuse, gerrymandering, the budget reconciliation maze—these matter. But fixing them in the absence of a more egalitarian economic settlement will feel, to most citizens, like rearranging furniture. If we want representative democracy to recover its legitimacy, we should worry less about whether deliberative polling will distract us from Congress and more about whether Congress is capable of writing laws that visibly improve life for the bottom three-quarters of the income distribution. That, not procedural piety, is what saved democracy the last time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The institutional symptoms of democratic decline, such as polarization and the influence of money, are downstream effects of a political economy that favors the affluent over the median voter.
  2. 02The New Deal successfully revitalized democracy not through procedural improvements, but by delivering tangible material benefits and economic security to ordinary citizens.
  3. 03Public disillusionment and the rise of populist movements are rational responses to decades of legislation that has consistently widened the wealth gap or prioritized powerful interests.
  4. 04The surest way to protect representative democracy from illiberal alternatives is to enact policies that redistribute wealth and buffer citizens against market shocks rather than focusing on procedural reforms.
  5. 05Procedural neutrality and background consensus are impossible to maintain when the underlying economic system produces systematically lopsided results that favor the elite.
