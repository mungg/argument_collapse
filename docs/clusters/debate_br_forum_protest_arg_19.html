"The current suppression of campus protest is the result of a dangerous, long-standing trend—supported by both the left and the right—that incorrectly equates subjective psychological discomfort with actionable harm."

Alex Gourevitch is right on the principle, and it is both bracing and necessary to hear it stated so bluntly. His essay is a moral call to arms for anyone who cares about the free and vibrant university: the right to protest is not a convenience we grant when it doesn’t bother us—it is a right precisely for when it does. And the recent administrative and governmental onslaught against student protesters has been a disgrace, not merely because of its cruelty but because of the way in which it has repurposed the language of safety and harm to strangle dissent. Yet the essay’s chief service is not its condemnation of the usual suspects—the Trump administration, the spineless administrators, the bad-faith conservative activists—but its unflinching examination of the progressive roots of the current repression. By tracing the logic of “feeling unsafe” back to the speech codes and deplatforming campaigns that emerged from the campus left over the past two decades, Gourevitch exposes a chilling through-line: the idea that subjective discomfort constitutes actionable harm, and that the most effective response is always to call in institutional power to silence the offending speaker.

This is a deeply unpopular argument on parts of the left, but it is essential. As Gourevitch notes, it is not that the feelings are unreal or that they should be dismissed out of hand. It is that elevating them into a legal or administrative standard is a recipe for selective enforcement. It hands the police power—literal and metaphorical—to whatever faction happens to have the most political clout with the relevant authorities. When the definition of a “hostile environment” depends on who feels threatened, the result is inevitable: those whose claims of vulnerability resonate with donors, trustees, and congressional majorities will be protected, and everyone else will be told to develop thicker skin. That is exactly the dynamic that has played out in real time, where Jewish and Israeli students (and their supporters) have been granted immense institutional solicitude while Muslim and Palestinian students, despite reporting higher levels of fear and harassment, have been met with silence and suspicion.

But it is worth pushing Gourevitch’s argument a step further, not just to defend the right to protest but to insist on the positive value of a campus where such protest is routine. The university is one of the last public-ish spaces in American life where people are forced to encounter ideas and bodies they find offensive, unsettling, or even repugnant. The erosion of that encounter—whether through hyper-policing, algorithmic sorting, or the self-segregation of residential life—is a civic catastrophe. Protest, even when it is noisy, uncomfortable, and hostile, is a precious corrective. It reminds students that the world is not arranged for their psychic comfort, and that the ideas they hold dear are not beyond challenge. That is a painful lesson, but it is precisely the kind of pain a liberal education should deliver. Without it, we produce graduates perfectly suited for a technocratic managerial order but incapable of democratic citizenship.

Gourevitch is also right that a commitment to the right to protest cannot be conditional on the content of the speech. The pro-life Genocide Awareness Project is as entitled to its grotesque visual displays as anti-war students are to their encampments. This is not a popular view in my own circles, but it is the only one that can sustain a meaningful commons. The moment we carve out exceptions for speech that we deem uniquely dangerous or hurtful, we hand the knife to the very authorities who will, in the next season, carve us out. That is not a hypothetical; it is the lived experience of the past year. The Gaza protesters are now learning what the anti-abortion protesters and the critics of Israeli policy before them learned: that the benevolent administrator who protects your feelings today is the same one who will call in the riot police tomorrow.

What, then, are the practical implications? Gourevitch calls for a restoration of modest, proportionate punishment and a return to the standards of the pre-2024 era. That is a necessary start, but it is insufficient. The problem is deeper than any single policy decision; it is a structural condition. Universities have become dependent on federal money, captivated by donor sentiment, and bloated with administrative functionaries whose careers are dedicated to risk management. A return to the old norms cannot happen without a political movement that contests that structure. That movement must include not just students and faculty but also alumni, staff, and the broader public, and it must be willing to make sustained demands that disrupt the very institutional logic that led to the crackdowns. It will not be enough to argue for the right to protest; we will have to exercise it—noisily, inconveniently, and without apology.
