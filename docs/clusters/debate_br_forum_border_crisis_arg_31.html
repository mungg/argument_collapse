Diversified Gemini

"While global class solidarity is a noble ideal, the left must prioritize practical labor market regulations and legal reforms over abstract internationalism to prevent capital from using precarious migrant labor to undermine domestic worker power."

Lea Ypi’s sweeping essay on the border crisis rightly identifies the hypocrisy of global migration policies and the profound failures of the neoliberal order. Her vision of replacing cultural conflict with global class solidarity is intoxicating, particularly for those of us in the labor movement. She is absolutely correct that capital desires "rightless workers" rather than truly free movement, and that the border often functions as a tool of labor discipline. However, from the perspective of on-the-ground labor organizing, Ypi’s analysis skips over the harsh, granular realities of building power. In her rush to condemn the social democratic compromise and call for internationalism, she underestimates the immediate, practical necessities of labor market regulation.

Ypi points to the "Make Amazon Pay" campaign as evidence that transnational labor solidarity is the way forward, arguing that working-class natives and working-class immigrants share aligned interests against economic elites. Conceptually, this is true. A worker is a worker. But conceptually aligned interests do not automatically translate into functional solidarity on the shop floor, especially when the legal and economic architecture of the state actively pits them against one another.

To understand why simply chanting "class solidarity" isn't enough, we have to look at how immigration actually impacts labor markets under our current legal regimes. When undocumented workers, or workers on highly restrictive visas (like the H-2A in the US or various temporary agricultural schemes in Europe), enter the labor market, their precarity is weaponized by employers. Because these workers face deportation if they complain, unionize, or demand minimum wage, they are inherently unorganizable under current conditions.

When Ypi dismisses the concerns of domestic workers regarding immigration as a failure of "political hegemony" and a distraction from class coordination, she sounds like someone who has never tried to organize a construction site where half the workforce is terrified of ICE or Border Force. The threat to domestic workers is not the *foreign worker* as a person; it is the *two-tiered legal system* that the foreign worker is trapped within. When a contractor can hire a crew of undocumented laborers for half the union rate, knowing they will never file an OSHA complaint, the union’s leverage is destroyed.

The social democratic politicians Ypi criticizes for recognizing this "trade-off" are not all cynical racists capitulating to right-wing hegemony. Many of them are listening to trade unionists who are watching their collective bargaining agreements be shredded by subcontractors using hyper-exploited migrant labor. You cannot build a high-wage, high-benefit social democracy in a porous labor market where capital has infinite access to a reserve army of rightless labor.

This doesn't mean the left should embrace the xenophobic border closures of the far-right. But it does mean we need a much more sophisticated approach than simply decrying borders as capitalist tools. We need a pro-labor migration policy.

What does that look like in practice in 2025? It means fighting for a system where migration is tied strictly to labor standards. First, we must end the system of employer-tied visas. If a migrant worker’s right to remain in the country is tied to a specific employer, that worker is essentially an indentured servant. Visas must be portable, allowing workers to quit abusive bosses without fearing deportation. This immediately restores their bargaining power.

Second, we need sectoral bargaining that legally binds all employers in an industry to union-negotiated wages, regardless of the immigration status of their workers. If a meatpacking plant is legally required to pay a high base wage and provide stringent safety standards, the incentive to smuggle in desperate, undocumented workers to undercut domestic labor disappears. The employer can no longer use the border as a tool for wage arbitrage.

Third, we must advocate for massive, unconditional amnesty and regularization programs for the undocumented workers already living in our countries. As long as millions of people are living in the shadows, they will be exploited by capital. Bringing them into the legal light is the only way to bring them into the union hall.

Ypi is right that we cannot separate problems of admission from problems of integration, but she jumps to the conclusion that we must therefore abandon the logic of the national labor market entirely. But the national labor market, governed by state laws, is the only arena where unions currently have the legal mechanisms to enforce contracts. Transnational strikes are inspiring, but they are episodic. Enduring labor power requires legally enforceable contracts, and those contracts require jurisdiction.

The left’s task is not to pretend that borders don’t matter, or that open migration doesn't disrupt domestic labor power. The task is to reform the legal mechanisms of migration so that foreign workers enter the country with the exact same rights, protections, and bargaining power as native workers. Only when a migrant is fully protected by the law can they become a comrade on the picket line. Until then, dismissing border enforcement as mere right-wing ideology ignores the material reality of the workers we are trying to organize.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary threat to domestic labor is not the migrant worker themselves, but a two-tiered legal system that renders undocumented or visa-restricted workers unorganizable and exploitable.
  2. 02Social democratic concerns regarding immigration are often rooted in the material reality of trade unionists seeing their collective bargaining leverage destroyed by subcontractors using hyper-exploited labor.
  3. 03To protect all workers, the left should advocate for the end of employer-tied visas to ensure migrant workers have the freedom to quit abusive bosses without fear of deportation.
  4. 04Implementing sectoral bargaining is essential to ensure that union-negotiated wages apply to all workers in an industry, removing the incentive for employers to use migration for wage arbitrage.
  5. 05Massive amnesty and regularization programs are necessary to bring undocumented workers out of the shadows and into the union hall where they can exercise collective power.
  6. 06The national labor market remains the only viable arena for enforcing labor contracts, making it necessary to reform migration laws within the state's jurisdiction rather than abandoning the concept of borders entirely.
