"While a draft could potentially reduce PTSD by limiting repeated deployments or discouraging open-ended wars, it is not a definitive solution because conscription does not eliminate the inherent trauma of combat and may even create new discipline and morale issues."

A draft might reduce one important risk factor for post-traumatic stress: repeated exposure to combat. By 2012, many soldiers and Marines had served three, four or more tours in Iraq and Afghanistan, often with too little time at home to recover, retrain and reconnect with families. Mental health researchers and military physicians have repeatedly warned that longer deployments, multiple deployments, traumatic brain injury, blast exposure, sleep deprivation and strain on marriages all compound the risk of post-traumatic stress, depression, substance abuse and suicide. If a larger pool of service members allowed the Pentagon to lengthen dwell time and limit repeat tours, some individuals would almost certainly be spared cumulative harm.

But that is not the same as saying a draft would reduce post-traumatic stress overall. PTSD is not produced only by unfair distribution of war; it is produced by war itself. If conscription simply supplied more bodies for the same missions, the country might spread trauma more evenly while creating as many, or more, total cases. Draftees would not necessarily be better prepared for counterinsurgency, repeated ambiguity, roadside bombs or the moral injuries of accidentally killing civilians or being unable to protect them. A reluctant conscript army could also have discipline and morale problems of its own, as Vietnam showed.

The stronger argument for a draft is political, not clinical. The all-volunteer force has allowed most Americans to treat Iraq and Afghanistan as distant commitments borne by a narrow slice of the country: military families, rural and working-class communities, reservists and career soldiers. If the sons and daughters of a broader public, including the affluent and politically connected, faced the possibility of deployment, Congress and the White House might be less willing to sustain open-ended wars. In that indirect sense, a draft could reduce PTSD by reducing the number or duration of wars.

Still, conscription is a blunt and unjust tool if the real goal is mental health. The immediate obligations are clearer: end missions that are not worth the human cost, expand the force only if necessary, enforce adequate time between deployments, improve screening and treatment, and stop pretending that patriotism makes repeated combat exposure harmless. A draft might democratize sacrifice, but it would not make war psychologically safe.

"While a draft might reduce individual PTSD risk by shortening deployments, it would likely increase the total number of cases by exposing more people to combat, meaning its true value lies in creating political pressure to avoid war rather than in clinical benefits."

The premise behind this question deserves scrutiny. PTSD is not primarily a function of who wears the uniform; it is a function of exposure to combat trauma, the duration and frequency of that exposure, and the support soldiers receive before, during, and after deployment. A draft, by itself, would do nothing to make war less psychologically damaging. What a draft could plausibly do is enlarge the pool of available soldiers, which in turn could shorten tours and reduce the brutal cycle of three, four, and five redeployments that have characterized service in Iraq and Afghanistan. The RAND Corporation's 2008 study found that the risk of PTSD rises sharply with cumulative months in theater and with multiple deployments. If a draft reduced that exposure per soldier, individual risk would likely fall.

But the aggregate number of PTSD cases is another matter. Spreading combat exposure across a larger population means more Americans see combat, even if each sees less of it. Vietnam, our last drafted war, produced an estimated 700,000 veterans with PTSD-like symptoms, according to the National Vietnam Veterans Readjustment Study. A draft does not magically lower the human cost of war; it redistributes it.

That redistribution is the real argument for conscription, and it is a political argument, not a clinical one. When the burden of war falls on a self-selected one percent—often from rural areas and military families—the broader public has little incentive to ask hard questions about whether a war is worth fighting or how long it should last. A draft would force those questions, and that political friction, more than any therapeutic benefit, might be what shortens deployments and limits open-ended commitments like Afghanistan. The cure for war's psychological toll is fewer wars, not more draftees.

The debate over whether a draft would reduce PTSD cases hinges on a central misunderstanding: post-traumatic stress arises from exposure to trauma, not from the fairness of who serves. While the arrest of Staff Sgt. Bales has rightly focused attention on the strain of repeated deployments, conscription would not be a psychiatric balm. In fact, it might worsen the problem.

The current all-volunteer force endures a brutal pace: multiple combat tours, extended stop-loss orders, and insufficient dwell time between rotations. This relentless cycle elevates the risk of PTSD by increasing the cumulative dose of traumatic events. A draft could theoretically widen the pool, reducing the number of deployments per individual and allowing longer recovery periods. If a conscript served one tour instead of four, his or her exposure might indeed be lower.

But this arithmetic ignores the psychological realities of coerced service. Volunteers, however strained, typically enter with a degree of buy-in and self-selection that can be protective. Conscripts, forced into danger, would arrive with higher baseline resentment, lower unit cohesion, and less commitment to the mission—all known risk factors for mental injury. The Vietnam-era draft, which exposed a generation to guerrilla warfare and an ambiguous mission, demonstrated that mass mobilization does not inoculate a force against psychological wounds; it simply distributes them across a broader demographic, while adding the moral injury of forced participation.

Moreover, a draft would not magically end multiple deployments; it would just spread them across a larger force. The total number of Americans exposed to combat could rise, potentially producing more total PTSD cases. The core issue is not the absence of a draft but the mismatch between our military commitments and the size of the standing force. Reducing the operational tempo, providing adequate mental-health resources, and honestly reassessing the length of our wars would do far more for service members’ minds than reviving conscription ever could.

"Implementing a military draft would not reduce the number of post-traumatic stress cases and would likely exacerbate the problem."

The premise that a draft would reduce PTSD rests on a comforting but mistaken assumption: that trauma is a function of frequency rather than of combat itself. The data tell a different story. Studies of Vietnam-era conscripts—men who typically served a single 12-month tour—found PTSD prevalence estimates ranging from 15 to 30 percent, comparable to or higher than rates currently observed among Iraq and Afghanistan veterans. If a single tour by a draftee produces PTSD at roughly the rate of multiple tours by a volunteer, the deployment-frequency hypothesis loses much of its force.

There are good reasons to think conscripts might fare worse, not better. Volunteer soldiers self-select for the work; they undergo extensive pre-deployment training, build cohesive unit identities, and largely believe in what they are doing. Conscripts, by contrast, are pulled from civilian life often against their will, trained in haste, deployed alongside strangers, and may regard the mission with skepticism or resentment. Unit cohesion and a sense of meaning are among the strongest known protective factors against combat trauma. A draft would erode both.

There is also a darker version of the Bales story that draft advocates ignore. Bales was a veteran of four deployments, yes, but he was also a man with a head injury, financial stress, family problems, and possibly alcohol involvement. Multiple tours did not, on their own, produce the massacre at Panjwai; a complex set of risk factors did.
