The Right Frame, the Wrong Ceiling

Jennifer Piscopo's essay is the most useful piece of writing on women's representation I've read in the suffrage centennial year, and I want to say so plainly before pushing her argument further than she takes it. Piscopo gives American feminists something we have desperately needed: a vocabulary for talking about representation as a political right rather than a personal aspiration. The "lean in and win" framework that dominates U.S. women-in-politics organizations, however well-intentioned, accepts the structural conditions of American electoral life and asks individual women to compensate for them. Piscopo's reframing — from active to passive suffrage, from negative to positive rights, from candidacy as freedom from interference to candidacy as guaranteed access — is not a rhetorical flourish. It is a complete repositioning of what equality demands.

What I want to suggest, however, is that the gender-quota model Piscopo champions is the floor of ambition, not the ceiling. If U.S. feminists adopt the "elect and be elected" framework, we should use it to ask for more than a Latin American–style quota law. Three points are worth making.

First, candidate quotas presuppose competitive party nominations as the choke point. In closed-list proportional representation systems, this is exactly right: parties compose the list, voters ratify the party. A 50/50 zipper requirement in such a system is mechanically effective. But the United States runs primarily single-member-district plurality elections with primaries that are open to anyone who can collect signatures. Piscopo acknowledges this and notes that the policy menu must adapt. I want to underline how serious the adaptation must be. A candidate quota in a U.S. primary would mean either that the party's central committee endorses two candidates, one of each sex (which gives party elites enormous new power), or that voters choose between sex-balanced finalists in a runoff (which restructures the whole nominating calendar). Neither is impossible, but neither is what gender quotas mean in Argentina or France. We need to be honest that translating "positive action" to American conditions probably requires translating the underlying electoral system as well. Multi-member districts and ranked-choice voting, both of which have been quietly gaining ground in U.S. localities, are natural partners to any serious gender-representation reform.

Second, Piscopo's essay focuses on legislatures, where descriptive representation has clear theoretical and empirical traction. But the American glass ceiling is not in Congress alone. It runs through governorships (we have nine women governors as of 2020), state attorneys general, mayoralties of large cities, and most pointedly the presidency. The right to be elected, taken seriously, has to grapple with executive offices, which by their nature cannot be quota-allocated. Some of what Piscopo recommends — party-board gender balance, parity on shortlists, public-funding incentives — is portable to executive contests. But the deeper work is cultural and institutional: dismantling the implicit assumption that the chief executive is male. The 2020 vice-presidential selection, in which Joe Biden has now chosen Kamala Harris, was made possible in part by an explicit pledge to nominate a woman. That pledge is the kind of positive commitment Piscopo's framework legitimates. We should generalize it.

Third — and this is where I want to part company with the cleaner versions of Piscopo's argument — descriptive representation is not the same as substantive representation, and the gender-quota literature has been honest about this. Argentina's quota produced more women in Congress; it did not produce, on its own, transformative gender policy. Mexico's quota expanded steadily, yet feminicide remains a crisis. Rwanda's reserved-seats system has put women in formal positions while the broader political order remains autocratic. None of this discredits the quota; it just disciplines our expectations. Numerical presence is necessary but not sufficient. Piscopo cites Anne Phillips, but Phillips herself, in The Politics of Presence, was careful to distinguish presence from policy outcomes. American advocates should not oversell quotas with the promise that more women in office mechanically produces better policy for women. That is the kind of instrumental argument Piscopo rightly worries about, because it conditions women's inclusion on their political utility.

The strongest argument, which Piscopo emphasizes and I want to amplify, is the justice argument. Women constitute roughly half the population. Their systematic underrepresentation in deliberative bodies is itself an injustice, regardless of whether their inclusion produces particular policy goods. If U.S. courts have been receptive to anything in the political-rights literature, it has been arguments grounded in equal protection. The Nineteenth Amendment's centennial is a moment to reread it not as the conclusion of a story but as a starting premise. The Amendment guaranteed that the right to vote shall not be abridged on account of sex. The interesting interpretive question — and one that current jurists have barely touched — is whether, a century later, persistent and dramatic underrepresentation in elected bodies itself constitutes an abridgment of that right's promise.

I would add one consideration Piscopo treats only briefly: the political violence problem. She mentions in passing that some countries now classify sexist harassment of women politicians as political violence. This is one of the most significant legal innovations of the past decade, pioneered by Bolivia and Mexico. In the United States, where the harassment of women officeholders — from local school board members to members of Congress — has intensified with the rise of social media and the partisan radicalization of the past five years, this is not a peripheral concern. Women cannot meaningfully exercise the right to be elected if exercising it routinely entails death threats, doxxing, and stalking. American law treats this as an individual safety question, if it treats it at all. The Latin American framework treats it as a structural violation of political rights. That alone would be a transformative import.

Piscopo ends with cautious optimism, citing Emily Zackin's argument that positive rights are familiar to American state constitutions even if absent from the federal one. I share that optimism, with one caveat. The most successful positive-rights mobilizations in U.S. state constitutional history — for education, for labor protections, for environmental rights — succeeded because they had committed coalitions willing to pursue a multi-decade strategy of litigation, lobbying, and ballot initiatives. The women's representation movement in the United States does not yet have that infrastructure. EMILY's List and its cousins are election-cycle organizations. The Latin American activists Piscopo describes spent twenty or thirty years building the legal and rhetorical groundwork for quotas. If U.S. feminists want what they have, they need to be prepared to build for the long haul, beyond any single candidate or any single election.

The centennial is a useful prompt, but the project Piscopo invites us into is generational. The question is whether American feminism, accustomed to thinking about representation in the language of individual ambition and electoral cycles, can shift to the slower, harder vocabulary of constitutional rights. It can. The Latin American example proves it. We just have to start.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Implementing gender quotas in the United States would require a fundamental overhaul of the electoral system, such as adopting multi-member districts and ranked-choice voting, to address the unique challenges of the primary system.
  2. 02The framework for women's representation must extend beyond legislative bodies to executive offices, requiring both institutional pledges and a cultural dismantling of the male-as-chief-executive assumption.
  3. 03Advocates should prioritize the justice-based argument for numerical presence over instrumental claims about policy outcomes, as descriptive representation does not automatically guarantee substantive policy changes.
  4. 04The U.S. should adopt the Latin American model of treating the harassment and political violence directed at women officeholders as a structural violation of political rights rather than a private safety issue.
  5. 05Achieving these reforms requires U.S. feminist organizations to shift from short-term election-cycle mobilization to a long-term, multi-decade strategy of constitutional and legal advocacy.
