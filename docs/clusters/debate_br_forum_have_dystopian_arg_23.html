"Daron Acemoglu’s framework for redirecting AI is insufficient because it fails to address the unique economic and social realities of the care sector, which is the fastest-growing and most labor-intensive part of the modern economy."

What about the Care Economy?

Daron Acemoglu's essay is a contribution I welcome, but reading it I was struck by a curious absence. For 6,500 words about the future of work and shared prosperity, there is almost nothing about the largest, fastest-growing, and most labor-intensive sector of the modern economy: care work. Education gets a paragraph. Healthcare gets a paragraph. Childcare, eldercare, social work, home-health aides, and the entire infrastructure of social reproduction get almost nothing. This is a serious gap, because it is precisely in care work that the choices Acemoglu wants us to make about AI will be most consequential—and where the binary of "automation" and "augmentation" is most likely to mislead us.

Consider the demographics. The U.S. workforce is aging. By 2030, one in five Americans will be over 65. Demand for home-health aides is projected to grow by more than a third over the next decade, making it the largest source of net job growth in the entire economy. Childcare and pre-K education are similarly expanding, both because labor-force participation by mothers continues to rise and because we are belatedly recognizing the developmental importance of the first five years. The jobs that AI will or will not "augment" over the next twenty years are disproportionately care jobs. Whatever we decide—or fail to decide—about AI in these sectors will shape the working conditions and wages of tens of millions of workers, most of them women, and many of them women of color.

Acemoglu's framework, oriented as it is toward "productivity" and the "labor share of value added," struggles to handle care work for a fundamental reason: care work is not productivity-improvable in the same way that manufacturing is. The "output" of a home-health aide is a frail elderly person who feels cared for, eats regular meals, takes their medications, and avoids hospitalization. The "output" of a kindergarten teacher is a class of five-year-olds who learn to share, to attend, to read. These outputs are not amenable to the kind of scaling that defines productivity growth in goods production. As William Baumol pointed out half a century ago, sectors like these face a "cost disease": their relative cost rises over time because their productivity cannot keep pace with the rest of the economy, while their wages must rise enough to retain workers who could go elsewhere.

This has two implications that Acemoglu's essay does not address.

First, the labor share framework is the wrong lens for care work. In a sector where productivity gains are intrinsically limited, the labor share will always be high, but this tells us nothing about whether the work is being done well or whether workers are being paid fairly. Currently the labor share in U.S. home-health care is very high, but home-health aides earn a median wage of less than $13 per hour, frequently with no benefits and no scheduled hours. The labor share is misleading because the value the workers produce is captured by households (in the form of below-market-rate services) and by Medicare and Medicaid (in the form of suppressed reimbursement rates). What matters in care is not the labor share but the wage level and the quality of working conditions, and these are set by labor law, immigration policy, public funding, and union density, not by the labor-displacing or labor-augmenting character of technology.

Second, AI in care work raises questions that the automation-versus-augmentation binary cannot resolve. Consider a "smart" home-monitoring system that tracks an elderly person's movements, medication compliance, and vital signs. Is this automation (replacing the human aide who would otherwise check in) or augmentation (giving the aide better information about her client)? The answer depends entirely on how it is deployed. If the system replaces visits, it is automation, and the elderly person loses the human contact that is, for many, the most valuable part of the aide's visit. If it supplements visits, it is augmentation, and the aide can focus her time on what matters most. The same technology, two opposite outcomes—determined by reimbursement rules, staffing decisions, and labor power, not by the technology itself.

A serious AI agenda for care work would start with a different question than Acemoglu's: not "how do we redirect research toward labor-augmenting applications?" but "how do we make care work into work that is dignified, decently paid, and meaningfully supported by technology?" The answer would involve several pieces that the essay does not address.

It would require public investment in care infrastructure. The American Jobs Plan and the American Families Plan currently under discussion include hundreds of billions of dollars for home- and community-based services, universal pre-K, and childcare subsidies. These are exactly the kinds of investments that, combined with sectoral wage standards, would transform care work from precarious low-wage employment into something resembling the "good jobs" Acemoglu admires. Without this public funding, no AI redirection will matter, because the demand for care will continue to be met by underpaid, often immigrant, often informal labor.

It would require sectoral bargaining for care workers. Domestic Workers United and the National Domestic Workers Alliance have spent two decades trying to bring home-care workers under standard labor protections, often against the resistance of employer interests and against the legal exclusion of domestic workers from the National Labor Relations Act. A revitalized labor framework for the care economy would establish industry-wide wage floors, training standards, and collective bargaining rights. This is the closest analog to the postwar industrial unions that Acemoglu invokes, and it is the appropriate model for a 21st-century care workforce.

It would require regulation of care-tech specifically. As AI penetrates eldercare and childcare, we will face questions about whether algorithmic monitoring substitutes for human attention, whether facial-recognition and emotion-detection systems in classrooms are appropriate, and how to balance the privacy of care recipients against the efficiencies of monitoring. The current regulatory framework barely exists. Acemoglu's general worry about surveillance applies with particular force in care settings, where the people being monitored often cannot consent meaningfully (because they are children, or have dementia, or are economically dependent on the household where the system is installed).

It would require a feminist reframing of the productivity debate. The reason care work has been underpaid and undervalued for so long is not a market failure or a technological accident; it is that care has historically been done by women, often within the household, as unpaid labor. When it has been brought into the formal economy, it has been brought in at correspondingly low wages. The story Acemoglu tells about postwar prosperity tends to elide this: the male breadwinner with the good union job was supported by an unpaid female workforce doing care, household management, and emotional labor. A 21st-century version of shared prosperity has to internalize that workload as paid work, with social respect and decent compensation. This is a project not just of redirecting AI but of revaluing labor that has long been invisible.

I do not mean to suggest that Acemoglu would disagree with any of this; my guess is that he would endorse most of it. My point is that the essay's framing—productivity, labor share, automation versus augmentation—is poorly suited to the sector where most of the action will actually be. We need a vocabulary for thinking about care work and technology that does not treat it as a residual category. Without that, "redirecting AI" will mean redirecting toward the sectors where Acemoglu's framework applies most easily—manufacturing, logistics, white-collar professional services—while the largest and most consequential transformation of work happens in care, in the dark.

The future of work is the future of care work. Any AI agenda that does not put care at the center is missing the main event.
