**The Quiet Revolution Deserves a Louder Reception**

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer have written a careful, almost understated, manifesto for what is genuinely the most important methodological shift in development economics in a generation. Reading their essay, one is struck not by the boldness of any single claim, but by the cumulative force of dozens of small, well-documented findings: that a six-dollar uniform changes a girl's life trajectory; that a lentil bribe quintuples immunization rates; that a chlorine dispenser at a spring outperforms years of social-marketing campaigns. The temptation, having read this, is to nod politely and move on. That would be a mistake. The implications are larger than the authors let on.

For half a century, development economics oscillated between grand theories. Rostow's stages, Prebisch's structuralism, the Washington Consensus, the "Big Push" revival under Sachs—each was sold as the master key, and each, in turn, was discredited by the embarrassing variance of actual experience. The reason these debates never closed is that they could not. Cross-country regressions, however clever the instrument, will never settle whether aid works or whether institutions cause growth, because the units of observation are too few, too heterogeneous, and too endogenously linked. We have argued in circles because the evidence permitted nothing better.

Randomized evaluations break that circle in one specific but vital domain: the design of programs. We may never know, with certainty, whether Ghana should liberalize its capital account. But we now know, with something close to certainty, that deworming pills in western Kenya raised school attendance dramatically at a cost of pennies per child. That is not a small thing. It is the kind of knowledge on which a profession can build.

The critics will say—indeed they have said, loudly—that all this micro-knowledge tells us little about the truly large questions of growth, structural transformation, and political order. There is something to this. No one has yet randomized a constitution. But the critics overstate the dichotomy. Glennerster and Kremer's essay quietly demonstrates how the accumulation of small findings can produce something resembling a general theory. The pattern that emerges—that the poor, like the rich, are sensitive to small upfront costs, that procrastination and present bias are not features of affluence but of humanity, that convenience and default options matter everywhere—is not a trivial observation. It is the foundation of a more honest behavioral picture of poverty than anything Sachs or Easterly has offered in their dueling polemics.

I would push the authors further on one point, however. They are too modest about what their findings imply for ideology. The user-fees debate, which they treat almost as a technical matter, is in fact one of the most consequential policy fights of the past twenty years. The World Bank and the IMF, through structural adjustment, pushed user fees on health and education across sub-Saharan Africa during the 1980s and 1990s on the explicit theory that prices ration scarce resources toward those who value them most. Glennerster and Kremer have evidence that this theory is straightforwardly wrong for preventative health and basic schooling. Those who pay are not those who use; those who use are not those who benefit; and even modest fees collapse demand. This is not a marginal refinement. It is a refutation, and one with millions of lives in the balance. The authors might have said so more plainly.

A second area where they could be bolder concerns the supply side. Their essay focuses, naturally, on consumer behavior, because that is where most of the early experimental work has been done. But the same logic—that small incentives, defaults, and information shifts produce large behavioral changes—surely applies to the providers of health and education as well. Teacher absenteeism in India, which Kremer himself has studied, is not a mystery of culture but a predictable response to incentive structures. The same camera-monitoring experiment that increased teacher attendance could, in principle, be replicated and refined across dozens of bureaucratic contexts. The agenda is not exhausted; it has barely begun.

There is one critique of the randomista program I do find compelling, and which Glennerster and Kremer address only obliquely: the risk of measuring what is measurable rather than what matters. Diarrhea cases averted are countable; civic capacity, political voice, and the long-run trajectory of an institution are not. If we only fund what we can evaluate, we will accumulate evidence about the things at the bottom of Maslow's hierarchy and remain agnostic about everything above. That is a real cost, and the field needs to wrestle with it more openly. But it is a reason to expand the methodological toolkit, not to retreat from rigor.

Finally, a word on the Stellaluna ending, which I confess I find more powerful than its sentimentality should allow. Development economics has long carried a residue of orientalism—an assumption that the behavior of poor people in poor countries is governed by exotic cultural variables that "we" must learn to respect. There is a kinder version of this view and a crueler one, but both share the premise of fundamental difference. The accumulated evidence Glennerster and Kremer summarize suggests something less flattering to the field and more humane in implication: the Kenyan farmer who wants a commitment device looks rather like the American graduate student who pays for a gym membership she will not use. Both procrastinate, both respond to defaults, both undervalue future health relative to present convenience. We are not so different. Recognizing this should make development policy less paternalistic in tone and more behaviorally serious in design.

The randomista revolution is not the answer to every question in development. But it is finally producing knowledge that accumulates rather than dissipates, that survives the next ideological cycle, and that translates directly into programs that save lives at startling cost-effectiveness. That is more than the field has accomplished in any previous decade. Glennerster and Kremer deserve credit for describing it modestly; the rest of us should be willing to celebrate it loudly.

  4. 04The behavioral insights gained from consumer studies should be more boldly applied to improving the performance and accountability of service providers.

"The most significant contribution of the randomized-evaluation movement is not any specific finding, but the creation of a cumulative, iterative research infrastructure that allows development economics to function like a mature science."

**Iteration as a Public Good**

Most of the commentary on the randomized-evaluation movement, including some of the criticism the movement has earned, focuses on what individual trials can or cannot prove. I want to suggest that this framing misses the most important contribution of the work Glennerster and Kremer describe. The deepest payoff of the experimental turn is not any particular finding. It is the construction of a cumulative, iterative research apparatus — something development economics has not previously possessed, and something that may, over time, matter more than any of the specific results it produces.

To see the point, consider the counterfactual. Before the experimental wave, development knowledge accumulated in a peculiar pattern. Big ideas — import substitution, structural adjustment, microfinance, the millennium villages — would arrive with great enthusiasm, get implemented at scale, generate ambiguous results, and eventually be displaced by the next big idea, often without anyone establishing definitively whether the previous one had worked. The pattern repeated every decade or two. Each cycle left behind some adherents, some skeptics, a great deal of folklore, and very little settled knowledge. Disagreements about whether the prior idea had failed because it was wrong in principle or wrong in execution were unresolvable, and so the field oscillated between fashions rather than converging on understanding.

The experimental turn breaks this pattern, not primarily because any single trial proves anything, but because the methodology supports incremental refinement. A study finds that free bed nets are taken up more than partially subsidized ones. A follow-up study examines whether the free recipients actually use them, and whether their use of free nets predicts later willingness to pay. A third study tests whether the social-norm effects spill over to neighbors. A fourth tries a delivery mechanism that combines free distribution with reminders. Each study is narrowly designed, each is open to critique, but the sequence is producing something that did not exist before: a structured body of knowledge in which subsequent investigation builds on, and corrects, prior findings.

This is what mature sciences do. Medicine reached this stage roughly a century ago. Agricultural science reached it earlier. Development economics, despite its long history, did not — not because its practitioners were less capable, but because its methods did not permit accumulation. The experimental turn is, finally, providing the missing infrastructure. The chlorine-dispenser program Glennerster and Kremer describe is itself a product of this iteration: an intervention designed in light of prior findings about price sensitivity, convenience, social norms, and waste, then tested rigorously, then refined. This is not a one-off study. It is the visible output of a research community that has learned how to learn.

This iterative capacity has implications for how we should think about external validity, the standard critique of the program. Critics ask: does a finding from Kenya transfer to Bangladesh? The honest answer is sometimes yes and sometimes no, and we cannot always tell in advance. But the iterative apparatus is itself the response. If we have built a research infrastructure capable of running a careful trial in Bangladesh next year, the question of transfer becomes empirically tractable in a way it was not before. The complaint that "you cannot generalize from one context" loses much of its force when the same methodology can be deployed across many contexts, with each new deployment refining our understanding of which features of context matter. We are building a map. Each trial is a survey point. The criticism that any one point fails to describe the whole territory is correct but beside the point.

The contrast with earlier development debates is striking. Consider the perennial argument about whether aid works in the aggregate. Sachs says yes, Easterly says no, decades pass, neither side moves. Cross-country regressions, sensitive to specification, cannot resolve the question. The aid effectiveness literature has consumed extraordinary intellectual resources and produced very little knowledge that anyone trusts. By contrast, the experimental literature on, say, conditional cash transfers has produced robust knowledge in less than two decades: we know that CCTs increase school enrollment, we have good estimates of the magnitudes, we understand how the effects vary with program design, and we can compare cost-effectiveness across delivery mechanisms. This is real progress. It does not answer the aggregate aid question, but it answers a specific operational question with a confidence that cross-country regressions can never provide.

I would push the authors further on one implication of all this. The infrastructure of the experimental program — the partnerships with NGOs, the trained field staff, the data systems, the protocols for IRB approval, the platforms like J-PAL and IPA — is itself a global public good. Maintaining it requires sustained investment, and the case for that investment is stronger than the case for any individual study. Donors and governments often think about evaluation as an add-on to programs. They should think about it as core infrastructure, like a statistical agency, that requires ongoing support whether or not any particular trial produces a politically convenient finding.

I would also urge more attention to the meta-question of how the field synthesizes its findings. As the number of trials grows, the bottleneck shifts from running trials to interpreting them collectively. Systematic reviews and meta-analyses become essential. So does a culture that treats null findings and failed replications as valuable rather than as failures. The experimental movement has not yet fully developed this culture; it remains too easy to publish positive results and too hard to publish their qualifications.

Glennerster and Kremer's essay is admirably free of triumphalism. They acknowledge what they do not know. They emphasize iteration and humility. I would only add that the iteration itself is the achievement worth defending most fiercely, against critics from the left who want grander narratives and critics from the right who want simpler verdicts. Knowledge that compounds is rare in social science. We have begun to build it in development. That is worth more than any of its individual products.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The experimental approach breaks the historical cycle of 'big-think' fads by allowing for incremental refinement and the building of a structured body of knowledge.
  2. 02The iterative apparatus makes the problem of external validity empirically tractable by allowing researchers to systematically test and refine findings across different contexts.
  3. 03Experimental methods produce robust, operational knowledge and settled understanding in a way that traditional cross-country regressions and aggregate aid debates cannot.
  4. 04The institutional infrastructure required for these evaluations, such as research platforms and field protocols, should be viewed and funded as a global public good.
  5. 05To reach its full potential, the field must shift its focus toward the synthesis of collective findings and foster a culture that values null results and replications.
