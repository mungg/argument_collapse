"Public skepticism toward GMOs is not merely a product of scientific illiteracy or fear, but a rational response to the institutional and political frameworks governing biotechnology."

The controversy over genetically engineered food is often described as a battle between science and fear. Pamela Ronald’s essay largely accepts that frame. There is truth in it: many claims about GMOs are exaggerated, and some are plainly false. But if advocates of biotechnology want public trust, they must understand that trust is not produced by evidence alone. It is produced by institutions that deserve confidence.

Ronald is correct that there is no scientific basis for treating all genetically engineered crops as dangerous. The method is not the hazard. A gene conferring virus resistance in papaya raises different questions from a trait conferring herbicide tolerance in soybeans. Major scientific organizations have concluded that foods now on the market are not inherently riskier than conventionally bred foods. The discredited rat-tumor study promoted by Gilles-Éric Séralini should not be treated as equal to a broad literature of better-designed research.

But public suspicion does not arise only from ignorance of molecular biology. It arises from experience with power. Citizens have seen tobacco companies manipulate science, chemical companies minimize pollution, pharmaceutical firms conceal unfavorable results, and financial regulators miss obvious dangers. They know that “no evidence of harm” can mean either genuine safety or inadequate scrutiny. When the same companies that profit from a technology fund studies, lobby regulators, and fight labeling, skepticism is not irrational. It may be poorly directed, but it is understandable.

Ronald notes that one-third of the studies in a safety database are not industry-funded. That is reassuring as far as it goes, but the number also reveals the problem. If two-thirds are connected to interested parties, then public science is under-resourced. We should not have to rely so heavily on companies to generate knowledge about products from which they profit. The appropriate response is not to reject the science, but to build more independent science.

This is where the vaccine analogy becomes unhelpful. Anti-vaccine activists reject an intervention with overwhelming public health benefits and thereby endanger others through loss of herd immunity. GMO skepticism is more heterogeneous. Some people fear imaginary health effects; others object to herbicide regimes, seed patents, market concentration, inadequate regulation, or the loss of democratic control over food systems. Lumping all these concerns together as anti-science may rally the already convinced, but it will not persuade the doubtful.

There is also a moral difference between saying “this crop is safe to eat” and saying “this agricultural system is good.” The first is a toxicological and nutritional claim. The second involves ecology, economics, law, and justice. A glyphosate-tolerant soybean may be safe for consumers while still contributing to weed resistance or corporate concentration. A Bt cotton plant may reduce insecticide poisoning while still raising questions about seed price and farmer debt in particular regions. These are not pseudoscientific questions. They are governance questions.

The best path forward is neither blanket rejection nor cheerleading. It is responsible innovation. That means case-by-case assessment, independent testing, transparent data, post-market ecological monitoring, and public participation before deployment. It means asking not only “Can we insert this gene?” but “Who benefits, who bears risk, who controls the seed, and what alternatives are being neglected?”

Labeling is a useful example. Ronald argues that GMO labels imply a warning where none is warranted. She has a point. A label that functions as a scarlet letter can misinform. But refusing disclosure can also corrode trust. A neutral process label, accompanied by serious public education, might be less damaging than prolonged political warfare. People often want to know how food is produced for ethical, environmental, or religious reasons, not only for immediate health risk. The challenge is to disclose without stigmatizing.

Scientists also need humility about prediction. Seventeen years without documented adverse health effects is meaningful. It is not omniscience. Ecological effects such as resistance can take time and depend on adoption patterns. Socioeconomic effects are even more contingent. The proper scientific attitude is confidence proportionate to evidence, combined with willingness to monitor and revise.

Ronald’s opening story of Mama Moses is compelling because it reminds us that precaution cuts both ways. Blocking a disease-resistant banana may impose severe risks on poor farmers. Inaction is not neutral. But introducing a crop through unaccountable institutions can impose risks too. The ethical question is not whether to be cautious, but how to be cautious about both action and inaction.

A better public conversation would separate three claims. First, genetic engineering as a technique is not inherently unsafe. This is well supported. Second, some engineered crops have delivered real environmental and economic benefits. This is also true. Third, therefore opposition to particular GM crops, labeling demands, or critiques of agribusiness are irrational. That does not follow.

Trustworthy biotechnology will require trustworthy politics. Public breeders should be funded. Regulatory agencies should be insulated from industry capture. Data should be open to independent review. Farmers should participate in setting research priorities. Companies should accept stewardship obligations. Activists should stop spreading bogus health scares. And scientists should stop assuming that better facts alone will settle disputes about power.

The truth about GMOs is not only in the genes. It is in the institutions that decide which genes matter, whose crops are improved, whose knowledge counts, and who gets to choose.
