"Rather than passively accepting terrorism as an inevitable reality, society must address the domestic social alienation and foreign policy failures that create the conditions for extremism to thrive."

Before asking whether we can “live with terrorism,” we should ask whose lives are being organized around the term. In Paris, Nice and Brussels, terrorism is experienced as a shocking rupture. In Baghdad, Kabul, Maiduguri, Beirut and many Syrian towns, mass death has become appallingly routine. Western governments helped create, arm, invade, neglect or misunderstand parts of this landscape, and then often discuss terrorism as though it has fallen from the sky.

That does not excuse the murderer in Nice. Nothing about Iraq, Syria, colonial history or discrimination justifies driving a truck through families watching fireworks. Moral clarity about the crime is essential. But policy clarity requires a wider lens. If “living with terrorism” means accepting occasional massacres at home while continuing policies that generate chaos abroad and alienation at home, then it is not realism. It is avoidance.

The Islamic State grew out of a shattered Iraq and a Syrian catastrophe the world failed to stop. Western airstrikes may be necessary against its military capacity, but bombs cannot by themselves produce legitimate politics in Mosul, Raqqa or Sirte. Nor can European security services solve the problem if prisons and impoverished suburbs continue to serve as recruiting grounds for men who feel no stake in the country around them. France’s republican model promises equal citizenship, but too many French citizens of North African or Muslim background encounter discrimination in employment, policing and housing. Extremists exploit this gap between promise and experience.

The danger now is that France will respond to Nice by narrowing the circle of belonging. The state of emergency, raids and expanded police powers may be justified in specific cases, but they also carry costs. When extraordinary measures become normal, they can teach an entire generation that the republic sees them first as threats. That is not only unjust; it is strategically foolish.

Living with terrorism should mean living with the consequences of our choices. It should mean admitting that there is no purely domestic solution to a transnational crisis, and no purely military solution to a political one. It should mean investing in schools, jobs and fair policing as seriously as in intelligence databases. It should mean diplomacy to end wars, not just rhetoric about civilization.

The public understandably wants protection now, and governments must provide it. But a permanent emergency cannot substitute for a long-term strategy. If we merely harden borders, expand surveillance and bomb more targets, we may reduce some plots while replenishing the anger on which jihadist movements feed. The goal should be not to “live with” terrorism, but to stop helping produce the conditions in which it thrives.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Domestic social and economic discrimination against minority communities creates a sense of alienation that extremist recruiters exploit.
  3. 03Relying solely on military force and expanded police powers is insufficient because these methods do not address the underlying political and social roots of radicalization.
  4. 04A permanent state of emergency and aggressive security measures risk further marginalizing vulnerable populations and fueling the cycle of anger.
  5. 05A long-term strategy against terrorism requires prioritizing diplomacy, social investment, and equitable domestic policies over purely reactive security measures.

"Society should not simply accept terrorism as an inevitable reality, but should instead actively eliminate it by addressing the foreign policy failures and domestic social marginalization that fuel radicalization."

When Prime Minister Valls tells us to “live with terrorism,” he is unwittingly asking us to accept the consequences of a broken foreign policy. What we are living with is not an immutable hazard like earthquakes, but the blowback from decades of Western military intervention, from the bombing of Libya to the disastrous occupation of Iraq, from the arming of Syrian rebels to the tacit support for Saudi extremism. France, in particular, has intervened in its former African colonies and across the Middle East while largely failing to integrate its own Muslim citizens. The result is a conveyor belt of radicalization that will not stop until we change course.

It is not an apology for terrorism to connect the dots. The Islamic State did not emerge from a vacuum. It grew in the chaos of post-invasion Iraq and the power vacuum left by the destruction of the Libyan state in 2011 — a campaign in which France played a leading role. The weapons and ideology flooding into Syria have been nurtured by regional allies whose human rights abuses we overlook. At home, the economic marginalization of Muslim communities, rampant discrimination, and the banlieue ghettos provide fertile ground for recruiters. The young man who drove a truck through Nice was a Tunisian resident with a history of petty crime and family violence; he was not an external invader but a product of a fractured society.

“Living with terrorism” therefore becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy so long as we refuse to address the root causes. We can harden every soft target, extend every state of emergency, and still lose because the ideology finds new adherents faster than we can kill them. A more effective strategy would begin by winding down military entanglements that fuel anti-Western sentiment, and by redirecting those resources toward massive investments in education, integration, and community policing. We should stop labeling all criticism of foreign policy as treason and start listening to the diplomats and intelligence analysts who have warned for years that the blowback cycle is real.

This is not a call for appeasement. It is a call for a smarter, long-term counterterrorism that treats jihadi violence as a symptom of deeper political and social failures, rather than a permanent curse to be endured. The “clash of civilizations” narrative only serves the extremists on both sides. France, with its proud tradition of republican universalism, has the intellectual resources to build a society where a young Tunisian-Frenchman can see himself as a full citizen, not a potential murderer. That is the only way we stop living with terrorism and start overcoming it. Valls should have said: we will change the conditions that breed terrorists, not just brace for their attacks.

  4. 04A more effective counterterrorism strategy requires winding down foreign military entanglements and redirecting resources toward education and community integration.

"Society should not passively accept terrorism as an unchangeable reality, but should instead actively reduce it by addressing the political conditions and social grievances that fuel extremist recruitment."

The phrase “live with terrorism” risks treating terrorism as weather: tragic, unpredictable and essentially beyond politics. But terrorism is political violence. It grows in particular conditions, feeds on particular conflicts and is strengthened or weakened by choices governments make. We should not “live with” it as though nothing can be changed.

This does not mean excusing the murder of civilians in Nice, Paris, Brussels, Istanbul, Baghdad or Orlando. The person who drives a truck through families watching fireworks is responsible for that act. But responsibility for an act is not the same as explanation of a phenomenon. If Europe wants fewer attacks, it must look beyond police powers after the fact.

Part of the answer lies abroad. The wars in Iraq, the collapse of Libya, the Syrian catastrophe and the rise of the Islamic State have created a theater of grievance, recruitment and training. Western governments did not cause every aspect of this crisis, and jihadist movements have their own ideology and agency. Still, repeated military interventions without stable political settlements have helped produce the chaos in which such movements thrive. Bombing may sometimes be necessary to stop armed groups, but bombing cannot be the whole strategy.

Part of the answer lies at home. France’s republican model promises equal citizenship, yet many citizens of North African or Muslim background experience discrimination in housing, jobs and policing. The banlieues are not factories of terrorism; most people who live there reject violence and want ordinary dignity. But alienation creates openings for recruiters who say democracy is hypocrisy and belonging is impossible. Every indiscriminate raid, every proposal to mark Muslims as culturally suspect, helps that lie.

That is why civil liberties are not sentimental luxuries. They are counterterrorism tools. Due process, equal treatment, freedom of worship and protection against collective blame make it easier for citizens to cooperate with police and harder for extremists to claim that Muslims can never be fully French. If the answer to terrorism is a permanent emergency, then the extremist narrative has won a powerful exhibit.

Society will have to live with some risk; no policy can abolish fanaticism or individual cruelty. But it should not live with the conditions that magnify them. A better response would combine precise policing with investment in neglected communities, serious anti-discrimination enforcement, prison reform, mental health services, and a foreign policy less addicted to military solutions.

The choice is not between naïve tolerance and ruthless security. The choice is between a politics that reacts to each atrocity with fear and a politics that reduces the supply of future recruits. Refusing to undermine our freedoms is not weakness. It is part of how terrorism is defeated.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Western military interventions in the Middle East have created a vacuum of chaos and a theater of grievance that allow jihadist movements to thrive.
  2. 02Domestic social alienation and systemic discrimination against Muslim citizens provide extremists with a narrative to claim that democratic belonging is impossible.
  3. 03Protecting civil liberties and avoiding collective blame are essential counterterrorism tools that encourage community cooperation with law enforcement.
  4. 04A comprehensive strategy against terrorism must prioritize long-term political and social solutions, such as anti-discrimination enforcement and prison reform, over purely military or emergency measures.
