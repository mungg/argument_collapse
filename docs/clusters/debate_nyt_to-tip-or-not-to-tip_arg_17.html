"While more restaurants should experiment with service-included dining to challenge tipping culture, it is not a universally applicable business strategy and requires broader policy reform to be sustainable."

As a principle, service-included dining is appealing. As a business strategy, it is treacherous. That is why the answer should be: yes, more restaurants should experiment with it, but no, we should not pretend that every restaurant can simply do what Sushi Yasuda has done.

A well-known Midtown sushi restaurant with a distinctive clientele has advantages that a neighborhood diner, family Mexican restaurant or sports bar does not. Its customers may be more willing to accept a cultural explanation, a higher check average and a formal no-tipping policy. In a more price-sensitive setting, a 20 percent increase on the menu can look like a price hike even when the final bill is unchanged. Customers compare menu prices before they compare all-in costs.

There is also the problem of coordination. If one restaurant pays full wages and builds service into prices while its competitors keep lower menu prices and rely on tips, the reformer may look expensive. The same dynamic traps many decent employers: they can improve conditions only to a point before the market punishes them. That is not an argument against change; it is an argument for policy.

The federal tipped minimum wage of $2.13 an hour is indefensible. Raising it would do more for restaurant workers than asking a handful of individual owners to redesign American dining culture one menu at a time. Stronger enforcement of wage laws, clearer rules on service charges, and more transparent tip pooling would also help. If society wants restaurant workers to have stable incomes, society should not leave the matter entirely to voluntary experiments by high-end establishments.

Still, experiments matter. Sushi Yasuda may help customers understand that service has always been part of the cost of a meal. Other restaurants should watch carefully: What happens to staff income? Turnover? Customer satisfaction? Repeat business? The goal should be neither nostalgia for tipping nor romantic faith in service charges. It should be a compensation system that works for workers, customers and businesses. Getting there will require more than a note at the bottom of the bill.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01High-end restaurants like Sushi Yasuda have a specific clientele that is less price-sensitive than customers at neighborhood diners or family restaurants.
  2. 02Restaurants that eliminate tipping risk appearing more expensive than competitors because customers often compare menu prices rather than total all-in costs.
  3. 03Individual business experiments cannot replace the need for systemic policy changes, such as raising the federal tipped minimum wage and strengthening wage law enforcement.
  4. 04The success or failure of these experiments should be used as data to develop a compensation system that balances the needs of workers, customers, and business owners.
