The tax code’s mixed message is not a moral statement about marriage so much as the predictable result of trying to satisfy incompatible goals. We want progressive taxation; we want married couples with the same combined income to pay the same tax; and we want two people to owe the same amount whether they marry or not. All three cannot be achieved at once. Joint filing with progressive rates inevitably creates bonuses for some couples and penalties for others.

Still, the current arrangement is hard to defend. A one-earner couple often receives a marriage bonus because joint brackets allow the earner to spread income over two people. A two-earner couple, especially when both spouses have similar incomes, can be pushed into higher brackets or lose deductions and credits. For low-income families, the problem can be worse: the Earned Income Tax Credit, Medicaid, housing assistance and other phaseouts can combine to make marriage financially costly. That is a particularly perverse signal, because these are households least able to treat tax penalties as symbolic.

The better approach is to stop using marriage as the central tax unit. The income tax should move much closer to individual filing: two workers should not pay more simply because they marry, and a household should not get a large bonus merely because one spouse stays out of the labor market. Concerns about children and caregiving should be addressed directly, through child credits, dependent-care credits, and support for low-income workers, not indirectly through a marital status preference.

This would not eliminate every complication. Married couples do share resources, and a spouse who provides unpaid care is contributing real economic value. But those facts do not require a system that penalizes second earners, who are often women, or that treats marriage as a tax shelter for one-earner households. Congress could also reduce the harshest penalties by widening brackets for couples with two earners and smoothing benefit phaseouts, especially in the EITC.

As marriage law itself is being reconsidered, the tax system should aim for neutrality. Government may recognize marriage, but it should not make the decision to marry a tax gamble.

"The United States should transition to an individual-based tax system to eliminate the marriage penalty and ensure that marriage remains fiscally neutral."

The tax code's mixed message on marriage is the artifact of trying to satisfy three goals that cannot all hold at once: that the system be progressive, that couples with equal household income pay equal tax, and that a couple's tax bill not change when they marry. Pick any two and you must abandon the third. The United States chose the first two in 1948, when joint filing was introduced, and the marriage penalty (and bonus) is the price.

The 2001 and 2003 tax cuts, made permanent in this January's fiscal cliff deal, narrowed the penalty considerably in the lower brackets by setting the married-filing-jointly bracket thresholds and standard deduction at exactly twice the single levels. But the penalty reappears sharply in the upper brackets — particularly now that the new 39.6 percent rate kicks in at $400,000 for singles but only $450,000 for couples — and it lurks throughout the code in phase-outs for the EITC, education credits, the new Medicare surtax, and the PEP/Pease revival. For low-income working couples, the EITC penalty can run into thousands of dollars and is genuinely a disincentive to marry; for high earners, it's an annoyance but unlikely to change behavior.

The cleanest fix is the one most other OECD countries have already made: tax individuals, not households. Each spouse files on his or her own earnings, with transferable allowances for dependents. This eliminates both the penalty and the bonus, removes the secondary-earner's high marginal rate (a well-documented drag on married women's labor supply), and treats marriage as fiscally neutral — appropriate in an era when two-earner households are the norm and the state is being asked to recognize a wider range of marital arrangements.

The objection is lost progressivity at the household level: a surgeon married to a homemaker would pay less than two surgeons earning the same total. That's a real tradeoff, but a defensible one.

The U.S. tax code sends a bewildering signal: if you both work, you’ll likely pay a marriage penalty; if only one does, you’ll probably get a bonus. This mixed message doesn’t make sense. Designed for a postwar breadwinner-homemaker norm, the code now clashes with the reality of dual-earner families, which account for most married couples. It actively discourages work and penalizes marriage for those who need two incomes most.

The mechanics are simple. Joint filing gives married couples rate brackets less than twice as wide as singles’. Two incomes thus get pushed into higher marginal rates than if each partner filed singly. By contrast, a single-earner couple effectively transfers the non-working spouse’s unused lower brackets onto the earner, creating a tax shelter. The result is a hidden tax on working wives, which research has long shown reduces women’s labor supply—a perverse outcome when families are straining and the economy needs all hands on deck. The penalty can cost dual-earners thousands of dollars.

A better approach is individual-based taxation. Every adult would file on their own income, regardless of marital status, instantly ending both penalty and bonus and making the tax code marriage-neutral. The United States is an outlier here: most OECD countries already tax individuals separately. To soften the impact on single-earner couples with unequal incomes, we could keep a refundable dependent credit or a transferable personal allowance that phases out at higher levels—preserving progressivity without distorting who marries or who works. Such a system would also simplify filing, ending the need to calculate taxes both ways.

Critics may claim joint filing honors a communal view of marriage, but in practice it breeds complexity, invites gaming, and punishes working couples. Marriage should be about commitment, not an I.R.S.-driven financial calculation. A shift to individual taxation would be a far cleaner, fairer reform, respecting both individual autonomy and the way families actually live. It’s time to stop using the tax code to pick winners among family structures.

"The United States should eliminate the marriage penalty and simplify the tax code by abandoning joint filing in favor of taxing every adult as an individual."

The so-called "marriage penalty" is really a symptom of a deeper design problem: the United States tries to tax both individuals and households at the same time, and the two goals are mathematically irreconcilable under a progressive rate schedule. You cannot simultaneously have (1) progressive rates, (2) equal taxation of married couples with equal combined incomes, and (3) tax liability that is unaffected by marriage. Pick any two and you must sacrifice the third.

Since 1948, we have chosen the first two, and the result is that two earners who marry often pay more than they did as singles, while a single-earner couple gets a windfall from "splitting" the breadwinner's income across two bracket schedules. The Bush-era reforms widened the lower brackets and standard deduction for joint filers to roughly twice the single amounts, which softened the penalty for most middle-income couples. But it kicks in hard at higher incomes, in the phase-outs of credits like the EITC, and now in the new 3.8 percent investment surtax and 39.6 percent top bracket created by ATRA.

The cleanest fix is to abandon joint filing and tax every adult as an individual, as most OECD countries already do. Sweden, the U.K., and Canada moved this direction decades ago, and they found that individual filing increases labor-force participation among married women, who currently face very high effective marginal rates as "secondary earners" stacked on top of their husbands' income. The CBO and Congressional Research Service have repeatedly noted this distortion.

Individual filing would also end the absurd situation in which the IRS has to know, and care, about your romantic life. It would simplify withholding, reduce audit complexity around innocent-spouse relief, and remove the federal tax code from the same-sex-marriage debate currently before the Supreme Court in Windsor. Whatever the Court decides about DOMA, the underlying issue — that the federal government writes thousands of rules turning on marital status — would be diminished.

The objection, of course, is "horizontal equity": shouldn't two couples with the same combined income pay the same tax? Perhaps, but that principle assumes the married couple is the natural taxable unit, which is increasingly out of step with how Americans actually live. Nearly half of adults are unmarried; cohabitation is common; and dual-earner households are the norm. The tax code should catch up. Tax people, not marriages.

The marriage penalty debate is usually framed in gender-neutral language about "dual-earner couples" and "single-earner couples," but the reality is gendered, and we should say so. The penalty falls hardest on wives, and the bonus rewards husbands whose wives stay home. The tax code, in other words, still subtly nudges American families toward the Ozzie-and-Harriet template, more than half a century after most women began working for pay.

Here is the mechanism. Under joint filing, the second earner's income is stacked on top of the first earner's, so her first dollar of wages is taxed at his marginal rate. For a woman married to a man earning $80,000, taking a $30,000 part-time job means her earnings are taxed starting at 25 percent — plus payroll tax, plus phased-out credits, plus child care. Economists have long shown that secondary-earner labor supply is far more elastic than primary-earner labor supply, meaning these high effective rates actually deter work. The Congressional Budget Office has flagged this; so has the OECD in comparing the U.S. with peer countries that have moved to individual filing.

Meanwhile, the marriage bonus — which gets less press than the penalty — flows to households where one spouse out-earns the other dramatically. A doctor whose wife stays home pays substantially less under joint filing than he would as a single man with the same income, because his earnings are "split" across two bracket schedules. The tax code is, in effect, paying him to keep his wife out of the workforce.

This is not just inefficient; it is regressive and outdated. Lower-income families almost always need two earners to make ends meet, while the income-splitting bonus is most valuable to high-income families who can afford a stay-at-home spouse. We have built a structure that subsidizes the family arrangements of the affluent and taxes those of everyone else.

The remedy is to file taxes individually, as the United Kingdom, Sweden, and most of continental Europe now do. Each adult would face her own bracket schedule, regardless of whom she married. The marriage penalty disappears. The marriage bonus disappears. And — not incidentally — the federal government stops underwriting a particular vision of who should be earning the family's income. With the Supreme Court currently reconsidering what marriage even means for federal purposes in Windsor, this is exactly the right moment to ask whether marriage should drive tax liability at all.
