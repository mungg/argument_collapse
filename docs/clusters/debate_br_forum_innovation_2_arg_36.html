Diversified Gemini

"While Breznitz correctly identifies the flaws of the Silicon Valley model, his focus on local economic prosperity ignores the reality that reclaiming all stages of industrial production is now a matter of national security and geopolitical survival."

Dan Breznitz’s dissection of the Silicon Valley myth is a timely and necessary intervention. By mapping out the four stages of innovation—novelty, design, component innovation, and assembly—he provides a lucid framework for understanding how globalized production actually functions. However, writing in the fall of 2021, amidst a global pandemic that has shattered supply chains and exposed the fragility of the international order, Breznitz’s prescription for "local inclusive prosperity" feels dangerously naive. He treats the four stages of innovation as a menu from which local communities can freely choose, optimizing for jobs and regional wealth. But innovation is not merely a matter of local economic development; it is a matter of national security and geopolitical survival.

Breznitz correctly notes that the United States has largely surrendered Stage 3 (second-generation components) and Stage 4 (production and assembly) to nations like Taiwan and China. He frames this as an economic mistake that led to inequality, as the U.S. focused too narrowly on the high-end, VC-backed Stage 1. While the inequality argument is sound, it misses the far more urgent crisis: we have hollowed out our industrial base to the point of profound strategic vulnerability.

Consider the semiconductor industry, which Breznitz highlights as a triumph of Taiwan’s focus on Stage 3 innovation. He praises TSMC and the network of local suppliers that have generated thousands of jobs. But what he ignores is the strategic consequence of this concentration. Today, the world is entirely dependent on a small handful of facilities in Taiwan for the advanced microchips that power everything from our smartphones to our fighter jets, medical equipment, and the entire automotive industry. As tensions escalate in the Taiwan Strait, the fact that the U.S. outsourced this crucial component innovation is no longer just a failure of "inclusive prosperity"—it is a critical national security threat. The ongoing chip shortage of 2021, which has idled auto plants across the American Midwest, is a glaring preview of what happens when a nation loses its Stage 3 and Stage 4 capabilities.

Similarly, the early days of the COVID-19 pandemic revealed that the United States lacked the capacity to rapidly produce basic personal protective equipment, active pharmaceutical ingredients, and critical medical devices. China’s dominance in Stage 4 assembly meant that when borders closed and supply chains seized up, the U.S. was left scrambling for basic necessities.

Breznitz points to Operation Warp Speed and the funding of SiO2 Materials Science for vaccine vials as an example of how the government can support "tech teens." He is right to praise this, but we must take the lesson much further. Reclaiming industrial capacity cannot be left to the whims of local communities trying to find a niche in the global market. It requires a massive, coordinated, and unapologetic federal industrial policy.

We are currently debating legislation in Congress—such as the United States Innovation and Competition Act (USICA) and the CHIPS for America Act—that aims to do exactly this. The goal is not simply to foster local economic well-being, but to physically rebuild the entire stack of innovation within our borders or the borders of allied nations. The U.S. must re-integrate the stages of innovation that globalization tore apart.

When Schwinn outsourced its production and eventually went bankrupt, as Breznitz details, it lost more than just factory jobs; it lost "its ability to innovate altogether." This phenomenon, known as "industrial commons" decay, proves that you cannot cleanly separate Stage 1 design from Stage 3 and 4 production. When manufacturing moves offshore, the engineering and design capabilities inevitably follow. You cannot iterate and improve on a product if you are not deeply involved in making it. By allowing Stage 4 to move to Shenzhen and Stage 3 to Taiwan, the U.S. is slowly losing its grip on Stage 1 and 2 as well.

Therefore, the paradigm of "picking a stage" for local prosperity is obsolete. The federal government must use subsidies, tariffs, massive R&D investments, and guaranteed procurement contracts to force the re-shoring of critical supply chains. If this leads to higher prices for consumer goods in the short term, that is a necessary premium we must pay for resilience and security.

Breznitz is correct that the Silicon Valley model is insufficient. But his alternative—a peaceful, globalized division of labor where every region finds its happy place on the supply chain—is a relic of the 1990s end-of-history consensus. We have entered an era of great power competition and systemic environmental shocks. Innovation policy must now be subordinate to national resilience. We need to rebuild our capacity to invent, design, iterate, and manufacture all under one roof, not just for the sake of local jobs, but to ensure that the nation can withstand the crises of the 21st century.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The outsourcing of component innovation and assembly has created profound strategic vulnerabilities, as evidenced by the semiconductor shortage and the lack of domestic medical supplies during the pandemic.
  2. 02The 'industrial commons' decay proves that Stage 1 design cannot be separated from Stage 3 and 4 production, as losing manufacturing eventually erodes a nation's ability to innovate at any level.
  3. 03Rather than allowing local communities to choose a single stage of innovation, the federal government must implement a coordinated industrial policy to re-shore the entire innovation stack within domestic or allied borders.
  4. 04Innovation policy must shift from a focus on globalized division of labor to a focus on national resilience, prioritizing the ability to withstand systemic shocks over short-term consumer costs.
