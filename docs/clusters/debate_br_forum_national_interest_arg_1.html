"The United States should adopt a strategy of reformed selective engagement that maintains active global leadership and alliances while exercising greater diplomatic discipline and restraint, rather than retreating to an offshore balancing approach."

Stephen Walt is surely right about the first principle: American power is a precious national asset, not a toy to be displayed or a solvent that can dissolve every political problem. The United States is strong enough to make grave mistakes and survive them, but not strong enough to make them cost-free. Iraq has already taught that a brilliant campaign can be followed by a grinding occupation, and that legitimacy, intelligence, language, and local politics are not luxuries appended to military power; they are part of power itself. Walt is also right that preventive war is a dangerous doctrine when elevated from an exceptional possibility into a general rule. A superpower that announces a right to strike when and where it chooses should not be surprised when others seek deterrents or when allies become anxious.

But Walt’s proposed cure—offshore balancing—risks learning too much from Iraq and too little from the broader history of American success. The problem with the Bush administration’s first term was not that America remained too engaged in the world. It was that engagement was too often impatient, unilateral, and inattentive to the political foundations of consent. The answer is not to make America “hard to get,” but to make American leadership more disciplined, more legitimate, and more reliable.

Offshore balancing sounds prudent because it promises selectivity. In practice, however, the line between prudent restraint and dangerous absence is hard to draw before the crisis arrives. Walt says the United States should rely on local actors to preserve regional balances, intervening only when they fail. But local actors often fail precisely because they doubt American staying power or because they fear abandonment more than domination. In Asia, for example, the American presence does not merely prepare for a possible future balance against China; it also dampens the security competition among Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China. A reduced or ambiguous American commitment could encourage Japan to remilitarize faster, South Korea to hedge, Taiwan to take risks, or China to test limits. The American role is not simply a tripwire against hegemony; it is part of the political architecture that prevents miscalculation.

The same is true, though differently, in Europe. Walt is correct that Europe no longer faces the Red Army, and the United States should expect wealthy democratic allies to bear more of the burden. But NATO has not been merely a military garrison. It has been the principal mechanism by which Germany’s power was embedded, Europe’s security dilemmas were softened, and new democracies in Central and Eastern Europe were anchored to the West. A drastic American drawdown might not produce immediate catastrophe, but it would weaken an institution that has magnified American influence at modest cost. The United States should not keep troops abroad out of habit, but neither should it treat forward presence as a mere imperial footprint. Sometimes presence is reassurance, and reassurance is cheaper than rescue.

Walt’s strongest case concerns the Middle East. The United States should not aspire to run the region, and the occupation of Iraq shows how quickly liberation can become entanglement. Yet even here, offshore balancing is too tidy. For decades Washington did rely on regional pillars—Iran under the Shah, then Iraq against revolutionary Iran, then conservative Arab monarchies—and the results were mixed at best. Balancing from offshore did not spare us from association with autocracy, oil politics, radical resentment, or war. Nor is it clear that a lighter American footprint by itself would drain anti-Americanism. Bin Laden’s indictment of the United States includes troops in Saudi Arabia, but also sanctions on Iraq, support for Israel, support for Arab regimes, and the cultural and political influence of the West. Some grievances can be reduced by wiser policy; others will persist because they are rooted in ideological hostility to pluralism, secular law, and American society itself.

On Israel and Palestine, Walt is right that unconditional American support for settlement expansion is bad for America and bad for Israel. No serious friend of Israel should pretend that permanent occupation can be reconciled with democracy, Jewish self-determination, and Palestinian political rights. The Clinton parameters remain the best available outline: two states, security guarantees, territorial compromise, a divided or shared Jerusalem, and a realistic solution to refugees. Washington should be prepared to press both sides much harder. But Walt understates Palestinian agency and the political difficulty created by terrorism. The second intifada, suicide bombings, and the collapse of trust after Camp David and Taba have profoundly shaped Israeli opinion. Pressure on Israel must be matched by unmistakable demands that Palestinian leaders dismantle terrorist organizations, end incitement, and build accountable institutions. With Arafat gone and a new Palestinian leadership emerging, there may be an opening. It will be lost if America appears simply to switch clients rather than insist on reciprocal obligations.

Walt’s nuclear argument is also persuasive in part. The United States cannot credibly preach nonproliferation while implying that nuclear weapons are the ultimate badge of security and status. Reducing excessive arsenals, securing Russian materials, strengthening inspections, and shutting down illicit networks should be central tasks. But nonaggression pledges to regimes such as North Korea and Iran are not magic keys. These governments seek nuclear capabilities not only because they fear America, but because nuclear weapons enhance regime prestige, regional leverage, and domestic legitimacy. A grand bargain is worth exploring, especially through multilateral talks, but it must be backed by verification, sanctions for cheating, and a willingness to contain if diplomacy fails.

The deepest issue is democracy promotion. Walt rightly rejects democracy by invasion. Still, the United States should not retreat to example alone. The democratic revolutions in Eastern Europe, the enlargement of NATO and the European Union, support for civil society, election monitoring, broadcasting, and pressure on dictators have all mattered. Even in the Middle East, the desire for accountable government is not an American invention. The Iraqi election just held, despite violence and Sunni alienation, reminds us that many people will risk death to vote. We should be humble about what that means, but not cynical.

The better grand strategy is reformed selective engagement: preserve alliances, reduce unnecessary military exposure, renounce preventive war as doctrine, repair legitimacy, invest in diplomacy and intelligence, and use American power with patience. Primacy will endure not because America is offshore, but because others believe its presence is preferable to the alternatives. That requires restraint, yes—but also steadiness.

"The United States should adopt a strategy of offshore balancing to preserve its primacy by husbanding its resources and intervening only when vital regional balances of power are directly threatened."

Stephen Walt’s central claim is both unfashionable and necessary: American power is most useful when it is held in reserve. In Washington, “leadership” has come to mean visibility, activism, and the constant display of resolve. Walt reminds us that a great power can lead in another way—by setting priorities, husbanding resources, and letting others carry burdens when their own interests are more directly engaged. That is not isolationism. It is strategy.

The strongest part of Walt’s argument is his insistence that primacy is a wasting asset if it is used promiscuously. The United States entered the post–Cold War era with advantages unmatched in modern history: no peer competitor, friendly neighbors, control of the seas, a dynamic economy, extraordinary universities, and a network of alliances built over decades. The question after 1991 was not whether America could knock over hostile regimes. Of course it could. The question was whether doing so would make the United States safer at tolerable cost. Iraq has supplied the answer. The U.S. military destroyed Saddam Hussein’s regime with astonishing speed, but the war’s political aftermath has exposed the limits of American power. Armies can seize capitals; they cannot easily manufacture legitimacy, reconcile sectarian communities, or erase nationalist resentment.

Offshore balancing is attractive because it begins with geography. The United States is not Prussia, France, Russia, or China. It is protected by oceans and bordered by weak or friendly states. It does not need to garrison the world to survive. It needs to prevent hostile domination of three key regions: Europe, East Asia, and the Persian Gulf. That goal has historically required American intervention only when local balances fail. In both world wars, the United States entered late, not because it was indifferent, but because it could afford to wait until the danger became unmistakable. During the Cold War, the Soviet Union’s power and ideology made permanent forward deployments more plausible. But the Cold War is over. A strategy designed for containing the Red Army in central Europe should not be treated as a permanent feature of nature.

Walt is also right that other states do not see American power as Americans see it. We tend to regard our power as benign because we know our own intentions. Others judge by capabilities, proximity, and experience. When U.S. officials reserve the right to overthrow regimes that might someday threaten us, they should not be surprised if threatened regimes seek the one deterrent that appears to work: nuclear weapons. North Korea’s lesson from Iraq is not subtle. A country without a credible deterrent may be invaded; a country with one receives negotiations. If we wish to reduce proliferation, we must reduce the incentives to proliferate. That does not mean trusting Kim Jong Il or the Iranian clerical regime. It means recognizing that their fear of American power is part of the problem.

Still, Walt’s essay is stronger as a diagnosis than as a full prescription. Offshore balancing can sound cleaner in theory than it is in practice. When exactly do local balances fail? How much warning will we have? How do we reassure allies sufficiently that they do not acquire nuclear arsenals of their own, while also persuading them to do more for themselves? A distant balancer must maintain credibility despite distance. If Japan or South Korea concluded that the United States was becoming “hard to get” in the wrong sense, the result might not be a healthier regional equilibrium. It might be a nuclear Northeast Asia.

The same problem arises in the Persian Gulf. Walt is right that the United States should not occupy the region or attempt to transform it by force. But a pure reliance on local balancing has often been difficult there because the local states are weak, divided, and internally brittle. The Gulf monarchies want American protection but fear American visibility. Iraq under Saddam was a threat to Kuwait, Iran, and Saudi Arabia; Iran’s size and revolutionary history worry its neighbors; Saudi Arabia’s internal politics pose their own dangers. A successful offshore strategy would require not simply withdrawal, but a careful architecture of access agreements, naval presence, intelligence cooperation, and rapid deployment capability. The term “offshore” should not obscure the amount of diplomatic work such a posture requires.

Walt’s discussion of Israel and Palestine is likely to provoke the sharpest objections, but his basic strategic point is hard to dismiss. The United States has allowed its Middle East policy to be identified with Israeli preferences to a degree that harms American interests and, arguably, Israel’s long-term interests as well. No serious peace will be possible without Israeli security, but no lasting peace is possible while Palestinians remain stateless, humiliated, and fragmented by settlements and barriers. The Clinton parameters remain the closest thing to a plausible settlement: two states, borders based on 1967 lines with swaps, a divided or shared Jerusalem, security guarantees, and a negotiated refugee solution. The United States should not pretend that settlement expansion is compatible with such an outcome. If American aid gives Washington leverage, then refusing to use that leverage is a choice.

Yet here, too, Walt may understate the political and moral complexity. Israel is not merely another small state. It is a democracy born after genocide, surrounded for decades by enemies, and still facing terrorism deliberately aimed at civilians. To say that American and Israeli interests are not identical is correct; to imply that pressure on Israel is the master key to Muslim opinion is too simple. Anti-Americanism in the Arab and Islamic world also draws on authoritarian propaganda, social stagnation, resentment of modernity, and internal political failures. Solving the Palestinian question would help enormously. It would not transform the region overnight.

Where Walt is most persuasive is on legitimacy. The United States cannot win a struggle of ideas while appearing indifferent to law, civilian casualties, and the opinions of friendly publics. Legitimacy is not a decorative extra; it is a force multiplier. The 1991 Gulf War succeeded not only because American arms were superior, but because Iraq had plainly committed aggression, the coalition was broad, and the war aim was limited. Kosovo was more controversial, but even there the United States acted with allies after exhausting diplomacy. Iraq in 2003 lacked that legitimacy. The cost has been measured not only in dollars and lives, but in trust.

A mature great power should know the difference between strength and restlessness. It should not confuse every hostile regime with Hitler, every crisis with Munich, or every doubt with appeasement. Nor should it imagine that restraint is weakness. Restraint is often what makes power sustainable. Walt’s offshore balancing needs refinement, especially in Asia and the Gulf. But its animating insight is right: America’s primacy will last longer if others see it as tolerable, predictable, and limited. The United States should be powerful enough to act when it must, wise enough not to act when it need not, and patient enough to let history do some of its work.

"The United States should reject offshore balancing in favor of a disciplined liberal internationalism that maintains active global engagement to address non-state threats and preserve international institutions."

Stephen Walt has written a serious essay that deserves a serious reply, and on most of its critique of the Bush administration's record I find myself nodding along. The preventive-war doctrine, the contempt for institutions, the rhetorical recklessness of the "axis of evil," the captured intelligence process, the catastrophic post-war planning in Iraq, the failure to invest political capital in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict — all of this is real, and historians will be unkind. Where I part company with Walt is in the alternative he proposes, which I think misdiagnoses what kind of world we live in and what kind of country we have become.

Walt's grand strategy is realism in its classical, Mearsheimer-influenced form: states are the primary actors, power is the primary currency, regional balances of power are the primary mechanisms of stability, and the United States should husband its strength by intervening only when concentrated hostile power threatens vital interests. This was a workable framework for the nineteenth century and not a bad one for the Cold War. It is incomplete for the world we now inhabit.

Three features of the contemporary international system trouble Walt's framework. First, the principal threats to American security do not come from concentrations of state power in strategic regions. They come from the intersection of weak states, transnational networks, and dangerous technologies. Walt acknowledges this in his discussion of nuclear terrorism — indeed, he identifies it as the gravest threat we face — but he does not seem to register that this threat is essentially unbalanceable. There is no offshore equivalent of A.Q. Khan's network. There is no regional ally we can prop up against an unsecured Russian nuclear depot or a leaking Pakistani enrichment facility. Dealing with these threats requires precisely the kind of intrusive, sustained, multilateral engagement that Walt's strategy tends to minimize.

Second, the legitimacy and effectiveness of international institutions are not free goods. They require investment, leadership, and presence. Walt rightly notes that the United States gains by working through the UN, NATO, the WTO, and other bodies. But these institutions are not vending machines into which we can deposit coins of legitimacy and receive coalitions in return. They reflect, embody, and produce shared expectations about how power will be exercised. An "offshore" America that intervenes only when its direct interests are threatened, that lets regional powers handle their own problems, will gradually lose its capacity to shape the rules under which everyone operates. China and India will fill the vacuum. The institutions Walt values will atrophy without American oxygen.

Third, the connection between domestic political character and international behavior matters more than Walt allows. He treats democracy promotion as a quixotic neoconservative obsession that produces only resentment. But the deepest reason most Americans have supported active engagement abroad since 1945 is not crusading idealism but a sober assessment that democracies are easier to live with, less likely to harbor catastrophic terrorists, more likely to honor agreements, and more likely to develop the kinds of economies that integrate with our own. The democratic peace literature is hardly airtight, but it is not a fantasy. The Bush administration has implemented democracy promotion badly — at the point of a gun, with insufficient understanding of local conditions, conflated with regime change — but the underlying impulse is sound and importantly American. Walt's framework has nothing to say about how to encourage liberalization in places where we have leverage. He treats it as someone else's job.

I would also push back on Walt's characterization of "selective engagement." His criticism is that it was "not selective enough" — that we kept too many troops in Europe, that we tried to do too much. I think the better criticism is the opposite: selective engagement was inconsistent. It produced the genuine successes of the 1990s — German reunification, NATO enlargement, the Dayton Accords, Kosovo — but it also produced Rwanda, the early Clinton-era flailing in Somalia and Haiti, and a long, slow drift in policy toward Iraq that never quite resolved itself. The lesson is not that we were too engaged but that we were not strategically engaged enough.

There are aspects of Walt's program I unambiguously support. The "grand bargain" approach to nonproliferation seems exactly right: we cannot expect other states to constrain their nuclear options while we develop new warheads and walk away from our Article VI obligations. Mohamed El Baradei's cigarette analogy stings because it is accurate. A serious nonproliferation strategy requires the United States to demonstrate, in word and deed, that it is winding down its dependence on these weapons, not enhancing it.

I also support Walt's instinct that we have neglected the Israeli-Palestinian question at grave cost. The Bush administration's outsourcing of that conflict to Sharon — with the "road map" gestured at and then abandoned — has been a strategic disaster. We should be applying serious, sustained, and yes, sometimes uncomfortable pressure to both parties. The Clinton parameters remain the rough outline of any feasible settlement.

But I find Walt's framing of the Israel question politically reckless and analytically reductive. The idea that ending the occupation would dissolve Arab anger or pull the rug out from under bin Laden mistakes a real grievance for the central grievance. Bin Laden's quarrel with the United States is older than Sharon's premiership and broader than Palestine. Solving the conflict is a moral and strategic necessity in its own right. We should not oversell what it will buy us in the war against jihadism.

Finally, Walt's call for greater self-restraint and humility is one I endorse, but it lands strangely against his confident prescriptions about how the Middle East balance should be managed, how Israel should be pressured, how the nuclear bargain should be structured. There is more than a whiff of the foreign-policy intellectual telling the world how it should run while criticizing other foreign-policy intellectuals for telling the world how it should run.

What we need is not a return to offshore balancing but a more disciplined liberal internationalism: serious about institutions, serious about democracy as a long-term project, serious about non-state threats, willing to use force but reluctant to do so unilaterally or without consent. That tradition runs from Acheson through Kennan through Nye. It is harder to caricature than either neoconservatism or realism, which may be why it is currently out of favor with the publishing class. It is, nonetheless, the framework most likely to keep us safe.

"While Stephen Walt’s 'offshore balancing' is a step in the right direction, it does not go far enough because it still maintains an imperial premise that keeps the United States unnecessarily entangled in foreign regions."

Walt's essay is the most useful thing written about American grand strategy since September 11, and the conventional foreign-policy community will accordingly try to ignore it. Let me say something different: Walt does not go far enough.

The "offshore balancing" position Walt defends is plausible, well-argued, and represents what passes for radicalism inside the universe of think-tanks and quarterlies that takes American primacy as its baseline. But Walt accepts too much of the imperial premise. He still wants air and naval bases in Asia. He still wants the capacity to swing into the Persian Gulf when necessary. He still wants to maintain a balance against China, which is to say, to organize East Asian security around containing a country that has not attacked us and does not threaten us. He still describes the United States as having "vital interests" in three regions — Europe, industrialized Asia, and the Persian Gulf — that he is willing to defend by force.

This is offshore balancing only by comparison with the maximalist neoconservative project. Compared with any sane reading of the actual American national interest, it is still imperial in scope, still places our citizens on the front lines of other people's quarrels, and still treats the entire globe as a chessboard on which American military planners must have a pre-positioned piece.

Consider the Persian Gulf. Walt wants us to ensure that no single power dominates the region. Why? The pat answer is "oil." But oil is a commodity, and commodities flow according to price. Whoever controls Gulf oil — Saddam's Iraq, Khomeini's Iran, a future Saudi government in jihadist hands — will want to sell it, because oil unsold is worthless rock. The economic argument for the Carter Doctrine never really held up under examination, and it holds up even less in a world of diversified suppliers, where Gulf oil represents a declining share of global production. We have spent fifteen years and trillions of dollars defending an interest that markets would defend for free.

The strategic argument is no stronger. Walt himself notes that the "dual containment" policy of the 1990s helped produce al Qaeda. He understands the role of American basing in Saudi Arabia in radicalizing bin Laden. Yet he proposes to continue the practice in slightly leaner form. The lesson of the last fifteen years is not that we should run a more elegant version of the Carter Doctrine. The lesson is that the Carter Doctrine itself was a mistake, that our forward presence in the Gulf manufactures more enemies than it neutralizes, and that the cheapest and safest course is the one Walt cannot quite bring himself to recommend: leave.

Consider East Asia. Walt wants to maintain "cooperative security partnerships" with our Asian allies as the foundation for a future containment of China. But China is not the Soviet Union. It has no expansionist ideology, no fifth columns in foreign capitals, no doctrine of global revolution. It has territorial disputes with its neighbors — Taiwan, the Senkakus, the Spratlys, parts of the Indian border — that are local in scope and not directly threatening to the United States. By organizing East Asian security around containment-in-waiting, we virtually guarantee the rivalry we claim to be hedging against. Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, and India are perfectly capable of balancing China among themselves if and when balancing becomes necessary. Our presence is not a stabilizer; it is a tripwire that converts any local dispute into a potential great-power war.

Consider Europe. Here Walt is closest to right, but he still proposes to remain in NATO and to maintain "small contingents" for symbolic purposes. Why? Europe is wealthier than the United States in aggregate, technologically sophisticated, and faces no significant external threat. Russian power is a shell of what it was. The European Union has perfectly adequate means to defend itself if its members chose to invest in doing so. The reason they have not is that we have generously subsidized their security for sixty years, freeing them to build welfare states while we shouldered the burden of armaments. The kindest thing we could do for European maturity is to end the subsidy.

Walt's resistance to a more thoroughgoing retrenchment is rooted, I think, in a belief that the world will descend into chaos without American forward presence. This is the great unexamined assumption of the entire foreign-policy establishment. It has been repeated so often that it has become axiomatic. But it is empirically untested. We do not actually know what regional dynamics in Asia, Europe, or the Middle East would look like without American garrisons, because we have not seen such a world since 1945. What we do know is that the costs of our presence are real, measurable, and rising: in dollars, in lives, in attention diverted from genuine domestic priorities, in radicalization of populations that resent our military footprint.

There is also a constitutional argument that Walt does not engage. The American republic was not designed for empire. Standing armies, permanent overseas commitments, executive war-making, vast intelligence bureaucracies operating outside meaningful oversight — these are not the institutional furniture of a healthy democracy. They corrode the very polity they purport to defend. The Founders knew this; they warned us repeatedly; we forgot. Walt's offshore balancing does not address this corrosion because it does not seriously challenge the size or scope of the national security state. A genuinely realist policy would have to.

I do not propose isolationism in the caricatured sense — closed borders, autarkic economics, indifference to atrocity. I propose what John Quincy Adams proposed: America the well-wisher of liberty everywhere, the champion of her own only. Robust diplomacy, open trade, generous humanitarian assistance, principled rhetoric in defense of human rights — and no garrisons, no permanent alliances, no automatic tripwires. If a hostile great power ever emerges that genuinely threatens this continent, we have the wealth and population to mobilize against it in time. We did so twice in the twentieth century.

Let me illustrate with three of Walt's specific recommendations.

First, the Israeli-Palestinian leverage point. Walt proposes that the United States make support for Israel conditional on Israeli acceptance of a settlement based on the 1967 borders, with American aid cut off if Israel refuses. Treat this as an operational question: how, exactly, does an American president do this?

The president would have to persuade Congress to revise or condition Israeli aid appropriations. Walt is aware that this is politically difficult — he attributes the difficulty to "the Israel lobby" — but he understates how thoroughly bipartisan and durable American support for Israel actually is. The 2003 vote on the Syria Accountability Act passed 408-8 in the House. The 2004 emergency supplemental for Israel passed by similar margins. These margins do not reflect lobby capture of a reluctant Congress. They reflect the genuine convictions of legislators across both parties, responding to genuine constituent preferences. No American president since Eisenhower has successfully threatened to suspend aid to Israel for policy non-compliance, and Eisenhower did it in conditions (the Suez crisis, with the British and French as primary targets) that have not recurred. A president who tried to do what Walt proposes would face an immediate revolt in Congress, almost certainly bipartisan, that would consume the administration's political capital and likely fail. The result would be an empty threat — worse than the present posture, because it would advertise American impotence to apply leverage Walt thinks we possess.

This is not an argument that we should never push Israel. We should, and presidents do, but quietly, on specific issues, with sustained patience. The "use leverage" prescription Walt offers underestimates how political the leverage actually is.

Treaty ratification requires two-thirds of the Senate. The reductions Walt proposes would require modifying the Moscow Treaty framework and reopening discussions with Russia. The country needs better grand strategy than it is currently getting. It also needs grand strategy that takes seriously the messy institutional realities through which strategy must be made real. Walt has given us half of what we need. The other half is the harder half.
