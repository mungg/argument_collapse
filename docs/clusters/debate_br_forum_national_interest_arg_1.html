"The United States should adopt a strategy of reformed selective engagement that maintains active global leadership and alliances while exercising greater diplomatic discipline and restraint, rather than retreating to an offshore balancing approach."

Stephen Walt is surely right about the first principle: American power is a precious national asset, not a toy to be displayed or a solvent that can dissolve every political problem. The United States is strong enough to make grave mistakes and survive them, but not strong enough to make them cost-free. Iraq has already taught that a brilliant campaign can be followed by a grinding occupation, and that legitimacy, intelligence, language, and local politics are not luxuries appended to military power; they are part of power itself. Walt is also right that preventive war is a dangerous doctrine when elevated from an exceptional possibility into a general rule. A superpower that announces a right to strike when and where it chooses should not be surprised when others seek deterrents or when allies become anxious.

But Walt’s proposed cure—offshore balancing—risks learning too much from Iraq and too little from the broader history of American success. The problem with the Bush administration’s first term was not that America remained too engaged in the world. It was that engagement was too often impatient, unilateral, and inattentive to the political foundations of consent. The answer is not to make America “hard to get,” but to make American leadership more disciplined, more legitimate, and more reliable.

Offshore balancing sounds prudent because it promises selectivity. In practice, however, the line between prudent restraint and dangerous absence is hard to draw before the crisis arrives. Walt says the United States should rely on local actors to preserve regional balances, intervening only when they fail. But local actors often fail precisely because they doubt American staying power or because they fear abandonment more than domination. In Asia, for example, the American presence does not merely prepare for a possible future balance against China; it also dampens the security competition among Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China. A reduced or ambiguous American commitment could encourage Japan to remilitarize faster, South Korea to hedge, Taiwan to take risks, or China to test limits. The American role is not simply a tripwire against hegemony; it is part of the political architecture that prevents miscalculation.

The same is true, though differently, in Europe. Walt is correct that Europe no longer faces the Red Army, and the United States should expect wealthy democratic allies to bear more of the burden. But NATO has not been merely a military garrison. It has been the principal mechanism by which Germany’s power was embedded, Europe’s security dilemmas were softened, and new democracies in Central and Eastern Europe were anchored to the West. A drastic American drawdown might not produce immediate catastrophe, but it would weaken an institution that has magnified American influence at modest cost. The United States should not keep troops abroad out of habit, but neither should it treat forward presence as a mere imperial footprint. Sometimes presence is reassurance, and reassurance is cheaper than rescue.

Walt’s strongest case concerns the Middle East. The United States should not aspire to run the region, and the occupation of Iraq shows how quickly liberation can become entanglement. Yet even here, offshore balancing is too tidy. For decades Washington did rely on regional pillars—Iran under the Shah, then Iraq against revolutionary Iran, then conservative Arab monarchies—and the results were mixed at best. Balancing from offshore did not spare us from association with autocracy, oil politics, radical resentment, or war. Nor is it clear that a lighter American footprint by itself would drain anti-Americanism. Bin Laden’s indictment of the United States includes troops in Saudi Arabia, but also sanctions on Iraq, support for Israel, support for Arab regimes, and the cultural and political influence of the West. Some grievances can be reduced by wiser policy; others will persist because they are rooted in ideological hostility to pluralism, secular law, and American society itself.

On Israel and Palestine, Walt is right that unconditional American support for settlement expansion is bad for America and bad for Israel. No serious friend of Israel should pretend that permanent occupation can be reconciled with democracy, Jewish self-determination, and Palestinian political rights. The Clinton parameters remain the best available outline: two states, security guarantees, territorial compromise, a divided or shared Jerusalem, and a realistic solution to refugees. Washington should be prepared to press both sides much harder. But Walt understates Palestinian agency and the political difficulty created by terrorism. The second intifada, suicide bombings, and the collapse of trust after Camp David and Taba have profoundly shaped Israeli opinion. Pressure on Israel must be matched by unmistakable demands that Palestinian leaders dismantle terrorist organizations, end incitement, and build accountable institutions. With Arafat gone and a new Palestinian leadership emerging, there may be an opening. It will be lost if America appears simply to switch clients rather than insist on reciprocal obligations.

Walt’s nuclear argument is also persuasive in part. The United States cannot credibly preach nonproliferation while implying that nuclear weapons are the ultimate badge of security and status. Reducing excessive arsenals, securing Russian materials, strengthening inspections, and shutting down illicit networks should be central tasks. But nonaggression pledges to regimes such as North Korea and Iran are not magic keys. These governments seek nuclear capabilities not only because they fear America, but because nuclear weapons enhance regime prestige, regional leverage, and domestic legitimacy. A grand bargain is worth exploring, especially through multilateral talks, but it must be backed by verification, sanctions for cheating, and a willingness to contain if diplomacy fails.

The deepest issue is democracy promotion. Walt rightly rejects democracy by invasion. Still, the United States should not retreat to example alone. The democratic revolutions in Eastern Europe, the enlargement of NATO and the European Union, support for civil society, election monitoring, broadcasting, and pressure on dictators have all mattered. Even in the Middle East, the desire for accountable government is not an American invention. The Iraqi election just held, despite violence and Sunni alienation, reminds us that many people will risk death to vote. We should be humble about what that means, but not cynical.

The better grand strategy is reformed selective engagement: preserve alliances, reduce unnecessary military exposure, renounce preventive war as doctrine, repair legitimacy, invest in diplomacy and intelligence, and use American power with patience. Primacy will endure not because America is offshore, but because others believe its presence is preferable to the alternatives. That requires restraint, yes—but also steadiness.

"The United States should reject offshore balancing in favor of a disciplined liberal internationalism that maintains active global engagement to address non-state threats and preserve international institutions."

Stephen Walt has written a serious essay that deserves a serious reply, and on most of its critique of the Bush administration's record I find myself nodding along. The preventive-war doctrine, the contempt for institutions, the rhetorical recklessness of the "axis of evil," the captured intelligence process, the catastrophic post-war planning in Iraq, the failure to invest political capital in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict — all of this is real, and historians will be unkind. Where I part company with Walt is in the alternative he proposes, which I think misdiagnoses what kind of world we live in and what kind of country we have become.

Walt's grand strategy is realism in its classical, Mearsheimer-influenced form: states are the primary actors, power is the primary currency, regional balances of power are the primary mechanisms of stability, and the United States should husband its strength by intervening only when concentrated hostile power threatens vital interests. This was a workable framework for the nineteenth century and not a bad one for the Cold War. It is incomplete for the world we now inhabit.

Three features of the contemporary international system trouble Walt's framework. First, the principal threats to American security do not come from concentrations of state power in strategic regions. They come from the intersection of weak states, transnational networks, and dangerous technologies. Walt acknowledges this in his discussion of nuclear terrorism — indeed, he identifies it as the gravest threat we face — but he does not seem to register that this threat is essentially unbalanceable. There is no offshore equivalent of A.Q. Khan's network. There is no regional ally we can prop up against an unsecured Russian nuclear depot or a leaking Pakistani enrichment facility. Dealing with these threats requires precisely the kind of intrusive, sustained, multilateral engagement that Walt's strategy tends to minimize.

Second, the legitimacy and effectiveness of international institutions are not free goods. They require investment, leadership, and presence. Walt rightly notes that the United States gains by working through the UN, NATO, the WTO, and other bodies. But these institutions are not vending machines into which we can deposit coins of legitimacy and receive coalitions in return. They reflect, embody, and produce shared expectations about how power will be exercised. An "offshore" America that intervenes only when its direct interests are threatened, that lets regional powers handle their own problems, will gradually lose its capacity to shape the rules under which everyone operates. China and India will fill the vacuum. The institutions Walt values will atrophy without American oxygen.

Third, the connection between domestic political character and international behavior matters more than Walt allows. He treats democracy promotion as a quixotic neoconservative obsession that produces only resentment. But the deepest reason most Americans have supported active engagement abroad since 1945 is not crusading idealism but a sober assessment that democracies are easier to live with, less likely to harbor catastrophic terrorists, more likely to honor agreements, and more likely to develop the kinds of economies that integrate with our own. The democratic peace literature is hardly airtight, but it is not a fantasy. The Bush administration has implemented democracy promotion badly — at the point of a gun, with insufficient understanding of local conditions, conflated with regime change — but the underlying impulse is sound and importantly American. Walt's framework has nothing to say about how to encourage liberalization in places where we have leverage. He treats it as someone else's job.

I would also push back on Walt's characterization of "selective engagement." His criticism is that it was "not selective enough" — that we kept too many troops in Europe, that we tried to do too much. I think the better criticism is the opposite: selective engagement was inconsistent. It produced the genuine successes of the 1990s — German reunification, NATO enlargement, the Dayton Accords, Kosovo — but it also produced Rwanda, the early Clinton-era flailing in Somalia and Haiti, and a long, slow drift in policy toward Iraq that never quite resolved itself. The lesson is not that we were too engaged but that we were not strategically engaged enough.

There are aspects of Walt's program I unambiguously support. The "grand bargain" approach to nonproliferation seems exactly right: we cannot expect other states to constrain their nuclear options while we develop new warheads and walk away from our Article VI obligations. Mohamed El Baradei's cigarette analogy stings because it is accurate. A serious nonproliferation strategy requires the United States to demonstrate, in word and deed, that it is winding down its dependence on these weapons, not enhancing it.

I also support Walt's instinct that we have neglected the Israeli-Palestinian question at grave cost. The Bush administration's outsourcing of that conflict to Sharon — with the "road map" gestured at and then abandoned — has been a strategic disaster. We should be applying serious, sustained, and yes, sometimes uncomfortable pressure to both parties. The Clinton parameters remain the rough outline of any feasible settlement.

But I find Walt's framing of the Israel question politically reckless and analytically reductive. The idea that ending the occupation would dissolve Arab anger or pull the rug out from under bin Laden mistakes a real grievance for the central grievance. Bin Laden's quarrel with the United States is older than Sharon's premiership and broader than Palestine. Solving the conflict is a moral and strategic necessity in its own right. We should not oversell what it will buy us in the war against jihadism.

Finally, Walt's call for greater self-restraint and humility is one I endorse, but it lands strangely against his confident prescriptions about how the Middle East balance should be managed, how Israel should be pressured, how the nuclear bargain should be structured. There is more than a whiff of the foreign-policy intellectual telling the world how it should run while criticizing other foreign-policy intellectuals for telling the world how it should run.

What we need is not a return to offshore balancing but a more disciplined liberal internationalism: serious about institutions, serious about democracy as a long-term project, serious about non-state threats, willing to use force but reluctant to do so unilaterally or without consent. That tradition runs from Acheson through Kennan through Nye. It is harder to caricature than either neoconservatism or realism, which may be why it is currently out of favor with the publishing class. It is, nonetheless, the framework most likely to keep us safe.

"While Stephen Walt’s 'offshore balancing' is a step in the right direction, it does not go far enough because it still maintains an imperial premise that keeps the United States unnecessarily entangled in foreign regions."

Walt's essay is the most useful thing written about American grand strategy since September 11, and the conventional foreign-policy community will accordingly try to ignore it. Let me say something different: Walt does not go far enough.

The "offshore balancing" position Walt defends is plausible, well-argued, and represents what passes for radicalism inside the universe of think-tanks and quarterlies that takes American primacy as its baseline. But Walt accepts too much of the imperial premise. He still wants air and naval bases in Asia. He still wants the capacity to swing into the Persian Gulf when necessary. He still wants to maintain a balance against China, which is to say, to organize East Asian security around containing a country that has not attacked us and does not threaten us. He still describes the United States as having "vital interests" in three regions — Europe, industrialized Asia, and the Persian Gulf — that he is willing to defend by force.

This is offshore balancing only by comparison with the maximalist neoconservative project. Compared with any sane reading of the actual American national interest, it is still imperial in scope, still places our citizens on the front lines of other people's quarrels, and still treats the entire globe as a chessboard on which American military planners must have a pre-positioned piece.

Consider the Persian Gulf. Walt wants us to ensure that no single power dominates the region. Why? The pat answer is "oil." But oil is a commodity, and commodities flow according to price. Whoever controls Gulf oil — Saddam's Iraq, Khomeini's Iran, a future Saudi government in jihadist hands — will want to sell it, because oil unsold is worthless rock. The economic argument for the Carter Doctrine never really held up under examination, and it holds up even less in a world of diversified suppliers, where Gulf oil represents a declining share of global production. We have spent fifteen years and trillions of dollars defending an interest that markets would defend for free.

The strategic argument is no stronger. Walt himself notes that the "dual containment" policy of the 1990s helped produce al Qaeda. He understands the role of American basing in Saudi Arabia in radicalizing bin Laden. Yet he proposes to continue the practice in slightly leaner form. The lesson of the last fifteen years is not that we should run a more elegant version of the Carter Doctrine. The lesson is that the Carter Doctrine itself was a mistake, that our forward presence in the Gulf manufactures more enemies than it neutralizes, and that the cheapest and safest course is the one Walt cannot quite bring himself to recommend: leave.

Consider East Asia. Walt wants to maintain "cooperative security partnerships" with our Asian allies as the foundation for a future containment of China. But China is not the Soviet Union. It has no expansionist ideology, no fifth columns in foreign capitals, no doctrine of global revolution. It has territorial disputes with its neighbors — Taiwan, the Senkakus, the Spratlys, parts of the Indian border — that are local in scope and not directly threatening to the United States. By organizing East Asian security around containment-in-waiting, we virtually guarantee the rivalry we claim to be hedging against. Japan, South Korea, Vietnam, and India are perfectly capable of balancing China among themselves if and when balancing becomes necessary. Our presence is not a stabilizer; it is a tripwire that converts any local dispute into a potential great-power war.

Consider Europe. Here Walt is closest to right, but he still proposes to remain in NATO and to maintain "small contingents" for symbolic purposes. Why? Europe is wealthier than the United States in aggregate, technologically sophisticated, and faces no significant external threat. Russian power is a shell of what it was. The European Union has perfectly adequate means to defend itself if its members chose to invest in doing so. The reason they have not is that we have generously subsidized their security for sixty years, freeing them to build welfare states while we shouldered the burden of armaments. The kindest thing we could do for European maturity is to end the subsidy.

Walt's resistance to a more thoroughgoing retrenchment is rooted, I think, in a belief that the world will descend into chaos without American forward presence. This is the great unexamined assumption of the entire foreign-policy establishment. It has been repeated so often that it has become axiomatic. But it is empirically untested. We do not actually know what regional dynamics in Asia, Europe, or the Middle East would look like without American garrisons, because we have not seen such a world since 1945. What we do know is that the costs of our presence are real, measurable, and rising: in dollars, in lives, in attention diverted from genuine domestic priorities, in radicalization of populations that resent our military footprint.

There is also a constitutional argument that Walt does not engage. The American republic was not designed for empire. Standing armies, permanent overseas commitments, executive war-making, vast intelligence bureaucracies operating outside meaningful oversight — these are not the institutional furniture of a healthy democracy. They corrode the very polity they purport to defend. The Founders knew this; they warned us repeatedly; we forgot. Walt's offshore balancing does not address this corrosion because it does not seriously challenge the size or scope of the national security state. A genuinely realist policy would have to.

I do not propose isolationism in the caricatured sense — closed borders, autarkic economics, indifference to atrocity. I propose what John Quincy Adams proposed: America the well-wisher of liberty everywhere, the champion of her own only. Robust diplomacy, open trade, generous humanitarian assistance, principled rhetoric in defense of human rights — and no garrisons, no permanent alliances, no automatic tripwires. If a hostile great power ever emerges that genuinely threatens this continent, we have the wealth and population to mobilize against it in time. We did so twice in the twentieth century.

Walt has done the country a service by reopening a conversation that the foreign-policy consensus has tried to foreclose. Now we need to push the conversation past where he has taken it, and ask what an American foreign policy designed for an American republic — rather than for an American empire — would actually look like.

"The United States must reject the strategy of 'offshore balancing' and maintain a policy of forward engagement and democratic transformation to ensure global security."

Stephen Walt's essay is elegant, learned, and wrong in most of the ways that matter. He has produced what amounts to a counsel of retreat dressed in the language of prudence, and we should be candid about what his program would mean: a United States that watches from the horizon while tyrannies consolidate, proliferators advance, and our friends are picked off one by one. He calls this "offshore balancing." History will likely call it something less flattering.

Begin with the central premise. Walt argues that American primacy provokes resistance, and that the way to soften resistance is to husband our power, defer to local balancers, and abandon the project of democratic transformation. There is a kernel of truth here: nobody enjoys being lectured by a hegemon, and clumsy diplomacy costs us. But Walt mistakes a problem of execution for a problem of strategy. The Bush Doctrine has not failed because it sought to enlarge the democratic peace; it has had a hard time because liberating and reconstructing Iraq is genuinely difficult, because we underestimated the post-war task, and because the enemies of decent governance fight back. The remedy is to fight more skillfully, not to retreat to the offshore.

Consider the actual record Walt elides. Afghan women voted last fall — voted — in numbers nobody predicted. Iraqis went to the polls a week ago under threat of murder, holding up purple fingers in defiance of the Baathist and jihadist holdouts who think they can reclaim that country. The Palestinians, after Arafat's death, just elected a moderate. Lebanese citizens are beginning to push openly against Syrian occupation in the wake of the Hariri investigation. Mubarak is being forced to gesture, however inadequately, toward contested elections. Libya has surrendered its WMD program. None of this is the fruit of "offshore balancing." It is the fruit of forward engagement, of an American president willing to say plainly that the Arab democratic deficit is a security problem for everyone.

Walt's treatment of preventive war is similarly tendentious. He insists that no rogue state would ever transfer WMDs to terrorists because that would invite American retaliation. But this assumes a level of strategic rationality, plus a perfect attribution capability, that the historical record does not support. Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait expecting we would not respond. The Taliban harbored al Qaeda after multiple American warnings. The argument that deterrence will always hold is precisely the argument that complacent realists made during the 1990s about Saddam's reconstitution capacities. We were lucky once that the WMD picture in Iraq turned out to be less dire than feared. Counting on luck is not a strategy.

On Israel, Walt has crossed a line that deserves to be named. He argues, in effect, that an "Israel lobby" has hijacked American foreign policy and that withdrawing support for our democratic ally would buy us standing in the Arab street. This gets the causation backwards. Bin Laden's grievance list shifts opportunistically: the Crusaders in Arabia, the apostate House of Saud, Andalusia, Israel, cartoon depictions, women's education. The radical project is the restoration of the caliphate and the humiliation of the West, not a real-estate dispute over the West Bank. Throwing Israel under the bus would not buy us a single jihadist convert back. It would, however, signal to every American ally that our commitments are conditional and discardable.

Walt's "grand bargain" on nuclear weapons is equally fanciful. He proposes that the United States reduce its arsenal to a few hundred warheads and sign nonaggression pacts with Iran and North Korea in exchange for verifiable abandonment of their programs. But this assumes the very thing in dispute: that these regimes seek nuclear weapons primarily out of fear of us, and would gladly give them up if reassured. The evidence points the other way. The North Korean program began under Kim Il-Sung in the 1980s, when the United States posed no credible threat of regime change. Iran's program predates the Bush Doctrine by a decade and accelerated under Khatami, the reformer. These regimes seek nuclear weapons because nuclear weapons confer regional prestige, deter intervention against their own aggression, and underwrite the survival of their ruling cliques. American restraint will not change that calculus.

Walt is on stronger ground, I concede, when he criticizes the execution of our public diplomacy and our coalition management. The State Department's outreach to the Arabic-speaking world has been embarrassing. Karen Hughes's appointment may help; it may not. We should be training thousands more Arabic speakers, funding al-Hurra more seriously, and consulting allies before we surprise them. None of this requires abandoning the larger strategic vision.

What Walt is really offering, beneath the academic vocabulary, is the foreign policy of the first Bush administration — the policy that left Saddam in power in 1991, that watched Bosnia bleed for three years, that allowed Rwanda to happen, that treated the Taliban as a manageable curiosity. That policy, pursued through the 1990s under Clinton with marginal adjustments, produced the conditions of September 11. To return to it now, after the wake-up call, would be a remarkable act of forgetting.

There is also something condescending in Walt's framing. He repeatedly tells us that Americans are "immature," that our public is ignorant, that our political class is captured by lobbies. The condescension obscures a more interesting reality: the American electorate, having watched a city burn and three thousand of its compatriots murdered, made a considered judgment that the status quo in the Middle East was no longer tolerable. They re-elected the president responsible for changing it. They may yet change their minds; democracies do. But Walt's essay is not really an argument with President Bush. It is an argument with the American voter, who has reached conclusions Professor Walt finds unsophisticated.

The world is not going to manage itself from offshore. China is building blue-water capabilities. Iran is enriching uranium. North Korea has tested longer-range missiles. Russia is sliding back into authoritarianism and bullying its near abroad. Pakistan's nuclear stockpile sits within reach of jihadist sympathizers in its own military. In every one of these cases, distance reduces our influence rather than increasing it. Offshore balancing is what we did between 1919 and 1941, and we know how that ended.

By all means, let us be wiser, more multilateral where we can be, more careful in our rhetoric, more strategic in our deployments. But the project of American leadership cannot be conducted from over the horizon. It requires presence, and presence costs.

The doctrine of preventive war has damaged the very nonproliferation norms it purported to defend. Washington’s disdain for allies has made America less, not more, effective. On these matters, liberal internationalists should have no quarrel with Walt. Many of us opposed the Iraq war not because we doubted that Saddam Hussein was brutal, but because the war lacked legitimacy, necessity, and a realistic plan for the aftermath. The administration converted a contained dictator into an open-ended American responsibility.

But Walt draws from Iraq a broader lesson that is too austere. He seems to believe that the United States should cease trying to “manage” international order, except in a few regions where a hostile hegemon might arise. That view underestimates how much American security and prosperity depend on an open, rule-governed world. The United States benefits from more than the absence of hostile great powers. It benefits from stable financial systems, open trade, secure sea lanes, public health cooperation, functioning states, arms-control regimes, and the expectation that aggression and mass atrocities will meet organized resistance. These goods do not maintain themselves. They require leadership, institutions, money, and sometimes force.

Offshore balancing is a poor guide to problems that do not look like classic great-power threats. The attacks of September 11 did not come from a state seeking regional hegemony. They came from a transnational network operating from the territory of one of the world’s poorest and most broken countries. Afghanistan was not strategically vital in the old geopolitical sense. Yet its collapse mattered. So might the collapse of Pakistan, Nigeria, Indonesia, or other states where terrorism, disease, criminal networks, and humanitarian catastrophe can spill across borders. A grand strategy that treats most of the world as peripheral until a regional hegemon appears will often notice danger too late.

Nor is it enough to say that the United States can intervene when necessary and leave quickly. The hardest security problems today are not always solved by brief interventions. Peacekeeping, state-building, police training, judicial reform, and economic reconstruction are tedious, expensive, and politically unrewarding. But the alternative is sometimes worse. Bosnia and Kosovo did not require American occupation on the scale of Iraq, but they did require sustained NATO engagement. East Timor required international administration. Afghanistan still requires more than counterterrorism raids; it requires security beyond Kabul, roads, schools, and a government that can compete with warlords. If we reject empire, we should not reject all forms of international responsibility.

Walt is right that American power provokes fear when unrestrained. But institutions are more than devices for reassuring others about American intentions. They are also mechanisms for solving problems that no state can solve alone. The United Nations is imperfect, often infuriating, and frequently paralyzed by the interests of its members. NATO struggles to define its role. The World Trade Organization has generated backlash as well as growth. But these institutions embody a crucial principle: power should be embedded in rules. The United States helped build this system after 1945 not as charity, but because rules made American leadership more acceptable and effective. We should repair that system rather than edge away from it.

The same applies to democracy promotion. Walt rightly rejects imposing democracy at gunpoint. The Bush administration’s rhetoric has been grandiose and inconsistent, especially when paired with indulgence toward friendly autocrats. Yet the answer is not to demote democracy to the realm of example alone. Democratic governance matters for human dignity, but also for security. Closed regimes breed corruption, conspiracy theories, radicalism, and brittle politics. Arab authoritarianism has not produced stability; it has produced stagnation and rage. Supporting reform through civil society, education, independent media, women’s rights, election monitoring, and conditional aid is not imperialism. It is long-term engagement.

Here the Iraqi election of January 30, 2005, complicates the picture. It does not vindicate the war. A courageous turnout by Iraqis cannot retroactively make the invasion wise or legal. But it does remind us that people living under tyranny may want political freedom, and that American critics of the war should not sound indifferent to that aspiration. The challenge is to align American policy with democratic forces without turning those forces into instruments of U.S. power. That requires humility, multilateral support, and patience—not disengagement.

Walt’s discussion of the Israeli–Palestinian conflict is among the most important parts of his essay. The United States cannot credibly speak of freedom while acquiescing in permanent occupation and settlement expansion. A serious American peace effort would serve American interests, Palestinian rights, and Israel’s future as a democratic Jewish state. But the problem is not simply that America is too engaged. It is that America is engaged in the wrong way: as Israel’s indulgent lawyer rather than as a firm mediator committed to two states. Here again, the cure is not offshore balancing but better diplomacy.

On nuclear proliferation, Walt’s proposed grand bargain has real merit. The United States cannot preach restraint while modernizing its own nuclear arsenal and expanding doctrines for nuclear use. Article VI of the Non-Proliferation Treaty is not ornamental. If we want tighter controls on enrichment and reprocessing, stronger inspections, and cooperation against smuggling, we must show that nonproliferation is a reciprocal bargain, not a hierarchy in which the powerful keep their privileges forever. But nuclear diplomacy also requires alliances, institutions, and persistent engagement—the very habits offshore balancing tends to undervalue.

The United States should indeed reduce its military footprint where it is needlessly provocative. It should close unnecessary bases, rely less on unilateral force, and stop treating credibility as a reason to intervene everywhere. But American leadership is not reducible to troop deployments. It includes foreign aid, climate policy, trade policy, disease prevention, human rights advocacy, and institution-building. Walt mentions some of these, but his strategic frame does not give them sufficient weight.

The real choice is not between empire and offshore balancing. It is between reckless unilateralism and disciplined internationalism. The United States should remain deeply engaged in the world, but through rules, alliances, and legitimacy rather than preventive war and swagger. It should use power to sustain an order in which others have a voice and a stake. That will sometimes require saying no to allies, sometimes negotiating with enemies, and sometimes contributing troops to missions that do not fit a narrow definition of vital interest.

Walt fears that America will squander primacy by doing too much. He is right. But it can also squander primacy by doing too little, or by defining its interests too narrowly for an interdependent age. The United States should not try to run the world. But neither should it stand offshore while the institutions and norms that make American power tolerable decay. The wiser course is engaged restraint: less imperial ambition, more multilateral leadership, and a recognition that legitimacy is not a constraint on power but one of its principal sources.

"The United States should reject the proposed strategy of offshore balancing because it misinterprets American history and would likely repeat the catastrophic security failures of the inter-war period."

Stephen Walt's essay is the most sophisticated case currently in print for the strategy he calls "offshore balancing." It deserves engagement on its own terms, and I want to offer that engagement in the form of a question: is offshore balancing actually what the United States used to do, and would returning to it actually work?

Walt presents offshore balancing as "America's traditional grand strategy." This claim deserves scrutiny. The history is more complicated than the term implies.

For the first century and a half of the republic, American grand strategy was something we might more accurately call continental expansionism plus hemispheric primacy. We were not balancing Eurasian powers from offshore; we were ignoring them while we conquered a continent, then enforcing the Monroe Doctrine to keep them out of our neighborhood. The classical offshore-balancer role — the one Walt has in mind — was Britain's, not ours. We did not act in the European balance of power in any sustained way until 1917, and even then reluctantly and incompletely.

When we did intervene — in 1917, in 1941 — the pattern was not the deft, calibrated balancing Walt describes. It was waiting too long, intervening massively, defeating the hegemonic challenger, and then withdrawing. The 1919 withdrawal was rapid and largely indifferent to the consequences in Europe. The result of that withdrawal — combined with our retreat from the League of Nations, our protectionism, our isolationism in the 1930s — is part of what produced the Second World War. "Offshore balancing" in its inter-war American form did not preserve a workable balance of power in Eurasia. It produced two existential threats that we then had to defeat at enormous cost.

After 1945, American leaders consciously chose not to repeat this pattern. They built forward-deployed alliances, garrisoned Germany and Japan and Korea, kept fleets in the Mediterranean and the Western Pacific, and committed themselves to defending allies in advance rather than waiting until aggression had succeeded. This was not offshore balancing. This was, in the lexicon Walt himself uses, selective engagement — and it produced, over forty years, the most successful peacetime outcome in great-power politics in modern history. The Cold War ended without a great-power war. The Soviet bloc collapsed without a shot fired in anger between the major powers. Western Europe, East Asia, and eventually Eastern Europe converted to liberal democracy and market economies. This is the world we now inherit. It was not built by offshore balancing.

So when Walt urges us to return to offshore balancing, he is not really urging a return to tradition. He is urging us to abandon the strategic posture that worked spectacularly well from 1947 to 1991 and to adopt instead something closer to the strategic posture that failed spectacularly between the World Wars. The case for this would have to be that the conditions which made forward engagement necessary in 1947 have changed sufficiently that we can now return to the inter-war model without inviting inter-war consequences. Walt asserts this; he does not demonstrate it.

Consider the conditions. We no longer face a Soviet adversary, true. But we face several emerging challenges that the inter-war American republic did not face. A nuclear North Korea. A nuclearizing Iran. A rising China. A nuclear-armed Pakistan whose government may not survive its own internal contradictions. Transnational terrorist networks willing and possibly able to acquire weapons of mass destruction. The vulnerability of our economy to disruptions of global trade, oil supply, and the financial system. The inter-war republic could afford to retreat across the oceans because the oceans were genuine moats and the threats were genuinely distant. Our oceans are no longer moats. The threats are no longer distant. Walt's framework, applied to this environment, would not preserve our security. It would expose us to risks we used to be able to outrun by virtue of geography, but no longer can.

Consider also the counterfactual Walt himself proposes for Iraq. He says that had we continued the inspections process, the United States "would have scored a resounding diplomatic victory" and Saddam would have been "contained." Perhaps. But this assumes that the inspections regime was sustainable indefinitely. By late 2002 the sanctions architecture was crumbling — Russia, France, and China were lobbying for relaxation, smuggling was rampant, the oil-for-food program was riddled with corruption that we now know reached into the upper echelons of the UN itself. Walt's "containment" was not stable. It was a strategic position that was eroding by the month. Whether the war was the right answer is a legitimate question. Whether the status quo ante was sustainable is a different question, and Walt finesses it.

I want to make a more general point. Strategic concepts like "offshore balancing," "selective engagement," and "primacy" are heuristics. They illuminate trade-offs, but they do not by themselves prescribe policy. The hard work is in the particulars: what to do about Iran, what to do about Taiwan, what to do about Pakistan, what to do about the Saudi succession, what to do about the Mexican northern border, what to do about North Korean missile tests, what to do about the Russian energy lever in Europe. Walt's essay is largely silent on these specific cases or treats them in passing. Real strategy is built case by case, with constant attention to how the cases interact. The offshore-balancing framework can illuminate some of them. It cannot answer most.

There is one point on which I am in full agreement with Walt: American foreign policy under the current administration has been less prudent, less consultative, and less strategically disciplined than the situation requires. Many of the specific reforms he proposes — abandoning the preemption doctrine as declaratory policy, investing seriously in public diplomacy, engaging the Israeli-Palestinian conflict more energetically, taking proliferation seriously as the gravest threat — are reforms I would endorse. We can pursue these reforms within a strategic posture closer to selective engagement than to offshore balancing. The errors of the past four years do not require us to abandon the framework that brought us through the Cold War. They require us to recover its discipline.

Walt's essay is, in the end, less a defense of a tradition than a proposal for a new tradition. It deserves serious debate on those grounds, and not on the false ground of historical recovery.

Stephen Walt has produced the most coherent case yet for what a post-Iraq American grand strategy ought to look like, and much of his diagnosis is hard to dispute. Start with the Persian Gulf, which Walt identifies as one of three regions of vital interest while simultaneously urging that we withdraw to a balancing posture there. The trouble is that the local materials for a balance are thin. Iraq is shattered, Saudi Arabia is a brittle monarchy whose conventional forces have never been impressive, and the remaining Gulf states are micro-states. The natural regional hegemon, were the United States to step back, is Iran—a state Walt elsewhere identifies as a proliferation concern. There is something to this: free-riding is endemic to alliance politics, and Europeans in particular have grown comfortable consuming American security as a public good. But the same logic that gets a Saudi base agreement can get a Japanese nuclear weapon. Tokyo, Seoul, Riyadh, and Ankara have all entertained nuclear options in the past and were dissuaded largely by ironclad American commitments. Play hard-to-get with such partners and you may discover that the cost of primacy includes a more proliferated world—the very outcome Walt rightly identifies as our most urgent danger.

It assumes regimes are unitary rational actors with tight command over their arsenals. The A.Q. The same Pakistani state that Walt would presumably treat as a partner in counterterrorism contains scientists who knowingly transferred centrifuge designs to Libya, Iran, and North Korea. Bin Laden's own writings, including the 1996 and 1998 declarations, foreground the American military presence on the Arabian peninsula, sanctions against Iraq, and apostate regimes from Riyadh to Cairo. Offshore balancing, in short, is the right corrective to the current administration's overreach, but it is not quite the finished doctrine Walt suggests. Stephen Walt’s essay is a lucid brief for modesty, but it is a brief for an America that no longer exists. The world that gave rise to offshore balancing—a world of great-power rivals whose ambitions could be checked by local proxies—has been fundamentally altered by the confluence of terrorism, rogue-state proliferation, and the political vacuum of failed states. The strategy of global hegemony that Walt dismisses is, in fact, the only serious answer to these threats, provided it is pursued with the intellectual honesty and operational sophistication that the Bush administration has too often lacked. The failures in Iraq are not proof that the strategy is infeasible; they are proof that incompetently waged hegemony is as dangerous as incompetently waged anything. The central question is not whether the United States should seek to shape the international order in its image—it already does, by dint of its preponderant power—but whether it will do so deliberately, consistently, and with a clear-eyed understanding that the spread of liberal democracy and the preclusion of WMD threats require the credible use of force.

Walt’s chief error is to conflate the Bush administration’s bungled execution with the inherent impossibility of a forward-leaning grand strategy. The war in Iraq need not have been the quagmire it became. A larger invasion force, a plan for post-conflict stability that did not assume a clean handover, and a serious commitment to reconstruction rather than ideological outsourcing to crony contractors could have produced a very different outcome. Even now, with the election of a provisional Iraqi government in January 2005, there are signs—fragile, tentative signs—that a representative politics can take root. Walt writes as though the failure is already final, but history tells us that the stabilization of occupied territories is a long and arduous business, one that the United States succeeded at in Germany and Japan after far more destructive wars. The difference is that we have lost the patience and the willingness to endure the costs. But if the strategy of preventive regime change is reserved for only the most dangerous states—those that combine aggressive intent, WMD programs, and links to international terrorism—then the investment may still pay a strategic dividend that offshore balancing could never replicate.

The deeper argument for global hegemony is that in an age of catastrophic terrorism, the luxury of waiting for threats to become imminent before acting is a luxury the nation can no longer afford. Walt rests his case against preventive war on the remarkable assertion that newly nuclear-capable states will reliably secure their arsenals and never transfer them to terrorists. This is a breathtaking act of faith. The North Korean regime is a dynastic cult of personality that has sold missiles and nuclear technology to anyone with hard currency. Iran’s theocratic leadership has a demonstrated track record of support for Hezbollah and other groups that wage terrorist campaigns abroad. The argument that these regimes would never give a weapon to a terrorist group because they could not control its use ignores the fact that a state might calculate that a nuclear attack on the United States, if plausibly deniable, could cripple the superpower without exposing the donor to certain retaliation. In an era of loose nukes and radical ideologies, Walt’s confidence that deterrence will work in all cases is the policy equivalent of whistling past the graveyard. Global hegemony, by contrast, insists that regimes that sponsor terrorism cannot be allowed to acquire the ultimate weapon, and if they persist, they must be disarmed, by force if necessary. The logic of preemption—genuine preemption, not mere prevention—applies squarely to a hostile state that is on the verge of a nuclear breakout.

Walt is right to note that the Bush administration has done immense damage to America’s reputation. But the remedy is not to abandon the project of democratization; it is to pursue it more wisely. American power must be used to create conditions in which liberal politics can take root, not to impose them at the point of a bayonet. That means massive investment in soft power—cultural exchange, economic development, the encouragement of civil society—alongside the credible threat of military action. The Libyan example that Walt himself cites as a model of “divide and conquer” actually reinforces the case for hegemony: it was the demonstration effect of Iraq’s overthrow, however messy, that concentrated Qaddafi’s mind. No amount of offshore balancing would have produced that surrender. Similarly, the prospect of a democratic Iraq, however distant it may seem, is already altering the political calculus of the region, as the stirrings of reform in Lebanon and Egypt suggest. Liberal internationalism recognizes that severe human rights abuses and state failures are not just moral tragedies; they are the breeding grounds for the instability and extremism that eventually threaten international peace.

Walt’s proposed "grand bargain" on nuclear weapons is perhaps his most constructive idea, recognizing that American hypocrisy on proliferation undermines global norms. However, he treats non-proliferation as a transactional agreement rather than a regime that requires constant, institutionalized enforcement. A true grand bargain would require vastly empowering the International Atomic Energy Agency (IAEA) and reforming the UN Security Council to enforce compliance automatically—steps that require an American commitment to global governance, not just a strategic retreat.

Even in the Middle East, where Walt’s critique of unconditional support for Israeli expansionism is brave and largely correct, his solution is flawed. We cannot simply “balance” the Middle East by playing powers against each other as we did during the Iran-Iraq war—a policy that directly contributed to the current mess. The United States must indeed push for a just two-state solution, but it must also lead a multilateral effort to integrate the Middle East economically and politically into the global system. This requires more engagement, not less; it requires building civil society, fostering trade agreements, and supporting educational initiatives, all in concert with European and Asian partners.

Offshore balancing is a seductive illusion. It promises security on the cheap, offering the benefits of global primacy without the burdens of global leadership. But in a globalized era, there is no “offshore.” The oceans no longer protect us from the consequences of state failure, environmental degradation, or ideological extremism. To secure our future, we must not retreat from the world to play the role of a detached geopolitical mechanic. Instead, we must recommit to the hard, unglamorous work of building and sustaining a rules-based international order. We must lead not by balancing nations against one another, but by binding them together.

  1. 01Offshore balancing focuses on state-centric power dynamics and fails to address borderless challenges like global warming, pandemics, and the financial networks of transnational terrorism.
  2. 02International institutions should be viewed as essential machinery for global governance and absolute gains rather than mere public relations tools used to legitimize American military power.
  3. 03Withdrawing U.S. forces from regions like Europe risks dismantling the post-WWII security architecture and could trigger a destabilizing renationalization of regional defense policies.
  4. 04A strategy based on cold realism and cost-benefit analysis for humanitarian crises ignores the fact that state failures and human rights abuses are the primary breeding grounds for global instability.
  5. 05Effective non-proliferation and Middle East stability require deeper multilateral integration and the strengthening of global regimes rather than a strategic retreat to a 'detached mechanic' role.
Diversified Gemini

"Stephen Walt’s strategy of offshore balancing is a cynical attempt to preserve American global hegemony through proxy imperialism rather than a genuine alternative to the imperialist mindset he critiques."

Stephen M. Walt’s “In the National Interest” provides a devastating inventory of the Bush administration’s blunders, from the illegal invasion of Iraq to the reckless rhetoric of the “axis of evil.” Yet, for all its incisive critique of neoconservative overreach, Walt’s essay remains trapped in the same imperial mindset he claims to oppose. Walt does not object to American empire; he merely objects to its current mismanagement. His proposed strategy of “offshore balancing” is not a blueprint for a more just or peaceful world, but a manual for empire on the cheap—a way to maintain global dominance while outsourcing the dirty work and minimizing the blowback.

Walt is refreshingly honest about his ultimate goal: “the central aim of American grand strategy in foreign policy should be to preserve its current position [of primacy] for as long as possible.” He views American economic and military dominance not as a historical anomaly to be transitioned into a multipolar cooperative order, but as a possession to be jealously guarded. From this starting point, any claim to morality or progressive values is instantly subordinated to the cold calculus of maintaining supremacy.

Consider what offshore balancing actually means in practice. Walt argues that the U.S. should rely “primarily on local actors to uphold the regional balance of power.” In the Middle East, this means reverting to the pre-1990 strategy of propping up regional proxies to fight our wars and suppress their own populations. It was precisely this strategy—arming the Shah of Iran, and later Saddam Hussein—that laid the groundwork for the anti-Americanism Walt now laments. Offshore balancing means continuing to arm authoritarian regimes in Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and elsewhere, turning a blind eye to their domestic repression so long as they serve U.S. geopolitical interests. It is a strategy of proxy imperialism, where the blood shed is overwhelmingly non-American, making the empire more sustainable for the American public precisely because it is less visible.

Furthermore, Walt’s treatment of the Global South is dismissive. He explicitly states that offshore balancing assumes “only a few areas of the globe are of strategic importance... Europe, industrialized Asia, and the Persian Gulf.” Entire continents—Africa, Latin America, large swathes of Southeast Asia—are relegated to the geopolitical backburner. Walt explicitly notes that the U.S. should not intervene in cases of mass murder or genocide unless it can be done at an “acceptable cost.” This is the dark heart of realist theory: a Rwandan genocide or a Darfur crisis is strategically irrelevant unless it threatens the core centers of global capital or oil.

Even Walt’s most progressive-sounding proposal—his critique of the U.S.-Israel relationship and his demand for a just settlement for the Palestinians—is framed entirely in instrumental terms. He does not advocate for Palestinian rights because self-determination is a universal human right, but because unconditional support for Israel is “self-defeating” for American primacy and fuels terrorism against the U.S. While the policy outcome he demands (a viable Palestinian state) is correct, his reasoning reveals a worldview where justice is merely a tactic for securing American hegemony.

Similarly, his “grand bargain” on nuclear weapons is essentially a pragmatic protection racket. He recognizes that U.S. hypocrisy on nuclear arms drives proliferation. But his solution is not genuine disarmament toward a nuclear-free world; it is to reduce the arsenal just enough to look “less hypocritical” while retaining “a few hundred warheads as a deterrent.” The underlying assumption remains that the United States alone has the right to decide who is allowed to possess weapons of mass destruction, using multilateral institutions merely as a “buddy system” to legitimize its monopoly on violence.

What Walt fails to grasp is that the intense global resistance to American power is not just a reaction to *how* that power is used, but to the *fact* of that immense, unaccountable power itself. The world does not want a “smarter” hegemon; it wants an equitable international system. As long as the United States pursues primacy—the explicit goal of ensuring no other nation can ever challenge its dominance—it will inevitably engender resistance, balancing, and resentment.

A truly alternative grand strategy would not seek to husband primacy, but to democratize international relations. It would mean dismantling the massive U.S. military-industrial complex, closing the hundreds of overseas military bases that Walt would likely just shuffle around, and diverting those hundreds of billions of dollars toward global poverty reduction, fighting the HIV/AIDS pandemic, and combating climate change. It would mean treating the United Nations not as a PR tool for “reassurance,” but as the actual locus of international law, submitting U.S. actions to the same legal constraints we demand of others (including joining the International Criminal Court).

Walt is right that the Bush doctrine of preventive war is a disaster. But replacing the neoconservative crusader with the realist offshore balancer is merely swapping the arrogant sheriff for the cynical mafia boss. Both seek to run the neighborhood; they only disagree on the most efficient way to collect the tribute. If we truly want to end the hatred directed at the United States, we must abandon the imperial project altogether, not just find a cheaper way to manage it.

  2. 02The strategy dismisses the intrinsic value of the Global South, viewing regions like Africa and Latin America as strategically irrelevant and suggesting that humanitarian intervention should only occur if the cost is acceptable to U.S. interests.
  3. 03Walt’s support for Palestinian rights and nuclear non-proliferation is framed as a tactical necessity for securing American primacy rather than a commitment to universal justice or genuine global disarmament.
