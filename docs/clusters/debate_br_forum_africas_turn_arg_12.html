"While foreign aid cannot single-handedly produce economic growth, well-designed and transparent aid is a vital tool for building human capital, saving lives, and creating the necessary conditions for Africa’s long-term recovery."

Edward Miguel’s skepticism about foreign aid is useful as a warning against magical thinking, but it risks understating the ways aid has contributed—and could contribute—to Africa’s recovery. If we ask whether aid alone can produce growth, the answer is plainly no. But if we ask whether well-designed public investments financed partly by aid can save lives, build human capital, and support the conditions for growth, the answer is just as plainly yes.

The debate between Jeffrey Sachs and William Easterly is often framed as a choice between “more aid” and “aid does not work.” That framing is too crude. Aid is not a single thing. Emergency food shipments, antiretroviral treatment, road construction, vaccination campaigns, budget support, technical consultants, school grants, and military assistance all fall under the same label. Some are transformative, some are useless, and some are harmful. To judge aid by aggregate correlations with GDP is like judging medicine by comparing all pills ever swallowed with all changes in body temperature.

Miguel is correct that Africa received substantial aid during years of economic decline. But this does not prove much. Much aid during the Cold War was not primarily developmental. It was geopolitical rent paid to friendly rulers. Mobutu’s Zaire did not receive Western support because it was a promising development laboratory. It received support because it was useful. Aid that props up dictators is not evidence against aid for public health or rural roads; it is evidence against using aid as a bribe.

Moreover, the benefits of aid may appear first in indicators other than GDP. Miguel acknowledges this, but the point deserves more weight. A child who survives malaria because of insecticide-treated bed nets, or stays in school because fees are abolished, may not immediately raise national income. The payoff comes later and is morally significant even if it never appears as a dramatic growth acceleration. The campaign to expand access to antiretroviral drugs is not a footnote to development. In countries where HIV/AIDS has devastated teachers, nurses, civil servants, soldiers, parents, and farmers, keeping adults alive is an economic intervention as well as a humanitarian one.

The same is true of education. Kenya’s move to free primary education after 2002, supported by donors, brought large numbers of children into school. Enrollment is not learning, and schools require teachers, books, and accountability. But the expansion of schooling is a foundation for any future manufacturing or service economy. No serious account of Asia’s rise ignores human capital. Africa’s “turn,” if it comes, will depend not only on export prices and elections, but on whether millions of children acquire literacy, numeracy, and health.

Aid can also support precisely the kind of conflict-prevention agenda Miguel proposes. In fact, his “rapid conflict prevention support” is an argument for more intelligent aid, not less aid. If droughts, commodity collapses, and income shocks increase the risk of civil war, then insurance-like transfers could be among the highest-return investments available. Wars destroy roads, schools, clinics, trust, and fiscal capacity. Preventing one civil war may repay decades of aid.

The challenge is implementation. Easterly’s strongest criticism is not that outsiders should be indifferent to African poverty; it is that planners often overpromise and ignore feedback. That criticism should lead to humility and experimentation, not withdrawal. Aid programs should be evaluated, adapted, and shut down when they fail. Miguel’s own career in program evaluation shows that we can learn what works in specific domains. Deworming, conditional or unconditional transfers, school incentives, bed nets, fertilizer subsidies, and local infrastructure projects can be studied. Evidence does not eliminate politics, but it improves the odds.

One reason aid disappoints is that donors frequently prefer their own priorities to recipient accountability. They create parallel systems, hire away talented civil servants, demand endless reports, and shift fashions every few years: structural adjustment, governance, gender mainstreaming, poverty reduction strategies, HIV/AIDS, now climate adaptation. African ministries spend too much time satisfying donors and too little time building durable systems. If aid is to support Africa’s recovery, donors must become more predictable and less theatrical.

That means funding recurrent costs when appropriate. Donors love capital projects because they are visible, but clinics need nurses’ salaries, roads need maintenance, and schools need textbooks year after year. Refusing to fund recurrent costs in very poor countries can be irrational. Of course such funding should be transparent and tied to performance, but the alternative is often empty buildings.

It also means recognizing that corruption is not solved by bypassing the state forever. Many donors respond to weak governments by routing aid through NGOs. NGOs can be effective, especially in emergencies, but no country has developed through NGOs substituting permanently for public authority. The state must eventually collect taxes, regulate markets, provide security, and deliver services. Aid should help build that capacity, while supporting civil society and media to monitor it.

Miguel’s essay rightly emphasizes the injustice of rich-country trade policy. Here aid and trade should not be pitted against each other. Ending cotton subsidies would help West African farmers; so would rural roads, agricultural research, credit access, and health services. Opening markets is necessary but not sufficient. A farmer cannot benefit from export opportunities if crops rot before reaching port or if children are too sick to attend school.

China’s role complicates the aid debate further. Chinese finance is often attractive because it delivers infrastructure quickly and with fewer lectures. Western donors should learn from that. Africans need roads, power plants, and ports, not only workshops on governance. But Chinese no-strings financing also underscores the importance of accountable domestic institutions. If Western aid is to remain relevant, it must combine practical investment with transparency, labor standards, and citizen oversight—not paternalistic conditionality, but public accountability.

The question “Is it Africa’s turn?” should not become an excuse for donors to declare victory and move on. Nor should Kenya’s crisis or Zimbabwe’s tragedy become excuses for cynicism. The right lesson is that development is cumulative and vulnerable. Health gains can be reversed by war; roads can decay; schools can deteriorate; democratic openings can close. Aid cannot substitute for African leadership, but it can widen the room in which that leadership operates.

The most defensible case for aid is modest but powerful: help people survive, learn, connect, and insure themselves against catastrophe. Do this transparently, evaluate honestly, coordinate better, and support rather than undermine accountable states. If Africa’s recent growth is to become more than a commodity boom, aid will not be the whole answer. But neither will Africa’s turn come faster because outsiders congratulate themselves on their skepticism and leave useful tools unused.
