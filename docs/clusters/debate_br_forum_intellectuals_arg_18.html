Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that intellectuals must reject the call to become militant partisans and instead uphold their primary duty to objective truth and institutional neutrality to preserve the university as a bulwark against authoritarianism."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s “The Responsibility of Intellectuals in the Age of Fascism and Genocide” is a passionate, searing, and profoundly alarming document. It is alarming not only because of the very real political crises it diagnoses—the resurgence of authoritarianism under a second Trump administration, the devastation in Gaza, and the intense pressures facing higher education—but because of the solution it proposes. Confronted with a political environment hostile to academic freedom and critical inquiry, Kelley urges intellectuals to abandon the traditional defense of objective truth-telling in favor of becoming militant partisans. In his view, the Chomskyan ideal of the intellectual who speaks truth to power is quaint and inadequate. Instead, Kelley calls for intellectuals to transform themselves into “organic intellectuals” in the Gramscian mold, entirely embedded within and fighting for an insurgent political class. While his frustration is understandable, his prescription represents a dangerous capitulation. By urging scholars to discard the normative constraints of the academy in favor of outright ideological warfare, Kelley risks destroying the very mechanisms of institutional independence and objective inquiry that make the university a valuable bulwark against authoritarianism in the first place.

To understand the gravity of Kelley’s departure, we must return to the foundational premise of Noam Chomsky’s 1967 essay, and before that, to Julien Benda’s 1927 classic, *The Treason of the Intellectuals* (*La Trahison des Clercs*). For Benda, and later for Chomsky, the intellectual’s primary duty was to universal principles of truth, justice, and reason, standing apart from the immediate political passions of the day. Chomsky certainly did not advocate for a sterile, apolitical detachment; his own life is a testament to fierce political engagement. However, Chomsky’s engagement was always rooted in an epistemological commitment to empirical reality. His task was to “expose the lies of governments” by bringing suppressed facts to light, trusting that a democratic public, once informed, would act. This requires a fundamental belief in a shared reality and in the intrinsic power of truth over propaganda.

Kelley explicitly rejects this premise. “Chomsky’s injunction to ‘speak the truth and to expose lies’ feels incomplete today,” he writes, declaring that “whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished.” In its place, he advocates for the Gramscian model, wherein the intellectual’s responsibility is “to choose a side and fight.” But this pivot from truth-telling to side-choosing is precisely what Benda warned against. When intellectuals decide that empirical truth is secondary to political solidarity, they cease to be intellectuals and become propagandists. They surrender the unique moral authority that comes from intellectual independence and reduce themselves to mere functionaries of a political movement.

The danger of this approach becomes glaringly apparent when we consider the right-wing parallel that Kelley himself identifies. He rightly notes that the MAGA movement has developed its own robust cadre of organic intellectuals—figures like Christopher Rufo, Stephen Miller, and the architects of Project 2025. These right-wing intellectuals operate exactly on the Gramscian model Kelley praises: they are completely embedded in their political bloc, they view institutions as sites of ideological warfare, and they freely subordinate objective truth to the pursuit of power. When Kelley suggests that left-wing intellectuals must adopt the same instrumentalist posture—that they must cast their lot with an “insurgent class” and use the university as a base for political mobilization—he is proposing a symmetrical warfare that the left cannot win, and which will invariably destroy the university in the process.

If both the right and the left agree that the university is nothing more than a contested terrain for ideological hegemony, then the conservative argument that higher education is merely a taxpayer-funded left-wing indoctrination camp is retrospectively validated. When conservative state legislatures and federal agencies threaten to defund universities, they do so on the premise that these institutions have abandoned their educational mission in favor of partisan activism. By demanding that universities formally align themselves with insurgent movements, Kelley provides the right with all the ammunition it needs to dismantle higher education entirely. The defense of the university must rest on its unique role as a sanctuary for free inquiry, objective research, and the pursuit of truth, regardless of where that truth leads. If we abandon that defense, we have no principled ground left to stand on when the state intrudes.

Furthermore, Kelley’s framework fundamentally misunderstands the role and responsibility of university administrators. He castigates administrators who have disciplined protesters or cleared encampments, accusing them of cowering before donors and capitulating to fascism. But this critique ignores the complex realities of institutional stewardship. The university is a pluralistic community that must serve thousands of students from vastly different backgrounds, holding wildly divergent political views. When an encampment occupies a central campus plaza, disrupts classes, or creates an environment where certain students feel unsafe or marginalized, the administration has a duty to intervene—not to crush dissent, but to maintain the functional operations of the educational institution.

Academic freedom protects the right of scholars like Tiffany Willoughby-Herard to express controversial views, to write radical critiques, and to participate in public debates without fear of professional reprisal. It does not, however, confer a right to indefinitely occupy campus property or to force the institution to adopt a specific foreign policy stance. By conflating academic freedom with the right to institutionalize a specific political agenda, Kelley overstretches the concept to the breaking point. The university cannot be a vanguard party; its bureaucratic and institutional structures are designed for education and research, not for prosecuting global social justice campaigns. When scholars demand that their institutions divest from Israel, sever ties with foreign universities, and issue declarations on global conflicts, they are asking the university to abandon its institutional neutrality. The Chicago Principles and the Kalven Report remain vital precisely because they recognize that if the institution takes a political stance, it chills the free expression of any scholar who might disagree with that stance.

Moreover, Kelley’s historical analogies, while emotionally resonant, obscure critical differences between the past and the present. He draws extensively on the history of Black radical antifascism in the 1930s and 1940s, citing figures like St. Clair Drake, Claudia Jones, and W. E. B. Du Bois. These organic intellectuals were fighting a regime of legalized apartheid in the Jim Crow South and pervasive, explicit white supremacy nationwide. Their struggle was against a system that legally barred them from basic participation in civic life. To equate the position of highly privileged, tenured professors at elite modern universities with the existential struggles of marginalized Black radicals in the early 20th century is a profound category error.

Today’s academic left possesses immense institutional power, controlling the curricula, the hiring committees, and the disciplinary norms of virtually every major humanities and social science department in the country. When conservative actors like the Heritage Foundation attack the university, they are responding to this reality. This is not to justify the right’s draconian overreach, which must be fiercely resisted. But resisting it requires acknowledging that the university is not an oppressed subaltern class. It is a powerful, elite institution, and its defense must be articulated in terms of its value to the broader, pluralistic democratic society.

Kelley’s dismissal of “truth-telling” is perhaps the most tragic element of his essay, for it underestimates the enduring power of facts. He laments that “there is ample scholarship proving that immigrants do not take jobs... that the 2020 election wasn’t stolen,” implying that because the right ignores these facts, the facts themselves are useless. This is a fatalistic and ultimately nihilistic view. The struggle for truth is always arduous, and propagandists will always try to drown it out. But the exposure of lies remains the most potent weapon against tyranny. When authoritarian regimes consolidate power, their first target is not the political activist, but the independent journalist and the objective historian. They fear the truth far more than they fear a rival ideology.

If the left abandons the terrain of objective truth to fight purely on the terrain of power and solidarity, it fights on the enemy’s terms. The moral authority of the anti-war movement during Vietnam, which Chomsky so brilliantly articulated, rested on the fact that the government was lying, and the intellectuals could prove it. The moral authority of the Civil Rights movement rested on the undeniable truth of American hypocrisy, exposing the gap between the nation’s founding ideals and its racist realities. These movements did not say, “Truth doesn’t matter; only our side matters.” They weaponized the truth.

In this dark political moment, the responsibility of the intellectual is not to become a foot soldier for a political faction, nor to transform the university into an activist NGO. The responsibility of the intellectual is to fiercely defend the institutional boundaries that make independent thought possible. It is to insist on nuance where partisans demand simplicity, to demand evidence where ideologues offer slogans, and to protect the university as a space where even the most uncomfortable truths can be pursued without fear.

Kelley envisions a “new university” built entirely around political solidarity and the explicit goal of transforming the world. But such an institution would not be a university at all; it would be a political action committee with a library attached. Defeating authoritarianism requires robust political movements, yes, but it also requires institutions that stand apart from the political fray, guarding the flame of objective inquiry. If intellectuals betray that calling in the name of political expediency, they will have truly handed a victory to the very forces they wish to defeat.
