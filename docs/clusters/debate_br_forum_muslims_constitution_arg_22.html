"The domestic persecution and surveillance of Muslims in America are not merely civil rights issues but are necessary symptoms of a U.S. foreign policy rooted in military imperialism and global hegemony."

In their lead essay, Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis provide a crucial intervention into the current liberal panic over the Trump administration’s travel ban. They rightly observe that the outrage directed at Donald Trump, while justified, suffers from a profound myopia. The architecture of state violence directed at Muslims was not drafted in January 2017; it was meticulously constructed over the last fifteen years by both George W. Bush and Barack Obama. However, while Akbar and Theoharis astutely dismantle the domestic legal and cultural paradigms of Islamophobia, they leave their most vital point—Point Six, the role of U.S. imperialism—until the very end. I argue that this is a structural mistake. The domestic policing of Muslims in America is not a parallel phenomenon to global war; it is a direct, necessary symptom of a U.S. foreign policy fundamentally dependent on military hegemony and the control of the Global South.

To understand the domestic War on Terror, we must first understand that the United States is an empire in a state of perpetual war. Since the end of the Cold War, and accelerating dramatically after September 11, 2001, the U.S. military-industrial complex has required a new, omnipresent enemy to justify its staggering budgets, its sprawling network of global military bases, and its uninterrupted campaigns of resource extraction and geopolitical dominance in the Middle East and North Africa. The "War on Terror" provided this exact justification.

When the U.S. state drops 23,000 bombs in a single year on Muslim-majority nations, as it did in 2015 under a Nobel Peace Prize-winning Democratic president, it requires a powerful ideological apparatus to manufacture consent among the domestic populace. You cannot sustain a global campaign of targeted assassinations, drone strikes, proxy wars, and occupations without first dehumanizing the populations you are incinerating. Islamophobia is the ideological lubricant that allows the machinery of U.S. imperialism to function without grinding to a halt under the weight of domestic anti-war resistance.

Therefore, the surveillance, the material support prosecutions, and the Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs that Akbar and Theoharis detail are not merely domestic civil rights abuses; they are counter-insurgency tactics brought home. The FBI and the NYPD do not surveil Muslim communities simply because of blind, irrational prejudice. They do so because the state inherently views diasporic communities linked to regions it is bombing as potential fifth columns. The state understands that legitimate political dissent against American foreign policy—particularly from within the Muslim community—poses a threat to the imperial consensus.

This is why the material support statute is so broadly interpreted. It is designed to sever the organic ties of solidarity between Muslims in the imperial core and those in the neo-colonies. By criminalizing charitable giving and political speech, the U.S. government effectively quarantines American Muslims, forcing them to disavow the suffering of Palestinians, Yemenis, and Iraqis to prove their loyalty to the state that is causing that suffering.

The authors astutely point out the bipartisan consensus that demands Muslims sacrifice their bodies or their rights to be accepted, highlighting Bill Clinton’s deeply conditional welcome at the DNC. But this conditional welcome is the classic bargain offered by empires to their internal minorities: you may enjoy the spoils of the imperial core, provided you enthusiastically participate in the subjugation of the periphery. The elevation of Khizr Khan is the perfect embodiment of this dynamic. He was celebrated not because he was a Muslim demanding equal rights, but because he was a Muslim whose son died serving the very military apparatus that devastated the Middle East. The liberal establishment loves the “good Muslim” precisely because the good Muslim validates the empire.

By treating the domestic civil rights abuses as the primary disease rather than a symptom, we risk engaging in a futile game of legal whack-a-mole. We can advocate for the closure of Guantánamo, the reform of the No Fly List, and the curtailing of the NSA's domestic surveillance. But if the fundamental driver of these policies—the need to maintain unquestioned U.S. hegemony in the Middle East—remains intact, the national security state will simply invent new legal fictions and operational paradigms to control the domestic Muslim population.

Furthermore, we must recognize that the violence inflicted on Muslim communities is deeply intertwined with global capitalism. The regions targeted by U.S. aggression are rich in energy resources and sit on vital strategic trade routes. The devastation of Iraq, the destabilization of Libya, and the ongoing support for the Saudi war in Yemen are not accidents of a poorly executed counterterrorism strategy; they are calculated moves to maintain global economic dominance. The domestic Muslim—stigmatized, surveilled, and precariously positioned—serves as a convenient scapegoat for the domestic failures of neoliberalism. As austerity guts social services and globalization destroys working-class communities at home, the state redirects the resulting anger toward the fabricated threat of the "radical Islamic terrorist."

Trump’s genius—if it can be called that—was to strip away the polite, technocratic veneer of Obama’s imperialism and expose the raw, racist id of the American empire. The travel ban, which explicitly targets refugees fleeing the very wars the U.S. initiated or fueled, is a grotesque manifestation of this dynamic. But we cannot fight Trumpism by nostalgically retreating to the Obama-era norms of covert drone strikes and polite, institutionalized surveillance.

If we are to take Akbar and Theoharis’s essay seriously, we must recognize that a genuine defense of Muslim communities requires a radical, anti-imperialist politics. It is not enough to demand First Amendment protections for Muslims in New York or Detroit; we must demand an end to the U.S. war machine in Sana'a, Kabul, and Raqqa. We must explicitly link the fight against domestic surveillance and police militarization to the fight against the military-industrial complex. We must reject the very premise of the War on Terror, recognizing it not as a flawed security strategy, but as a project of global domination that inevitably requires the suspension of democracy at home.

In this moment of profound political crisis, the resistance cannot merely be a defense of the status quo ante. It must be a wholesale rejection of the imperial consensus. Until we are willing to confront the United States' role as the primary purveyor of violence in the world, the trial of Islam—and the subjugation of Muslim bodies both at home and abroad—will continue unabated.
