What the Forum Misses: A View from Inside the Community

I want to begin by thanking Amna Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis for an essay that names what too many of us inside Muslim American communities have been saying for years and have not been heard saying. The bipartisan architecture of suspicion they describe is not a theoretical construct to us. It is the FBI agent who came to my cousin's college dorm in 2011 and asked about his prayers. It is the imam in our Friday congregation who quietly told us, in 2014, that he would no longer take certain questions at the open-mic session because he could not be sure who in the room was reporting back. It is the cousin's friend's brother who took a guilty plea after eighteen months of solitary pretrial confinement because his lawyer told him no jury would acquit a young Yemeni-American whose Facebook posts the government would read aloud in court.

So when the lead essay says that the airport protests rest on a "particularized notion of Muslim innocence," I understand exactly what the authors mean, and I want to complicate it from inside.

It is true that the public energy of January 28 and 29 was organized around a particular kind of victim: the doctor returning to her hospital, the elderly mother coming to meet her grandchildren, the Iraqi interpreter who had risked his life for American soldiers and now had a green card in his pocket. The visibility of those victims served a strategic purpose. It pierced the cable-news abstraction of "the ban" with bodies. It told the rest of America: these are not the people you fear, and you should be ashamed for letting your government treat them this way. That framing brought hundreds of thousands of people to airports and pressured judges and lit up congressional offices. That is not nothing. It is, in many ways, the most public Muslim defense the United States has seen in fifteen years.

But the framing came at a cost, and the cost falls on people in my community who do not look like the doctor or the grandmother. It falls on the Yemeni teenager whose Twitter feed includes angry posts about Saudi airstrikes. It falls on the Pakistani graduate student who attended a conference where a speaker said unkind things about U.S. drone policy. It falls on the Somali kid in Minneapolis whose older brother went to fight—on the wrong side of a confusing civil war—and who is now an "associate" in some FBI file. The protests at JFK were not for them. The judge in the Eastern District of New York did not enjoin their predicament. They remain in the place American Muslims have lived since 2001: under suspicion, with their innocence forever provisional, awaiting the next ambiguous social media post or unwise friendship that will turn them into the case study.

This is the deeper point Akbar and Theoharis are making, and it deserves more elaboration than the essay gives. The category of the "good Muslim" is not just a frame imposed from outside. It is a discipline internalized within our communities, often in painful and divisive ways. The imam learns to preach a sermon that will read innocuously if transcribed by an informant. The mother counsels her son to stay away from political activity, to avoid Palestine rallies, to never share an opinion about Syria online. The civic organization writes its mission statement to include endless invocations of "American values" and quietly removes from its programming any speaker who has been critical of U.S. foreign policy. These are not just rhetorical adjustments. They are the slow, granular reshaping of religious and political life under conditions of surveillance, and they happen whether or not anyone is actually watching.

CVE was perhaps the most insidious version of this discipline because it offered grant money. The Obama administration's pilot programs in Boston, Los Angeles, and Minneapolis invited Muslim community organizations to apply for funds to address "radicalization," which in practice meant accepting the premise that radicalization was the primary issue facing our youth—not Islamophobic bullying, not police harassment, not the trauma of having a parent in immigration detention, not the ordinary travails of growing up Muslim in post-9/11 America, but radicalization, full stop. Some organizations took the money and tried to redirect it. Others refused on principle. The split tore at communities. People who had worked together for years stopped trusting each other. The Trump administration's reorientation of CVE makes this worse, but the damage was already done.

I want to register one place where I think the lead essay could have gone further. The authors note correctly that "Muslims and terrorism are seen as inseparable" in much the same way that "blackness and criminality have been conflated." This is right and important. But the framing risks treating Muslims and Black Americans as parallel rather than overlapping categories. A quarter or more of American Muslims are Black—descendants of enslaved Africans, members of historically African American Muslim communities, immigrants from East and West Africa. For these Muslims, the war on terror and the war on drugs have never been separate. They live both at once. The Black Somali kid in Minneapolis who is profiled by Minneapolis PD on the way to school and surveilled by JTTF in the evening does not experience two distinct regimes; he experiences one continuous regime of suspicion calibrated to his particular intersection.

The lead essay gestures at this with the line about Ferguson tanks, but I would push harder. The tools of mass incarceration—prosecutorial overcharging, plea coercion, pretrial detention, civil asset forfeiture, gang databases—translate almost seamlessly into the terrorism context. The terrorism informant program is genetically related to the drug informant program. The DEA built it first; the FBI scaled it up. The targeting of mosques in the NYPD's Demographics Unit was performed by officers trained in the same techniques used to target housing projects. We will not understand any of this if we separate the regimes analytically just because we encounter them through different bureaucracies.

Where I find myself slightly hesitant about the lead essay is its tone toward the airport protestors. The authors are scrupulous, but the implication—that the protests rested on a problematic frame—reads, in places, as a critique of the mobilization itself. I would frame it differently. The airport protests were a beginning, not an end. They were what was politically possible in the first 72 hours of a shocking executive action. The work of expanding that solidarity outward—to the No Fly List, to the prosecutions, to the drone war, to the deportation of Somali asylum seekers, to the ongoing CVE program—is the work of the next several years, and it will be done in part by the people who were at the airports. To begin that work by telling them their first instinct was tainted is, I worry, bad politics, even if the underlying analysis is right.

What I would have wanted in the lead essay is a clearer sense of where the political coalition goes next. The airport protests reached people who had not previously been activated. Many of them are open to the deeper critique. Many of them have Muslim coworkers, neighbors, friends, in-laws. Many of them are now reading their first articles about the No Fly List, the material support statute, FISA, Special Administrative Measures. The pedagogical opportunity is enormous. What we need now are organizers, lawyers, scholars, and journalists who can take the energy of the airports and walk it through the corridors of the federal courthouse, the FISA Court, the immigration detention center, the CVE office, the drone targeting room.

Akbar and Theoharis have written, I think, the opening argument of that pedagogy. I am grateful for it, and I am hopeful, with the kind of hope that comes from having seen our communities survive worse and from believing that this country is capable of telling itself a better story than the one it has been telling about us for the last fifteen years.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The focus on 'innocent' victims during airport protests, while strategically effective for mobilization, reinforces a harmful dichotomy that leaves more vulnerable or politically active Muslims under permanent suspicion.
  2. 02State-sponsored programs like Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) have forced Muslim communities to internalize state suspicion, causing deep internal divisions and the self-censorship of religious and political life.
  3. 03The 'War on Terror' and the 'War on Drugs' are not merely parallel but are a single continuous regime of suspicion for Black Muslims who face the combined tools of mass incarceration and national security surveillance.
  4. 04The legal and policing tactics used against Muslims, such as informant programs and aggressive surveillance, are direct descendants of techniques originally developed for the domestic war on drugs and the policing of urban housing projects.
  5. 05Rather than critiquing the 'tainted' framing of the airport protests, activists should view that energy as a pedagogical starting point to lead the public toward a deeper critique of the broader national security state.
