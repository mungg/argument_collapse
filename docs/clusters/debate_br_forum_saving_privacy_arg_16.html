Diversified Gemini

"Reed Hundt’s proposal to save privacy fails because it ignores the predatory nature of commercial data harvesting and places an unrealistic burden on individuals to use complex encryption tools rather than advocating for the direct regulation of the tech industry."

In "Saving Privacy," Reed Hundt rightly rings the alarm about the vast surveillance apparatus exposed by Edward Snowden over the past year. However, in his eagerness to champion a new era of digital rights, Hundt makes a profound error: he treats the corporate harvesting of personal data as a benign side effect of the digital economy, focusing his ire almost entirely on government overreach. In doing so, he lets Silicon Valley off the hook and proposes tech-utopian solutions that are utterly unworkable for the average citizen.

Hundt argues that the corporate and government goals are different. Firms just want to sell ads, he claims, and "individuals rarely object to seeing ads as a price they pay for the free services of social networks." This drastically understates the reality of the commercial surveillance economy in 2014. The issue is not merely that users see targeted advertisements. The issue is that the business model of the modern internet requires the ceaseless, ubiquitous tracking of human behavior across the web and physical space.

Google, Facebook, and shadowy data brokers are not just predicting shopping preferences; they are cataloging our political affiliations, health anxieties, financial struggles, and social vulnerabilities. Consider the ongoing investigations by the Federal Trade Commission into data brokers who sell lists of consumers categorized by their vulnerabilities—such as "rural and barely making it" or "gullible elderly." This is not a harmless exchange of data for services; it is a structural exploitation of the public. When Hundt writes that citizens "become statistically defined groups" under government surveillance, he misses that corporations are already doing this to dictate our life chances and shape our behaviors.

The government’s surveillance apparatus, which Hundt rightly criticizes, does not exist in a vacuum. It is built entirely atop the commercial surveillance infrastructure. The NSA did not have to build a global network of sensors from scratch; Silicon Valley built it for them. Programs like PRISM are the inevitable result of a business model that centralizes the private lives of billions of people on a few corporate servers. If we want to solve the problem of government surveillance, we cannot merely tweak the rules of when the government is allowed to look at the data. We must fundamentally attack the corporate practice of collecting and hoarding that data in the first place.

Hundt’s proposed solution—that individuals should protect themselves by retreating to encrypted enclaves like Tor and managing their transactions via Bitcoin—is a libertarian fantasy that ignores the realities of daily life. It places the entire burden of privacy on the user. We are effectively told that if we do not wish to be surveilled, we must become amateur cryptographers, managing secure keys, navigating the notoriously slow Tor network, and abandoning the dominant social platforms where civic and family life now take place.

This is not a recipe for democratic empowerment; it is a recipe for a two-tiered society. A small, technically literate elite will enjoy privacy in their encrypted walled gardens, while the vast majority of citizens—who lack the time, resources, or technical knowledge to manage these tools—will remain subject to total corporate and state surveillance. Privacy should not be a luxury good or a prize for the technologically savvy. It should be a fundamental right protected by law.

Instead of subsidizing Bitcoin or running public service campaigns about Tor, the government needs to exercise its regulatory power to rein in the tech monopolies. A true digital bill of rights would not focus on empowering pseudonymous communities; it would establish strict data minimization laws. It would declare that companies simply cannot collect and retain data that is not strictly necessary for the service they provide. It would implement robust "opt-in" requirements for data sharing, rather than the deceptive "opt-out" labyrinths we face today. It would hold tech executives legally liable for negligent data practices.

Hundt claims the fundamental question is "Who rules?" But in a world where digital infrastructure is entirely owned by unaccountable corporations whose sole imperative is maximizing engagement and data extraction, the answer is certainly not "the individual." Until we are willing to treat the massive concentration of corporate data as a regulatory and antitrust crisis, no amount of encryption or blockchain innovation will save our privacy. We must dismantle the commercial surveillance machine at its roots, not just teach a few savvy users how to hide from it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Corporate data collection is not a benign exchange for free services but a structural exploitation that categorizes and manipulates vulnerable populations.
  2. 02Government surveillance is a parasitic outgrowth of the commercial surveillance infrastructure, meaning state overreach cannot be solved without first dismantling corporate data hoarding.
  3. 03Relying on tools like Tor and Bitcoin creates a two-tiered society where privacy is a luxury for the technically elite rather than a universal right.
  4. 04A meaningful digital bill of rights must prioritize strict data minimization laws and legal liability for tech executives over tech-utopian solutions like the blockchain.
  5. 05Privacy can only be restored by treating the concentration of corporate data as a regulatory and antitrust crisis that requires state intervention to dismantle the surveillance business model.
