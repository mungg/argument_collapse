Diversified MiniMax

"While Peter Singer’s effective altruism provides a valuable framework for individual giving, it is limited by an individualistic bias that prioritizes measurable personal charity over the collective political and economic action needed to address the root causes of suffering."

Peter Singer makes a compelling case that we should use evidence and reason to determine how best to help others. But I am struck by what the essay does not discuss: the political and economic structures that create and perpetuate suffering. Effective altruism is presented as an individual project—as a matter of personal choices about careers, donations, and lifestyle. But the great moral crises of our time are collective problems requiring collective action.

Consider global poverty. Singer recommends donating to organizations that provide bed nets, deworming treatments, or cash transfers to the global poor. These interventions can help individuals, but they do not address why poverty exists in the first place. The answer lies in historical and ongoing structures: colonialism and its aftermath, unfair trade rules, intellectual property regimes that prevent poor countries from accessing life-saving medicines, climate change caused primarily by wealthy nations, and many other factors.

An effective response to global poverty would include advocating for policy changes that address these root causes. But such advocacy is less tractable than buying bed nets, and its impact is harder to measure. Singer acknowledges that effective altruists might choose to focus on systemic issues, but the movement's culture and incentives favor direct interventions with clear outcomes.

This individualistic bias has political implications. If we believe that moral progress depends primarily on individual choices about giving, we are less likely to demand systemic change. Why push for higher foreign aid budgets, debt cancellation, or fair trade rules when we can assuage our guilt by donating to effective charities? The result is a politics that locates moral responsibility in personal virtue rather than public policy.

Singer might respond that effective altruism is compatible with political engagement. He mentions that effective altruists can choose different causes, including those that address structural problems. But the movement's organizational center of gravity—theGiveWell model, the emphasis on measurable outcomes, the career guides that recommend high-earning professions—favors individual optimization over collective action.

Consider the Matt Wage example again. A talented young person chooses a lucrative career specifically to maximize his donations. This is presented as a noble sacrifice. But what if Wage had used his talents to become a legislator, a journalist, or a policy advocate? These paths might have allowed him to influence decisions that affect millions of people, potentially doing more good than any individual donation. They would also require different skills—political judgment, coalition-building, public persuasion—than those valued by the effective altruism movement.

I do not mean to suggest that personal giving is worthless. Singer is right that we should take seriously our obligation to help others and that we should think carefully about how to do so effectively. But effective altruism, as currently practiced, risks substituting individual charity for political responsibility. A truly effective approach to reducing suffering would include both, but the balance should shift more toward collective action than Singer acknowledges.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Effective altruism focuses on direct interventions like bed nets while ignoring the systemic structures—such as colonialism, unfair trade, and climate change—that perpetuate global poverty.
  2. 02The movement’s insistence on quantifiable and tractable outcomes inherently disincentivizes advocacy for policy changes, which are harder to measure but potentially more impactful.
  3. 03By framing moral responsibility as a matter of personal virtue and lifestyle choices, effective altruism risks undermining the public pressure necessary for systemic reforms like debt cancellation or increased foreign aid.
  4. 04The movement’s career advice encourages high-earning professions for 'earning to give' rather than influential roles in legislation, journalism, or advocacy that require collective-action skills like coalition-building.
  5. 05A truly effective approach to reducing suffering must balance personal charitable obligations with a commitment to political responsibility and structural change.
Diversified MiniMax

"While Peter Singer’s effective altruism provides a useful framework for individual giving, it is ethically limited because it prioritizes personal philanthropy over the systemic political and economic reforms necessary to address the root causes of global suffering."

Peter Singer writes as if the great moral question of our time is how individuals should allocate their charitable donations. This framing is seductive—it places moral responsibility squarely on personal choice and implies that with sufficient reason and evidence, we can each become ethical heroes. But this focus on individual giving obscures a more fundamental truth: that poverty, suffering, and premature death are primarily products of political and economic structures, and that changing those structures is both more important and more difficult than optimizing our personal philanthropy.

Singer mentions that Americans give $200 billion to charity annually, plus $100 billion to religious congregations. This is presented as a problem of allocation—that too much of this money goes to ineffective causes. But the more striking fact is that $300 billion in private giving is necessary to address needs that should be met through public provision. In a just society, children would not die of malaria because private donors chose to fund bed nets. They would not die of preventable diseases because public health systems provided basic care. The failure is not that rich people give to the wrong charities; it is that political decisions have created a world where billions of people lack access to resources that could save their lives.

Singer's framework treats the existing distribution of wealth as given, then asks how those with surplus resources should allocate them. But this approach accepts an unjust baseline. If the global economy were structured differently—if corporations paid fair wages, if trade agreements protected workers and farmers in developing countries, if wealthy nations honored their aid commitments—we might not need effective altruism at all. The moral urgency of individual giving is partly a symptom of systemic failure.

Consider the malaria example. Singer recommends donating to the Against Malaria Foundation, which provides bed nets. This is effective, as far as it goes. But why do millions of people live in regions where malaria is endemic? In part because colonial powers extracted resources and created borders without regard for indigenous communities, because postcolonial governments have been corrupted and destabilized, because global pharmaceutical companies prioritize profitable markets over diseases that primarily affect the poor. The bed nets address symptoms; the causes remain.

Some effective altruists respond that we should focus on what we can control. Individual donors cannot change global trade rules, but they can buy bed nets. This is pragmatically true but ethically limiting. Singer himself argues that we should use evidence and reason to do the most good. If evidence shows that political advocacy and collective action can address root causes more effectively than private charity, shouldn't effective altruists support those approaches too?

The Matt Wage example illustrates the problem. A brilliant young philosopher chooses arbitrage trading in order to maximize his donations. But what if he had used those same intellectual gifts to research economic policies that benefit the global poor? What if he had become a policy advocate, an academic studying development economics, or a civil servant working to reform international institutions? These paths might have less obvious impact, but they could address structural causes in ways that individual donations cannot.

I do not mean to dismiss the value of effective interventions that save lives now. Singer is right that we should not let perfect be the enemy of good. But effective altruism needs to expand its ambition beyond individual charitable allocation. A truly effective approach to global suffering would include advocacy for policy change, support for democratic movements, engagement with electoral politics, and pressure on corporations and governments. These are less tractable than buying bed nets, but they are where the real leverage lies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The need for massive private charity is a symptom of systemic failure and the absence of essential public health systems that should be guaranteed through political action.
  2. 02Singer’s framework erroneously treats the current unjust distribution of wealth as a fixed baseline rather than questioning the economic structures that create such inequality.
  3. 03Focusing on immediate interventions like bed nets addresses only the symptoms of poverty while ignoring the historical, colonial, and corporate drivers of global crises.
  4. 04The movement’s emphasis on 'earning to give' overlooks the potentially greater impact individuals could have through policy advocacy, academic research, or institutional reform.
  5. 05To be truly effective, altruism must expand its scope to include collective action and political engagement aimed at changing the global structures that perpetuate suffering.
