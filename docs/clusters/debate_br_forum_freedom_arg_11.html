"A truly emancipatory left-wing politics must reject the artificial separation between domestic liberty and foreign policy by confronting the imperial structures that define American freedom through the coercion of others."

Aziz Rana’s most important contribution is to refuse the artificial separation between domestic freedom and foreign policy. In the United States, that separation has always been ideological. The frontier, the plantation, the reservation, the colony, the military base, the sanctions regime, the arms shipment, the border checkpoint, and the workplace are not identical institutions, but they belong to a shared history of power. Americans have repeatedly been invited to experience themselves as free through arrangements that deny self-rule to others. A left that does not confront this fact will never build a genuinely emancipatory account of freedom.

Rana’s genealogy of settler freedom is persuasive because it explains why anti-statism in the United States is so selective. The state is tyranny when it taxes, regulates, desegregates, recognizes labor rights, protects abortion, or limits firearms. The state is freedom when it removes Indigenous nations, polices Black neighborhoods, cages migrants, funds prisons, subsidizes suburbs, invades foreign countries, or supplies weapons to allies. This is not hypocrisy in the ordinary sense. It is a political tradition in which the legitimate subject of freedom is defined against populations available for coercion.

The same structure has shaped U.S. global power. After World War II, American officials presented their country as leader of the “free world,” but freedom was often defined as alignment with U.S. strategic and economic priorities. When decolonizing countries sought land reform, resource sovereignty, nonalignment, or socialized development, they were frequently treated as immature, communist-influenced, or dangerous. The point is not that every anticolonial leader was democratic or every U.S. concern was invented. The point is that the United States claimed the authority to decide which forms of self-rule counted. That authority itself was imperial.

The New International Economic Order, which Rana mentions, is crucial here. It reminds us that postcolonial freedom was not merely flag independence. Leaders and movements across the Global South understood that juridical sovereignty meant little if trade, debt, investment, technology, and commodity prices remained structured by the old imperial powers and their successors. They asked a question Americans rarely ask: can a people be politically free if the terms of economic life are set elsewhere? That question applies to countries, but also to workers, tenants, and debtors.

This is why the left should not treat anti-imperialism as a boutique cause or a moral supplement to bread-and-butter politics. Empire is bread-and-butter politics. The military budget competes with social spending. Arms production shapes congressional coalitions. Fossil fuel geopolitics delays climate transition. Sanctions affect migration. Trade rules empower corporations. Wars produce surveillance and racism at home. Veterans return to underfunded systems of care. Police departments receive military equipment and training. Border enforcement imports counterinsurgency logics. The empire comes home because it was never truly abroad.

The post-9/11 era should have ended any innocence about this. The war in Afghanistan began after a real atrocity, but it became a twenty-year demonstration of the limits of military domination. The Iraq War was sold through fear, falsehood, and liberationist fantasy; it killed and displaced vast numbers of Iraqis, destabilized a region, empowered contractors, normalized torture, and corroded American democracy. “Operation Enduring Freedom” is now an emblem of how the word freedom can be emptied by force. If freedom means anything, it cannot mean the right of one country to remake another through invasion.

The current war in Gaza has again exposed the gap between official American freedom talk and global perception. Hamas’s October 7 attacks on civilians were atrocities and should be condemned without equivocation. But the Israeli government’s massive bombardment of Gaza, siege conditions, displacement of civilians, and intensifying violence in the West Bank have produced a humanitarian catastrophe. Much of the world sees the United States not as a neutral defender of freedom but as the indispensable backer of Israeli impunity. American officials speak of a rules-based order, yet many in the Global South hear rules applied selectively: Ukraine’s sovereignty matters, Palestine’s self-determination is deferred; civilian life is sacred in one theater, collateral in another. The damage to U.S. legitimacy is not a public relations problem. It is a moral and political problem.

Rana calls for integrating an anti-imperial ethic into everyday American interests. That is exactly right, but we should be bolder about what it requires. First, the United States must dramatically reduce military spending. A country that spends nearly as much on defense as the rest of the world combined cannot plausibly claim that its priorities are democratic need. The issue is not only waste. It is the political economy of militarism: jobs tied to weapons, universities tied to defense grants, legislators tied to bases and contractors, foreign policy tied to corporate profit. Conversion planning—turning military-industrial capacity toward renewable energy, transit, housing, disaster preparedness, and care infrastructure—should be central to any freedom agenda.

Second, the United States should end support for authoritarian clients and condition military aid on human rights and international law, including for close allies. This will be denounced as naïve, but the true naïveté is believing that unconditional backing produces security. It produces dependency, resentment, and moral hazard. A freedom-centered foreign policy would not assume that friends of Washington are friends of democracy.

Third, sanctions policy needs democratic scrutiny. Broad sanctions often punish civilian populations while entrenching ruling elites. They allow American politicians to appear tough without accepting responsibility for humanitarian consequences. Targeted measures against specific officials may sometimes be justified, but economic warfare should not become the default tool of a country unwilling to negotiate with adversaries.

Fourth, immigration must be understood as part of imperial history. People do not move only because of U.S. policy, but U.S. policy has shaped the conditions of movement: interventions, trade arrangements, drug war militarization, climate emissions, support for repressive governments, and economic restructuring. A humane immigration system is not charity. It is partial accountability. Decriminalizing entry, expanding asylum, protecting labor rights, and creating broad legalization would weaken the border as a caste-making institution.

The objection will be that this agenda ignores threats. It does not. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine is a violation of sovereignty. China’s repression in Xinjiang and Hong Kong and pressure on Taiwan raise real concerns. Armed groups can commit atrocities. States have security interests. But anti-imperialism does not require pacifist denial. It requires that the United States give up the presumption that its dominance is synonymous with order. It requires strengthening international institutions even when they constrain Washington. It requires diplomacy not as a pause before coercion but as a practice of coexistence. It requires asking how others define their freedom.

Rana is also right that freedom struggles never fully escape domination. Anti-imperial movements can become authoritarian. Postcolonial states can oppress minorities. Multipolarity alone is not justice; a world with several empires is not liberation. That is why the standard must be self-rule joined to equality, not merely sovereignty. Still, the current U.S. order fails that standard dramatically.

The left’s freedom project will remain hollow if it promises democracy at home while accepting hierarchy abroad. Workers cannot learn solidarity from a state that teaches them the world is divided between those who command and those who must be disciplined. Citizens cannot build self-rule while their government claims exemption from rules it enforces on others. The American imagination of freedom has been imperial for too long. To free it, we must make the country less imperial.
