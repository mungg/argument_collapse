"While Scarry’s call to democratize national defense is a vital critique of authoritarian security models, her reliance on the 'citizen-soldier' ideal risks valorizing masculine sacrificial violence and coercive state practices like the draft."

Elaine Scarry’s essay is a welcome invitation to think about security in terms of citizens rather than subjects. By drawing our attention to the acts of the passengers on Flight 93, she reminds us that ordinary people, when equipped with knowledge and the freedom to act, can respond to violence with speed, courage, and moral clarity. Her critique of the centralized, secretive, and increasingly authoritarian model of “homeland security” that has emerged since last September is urgent, and her warnings about the corrosion of democracy by nuclear weapons and executive war-making deserve the widest possible hearing. Yet as a feminist and a civil libertarian, I find myself both drawn to her vision of an empowered citizenry and troubled by the undertones of her argument, which risk valorizing a form of masculine, sacrificial violence even as it seeks to democratize defense.

Scarry’s central example, Flight 93, is indeed a story of collective action under duress. The passengers gathered information, consulted with loved ones, prayed, voted, and then charged the cockpit. It is a moving testament to human agency in the face of certain death. But the framework Scarry builds around it—the “citizen-soldier,” the “egalitarian model,” the return to the militia—has a distinct gender and cultural valence. The passengers were, in her account, enacting a constitutional ideal of armed citizenship that has historically been closely tied to notions of republican manhood. The Second Amendment, which she invokes approvingly, was for much of American history the province of white male property-holders, a guarantee of their capacity for collective violence against external enemies and internal insurrections. To hold up this model as the democratic alternative to Pentagon bureaucracy is to overlook the ways in which such militias have also been instruments of exclusion, vigilantism, and state-sanctioned violence against marginalized communities.

The more immediate danger is that Scarry’s celebration of citizen action on planes may inadvertently underwrite the very erosion of civil liberties she deplores. In the months since 9/11, we have witnessed a frenzy of official hand-wringing about how to “empower” passengers to fight hijackers, alongside a massive expansion of surveillance, detention, and racial profiling. The “shoe bomber” incident last December was thwarted not by heroic passengers acting after a vote, but by flight attendants and passengers physically subduing a man—an act of immediate self-defense that, while commendable, required no elaborate democratic deliberation. The risk is that a culture of citizen vigilance, encouraged by the state, becomes indistinguishable from a culture of suspicion and snitching. The government that Scarry criticizes for proposing to commandeer cell phone networks is the same government that would happily deputize every airline passenger as a de facto law enforcement agent, shifting the burden of security onto individuals while the state hoards the real instruments of violence.

Scarry’s argument about consent is powerful but incomplete. She insists, correctly, that only the passengers themselves had the moral right to decide to sacrifice their lives to save others on the ground. But this principle of consent does not apply only in airplanes. The entire “war on terrorism” has been launched without any meaningful consent from the American people, let alone from the populations on whom it is being waged. Women in Afghanistan have been paraded as the war’s beneficiaries, yet their voices were absent from the deliberations that led to the bombing. The detainees held incommunicado in Guantánamo and in jails across America have no ability to consent to or contest their fate. If we take the Flight 93 model seriously, then no person’s life should be used as a means to a security end without their genuine participation. That principle demands an end to secret detentions, racial profiling, and wars justified by executive fiat.

The nuclear question, too, benefits from a feminist lens. Scarry rightly calls nuclear weapons “monarchic,” but they are also profoundly patriarchal artifacts, the ultimate expression of a logic of total domination and annihilation that has long been critiqued by feminist anti-militarists. The “hairtrigger” is not just a threat to democracy; it is the phallic symbol of a system that equates safety with the capacity for instantaneous, world-ending violence. A truly democratic defense would begin by renouncing this form of power altogether, as Scarry argues. But doing so requires more than a constitutional rebalancing; it requires a cultural transformation in which security is redefined in terms of human well-being rather than the capacity to inflict mass death.

Scarry’s proposal to reinstate the draft is particularly problematic from a feminist standpoint. Conscription has historically been a male-coded obligation of citizenship, tied to the exclusion of women from full political standing. Even if a future draft were gender-neutral, its effect would be to coerce bodies into state service, a violation of bodily autonomy that feminists have long resisted in contexts from reproductive rights to military service. There are better ways to ensure that the costs of war are democratically shared: robust public debate, genuine congressional oversight, and a tax system that makes the financial burden of militarism visible.

Ultimately, the great virtue of Scarry’s essay is its insistence that the emergency we face is as much about democracy as it is about security. But the democratic defense she envisions must be one that not only distributes the power to kill but also radically constrains it. The passengers of Flight 93 did not ask to be heroes. They were forced into a situation where violence was the only exit. A democratic society should aim to build a world where fewer and fewer people ever face that choice. That means demilitarizing our foreign policy, redirecting resources toward life-sustaining needs, and constructing a concept of security grounded not in weapons and warriors, but in justice, equality, and collective care. Scarry has opened a crucial conversation; let us push it further than perhaps she herself intended.
