The Pennsylvania Field as Mirror

I have read Elaine Scarry's essay three times now, and each time I have come away with a different sense of what it is doing. The first time I read it as a constitutional argument about war powers and nuclear weapons; the second time as a phenomenological meditation on time and judgment; the third time as something stranger and more interesting — an attempt to find, in the wreckage of September 11, a useable image of democratic citizenship at a moment when no other useable image was available. It is the third reading I want to develop here.

Consider the situation Scarry was writing from. By the summer of 2002, when the essay was presumably drafted, the public meaning of September 11 had been almost entirely captured by the executive branch and its allies. The dominant narrative ran something like this: a malevolent external enemy attacked American innocence; the President rose to the occasion; the country must now follow the President in a long, opaque, possibly nuclear war against an enemy whose composition keeps shifting. This narrative had certain political uses, and it foreclosed certain political possibilities. It cast the citizen as audience and the President as protagonist. It made deliberation into disloyalty and questioning into treason. It transformed a complex event into a simple story.

Scarry's essay can be read as an attempt to wrest one piece of September 11 free from this narrative — to find, within the event, an image that could serve as the basis for a different politics. The Pennsylvania field, with its scattered wreckage and its uncertain story, becomes in her telling a kind of democratic icon. Here, she says, is what citizens are capable of, when they are not infantilized by their leaders, when they have the information they need, when they are allowed to act on their own judgment. Here is the alternative to the centralized, opaque, hierarchical defense apparatus that failed at the Pentagon. Here is what the founders had in mind when they wrote about the militia.

This is an audacious move and I am of two minds about it. On one hand, it seems to me genuinely necessary. If the only image of September 11 available to American political imagination is the image of an attacked nation cohering around its President, then the political space for dissent has been preemptively closed, and the wars to come are essentially inevitable. To insist that there is another image, another lesson, another set of inheritances available — this is the work of a public intellectual at a moment when public intellectuals are largely failing to do their work. Scarry should be commended for it.

On the other hand, the move depends on a reading of Flight 93 that the available evidence cannot fully bear, and that the reader has reason to suspect of being shaped by the conclusion it is trying to support. We do not actually know what happened on Flight 93 in the final minutes. The cockpit voice recorder has not been released. The reconstructions are based on phone calls and inferences. The passengers may have voted; they may have improvised; they may have failed and the plane may have crashed in confusion rather than triumph. The narrative of the "town meeting at 30,000 feet" is a beautiful construction, and Scarry has built it carefully, but it is a construction, and constructions of national myth tend to be at least somewhat false.

A more candid version of Scarry's argument might be this: *we don't know exactly what happened on Flight 93, but the available evidence is consistent with a story of distributed citizen deliberation, and this is a story we badly need right now*. That would be honest, and I would assent to it. The version actually offered is more confident than the evidence warrants, and the confidence is in service of a political project.

But here is the thing: every political tradition is built on stories of this kind, and the question is not whether the stories are perfectly accurate but whether they are pointed in useful directions. The story of the Boston Tea Party is mostly mythology too, as is the story of Lexington and Concord, as is the story of the Constitutional Convention as a gathering of disinterested philosophers rather than property-owning men with property-owning interests. The American political tradition runs on stories, and the question is which stories. Scarry is offering one. The Bush administration is offering another. The argument between them is not really an argument about Flight 93; it is an argument about what kind of country we want to be.

What kind of country do we want to be? Scarry's answer, in the deep structure of the essay, is: the kind of country that takes the Constitution seriously as a constraint on power; the kind of country that does not delegate the question of war to a single official; the kind of country that does not maintain a stockpile of weapons capable of ending civilization; the kind of country whose citizens understand themselves as agents of their own defense rather than as wards of a national-security state. This is a recognizable answer; it is, broadly, the answer of the American republican tradition from Madison through Lincoln to (in some moods) Eisenhower. It is not a partisan answer, though it currently has more purchase on the left than on the right. It is an answer that has been losing ground for half a century, and that may now be losing ground decisively.

Whether Scarry's essay can change the direction of this loss is doubtful. The forces aligned against the republican tradition — the size of the military-industrial complex, the structure of the news media, the temperament of the post–September 11 electorate, the ambitions of the current administration — are immense, and they are not going to be dislodged by an essay in a small intellectual journal. But essays are not measured by whether they win; they are measured by whether they preserve possibilities that would otherwise be lost. Scarry has preserved a possibility. The possibility is that the Pennsylvania field is not just a grave but an image — an image of citizenship in extremis, available to a country that has nearly forgotten what citizenship looks like.

I do not know what we will make of this image in the coming years. The administration is preparing for a war with Iraq that I expect Congress will authorize without serious debate, and that I expect the country will accept without serious resistance. The image of Flight 93 will be invoked in that war, on both sides, and most invocations will be cynical. The work of holding the image to its better meanings — the meanings Scarry has tried to draw out — will fall to readers, teachers, writers, and citizens who refuse to allow the image to be flattened into propaganda. It is not a hopeful task. But it is the task we have, and Scarry has shown us, in this essay, what one version of it looks like.

We can do worse than to read her carefully, argue with her honestly, and try to imagine the country she is trying to imagine.
