Diversified Gemini

"While Elaine Scarry’s critique of centralized military failure is valid, her proposal for an 'egalitarian defense' model is flawed because it romanticizes civilian trauma and unfairly shifts the state's fundamental responsibility for security onto ordinary citizens."

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” is a beautifully written, intellectually ambitious piece that forces us to reckon with the failures of our national defense on September 11. However, in her effort to find a democratic silver lining in the horrors of that day, Scarry places an impossible and deeply unfair moral burden on the American civilian. By elevating the desperate, tragic struggle of the passengers on United Flight 93 into a foundational model for "egalitarian national defense," Scarry romanticizes trauma and misreads the fundamental obligations of a modern state to its people.

The narrative of Flight 93 is undoubtedly one of incredible heroism. The passengers, realizing they were on a suicide weapon, made a collective decision to sacrifice their own lives to prevent the plane from destroying the Capitol or the White House. But this was an act of absolute last resort, forced upon them by the catastrophic failure of the systems designed to protect them. To look at this horrific scenario and conclude that it represents a "successful" model of defense is deeply unsettling.

Scarry writes that “this is the one form of defense available to us as a country, which passengers are at liberty to exercise or refrain from exercising.” But what kind of liberty is this? It is the liberty of victims trapped in a flying metal tube, given a choice only between dying passively or dying violently while fighting back. We cannot build a national security paradigm around the expectation that civilians will—or should have to—engage in hand-to-hand combat with terrorists.

By labeling the passengers “citizen-soldiers,” Scarry blurs a crucial distinction. Soldiers are volunteers who are trained, equipped, and legally authorized by the state to engage in violence. They accept the risks of combat as a condition of their service. Civilians boarding a commercial airliner are doing no such thing. They are mothers, businessmen, grandfathers, and children simply trying to travel from one city to another. It is the explicit duty of the state and its security apparatus to ensure they can do so safely.

If we adopt Scarry’s view that civilian vigilance and direct action are the true pillars of homeland defense, we invite a host of dangerous sociological consequences. We are already seeing the dark side of this "distributed defense" in the year since 9/11. When citizens feel it is their personal duty to defend the homeland, the result is often vigilantism, racial profiling, and violence. The spike in hate crimes against Arab-Americans, Sikhs, and Muslims is a direct result of citizens taking it upon themselves to identify and neutralize perceived "threats." An egalitarian defense model in a climate of intense fear quickly devolves into a mob mentality.

Furthermore, Scarry’s argument that relying on the military "infantilizes" the population ignores the complex division of labor in modern society. We rely on experts for countless aspects of our survival—surgeons to operate on our hearts, engineers to build our bridges, and a professional military to track and intercept international threats. Trusting in these specialized institutions is not a relinquishing of our democratic rights; it is a rational choice that allows a complex society to function. We do not demand that citizens perform their own surgeries to avoid infantilization; neither should we expect them to secure the airspace.

Scarry is entirely justified in her criticism of the unchecked executive war powers and the madness of the nuclear arms race. The military-industrial complex needs massive reform, and Congress must reclaim its constitutional authority over war-making. But the solution to a flawed top-down system is a reformed, transparent, and legally accountable top-down system. It is not a retreat into an idealized vision of civilian militias.

The passengers of Flight 93 should be remembered and mourned for their bravery in the face of certain death. But their actions were a tragic symptom of a massive security failure, not a blueprint for the future. A society that relies on the martyrdom of its ordinary citizens to defend its capital has failed at the most basic level of governance. We must demand a state that protects us, so that no civilian is ever again forced to take a vote on how they will die.
