"To prevent another Dust Bowl, the federal government must reform the Farm Bill to link crop insurance subsidies to strict conservation compliance and eliminate incentives for farming on ecologically sensitive land."

If we want to prevent another Dust Bowl, we need to look closely at the perverse incentives embedded in our federal farm policies. The tragedy of the 1930s was not merely an act of nature; it was a man-made disaster driven by economic policies that encouraged farmers to plow up millions of acres of fragile, marginal land to cash in on high wheat prices. Today, the federal government is making the exact same mistake through the heavily subsidized federal crop insurance program.

Currently, taxpayer-subsidized crop insurance guarantees farmers a baseline revenue regardless of yield. While a safety net is important, the current structure of the program removes the financial risk of farming on ecologically sensitive land. When farmers know the government will cover their losses if the rains fail, they are incentivized to drain wetlands, tear up native prairie grass, and plant water-intensive crops like corn on land that is fundamentally unsuited for it.

As Congress debates the 2012 Farm Bill, it is imperative that we link crop insurance premium subsidies to strict conservation compliance. Farmers who receive taxpayer support must be required to implement basic soil erosion controls and protect wetlands. Furthermore, we must strengthen the "Sodbuster" provisions to penalize the conversion of native habitats into cropland.

The market alone will not protect our soil and water, especially when corn prices are hovering near record highs. We are effectively paying farmers to take ecological gambles, and when those gambles fail—as they are failing spectacularly in this current drought—the public bears both the financial and the environmental cost. Real prevention means reforming the Farm Bill so that it rewards stewardship, not reckless expansion.
