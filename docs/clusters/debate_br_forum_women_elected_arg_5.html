"The most effective and durable way to increase women's representation in the United States is to replace the current winner-take-all, single-member district system with multimember districts and proportional representation."

Jennifer Piscopo’s essay makes a powerful case that the United States has defined women’s political equality too narrowly. I share her impatience with a Congress that remains three-quarters male one hundred years after the Nineteenth Amendment. I also agree that the “women don’t run” story too often individualizes a structural problem. But I am less persuaded that the language of a “right to be elected,” or the family of reforms grouped under positive action, should be the center of American reform. The more durable path to women’s representation lies in changing the electoral system itself.

The countries Piscopo cites differ from the United States in many ways, but one difference matters enormously: many use proportional representation or party-list systems. Gender quotas work most naturally where parties present lists of candidates and voters choose among parties, or where party organizations have strong control over nomination. In that setting, it is possible to require that lists include a certain percentage of women, or that women and men alternate positions on the list. The rule attaches to a party’s slate, not to a single office.

The United States is different not simply because Americans are philosophically attached to negative rights. It is different because nearly all legislative elections are single-member, winner-take-all contests. One district elects one representative. That structure turns every nomination into a zero-sum fight. If a party says that a given district “belongs” to a woman candidate this year, it is necessarily saying that specific male aspirants cannot be nominated there. Of course politics is always zero-sum in this sense, and incumbency has long protected men. But the institutional form makes gender balancing harder, more visible, and more legally vulnerable.

Winner-take-all elections also slow representational change by protecting incumbents. Members of Congress often remain in office for many years. If most incumbents are men, and if incumbents usually win renomination and reelection, then parity depends heavily on retirements, scandals, wave elections, and redistricting. Candidate training can help; party pressure can help; donor networks can help. But the structure itself produces bottlenecks.

This is why reformers should focus more on multimember districts and proportional or semi-proportional voting systems. If a district elects five representatives rather than one, parties and voters can support a more diverse team without requiring every contest to become a symbolic battle over a single slot. Ranked-choice voting in multimember districts, sometimes called single transferable vote, would allow voters to rank candidates while producing more proportional outcomes. Other systems could be considered as well, including open-list proportional representation. The precise design matters less than the basic shift away from one seat, one winner.

Such reforms would help not only women but also racial minorities, ideological minorities, independents, and third-party voters. That is important in the American context. Piscopo notes, correctly, that gender and race cannot be separated here. A gender-only reform can end up advantaging white women if it is not designed with intersectionality in mind. Electoral system reform has the virtue of widening the representational aperture more broadly. It asks how a diverse public can be represented by a diverse legislature, rather than carving out one category at a time.

There is also a democratic legitimacy advantage. Quotas are often criticized as limiting voter choice. Defenders respond that existing systems already limit choice through party gatekeeping and structural bias. That is true. Still, a reform that gives voters more choices is politically easier to defend than one that appears to constrain them. Multimember proportional systems can increase the number of viable candidates on the ballot, allow voters to support women without fearing they are “wasting” a vote, and reduce the spoiler logic that haunts American primaries and general elections alike.

Consider what happens now in a Democratic primary for an open congressional seat in a safe blue district. A large field forms. Several women run, several men run, perhaps multiple candidates of color run, and the winner may prevail with a small plurality. In that setting, candidates from underrepresented groups can split constituencies while a familiar male candidate with institutional backing slips through. Ranked-choice voting would not guarantee a woman’s victory, but it would allow voters to express layered preferences and reduce the penalty for supporting a candidate who is not initially perceived as the safest bet. In multimember districts, the improvement could be greater still: a slate of winners could better reflect the electorate’s complexity.

The same logic applies within parties. Piscopo is right that parties should take responsibility for recruitment, endorsements, and funding. But if we retain the current electoral architecture, party gender rules will always be awkward. A requirement that every shortlist include women is sensible; a requirement that party committees distribute money equitably is sensible. Yet neither solves the problem of one-person districts dominated by incumbency. In fact, strong party intervention in primaries can backfire if voters perceive insiders as trying to preselect candidates. American voters are suspicious of party bosses even when party bosses are trying to do something defensible.

Electoral system reform would not eliminate sexism. Women would still face harassment, fundraising barriers, media double standards, and unequal family burdens. But it would change the incentives of political actors. Parties trying to win multiple seats in a district would have reason to present a balanced slate. Voters would have reason to consider not only “my one representative” but the composition of representation overall. Campaign organizations would have reason to recruit beyond the usual suspects. Representation would become less of a heroic individual struggle and more of an institutional expectation.

There are constitutional and statutory obstacles, but they are not insurmountable. Congress has authority over congressional elections. States and cities have experimented with different voting systems before. Ranked-choice voting is already used in some places and is gaining attention. The current system is not mandated by democratic theory; it is a choice, and it can be changed. The single-member district is so familiar that Americans mistake it for democracy itself, but it is merely one mechanism of representation.

That said, I do not reject all positive action. Public financing, childcare support for candidates, anti-harassment enforcement, transparent party recruitment data, and gender-balanced party committees are worthwhile. They address real barriers without pretending that a pep talk can undo structural inequality. I would also support voluntary party targets, especially for open seats and internal leadership positions. If parties want to be taken seriously as democratic institutions, they should measure and disclose whom they recruit and whom they support.

But I worry that framing women’s underrepresentation primarily as a violation of a “right to be elected” may promise more than law can deliver. No one can literally have a right to win an election. At most, citizens can have a right to fair opportunity to compete and a collective interest in institutions that produce representative outcomes. Piscopo knows this distinction, but public debate often blurs it. Opponents of reform will seize on that ambiguity and say, “No one is entitled to office.” Reformers need an answer. The best answer is not entitlement; it is institutional design.

Democracy is not only about removing barriers. It is about building systems that translate public diversity into political power. On that point, Piscopo’s essay is exactly right. But the most American version of that project may not be gender quotas modeled on Latin America or Europe. It may be a broader redesign of winner-take-all elections that currently distort representation in many ways at once.

If we want more women in office, we should stop asking only how to prepare women for the system we have. We should ask why the system wastes so much talent. A Congress elected from larger multimember districts, with ranked or proportional voting, public financing, and transparent party recruitment would not guarantee parity overnight. But it would make parity far more plausible, and it would do so by deepening voter choice rather than narrowing it. That is a reform agenda worthy of the suffrage centennial.
