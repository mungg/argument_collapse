"The demand for mandatory GMO labeling is a legitimate request for consumer transparency that should be honored regardless of whether the technology poses a health risk."

**On the Right to Know**

I want to focus on a single piece of Pamela Ronald's argument, because I think it reveals more than she intends. That is her dismissal of GMO labeling. She marshals the standard arguments: GMO isn't a coherent category, there's no health risk to warn about, the FDA opposes mandatory labels, virtually all food has been altered in some sense. Each of these points has merit considered in isolation. Together, they form an argument that I think most thoughtful citizens should reject.

Begin with the framing. Ronald treats the demand for labels as if it were a demand for a warning label, like the surgeon general's caution on cigarettes. But this is a category mistake. Most labels on food are not warnings. They are information. "Made in the USA." "Pasture-raised." "Kosher." "Organic." "From concentrate." "Contains wheat." None of these labels carries the implication of danger. They carry the implication that some consumers care, for reasons of their own, about how the food was produced, where it came from, or what is in it, and that they would like to know.

The right to know what you are eating does not require that what you are eating be dangerous. It requires only that you are eating it, and that the information is available to those who produce and sell it. This is the principle behind country-of-origin labeling, behind nutritional panels, behind organic certification. Ronald compares mandatory GMO labels to labeling fruits for sugar content. The comparison fails because no one is demanding the latter. The fact that GMO labeling has been a sustained, organized demand from consumers in the United States, Europe, and elsewhere is data about consumer preference, and in a market economy that data deserves a hearing.

Why does GE production matter to consumers, if it isn't a health risk? Many reasons, all of them legitimate. Some consumers want to avoid supporting an industrial agricultural model dominated by a few large firms. Some want to avoid contributing to the glyphosate-resistance treadmill in U.S. fields. Some prefer to support seed systems that allow farmers to save and replant. Some are religiously or philosophically committed to certain views about the proper relationship between humans and the natural world. Some simply prefer, as a matter of taste and habit, the foods they grew up with. None of these reasons requires that transgenic DNA be hazardous to eat. All of them are sufficient reasons for a consumer to want to make an informed choice.

Ronald's response — that "GMO" is not a scientifically coherent category — is true and beside the point. Neither is "organic" a scientifically coherent category; it is a regulatory and cultural one. "Kosher" is even less so. We define categories for labeling purposes when there is enough consumer interest to sustain the definition, and the definition does not need to track natural kinds. Process-based labeling is normal in food markets, not exceptional.

It is also revealing that the loudest opposition to mandatory labeling comes from the firms that produce GE seeds and the food manufacturers who use them. If labels were merely uninformative, as Ronald suggests, why would the industry spend tens of millions of dollars to defeat California's Proposition 37, and to fight similar measures in Washington, Oregon, and elsewhere? The honest answer is that the industry knows labels will reduce sales. That is, the industry knows that a meaningful subset of consumers, given the information, would choose differently. This is not market failure. This is the market working. The opposition to labeling is, in effect, an argument that consumer preferences in this domain should be overridden because they are based on insufficient understanding. Maybe so, but that is a paternalistic argument, and one that scientists in particular should be wary of making.

A final point. Ronald notes that the food industry already labels voluntarily — products marketed as "non-GMO" have grown rapidly. She uses this to suggest that mandatory labels are unnecessary; the market is providing. But voluntary "non-GMO" labels are inadequate for the same reason voluntary "organic" labeling was inadequate before federal certification: in the absence of a uniform definition, claims proliferate without verification, and consumers cannot reliably distinguish them. Mandatory labeling, properly designed, would actually clarify the market rather than confusing it.

I do not think GE foods are dangerous, and I do not think labels should imply that they are. I do think consumers have a defensible interest in knowing how their food is produced, that this interest is independent of safety, and that resistance to labeling is more about commercial interest than about scientific principle. Ronald's essay is otherwise admirable. On this point, I think she has confused her opponents' weaker arguments for their strongest, and dismissed a reasonable demand on grounds that do not survive examination.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Food labels serve as neutral information about production methods and consumer preferences rather than being limited to safety warnings.
  2. 02Consumers have valid ethical, economic, and environmental reasons for wanting to identify GE products that do not depend on the scientific safety of the food itself.
  3. 03The lack of a strict scientific definition for 'GMO' does not preclude it from being a useful regulatory or cultural category, similar to 'organic' or 'kosher' labels.
  4. 04Industry opposition to labeling suggests a paternalistic desire to override consumer choice to protect commercial interests.
  5. 05Mandatory labeling would provide a necessary uniform standard that voluntary 'non-GMO' claims currently lack.
