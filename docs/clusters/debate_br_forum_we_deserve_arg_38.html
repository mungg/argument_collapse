"While the lead essay's historical and philosophical critique of AI is valuable, we must prioritize the immediate, practical governance of existing AI systems over utopian reimagining."

Morozov raises important questions about AI's origins and orientation, but his utopian prescriptions risk distracting us from the urgent practical task of governing AI we already have. Large language models are deployed at scale today, making consequential decisions about healthcare, hiring, credit, and criminal justice. People are being harmed by these systems now. The question is not what AI might have looked like in an alternate history without Cold War pressures, but how we ensure that deployed AI serves democratic values.

Several concrete governance mechanisms merit serious attention. First, algorithmic impact assessments—similar to environmental impact assessments—could require developers to evaluate how their systems affect civil rights, democratic participation, and social equity before deployment. Second, mandatory disclosure of training data, model architecture, and evaluation procedures would enable external auditing and public debate about AI systems' strengths and limitations. Third, stronger liability frameworks would hold developers and deployers responsible for foreseeable harms, creating incentives for safety and fairness that market incentives currently undermine.

The European Union's AI Act represents a step in this direction, though its risk-based approach has limitations. Categorizing AI systems by application domain misses the ways that similar technologies can serve quite different purposes depending on context and implementation. A more robust approach would focus on the functions AI systems perform and the stakes involved, regardless of the specific application. High-stakes decisions affecting human welfare—employment, housing, criminal justice, healthcare—should be subject to stringent requirements regardless of whether the system is technically classified as "high-risk" under a predetermined taxonomy.

Morozov's emphasis on democratic participation is valuable but requires operationalization. What would it mean for citizens to genuinely participate in AI governance? Technical complexity creates barriers to meaningful participation, but these are not insurmountable. Public deliberation processes, citizen assemblies, and sunset provisions requiring periodic reauthorization of AI systems could all help. So could mandatory diversity requirements for AI development teams, ensuring that the perspectives of affected communities are incorporated from the start rather than addressed after harms have occurred.

The tension Morozov identifies between instrumental and ecological reason need not be resolved philosophically before we act. Practical governance can proceed by trial and error, with rules being strengthened as we learn what works. The cost of getting governance wrong is high—lives damaged, trust eroded, democratic legitimacy undermined—but so is the cost of governance paralysis. We cannot afford to wait for a complete theory of intelligence before regulating the systems shaping human lives today.

Morozov is right that democratizing AI requires more than technical fixes. But he underestimates how much technical governance can accomplish even within the existing political economy. Not every democratic victory requires revolutionary transformation; incremental improvements can accumulate into significant change. The question is whether we can build institutions capable of learning from experience and adapting to new challenges—precisely the kind of adaptive governance that Morozov's more ambitious visions tend to neglect.
