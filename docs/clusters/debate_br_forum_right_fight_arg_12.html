"Effective counterterrorism intelligence depends on local police maintaining trust with immigrant communities by acting as mediators who resist federal tactics that prioritize mass surveillance and immigration enforcement over community safety."

The most important sentence in Daniel Richman’s essay may be his warning that turning the beat cop into “a potential source of personal ruin” will produce a net intelligence loss. For Arab, Muslim, South Asian, and immigrant communities in the United States, this is not an abstraction. Since September 11, many families have learned that a knock on the door from government may mean an interview, a visa inquiry, a detention, a frozen bank account, a workplace raid, or a humiliating public suspicion that never quite disappears. In that environment, “information sharing” can sound less like public safety than like surveillance.

Richman deserves credit for recognizing that immigrant communities are not merely objects of counterterrorism policy. They are participants in the safety of the country. Their members work in airports, drive taxis, run shops, teach students, serve in the military, worship in mosques, and raise children who are as American as anyone else’s. They are likely to be among the first to notice if a troubled young man is being drawn into violence, if a charity has been captured by extremists, or if a newcomer’s story does not add up. But people share such concerns only when they believe government will distinguish between a genuine threat and a community’s ordinary life.

The federal government has too often failed that test. The post-9/11 interviews of thousands of Middle Eastern men, the special registration program, the detention of immigration violators under a cloud of terrorism suspicion, and public rhetoric that blurs Islam, immigration, and security have all sent the same message: your status is conditional, your associations suspect, your mistakes dangerous. Even when officials insist that they are merely enforcing neutral laws, the pattern is obvious to those on the receiving end. When enforcement concentrates on particular nationalities and religions, neutrality is hard to credit.

Richman’s argument is that local police can mediate this relationship. In some places, they already have. A local officer who has helped a mosque with traffic during Friday prayers, responded to vandalism, protected a business, or attended neighborhood meetings may be trusted in ways the FBI is not. Local departments have practical reasons to preserve that trust. They need witnesses to robberies, domestic violence reports, cooperation in gang investigations, and calm during international crises. They cannot afford to have entire neighborhoods go silent.

But mediation is not automatic. It depends on the character of the local department and the rules under which it operates. An officer cannot build trust on Monday and participate in an immigration sweep on Tuesday. A police chief cannot ask community leaders for cooperation while refusing to tell them whether local officers are collecting political or religious information for federal files. A mayor cannot boast of inclusion while allowing residents to be questioned because of their ethnicity rather than individualized suspicion. Trust is not created by outreach meetings alone. It is created by restraint when restraint is costly.

Immigrant communities are often told that if they have nothing to hide, they have nothing to fear. That slogan misunderstands both immigration law and human experience. Many families include people of mixed status. Some have pending applications, old paperwork errors, overstayed visas, or relatives who are undocumented. Others are citizens but fear being drawn into investigations they do not understand. Some come from countries where police and intelligence services are instruments of repression. Some have experienced discrimination in the United States. Fear is not proof of guilt. It is often the rational response of people who know that bureaucracies make mistakes and that the costs of those mistakes fall on the weak.

The challenge is to design counterterrorism cooperation that treats communities as citizens and neighbors, not as mines from which information is extracted. Several principles follow.

First, local police should not enforce civil immigration law except in narrow circumstances tied to serious crime or specific security threats. This is not “sanctuary” for terrorism; it is sound policing. If a domestic violence victim fears deportation, she will not call. If a store owner believes reporting extortion may expose his cousin, he may stay quiet. If a mosque leader thinks a vague concern about a visitor will bring immigration agents down on half the congregation, he may handle the matter privately. That is bad for everyone.

Second, interviews should be based on facts, not categories. After September 11, federal authorities sought interviews with thousands of men largely because of age, gender, nationality, and immigration status. Such programs may look productive from Washington, but on the ground they announce that identity itself is suspicious. A targeted interview based on a lead is one thing; a census of fear is another.

Third, community outreach must be separated from intelligence recruitment. Of course relationships sometimes generate information, and no one should pretend otherwise. But if every town-hall meeting is perceived as an intelligence operation, the meetings will fail. Communities need spaces where they can raise civil rights complaints, hate crimes, school bullying, airport profiling, and immigration concerns without being treated as sources to be cultivated. Federal and local officials should listen even when the criticism is uncomfortable.

Fourth, local oversight matters. Richman is right that cities and counties can speak for local concerns more legitimately than distant federal agencies. But “community” must be understood broadly. Government should not rely only on a handful of male religious leaders or business elites. Muslim and Arab communities are internally diverse: black Muslims, immigrants from many countries, converts, secular Arabs, women’s organizations, students, professionals, recent refugees, and second-generation Americans. Some may distrust one another. If law enforcement hears only from the most convenient intermediaries, it will misunderstand the community and miss important information.

Fifth, anti-discrimination enforcement should be part of counterterrorism strategy. When mosques are vandalized, women wearing hijab are harassed, or students are bullied, public officials should respond visibly. Protecting communities from backlash is not a favor; it is a statement that they belong. A resident who sees police guarding a mosque during a tense period may be more willing to believe that the same police department is concerned with safety rather than suspicion.

Richman’s essay perhaps underplays the emotional reality of the last three years. For many Muslim Americans, the threat is twofold: fear of terrorism and fear of being blamed for terrorism. They watched the towers fall with horror like other Americans. Then many watched their neighbors look at them differently. Children were taunted. Charities were scrutinized. Travelers were pulled aside. Men disappeared into detention. Public officials sometimes spoke carefully, but the machinery of enforcement spoke more loudly.

That experience matters because counterterrorism depends on legitimacy. A government that wants help must show that it can be fair. It must admit mistakes. It must punish abuse. It must avoid public announcements that imply guilt before evidence is tested. It must recognize that the Constitution is not a technical obstacle but the common ground on which diverse communities can cooperate.

Local police can help, but only if they are willing to say no to federal practices that would destroy their relationships. The most valuable local contribution may not be more names in a database. It may be a warning to Washington: this tactic will make us less safe; this raid will silence witnesses; this interview program will stigmatize children; this demand will turn partners into suspects. If federal agencies listen, they may gain something more valuable than compliance. They may gain credibility.

The fight against terrorism is real. The Madrid bombings this year reminded everyone that civilians remain targets. No responsible community leader can ignore the danger of political violence committed in the name of Islam or any other cause. But a strategy that pressures Muslim and immigrant communities while claiming to protect them will fail morally and operationally. Security built on humiliation is brittle. Security built on equal citizenship is stronger.

Richman is right to seek a structure in which effectiveness and accountability reinforce each other. From the perspective of immigrant communities, the test is simple: when local police join the national counterterrorism effort, do residents feel safer calling them? If the answer is yes, the partnership may work. If the answer is no, the network will collect fear and call it intelligence.
