"Daniel Richman’s proposal for counterterrorism federalism is overly optimistic because local police departments lack the historical record, institutional incentives, and oversight mechanisms to reliably act as a check on federal overreach or as protectors of civil liberties."

Daniel Richman’s proposal for a counterterrorism-based federalism has the appealing ring of realism: enlist local police as partners, give them a voice, and the resulting domestic intelligence network will be both more effective and more protective of civil liberties. The argument is meticulously crafted, grounded in the political economy of federal-local relations, and refreshingly attentive to institutional incentives. Yet for all its sophistication, it rests on a deeply questionable premise: that local police departments, as currently constituted, are likely to serve as bulwarks against federal overreach. The historical record and the contemporary realities of urban policing suggest a much less happy ending.

Richman’s optimism about the “community policing revolution” is central to his case. He contends that local police, because they need community trust to fight street crime, will resist federal demands that threaten that trust. But community policing has been, in many cities, more rhetorical than real. For every Ann Arbor or Dearborn where sensitive officers have built bridges, there is a Los Angeles, a Cincinnati, or a New York where policing remains predominantly aggressive, suspicious, and targeted at minority communities. The same departments that have adopted the language of problem-solving have also embraced zero-tolerance tactics, quality-of-life crackdowns, and street-level drug enforcement that often alienate the very communities they are supposed to serve. Are these departments likely to exercise the kind of informed restraint that Richman envisions? Or will they simply incorporate counterterrorism intelligence-gathering into their existing patterns of surveillance of young men of color?

The experience with the “war on drugs” is instructive. For two decades, the federal government has used grants, task forces, and asset-forfeiture laws to enlist local police in a national campaign that is widely seen, particularly in minority neighborhoods, as an occupying force. Local departments have rarely exerted the “local veto” Richman hopes for; instead, they have eagerly swallowed the carrot of funding, even when it meant militarizing their forces and souring community relations. The Bush administration’s immigration enforcement push follows the same script. Richman acknowledges that few local departments have formally signed on, but he underestimates the quiet cooperation that already occurs. In many jurisdictions, local police routinely share information with immigration authorities, whether through formal 287(g) agreements or informal networks. The fact that the International Association of Chiefs of Police expresses qualms does not mean those qualms will govern operational behavior when a federal agent calls with a request.

Moreover, political accountability at the local level has often proved an unreliable safeguard for unpopular minorities. Mayors and city councils may be elected, but immigrant communities—especially those with large numbers of non-citizens—have little electoral clout. In the current political climate, shaped by the 2004 election and by widespread anxiety about terrorism and immigration, it is not hard to imagine local officials concluding that there are votes to be won by being “tough on terror,” even if it means rough treatment of Arabs, Muslims, or other suspect groups. Richman’s model assumes that the local political calculus will mirror the institutional interests of the police chief in maintaining order. But if the majority of voters see immigrant communities as a potential threat rather than a constituency, democratic accountability will push in precisely the wrong direction.

Richman also underestimates the power of secrecy to defeat accountability. He acknowledges that federal regulations often restrict local civilian oversight bodies from accessing intelligence information, citing the struggles in Portland and Boise. But he treats this as a problem that can be worked out through negotiation. In practice, when national security is invoked, local oversight collapses. City councils and audit committees are not cleared; even if they were, the information they would need to evaluate the costs and benefits of intelligence operations is precisely the information that will be withheld on operational grounds. The result is a system in which the executive branch at all levels operates with minimal transparency. That is not accountability; it is a license.

If local police are not the automatic source of restraint Richman imagines, what then? The answer is not to abandon local involvement, which is inevitable and probably necessary, but to complement it with robust and independent oversight mechanisms at the federal level. The 9/11 Commission, whose report Richman otherwise praises, recommended the creation of a strong Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board within the executive branch, equipped with subpoena power and a mandate to review domestic intelligence activities across agencies. That board, if properly funded and staffed, could begin to monitor the local-federal nexus that Richman describes. Yet as of this writing, the board exists only on paper; the administration has been slow to appoint its members and has resisted giving it teeth. Strengthening such institutional watchdogs is a surer path to accountability than hoping that local police chiefs will, out of enlightened self-interest, serve as guardians of liberty.

The Church Committee concluded in 1976 that the most serious abuses of domestic intelligence arose not from a few bad apples but from a systemic failure of oversight and legal constraint. Richman is right that judicial and legislative remedies are limited—surveillance decisions are often discretionary and invisible—but that is an argument for creating new oversight institutions, not for relying on the fortuitous alignment of local incentives. If we are to construct a domestic intelligence network, we must also construct a dedicated oversight architecture that cuts across federal, state, and local lines. A commission patterned on Britain’s independent reviewer of terrorism legislation, or a U.S. equivalent of an inspector general with cross-jurisdictional authority, could begin to fill the gap.

Richman has performed a valuable service by mapping the intergovernmental dynamics that will shape any domestic intelligence system. But his “happier ending” requires us to believe that local police departments, left to their own devices and their own parochial calculations, will reliably restrain the federal security apparatus. That is too much faith in institutions that have not earned it. The right fight is not merely to give local government a seat at the table, but to build a table strong enough to bear the weight of democratic scrutiny.
