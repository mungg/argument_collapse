"While effective altruism offers valuable practical correctives for philanthropy, its underlying framework of maximizing consequentialism is a flawed moral theory that flattens the complex, incommensurable values of human life into a single utility calculus."

Peter Singer’s effective altruism begins from a powerful intuition: if we can prevent great suffering at little cost to ourselves, we ought to do so. That intuition is hard to dispute. But the moral framework Singer builds upon it—a maximizing consequentialism that measures all value in terms of its contribution to aggregate well-being—is far more controversial than he lets on. By treating all goods as commensurable and all lives as units in a global utility pool, Singer flattens the rich complexity of our moral landscape and threatens to justify acts that should remain unthinkable. A more plausible ethics recognizes that some values, like justice and respect for persons, have a standing that cannot be traded off merely for the sake of producing better outcomes.

Singer’s essay repeatedly relies on the assumption that “saving a life is better than making a wish come true,” and that “saving three lives is better than saving one.” In most circumstances, these are unobjectionable. The problem arises when he extends this logic to choices about how we structure our lives, our relationships, and our society. He suggests that we should reject the widespread practice of leaving one’s estate to one’s children because the money could do more good elsewhere. He argues that giving to opera houses and museums isn’t doing the most good you can. He even implies that taking a Wall Street job and donating the proceeds is morally superior to pursuing a less lucrative but socially valuable career in, say, teaching or medicine. In each case, the specific goods at stake—parental love, artistic beauty, intellectual vocation—are treated as fungible inputs into a single optimization machine. That is a category mistake.

Consider the parent-child relationship. Singer concedes that parents may permissibly show preference to their own children, but only because “it would be unrealistic” to require strict impartiality, and because close families benefit children. This is a purely instrumental defense. It leaves no room for the thought that my child has a special claim on me that is not reducible to the impersonal benefits of family bonding. For most people, the duty to provide for one’s children is not a grudging concession to human weakness; it is a fundamental aspect of what it means to be a father or mother. To treat it as a second-best compromise with the demands of impartial maximizing is to misunderstand the very nature of parental love. It is the kind of alienation from one’s deepest commitments that makes a theory suspect.

Similar worries arise with regard to justice and rights. Singer briefly acknowledges that effective altruists “recognize that breaking moral rules against killing or seriously harming an innocent person will almost always have worse consequences than following these rules.” But his “almost always” is the key qualifier. In a purely consequentialist framework, the prohibition against killing an innocent person is merely a useful heuristic, not a principled constraint. If a convincing case could be made that a particular violation would produce a net increase in welfare, the theory must, on pain of inconsistency, endorse it. Singer warns against speculative utopian reasoning, citing the horrors of Lenin, Stalin, and Mao. But that warning is pragmatic, not principled. It leaves intact the theoretical possibility that some future effective altruist, armed with sufficiently robust evidence, might find herself obligated to commit grave injustices in the name of the greater good. A deontological constraint, by contrast, rules out such actions categorically, not merely because they are usually counterproductive but because they violate the dignity of persons. That difference matters.

Singer’s treatment of the arts exposes another vulnerability. He concedes that promoting the arts would be a worthy goal in a world without extreme poverty, but in our world, donating to museums is not an effective use of money. This judgment assumes that the value of art is purely a matter of the experiences or satisfactions it produces. But many people, including many effective altruists, would hold that great art has intrinsic worth, independent of its effects on well-being. A symphony or a painting is not simply a vehicle for generating utility; it is a bearer of beauty and meaning that enriches human existence in ways that resist reduction to a single metric. If that is so, then the choice between funding an art institution and saving a life is not a simple comparison of two quantities on the same scale. It is a tragic choice between incommensurable values. Effective altruism, by forcing everything onto a single scale, pretends that such tragedy can be avoided by better measurement. It cannot.

None of this is to deny that effective altruism has made valuable contributions. Its emphasis on evidence and its critique of thoughtless donation are important correctives. But as a comprehensive moral theory, it is dangerously incomplete. To live well, we need more than a utility calculus. We need an ethics that can honor the distinctive claims of love, justice, and beauty without reducing them all to the same unadorned question: how much good does it do?
