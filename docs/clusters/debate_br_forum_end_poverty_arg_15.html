"Technology should be viewed as a secondary instrument rather than a primary solution for poverty, as its effectiveness is entirely dependent on the strength of the human systems and institutions it is meant to serve."

Kentaro Toyama’s essay is most persuasive where it is least fashionable: in its insistence that poverty is not a technical malfunction. The temptation he describes is real. A laptop in a classroom, a telecenter in a village, or a mobile phone in the hand of a farmer gives donors, engineers, and ministers something visible to point to. It photographs well. It can be counted. It can be procured in bulk. It carries the sheen of modernity. But poverty is made of less photogenic things: weak schools, bad roads, indifferent bureaucracies, caste exclusions, gender violence, low literacy, insecure land tenure, absent health systems, and the quiet exhaustion of people who have had too few chances for too long.

Toyama’s “technology as magnifier” formulation captures something development agencies routinely forget. A computer in a good school may help a teacher show simulations, assign essays, and connect students to a wider world. A computer in a school where the teacher is absent, the electricity fails, and children cannot read is more likely to become a locked box, a trophy, or a gaming machine. This is not because poor children are less curious or poor parents less ambitious. It is because tools yield returns only when embedded in routines, institutions, skills, and incentives.

The strongest evidence for Toyama’s view comes from the examples he gives of his own relative successes. Digital Green did not work because a camcorder is magical. It worked because it was attached to agricultural extension: existing groups, trusted intermediaries, local content, repeated meetings, and practical demonstrations. The technology lowered the cost and increased the persuasive power of a human process. That is exactly what a magnifier does. It made something already valuable travel farther.

This distinction matters because the politics of development budgets is a politics of substitution. When enthusiasts say “technology can scale,” they often mean, “technology can scale without the slow expense of building human systems.” But that is precisely the promise that should make us suspicious. If a government cannot train, pay, supervise, and motivate teachers, why should we believe it can deploy and maintain a million laptops in a pedagogically useful way? If a health ministry cannot keep rural clinics stocked with antibiotics, what will an SMS reminder accomplish beyond reminding patients that the clinic has none? If a land office is corrupt, digitizing land records may simply allow corruption to proceed faster, with a better database.

The lesson is not that ICT4D should be abandoned. It is that it should be demoted from ideology to instrument. We should ask of every project: What human system is this technology strengthening? Who will use it, for what purpose, with what training, under what incentives, and with what recurring budget? What would happen if the same money were spent on teachers, nurses, roads, nutrition, or deworming? Who is responsible when the pilot ends? These questions are dull compared with “Can the cellphone end poverty?” but they are the questions that determine whether anything changes.

Toyama is also right to challenge the careless rhetoric of “rights” around Internet access. There is a case for treating communication as a public good, and perhaps even for defending freedom of expression online as a civil liberty. But to call Internet access a fundamental right in the same breath as water, sanitation, basic education, and protection from violence risks moral inflation. A child weakened by worms, a woman walking miles for water, or a farmer trapped by debt does not need us to declare the Internet sacred. She needs us to make hard choices among competing goods. Those choices may sometimes include connectivity, but they should not begin with connectivity as an article of faith.

Still, one should be careful not to turn the magnifier thesis into a counsel of despair. Human capacity is not fixed. Institutions can be built. The poor are not empty vessels awaiting enlightened administrators. In many places, poor communities already possess dense knowledge, practical intelligence, and mutual-aid networks. The problem is often that formal systems refuse to recognize or support them. Technology can help when it is introduced as a servant of those capacities: a mobile phone that lets a migrant worker remit money safely; a radio program that reinforces vaccination campaigns; a database that helps a competent nonprofit track patients; a camera that lets farmers see people like themselves adopting a new seed practice.

What must go is the fantasy of bypass. Technology cannot bypass politics, institutions, or culture. It cannot bypass the need for teachers by dropping laptops into classrooms. It cannot bypass gender inequality by handing a phone to a woman whose husband controls her movements and income. It cannot bypass corruption if the corrupt still control the records and the remedies. Indeed, as Toyama notes, technology may magnify predation as readily as it magnifies service.

The development community needs a rule of thumb: fund technology only where the non-technological program would be worth funding without it. If an agricultural-extension project has sound agronomy, trusted field staff, and credible evidence that farmers adopt its advice, then video or mobile tools may be a bargain. If a school has teachers who teach and administrators who care, computers may enrich learning. If a clinic has medicines and nurses, mobile reminders may improve adherence. But if the underlying program is hollow, technology will not fill it.

The boy in Retawadi typing “Alaska” is a haunting image because it captures the mismatch between aspiration and relevance. His parents were not foolish; they were doing what poor parents everywhere do, buying a small claim on a future they had been told was modern and English-speaking and computerized. The tragedy is not that they wanted too much from technology. It is that the institutions around them offered so little else. A humane development agenda would honor that aspiration by investing first in the capacities that let tools matter: literacy, health, accountable government, capable organizations, and economic opportunity. Then, and only then, the machines can magnify something worth magnifying.
