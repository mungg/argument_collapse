"To transform Daniel Richman’s federalist counterterrorism vision into reality, the United States must implement concrete institutional reforms that formalize local authority, restructure funding, and mandate reciprocal information sharing."

Daniel Richman's essay offers a compelling vision of federalist counterterrorism, but it needs concrete institutional mechanisms to become more than aspiration. The 9/11 Commission Report, released in July 2004, provides both momentum and a framework for action. What follows are specific proposals for realizing the federalist vision that Richman describes.

First, Congress should establish a formal intergovernmental liaison structure for counterterrorism intelligence. This body should include representatives from municipal police departments (not just state-level officials, as Richman correctly criticizes), federal agencies, and community organizations. It should meet regularly, not just in crisis moments, and should have genuine authority to set priorities and review practices. The current ad hoc approach—where cooperation depends on personal relationships and temporary initiatives—has proven inadequate.

Second, homeland security funding should be restructured to flow directly to local departments based on risk assessments rather than population formulas. Richman correctly documents the absurdity of current allocations: California receiving $14 per person while Wyoming receives $61. The formula appears to reflect political rather than security considerations. A risk-based formula would account for population density, presence of potential targets, and intelligence about specific threats. This would both improve security and reduce local grievances about federal priorities.

Third, any local police involvement in immigration-related activities should be explicitly limited and defined. The CLEAR Act should be rejected in favor of legislation that prohibits local police from immigration-specific enforcement while preserving their authority to cooperate in genuine emergency situations. Federal agencies, not local departments, should bear primary responsibility for immigration enforcement.

Fourth, the FBI's information-sharing practices should be reformed. Local departments routinely complain that information flows up to federal agencies but does not return. This asymmetry undermines cooperation. The FBI should be required to share threat assessments with state and local partners at a level of detail appropriate to their operational needs. The classification system, which currently restricts far too much information, should be reformed to allow broader sharing with law enforcement partners.

Fifth, civil liberties oversight should be built into the intergovernmental structure, not left to external review. Local police departments have strong incentives to gather intelligence even when that intelligence is not operationally useful; the pressure to "do something" after an attack is intense. Oversight mechanisms should include community representatives, not just law enforcement officials, and should have authority to review practices and recommend changes.

These proposals would not eliminate the tension between effectiveness and accountability that Richman identifies, but they would structure that tension more productively. The goal is not perfect security achieved through perfect cooperation but rather a system that acknowledges trade-offs and attempts to minimize the damage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Congress should establish a formal intergovernmental liaison structure that gives municipal police and community organizations genuine authority to set intelligence priorities.
  2. 02Homeland security funding must be redirected from population-based formulas to direct, risk-based allocations for local departments to address actual security needs.
  3. 03Federal legislation should explicitly prohibit local police from general immigration enforcement to protect their community relationships and intelligence-gathering capabilities.
  4. 04The FBI must reform its information-sharing practices to eliminate the current asymmetry where data flows up from local agencies but threat assessments are not returned.
  5. 05Civil liberties oversight involving community representatives should be integrated directly into the intergovernmental intelligence structure rather than relying on external review.
Diversified MiniMax

"While local police involvement is essential for effective counterterrorism, Richman’s optimistic vision of structural accountability will fail without formal institutional reforms that mandate federal information sharing and protect local autonomy."

Richman's central insight—that effectiveness and accountability in domestic counterterrorism are structurally linked rather than opposed—is compelling, and his analysis of the federalism dynamics is largely correct. But his optimism about the "happier ending" glosses over institutional obstacles that deserve more careful attention. The question is not whether local engagement would improve the system, but whether the mechanisms he proposes can actually deliver that improvement given the political and bureaucratic realities of federal-state-local relations.

Consider the National Criminal Intelligence Sharing Plan that Richman mentions approvingly. Launched in May 2004, this initiative is indeed a step in the right direction, but its very structure reveals the limitations of voluntary cooperation. The plan depends on the good faith of local departments and the willingness of federal agencies to genuinely share authority. As Richman himself documents, the track record here is poor. Local police have complained consistently about information flowing up but not down. FBI officials are notoriously reluctant to share intelligence with state and local partners, viewing such sharing as potentially compromising ongoing investigations—though critics would note that this justification often masks institutional self-protection.

The JTTF model that Richman cites as a success is instructive precisely because it shows where cooperation works and where it fails. The task forces function well in operational coordination—joint raids, undercover stings, intensive surveillance—because everyone understands the mission and the lines of authority are clear. But this is the easy part. The hard part is the ongoing, diffuse intelligence collection that might prevent an attack before it occurs. This requires the kind of community relationships that local police possess in abundance, but it also requires the trust that those relationships can survive federal involvement. And that trust remains fragile.

Richman's argument about immigration enforcement deserves particular scrutiny. He correctly identifies the "chilling effect" that immigration enforcement would have on community cooperation, and he rightly criticizes pending legislation that would mandate such enforcement at the local level. But his proposed solution—that the dimensions of police-ICE interaction "need to be a matter of negotiation, not federal fiat"—assumes a negotiating dynamic that doesn't exist. Local departments have little leverage when the alternative is losing federal funding. The National League of Cities' opposition to unfunded mandates is correct but toothless. Without structural changes to the funding formulas, "negotiation" will mean acceptance on federal terms.

The deeper problem is accountability. Richman argues that local police involvement will make the intelligence network more accountable because police chiefs report to elected mayors, who face voters. This is true but incomplete. Mayors have strong incentives to be seen as tough on crime and terrorism; they have weaker incentives to protect civil liberties in immigrant communities that may not vote in large numbers. The history of local police abuse—from surveillance of political groups to discriminatory enforcement against minorities—shows that local accountability is no guarantee of rights protection.

What would actually make the system work? First, genuine information sharing requires institutional guarantees that currently don't exist. The FBI should be required to share threat assessments with state and local partners, not merely solicit their cooperation. Second, homeland security funding should flow directly to local departments, not through state intermediaries that have different priorities. Third, any local police involvement in immigration-related intelligence should be limited to genuine emergencies, with clear protocols that prevent the information from reaching federal immigration authorities.

Richman is right that we need to engage local agencies rather than dismiss them. But engagement requires not just cooperation but also genuine constraints on federal authority—and the history of federal-state relations in criminal law suggests that those constraints are unlikely to emerge from goodwill alone. We need institutional reforms that make accountability structural rather than aspirational.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current voluntary model of intelligence sharing is insufficient because federal agencies, particularly the FBI, are historically resistant to sharing information downward with local partners.
  2. 02The 'negotiation' between local departments and federal immigration authorities is an illusion because the federal government uses the threat of withholding funds to force local compliance.
  3. 03Local electoral accountability is an unreliable safeguard for civil liberties because mayors often prioritize 'tough on crime' optics over the rights of marginalized immigrant communities.
  4. 04Effective domestic intelligence requires bypassing state-level intermediaries to provide direct federal funding to the local departments that actually manage community relationships.
  5. 05Structural accountability must be codified through legal requirements for federal transparency and strict protocols that decouple local intelligence gathering from federal immigration enforcement.
