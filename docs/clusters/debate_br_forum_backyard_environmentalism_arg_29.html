Beyond the Backyard: Scale, Resources, and Real Participation

The lead essay celebrates the evolution from first-generation "backyard environmentalism"—where communities organized to resist harmful activity in their neighborhoods—toward second-generation forms of participation that involve citizens as joint architects of environmental solutions. This trajectory is compelling in theory. But practice reveals severe constraints on the "deep participation" the authors endorse.

The Chesapeake Bay Program's evolution through successive phases of citizen involvement illuminates these constraints. In the 1960s and early 1970s, participation meant "conventional public education through publications, public meetings, hearings, and mass media." When monitoring requirements exceeded governmental capacity, emphasis shifted to teaching volunteers "to mimic the monitoring and reporting protocols developed by scientific experts." Citizens became "quasi-experts by imitation." This approach—replicating expert protocols—remains limited because it does not enable genuine deliberation about what should be monitored and how data should be interpreted.

The tributary strategy that emerged later attempts something more ambitious: devolving responsibility to semi-autonomous teams comprising government officials, scientific experts, agricultural and industry representatives, and citizen volunteers. The authors describe how these teams "draw on a unique mix of local knowledge, expert science (adapted to local needs), and basin-wide experience." But what enables ordinary citizens to participate effectively in such teams? The authors offer no systematic account of the resources, training, and time that meaningful participation requires.

Consider the Habitat Conservation Plans in Southern California. These agreements involve developers, public officials, conservationists, and scientists negotiating complex conservation strategies for millions of acres. The participants bring vastly different resources to these negotiations. Developers and industry representatives typically employ professional lobbyists, attorneys, and environmental consultants. Conservation organizations may have staff and technical expertise, though often at lower levels than their adversaries. Local community members—those who will experience the environmental consequences of decisions—frequently lack the time, expertise, and financial resources to participate effectively.

The authors acknowledge that "the quality of participation in local deliberation is everywhere uneven." They propose that national environmental organizations "reinvent themselves as independent monitors of local performance and poolers of best practices." But this vision reconstitutes rather than democratizes expertise. Instead of federal regulators making decisions, we have national environmental organizations reviewing local agreements. The citizen's role may remain as spectator rather than participant.

Genuine deep participation requires capacities that the rolling-rule regime does not systematically create. Training in ecological science, legal expertise, negotiation skills, and access to technical information—these resources are not equally distributed. A framework that assumes participatory equality while ignoring the structural inequalities that pervade environmental negotiations risks mystifying power relations. The language of deliberation and mutual accountability may mask the reality that some voices carry authority while others remain marginal.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The evolution of citizen involvement in the Chesapeake Bay Program shows that participation often remains limited to mimicking expert protocols rather than enabling true deliberation.
  2. 02The authors provide no account of how ordinary citizens are supposed to acquire the necessary time, training, and technical resources to negotiate as equals against well-funded industry professionals.
  3. 03In complex negotiations like Habitat Conservation Plans, the vast disparity in financial and legal resources between developers and local residents undermines the possibility of fair outcomes.
  4. 04The suggestion that national environmental organizations act as monitors risks merely replacing one set of distant experts with another, leaving the local citizen as a spectator rather than a participant.
  5. 05Without a systematic effort to redistribute the capacities required for participation, the language of 'mutual accountability' serves to mask existing power imbalances.
