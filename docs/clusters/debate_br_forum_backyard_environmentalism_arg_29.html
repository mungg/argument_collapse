The Miller Bill and the Future of Experimentalist Regulation

The lead essay concludes with an extended discussion of the Miller Bill (HR 960), which would establish minimum requirements for Habitat Conservation Plans and create a framework for monitoring their implementation. The authors see this legislation as potentially transformative—a way to "crystallize this democratic regulatory reorientation in political discussion" by inserting it explicitly into the reform agenda. I want to examine whether this optimistic assessment is justified.

The Miller Bill's core provisions require HCPs to incorporate "objective, measurable biological goals aimed at species recovery," monitoring regimes, regularized reporting, and "appropriate adaptive management measures." Large-scale HCPs would require "substantial public participation," and the Secretary of the Interior would review each plan triennially, publishing annual reports on the status of all HCPs.

These requirements respond directly to the deficiencies the authors identify: inadequate monitoring, insufficient public access, and the inability of the current system to learn from local innovations. If enacted, the Miller Bill would create the informational infrastructure that the rolling-rule regime requires. Triennial reviews would provide the higher-order mechanism that the authors argue earlier programs lacked—the capacity to "adjust the frame of intervention itself according to the findings of initial investigations."

Yet the Bill's prospects are uncertain. Environmental organizations remain divided about HCPs, with some preferring strict Section 9 enforcement to negotiated development permits. Property rights advocates view HCPs as government coercion disguised as collaboration. The Bill attempts to thread between these positions by establishing minimum standards while preserving local flexibility. This balancing act may satisfy neither side.

More fundamentally, the Bill addresses a single program—HCPs—rather than the broader regulatory transformation the authors envision. TRI, TURA, and the Chesapeake Bay Program operate under different statutory authorities with different institutional structures. Creating an effective framework for one program does not guarantee that the rolling-rule regime will diffuse to others. The authors acknowledge that "Congress is noticing the anomalies of the new regimes as viewed in the light of the legislation from which their authorization is derived." Reforming each statute to accommodate experimentalist governance would require sustained political attention that may not materialize.

The authors suggest that passage of the Miller Bill would "subtly modify both its own legislative role and that of the administrative agency." Congress would shift from setting circumscribed goals and delegating implementation to authorizing the framework within which citizens determine how to pursue broad purposes. The Department of the Interior would police the framework rather than crafting agreements and determining acceptability.

This vision of institutional transformation is compelling. But it requires not merely legislative action but a change in how environmental policy is understood. The current frame—national standards implemented by federal and state agencies, enforced through administrative and judicial mechanisms—has deep roots in Progressive Era reforms. Transforming this frame to accommodate experimentalist governance would require not only new statutes but new patterns of agency behavior, new forms of public participation, and new expectations about what environmental regulation can achieve.

The Miller Bill offers an opportunity. Whether it becomes a stepping stone to broader transformation or merely a technical fix for HCP implementation remains to be seen. The authors have provided a compelling intellectual framework for understanding what is at stake. The political work of translating this framework into institutional reality lies ahead.
