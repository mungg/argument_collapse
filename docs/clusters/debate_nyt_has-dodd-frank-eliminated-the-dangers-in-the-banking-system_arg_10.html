"While Dodd-Frank has made traditional banks safer, it is insufficient on its own because it has not fully addressed the systemic risks and potential for panic within the broader shadow-banking system."

The debate over Dodd-Frank and the largest banks is important, but it is too narrow if it asks only whether JPMorgan Chase or Bank of America can be put through bankruptcy. The deeper question is whether the financial system’s capacity for panic has been contained. On that question, the answer is mixed.

Dodd-Frank made traditional banks safer. They are better capitalized, more closely supervised and less free to hide risks off balance sheet. The living-will rejection is a useful prod. It tells the banks that legal complexity, internal funding arrangements and global operations must be made simpler if failure is ever to be credible. That matters.

But financial crises do not respect the boundary between “banks” and “nonbanks.” The 2008 panic ran through repo markets, securitization vehicles, money-market funds, investment banks, derivatives exposures and A.I.G.’s insurance-related activities. Much of modern credit is created outside the familiar deposit-and-loan model. If regulation makes banks safer while risk migrates to less visible institutions, the public has not gained as much as it thinks.

This is why both sides of the current political argument are incomplete. Defenders of Dodd-Frank sometimes speak as if passing the law settled the matter. It did not. Many of its most important pieces depend on vigilant regulators, adequate budgets and continued political support. The recent court fight over the designation of large nonbank firms shows how contested the new regime remains. Derivatives clearing, while an improvement over the old bilateral market, also concentrates risk in central clearinghouses that must themselves be resilient and resolvable. Money-market fund reforms are still being phased in. Repo markets remain a potential source of runs.

At the same time, breaking up the biggest banks would not by itself solve the shadow-banking problem. A world of smaller banks can still finance a housing bubble. Hedge funds, asset managers or finance companies can still create crowded trades and fire sales. Foreign banks operating in dollar markets can still transmit stress to the United States. The next crisis may not look like the last one, which is why a reform agenda built only around the last crisis will always be late.

The better approach is layered: continue forcing large banks to simplify themselves; raise and enforce capital and liquidity standards; monitor short-term wholesale funding wherever it appears; make central clearinghouses subject to credible resolution plans; and give regulators the data and authority to respond to systemwide leverage before it becomes a panic.

Dodd-Frank was necessary, but not sufficient. It reduced some dangers in the banking system while leaving broader financial fragilities unresolved. The public should demand neither complacent reassurance nor one-sentence solutions, but a continuing effort to make failure ordinary rather than catastrophic.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Dodd-Frank successfully improved bank safety by increasing capital requirements, enhancing supervision, and forcing banks to simplify their internal structures for potential bankruptcy.
  2. 02Financial crises often originate in nonbank entities and markets, such as repo markets and derivatives, where risk can migrate when traditional banks are more strictly regulated.
  3. 03The effectiveness of Dodd-Frank is limited by its dependence on consistent regulatory vigilance, adequate funding, and the resolution of ongoing legal challenges regarding nonbank oversight.
  4. 04Breaking up large banks is an incomplete solution because smaller institutions and diverse financial entities can still contribute to asset bubbles and market-wide panics.
  5. 05A truly resilient financial system requires a layered approach that includes monitoring short-term wholesale funding and ensuring central clearinghouses have credible resolution plans.
