Diversified Gemini

"While the lead essay's critique of neoliberal AI is valid, its proposed shift toward 'ecological reason' and purposeless play is a privileged perspective that ignores the essential role of goal-oriented 'instrumental reason' in sustaining human life and labor."

In “The AI We Deserve,” Evgeny Morozov constructs a compelling dichotomy between the “instrumental reason” of mid-century bureaucracy and the “ecological reason” of playful, purposeless eolithism. He argues that modern AI, forged in the crucible of Cold War teleology, is trapped in a paradigm of problem-solving that serves the “Efficiency Lobby.” To rescue AI, he suggests, we must reconnect it with a more fluid, contextual, and exploratory human intelligence, utilizing the technology to model alternative futures rather than simply automating the present. While Morozov’s critique of the neoliberal framing of AI is astute, his proposed solution—a retreat into the aestheticized, intellectualized play of the “flâneur”—betrays a profound detachment from the material realities of labor, particularly care work and social reproduction, which cannot simply be “played” with or abstracted away.

Morozov’s hero is Hans Otto Storm’s prehistoric wanderer, a man who is “fed, contented, thinking preferably about nothing at all,” who stumbles upon a stone and serendipitously imagines it as a spearhead or a toy. This state of idle curiosity is held up as the pinnacle of human cognitive freedom, the antidote to the rigid, goal-oriented mindset of the organization man. But one must ask: who fed this flâneur? Who ensured he was contented? Who is watching his children while he strolls the stonefield thinking about nothing?

The luxury of purposelessness is exactly that—a luxury, historically subsidized by the invisible, highly purposive, and relentless labor of women, servants, and the working class. By framing “ecological reason” around the idle play of the unburdened individual, Morozov inadvertently replicates the very patriarchal and class-blind biases he claims to critique in the Cold War AI pioneers. Instrumental reason—the necessity of solving problems efficiently and reliably—is not merely a corporate pathology; it is the fundamental requirement of sustaining human life.

Consider the realms of healthcare, education, and eldercare. These are domains defined by urgent, concrete problems that require reliable, goal-oriented solutions. A nurse administering medication, a teacher managing a classroom of thirty children, or a social worker navigating an overwhelmed welfare system does not have the privilege of engaging in “eolithic bricolage.” They are engaged in the urgent maintenance of human survival and dignity. When AI is introduced into these spaces, the primary concern of the workers is not whether the tool allows for “purposeless purpose,” but whether it functions safely, reduces their crushing administrative burden, and allows them more time for the deeply human, relational aspects of their work.

Morozov dismisses the desire for efficiency as the domain of the “Efficiency Lobby,” associating it solely with capitalist extraction and militaristic control. But efficiency in the realm of social reproduction is a matter of life and death, of sanity and exhaustion. If an AI system can reliably summarize patient records or automate the scheduling of home care visits—tasks deeply rooted in instrumental reason—it represents a tangible, desperately needed relief for an overworked, predominantly female workforce. To tell these workers that they should instead be using AI to “simulate possibilities” or engage in “idle curiosity” is patronizing and out of touch with the brutal demands of the care economy.

Furthermore, Morozov’s turn to Latin American state-socialist projects like Project Cybersyn as models for “ecological reason” is deeply paradoxical. Cybersyn, masterminded by Stafford Beer for the Allende government, was the apotheosis of cybernetic instrumentalism. It was designed to manage industrial production, monitor quotas, and optimize logistics in real-time. It was a tool of statecraft and economic survival, not a sandbox for eolithic play. The fact that it was intended to serve a socialist project does not make it any less teleological. It demonstrates that the working class and socialist governments desperately need instrumental reason and problem-solving technologies to survive against the pressures of domestic reaction and international capital.

The problem with AI is not that it is goal-oriented. The problem is who gets to set the goals, and who captures the value generated by achieving them. Morozov rightly points out that under corporate control, AI’s goals are aligned with profit maximization and the commodification of user behavior. The solution, however, is not to abandon problem-solving for a romanticized, aimless “ecological reason.” The solution is the democratic socialization of the goals themselves.

We need a “Humanity Lobby” that fights for AI that is designed, governed, and owned by workers and communities. This means involving nurses in the design of diagnostic algorithms to ensure they augment rather than deskill clinical judgment. It means giving teachers the power to shape how personalized learning tools are deployed in their classrooms, protecting student privacy over corporate data harvesting. It means using optimization algorithms to distribute public resources fairly and efficiently, directly addressing the material needs of the most vulnerable.

Morozov’s vision of using AI to model “diverse social trajectories” and foster public deliberation is a worthy addition to democratic life. But it must be layered on top of, not positioned in opposition to, the hard, purposive work of social maintenance. A truly democratic AI must first serve the needs of those whose daily labor sustains the world, providing the efficient, reliable tools necessary to ease their burdens. Only when the foundational problems of material survival and care are solved can society as a whole enjoy the idle curiosity of the eolithic flâneur. Until then, rejecting instrumental reason is a privilege few can afford.

Diversified Gemini

"While Evgeny Morozov’s critique of corporate-controlled AI is valuable, his proposal to replace 'instrumental reason' with 'eolithism'—a philosophy of aimless, playful discovery—is a flawed romanticization that ignores the rigorous engineering required to make AI useful and the urgent global problems that demand goal-oriented solutions."

Evgeny Morozov’s “The AI We Deserve” is a profound meditation on the philosophical underpinnings of artificial intelligence, tracing a through-line from the instrumental rationality of Cold War cybernetics to the neoliberal commercialization of generative AI. He argues that our current technological paradigm is obsessed with efficiency and problem-solving, leaving no room for “ecological reason”—the open-ended, contextual, and playful discovery embodied by Hans Otto Storm’s concept of “eolithism.” By invoking Latin American experiments in socialist cybernetics, Morozov calls for a radical political project to untether AI from the market and place it in the service of democratic deliberation. While his critique of the “Efficiency Lobby” is vital, Morozov’s attempt to transpose the concept of “eolithism” onto digital technologies—particularly generative AI—is fundamentally flawed. By romanticizing the aimless flâneur, he obscures the intense, highly structured cognitive labor required to actually make AI useful, and inadvertently promotes an aestheticized view of technology that ignores the urgent need for robust, reliable engineering in a world facing cascading crises.

Morozov relies heavily on Storm’s imagery of the prehistoric wanderer who picks up a stone and, in a moment of idle curiosity, envisions it as a spearhead. He contrasts this with “design,” which begins with a fixed goal. He argues that ChatGPT acts as a sort of experimental workshop for this eolithic process, allowing users to “mess about” without a predefined telos. But this analogy collapses upon close inspection. The stone in Storm’s example is a naturally occurring object. An LLM is a hyper-engineered, mathematically rigid artifact containing billions of parameters optimized through gradient descent.

When a user sits down at ChatGPT, they are not a prehistoric flâneur strolling through a field. They are a pilot stepping into the cockpit of a massively complex semantic engine. Interacting with an LLM effectively requires a highly intentional, structured form of communication known as "prompt engineering." To get a generative AI to produce anything of sustained value—whether it is a functional language-learning app or a coherent block of code—requires rigorous, iterative, goal-oriented refinement. The user must constrain the model’s latent space, correcting its hallucinations and guiding its outputs. This is not purposeless play; it is the very essence of instrumental reason. It is design.

By labeling the interaction with AI as “eolithic,” Morozov conflates the *versatility* of a tool with a lack of purpose. A Swiss Army Knife can be used for many things, but one does not use it aimlessly. Generative AI is a general-purpose technology, but its utility is entirely dependent on human teleology. If we abandon problem-solving and goal orientation, as Morozov’s “ecological reason” seems to suggest, we are left with nothing more than the digital equivalent of a kaleidoscope—a mesmerizing, hallucinatory toy that produces endless variations of text and image without any anchor to reality.

Furthermore, Morozov’s dismissal of “instrumental reason” as a relic of Cold War militarism and neoliberal efficiency ignores the critical necessity of problem-solving in the 21st century. We are not living in a prehistoric idyll where we can afford to think “preferably about nothing at all.” We are facing urgent, existential problems: climate change, pandemics, logistical bottlenecks, and extreme wealth inequality. Overcoming these challenges will require massive, coordinated, highly efficient interventions.

We need AI to optimize energy grids, accelerate the discovery of new materials for solar cells, model complex epidemiological scenarios, and manage global supply chains to reduce waste. These are deeply teleological tasks. They require exactly the kind of rigorous, goal-oriented “design” that Morozov critiques. If the “Humanity Lobby” adopts eolithism as its guiding philosophy, it will relegate itself to producing interactive art and participatory budgeting dashboards while the Efficiency Lobby continues to build the infrastructure that actually runs the world.

Morozov’s invocation of Salvador Allende’s Project Cybersyn is ironic in this context. He praises Cybersyn as an example of posing problems politically rather than technologically. Yet, Cybersyn was a masterpiece of instrumental reason. It was designed to solve a very specific, life-or-death problem for the Chilean economy: how to manage national production and bypass reactionary strikes in real-time. It was not built for eolithic play; it was built for survival. The tragedy of Cybersyn is not that it lacked “ecological reason,” but that it lacked the computational power and political time to fully execute its teleological goals.

The solution to the democratic deficit in AI is not to abandon problem-solving for a romanticized notion of play. The solution is to democratize the power to define the problems. The issue with modern AI is not that it is efficient; the issue is that it is efficient at maximizing engagement and generating profit for a handful of tech monopolies, rather than being efficient at solving problems for the public good.

We do need a radical political project to reclaim AI. But that project must embrace, rather than reject, the power of engineering and design. We must demand public funding for AI research directed at climate mitigation, public health, and sustainable logistics. We need democratic oversight of datasets and algorithms to ensure they serve public needs rather than corporate surveillance. The “AI we deserve” is not an eolithic stone to play with; it is a meticulously designed engine to help us build a survivable future. We cannot afford to be flâneurs; we must be architects.
