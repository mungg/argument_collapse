"Judith Jarvis Thomson’s dismissal of the embryo's personhood is based on a narrow, empiricist view of reason that fails to account for the coherent natural law tradition grounding rights in the inherent nature of a human being."

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s essay is a model of clear reasoning, but its central epistemological claim—that reason does not compel acceptance of the personhood of the early embryo—rests on an impoverished conception of reason itself. By treating “reason” as a faculty that operates only on empirical data about psychological states, she ignores an entire tradition of moral reasoning that grounds human rights in the nature of the human person, not in transient attributes. It is this tradition that animates the papal encyclical Evangelium Vitae, and it deserves a more careful response than Thomson’s dismissal.

The encyclical states that the right to life from conception is “based upon the natural law” and is “knowable by reason itself.” Thomson retorts that this claim “simply will not do.” But what is natural law reasoning, and how does it differ from the empiricist psychology she assumes? Natural law ethics begins with the observable fact that we are living human organisms directed toward certain ends: we naturally seek to preserve our existence, to develop our capacities, to live in society, and to know the truth. The first precept of practical reason is that good is to be done and evil avoided. Because life is a fundamental good, intentionally destroying an innocent human life is always morally wrong. The crucial question is: what counts as a human life? The natural law answer is that a human life is a living individual substance of a rational nature. The embryo from conception is precisely such a substance: it is a distinct living organism that belongs to the human species and has the inherent active potential to develop the higher functions of rationality, sensation, and self-movement. It is not a mere “piece of human tissue,” as Thomson asserts, any more than a newborn is a mere “piece” because it cannot yet walk. It is a human being at an early stage of development.

Thomson’s objection—that the embryo lacks psychological states and therefore lacks interests and thus rights—misses this ontological point. The foundation of a right to life is not the actual exercise of capacities but the nature of the being. We recognize that a person in a reversible coma retains the right to life, not because she is currently experiencing anything, but because she remains a human person with a rational nature. Similarly, a human embryo, by virtue of being a living organism of the human species, is a person with that same nature, albeit in its initial stages. The capacity for rational thought is present not as an occurrent attribute but as a natural potency rooted in the organism’s constitution. If this were not sufficient, then one could never say when, in the gradual development from embryo to fetus to newborn, the right to life suddenly appears. Any line would be arbitrary.

Thomson suggests that one could reasonably think that the future development of the embryo into a person lends “no weight at all” to the idea that it is a person now. But this is to treat the embryo as a mere passive thing that will become a person, rather than as a being that is already a human individual with a built-in developmental program. There is no moment at which a non-human entity becomes human; the transformation is continuous, and the substance remains the same. The encyclical’s teaching, therefore, is not an irrational dogmatism but a coherent philosophical account of personal identity.

Furthermore, Thomson’s characterization of the encyclical as a “thoroughly groundless assault” on the moral integrity of those who support legal abortion is unfair. The encyclical’s language about a “crisis of the moral sense” is a diagnosis of a cultural shift that has numbed the conscience to the value of life, not a personal indictment of every pro-choice individual. It calls for conversion, not condemnation. And indeed, if the natural law case is sound, then there is objective moral truth at stake. In a pluralistic society, one may advance such a claim in the public square without thereby “persecuting” others. Thomson’s Bossuet analogy fails precisely because the pope does not ask for lawful power to persecute; he calls for laws that protect the innocent. A law banning abortion is not a law against women; it is a law against killing.

Finally, Thomson’s political argument—that a deeply valued liberty may not be constrained on grounds the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting—would have the effect of shrinking the public sphere to the lowest common denominator of moral agreement. But law often constrains liberty on controversial moral grounds: consider the prohibition of assisted suicide, or the ban on polygamy. The fact that some find such laws unreasonable does not make them exercises of bare force. The legitimacy of law rests on its aim to secure justice, not on the assent of every possible dissenter. If the just society requires protecting the most vulnerable, the law may and must do so, even if the powerful disagree.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Natural law reasoning identifies the embryo as a distinct human individual with an inherent active potential for rationality, rather than a mere piece of tissue.
  2. 02The right to life is based on the ontological nature of being a human organism rather than the current exercise of psychological states or interests.
  3. 03Any developmental point chosen to grant the right to life other than conception is arbitrary because the transformation from embryo to adult is a continuous process of a single substance.
  4. 04The papal encyclical’s critique of a 'crisis of the moral sense' is a philosophical diagnosis of cultural values rather than a groundless personal assault on the integrity of individuals.
  5. 05The legitimacy of law depends on its pursuit of objective justice and the protection of the vulnerable, even when the moral grounds for such constraints are contested by those whose liberty is restricted.
