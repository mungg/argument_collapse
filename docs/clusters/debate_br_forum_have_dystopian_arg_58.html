"The priority for reform must be shifted from redirecting AI technology to strengthening democratic institutions, as a functional democracy is a prerequisite for any effective oversight of AI."

Acemoglu correctly identifies democratic backsliding as both a cause and consequence of AI's current trajectory. But this insight should reshape the essay's priorities entirely. If democracy is the condition that enables all other reforms, then democratic strengthening must precede AI redirection.

The essay notes three factors weakening democracy: polarization, media fragmentation, and money in politics. These aren't independent of AI but are deeply intertwined with it. Social media algorithms amplify polarization; microtargeting enables personalized political manipulation; surveillance chilling effect reduces civic participation. AI isn't merely exacerbating democratic dysfunction—it's actively transforming its character.

This suggests a different sequence of priorities. Acemoglu proposes that we first redirect AI, then rely on democratic oversight to maintain the new direction. But if democratic institutions are compromised, how will they exercise the oversight required? The current U.S. Senate can't pass voting rights legislation. What makes us think it can implement a coherent AI policy?

The essay's treatment of democratic reform is surprisingly thin given how central it is to the argument. References to "democratic input and discourse" acknowledge the need but don't grapple with the institutional requirements. What specific reforms would enable democratic governance of AI? How would democratic institutions acquire the technical capacity to evaluate AI systems?

Consider the EU's approach. The proposed AI Act represents an attempt at democratic governance of AI. But it's criticized for being drafted primarily by industry advisors, for creating compliance burdens that favor large firms, and for lacking technical capacity to enforce its provisions. The gap between democratic aspiration and implementation reveals how difficult governance actually is.

A serious democratic reform agenda would include: technical advisory capacity insulated from industry capture; liability rules creating private rights of action for algorithmic harm; discovery and transparency requirements for AI systems affecting civil rights; and institutional designs that give affected communities meaningful voice. None of this requires the industrial policy Acemoglu advocates; it requires the institutional infrastructure of accountability.

The essay correctly observes that democratic backsliding creates a vicious cycle: weak democracy can't redirect AI, and ungoverned AI further weakens democracy. Breaking this cycle requires starting with democratic strengthening, not AI direction. The technology can wait; democracy cannot.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01AI is not just an external factor but is actively transforming and degrading the character of democracy through algorithmic polarization and political manipulation.
  2. 02Current political dysfunction, such as legislative gridlock, suggests that existing institutions lack the capacity to implement the complex AI policies Acemoglu proposes.
  3. 03Effective governance requires building specific institutional infrastructure, such as technical advisory bodies insulated from industry capture and liability rules for algorithmic harm.
  4. 04The 'vicious cycle' between weak democracy and ungoverned AI can only be broken by prioritizing democratic accountability over industrial policy.
  5. 05The gap between democratic aspirations and actual implementation in existing frameworks like the EU's AI Act highlights the urgent need for institutional reform over mere policy goals.
